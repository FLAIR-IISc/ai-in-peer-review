## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for learning neural network control policies for complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with supervised learning. CIO is used to generate a dataset of optimal trajectories, and a neural network is then trained to mimic these trajectories, enabling real-time interactive control.  The authors address challenges of scaling to high-dimensional spaces and ensuring stability through techniques like noise injection, distributed stochastic optimization, and careful network initialization. They demonstrate the method's effectiveness across diverse character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without requiring hand-tuning or specialized architectures for each.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of control, reinforcement learning, and neural network control. They acknowledge existing work leveraging deep learning for control (e.g., [3, 6, 14]) and trajectory optimization combined with learning ([9, 11]). The key contribution, as they highlight, is scaling this approach to significantly more complex 3D characters and demonstrating stable locomotion, something previous works struggled with. They also situate their work against traditional physics-based controllers that often rely on task-specific architectures ([21, 18, 13, 8, 7]), aiming for a more unified approach.  The reference to related work is generally comprehensive.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability to Complex Systems:** The most significant strength is the demonstrated ability to learn stable and realistic locomotion for diverse 3D characters. This represents a genuine advance over previous work focused on 2D scenarios.
* **Unified Control Framework:**  The method offers a compelling vision for a universal control framework, capable of handling different morphologies without extensive manual tuning. 
* **Effective Combination of Techniques:** The synergistic combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is well-motivated.  CIO provides high-quality training data, while the neural network enables real-time execution and interaction.
* **Robustness through Noise Injection:**  The principled noise injection strategy, leveraging optimal feedback gains, appears to be a key component contributing to stability and generalization.
* **Detailed Implementation Details:** The paper provides a good level of detail regarding the distributed training architecture and algorithmic constants, aiding reproducibility.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The presented results, along with the suggestion to view the supplementary video, demonstrate visually convincing locomotion across different character types.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the high computational cost of generating training data via trajectory optimization, and that this often outweighs the cost of learning. This is a significant practical limitation.  A more detailed breakdown of compute resources and training times for each character type would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the authors present ablation studies (Figure 2 and Table 1), a more thorough comparison with other control methods (e.g., Model Predictive Control, direct Reinforcement Learning) would strengthen the paper. The claim of "more realistic and purposeful behaviors" compared to direct methods (paragraph 3) feels somewhat anecdotal without more rigorous evaluation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The constant values used (listed at the end of Section 8) are many, and sensitivity to these is not explicitly discussed. It's not clear how robust the method is to changes in these parameters.
* **Lack of Generalization Analysis:** While the method demonstrates good performance across different characters *within* the training setup, the paper doesn’t discuss how well the learned policies generalize to new environments, tasks, or character variations *outside* the trained distribution.
* **Optimization Horizon:**  The paper mentions using a horizon of T=1 in the real-time execution phase. It would be beneficial to explore the impact of increasing the horizon, even at the cost of increased computational complexity.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging problem in control with a novel and effective approach.
* The demonstrated scalability to 3D characters and diverse morphologies is a significant contribution.
* The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is well-motivated and appears to offer a promising path forward.
* The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high computational cost of training data generation is a significant limitation.
* The lack of a more thorough comparative analysis weakens the claims of superiority over other methods.
*  Limited discussion of generalization performance raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in robotics and animation.  The authors present a compelling method for learning neural network control policies for complex characters, and demonstrate its effectiveness through impressive empirical results. While the computational cost and lack of comprehensive comparative analysis are weaknesses, the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10. I believe the paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, but further investigation into the computational cost and generalization capabilities would strengthen the work.
