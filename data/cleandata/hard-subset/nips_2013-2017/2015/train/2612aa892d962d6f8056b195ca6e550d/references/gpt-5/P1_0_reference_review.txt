Summary
The paper proposes a practical, scalable method to synthesize real-time neural network controllers for physically simulated characters by alternating between trajectory optimization and supervised learning. The approach uses Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate optimal trajectories for diverse tasks and morphologies (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion) and fits a neural policy via regression to map noisy sensory inputs to corresponding actions. A key idea is to inject Gaussian noise into sensory inputs during training and to adjust the regression targets using first-order optimal feedback gains computed around each optimized trajectory. The authors present a distributed, asynchronous training pipeline (multiple CPU nodes for trajectory optimization + a GPU for NN training) and a real-time execution scheme that solves a short-horizon (T=1) inverse-dynamics optimization at each step to enforce dynamics constraints while tracking the policy’s desired velocities, memory updates, and contact forces. They report stable, realistic behaviors across varied 3D morphologies, ablations (no joint optimization; no noise), and network-size comparisons, and a qualitative comparison to MPC.

Relation to prior work
The method is closely related to guided policy search/work that combines trajectory optimization with supervised policy fitting (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14), but extends to harder 3D character control settings and introduces a specific way to compute feedback gains directly from a direct trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation that reuses the Gauss–Newton inverse. The paper also connects to neural control for animation (Grzeszczuk et al.), RL in continuous control (TRPO), and contact-rich trajectory optimization (CIO). Compared to MPC, the learned policies regularize toward predictable cyclic behaviors.

Strengths
- Unified framework: One architecture and training pipeline yields interactive controllers for multiple, very different 3D characters without motion capture or hand-designed state machines.
- Technical contributions:
  - Noise-injection training that uses first-order corrections a + As ε with feedback gains computed efficiently from the trajectory optimizer (clever reuse of CXX−1 and Woodbury).
  - Asynchronous, proximal-regularized block-alternating optimization integrating trajectory optimization and NN regression; practical and scalable (2.5h on AWS).
  - Real-time execution via a short-horizon optimization that stabilizes integration and enforces (soft) dynamics/contacts.
- Empirical scope: Diverse 3D behaviors (swimming, flying, biped/quadruped gaits) are hard; achieving stability in closed loop is notable.
- Clarity of the high-level algorithm and system engineering decisions; parameters and implementation details are given.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation: Results are largely qualitative. The ablation shows a two-orders-of-magnitude improvement over “static data” training, but there are no standardized metrics (e.g., success rate, distance-to-goal over time, energy, falls, robustness to perturbations), no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., guided policy search variants, TRPO on the same tasks), and no statistics across seeds.
- Physical realism and applicability to hardware: Dynamics/contacts are enforced softly; “root forces” may appear at execution. Actions include desired contact forces, which are not directly actuated on most robots. Claims of robotics applicability are therefore speculative without hard-contact enforcement and torque-level control results.
- Theory and convergence: The alternating, asynchronous scheme with penalty α and proximal weight η lacks convergence analysis or sensitivity studies. The effect of λ in the feedback-gain computation on stability/conditioning is not explored.
- Reproducibility gaps: Key task costs and feature definitions φ are only sketched; exact formulations, state/action dimensionalities, and solver tolerances are not fully specified. Figures cited (e.g., performance plots, table of architectures) are not available in the text; the evaluation relies on video.
- Comparison to MPC: The claim that learned policies produce more regular cyclic motions than MPC is interesting but qualitative; no trade-off metrics (e.g., task completion time, energy, robustness) are provided.

Quality
Technically sound overall and carefully engineered. The feedback-gain derivation is plausible and leverages computations already done by CIO; however, more detail on the choice and effect of λ, conditioning of As, and numerical stability would strengthen the claims. The execution-time T=1 optimization is elegant but the reliance on soft constraints and possible root forces limits physical fidelity. Experimental support for robustness and generalization is insufficiently quantified.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The high-level pipeline and algorithm are clear. Some notation (e.g., φ, exact sensory features, state/action dimensions) could be made more explicit to aid reproducibility. The results section depends heavily on a supplementary video; adding quantitative plots and tables would help.

Originality
Moderate. The overall paradigm (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning) is known, but the paper contributes:
- A perturbation-based way to obtain feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- A specific noise-injection scheme using those gains as targets.
- A practical asynchronous, proximal-regularized training architecture demonstrated at scale on diverse 3D characters.
These are meaningful incremental contributions in a challenging domain.

Significance
High for animation/graphics and model-based control communities seeking interactive character control without motion capture. Moderate for the NeurIPS audience more broadly; significance would be higher with stronger quantitative comparisons to state-of-the-art RL/GPS baselines and robustness analyses. The approach could inspire hybrid systems that leverage trajectory optimization to supervise expressive policies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative metrics: success rates, distance/orientation error, energy/torque usage, foot-slip/contact violations, perturbation recovery, and runtime per control step.
- Compare against stronger baselines: guided policy search variants, TRPO (already cited), and an MPC baseline with comparable costs.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ, α, η, σε, and σγ; report conditioning of As and its effect on rollout stability.
- Clarify physical constraints at execution: eliminate or quantify root forces; discuss torque-level control; consider hard-contact constraints.
- Expand details on φ, costs, and dataset size/coverage; report generalization to unseen goals and initial states.

Pros
- Demonstrates stable 3D behaviors across diverse morphologies.
- Elegant integration of CIO with NN policy training via gain-informed noise targets.
- Practical, scalable system; clear engineering contribution.
- Potentially impactful for interactive control and animation.

Cons
- Largely qualitative evaluation; limited baselines.
- Soft-contact/root-force execution undermines physical realism and robotics claims.
- Missing sensitivity and convergence analyses.
- Reproducibility details are incomplete.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. Strong system-level contribution with a neat technical idea (gain-informed noise) and impressive qualitative results on hard 3D tasks. Acceptance would be strengthened by adding quantitative evaluations and stronger baselines; if those can be included in the final version, the paper would make a valuable contribution to NeurIPS.
