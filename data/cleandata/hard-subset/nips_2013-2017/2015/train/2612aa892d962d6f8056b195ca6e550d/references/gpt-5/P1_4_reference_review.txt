Summary
The paper proposes a framework for learning interactive, real-time control policies for complex, physically simulated characters (flying, swimming, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion) in 3D. The key idea is to combine direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural network policy learning in an alternating, distributed fashion. Each iteration: (i) re-optimizes a batch of trajectories with a penalty that encourages agreement with the current policy under injected sensory noise, and (ii) regresses a deep policy to noisy sensory–action pairs that include local linear feedback corrections, a + As ε, where As are “optimal feedback gains” computed efficiently as a byproduct of CIO via a soft-constraint perturbation method (reusing CXX−1 and Woodbury identity). The result is a policy that runs in real time; execution uses a 1-step dynamics-constrained optimization (implicit, T=1 MPC) to satisfy equations of motion. The training is distributed across many CPU nodes (CIO) with a single GPU (NN), and proximal regularization stabilizes asynchronous updates. Empirically, the approach yields stable 3D behaviors across morphologies; ablations show large degradation without joint alternation or noise.

Relation to prior work: This work is close in spirit to trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14), as well as model-based control/MPC in graphics and RL. Compared to those, the paper emphasizes (i) 3D character locomotion/flying/swimming, (ii) a CIO-compatible method to compute stabilizing feedback gains for policy targets without an extra LQG pass, (iii) distributed stochastic alternating optimization with proximal terms, and (iv) policy execution with a 1-step implicit dynamics solve.

Strengths
- Ambitious and practically relevant goal: learning interactive controllers for diverse 3D morphologies with a single architecture. The demonstrations (biped/quadruped, bird, swimmer) are impressive and of clear interest to ML/control/graphics communities.
- Solid integration of trajectory optimization and supervised learning. The alternating scheme with proximal regularization is simple, scalable, and empirically convergent in an asynchronous setting.
- Clever and potentially useful technical contribution: computing well-conditioned local feedback gains directly from the CIO solve via a soft-constraint perturbation, reusing CXX−1. This both avoids a separate LQG pass and provides smoother gains (tunable via λ).
- Robustness/regularization: principled sensor noise injection with corresponding linearized target actions (a + As ε), plus hidden-unit Gaussian noise, likely improves generalization and closed-loop stability.
- Engineering contribution: a practical distributed training pipeline that scales to many nodes; real-time execution via a T=1 implicit dynamics optimization.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative evaluation is sparse. The paper claims “two orders of magnitude” improvement in a biped task and better behavior than MPC, but lacks explicit metrics (e.g., task cost, distance-to-goal, fall rate, energy, success rate), statistics, and plots. Figures/tables referenced are not included here; even if present in the full paper, the text should report concrete numbers.
- Limited baselines. The most relevant prior methods (e.g., Guided Policy Search/ADMM variants; modern policy gradient methods like TRPO) are cited but not compared empirically. The claim of solving “substantially harder problems” than prior 2D work is plausible but not rigorously substantiated.
- Clarity issues:
  - The “recurrent memory” r is central but underspecified: its dimensionality, initialization, and role (learned latent state updated via ṙ) are not described in detail. The policy appears feedforward with an external memory state; more detail is needed to reproduce results.
  - The exact trajectory cost features and weights are only sketched; reproducibility would benefit from a full specification (even in an appendix).
  - The ablation study, network sweep (Table 1), and comparisons to MPC are mentioned but not quantified in text.
- Physical realism at execution: dynamics constraints are enforced as soft constraints, and the authors note possible non-zero “root forces.” This detracts from physical plausibility; an analysis of how often/where root forces are used and their impact would strengthen the claims.
- The linearization used for noisy targets assumes small ε; sensitivity to σε and λ is not explored. Similarly, stability/convergence of the asynchronous alternating procedure is only observed empirically.

Quality
- The algorithmic components are technically sound and thoughtfully combined. The As derivation via soft constraints and Woodbury reuse is a nice contribution. However, the experimental evaluation needs stronger quantitative evidence and broader baselines to fully support the claims.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well-organized, but important details are missing (r specification, cost features/weights, metrics). Some typos remain. Including full quantitative results and a reproducibility appendix would help.

Originality
- The overall scheme builds on known ideas (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning), but the feedback-gain computation within CIO and the distributed proximal alternating implementation are novel/useful. The breadth of 3D demonstrations is notable in this line of work.

Significance
- If substantiated with stronger quantitative evidence and comparisons, this could be an impactful approach for learning robust, interactive control in complex continuous domains, relevant to RL, control, and graphics. The As computation may be of independent interest.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Provide quantitative metrics across tasks: success rate, average task cost, perturbation robustness (e.g., push recovery), energy/torque usage, and failure modes. Report means/variances over seeds.
- Compare to at least one strong baseline: a GPS/ADMM variant, and a modern policy gradient (e.g., TRPO) on MuJoCo analogs, even if behaviors appear “chaotic.”
- Elaborate on r: dimensionality, update equations, and why this memory is needed versus a standard RNN. Include an ablation without r.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ (gain softness), σε (sensor noise), and σγ (hidden noise). Show how As conditioning varies with λ and how this affects policy performance.
- Clarify the degree of non-physical root forces at execution and, if possible, eliminate them (hard constraints or higher penalties). Compare T=1 execution to a short-horizon MPC.
- Include implementation details for reproducibility: cost feature definitions and weights, NN hyperparameters, learning rates, batch sizes, data sizes per character, wall-clock per component.
- Consider automatic differentiation for φX to reduce reliance on finite differences and improve scalability/precision.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method with compelling 3D demonstrations across diverse morphologies.
- Elegant technical contribution for extracting stabilizing gains from CIO.
- Clear engineering path to real-time interactive control.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and limited baselines.
- Some clarity gaps (memory state, costs), hindering reproducibility.
- Use of soft dynamics constraints at execution undermines physical realism.

Recommendation: Weak accept (conditional on strengthening quantitative evaluation and clarifications). Confidence: Medium.
