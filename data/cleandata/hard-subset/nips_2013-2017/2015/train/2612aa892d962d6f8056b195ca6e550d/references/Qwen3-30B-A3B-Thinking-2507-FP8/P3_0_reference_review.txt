

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of creating interactive, real-time controllers for diverse complex characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) in 3D environments. The authors propose a method combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with neural network training, using a distributed stochastic optimization approach that alternates between optimizing trajectories and training neural networks. The key innovation is a perturbation method for computing optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization, along with a principled noise injection strategy to create robust policies. The method is demonstrated on a variety of 3D characters with different morphologies, showing it can learn controllers that produce stable, realistic locomotion without task-specific hand-crafting.

This work extends previous trajectory optimization approaches [12, 21, 18, 13] and neural network policy learning methods [9, 11] by solving substantially harder 3D problems and providing a more robust training procedure. It builds on the authors' previous work on CIO [12] while addressing limitations of prior neural network control approaches that often produced "chaotic behaviors" [9, 11].

## Strengths

1. **Technical Innovation**: The paper's most significant contribution is the perturbation method for computing optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization (Section 3.2). This elegantly reuses computation from trajectory optimization to produce well-conditioned gains without modifying the cost function, addressing a key limitation in previous work [9, 11].

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide thorough ablation studies (Section 8.1) demonstrating the critical importance of their joint optimization framework and noise injection strategy. The results show their method outperforms naive approaches by two orders of magnitude, with no joint optimization or noise injection leading to rapid policy divergence.

3. **Broad Applicability**: The method successfully controls diverse 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) using a single network architecture without task-specific initialization. The demonstration of emergent cyclic gaits for bipeds and quadrupeds without explicit hand-crafting is particularly compelling.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The distributed training architecture (Section 6) is well-designed and shows good scaling properties. The authors provide sufficient detail to reproduce their results, including implementation specifics for the GPU training and trajectory optimization processes.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Reinforcement Learning Methods**: The paper acknowledges that they haven't systematically compared their method to more direct RL approaches (citing [3, 6, 14]), but this is a significant omission. The claim that "networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors" lacks empirical support. A proper comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Insufficient Failure Analysis**: While the paper shows successful examples, it doesn't discuss cases where the method fails (e.g., complex terrains, dynamic obstacles). Understanding the limitations would be valuable for practitioners.

3. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: The distributed stochastic optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) lacks theoretical convergence analysis. The paper notes convergence is observed but doesn't provide theoretical guarantees, which would be expected for a NIPS submission.

4. **Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper mentions training takes "hours" but doesn't compare computational requirements to alternative methods (e.g., MPC, standard RL). This is important for assessing practical applicability.

5. **"Interactive" Scope**: The interaction is limited to changing the goal position, without demonstrating more complex interactive scenarios (e.g., obstacle avoidance, dynamic environment interaction), which would better showcase the method's potential.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of character control and neural network policy learning. The technical innovation in feedback gain computation is particularly noteworthy, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over simpler approaches. While the weaknesses noted above are important, they don't undermine the core contribution. The paper addresses a challenging problem with a well-motivated, technically sound approach that produces impressive results.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a systematic comparison to standard RL methods (e.g., TRPO [14], PPO) on the same benchmark tasks to validate the claim about chaotic behaviors.

2. Include a subsection discussing failure cases and limitations of the current approach, particularly for more complex interactive scenarios.

3. Add a brief theoretical discussion of the convergence properties of the distributed optimization algorithm.

4. Provide a more detailed analysis of computational costs compared to alternative methods.

The paper's contribution is significant for both character animation and robotics communities, and it represents a clear advancement over previous work in this space. The method's ability to learn controllers for diverse morphologies without task-specific hand-crafting is particularly compelling. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.
