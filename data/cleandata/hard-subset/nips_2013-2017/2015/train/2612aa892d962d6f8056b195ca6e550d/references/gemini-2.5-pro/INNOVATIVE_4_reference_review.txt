### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper presents a method for creating interactive, real-time controllers for a diverse range of physically simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning of a neural network policy. Specifically, the authors use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate a dataset of optimal trajectories for various tasks (e.g., locomotion to a goal). A neural network is then trained to mimic the behavior of the optimizer.

The key contributions that distinguish this work from prior efforts (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) are its application to complex, 3D characters and a novel training scheme that significantly enhances policy robustness. This scheme involves jointly optimizing the trajectories and the network policy in an alternating fashion. Crucially, they introduce a principled method of noise injection: sensory inputs to the network are perturbed, and the target actions are adjusted using optimal feedback gains computed directly from the trajectory optimizer. This teaches the network not just to follow a trajectory, but how to correct for small deviations, leading to stable closed-loop control. The authors demonstrate the generality of their approach by applying the same algorithm and network architecture to generate walking, trotting, swimming, and flying behaviors for bipeds, quadrupeds, and other morphologies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Bold Vision and High Ambition:** The paper tackles a grand challenge in control and animation: creating a single, universal method for synthesizing controllers for arbitrary character morphologies and tasks. The success in controlling bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and fliers with the same underlying framework is a powerful demonstration of this vision and a significant step forward. This is precisely the kind of bold, unifying research that the community should encourage.

2.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The main technical innovation—training the policy on noisy sensory inputs paired with feedback-corrected optimal actions (`a + a_s ε`)—is both clever and highly effective. This goes far beyond simple data augmentation. It provides the network with local, first-order information about the optimal control landscape, directly teaching it how to create a stabilizing feedback policy. The ablation study (Figure 2) convincingly shows that this joint optimization with noise is critical for success, yielding orders of magnitude improvement over training on a static dataset.

3.  **Elegant Technical Contribution:** The method for deriving well-conditioned feedback gains directly from the direct trajectory optimization (CIO) framework is a valuable contribution in its own right. It avoids a costly and potentially unstable secondary LQG pass and is seamlessly integrated into the main optimization loop. This is a thoughtful piece of technical design that enables the core conceptual idea.

4.  **Impressive and General Results:** The empirical results are the strongest part of the paper. Demonstrating stable, natural-looking locomotion in 3D for such a wide variety of characters is a substantial achievement. Previous work in this area was largely confined to 2D. The emergence of gaits like walking and trotting without any explicit encoding is compelling evidence that the method discovers meaningful control strategies. The ability to control characters with fundamentally different dynamics (contact-based walking vs. fluid-dynamics-based swimming/flying) with a single approach is remarkable.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The paper dismisses direct reinforcement learning approaches with the brief qualitative statement that they produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors." While this may be true, the claim is unsupported. A more thorough discussion or even a simple quantitative comparison against a contemporary policy gradient method (e.g., TRPO) on one of the characters would have strengthened the paper's positioning within the broader control literature. Similarly, the comparison to MPC is brief and qualitative.

2.  **Methodological Complexity and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The full system involves a tight coupling of a sophisticated trajectory optimizer and a neural network trainer, with several key hyperparameters (α, λ, η, noise variances). While the authors provide the values used, the paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the method to these parameters. It is unclear how much tuning was required to achieve the presented results, which could be a barrier to reproducibility.

3.  **Clarity on Policy Execution:** The policy execution step (Section 7) involves solving a T=1 optimization problem at each timestep to evolve the state. This is an unusual choice and seems computationally intensive for real-time control. While the authors state it can be solved at real-time rates, it slightly muddles the claim of a pure feed-forward neural network controller. A more standard explicit integration scheme would be expected, and the paper could be clearer about why this "implicit integrator" approach was necessary and what its performance implications are.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a bold, novel, and highly effective approach to a very difficult problem.
*   The core idea of training with feedback-corrected targets is a significant conceptual contribution to learning control policies.
*   The empirical results are outstanding, demonstrating unprecedented generality across diverse 3D character morphologies.
*   The work opens up a promising new direction for combining the strengths of optimization and deep learning for complex control tasks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning is weak and anecdotal.
*   The system is complex, and its sensitivity to hyperparameters is not analyzed.
*   Some details, like the real-time execution model, could be clearer.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. While it has minor weaknesses in its comparative analysis and could benefit from more detailed ablation studies, these are forgivable in light of its primary contributions. The work introduces a significant new concept—using optimal feedback gains to structure the learning of a robust policy—and demonstrates its power on a set of impressively difficult and diverse 3D control problems. It moves the state-of-the-art in learning-based character control from 2D to 3D and provides a compelling example of a powerful synergy between trajectory optimization and neural networks. This is a foundational paper that other researchers are highly likely to build upon, making it an excellent fit for NIPS. I value its novelty and ambition far more than its methodological completeness.
