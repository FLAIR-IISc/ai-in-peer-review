Summary
The paper proposes a framework to learn real-time, interactive controllers for complex dynamical characters by coupling direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of neural network policies. The policy maps a sensory state (pose, previous velocity/forces, and a recurrent memory) to actions (desired pose velocity, contact forces, and memory update). The core idea is to alternate between (i) optimizing trajectories that satisfy physics and task costs, and (ii) training a neural network to imitate the optimized actions under injected sensory noise. A key technical component is computing local optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation method (reusing Gauss-Newton Hessians), allowing the training targets to reflect how optimal actions should change under small input perturbations. The algorithm runs in a distributed, asynchronous setting with proximal regularization to stabilize alternating updates, and uses GPU-accelerated SGD for network training. At runtime, the learned policy is queried and a one-step constrained optimization (T=1 CIO) updates the state, enabling stable execution at large timesteps. The method is demonstrated on diverse 3D characters (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds), achieving cyclic gaits and interactive goal tracking. Ablations suggest both joint alternation and noise are important for test-time robustness.

Relation to prior work
This is closely related to guided policy search-style approaches (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014), which also train policies from optimized trajectories with constraints tying policies and local controllers. The paper advances this line by (a) scaling to diverse 3D characters and contact-rich locomotion, (b) providing a practical method to extract feedback gains from a direct optimizer without a separate LQG pass, (c) injecting input and hidden noise during training to reduce overfitting and stabilize closed-loop rollouts, and (d) using an asynchronous, proximal alternating scheme suited to distributed training. Compared to MPC, the approach regularizes toward predictable, cyclic behaviors through the policy-matching terms.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization components (CIO with Gauss-Newton, proximal alternation, SGD) are standard and well-motivated. The derivation of feedback gains via soft constraints and Woodbury identity is plausible and reuses quantities already computed by CIO; while not fully derived in the paper, it is a reasonable and pragmatic contribution.
- Empirical support: The qualitative results are compelling for graphics/animation: multiple morphologies in 3D, cyclic gaits emerging without gait priors, and interactive goal tracking. Ablations (no joint alternation; no noise) substantiate the design choices. However, the evaluation lacks strong quantitative metrics (e.g., success rate, distance/orientation errors, energy/torque usage), robustness to perturbations, and timing at execution (latency, throughput). Comparisons to state-of-the-art policy learning baselines (e.g., TRPO or other model-free/model-based methods) are missing; the authors acknowledge this gap. The physical fidelity is limited by soft enforcement of dynamics and allowance of root forces, which constrains applicability to real robots.
- Completeness and honesty: The paper is candid about limitations (offline data generation, lack of comparison to direct RL) and provides many implementation details (network size, noise scales, proximal weights, cloud setup).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The high-level algorithm and its components are described with sufficient detail to reimplement by an expert familiar with CIO and MuJoCo. Some notational inconsistencies and missing specifics reduce clarity:
- The recurrent state r is under-specified (dimensionality, role, whether it improves performance).
- Action includes contact forces; this is nonstandard for deployment on real systems and warrants discussion.
- The execution step reuses a one-step CIO; a clearer description of constraints used in real time and measured runtimes would help.
- Some typos/duplications appear; figures/tables are referenced but not shown here.

Originality
Moderate to strong. While the overall paradigm (learning a policy from trajectory optimization with consistency constraints) is known, the paper contributes:
- A novel, efficient method to compute local feedback gains directly from a direct trajectory optimizer via a soft constraint formulation, avoiding a separate LQG pass and controlling gain conditioning.
- An asynchronous, proximal alternating training scheme adapted to large-scale distributed data generation.
- Demonstrated scaling to more challenging 3D, contact-rich tasks with a unified architecture without task-specific design.
Noise-injected inputs and hidden activations are known ideas, but applying them here with gain-augmented targets is a meaningful engineering advance.

Significance
For graphics, animation, and simulation-based control, the ability to train unified, real-time controllers for diverse 3D morphologies from a single pipeline is impactful and likely to be adopted. For ML/control, the work strengthens the case for combining trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning, particularly the pragmatic solution for feedback gain extraction. However, the lack of quantitative evaluation and comparisons to strong RL baselines limits conclusions about SOTA advancement in ML. The reliance on soft constraints and actioning contact forces reduces direct relevance to robotics, though the ideas could transfer.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated integration of CIO and neural policy learning with alternating optimization.
- Practical and novel feedback gain computation for direct trajectory optimization.
- Strong qualitative breadth: multiple 3D characters and emergent cyclic behaviors.
- Thoughtful noise injection and data generation via branching that improve robustness.
- Scalable distributed implementation; many reproducible implementation details.

Cons
- Limited quantitative evaluation; no standard metrics or robustness tests; no runtime numbers at execution.
- No comparisons to strong learning baselines (e.g., TRPO), making it hard to gauge ML significance.
- Physical realism compromised by soft constraints and root forces; action includes forces, limiting real-world deployment.
- Under-specified recurrent state and ablation of its contribution.
- No convergence analysis for the asynchronous proximal alternation (only empirical).

Suggestions for improvement
- Report quantitative metrics (goal error, success rate, torque/force costs, robustness to disturbances) and real-time latency.
- Compare against MPC and policy learning baselines on shared metrics.
- Clarify the role and benefits of the recurrent state; include ablations.
- Discuss implications of soft constraints/root forces, and feasibility for hardware.
- Provide convergence plots for the alternating scheme and sensitivity to proximal weight η and λ for gain softness.

Recommendation
Borderline accept for NeurIPS, leaning toward weak accept. The paper presents a solid, well-engineered contribution that advances policy learning from trajectory optimization to more complex 3D domains and adds a useful technique for extracting feedback gains. Strengthening the quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons would substantially increase its impact in the ML community. Confidence: medium.
