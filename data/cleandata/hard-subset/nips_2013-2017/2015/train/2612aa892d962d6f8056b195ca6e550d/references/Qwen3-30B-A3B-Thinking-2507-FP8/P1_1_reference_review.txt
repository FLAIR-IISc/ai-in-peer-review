

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary

This paper presents a method for generating neural network controllers that enable interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) through a combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training. The authors address a significant limitation in prior work, which was restricted to 2D environments, by extending the Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) approach to 3D. The core innovation lies in a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly optimizes trajectories and neural network parameters, with noise injection to improve robustness. This approach eliminates the need for task-specific controllers or motion capture datasets, enabling a single neural network architecture to control diverse character morphologies. The work builds on prior research in trajectory optimization (CIO [12]) and neural network control [9, 11], but significantly extends it to 3D environments with more complex dynamics.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a substantial technical contribution by extending trajectory optimization-based neural network controllers to 3D environments, where previous work was limited to 2D. This represents a meaningful advancement over prior methods [9, 11] that could not handle the complexity of 3D locomotion.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide thorough experimental validation across diverse character types (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flyer) and demonstrate the method's superiority over strong baselines (naive neural network training on static trajectories, no noise injection). The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of key components (noise injection, joint optimization).

3. **Practical Significance**: The work enables real-time, interactive control of complex characters without task-specific hand-crafted controllers, with potential applications in animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The demonstration of diverse behaviors (cyclical flapping, treading water, regular gaits) without explicit programming is compelling.

4. **Methodological Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the technical contributions, particularly the distributed stochastic optimization approach and the perturbation method for calculating optimal feedback gains. The implementation details (distributed training architecture, noise injection techniques) are sufficiently detailed for reproduction.

5. **Novelty**: While combining trajectory optimization with neural networks is not entirely new [9, 11], the extension to 3D, the distributed stochastic optimization framework, and the systematic analysis of noise injection provide a meaningful advancement that distinguishes this work from previous contributions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art RL**: The paper compares against naive neural network training and ablations of their own method, but lacks comparison with more recent reinforcement learning approaches for continuous control (e.g., [3, 6, 14]). This omission makes it difficult to assess how the method compares to the current state-of-the-art in neural network control.

2. **Computational Cost**: The training process requires significant computational resources (hours on a cluster with 30 optimization nodes per GPU), which might limit the practical applicability of the method. The paper doesn't discuss whether these costs can be reduced or if the method can be adapted for more resource-constrained settings.

3. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the method is well-motivated and validated empirically, the paper lacks deeper theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the distributed stochastic optimization algorithm or the generalization guarantees of the trained policies.

4. **Narrow Scope of Evaluation**: The experiments focus solely on locomotion tasks. It would strengthen the paper to include more challenging scenarios (e.g., dynamic obstacles, varying terrains, interactions with objects) to better demonstrate the robustness and versatility of the approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network-based control for complex character animation. The work successfully extends trajectory optimization-based neural network controllers to 3D environments, demonstrating the feasibility of a unified controller architecture for diverse character types. The methodological innovations (distributed stochastic optimization, noise injection, feedback gain calculation) are well-motivated and rigorously evaluated.

The paper addresses an important problem in character animation and robotics, and the results are convincing. The comparison to baselines shows clear improvements (two orders of magnitude better performance), and the ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of key components. The work is well-positioned to advance the state-of-the-art in interactive character control.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. The paper makes a significant technical contribution to the field with a well-executed approach that solves a challenging problem. The methodological innovations and comprehensive evaluation make it a valuable addition to the literature. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding comparison with recent RL methods and computational cost), these do not undermine the core contribution, which is substantial and clearly demonstrated.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), represents a meaningful extension of prior work (originality), and addresses an important problem with demonstrable impact (significance). The authors have appropriately acknowledged related work and clearly articulated how their approach differs from previous contributions.

The paper's focus on interactive control of diverse complex characters in 3D environments represents a meaningful advancement beyond previous work, making it highly relevant to NIPS. The work could inspire further research on neural network controllers for complex dynamical systems and has potential applications beyond character animation.
