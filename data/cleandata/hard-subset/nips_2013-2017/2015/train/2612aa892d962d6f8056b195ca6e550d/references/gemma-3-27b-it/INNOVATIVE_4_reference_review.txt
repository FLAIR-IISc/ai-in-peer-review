This paper tackles a remarkably ambitious problem: learning universal control policies for diverse, complex characters. The core idea – combining trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network learning to bridge the gap between offline planning and online control – is genuinely novel and represents a potentially significant step forward. The authors correctly identify a critical bottleneck in applying deep learning to control: the difficulty of obtaining sufficiently rich and stable training data. Leveraging trajectory optimization to *generate* this data is a clever and compelling solution. Furthermore, the successful application to 3D locomotion, where prior work has been largely confined to 2D, is a strong indicator of progress. The focus on injecting noise, informed by optimal feedback gains, is a particularly insightful addition, addressing the inherent instability issues in closed-loop learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of CIO and neural network learning for interactive control is a genuinely new approach.  It elegantly addresses the data scarcity problem in complex control tasks.
*   **Significance:**  A successful, generalizable approach to interactive character control has implications for robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The ability to control diverse morphologies with a single architecture is particularly compelling.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of both trajectory optimization and neural network training techniques. The incorporation of optimal feedback gains for noise injection is a strong technical contribution.
*   **Empirical Results:** The demonstration of stable 3D locomotion across a range of character types is impressive, particularly given the challenges inherent in this domain. The ablation studies (Fig 2) clearly demonstrate the importance of joint optimization and noise injection.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the ablation studies are valuable, a more thorough comparison with “direct” methods (e.g., those mentioned in the introduction - [3, 6, 14]) would strengthen the paper. The authors express an *impression* that those methods produce more chaotic behaviors, but a quantitative comparison is needed.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the distributed training architecture is a pragmatic solution, the description is somewhat high-level. More details on the computational cost and scaling behavior would be beneficial. While they mention hours of training, the resource demands could be prohibitive for researchers without access to cloud computing infrastructure.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The constants used in the algorithm (σε, σγ, α, λ, η) seem somewhat arbitrary.  A discussion of their sensitivity and justification for the chosen values would be helpful.
*   **Overfitting Mitigation:** The authors address overfitting with noise injection, but a more detailed discussion of generalization performance (e.g., transfer to slightly different environments or characters) would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially transformative approach to interactive character control.
*   The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating stable locomotion in 3D environments.
*   The authors address a significant challenge in applying deep learning to control – the data scarcity problem.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the use of optimal feedback gains for noise injection, are insightful and well-motivated.
*   The scale of the problem tackled is ambitious, pushing the boundaries of what’s currently possible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive comparative analysis with alternative control methods.
*   The high computational cost and potential scalability issues.
*   The limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and generalization performance. 



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a truly novel and significant contribution.  While the paper isn't *perfect* – a more rigorous comparison with existing methods and a more detailed discussion of scalability would be valuable – the core idea is compelling, and the results are impressive. I highly value the boldness of the approach and its potential to advance the field of interactive control. The minor flaws are outweighed by the originality and potential impact of the work. I recommend **acceptance**.  I encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above in a revised version, but even without those revisions, the paper remains a valuable contribution to NIPS.
