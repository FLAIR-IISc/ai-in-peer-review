This paper proposes a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization) with supervised learning. The core idea is to leverage trajectory optimization to generate training data, then use a neural network to learn a policy that mimics the optimized trajectories, enabling real-time interactive control. The authors address the challenges of applying deep learning to control problems – the need for precise real-valued outputs, closed-loop operation, and limited datasets – through noise injection and a distributed stochastic optimization scheme. The application focuses on locomotion across diverse character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal).

The primary strength of this work lies in its ambition and the demonstrated breadth of application. Successfully applying a unified control framework to such a diverse set of character types is a notable achievement. The authors correctly identify and address key challenges in learning controllers, particularly the data scarcity issue. The use of trajectory optimization to generate training data is a reasonable approach, and the inclusion of noise injection appears to improve robustness. The distributed training architecture is pragmatic given the computational demands.

However, several weaknesses raise significant concerns. First, the paper lacks a sufficiently rigorous comparison to established control methods. While the authors dismiss direct methods (e.g., reinforcement learning, supervised learning directly from demonstrations) with a claim that they yield “somewhat chaotic behaviors,” this claim is not substantiated with quantitative comparisons. Showing performance metrics (stability, tracking accuracy, efficiency) against baselines is crucial. Simply stating an impression is insufficient for a NIPS submission. The reference to prior work [9, 11] acknowledges related approaches, but the authors do not explain *why* their method significantly outperforms them beyond the application to 3D characters. A fair comparison on 2D tasks would be valuable to isolate the impact of dimensionality. 

Second, the methodological details, while present, are not always clear or fully justified. The choice of hyperparameters (noise levels, penalty weights, learning rates) appears somewhat arbitrary. A sensitivity analysis to demonstrate the robustness of the approach to these parameters is missing. Furthermore, the description of the trajectory optimization process relies heavily on referencing prior work [12] without providing enough detail to fully understand its implementation in the context of this paper. The perturbation method for calculating optimal feedback gains (section 3.2) seems reasonable in principle, but its practical impact and limitations aren’t adequately discussed.

Third, the results, while visually compelling (as evidenced by the suggestion to view the supplementary video), lack sufficient quantitative analysis. Performance is not quantified beyond vague statements about “successful” locomotion. Metrics like average speed, stability (e.g., number of falls), energy efficiency, or tracking error would provide a much stronger basis for evaluating the effectiveness of the approach.

Finally, while the authors acknowledge the computational cost, the resource requirements (hours of training, cloud computing) are substantial. This raises questions about the scalability and accessibility of the method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a unified control framework applicable to diverse character morphologies.
*   Addresses key challenges in learning controllers (data scarcity, closed-loop control).
*   Presents a pragmatic distributed training architecture.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lacks rigorous quantitative comparison to established control methods (RL, supervised learning).
*   Insufficient justification for methodological choices and hyperparameters.
*   Limited quantitative analysis of results, relying heavily on visual inspection.
*   High computational cost and scalability concerns.
*   Claims of superior performance lack strong supporting evidence.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting approach and demonstrates promising results, it falls short of the rigor and quantitative validation expected of a NIPS publication. The lack of comparison to baselines is a significant weakness, and the reliance on qualitative assessments is insufficient to justify the claims made. The computational cost is also a concern. I recommend the authors conduct a more thorough empirical evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art control methods, and provide a more detailed analysis of the results before resubmitting. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations and potential future directions of the work.
