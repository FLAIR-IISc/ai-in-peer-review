This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use CIO to generate training data and then train a neural network to mimic the behavior of the optimizer, enabling real-time control. The authors demonstrate this approach on several diverse character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipeds, quadrupeds), showcasing the versatility of their framework.

While the ambition and scope of this work are commendable, and the results demonstrate successful locomotion in 3D, I find a significant number of shortcomings that lead me to recommend rejection. The issues are not centered on a lack of novelty – the combination of techniques is reasonable – but rather on a lack of rigorous evaluation, insufficient detail in methodology, and persistent sloppiness in presentation that, cumulatively, undermine confidence in the claims made.

**Strengths:**

*   **Broad Applicability:** The demonstrated success across diverse character morphologies is a positive sign, suggesting the method isn't overly tailored to a specific problem.
*   **Real-Time Control:**  Achieving real-time control, particularly in 3D, is a non-trivial feat and a potential strength.
*   **Integration of Optimization & Learning:** The attempt to bridge the gap between offline trajectory optimization and online neural network control is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Quantitative Comparison:** The most glaring weakness is the near-complete absence of quantitative comparisons. While the authors state that their method yields "more realistic and purposeful behaviors" than direct methods (e.g., [3, 6, 14]), they provide *no* metrics to support this claim.  Simply asserting superiority is insufficient. A proper comparison, including metrics for stability, efficiency, tracking accuracy, or generalization ability is crucial. The ablation study in section 8.1 is minimal and focuses only on elements *within* their method, not against alternatives.
*   **Insufficient Methodological Detail:**  Several aspects of the methodology are glossed over. For example, the nature of the “task costs” used in trajectory optimization (section 3) is only described as “minimizing torque usage and contact force magnitudes.” This is insufficient; the exact cost function formulation is critical. Similarly, the details of the physics simulator (MuJoCo) configuration and parameters are missing. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility.
*   **Ill-Defined “Optimal Feedback Gains”:** The explanation of the “optimal feedback gains” (section 3.2) is convoluted and difficult to follow.  The motivation behind perturbing the state and the connection to LQG, while alluded to, isn't clearly explained. The claim that this method produces “softer” gains is unsubstantiated.  The mathematical derivation, while presented, feels unnecessarily complex and doesn’t offer significant insight.
*   **Vague Noise Injection:**  The justification for the specific noise levels (σ<sub>ε</sub> = 10<sup>-2</sup>, σ<sub>γ</sub> = 10<sup>-2</sup>) is entirely lacking. Why these values? What sensitivity analysis was performed? This critical hyperparameter is treated as arbitrary.
*   **Distributed Training Details:** The description of the distributed training architecture (section 6) is superficial. While mentioning the use of EC2 instances and NFS, it lacks details about the communication protocol, synchronization strategy, and the impact of asynchronous training on convergence.
*   **Presentation and Writing:** The writing is often imprecise and contains awkward phrasing. For example, “This is similar to denoising autoencoders [17] with one important difference” is followed by a subtle difference that is not particularly significant.  There are numerous instances of similar lack of precision throughout the paper.
*   **Algorithm 1's Notation**: In Algorithm 1, 'X̄i' is used both as an optimized trajectory and a variable being optimized, causing confusion.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The potential for creating general-purpose controllers for diverse characters is appealing.
*   Demonstrated success in 3D locomotion.

**Con:**

*   Lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation.
*   Insufficient methodological detail and reproducibility concerns.
*   Convoluted explanation of key components (optimal feedback gains).
*   Poor writing and imprecise language.
*   The ablation study is not comprehensive.




In conclusion, while the paper addresses an important problem and presents a potentially interesting approach, the numerous shortcomings in methodology, evaluation, and presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper needs substantial revisions, including a thorough quantitative evaluation, increased methodological detail, clearer explanations, and careful editing, to reach a standard suitable for NIPS.
