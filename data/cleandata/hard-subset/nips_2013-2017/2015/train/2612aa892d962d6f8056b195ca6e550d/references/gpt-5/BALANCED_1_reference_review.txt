Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a framework for learning real-time, interactive controllers for diverse 3D characters by combining trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised training of neural network policies. The policy maps a sensory state consisting of pose, recurrent state, and previous rates/forces to desired pose velocity, recurrent update, and contact forces. To make learned policies robust when rolled out, the authors inject Gaussian noise into the policy inputs and train on perturbed targets using local linear feedback gains a_s derived from the trajectory optimizer. They alternate between (i) optimizing multiple trajectories with a quadratic penalty that encourages agreement with the current policy (plus proximal regularization) and (ii) fitting a neural network to the noisy input–output pairs, in an asynchronous distributed manner. At execution, the learned policy outputs desired changes, and the next state is computed by solving a one-step constrained optimization (implicit/MPC-like update) using the same physics model.

The approach is closely related to guided policy search and trajectory-optimization-to-policy pipelines [Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014], but introduces: (1) a perturbation-based method to compute well-conditioned local feedback gains reusing Gauss–Newton Hessians from CIO; (2) stochastic policy inputs with targets adjusted by these gains; (3) a proximal, asynchronous alternating optimization; and (4) an execution scheme that solves a short-horizon (T=1) inverse-dynamics-like update in real time. The authors report qualitative results on 3D swimmers, fliers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, with emergent cyclic gaits and interactive goal changes.

Strengths
- Addresses a challenging setting (real-time control of diverse 3D characters) and demonstrates broad applicability across morphologies with a unified architecture.
- Technically sound use of trajectory optimization to generate high-quality supervision, combined with robustness-enhancing noise injection and local feedback targets.
- Novel, practical derivation of feedback gains directly from a direct trajectory optimizer by reusing CXX via Woodbury identity; likely of independent interest.
- Sensible alternating optimization with proximal regularization that supports asynchronous distributed training; scalability demonstrated.
- Practical execution strategy that avoids storing trajectories and leverages implicit dynamics-consistent updates.
- Ablations indicate the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection (as reported), and network depth/width study shows reasonable capacity choices.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is largely qualitative; there are no clear quantitative metrics (task success rates, deviation from targets, robustness to perturbations, energy/torque, constraint violations) across characters. Claims of “two orders of magnitude better” lack detailed data in the text.
- Limited baselines: comparisons to model-free methods (e.g., TRPO) and model-based MPC/iLQR with comparable costs are only briefly discussed and qualitative. No head-to-head on performance/robustness/compute.
- Physical realism is compromised at execution by soft enforcement of dynamics and possible “root forces.” It is unclear how large these violations are and whether learned policies rely on them.
- The action includes contact forces as commanded outputs, which is atypical for real systems and may limit transferability; clarity on how these map to feasible torques is needed.
- Some notational/definitional ambiguities (e.g., q̇ defined as finite differences but referred to as velocity; sensory vector composition; distinction between “desired” versus actual variables; whether the network truly learns a dynamics model given the physics-based execution).
- Convergence guarantees are not discussed; empirical convergence is claimed, but stability/sensitivity to α, λ, η, noise scales, and number of alternations is not characterized.
- Significant compute requirements (tens of EC2 nodes for hours) with no discussion of cost–benefit or reproducibility details (code, seeds, exact hyperparameters).

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The algorithmic components are generally sound, and the feedback-gain derivation is a nice contribution. However, the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and limited baselines weaken the empirical support. The reliance on soft constraints/root forces at execution raises questions about physical fidelity.
- Clarity: The paper is readable, but several definitions and modeling choices need clearer exposition (state/action definitions, execution dynamics, the role and magnitude of soft constraints). Figures and tables (referenced) would help, but more in-text detail is needed.
- Originality: The overall paradigm builds on prior guided policy search and CIO-based control; the key original elements are the feedback-gain computation, the specific stochastic alternating optimization with proximal terms, and its application to diverse 3D characters. The contribution is incremental but nontrivial.
- Significance: If robust and physically consistent, the approach could impact character animation and possibly robotics. Current evaluation is promising but not yet convincing enough to claim state-of-the-art advances; stronger empirical evidence would increase significance.

Questions for the authors
- How large are the dynamics/constraint violations and “root forces” during execution? Please report distributions and their effect on behavior quality.
- Why include contact forces in the action? Can the method work with torque-only policies and enforce complementarity as hard constraints at execution?
- Sensitivity to λ (feedback softness) and α, η, σε, σγ: can you provide ablative plots and convergence behavior?
- Quantitative metrics across tasks (success rate, distance/orientation error, energy/torque, fall rate) and robustness to disturbances?
- Comparison to MPC/iLQR with the same cost and a short horizon: real-time performance, stability, and behavior predictability?
- Does the policy generalize to novel goals/initializations and modest model errors? Any evidence on rough terrain or external pushes?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comprehensive quantitative evaluations and success metrics across all characters; include robustness tests and constraint violation stats.
- Provide stronger baselines (TRPO or other model-free continuous control, iLQR/MPC) with matched costs and report compute/time.
- Clarify state/action definitions and execution scheme; explicitly separate “desired” from realized quantities and detail how constraints are enforced.
- Report sensitivity to key hyperparameters and the impact of the proximal term on asynchronous stability.
- If possible, reduce or eliminate root forces at execution (harder constraints or penalty schedules) and quantify the effect.
- Discuss reproducibility (implementation details, code/data availability).

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, unified approach achieving interactive control for diverse 3D characters.
- Clever and useful derivation of feedback gains for direct trajectory optimization.
- Demonstrated scalable training and real-time execution.
- Empirically plausible behaviors without task-specific heuristics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative validation and limited baselines; hard to assess true performance/significance.
- Potential reliance on soft dynamics violations compromises physical plausibility.
- Incremental relative to guided policy search; novelty mainly in technical refinements rather than core paradigm shift.
- Reproducibility and sensitivity details are lacking.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The approach is promising and technically interesting, especially the feedback-gain computation and the practical system. However, the paper needs stronger quantitative evaluation, clearer comparisons to strong baselines, and a more rigorous treatment of physical realism at execution to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage resubmission with expanded experiments and clarified methodology. Confidence: medium.
