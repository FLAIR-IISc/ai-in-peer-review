### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a method for automatically synthesizing neural network controllers for the interactive, real-time control of physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with the real-time performance of neural network policies. The authors propose a framework that jointly optimizes a collection of optimal trajectories and the parameters of a neural network policy that can reproduce them.

The method iterates between two main steps in an alternating optimization scheme:
1.  **Trajectory Optimization**: For a set of tasks (e.g., locomotion to different goals), optimal trajectories are generated using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), a powerful direct trajectory optimization method. This optimization step not only minimizes a task and physics-based cost but also includes a term that encourages the trajectories to be reproducible by the current neural network policy.
2.  **Neural Network Regression**: The neural network parameters are updated via supervised learning to map sensory states to actions, using the data generated by the trajectory optimizer.

A key contribution is the method for generating robust policies that are stable in closed-loop execution. Instead of training on simple state-action pairs, the authors inject Gaussian noise into the sensory inputs. The corresponding target action is computed by adding a first-order correction based on optimal feedback gains, which are efficiently calculated as a byproduct of the direct trajectory optimization process. This trains the network to actively correct for small perturbations. The entire training process is implemented in a distributed, asynchronous architecture to manage the computational cost. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach by applying the same algorithm and network architecture to generate stable and plausible locomotion for a diverse set of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer.

This work builds upon prior research that learns neural network policies from trajectory optimization [9, 11]. Its primary novelty lies in scaling these ideas to complex 3D characters, which is enabled by several technical contributions, most notably the principled injection of noise and the use of optimal feedback gains to create a robust controller.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: (Is the paper technically sound? Are claims well-supported?)**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed joint optimization framework is well-formulated and provides a principled way to bridge offline optimization and online policy execution. The derivation of the optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a clever and valuable contribution that addresses a key challenge in making policies learned from optimizers robust.

The claims are well-supported by experimental results. The ablation study in Section 8.1 provides compelling evidence for the importance of the two core components of the method: joint optimization (vs. static training) and noise injection. The results show that omitting either component leads to a dramatic drop in performance and unstable behavior, validating the authors' design choices. The qualitative results, as shown in the supplementary video, are impressive and demonstrate that the method successfully generates stable and plausible gaits for a wide variety of character morphologies without task-specific engineering.

**Clarity: (Is the paper clearly written and well-organized?)**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The overall structure is logical, starting with a high-level overview and progressively detailing the components of the method. Algorithm 1 provides a clear summary of the iterative optimization procedure. The motivation for key design choices, such as the use of feedback gains to handle noisy inputs, is well-explained.

However, certain aspects could be clarified to improve reproducibility.
*   The exact formulation of the cost functions `C(X)` used in the trajectory optimization is not detailed. While the paper mentions minimizing torque and contact forces, the specific terms and their relative weights are omitted. This information is crucial for reproducing the results.
*   The policy execution method described in Section 7 is somewhat unorthodox. Rather than directly using the network's output velocities and forces to advance the simulation, the authors solve a single-step optimization problem at each timestep to match the network's target. This re-introduces an optimizer at runtime. The motivation for this choice over a more direct application of the policy output is not fully explained. It is unclear if this is essential for stability or simply an implementation choice. More justification and analysis of its computational cost would be beneficial.

**Originality: (Are the problems or approaches new? Is related work adequately referenced?)**

The high-level concept of using trajectory optimization to generate data for training a policy is not entirely new [9, 11]. The paper correctly positions itself relative to this prior work, claiming its contribution is a set of technical improvements that enable the solution of substantially harder 3D locomotion problems.

The originality lies in the specific formulation and key technical details that make this scaling possible:
1.  The joint, stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1) that iteratively refines both the training trajectories and the policy.
2.  The principled use of noise injection during training, where target actions for perturbed states are generated using optimal feedback gains.
3.  The novel and efficient method for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (CIO), which is a significant contribution on its own.

The combination of these elements into a unified and effective system is a novel and substantial contribution. Related work is adequately cited and discussed.

**Significance: (Are the results important? Are other researchers likely to use these ideas?)**

The significance of this work is high. It addresses the long-standing and difficult problem of creating general-purpose, interactive controllers for complex physical characters. The ability to generate robust locomotion for a diverse set of morphologies from a high-level task description, using a single unified algorithm, represents a major advance for character animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning.

The demonstrated results are state-of-the-art for the time and are likely to influence future work in data-driven and learning-based control. The techniques presented, particularly the method for robustifying policies learned from optimizers, are general and could be adopted by other researchers working on similar problems. The qualitative comparison to MPC, which suggests the joint optimization regularizes the optimizer towards more natural, cyclic behaviors, is an interesting and important finding.

A minor weakness in the evaluation is the limited quantitative comparison to other state-of-the-art methods, such as direct deep reinforcement learning approaches [14]. While the authors provide a qualitative argument, a direct quantitative comparison on at least one character would have further solidified the paper's claims about the benefits of its approach.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Strong Results**: The method is shown to work on a diverse and challenging set of 3D characters, a significant step beyond prior work in 2D.
*   **Technical Novelty**: The paper introduces a novel and effective method for robustifying learned policies by using optimal feedback gains derived from a direct trajectory optimizer.
*   **High Significance**: The work represents a substantial advance in automated controller synthesis with clear applications in graphics and robotics, and is likely to be highly influential.
*   **Sound Methodology**: The framework is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by a convincing ablation study.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Reproducibility Concerns**: Lack of detail on cost function formulation and ambiguity in the real-time execution step may hinder reproducibility.
*   **Limited Quantitative Comparison**: The experimental evaluation lacks a direct quantitative comparison against alternative state-of-the-art methods like deep RL or MPC.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of learning-based motor control. The technical approach is sound, the novel components are well-motivated and impactful, and the results are impressive. While there are minor weaknesses regarding the clarity of some implementation details and the scope of the experimental comparison, these do not detract from the core value of the work. The strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses.
