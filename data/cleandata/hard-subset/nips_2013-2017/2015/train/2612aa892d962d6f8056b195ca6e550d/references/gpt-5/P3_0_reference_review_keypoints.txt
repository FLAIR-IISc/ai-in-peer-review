- Proposes a hybrid learning-and-optimization pipeline: use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate trajectories, train a neural policy to imitate them, and alternate re-optimization with policy updates to improve robustness.
- At training time they add sensory noise, apply locally optimal feedback gains to correct targets, and use a proximal quadratic penalty to keep trajectories consistent with the current policy.
- Introduces a perturbation-based method to compute feedback gains directly from CIO by reusing Gauss-Newton Hessians and Jacobians, avoiding a separate LQG/iLQR pass.
- Runtime controller: policy outputs desired state velocities and contact forces, followed by a one-step constrained optimization (T=1) enforcing dynamics and contacts.
- Strong engineering contributions: distributed asynchronous training, practical training times, and emergent cyclic gaits across diverse 3D morphologies without hand-designed gaits.
- Methodologically coherent combination of alternating optimization, noise-injected supervised learning, and reuse of trajectory-optimizer derivatives.
- Novel and useful derivation of feedback gains via a soft constraint and Woodbury identity for direct trajectory optimization methods.
- Limited quantitative evaluation: mostly qualitative results and no systematic comparisons to strong baselines (GPS/iLQG, TRPO, MPC) on standardized metrics.
- Unclear separation of learned behavior vs. runtime optimizer: reliance on T=1 optimization raises questions about how much stability is due to the policy alone.
- Runtime latency and compute costs for the per-frame T=1 solve are not reported, making real-time feasibility unclear.
- Physical fidelity concerns: soft dynamics/contact enforcement and allowable root forces may produce unphysical motions and limit robot-transferability.
- Recurrent state r is under-specified (dimensionality, initialization, role) and its benefits are not ablated or clearly explained.
- Ablation studies are underspecified (metrics, plots, statistical significance); bold claims (e.g., “two orders of magnitude”) lack concrete numbers.
- Related work coverage is incomplete: missing detailed discussion of DAGGER, iLQG/iLQR, PILCO, and more recent policy-gradient/GPS variants.
- Clarity issues: terse derivations, missing implementation details (normalization, runtime costs, horizon/cost terms), and incomplete bibliographic references.
- Originality is moderate: overall template is known, but the feedback-gain computation within CIO is a novel technical contribution.
- Significance is promising for character animation engineering but limited for robotics/control without stronger quantitative comparisons and physical realism evidence.
- Recommendation: weak reject — promising and well-engineered but needs stronger empirical validation, clearer comparisons to baselines, and clarification of key implementation choices.
