Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that couples direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised training of a neural network policy for real-time interactive control of complex, physically simulated characters. The policy maps a “sensory” state s = [q, r, q̇t−1, ft−1] to an action a = [q̇t, ṙt, ft], i.e., desired velocities, contact forces, and a recurrent memory update. To improve robustness, the authors inject Gaussian noise into sensory inputs and hidden activations, and, crucially, they shape the regression targets using locally optimal feedback gains as computed from a perturbation analysis that reuses the Gauss–Newton Hessian from CIO. The overall objective relaxes a hard consistency constraint between the trajectories and the policy with a quadratic penalty and proximal regularization, solved in a distributed, asynchronous manner. At execution, a T=1 optimization step enforces dynamics while matching the policy’s desired quantities, enabling interactive control at 50 ms step times.

This line of work is closely related to trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (e.g., Guided Policy Search, GPS: Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), model-predictive control (MPC), and physics-based character control in graphics. Relative to prior 2D demonstrations, the paper emphasizes robust 3D behaviors (walking bipeds/quadrupeds, flying, swimming) and contributes a practical method to extract stabilizing feedback gains from a direct (non-LQG) optimizer to shape noisy imitation targets. The use of asynchronous alternating optimization with proximal terms is also a notable engineering contribution.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of policy search as an alternating trajectory optimization and supervised regression problem with a principled penalty tying them together.
- Novel and practical derivation of local feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method that reuses CXX−1 (Gauss–Newton), avoiding a separate LQG pass and enabling tunable “soft” gains via λ.
- Thoughtful robustness mechanisms: input noise with gain-shaped targets, hidden-unit noise, proximal regularization, and branched segment data generation to reflect interactive goal changes.
- Demonstrated diversity across challenging 3D morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, flyer) with a single training recipe, yielding visually plausible cyclic gaits without hand-crafted state machines.
- Scalable distributed training and practical real-time execution using a T=1 inverse-dynamics-like step.

Weaknesses and questions
- Quantitative evaluation is thin. Beyond an ablation (no joint training, no noise) and a single architecture sweep, there are no task-level metrics (success rate, distance-to-goal, energy/torque costs, robustness to perturbations), nor comparisons to strong baselines such as GPS/ADMM-based methods, TRPO, or modern actor-critic methods. The MPC comparison is anecdotal and limited to a single task.
- The policy outputs contact forces and desired velocities; execution relies on a soft-constrained T=1 optimization that can admit nonphysical root forces. This undermines physical realism and raises concerns for transfer to real robots (where one cannot directly command contact forces). More discussion and experiments with stricter dynamics enforcement or torque-only actuation would strengthen claims.
- The “recurrent memory” r is underspecified. Although the policy outputs ṙ, the network appears feedforward with r included in the input/output. How is r initialized/used, and does it materially aid performance? Results disentangling the effect of r would help.
- Stability and conditioning: while the gain derivation is elegant, there is no analysis of stability of the closed-loop policy or conditioning of the learned gains beyond qualitative statements. Reporting gain statistics and the effect of λ would bolster confidence.
- Asynchronous convergence is only empirically asserted. While a formal proof may be out of scope, clearer diagnostics (objective traces, constraint residuals, policy-trajectory consistency over iterations) would be useful.
- Data/compute requirements are significant (30 EC2 nodes + GPU). It is unclear how performance scales with fewer trajectories or nodes; sample-efficiency comparisons are absent.
- Some clarity issues: the exact cost terms/weights in CIO (beyond broad categories) are not provided; execution-time computational cost/frame rates are not reported; feature extraction φ and state/action Jacobian computation details are sparse.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with a well-motivated objective and a careful engineering design. However, empirical validation is primarily qualitative and ablation-limited; strong baselines and quantitative metrics are missing. The feedback-gain derivation is a useful contribution but lacks deeper analysis.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The algorithm is described clearly, and the derivation of gains is understandable. Areas needing more detail: exact CIO costs/weights, the role/initialization of r, execution-time performance numbers, and stricter description of the distributed optimization protocol.

Originality
Moderate to high. The overall paradigm (trajectory optimization guiding NN policy learning) is known (GPS), but the specific integration with CIO in 3D, the perturbation-based gain extraction reusing direct-optimization Hessians, the noisy target shaping a + asε, and the asynchronous proximal training are noteworthy. The paper distinguishes itself by the breadth of demonstrated morphologies and an engineering path to stable behaviors.

Significance
Promising for interactive character control and potentially for robotics (with caveats about actuation/contact). If better quantified and compared to contemporary baselines, the approach could influence how TO and NN policies are combined for complex, contact-rich tasks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative evaluations across tasks: goal tracking error, success rates, energy/torque use, robustness to external pushes and model/actuation noise; report real-time control FPS.
- Compare against GPS (ADMM/dual variants), TRPO (and/or a recent actor-critic), and an MPC baseline across at least one 3D task with matched costs.
- Analyze the effect of λ on gain magnitudes, conditioning, and policy performance; include statistics of as.
- Clarify r’s role with ablations (no r, different memory sizes).
- Report stricter dynamics enforcement experiments (harder constraints, torque-only actuation) and discuss implications for sim-to-real.
- Provide more details on CIO costs/weights and release code or supplementary implementation details to aid reproducibility.

Pros
- Elegant coupling of CIO with NN via noisy, gain-shaped supervision.
- Practical method to obtain stabilizing gains from a direct optimizer.
- Robustness techniques that demonstrably help generalization.
- Diverse, visually convincing 3D results with a unified recipe.

Cons
- Limited quantitative evaluation; weak baseline comparisons.
- Reliance on soft dynamics constraints and contact-force commands.
- High compute burden; unclear sample efficiency.
- Some missing clarity (role of recurrence, exact costs, runtime performance).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a practical and conceptually interesting advance in combining direct trajectory optimization with neural policies, with a useful feedback-gain contribution and impressive qualitative results across diverse 3D characters. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying limitations would make it a clear accept. My confidence is medium-high.
