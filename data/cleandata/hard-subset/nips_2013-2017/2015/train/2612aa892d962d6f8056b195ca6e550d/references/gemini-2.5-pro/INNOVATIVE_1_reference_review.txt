**Summary**

This paper presents a method for creating interactive, real-time controllers for a diverse set of complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning. Specifically, the authors use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate a dataset of physically realistic and task-oriented motion trajectories. A neural network policy is then trained to mimic the behavior of the optimizer. The key contribution lies in the joint, alternating optimization framework where the trajectories and the network policy are optimized together. This co-adaptation is stabilized by a principled approach to noise injection: noise is added to the network's sensory inputs, and the target output is adjusted using optimal feedback gains derived from the trajectory optimizer. This teaches the network not just to imitate a fixed trajectory, but to learn a locally robust policy. The authors demonstrate the power and generality of their approach by successfully training controllers for 3D bipedal and quadrupedal walking, swimming, and flying, all using the same underlying algorithm and network architecture. The resulting behaviors, such as natural walking gaits and flapping motions, emerge automatically from the optimization process.

**Strengths**

1.  **Bold Vision and High Significance:** The paper tackles a grand challenge in control and animation: the creation of a universal method for synthesizing controllers for arbitrary morphologies and tasks. The departure from character-specific, hand-engineered controllers (state machines, simplified models, etc.) towards a unified, data-driven approach is a significant and valuable direction for the field. The success across such a diverse set of 3D characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, flyer) provides compelling evidence that this approach is a major step towards that goal. The potential impact on gaming, animation, and robotics is substantial.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Technical Contributions:** While the paper combines several known components (trajectory optimization, neural networks), the synthesis is novel and the specific technical details are clever.
    *   The joint optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is the central idea. By having the trajectories and the policy co-evolve, the method avoids the classic pitfalls of behavioral cloning on a static dataset, where distribution mismatch can lead to catastrophic failures. The ablation study (Figure 2, "no joint" case) clearly demonstrates the critical importance of this joint approach.
    *   The method for handling noise injection is particularly insightful. Instead of just using noise as a regularizer, the authors calculate the optimal action for the perturbed state `s + ε` using locally linear feedback gains (`a + asε`). This provides the network with rich, corrective training signals, explicitly teaching it how to stabilize itself around the reference trajectories. This is a far more principled approach than simple data augmentation.
    *   The technique for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains directly from the direct trajectory optimizer is a useful contribution in its own right, applicable beyond the immediate scope of this paper.

3.  **Impressive and Convincing Results:** The ability to generate stable, cyclic, and visually plausible gaits for such varied characters without any explicit gait-specific priors is the paper's strongest selling point. The emergence of walking, trotting, and flapping from general task and physics costs is a powerful demonstration of the method's capabilities. The ablation studies effectively isolate and justify the key components of the proposed algorithm. The qualitative comparison with MPC, suggesting the neural network acts as a regularizer to find more structured and less chaotic solutions, is an interesting insight.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Quantitative Comparison to Modern RL:** The paper's primary comparison is to ablations of its own method. While it mentions direct RL methods (e.g., policy gradient methods like TRPO, which was concurrent work), the comparison is limited to a brief, qualitative impression that such methods produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors." A quantitative comparison on at least one of the characters against a state-of-the-art continuous control RL algorithm would have significantly strengthened the paper's claims about producing more "realistic and purposeful" behaviors.

2.  **Heuristic Optimization Scheme:** The block-alternating optimization scheme, while effective in practice, lacks formal convergence guarantees. The authors acknowledge that methods like ADMM might be more principled but are harder to adapt to their stochastic and asynchronous setting. Given the complexity of the problem, this is an understandable engineering trade-off, but it remains a theoretical weakness. The performance is demonstrated empirically, which is sufficient for a paper of this nature, but the lack of theoretical grounding for the overall optimization loop is a minor flaw.

3.  **Reproducibility Challenges:** The system described is highly complex, relying on a specific physics engine (MuJoCo), a sophisticated trajectory optimizer (CIO), and a custom distributed cloud computing architecture. While the paper outlines the components well, reproducing these results would be a formidable undertaking for an outside researcher. The cost functions are also described at a high level, and the specific tuning of their weights is likely critical to the final performance.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper presents a bold, ambitious, and largely successful attempt at a unified framework for controlling complex 3D characters. The core idea of jointly optimizing trajectories and a neural network policy, enhanced by a principled approach to noise injection via optimal feedback gains, is a significant conceptual contribution. The empirical results are outstanding and demonstrate a level of generality and quality that advances the state of the art in character animation and learning-based control.

While the paper could be improved with more rigorous comparisons to alternative methods (particularly deep RL) and a more formal analysis of its optimization scheme, these weaknesses are secondary to the novelty and significance of the core contribution. This work prioritizes a powerful new direction over perfecting an existing one, and the impressive results across a wide range of difficult control problems validate this approach. It is exactly the kind of innovative and forward-looking research that NIPS should champion. I strongly recommend acceptance.
