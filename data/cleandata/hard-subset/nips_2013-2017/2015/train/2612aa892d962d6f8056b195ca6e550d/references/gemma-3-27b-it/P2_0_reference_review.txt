## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex, physically simulated characters, enabling interactive real-time control. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with supervised learning. CIO is used to generate a dataset of optimal trajectories, and a neural network is then trained to mimic the control policy learned by the optimizer. Key contributions include addressing the challenges of scaling trajectory optimization-based learning to 3D characters and incorporating noise injection and distributed stochastic optimization to improve generalization and stability. The authors demonstrate successful control of diverse characters – swimmers, fliers, bipeds and quadrupeds – with a single network architecture, highlighting the versatility of their approach.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within a body of work aiming to automate control synthesis, contrasting with traditional manual methods, reinforcement learning, and stochastic optimal control. It acknowledges related work using neural networks for control (e.g., [9, 11]) but claims to solve harder problems (3D locomotion) and details technical improvements enabling this. The discussion of MPC as an alternative is also valuable. The connection to prior work on contact-invariant optimization [12] is well-established and forms the foundation of the data generation process.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Challenging Problem:** Learning controllers for complex, high-dimensional systems with realistic dynamics is a significant challenge. This work makes a clear contribution toward automating this process.
* **Scalability to 3D:** Successfully applying this approach to 3D locomotion is a notable achievement, as the authors highlight.  Prior work often focused on 2D scenarios.
* **Versatility:** Demonstrating the method’s effectiveness across a diverse range of character morphologies with a single architecture is a strong point.  This points to the generalizability of the approach.
* **Technical Contributions:** The combination of trajectory optimization, noise injection, distributed stochastic optimization, and the perturbation method for calculating feedback gains represent a well-engineered and thoughtful solution. The feedback gain calculation is particularly clever and efficient.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is helpful.
* **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies (section 8.1) provide valuable insights into the contribution of key components like joint optimization and noise injection.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** While the ablation studies are helpful, the comparison to "direct methods" (mentioned in the introduction) is only impressionistic. A more systematic quantitative comparison against alternatives like DDPG or TRPO would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the constants are listed, there's limited discussion about the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. A brief analysis of parameter tuning would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:**  The reliance on significant cloud computing resources is mentioned, but a more detailed breakdown of the computational costs (e.g., time per trajectory optimization, total training time) would be valuable for reproducibility.
* **Task Specificity:** While versatile across *character types*, the task itself is relatively simple (locomotion to a goal). Exploring more complex interactive tasks would demonstrate broader applicability.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successes. Discussing scenarios where the approach fails, or struggles, and potential reasons would enhance honesty and provide valuable direction for future work.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The combination of established techniques (CIO, neural networks) is novel in its application and the technical improvements (noise injection, feedback gain calculation) are well-justified. The experimental results support the claims.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, although the notation can be dense in places. The algorithm description helps, but some sections (e.g., the mathematical derivation of the feedback gains) could benefit from further simplification.
* **Originality:** The approach is a novel combination of existing techniques, addressing a challenging problem in a new way. The specific implementation details and the demonstrated success in 3D locomotion represent a significant contribution.
* **Significance:** The work is potentially significant. Automating the creation of complex controllers has broad implications for robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The ability to control diverse characters with a single network is a promising step toward more general-purpose control systems.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Solves a challenging problem with a well-engineered and effective solution.
* Demonstrates impressive results in 3D locomotion with diverse characters.
* Presents novel technical contributions (noise injection, efficient feedback gain calculation).
* Clearly written and well-motivated.

**Against:**

* Limited comparative evaluation against alternative control methods.
* Lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.
* Focus on a relatively simple task.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field and is **worthy of acceptance** to NIPS. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work addresses a significant challenge with a novel and effective solution. Addressing the weaknesses (particularly a more thorough comparative evaluation) could further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
