## NIPS Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and technically sound approach to learning interactive controllers for complex, 3D characters. It successfully bridges the gap between offline trajectory optimization and online, real-time control using a cleverly designed neural network architecture and training procedure. The results are impressive, demonstrating stable locomotion and diverse behaviors across a range of character morphologies – a significant step beyond prior work largely confined to 2D environments. While the presentation could be slightly more polished, the technical depth and the practical impact of the work strongly recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors tackle the challenging problem of creating universal controllers for complex characters, moving beyond the limitations of hand-crafted controllers and the difficulties of applying Reinforcement Learning in high-dimensional continuous spaces. Their key idea is to leverage the power of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) for generating high-quality trajectories, then train a neural network to mimic the behavior of the optimizer, enabling real-time interactive control. This approach smartly combines the strengths of both methods: CIO provides the expertise, while the neural network facilitates generalization and online adaptability. 

The paper clearly positions itself within the broader context of control, referencing historical neural network approaches, RL, and traditional biomechanics-inspired control strategies (state machines, simplified models). It accurately identifies recent related work [9, 11] and convincingly argues that their method surpasses these in terms of complexity and dimensionality (successful 3D locomotion). The emphasis on the computational cost of data generation versus learning is a crucial and often overlooked aspect of this kind of hybrid approach.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Technical Innovation:** The combination of CIO and neural network regression is a significant contribution. The introduction of noise injection, guided by optimal feedback gains, is particularly insightful.  This addresses the critical issue of stability and generalization in closed-loop control systems.
* **Scalability and Generalization:** Successfully applying this method to diverse 3D character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) is a major achievement. This demonstrates the generalizability of the approach and its potential for wide applicability.
* **Empirical Results:** The results are impressive, and the supplementary video is crucial for appreciating the quality and realism of the generated motions. The ablation studies (Figure 2) convincingly demonstrate the importance of both the joint optimization and noise injection techniques.
* **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide details regarding the distributed training architecture and computational costs, demonstrating that this method is feasible with current resources. 
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of interactive control in high-dimensional spaces and provides a compelling motivation for their approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Presentation:** While technically sound, some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations. For instance, the discussion of optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) is somewhat dense and could be made more accessible with illustrative examples.  The connection between the perturbation method and LQG could also be elaborated upon.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lists the values of algorithmic constants used, but doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the method to these parameters. A brief discussion on how parameter choices affect performance would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** While the authors briefly mention and dismiss direct methods (e.g., Reinforcement Learning) as producing “chaotic” behaviors, a more systematic comparison with other contemporary approaches would strengthen the paper. Quantifying the difference in performance (stability, realism, learning speed) would be highly informative.
* **Model Specificity:** The reliance on MuJoCo, while practical, introduces a dependency on a specific physics engine. While the core algorithm should be transferable, acknowledging this limitation is important.



**4. Originality:**

The combination of CIO with neural network learning, particularly the novel noise injection strategy guided by optimal feedback gains, is a genuinely original contribution. While the individual components (CIO, neural networks, noise injection) are not new, their synergistic application to this problem and the resulting performance are highly innovative.

**5. Significance:**

This work has the potential to significantly impact several fields:

* **Character Animation:** Enables the creation of realistic and interactive characters with minimal manual effort.
* **Robotics:** Provides a promising approach to learning robust and adaptable controllers for complex robots.
* **Biomechanics & Neuroscience:** Offers a computational framework for modeling and understanding biological motor control. 

The ability to generate diverse behaviors across different morphologies with a single, unified approach is particularly significant. This represents a substantial step towards building truly general-purpose control systems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging and important problem in control.
* Presents a technically sound and innovative solution.
* Demonstrates impressive empirical results on diverse 3D characters.
* Clear potential for impact across multiple fields.
* Well-motivated and clearly positioned within related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Presentation could be improved for clarity.
* Limited systematic comparison to alternative methods.
* Sensitivity to hyperparameters remains unexplored.



**Overall Recommendation:** **Strong Accept.**

This paper is a significant contribution to the field of interactive control. The technical innovation, compelling results, and potential for impact outweigh the minor weaknesses in presentation and comparison.  I highly recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS.  I suggest the authors address the clarity issues and consider adding a more comprehensive comparison to other relevant methods in a final revision.
