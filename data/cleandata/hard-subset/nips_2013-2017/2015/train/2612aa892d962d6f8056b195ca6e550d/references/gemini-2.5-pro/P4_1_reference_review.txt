**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a method for automatically generating neural network control policies for a diverse range of physically simulated 3D characters. The goal is to create a single, universal algorithm that can produce robust, interactive, and realistic locomotion for various morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) without task-specific engineering. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning. The authors formulate a joint optimization problem to find both a set of optimal trajectories and a neural network policy that can reproduce those trajectories.

This problem is solved using a distributed, block-alternating optimization scheme. In one step, multiple trajectory optimization processes (running in parallel) refine motion trajectories to satisfy task goals and physical constraints, while also trying to match the output of the current neural network policy. In the other step, the neural network is trained via regression to imitate the actions from these newly optimized trajectories.

Critically, the method incorporates stochasticity to improve robustness and generalization. Gaussian noise is added to the sensory inputs during training. The corresponding target actions are adjusted using optimal feedback gains, which account for the input perturbation. The paper introduces a novel and efficient method for calculating these feedback gains as a byproduct of the direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, or CIO), avoiding a separate, potentially unstable computation. This work builds upon prior research that combines trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but extends it to significantly more complex 3D characters and behaviors, enabled by the new feedback gain computation and a scalable distributed training architecture.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   (Strength) The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of the joint optimization problem and the alternating optimization scheme is a principled approach to a very challenging problem. The ablation study in Figure 2 provides strong empirical evidence that the key components—joint optimization and noise injection—are essential for achieving good performance.
*   (Strength) The method for computing optimal feedback gains from the Gauss-Newton Hessian of the direct trajectory optimizer is a significant technical contribution. It is well-motivated by the need to provide accurate regression targets for noisy inputs and appears more efficient and stable than alternatives like a separate LQR pass.
*   (Strength) The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a high degree of generality. The ability of a single algorithm, with a fixed network architecture and hyperparameters, to produce stable and plausible gaits for such a wide variety of 3D characters is a notable achievement.
*   (Weakness) The convergence of the overall distributed stochastic optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not theoretically analyzed. The authors state that they "still observe convergence," but a discussion of the conditions under which the alternating scheme is stable would strengthen the paper.
*   (Weakness) The method for executing the policy at runtime involves solving a single-timestep optimization problem. While this is claimed to be real-time, no performance metrics (e.g., frames per second, computation time per step) are provided. This step also introduces a potential mismatch between the training process and deployment.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

*   (Strength) The structure is logical, starting with a high-level overview before delving into the specifics of trajectory optimization and neural network regression. The motivation for each component of the method is clearly explained.
*   (Strength) The mathematical formulation is precise and the notation is consistent. The core optimization problem (Eq. 1) and the subsequent algorithm are presented clearly.
*   (Weakness) Section 8.1 refers to Figure 2 and Table 1, but these figures and tables are not included in the provided text, which makes it impossible to fully evaluate the comparative analysis. A caption for Figure 2, in particular, would be essential.
*   (Weakness) The description of the task cost functions is somewhat general ("minimizing torque usage and contact force magnitudes"). Providing a more detailed mathematical form of the cost function would enhance the reproducibility of the work.

**Originality:** The work contains significant novel contributions while building on a well-established line of research.

*   (Strength) While the high-level idea of distilling trajectory optimization into a policy network has been explored before, this paper makes it work for a much harder and more general class of problems (interactive 3D locomotion). This scaling-up is a contribution in itself.
*   (Strength) The primary novel element is the technique for extracting linear feedback gains directly from the CIO solver's internal computations (Section 3.2). This is an elegant and practical solution to a key challenge in training robust policies from offline optimizers.
*   (Strength) The design and implementation of the distributed, asynchronous optimization framework is a novel systems contribution that makes training on such large-scale problems feasible.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   (Strength) This work represents a major step towards the goal of automated controller synthesis for complex animated characters and robots. The demonstrated generality and robustness could significantly reduce the manual effort currently required in fields like robotics and game development.
*   (Strength) The ideas presented, particularly the feedback gain computation and the joint optimization framework, are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in control, robotics, and machine learning.
*   (Strength) The qualitative comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC) offers an interesting insight: the policy network appears to act as a strong regularizer that guides the optimization towards natural, cyclic solutions, which are often preferred over the more "chaotic" but equally effective solutions that a pure optimizer might find.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper successfully demonstrates a single, general algorithm for learning interactive controllers for a diverse set of complex 3D characters, a significant advance over prior work limited to 2D.
2.  It introduces a novel and technically sound method for computing optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer, which is a key enabler for the method's success and is of independent interest.
3.  The experimental results are impressive, well-supported by ablation studies, and clearly showcase the power and robustness of the proposed framework.
4.  The paper is very clearly written, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The convergence of the proposed optimization algorithm is demonstrated empirically but lacks theoretical guarantees.
2.  Key details regarding real-time performance and the precise formulation of cost functions are omitted, which slightly hinders a full assessment of practicality and reproducibility.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of machine learning for control.
