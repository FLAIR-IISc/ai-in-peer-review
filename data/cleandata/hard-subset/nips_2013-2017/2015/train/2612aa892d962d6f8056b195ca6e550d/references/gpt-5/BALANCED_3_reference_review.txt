Summary and relation to prior work
The paper aims to learn interactive, real-time controllers that generate complex, stable, and realistic motions for diverse 3D characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, bird) without hand-designed architectures or motion capture. The core idea is to couple a powerful offline trajectory optimizer—Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO)—with supervised learning of a neural policy, alternating between (i) optimizing many task-conditioned trajectories and (ii) fitting a neural network to imitate the (possibly perturbed) optimal actions along those trajectories. To improve robustness, the authors inject Gaussian noise into sensory inputs and adjust the training targets using locally optimal feedback gains computed from a novel perturbation-based method that reuses the Gauss-Newton Hessian already computed by CIO. Training is distributed across many cloud instances; policy execution runs in real time by querying the network for desired velocities, recurrent state update, and contact forces, followed by a T=1 constrained optimization step enforcing dynamics (softly).

The work is closely related to “learning from optimal control” and guided policy search (GPS) approaches that alternate between trajectory optimization and supervised policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014), as well as to classic LQG and MPC. Relative to prior GPS results, the paper emphasizes (i) 3D locomotion for multiple morphologies, (ii) a practical way to obtain stabilizing gains from a direct trajectory optimizer, and (iii) an asynchronous, distributed training loop. It contrasts with model-free RL (e.g., TRPO, deep Q-learning) and hand-designed locomotion controllers (e.g., ZMP/capture-point, CPGs), seeking a more unified pipeline across morphologies.

Strengths
- Ambitious and relevant goal: unified, interactive controllers for diverse complex characters in 3D.
- Principled integration of trajectory optimization with neural policy learning; the alternating scheme is practical and reuses existing tools.
- Noise-injection strategy coupled with locally optimal feedback targets is thoughtful and likely important for closed-loop robustness with limited data; the perturbation-based gain computation reusing Gauss-Newton structure is an elegant contribution.
- Asynchronous distributed implementation and GPU training details are useful for scaling.
- Empirical coverage across multiple morphologies is compelling for the animation/control community; cyclic gaits emerge without hand-engineered schedules.
- Ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) support the design choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation. Beyond an error curve for one biped task, there are few metrics (e.g., success rates, fall rates, energy/effort, tracking and disturbance-rejection performance, generalization to unseen goals, latency/runtime).
- Baseline comparisons are thin. Comparisons to GPS variants (which the work is closest to), MPC (beyond a qualitative claim about cyclicity), and model-free RL (e.g., TRPO) are not provided quantitatively.
- Execution relies on solving a T=1 optimization with soft dynamics constraints (including possible root forces). This weakens claims of physical realism and robotics applicability; it also blurs whether the system is a pure policy or policy+online optimizer. Real-time performance and the fraction of timesteps with significant residual/root forces are not reported.
- The “recurrent memory state r” is central but underspecified: dimensionality, initialization, and how it interacts with a nominally feedforward network are not clearly described.
- The convergence and stability of the alternating optimization (with asynchrony and proximal terms) are only observed empirically; theoretical guarantees are absent.
- The feedback-gain derivation is interesting but terse; sensitivity to λ and conditioning, and comparisons to an LQG pass are only briefly discussed.
- Some clarity issues: minor typos, incomplete reference formatting, and occasional ambiguity in notation (e.g., the role of aX, sX, subsets of φX).

Quality
Technically sound overall with reasonable design choices and a novel gain computation; however, key claims (robustness, physical realism, scalability, generalization) would benefit from stronger, more systematic experiments. The use of soft dynamics (allowing root forces) at execution time raises concerns about fidelity.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-structured, with a helpful overview and algorithm sketch. Important details are missing or underexplained (r state, runtime, constraint violations). Tightening notation and adding implementation specifics would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The overall paradigm follows GPS-style “optimize trajectories + supervised learning,” but the paper contributes: (i) an extension to diverse 3D locomotion, (ii) a practical, Hessian-reusing method for local feedback gains within a direct trajectory optimization framework, and (iii) an asynchronous distributed training recipe. The combination is a useful engineering advance, though conceptually close to prior GPS.

Significance
For animation/character control, the reported breadth across morphologies and interactive control is valuable. For the NeurIPS audience, significance hinges on rigorous demonstrations and comparisons; the current empirical evidence falls short of a strong claim of state-of-the-art generality or robustness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and challenging problem; breadth of demonstrated behaviors is appealing.
- Practical and principled integration of optimization and learning; elegant gain computation.
- Ablations indicate that the joint optimization and noise injection matter in practice.
- Scalable training architecture; actionable insights for practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons, especially versus GPS, MPC, and modern RL.
- Reliance on soft dynamics and T=1 optimization at test time undermines claims of physical realism and “pure policy” control.
- Key methodological details (recurrent state, convergence behavior, runtime) are lacking.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative metrics: success/fall rates, average return/energy, sensitivity to perturbations, generalization to out-of-distribution goals, runtime per control step, fraction and magnitude of dynamics constraint violations/root forces.
- Compare against GPS variants, MPC (with same cost), and at least one model-free RL method on identical tasks.
- Clarify and evaluate the recurrent state r (dimension, ablations with/without r).
- Detail the effect of λ, σε, σγ on stability and performance; include conditioning of feedback gains and a comparison to an LQG-based gain computation.
- Report scalability with DOF and task complexity, and discuss what is needed to deploy on hardware (hard contacts, torque outputs).
- Cite and discuss related dataset aggregation/imitation learning work (e.g., DAgger) and more recent GPS methods.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach with some neat technical elements and appealing demonstrations, but the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and strong baselines, plus reliance on soft dynamics at execution time, make it fall short of the NeurIPS bar. Strengthening the experiments and clarifying key details could change this assessment.
