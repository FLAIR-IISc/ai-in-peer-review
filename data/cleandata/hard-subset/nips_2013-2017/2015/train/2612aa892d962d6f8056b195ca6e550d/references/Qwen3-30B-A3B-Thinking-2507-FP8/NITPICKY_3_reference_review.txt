

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for training neural network controllers for interactive character control using a combination of trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization) and neural network regression. The key idea is to use trajectory optimization to generate training data and a neural network to learn from this data, enabling real-time interactive control. The method is applied to a variety of 3D characters (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) and is claimed to work in 3D where previous work was limited to 2D. The paper makes several technical contributions including a method for computing optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization, a stochastic training procedure with noise injection, and a distributed training architecture.

## Strengths
The paper presents a compelling approach that combines trajectory optimization with neural network learning to achieve interactive control for diverse character types. The method's ability to generalize across different character morphologies without specialized initialization is impressive. The technical details about the feedback gain computation and noise injection are novel and well-motivated. The distributed training architecture is practical and scalable, and the results on diverse 3D characters demonstrate significant capability.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies
1. **Notation Errors**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, in equation (2), "a(s + ε) = a + asε" uses "as" to denote a matrix, but this is not properly defined or formatted as a matrix (it should be a Jacobian matrix). The paper later refers to "as (alternatively dads)" which is confusing and non-standard notation.

2. **Mathematical Errors**: In section 3.2, the derivation of X̃s̄ contains inconsistent notation with the use of ">" for transpose, which is not standard in mathematical writing. The expression "s > X" should be "s^T X" or "s^T X" to denote matrix transpose.

3. **Technical Omissions**: The paper states "We choose cost functions for which cφ and cφφ can be calculated analytically" but never specifies what these cost functions are. This omission makes it difficult to assess the technical soundness of the approach.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. **Reference Formatting**: Several references have inconsistent formatting. For example, "Hessian matrix vs. gauss-newton hessian matrix, P. Chen, SIAM J. Numerical Analysis,, , ," contains extra commas and inconsistent capitalization. The reference to Wager et al. [19] is misattributed; the paper cites it for noise injection but the reference is about dropout training.

2. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper alternates between "three-dimensional" and "3D" without consistency. The action is defined as "at = [q̇t ṙt f t]" but later referred to as "[q̇des ṙdes f des]" without explaining the "des" suffix.

3. **Algorithm Notation**: In Algorithm 1, the notation "X̄i = argminX Ci(X) + ..." uses X̄i on both sides of the equation, which is confusing. It should be "X_i = argmin_X Ci(X) + ...".

### Methodological Concerns
1. **Lack of Quantitative Comparison**: The paper claims "our full method performs two orders of magnitude better than static training" but provides no actual numerical values to support this claim. Without concrete metrics, this comparison is unconvincing.

2. **Missing Implementation Details**: The paper states "we use 3 hidden layer neural networks with 250 hidden units in each layer" but doesn't specify activation functions beyond "tanh" (which is mentioned in section 4 but not in the main description). The exact network architecture details are missing.

3. **Citation Error**: The paper cites Wager et al. [19] as saying "this noise model makes hidden units tend toward saturated regions" but [19] is about dropout training, not noise injection. This is a significant citation error that undermines the paper's credibility.

### Minor but Notable Issues
1. **Inconsistent Noise Notation**: The paper uses "ε" for sensory noise and "γ" for hidden unit noise, but the notation for γ is inconsistent with the standard practice of using ε for noise.

2. **Informal Hardware Descriptions**: The paper refers to "Amazon Web Service's EC2 3.8xlarge instances" and "g2.2xlarge instance" - these should be "Amazon EC2 c3.8xlarge instances" and "g2.2xlarge instances" (without the "instance" part).

3. **Missing Details on Noise Injection**: The paper claims that "we inject different forms of noise during training" but doesn't specify what these different forms are, making it difficult to assess the contribution of this technique.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting approach with potential for significant impact in character animation and robotics, it suffers from multiple technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and methodological omissions that undermine its credibility as a scientific contribution. The paper makes strong claims about performance improvements (e.g., "two orders of magnitude better") without providing the necessary quantitative details to support these claims. The citation error regarding Wager et al. is particularly concerning as it suggests a lack of careful reading of the related work.

The paper would benefit from a complete revision to address these issues, including:
1. Correcting all mathematical notations and derivations
2. Providing specific details about cost functions, network architecture, and quantitative performance metrics
3. Fixing citation errors and reference formatting
4. Using consistent terminology and notation throughout

As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these minor but pervasive issues to be significant enough to warrant rejection. The paper's technical contributions are overshadowed by these fundamental presentation flaws, and the lack of sufficient detail makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and impact of the work.

## Pro/Con Acceptance
**Pro:**
- The approach combines trajectory optimization with neural networks in a novel way
- Demonstrates successful application to diverse 3D character types
- Provides a distributed training architecture that scales well

**Con:**
- Multiple technical notational inconsistencies
- Lack of quantitative performance metrics to support claims
- Citation error regarding noise injection technique
- Inconsistent terminology and formatting throughout
- Missing key implementation details

Given the paper's current state, I recommend rejection due to these significant presentation and technical issues that prevent the reader from properly evaluating the work.
