- Proposes combining contact-invariant trajectory optimization with neural network training to produce interactive, real-time controllers for diverse 3D characters.  
- Uses trajectory optimization to generate training data and injects noise during training to improve controller robustness.  
- Demonstrates a single neural network architecture controlling multiple morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) and various locomotion styles.  
- Extends prior trajectory-optimization-based NN control work from 2D to complex 3D characters.  

- Significant technical contribution: scales trajectory-optimization-based control to complex 3D character locomotion.  
- Methodological innovation: a distributed stochastic optimization algorithm, noise injection strategy, and perturbation-based feedback gain estimation.  
- Strong empirical results: diverse successful behaviors and convincing ablation studies showing importance of joint optimization and noise injection.  
- Practical impact: enables interactive control without hand-crafted controllers, relevant to animation, robotics, and biomechanics.  

- Lacks theoretical analysis or formal convergence guarantees for the distributed optimization approach.  
- Insufficient comparison to modern deep RL baselines (e.g., PPO, SAC) that are relevant alternatives.  
- Training requires substantial computational resources and the paper omits detailed discussion of inference cost.  
- Relies mainly on qualitative results (videos) and lacks quantitative performance metrics (energy efficiency, stability, human baseline comparisons).  

- Quality: technically sound with clear experiments, but weakened by missing theoretical analysis and quantitative metrics.  
- Clarity: well-organized and detailed enough for expert reproduction, though some parts (e.g., feedback-gain perturbation) could use more intuition.  
- Originality: novel combination of trajectory optimization and NN training for unified 3D control across morphologies.  
- Significance: meaningful advance toward unified control architectures, though impact is limited by lack of RL comparisons.  

- Recommendation: accept — novel, well-executed, and practically impactful despite addressable weaknesses.  

- Pro-accept: shows unified control of diverse 3D characters with one architecture, thorough ablations, and clear reproducibility.  
- Con-accept: missing theoretical guarantees, limited RL comparisons, no quantitative metrics, and nontrivial training costs.
