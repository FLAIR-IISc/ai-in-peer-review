This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use CIO to generate training data—diverse trajectories—and then train a neural network to mimic the optimal control policy derived from the optimizer. This approach aims to overcome the difficulties of directly training neural network controllers in high-dimensional, continuous control spaces. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a variety of characters, including swimming, flying, and legged robots, showing stable and realistic locomotion.

The paper builds upon previous work in both reinforcement learning and trajectory optimization, but distinguishes itself by successfully applying this combined approach to complex 3D characters, a feat not achieved in prior work like [9, 11] which were limited to 2D scenarios.  The emphasis on generating high-quality training data via trajectory optimization is a key contribution, contrasting with methods that rely on direct reinforcement learning or limited datasets [3, 6, 10, 14]. The injection of noise during training, along with the efficient computation of optimal feedback gains, are additional novel technical contributions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The method appears technically sound, drawing on established techniques (CIO, neural networks) and combining them in a logical and well-motivated manner. The mathematical formulation of the optimization problem is clear and the algorithmic steps are well-defined. The discussion of Hessian approximations and the perturbation method for computing feedback gains demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying concepts.
*   **Novelty:** The application of this combined CIO and neural network approach to high-dimensional, 3D character control is novel. The ability to achieve stable and realistic locomotion across diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture is a significant achievement. The noise injection strategy and efficient feedback gain computation are also novel contributions.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant. Achieving interactive, real-time control of complex characters is a challenging problem with applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.  Demonstrating this capability across various character types suggests a broad applicability of the proposed method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and the proposed solution. The algorithmic description is straightforward, and the presentation of results is comprehensive, with references to a supplementary video for qualitative evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the computational cost, the fact that “more computing resources are invested in generating the data than in learning from it” raises concerns about scalability. A more detailed analysis of the computational requirements and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions specific values for algorithmic constants (e.g., σe, σγ, α, λ, η) but provides limited insight into how these values were chosen and their impact on performance. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper.
*   **Comparison to Direct Methods:**  The authors state an impression that networks trained with direct methods yield “somewhat chaotic behaviors.” A more systematic and quantitative comparison to these methods would provide stronger evidence to support this claim. While they mention a plan for future work in this direction, the current paper lacks such a comparison.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While an ablation study is presented (Section 8.1), it focuses on the impact of joint optimization and noise injection. Exploring other aspects, such as network architecture variations beyond the ones tested, could provide further insights.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in control and animation.
*   The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on complex character control.
*   The technical contributions (CIO + NN, noise injection, efficient feedback gains) are novel and significant.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The high computational cost may limit the scalability of the method.
*   The lack of a comprehensive comparison to direct reinforcement learning methods weakens the claims of superiority.
*   The limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity hinders reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of character control. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a promising approach, and the authors have demonstrated its effectiveness on a variety of challenging tasks. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the computational cost, providing a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, and including a more systematic comparison to direct reinforcement learning methods.
