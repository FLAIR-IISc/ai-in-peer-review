**Summary**

This paper presents a method for training a neural network policy to control complex, physically-simulated 3D characters for interactive tasks like locomotion. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with the real-time performance of a neural network. Instead of using traditional reinforcement learning, the authors formulate the problem as a joint optimization over a set of "expert" trajectories and the parameters of the policy. An offline planner, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), generates physically realistic, goal-directed behaviors. The neural network then learns to imitate these behaviors. The key contribution is an alternating optimization scheme where the trajectories are refined to be more predictable for the network, and the network is trained to reproduce the (refined) trajectories. Crucially, the authors introduce a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, where the target actions are adjusted using optimal feedback gains derived from the trajectory optimizer. This framework is shown to successfully generate controllers for a diverse set of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers, using a single unified approach.

This work builds directly upon prior research that combines trajectory optimization and function approximation, most notably Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch et al. (2014). The primary novelty here is the successful extension of these ideas from 2D to significantly more complex and unstable 3D domains, enabled by several technical improvements, particularly the method for extracting and utilizing feedback gains to handle noisy inputs during training.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive and Diverse Results:** The most significant strength of this paper is the empirical demonstration of a single, unified algorithm producing high-quality, stable, and visually realistic locomotion for a wide range of 3D character morphologies (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flyer). This is a substantial step forward in generality for physics-based animation and control, moving beyond hand-crafted, morphology-specific controllers. The resulting behaviors appear purposeful and natural, avoiding the chaotic or jittery motions sometimes seen with direct reinforcement learning methods.

2.  **Novel and Principled Training Methodology:** The joint optimization framework is elegant. The key insight that the trajectory dataset and the policy should be optimized together, rather than in a simple two-stage (generate then learn) process, is powerful. The method for injecting noise (Section 2.1) is particularly clever. Instead of just training a denoising autoencoder on `(s+ε, a)`, the authors use optimal feedback gains `as` from the optimizer to compute a locally optimal target action `a + asε`. This provides the network with direct information on how to correct for perturbations, which is likely a major reason for the method's robustness and success in unstable 3D environments.

3.  **Useful Technical Contribution:** The method for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method like CIO (Section 3.2) is a valuable contribution in its own right. Indirect methods like LQR naturally produce such gains, but doing so for direct methods is non-trivial. The proposed perturbation approach, which re-uses the computed Hessian, is efficient and provides a tunable "softness" parameter `λ`, which appears to be important for generating usable gains. This technique could be of interest to the broader optimal control community.

4.  **Strong Ablation Study:** The comparative evaluation in Section 8.1 is convincing. It clearly demonstrates the critical importance of both the joint optimization ("no joint" case) and the stochastic training with noise injection ("no noise" case). The two-orders-of-magnitude performance drop when these components are removed provides strong evidence for their necessity and effectiveness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Reinforcement Learning:** The paper dismisses direct RL methods with the qualitative statement that they "give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors." While this may have been a fair characterization at the time, the paper would be much stronger with a direct quantitative comparison against a contemporary model-free RL algorithm (e.g., TRPO, which is cited). Applying an algorithm like TRPO to the same bipedal task would provide a crucial baseline and help contextualize the trade-offs between this "optimizer-distillation" approach and direct policy search. Without this, the claims of producing "more realistic and purposeful behaviors" are not fully substantiated.

2.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The method relies on repeatedly running a heavy-duty trajectory optimizer (CIO) across a distributed cluster of machines for several hours. This represents a significant computational barrier. While the results are excellent, the practicality of this approach is limited by its resource intensiveness. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the training time scales with character complexity, trajectory length, or the size of the dataset.

3.  **Ambiguity in Real-time Execution:** The policy execution step (Section 7) is not a simple forward pass of the neural network. It involves solving a small optimization problem at each timestep to find the next state that best matches the network's desired velocities while satisfying physics constraints. The authors state this can be solved at "real-time rates," but this adds complexity and computational overhead compared to a direct policy output. It would be valuable to clarify the computational cost of this per-frame optimization and to know if it's strictly necessary. For instance, what is the performance if the network's output is used to directly command PD targets in the simulator?

**Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, and the claims are supported by strong experimental results and a solid ablation study. The work is complete and presents a fully-realized system.
*   **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-written and organized. The core concepts of joint optimization and stochastic training with feedback gains are explained clearly. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the overall procedure.
*   **Originality:** High. While it builds on a known paradigm (learning from an optimizer), it makes several novel contributions. The extension to complex 3D characters is non-trivial, and the specific method for deriving and using feedback gains for robust training is a key innovation that pushes the state of the art.
*   **Significance:** Very High. This paper demonstrates a powerful and general method for creating high-quality interactive controllers. The results were state-of-the-art and have influenced subsequent work in character animation, robotics, and RL (e.g., in an offline RL or expert-guided context). The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by many researchers.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance in automated character control. The impressive results across a diverse set of complex 3D characters, supported by a novel and well-justified methodology, make a compelling case for its contribution. While the lack of a direct comparison to contemporary RL methods is a weakness, the quality, originality, and significance of the work are undeniable. This paper is a clear and valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
