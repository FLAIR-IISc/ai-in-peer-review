Summary
The paper proposes a framework for learning interactive, real-time controllers for complex physically simulated characters by alternating between trajectory optimization and neural network policy regression. The key ingredients are: (i) Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate optimal trajectories for multiple characters/tasks; (ii) a neural policy trained to match the optimizer’s actions from noisy sensory inputs, using first-order action perturbations a + As ε where As are local feedback gains; (iii) a novel perturbation-based method to extract stabilizing feedback gains directly from a Gauss-Newton CIO solve (via Woodbury) without a separate LQG pass; (iv) a distributed, asynchronous alternating optimization with proximal regularization; and (v) execution-time control via a T=1 implicit optimization step to satisfy dynamics/contacts while tracking network outputs. The method produces interactive locomotion for swimmers, a bird, bipeds, and quadrupeds in 3D, claiming regular cyclic behaviors without hand-engineered gaits. Ablations show that joint optimization and noise injection are crucial; a qualitative comparison to MPC suggests the learned policies encourage more predictable, cyclic motions.

Relation to prior work
This sits squarely in the Guided Policy Search (GPS)/trajectory-optimization-for-supervision family (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014). The use of CIO for rich contact dynamics and the explicit derivation of feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization are notable differences from iLQG/LQR-based GPS. The paper also positions itself relative to model-free deep RL (e.g., TRPO, DDPG) and prior graphics controllers (state machines, CPGs), arguing improved realism and purposefulness. MPC is used as a qualitative baseline.

Strengths
- Ambitious and unifying: One training recipe and one network architecture adapt across very different morphologies (fliers, swimmers, bipeds, quadrupeds) in full 3D. This breadth is rare and valuable.
- Technical novelty: The perturbation-based computation of local feedback gains leveraging the CIO Gauss-Newton system and Woodbury identity is elegant, efficient, and practically important. This is a substantive contribution beyond “just GPS.”
- Robustness via noise: Injecting sensory and hidden-unit noise, together with learning from locally optimal feedback targets, is well-motivated and empirically important (ablation evidence).
- Practicality: Asynchronous distributed training with proximal terms is pragmatic and seems to scale; execution-time control leverages the same machinery (implicit step), enabling 50 ms control rates.
- No mocap or hand-crafted gaits: Cyclic gaits and flapping emerge from the objective and the alternating scheme, aligning with the community’s push toward less hand-engineering.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation: Apart from an ablation on biped walking (two orders of magnitude error improvement vs. static data and the necessity of noise), there is little in the way of quantitative metrics (success/fall rates, robustness to pushes, energy/torque costs, interaction latency) across tasks. The MPC comparison is qualitative; no comparisons to GPS with iLQG, TRPO/DDPG, or other strong baselines on shared metrics.
- Physical realism concerns at execution: Dynamics constraints are enforced softly and may allow root forces. The policy outputs contact forces and desired velocities; feasibility is restored by a T=1 optimization each step. This raises questions about how much of the stability comes from the per-step optimizer vs. the learned policy, and how the approach translates to torque-limited real robots.
- Clarity/details: Some notational issues (e.g., Eq. (8) update uses X* on both sides), hyperparameter choices lack justification, and cost-feature definitions are under-specified. The role and benefit of the recurrent state r are not ablated. Reference formatting has several issues.
- Reproducibility and compute: The approach invests substantial compute in CIO data generation. The paper would benefit from clear counts (N, T), wall-clock per trajectory optimization, and generalization versus data size. No code or datasets are promised.
- Scope of novelty relative to GPS: While the paper acknowledges related work, a sharper positioning against GPS variants (e.g., iLQG-based, dual methods, proximal/ADMM formulations) and direct empirical comparisons would strengthen claims.

Questions for authors
- Please report quantitative performance across tasks: success/fall rates, goal-tracking error, energy/torque, response to external perturbations, and compute at test time (CPU/GPU).
- How large are the root forces at execution? Can you bound or eliminate them and still achieve stability? Any results with torque-only policies and hard constraints?
- Sensitivity to λ (gain smoothing), σε, σγ, α, η? An ablation on λ would clarify the trade-off between aggressiveness and stability.
- What is the contribution of the recurrent state r? Please add an ablation with r disabled.
- Could you compare directly against a GPS baseline using iLQG on at least one morphology?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, unified approach with broad demonstrations across morphologies in 3D.
- Novel, neat technique for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Practical distributed training and compelling qualitative results without hand-engineered gaits.
- Advances an important direction at the intersection of control, graphics, and deep learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Weak quantitative evaluation and limited comparisons to strong baselines.
- Execution-time soft constraints and root forces blur claims of physical realism; unclear path to hardware.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). The paper introduces a compelling and ambitious synthesis of CIO with neural policies, including a novel and practical method for feedback-gain extraction and convincing cross-morphology results. Despite evaluation gaps and some clarity issues, the conceptual advance and demonstrated scope justify acceptance at NIPS, and I expect this work to stimulate follow-up research.

Confidence
Moderate.
