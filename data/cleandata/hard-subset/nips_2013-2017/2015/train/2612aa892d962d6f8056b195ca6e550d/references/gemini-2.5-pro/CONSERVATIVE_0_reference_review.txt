**Summary**

This paper presents a method for creating interactive, real-time controllers for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with a neural network policy. Specifically, the authors use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), an established direct trajectory optimization method, to generate high-quality motion data for a variety of tasks (e.g., locomotion to a goal). A multi-layer neural network is then trained via supervised learning to mimic the behavior of the optimizer, effectively "distilling" the offline computation into a fast, online policy.

The main contributions are a joint optimization framework where the trajectory data and the network policy are refined together in an alternating fashion, and the principled injection of noise during training to improve robustness. To stabilize the learning process in the presence of this noise, the method computes and uses optimal feedback gains derived from the trajectory optimizer to define the learning targets. The authors demonstrate impressive results on a diverse set of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers, all controlled by the same general algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The paper is built on a solid foundation. Trajectory optimization, specifically CIO, is a powerful and well-understood technique for generating physically realistic motion. The use of supervised learning to approximate the resulting controller is a logical and established approach. The proposed framework for jointly optimizing the trajectories and the policy is a reasonable, if incremental, extension of prior work.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its results. The ability to generate stable, real-time controllers for such a wide variety of 3D morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) from a single, unified framework is a significant engineering achievement. The emergent gaits appear natural and purposeful, as shown in the supplementary video.

3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors perform crucial ablation studies to validate their specific technical contributions. The comparisons against training on a static dataset ("no joint") and training without noise injection ("no noise") clearly demonstrate that both the joint optimization and the stochastic elements are critical for achieving robust performance. This adds rigor to their claims about their proposed methodology.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the lack of rigorous comparison to established alternative methods. The introduction briefly mentions direct reinforcement learning (RL) methods but dismisses them with the subjective statement: "our impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors." This is not a substitute for a quantitative, empirical comparison. Given the progress in continuous control with RL (e.g., TRPO, PPO), a thorough comparison on the same character morphologies and tasks is essential to properly situate the contribution of this work. Without it, it is impossible to judge whether this complex, computationally intensive "optimize-then-learn" paradigm offers a tangible advantage over end-to-end RL approaches in terms of final policy quality, sample efficiency, or stability.

2.  **Limited Novelty of Core Concepts:** While the specific implementation and results are new, the core idea of using trajectory optimization to generate data for training a neural network policy is not. The paper cites related work by Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014) which explored similar concepts. The main novelties appear to be the extension to 3D, the specific formulation of the joint stochastic optimization, and the method for deriving feedback gains. While these are valuable technical contributions, the paper does not present a fundamentally new paradigm.

3.  **Computational Expense:** The proposed method is computationally demanding, requiring hours of training on a distributed cluster of machines and a GPU. While the authors present this as a practical architecture, a discussion of the cost-benefit tradeoff compared to other methods is missing. A state-of-the-art RL algorithm might achieve comparable or superior results with fewer computational resources, but this question is left unanswered.

4.  **Qualitative Comparison to MPC:** The comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC) is purely qualitative and based on a single figure. The authors claim their method produces more natural, cyclic behaviors. This is an interesting observation, but it should be supported by quantitative metrics (e.g., spectral analysis of joint angles, cost of transport) and a more detailed analysis rather than a brief observation.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a well-engineered system that produces high-quality results in character animation. The methodological components are sound, and the authors have done a good job of validating the importance of their specific technical choices through ablation.

However, from a NIPS perspective, the paper falls short in its evaluation and positioning within the broader machine learning literature. The lack of a rigorous, quantitative comparison against modern reinforcement learning algorithms is a critical omission. This makes it difficult to assess the significance of the contribution. Are the superior-looking motions a result of the inherent properties of the method, or simply the result of extensive computational effort and careful cost function engineering within the trajectory optimizer? The paper feels more aligned with a top-tier computer graphics conference, where visually impressive results and novel system design are primary contributions, than with a machine learning conference like NIPS, where rigorous comparison to the state of the art is a key requirement.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Demonstrates successful control of a diverse set of complex, 3D characters with a single algorithm.
*   The results are visually impressive and represent a step forward in realistic character animation.
*   The ablation studies clearly support the utility of the proposed joint, stochastic optimization framework.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Crucially lacks quantitative comparisons to modern, mainstream reinforcement learning baselines.
*   The conceptual novelty over prior "learn-from-optimizer" work is incremental.
*   The claims of superiority over methods like MPC and direct RL are not sufficiently supported by evidence.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

While the work is impressive from an engineering and application standpoint, its scientific contribution to the machine learning community is not fully established due to the lack of comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods. If the authors were to include rigorous comparisons to strong RL baselines on the same suite of tasks, this could be a very strong paper. As it stands, its claims of superiority are unproven.
