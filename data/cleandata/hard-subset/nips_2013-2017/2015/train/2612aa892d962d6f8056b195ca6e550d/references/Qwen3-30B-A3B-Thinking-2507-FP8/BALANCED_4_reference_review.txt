

This paper proposes a method to learn neural network controllers for interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) by combining trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with stochastic neural network training. The key innovation is a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly learns a policy from trajectories generated by CIO, injecting noise during training to enable robust real-time execution. The authors demonstrate successful locomotion across multiple character morphologies using a single network architecture without task-specific handcrafting.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Soundness & Clarity:** The method is well-motivated and rigorously implemented. The distributed optimization (Algorithm 1) effectively decouples trajectory optimization and policy learning, with ablation studies (§8.1) convincingly showing the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection. The feedback gain calculation (§3.2) is a clever, low-cost extension of CIO that avoids LQG inefficiencies.  
2. **Generality & Results:** The most compelling strength is the demonstrated generality—same network architecture achieves stable locomotion for 3D bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and fliers. The results (Fig. 2, supplementary video) show realistic, cyclic gaits emerging without manual design, a significant advance over prior 2D work [9, 11].  
3. **Addressing Key Challenges:** The paper effectively tackles critical issues in neural control: data scarcity (via CIO-generated trajectories), instability (via noise injection and proximal regularization), and task diversity (via goal-position interaction). The noise injection strategy (§2.1) is principled and well-analyzed.  
4. **Clarity of Contribution:** The distinction from prior trajectory-optimization-based methods [9, 11] is clear: this work solves harder 3D problems and provides a scalable distributed training framework.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Insufficient Comparison to Modern RL Baselines:** The paper lacks meaningful comparison to contemporary reinforcement learning (RL) methods (e.g., SAC [14], PPO [12]), which are now standard for continuous control in NIPS. While the authors cite RL work [3, 6, 10], they do not benchmark against these or discuss why their method outperforms them. For instance, SAC achieves real-time locomotion for quadrupeds [14]—a direct competitor this paper should address. This omission weakens the significance argument and makes the "advantage" of their approach unclear.  
2. **Limited Scope of "Interactivity":** The interaction is restricted to changing goal positions (§5), but the paper does not explore dynamic perturbations (e.g., pushing the character mid-motion) or multi-task switching—key aspects of real-world interactive control. The claim that "the neural network complements [CIO] in a way that yields interactive real-time control" is overstated without evidence of robustness to unexpected disturbances.  
3. **Underdeveloped Robotics Significance:** The conclusion (§9) claims applicability to robotics but provides no evidence (e.g., simulation-to-real transfer, hardware tests). The MuJoCo experiments are purely virtual, and the paper does not discuss how character model fidelity (e.g., friction, mass) affects real-world robustness. This weakens the broader impact.  
4. **Originality Context:** While extending CIO to 3D is valuable, the core idea (learning from trajectory optimization) is not novel [9, 11]. The paper should more explicitly contrast its *distributed stochastic optimization* with prior work (e.g., [11] uses ADMM, which is not asynchronous). The claim that "most of the components were already known" is accurate but insufficient for NIPS without a clear *new* technical contribution.

**Recommendation & Suggestions:**  
This is a strong technical contribution with compelling results for character animation. However, the lack of comparison to modern RL baselines—a critical omission for a NIPS submission—and underdeveloped claims about interactivity/robotics prevent a clear "accept" decision. The paper would benefit significantly from:  
1. **Adding RL baselines** (e.g., SAC, PPO) on the same locomotion tasks to quantify performance gains.  
2. **Expanding the "interactivity" scope** to include perturbations and dynamic task switching, with metrics on robustness.  
3. **Clarifying the robotics relevance** with a small-scale hardware experiment or detailed discussion of transfer challenges.  
4. **Refining the contribution statement** to emphasize the *distributed optimization framework* (Algorithm 1) and *feedback gain method* (§3.2) as the novel technical advances, rather than the overall approach.

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* High-quality technical execution; compelling 3D locomotion results across diverse characters; strong ablation studies; clear distributed training framework.  
*Con:* Lacks comparison to modern RL; overstates "interactivity"; insufficient robotics validation; contribution not sufficiently differentiated from prior trajectory-optimization work.  

**Decision:** **Weak Accept** (with major revisions). The work is technically sound and demonstrates significant progress for character animation, but the NIPS audience expects rigorous comparison to current control methods (especially RL) and clearer articulation of the *new* technical contribution. Addressing the weaknesses above would elevate this to a strong NIPS paper. Without these revisions, the paper risks being perceived as incremental within the broader RL community.
