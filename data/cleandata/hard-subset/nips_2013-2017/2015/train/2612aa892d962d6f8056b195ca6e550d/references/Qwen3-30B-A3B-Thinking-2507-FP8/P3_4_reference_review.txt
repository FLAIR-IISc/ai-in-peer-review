

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary and Context

This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex character motion through a joint optimization of trajectory generation and neural network policy learning. The authors combine Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with neural network training, using trajectory optimization to generate training data and a neural network to mimic the optimal control policy. The key innovation is the distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly optimizes trajectories and network parameters, with noise injection to improve robustness. The method is demonstrated on diverse 3D character types (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying), a significant advancement over previous 2D work.

The paper builds on related work by Levine et al. (ICML 2014) and Mordatch et al. (SIGGRAPH 2015), but claims to solve substantially harder 3D problems. While the combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is not entirely novel, the authors demonstrate a significant step forward in scaling this approach to complex 3D locomotion tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear technical contribution by extending the trajectory optimization + neural network approach to challenging 3D locomotion problems. The distributed stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is well-designed and effectively combines trajectory optimization with neural network training.

2. **Thorough Evaluation**: The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies that convincingly demonstrate the necessity of key components (joint optimization, noise injection) through quantitative comparisons. The "no joint" and "no noise" ablations clearly show the superiority of the proposed approach.

3. **Generalizability**: The method successfully learns controllers for diverse character morphologies (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) using a single network architecture, demonstrating the approach's generality. The results are visually compelling (as indicated by the supplementary video).

4. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the optimal feedback gains calculation (Section 3.2), which is a significant technical contribution that addresses a limitation of previous approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty**: While the method works well on 3D locomotion, the core idea of using trajectory optimization to generate training data for neural networks is not new (Levine et al. 2014, Mordatch et al. 2015). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its technical innovations from prior work, particularly regarding why their approach succeeds in 3D where previous work failed. The claim of "solving substantially harder problems" needs stronger justification.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Modern RL Methods**: The paper compares to naive neural network training and MPC, but omits comparison to recent reinforcement learning methods (e.g., SAC, PPO) that have achieved state-of-the-art results in continuous control tasks. This is a significant omission, as these methods might achieve similar performance with less computational overhead.

3. **Computational Cost**: The method requires "hours" of training time with cloud computing, which is prohibitive for many practical applications. The paper acknowledges this ("we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it"), but doesn't address how this limitation might be overcome or whether the approach is feasible for real-world robotics applications.

4. **Limited Task Scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on locomotion tasks, despite claiming to handle "arbitrary behaviors." This significantly limits the demonstrated scope of the method. The paper could have strengthened its claims by including at least one non-locomotion task.

5. **Quantitative Metrics**: While the paper includes qualitative results, it lacks detailed quantitative metrics to objectively compare performance (e.g., success rate, energy efficiency, stability metrics). The claim that "our method manages to learn successful controllers" would be more convincing with such metrics.

## Technical Assessment

The methodology is well-executed and the mathematical formulation is sound. The distributed stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is a reasonable approach to the problem, and the noise injection technique (Section 2.1) is well-motivated. The optimal feedback gains calculation (Section 3.2) is particularly noteworthy as it solves a problem that previous methods struggled with.

However, the paper could have provided more insight into why the approach works better than naive neural network training on static trajectories. The ablation study shows it works better, but doesn't explain why - is it the joint optimization, the noise injection, or something else?

The sensory representation (st = [qt rt q̇t-1 ft-1]) is simple but not justified. It would be valuable to see if alternative representations might improve performance, especially for more complex behaviors.

## Significance and Impact

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to character animation and control, demonstrating a practical method for generating realistic 3D locomotion behaviors across diverse character types. For the animation industry, this could be a valuable tool for creating diverse character movements without manual design.

However, the significance for the broader machine learning community is limited. The approach relies heavily on trajectory optimization to generate training data, which is a step away from the "end-to-end" learning paradigm that has been central to much recent progress in deep learning. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this approach is superior to modern reinforcement learning methods for control.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-executed method for learning neural network controllers for complex character motion, with strong empirical results on diverse 3D locomotion tasks. While the technical approach is sound and the evaluation thorough, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited by its reliance on established techniques combined with trajectory optimization. The omission of comparison to modern RL methods and the lack of quantitative metrics weaken the paper's contribution.

**Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions** (or borderline accept, depending on the program committee's threshold). The paper has significant value for the animation community and makes a clear technical contribution to the specific problem of character locomotion, but its broader significance for machine learning is limited. The authors should address the following points:

1. Provide a more detailed comparison to recent reinforcement learning methods for control (e.g., SAC, PPO).
2. Include quantitative metrics to objectively evaluate performance beyond visual comparisons.
3. Justify the choice of sensory representation more thoroughly.
4. Expand the discussion of limitations, particularly regarding computational cost and applicability to robotics.

The paper is well-written and makes a meaningful contribution to the specific problem domain, but needs to better position itself within the broader machine learning literature to fully justify its significance for NeurIPS.
