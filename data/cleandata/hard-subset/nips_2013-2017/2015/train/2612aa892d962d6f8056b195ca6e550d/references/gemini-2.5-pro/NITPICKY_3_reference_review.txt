### Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary**
This paper presents a framework for training neural network policies to control physically simulated 3D characters in real time. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of expert behaviors. Crucially, instead of a one-off data generation process, the authors propose a joint, alternating optimization scheme where the expert trajectories and the neural network policy are refined together. To enhance robustness, the method injects noise into the policy's sensory inputs and hidden layer activations during training. A key technical contribution is a method for efficiently calculating optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer, which are used to generate appropriate action targets for the noisy inputs. The authors demonstrate their approach by synthesizing locomotion controllers for a diverse set of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, all using the same general algorithm. This work extends similar prior methods, which were largely confined to 2D characters, to more complex and unstable 3D domains.

**Strengths**
1.  **Ambitious Scope and Impressive Results:** The paper tackles the challenging problem of generating a single, unified controller architecture for a wide variety of character morphologies and locomotion modes (walking, trotting, swimming, flying). The ability to produce stable, cyclic gaits in 3D without morphology-specific engineering is a significant achievement, and the results described (and presumably shown in the video) are compelling.
2.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The core concept of jointly optimizing the "expert" data source (the trajectory optimizer) and the "student" policy (the neural network) is a principled way to address the covariate shift problem common in imitation learning. This iterative refinement is more powerful than naively training on a fixed dataset.
3.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The proposed method for deriving soft feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is clever. It elegantly reuses computations from the Gauss-Newton Hessian to provide the policy with local linear models of the optimal control law, which is essential for the noise injection scheme. This technique could be of independent interest to the trajectory optimization community.

**Weaknesses**
The paper, while presenting promising ideas and results, suffers from a significant number of issues related to clarity, technical precision, experimental rigor, and overall presentation.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:**
    *   The definitions of state and action are confusing and non-standard. The state `s_t` is `[qt rt q̇t−1 f t−1]` while the action `a_t` is `[q̇t ṙt f t]`. This implies the action at time `t` determines the contact forces `f_t`, which is also part of the full character state `[q_t f_t r_t]`. This conflation of control targets with state variables is not well explained and makes the system dynamics difficult to parse. The claim that the "network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously" seems to be an overstatement based on this formulation.
    *   The description of policy execution in Section 7 is terse. It reveals that each real-time step requires solving a constrained optimization problem, which is a non-trivial computation. The claim that this "can be solved at real-time rates" is unsubstantiated, as no timings or complexity analysis are provided. This is a critical detail for a method whose main selling point is real-time performance.
    *   The mathematical notation is frequently sloppy. For instance, the update rule in Eq. (8) is written as `X* = X* - ...`, which is incorrect. Algorithm 1 uses `bar` notation inconsistently for both the previous iterate and the output of the current optimization step. Such lack of precision pervades the manuscript and hinders readability.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The comparative evaluation in Section 8.1 is weak. The "no joint" training ablation compares against a known-to-fail strawman baseline (static behavioral cloning). A more meaningful comparison would be against a well-established imitation learning algorithm like DAgger. The "no noise" ablation merely confirms the well-known regularizing properties of noise injection.
    *   The comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) is anecdotal and purely qualitative, based on a single figure. The claim that the proposed method yields "less chaotic behaviors" is subjective and not supported by any quantitative metrics (e.g., measuring gait periodicity, trajectory variance, or task cost).
    *   The paper introduces a large number of critical hyperparameters (`σε`, `σγ`, `α`, `λ`, `η`) but provides no sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen values. This makes the results difficult to reproduce and raises questions about the robustness of the method to these choices.

3.  **Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   The paper dismisses adapting ADMM or dual gradient descent as "non-trivial" and claims its simpler alternating optimization "still observe[s] convergence," but provides no theoretical justification or analysis. A learning curve is not a proof of convergence for a non-convex, block-alternating scheme.
    *   In Section 3.2, it is claimed that an LQG pass can produce "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" and that the proposed method produces "better-conditioned gains." This is an important point, but it is presented without any supporting evidence or quantitative comparison.

4.  **Poor Presentation:**
    *   The paper appears to have been prepared in a hurry. The reference list is glaringly incomplete and improperly formatted (e.g., Reference [1] is just a title and author, and many others are missing years, page numbers, or conference/journal details). This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier venue.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a difficult and important problem in control and graphics.
*   The results, if accurately represented, demonstrate a significant advance over prior work by successfully handling complex 3D characters.
*   The method contains a novel technical component for feedback gain estimation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a substantial lack of clarity in its core technical definitions and descriptions.
*   The experimental evaluation is weak, relying on poor baselines and subjective, qualitative comparisons.
*   Key claims regarding performance, convergence, and the superiority of technical components are not substantiated with evidence.
*   The overall presentation, particularly the incomplete reference list and sloppy notation, falls well below the expected standards of quality for NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the work presents a promising direction and visually impressive results, its current form is marred by a large number of flaws, ranging from minor notational sloppiness to major issues in experimental rigor and clarity. The accumulation of these issues undermines the reader's confidence in the technical details and makes the contribution difficult to fairly assess and build upon. The paper requires a thorough revision to address the lack of precision in its methodology, strengthen its empirical evaluation, and improve its overall presentation before it can be considered for publication.
