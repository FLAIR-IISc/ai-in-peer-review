- Proposes alternating between Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization and supervised neural policy learning with noise injection and local feedback labels.  
- Technical novelty: perturbation-based method to compute local feedback gains by reusing the CIO Gauss-Newton structure (CXX−1) instead of a separate LQG pass.  
- Training is asynchronous and distributed with proximal regularization; execution uses a 1-step inverse-dynamics optimization with soft dynamics/contact constraints.  
- Related to guided policy search and prior trajectory-optimization + policy-learning work; differences are CIO for contact-rich dynamics, direct gains derivation, and diverse 3D demonstrations.  
- Technical soundness: alternating optimization and CIO backbone are appropriate; feedback-gain trick is neat but derivation needs clearer dimensions, Woodbury use, and conditioning/λ discussion.  
- Execution caveat: soft enforcement and allowed root forces reduce physical fidelity, making robotics claims limited (acceptable for animation).  
- Empirical support: compelling qualitative demos and ablations support design choices, but lacks quantitative metrics, convergence characterization, and strong baseline comparisons.  
- Completeness/reproducibility: missing key implementation details (recurrent state r, observation features, action scaling, runtime, character/contact parameters, handling of predicted contact forces).  
- Clarity: generally well organized and motivated; notation and Section 3.2 derivation need clearer presentation and dimensional annotations; limitations should be stated earlier.  
- Originality: incremental within GPS family but contributes practical gain computation from CIO, noise-labeling via local gains, scalable alternating scheme, and diverse contact-rich 3D results.  
- Significance: impactful for graphics/animation and model-based control; broader influence limited without stronger quantitative evidence and reduced reliance on root forces.  
- Pros: end-to-end practical pipeline, efficient reuse of CIO computations for gains, varied 3D morphologies with emergent gaits, and supportive ablations.  
- Cons: limited quantitative evaluation and baselines, underdeveloped derivation/conditioning analysis, execution relies on soft/root forces, and insufficient policy architecture details.  
- Suggestions: add quantitative metrics (success, error, energy, speed, robustness, runtime), compare to GPS/model-free baselines, clarify r and network architecture, expand derivation and conditioning study, report contact-force handling and root-force magnitudes, and release implementation details.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept (weak accept); stronger quantitative evaluation and clearer derivations would substantially strengthen the paper.
