This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use trajectory optimization to generate training data, and then train a neural network to mimic the optimal control policy learned by the optimizer. The authors claim to achieve stable and realistic locomotion in 3D, surpassing prior work limited to 2D characters. They address challenges related to high-dimensional control, non-i.i.d. data, and overfitting through noise injection and a distributed optimization scheme.

The paper is conceptually interesting and tackles a challenging problem – bridging the gap between offline trajectory optimization and online, real-time control. The results, demonstrated through diverse character locomotion examples (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking), appear promising, especially the extension to 3D locomotion, which is a significant advancement over previous work. The inclusion of optimal feedback gains calculated through a perturbation method during trajectory optimization is a clever addition, aiming for robustness to noise.

However, despite the potential, the paper suffers from a significant number of shortcomings, many of which are surprisingly superficial given the complexity of the core idea. The writing, while generally understandable, is often verbose and lacks precision. Several statements are made without sufficient justification or supporting evidence. The methodological details are often glossed over, leaving the reader with questions about implementation specifics. Furthermore, the presentation of results lacks rigor.

Here's a detailed breakdown of concerns:

**Technical Soundness & Completeness:**

* **Algorithm 1: Lack of Detail.** The algorithm description is too high-level. What specific optimization algorithm is used for trajectory optimization (step 2)? What solver is used for neural network regression (step 3)? What batch size, learning rate, and other hyperparameters were used? These are critical details for reproducibility.
* **Noise Injection – First-Order Expansion.** Equation (2) assumes the action space is locally linear, and its validity is not discussed.  The justification for this approximation feels weak, especially given the complexity of the characters.  How sensitive is the method to deviations from this linearity?
* **Quadratic Penalty.**  The choice of the penalty weight α in equation (3) seems arbitrary. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of α on performance is missing. 
* **Recurrent State ‘r’.** The recurrent state 'r' is introduced as part of the state definition but its role and how it is updated remains vague. The paper states the network "learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously," but there’s no elaboration on how 'r' facilitates this.
* **Optimal Feedback Gains – Perturbation Method.** While the perturbation method for calculating optimal feedback gains is interesting, the justification for choosing the perturbation weight λ is missing. Is there a principled way to select λ, or is it simply tuned empirically? The claim that it produces "softer" gains without modifying the cost function is intriguing but needs further explanation.
* **Trajectory Optimization – Soft Constraints.** The paper mentions using soft constraints, but the details of their implementation are lacking. What specific penalty functions are used for the non-penetration and force complementarity constraints?

**Clarity & Organization:**

* **Section 2 – Overloaded Notation.** The state and action definitions in Section 2 are dense and potentially confusing. Using more descriptive variable names would improve readability.
* **Section 3.2 – Confusing Language.** The explanation of the perturbation method for optimal feedback gains is unnecessarily convoluted. Simplifying the explanation and providing a concrete example would greatly enhance understanding.
* **Figure & Table References.** The references to figures and tables (e.g., "figure 2", "table 1") are made without context within the main text. Each figure and table needs to be discussed and its relevance explained.
* **Section 8 – Abrupt Transitions.** The Results section jumps between different character types without a clear narrative flow. A more structured presentation of results, perhaps organized by character type or task, would be more effective.

**Originality & Related Work:**

* **Limited Comparison.** While the authors mention comparing their method to other approaches, the comparison is superficial. A more systematic evaluation against state-of-the-art methods (e.g., MPC, direct reinforcement learning) is needed.
* **Lack of Depth in Related Work.** The related work section feels cursory. The authors should more thoroughly discuss existing work on neural network controllers, trajectory optimization, and reinforcement learning.

**Significance & Impact:**

* **Reproducibility Concerns.** The lack of implementation details makes it difficult to assess the reproducibility of the results. 
* **Generalizability.** While the authors demonstrate success on a variety of characters, it is unclear how well the method would generalize to even more complex morphologies or tasks. 
* **Computational Cost.** The statement that “we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it” is concerning. This implies a significant computational burden, which may limit the practicality of the approach.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks for complex character control.
* Successful demonstration of 3D locomotion, exceeding prior work.
* Interesting use of optimal feedback gains for robustness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Numerous methodological details are missing or glossed over.
* Lack of a rigorous evaluation and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* Insufficient justification for key design choices (e.g., penalty weights, noise levels).
* Poorly written and organized presentation.
* Concerns about reproducibility and computational cost.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, the numerous flaws in its technical details, presentation, and evaluation outweigh its strengths. The lack of precision and rigor raises concerns about the reliability and generalizability of the results. I recommend **rejection**.  The authors need to significantly revise the paper, addressing the concerns outlined above, before it can be considered for publication at NIPS. Specifically, they should provide a complete and detailed description of the algorithm, a thorough evaluation against baseline methods, and a more clear and concise presentation of the results.
