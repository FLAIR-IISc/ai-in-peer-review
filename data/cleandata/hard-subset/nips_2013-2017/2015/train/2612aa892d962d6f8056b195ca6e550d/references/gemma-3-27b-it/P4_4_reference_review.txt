This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters via a combination of trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) and supervised learning. The core idea is to use trajectory optimization to generate training data, and then train a neural network to mimic the behavior of the optimizer, enabling real-time control. The method addresses challenges inherent in learning controllers for high-dimensional, continuous spaces, such as data scarcity and closed-loop instability, through noise injection and a distributed optimization scheme. The paper demonstrates the approach on a diverse set of characters, including swimming, flying, and legged creatures, achieving stable and realistic locomotion.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the difficulties of traditional control methods and the potential of deep learning in this domain. The authors position their work effectively within the existing literature, referencing relevant work in reinforcement learning, optimal control, and character animation. The combination of trajectory optimization for data generation and neural network learning for real-time execution is a logical and promising approach, particularly given the successes of CIO in generating complex behaviors. The inclusion of noise injection as a form of regularization and stabilization is a valuable contribution. The distributed training architecture is a practical consideration for tackling the scale of the problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a challenging problem:** Interactive control of complex characters is a difficult problem with broad applications.
*   **Novel combination of techniques:** Combining trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network learning is a novel approach.
*   **Demonstrated scalability:** The method demonstrates successful application to 3D characters, overcoming limitations of prior work primarily focused on 2D.
*   **Addresses key challenges:** The paper acknowledges and addresses crucial challenges such as data scarcity, closed-loop instability, and overfitting.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The results showcase stable and realistic locomotion for a variety of characters, supported by a supplementary video.
*   **Detailed explanation of the algorithm:** The paper provides a comprehensive explanation of the algorithm, including the optimization process, noise injection scheme, and distributed training architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited comparative evaluation:** While the ablation studies are informative, a more comprehensive comparison with alternative approaches (e.g., direct reinforcement learning methods like those mentioned in the introduction [3, 6, 14]) would strengthen the paper. The authors state an "impression" that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors" – this should be supported by quantitative comparisons.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The algorithm involves several hyperparameters (e.g., noise levels, regularization weights). The sensitivity of the method to these parameters and guidance on their selection would be valuable.
*   **Computational cost:** The paper acknowledges the high computational cost of trajectory optimization. A more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required and potential strategies for reducing the cost would be beneficial. The statement that more resources are spent on data generation than learning is intriguing but lacks further analysis.
*   **Lack of generalization analysis:** While the method works well on the tested characters, the extent to which it generalizes to unseen character morphologies or environments is not thoroughly explored.
*   **MPC Comparison:** The comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) feels superficial. Saying MPC's solutions aren't cyclical doesn't adequately justify the approach in the paper. A more detailed comparison of computational costs and advantages/disadvantages would be helpful.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem in character control.
*   The method demonstrates strong empirical results on a diverse set of characters.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly explains the algorithm, and provides a thorough analysis of the results.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact areas such as robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The comparative evaluation is limited, lacking a thorough comparison with alternative approaches.
*   The computational cost of the method is high, and the paper does not provide sufficient guidance on reducing it.
*   The generalization capabilities of the method are not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of character control. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a promising approach, and the authors have demonstrated its effectiveness on a variety of complex characters. While there are some areas for improvement, particularly in terms of comparative evaluation and computational cost analysis, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, adding more quantitative comparisons to existing methods and a more in-depth discussion of computational cost would be beneficial.
