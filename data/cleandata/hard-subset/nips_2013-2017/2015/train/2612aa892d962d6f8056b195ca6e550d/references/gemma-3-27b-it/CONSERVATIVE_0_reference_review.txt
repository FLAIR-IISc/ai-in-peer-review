This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use CIO to generate training data, then train a neural network to mimic the learned behaviors, enabling real-time control. The authors demonstrate this on a variety of 3D characters – swimmers, fliers, bipeds, and quadrupeds – arguing that their approach yields more realistic and stable locomotion compared to purely data-driven methods.

While the problem addressed – automating controller design for complex characters – is undeniably important and has been a long-standing goal in robotics, animation, and related fields, this paper falls short of convincing me that the proposed solution represents a significant advance. The approach feels largely incremental, relying heavily on established techniques (CIO and supervised learning) and applying them to a higher-dimensional problem. The primary novelty seems to reside in the scaling of the method and the integration of noise injection and optimal feedback gains, but the gains from these improvements aren’t rigorously demonstrated.

The most significant weakness lies in the comparative evaluation. The authors claim their trajectory-optimization-based approach yields more realistic behaviors than “direct methods” (e.g., reinforcement learning) but provide only an “impression” rather than a systematic comparison. This is a critical omission. Without benchmarks against standard reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) or other established control techniques, it’s impossible to assess the true benefit of their method. Furthermore, the ablation study, while present, is limited. Showing superior performance against a static dataset *after* trajectory optimization is unsurprising, and disabling noise injection demonstrates a known problem of overfitting, rather than a flaw in the core concept. The comparison to MPC is interesting but doesn’t offer a direct performance metric; stating different resulting trajectories isn't a compelling argument for superiority. 

The methodological details, while generally sound, lack sufficient depth in certain areas. For example, the choice of network architecture (3 layers, 250 units) appears somewhat arbitrary and isn't justified by a thorough exploration of different configurations. The parameter choices for the optimization process (α, η, σ) are stated but lack rationale. Furthermore, the claim of using cloud computing and GPU implementation to handle the scale of the problem is expected for this type of work and doesn't constitute a significant contribution in itself. The description of the distributed training architecture is superficial and lacks details on communication overhead or synchronization strategies.

The paper is reasonably well-written and clearly organized. However, the language is often overly assertive, presenting improvements as definitively proven rather than empirically demonstrated. The supplementary video is helpful, but visual results alone aren't sufficient to support the claims made in the paper. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   Demonstrates the ability to generate controllers for a variety of complex characters.
*   Combines established techniques in a potentially useful way.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks a rigorous comparative evaluation against baseline methods (RL, MPC).
*   Limited ablation study and insufficient justification for parameter choices.
*   Claims of superiority are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence.
*   Methodological details are sometimes superficial.
*   Incremental advance that doesn’t fundamentally change the field.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. 

While the work is technically competent and addresses an interesting problem, it does not present a compelling case for its novelty or significance. The lack of a thorough comparative evaluation, the superficiality of certain methodological details, and the overly assertive claims leave me unconvinced that this paper meets the standards for acceptance at NIPS. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by conducting a more comprehensive benchmark against state-of-the-art control algorithms, before resubmitting.
