This paper presents a compelling approach to learning controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with neural network regression. The core idea – leveraging the optimizer to generate training data for a neural network that can then execute control in real-time – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in control: scaling to high-dimensional, complex systems. The authors demonstrate impressive results across a diverse range of character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal), showcasing the generality of their method.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The method is technically well-grounded, drawing on established techniques (CIO, neural networks, stochastic optimization). The explanation of the combined approach is clear and logical. The inclusion of optimal feedback gains to improve robustness is a particularly clever addition.
* **Originality:** While combining learning and optimization isn’t novel *per se*, the scale and generality of this approach – achieving stable 3D locomotion across diverse characters – *is* a significant step forward. Previous work often focused on 2D systems or simpler control tasks. The distributed stochastic optimization scheme is also a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:** The ability to automatically generate controllers for complex characters has broad implications for robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The demonstrated versatility across different morphologies is particularly promising.  The reduction in reliance on hand-engineered controllers is a major advantage.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors provide sufficient detail to understand the key components of the method. The algorithm is clearly presented, and the description of the training architecture is adequate.
* **Thoroughness:** The ablation studies (Figure 2) demonstrate the importance of the joint optimization and noise injection components, lending credence to the authors’ claims.  The comparison to MPC, though brief, highlights a distinct characteristic of the learned controllers (cyclical behavior).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the ablation studies are helpful, a more comprehensive comparison to other learning-based control methods (e.g., those cited in the introduction – [3, 6, 14]) is needed. A quantitative comparison, even on a limited set of tasks, would strengthen the paper. The authors state their impression that direct methods yield "chaotic" behaviors, but this needs to be substantiated with data.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lists the hyperparameters used, but doesn't discuss their sensitivity or provide guidance on how to tune them for different characters or tasks. A brief discussion of this would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the substantial computational cost (hours of training, more resources spent generating data than learning), a more detailed breakdown of the costs would be useful.  Specifically, how does the computational cost scale with character complexity or the desired level of precision?
* **Implementation Details:** The description of the physics simulator (MuJoCo) is brief. More details about the character models themselves (e.g., degrees of freedom, mass distribution) would provide important context.
* **Noise Injection Rationale:** The explanation for *why* injecting noise at each layer of the neural network is beneficial is somewhat hand-wavy.  More justification, perhaps referencing related work on regularization in deep learning, would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper addresses a challenging problem in control with a technically sound and original approach.
* The results are impressive, demonstrating the ability to learn controllers for diverse and complex characters.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.
* The ablation studies provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a more comprehensive comparative analysis weakens the claim of superiority over existing methods.
* The discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed method is a valuable contribution to the field of character control, and the results are compelling. While the weaknesses mentioned above should be addressed, they do not fundamentally undermine the quality of the work. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance, but *conditional on* the authors addressing the points raised regarding comparative analysis and providing more details on hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.  Specifically, I recommend adding a table comparing performance metrics against at least 2-3 relevant existing methods (even if it requires running additional experiments) and including a short section in the appendix discussing hyperparameter tuning strategies.
