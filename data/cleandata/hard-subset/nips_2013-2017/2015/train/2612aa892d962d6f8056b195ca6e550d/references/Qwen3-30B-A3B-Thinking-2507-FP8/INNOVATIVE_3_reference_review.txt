

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural network controllers for complex character animation through the integration of trajectory optimization and neural network learning. The core idea is to use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate high-quality training trajectories for a neural network policy that can then execute interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, flying, swimming) without requiring specialized controllers for each character type. The authors introduce a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly optimizes trajectories and neural network parameters, along with a clever method for computing optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.

The paper makes several significant contributions: First, it demonstrates the first successful application of neural network controllers for interactive 3D character locomotion, moving beyond the 2D limitations of prior work [9, 11]. Second, it proposes a unified framework that can handle diverse character morphologies and locomotion styles (bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting, swimming, and flying) with a single controller architecture. Third, it introduces a distributed stochastic optimization approach (Algorithm 1) that effectively handles the joint optimization of trajectories and neural network parameters, which previous work [9, 11] had struggled with in asynchronous settings.

The technical novelty is compelling. The key insight that trajectory optimization can serve as a data generator for neural network policies—rather than being a separate offline step—represents a fresh perspective on the neural control problem. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method produces significantly more stable and realistic behaviors than naive neural network training on static trajectories (Figure 2), and that their noise injection technique prevents overfitting (Table 1). The ablation studies in Section 8.1 provide strong empirical support for their key innovations.

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a major pain point in character animation and robotics: the need for specialized controllers for different character types and behaviors. By providing a unified approach that works across diverse morphologies in 3D, the authors open up new possibilities for interactive character control. The demonstration of successful 3D locomotion across multiple character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) represents a clear advancement over prior work limited to 2D scenarios.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The mathematical formulation is clear, the algorithm is well-described, and the experimental results are thorough and convincing. The supplementary video (as mentioned) would be essential for fully appreciating the quality of the generated behaviors.

The main limitations are that the method remains computationally intensive (2.5 hours of training time) and focused primarily on locomotion rather than more complex interactions. The paper also doesn't directly compare with state-of-the-art RL methods for control tasks, though the authors make a reasonable case that direct RL methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors" compared to their approach. However, these limitations are minor compared to the paper's significant novelty and scope.

This paper stands out as a bold and novel contribution that successfully bridges trajectory optimization and neural network learning for character control. It demonstrates a clear path toward a unified framework for interactive character control that could transform animation pipelines and robotics control. While the method isn't perfect, the paper's core idea is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance. The work represents a significant step toward the ambitious goal of "universal synthesis methods applicable to arbitrary behaviors, body morphologies, online changes in task objectives."

Strengths: Novel approach combining trajectory optimization with neural networks for 3D character control; unified framework for diverse character types; strong empirical results across multiple locomotion styles; clear technical contribution in distributed stochastic optimization.

Weaknesses: Limited comparison with state-of-the-art RL methods; computational cost remains relatively high; focuses on locomotion rather than more complex behaviors.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and represents an important step toward more generalizable character control systems. The novelty of the approach, combined with its strong empirical validation across multiple character types in 3D, makes it a compelling submission for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
