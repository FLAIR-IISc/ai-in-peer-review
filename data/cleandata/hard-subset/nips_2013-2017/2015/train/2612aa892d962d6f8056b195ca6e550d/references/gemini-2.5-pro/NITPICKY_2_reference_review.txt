### Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary**

This paper presents a method for synthesizing interactive, real-time controllers for physically simulated characters with diverse morphologies. The core idea is to couple an offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), with the training of a neural network policy. The framework jointly optimizes a set of reference trajectories and the parameters of the neural network in an alternating, distributed fashion. The network learns to mimic the behavior of the optimizer, effectively creating an online policy that can be executed in real-time. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, and a novel technique for extracting stable feedback gains directly from the CIO method. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by generating locomotion controllers for various 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flying creature, all using the same general algorithm without task-specific engineering. This work extends similar prior efforts [9, 11] from 2D to more challenging 3D domains.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant and Impressive Results:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its demonstrated results. Successfully generating stable, realistic, and interactive gaits for a wide variety of complex 3D characters from a single, unified framework is a notable achievement. The emergence of stereotypical gaits (e.g., bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting) without explicit encoding is compelling evidence for the method's power and generality.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution (Feedback Gains):** The method proposed in Section 3.2 for deriving "softer," well-conditioned feedback gains directly from a trajectory optimization method is a valuable and clever contribution. By re-framing the problem as finding the optimal response to a soft state perturbation, the authors cleverly reuse computations from the optimization's Newton step (specifically, the inverse Hessian) and introduce a regularizing parameter `λ`. This technique could be of independent interest to the optimal control community.

3.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The comparative evaluations in Section 8.1 are well-conceived. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the necessity of both the joint optimization scheme (vs. training on a static dataset) and the noise injection procedures. The performance drop in the "no joint" and "no noise" conditions is dramatic and convincingly supports the authors' claims. The qualitative comparison with MPC also provides useful insight into the regularizing effect of the policy network.

**Weaknesses**

While the high-level ideas and results are strong, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, ambiguities, and presentation issues that collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Core Methodology:**
    *   **State-Action Representation:** The definitions of the sensory state `s_t` and action `a_t` in Section 2 are confusing and poorly justified. `s_t` includes `q̇_{t-1}` and `f_{t-1}`, while `a_t` includes `q̇_t` and `f_t`. This temporal misalignment is non-standard and its motivation is never explained. The notation `q̇t , qt+1 − qt` is ambiguous; does the comma represent "or" or "and"? This lack of precision is a critical barrier to understanding and reproduction.
    *   **Network's Role in Dynamics:** The paper claims the "network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously." This appears to be an overstatement. During execution (Section 7), the network outputs a *target* action `q̇des`, which is then fed to a *separate* single-timestep trajectory optimization problem to evolve the state. The network is not a dynamics model in the traditional sense; rather, it provides targets for a model-based controller. This distinction is important and should be clarified.

2.  **Insufficient Justification and Analysis:**
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper provides a list of hyperparameters used in all experiments (`σε`, `σγ`, `α`, `λ`, `η`). However, there is no justification for these specific values, nor is any sensitivity analysis presented. How robust is the method to these choices? For instance, the parameter `λ` in Section 3.2, which controls the "softness" of the feedback gains, seems crucial, yet its effect is not explored.
    *   **Unsupported Claims:** The introduction motivates the work by stating the authors' "impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors." This is a key claim used to position the paper against a major branch of related work, yet it is presented as subjective "impression" without empirical evidence or citation.

3.  **Sloppy Presentation and Scholarship:**
    *   **Mathematical Notation:** The mathematical notation is inconsistent and at times incorrect. In Equation (6), `d(q)>f = 0` uses non-standard notation for what is presumably a dot product or transpose (`d(q)^T f`). In Equation (8), the recursion for Newton's method is written sloppily as `X* = X* - C^{-1}_{XX} C_X`, which is confusing.
    *   **Typos:** There are several typographical errors, such as "unity" instead of "unify" in the introduction.
    *   **Incomplete References:** The reference list is in poor shape. Several entries are incomplete, most notably reference [1] ("Hessian matrix vs. gauss-newton hessian matrix, P. Chen, SIAM J. Numerical Analysis,, , ,"), which is missing the year, volume, and page numbers. References [7], [17] and others also appear to be malformed. This lack of attention to detail in the bibliography is concerning for a submission to a top-tier venue like NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the difficult problem of general-purpose motor control for complex 3D characters, advancing the state-of-the-art demonstrated in prior work.
*   The results are visually impressive (as suggested by the descriptions and appeal to the supplementary video) and show a high degree of generality.
*   The method for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization is a novel and useful technical contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in core methodological details, hindering reproducibility and a deep understanding of the work.
*   Key claims are insufficiently supported, and the choice of hyperparameters is not justified.
*   The manuscript exhibits a general lack of polish, with numerous notational inconsistencies, typographical errors, and a seriously flawed reference list. The accumulation of these minor issues suggests the work is not yet ready for publication.

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**. While the research direction is promising and the high-level results appear significant, the paper in its current form does not meet the standards of quality and clarity expected at NIPS. The numerous flaws—ranging from unclear methodological descriptions to sloppy notation and an incomplete bibliography—create a substantial burden on the reader and prevent a confident assessment of the work's technical soundness and reproducibility. The authors have a strong foundation for an excellent paper, but it requires a thorough revision to address the issues of clarity, rigor, and presentation before it can be considered for publication.
