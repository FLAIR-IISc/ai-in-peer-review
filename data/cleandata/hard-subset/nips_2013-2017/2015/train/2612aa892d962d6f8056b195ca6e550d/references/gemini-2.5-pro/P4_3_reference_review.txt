This paper presents a method for training neural network policies for interactive, real-time control of diverse and complex physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline, computationally expensive trajectory optimizer to generate high-quality training data for the neural network. The training is formulated as a joint optimization problem over both a set of optimal trajectories and the neural network parameters. This large, non-convex problem is solved using a distributed, block-alternating optimization scheme, where trajectory optimization steps and neural network regression steps are performed iteratively. This approach builds upon recent work that also combines trajectory optimization with function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). However, the key contribution of this work is a set of technical improvements that enable the method to scale to substantially harder problems, specifically demonstrating stable, realistic locomotion for a variety of complex 3D characters, a significant step beyond previous 2D results.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Quality:** The paper addresses a long-standing and important problem in computer graphics, robotics, and reinforcement learning: the automatic synthesis of robust, real-time controllers for high-dimensional, physically-simulated systems. The demonstrated results, controlling diverse morphologies (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flier) with a single, unified algorithm, represent a significant advancement in the state of the art. The ability for complex, cyclic gaits to emerge naturally without task-specific engineering is particularly impressive. The work is a complete and substantial contribution.

*   **Originality and Technical Soundness:** While the high-level idea of learning from an optimizer is not new, the paper introduces several novel and technically sound components that appear crucial for its success.
    1.  The method for injecting noise during training is principled. Instead of simply adding noise to states and actions, the authors inject noise into the sensory inputs and compute the corresponding first-order correction to the optimal action using feedback gains (Section 2.1). This provides the network with locally valid guidance on how to recover from perturbations.
    2.  The paper presents a novel and efficient method for deriving these optimal feedback gains directly from a direct trajectory optimization method (Contact-Invariant Optimization) (Section 3.2). This reuses computation from the optimization and provides a way to regularize the gains, which is a valuable contribution in its own right.
    3.  The ablation study in Section 8.1 provides strong evidence for the necessity of the core components. It clearly shows that naive training on a static dataset ("no joint") or training without noise injection ("no noise") leads to policies that are unstable and perform orders of magnitude worse at test time.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The overall optimization problem is clearly formulated in Equation (1), and the decomposition into an alternating algorithm is logically explained. The roles of the trajectory optimizer and the neural network regressor are distinct and well-defined. The description of the distributed training architecture in Section 6 enhances the practicality and reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Convergence Properties:** The paper employs a block-alternating optimization scheme with proximal regularization terms. While the authors state that this converges in practice, the paper lacks a theoretical discussion of the algorithm's convergence properties. Given that prior work used more established methods like ADMM, a brief discussion on why the chosen method is preferable for this asynchronous and stochastic setting, and its convergence guarantees (or lack thereof), would strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding.

*   **Physical Fidelity during Execution:** The policy execution step (Section 7) involves solving a small optimization problem at each timestep to determine the next state. While this avoids instabilities associated with explicit integration, it enforces physics dynamics as soft constraints. The paper mentions that this "may include some root forces in simulation," which implies non-physical behavior (i.e., external "hand of God" forces). The paper would be more convincing if it quantified the magnitude of these constraint violations during typical interactive sessions to provide a clearer picture of the controller's physical accuracy.

*   **Comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC):** Section 8.1 provides a qualitative comparison to MPC, noting that the proposed method produces more predictable, cyclic behaviors. While insightful, this comparison could be stronger. A quantitative comparison on metrics such as task completion cost, computational cost per step, and trajectory smoothness would provide a more objective assessment of the trade-offs between the two approaches.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper demonstrates a significant leap in capability over prior art, successfully synthesizing controllers for a diverse range of complex 3D characters.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the principled approach to noise injection and the method for deriving feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization, are novel and impactful.
*   The empirical evaluation is strong, featuring a variety of distinct character types and a compelling ablation study that validates the key design choices.
*   The work is of high significance to researchers and practitioners in animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm is limited.
*   The physical accuracy of the controller during real-time execution is not rigorously quantified.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly the impressive empirical results and novel technical contributions that enable them, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The work represents a clear and significant advance on a difficult problem.
