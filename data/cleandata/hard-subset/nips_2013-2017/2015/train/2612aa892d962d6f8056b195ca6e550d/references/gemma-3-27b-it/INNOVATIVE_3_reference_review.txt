This paper presents a compelling approach to learning neural network controllers for complex character locomotion by bridging trajectory optimization and supervised learning. The core idea – leveraging the power of CIO to generate training data and then distilling that knowledge into a neural network for real-time control – is both novel and promising. It’s a bold attempt to unify disparate approaches to character control and move beyond hand-crafted controllers or limited-scope RL solutions. The extension to 3D locomotion, a known challenge for many existing methods, is particularly noteworthy. 

The strengths of this work are significant. The ability to synthesize diverse locomotion behaviors across different character morphologies with a single network architecture is a substantial achievement. The stochastic optimization framework, while complex, appears well-motivated and addresses the challenges of data scarcity and overfitting inherent in learning controllers. The inclusion of noise injection and the clever reuse of computation from trajectory optimization to calculate feedback gains are intelligent design choices. The results, as presented and exemplified in the supplementary video, are impressive, demonstrating stable and realistic movement. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous work and position their contribution as a significant step forward. The distributed training architecture is practical and necessary for the scale of the problem.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the need for a systematic comparison to “direct methods” (e.g., purely supervised learning or RL), this comparison remains largely impressionistic. A more rigorous quantitative evaluation against state-of-the-art baselines would significantly strengthen the claims of superiority. The ablation study is helpful, but more exhaustive analysis of the hyperparameters (beyond network size) and the impact of different noise levels would be beneficial. The discussion of the algorithm’s convergence is somewhat hand-wavy; more detailed analysis of the optimization process and its stability would inspire greater confidence. The reliance on MuJoCo as the physics simulator introduces a potential bias, and exploring performance in other environments could broaden the generalizability assessment. Finally, while the authors mention future work on incorporating muscle actuation, the current focus on kinematic control feels somewhat limiting.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **High novelty:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks in this manner is a fresh perspective on the problem of learning controllers for complex characters.
* **Significant potential impact:** The ability to create general-purpose controllers for diverse morphologies has broad implications for animation, robotics, and biomechanics.
* **Strong empirical results:** The demonstrated locomotion behaviors are compelling and represent a significant step beyond prior work, particularly in 3D.
* **Bold approach:** Tackling the challenges of high-dimensional control with a principled and comprehensive approach is commendable.
* **Well-articulated problem & solution:** The authors clearly identify the shortcomings of existing methods and present a well-motivated and technically sound solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Lack of rigorous baseline comparison:** The claim of outperforming “direct methods” is not fully substantiated by quantitative evidence.
* **Limited hyperparameter analysis:** The sensitivity of the method to different parameter settings remains largely unexplored.
* **Convergence analysis is weak:** The description of the optimization process is somewhat vague, and more detailed analysis would be beneficial.
* **Dependency on a specific physics simulator:** The reliance on MuJoCo may limit the generalizability of the results.



**Overall assessment:**

Despite the minor flaws, this paper presents a highly innovative and promising approach to learning controllers for complex characters. The novelty of the idea, the potential impact of the results, and the authors' boldness in tackling a challenging problem outweigh the limitations. While a more rigorous evaluation and more detailed analysis would be desirable, the work, as presented, represents a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more comprehensive baseline comparison and a more detailed discussion of the optimization process. The potential for this work to inspire new directions in character control and robotics is substantial.
