## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for learning neural network control policies for complex, 3D articulated characters through a combination of trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) and supervised learning. The core idea is to use CIO to generate a dataset of optimal trajectories and then train a neural network to mimic these trajectories, enabling real-time, interactive control. The authors address challenges related to scaling this approach to high-dimensional spaces, including noisy training data and potential overfitting, through techniques like noise injection and distributed stochastic optimization. They demonstrate their method on a diverse set of characters (swimming, flying, bipeds, quadrupeds) and show that it produces stable and realistic locomotion without requiring hand-crafted controllers or motion capture data.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in reinforcement learning, optimal control, and neural network-based control. It acknowledges prior work in trajectory optimization (CIO [12]) and neural network control [3, 6, 9, 10, 11, 14], and positions itself as a method that can handle more complex problems – specifically, stable 3D locomotion – than previous approaches. The work also draws inspiration from techniques used in computer graphics for physically-based animation. The authors correctly identify that their method differs from direct reinforcement learning approaches, focusing on a supervised learning paradigm guided by trajectory optimization.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The method demonstrably scales to complex, 3D characters, a significant advancement over previous work primarily limited to 2D environments. This is a major achievement.
* **Generality:** The ability to control diverse character morphologies (swimming, flying, walking) with a single network architecture is impressive and showcases the method’s generality. This reduces the need for specialized controller design for each character.
* **Stability and Realism:** The learned controllers exhibit stable and realistic locomotion, avoiding the “chaotic behaviors” often seen in purely data-driven approaches.
* **Principled Noise Injection:** The inclusion of noise, coupled with the calculation of optimal feedback gains, is a clever approach to improve robustness and generalization, addressing a key challenge in learning controllers.
* **Efficient Training Paradigm:**  The approach strategically allocates computational resources, prioritizing trajectory optimization (the "heavy lifting") and using the neural network as a learned function approximator.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the key concepts and algorithmic details clearly. The ablation study (Section 8.1) is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** While the ablation study demonstrates the importance of joint optimization and noise injection, a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art control methods (e.g., Model Predictive Control, reinforcement learning algorithms like PPO or SAC) would strengthen the paper. The claim that direct methods yield “successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors” feels unsubstantiated without a more direct comparison.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., noise variances, penalty weights) seems somewhat arbitrary. A discussion of sensitivity to these parameters and a more systematic hyperparameter tuning process would be beneficial.
* **Black Box Nature:** While the method generates effective controllers, it offers limited insight into *why* certain behaviors emerge.  Understanding the learned representations within the neural network could provide valuable insights.
* **Dependence on CIO:** The method's success is heavily reliant on the quality of the trajectories generated by CIO. Any limitations or biases in CIO will likely propagate to the learned controller. A discussion of potential failure modes of CIO and how they might affect the overall system is warranted.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors highlight the distributed training architecture, the overall computational cost (hours of training) remains substantial, potentially limiting accessibility for researchers with limited resources.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant advance in the field of neural network-based control, demonstrating the ability to learn stable and realistic locomotion for complex 3D characters.
* The method is highly generalizable, capable of controlling a diverse set of morphologies with a single network architecture.
* The principled approach to noise injection and distributed optimization addresses key challenges in learning high-dimensional control policies.
* The ablation study provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The comparative evaluation is limited, lacking a direct comparison to other state-of-the-art control techniques.
* The hyperparameter selection process is not fully justified.
* The reliance on CIO as a pre-processing step introduces a potential point of failure.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in robotics and animation. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates impressive results. While the comparative evaluation could be strengthened and some aspects of the method (e.g., hyperparameter tuning) could be further explored, the overall contribution is significant. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and recommend **acceptance**. 

**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
