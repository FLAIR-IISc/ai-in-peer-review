- Proposes alternating optimization coupling Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory synthesis with supervised neural policy training for real-time 3D character control.
- Uses CIO to generate goal-directed trajectories and trains a policy to match actions under noise-injected sensory inputs.
- Computes local optimal feedback gains around trajectory points and forms noise-conditioned targets a + a_s ε to mitigate compounding errors.
- Introduces a perturbation-based method reusing the Gauss-Newton Hessian inverse from CIO to efficiently extract feedback gains.
- Alternates between re-optimizing trajectories with a quadratic penalty for policy consistency and regressing the policy via SGD with sensory and hidden-unit noise.
- At test time the policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent state changes, and contact forces; next state obtained by solving a short-horizon (T=1) inverse-dynamics optimization.
- Experiments are mainly qualitative (video) with ablations showing benefits of joint optimization and noise injection, plus a brief MPC comparison.
- Close relation to Guided Policy Search and prior trajectory-optimization-based policy learning; novelty in using CIO, the perturbation gain extraction, and 3D demonstrations.
- Clear problem setup unifying trajectory optimization and policy learning with a practical distributed implementation.
- Elegant Woodbury-based derivation for extracting stabilizing gains from direct optimization, avoiding ill-conditioned naive LQG gains.
- Principled use of sensory and hidden-unit noise with ablations supporting their importance for robustness.
- Demonstrations on diverse 3D morphologies with emergent cyclic gaits from a single policy architecture.
- Asynchronous, scalable training pipeline that appears practically robust.
- Comparison to MPC suggests joint training regularizes toward predictable cyclic solutions.
- Policy architecture and role/implementation of recurrent state r are under-specified, harming reproducibility.
- Per-step inverse-dynamics execution with soft dynamics/contact enforcement (including root forces) weakens physical realism and robotics applicability.
- Evaluation is mostly qualitative; missing quantitative metrics like tracking error, falls, energy, foot slip, GRFs, robustness to disturbances, and runtime benchmarks.
- No direct quantitative baselines against GPS variants, RL algorithms (e.g., TRPO), or tuned MPC; unclear if alternatives fail on these tasks.
- Feedback-gain derivation lacks analysis of conditioning, sensitivity to λ, scaling with noise, and stability guarantees.
- CIO details, task costs, and feature list φ are underspecified, limiting reproducibility.
- Data efficiency and computational cost dependence on number of trajectories/segments and CIO success are not explored or quantified.
- Generalization is limitedly assessed—interactive control only varies goals near training distribution; robustness to large changes/terrains/pushes not tested.
- Claim of unifying disparate controllers is aspirational; policies are morphology-specific and don’t transfer across morphologies.
- Requests for clarification: explicit policy architecture (recurrent vs feedforward, r update), full cost-term table for C(X), training noise magnitudes (ε, σγ) and λ effects, per-step runtime, quantitative metrics (success rate, falls, energy, GRFs) with variance, and quantitative comparison to GPS baseline.
- Pros: solid coupling of CIO and policy learning, novel feedback-gain extraction, diverse 3D demos, and practical distributed pipeline.
- Cons: insufficient quantitative evaluation and baselines, ambiguous architecture and execution physics undermining reproducibility, heavy reliance on per-step optimization at test time.
- Recommendation: Borderline reject — promising technical idea and qualitative results but needs clearer architecture, stronger quantitative evaluation, and reduced reliance on soft per-step optimization to justify acceptance.
