**Summary**

This paper presents a method for training neural network control policies for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine an offline, direct trajectory optimization method (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning. The system operates in an alternating fashion: the trajectory optimizer generates optimal state-action trajectories for various tasks, and a neural network is then trained to regress onto these trajectories. Crucially, this is not a one-off process; the trajectories and the network policy are optimized jointly. The authors introduce two key technical contributions to make this work. First, they inject noise into the sensory inputs during training and use optimal feedback gains, derived from the trajectory optimizer, to compute the corresponding "correct" action for the noisy state. This is intended to improve robustness. Second, they propose a method to efficiently extract these feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer, which they argue is more stable than using a standard LQR pass. The authors demonstrate their approach by creating interactive locomotion controllers for a diverse set of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Qualitative Results:** The ability to generate stable, interactive controllers for such a wide variety of 3D character morphologies using a single, unified algorithm is a significant achievement. The resulting behaviors, as described and presumably shown in the video, appear realistic and plausible (e.g., emergent gaits like trotting for quadrupeds). This demonstrates the power and flexibility of the underlying trajectory optimization framework.

2.  **Novel Technical Contributions for Policy Distillation:** The approach of using optimal feedback gains to create training targets for noisy states (`a + a_s ε`) is a principled and well-motivated way to improve policy robustness. This goes beyond simple data augmentation and provides the network with information about the local dynamics around the optimal trajectory. The proposed method for extracting these gains from a direct optimizer like CIO is also a valuable and non-trivial contribution that could be useful in other contexts.

3.  **Strong Ablation Study:** The comparative evaluation in Figure 2 provides convincing evidence for the authors' specific design choices. The stark performance difference between the full method and the ablations ("no joint" optimization and "no noise") clearly demonstrates that both the joint training procedure and the principled noise injection are critical to the method's success.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the lack of comparison to established, alternative methods for continuous control. The introduction explicitly mentions reinforcement learning (RL) approaches ([3, 6, 14]) but provides no quantitative or even qualitative comparison. The authors themselves state that "A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done." For a NIPS paper, this is a major omission. Without comparing to prominent policy gradient methods like TRPO [14] (which is cited), it is impossible to assess whether this complex, multi-stage optimization process offers a tangible advantage over end-to-end RL approaches in terms of sample efficiency, final performance, or robustness. The evaluation is largely internal, showing that the authors' proposed method is better than weaker versions of itself, but not that it advances the state of the art.

2.  **Weak Comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC):** The paper positions itself as an alternative to online optimization methods like MPC. However, the comparison in Section 8.1 is purely qualitative and anecdotal. The claim that MPC trajectories are "not cyclical" and "chaotic" while the learned policy's are "predictable" is not substantiated with any quantitative metrics. This comparison should be rigorized with metrics for trajectory smoothness, periodicity, or energy efficiency to be convincing.

3.  **Framing and Originality:** The method is essentially a sophisticated form of policy distillation or imitation learning, where the "expert" is a trajectory optimizer. While the joint optimization is a nice extension, the core concept of training a network to mimic an expensive planner is not new (e.g., [9, 11]). The paper should be more clearly framed in this context, acknowledging that the network's performance is fundamentally bounded by the capabilities of the CIO planner. The claim of unifying disparate control architectures is also somewhat overstated; the algorithm is applied to different morphologies, but the task is always locomotion to a goal. It is not demonstrated that a single policy can handle fundamentally different tasks (e.g., walking and getting up).

4.  **Complexity and Reproducibility:** The overall system is highly complex, involving a specific physics simulator (MuJoCo), a specific trajectory optimizer (CIO), a custom method for calculating feedback gains, a distributed asynchronous training architecture, and numerous hyperparameters (σε, σγ, α, λ, η). This high degree of complexity raises concerns about the difficulty of reproducing the results and the sensitivity of the method to its many parameters.

**Recommendation**

While the engineering achievement is impressive and the technical idea of using feedback gains for robust policy distillation is sound, the paper's evaluation is not up to NIPS standards. The lack of any comparison to modern reinforcement learning baselines makes it impossible to situate the contribution within the broader field or to verify its claimed superiority. The comparison to MPC is likewise weak and unsubstantiated. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors provided a rigorous comparison against at least one state-of-the-art policy gradient RL algorithm on one of their benchmark characters.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Demonstrates successful control of a diverse set of complex 3D characters, which is a difficult problem.
*   The method for using optimal feedback gains to generate noisy training data is technically interesting and well-motivated.
*   The results appear visually compelling and the internal ablation study is solid.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Major weakness:** Fails to compare against relevant and established baselines from the reinforcement learning literature (e.g., TRPO), despite acknowledging them.
*   The comparison to MPC is qualitative and lacks rigor.
*   Claims of "unifying" control methods are not fully supported, as only locomotion tasks are demonstrated.
*   The overall system is very complex, potentially hindering reproducibility.
