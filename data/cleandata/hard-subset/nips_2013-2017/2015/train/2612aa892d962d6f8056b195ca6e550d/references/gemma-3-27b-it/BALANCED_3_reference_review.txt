This paper presents a novel approach to interactive control of complex characters by combining supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO). The core idea is to leverage the power of CIO to generate training data for a neural network, which can then be used for real-time control. The authors address the challenges of scaling these methods to high-dimensional spaces and achieving stable, realistic locomotion, surpassing previous work that was largely limited to 2D characters. A key contribution is the method for generating optimal feedback gains alongside trajectories, as well as the distributed stochastic optimization scheme for efficient training.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying the limitations of traditional control methods and the challenges of applying deep learning to complex control problems. The problem addressed is significant; automating the creation of controllers for diverse characters with realistic movement is a long-standing goal in robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The approach of using trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network is a sensible one, and the results presented demonstrate a clear improvement over previous methods, achieving stable 3D locomotion for various character types. The technical details are generally well-explained, and the inclusion of ablation studies and comparisons to related work strengthens the claims made.  The distributed training architecture is also a notable contribution given the computational demands of the problem.

However, there are several areas that could be improved. Firstly, while the paper positions itself as surpassing previous work [9, 11], the comparison feels somewhat superficial.  A more detailed quantitative comparison, perhaps on a standardized set of tasks, would be valuable. The authors mention that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors," but lack concrete evidence to support this claim beyond an "impression." Providing metrics (e.g., tracking error, stability margins) would make this comparison more compelling. 

Secondly, the ablation study in Section 8.1, while insightful, is limited. It would be beneficial to investigate the impact of different noise levels (σe, σγ) on performance and robustness.  Further analysis into the effect of network architecture (beyond layer/unit counts) is warranted.  Specifically, exploring different activation functions or recurrent network architectures could reveal valuable insights.

Thirdly, the explanation of the "sensory state" (st) could be more detailed. While the authors state it’s a simple and general-purpose representation, its limitations should be discussed. Are there cases where this representation is insufficient for certain behaviors? How sensitive is the performance to variations in the sensory state?  The reasoning behind including q̇t−1 and f t−1 could be elaborated.

Fourthly, the paper mentions a reliance on MuJoCo for dynamics calculations. While this is a standard simulator, a discussion of the potential limitations and biases introduced by using a specific physics engine would be appropriate.  The transferability of the learned policies to other simulators or real-world systems should be addressed, even if only speculatively.

Finally, the writing, while generally clear, could be more concise in certain sections (e.g., the introduction is a bit lengthy).  More figures illustrating the training process and the resulting behaviors would also improve readability. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning for complex character control is a novel and promising approach.
*   **Significance:**  The ability to generate stable, realistic locomotion in 3D for diverse characters is a significant achievement.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and a well-defined algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach through experiments on a variety of characters.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The comparison to related work could be more rigorous and quantitative.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Study:**  The ablation study is limited in scope and could benefit from more in-depth analysis.
*   **Lack of Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could more thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach and potential areas for future work.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of character control. The authors address a challenging problem with a novel and effective approach. While further experimentation and analysis would strengthen the claims, the current work is a significant step forward. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above.  Specifically, strengthening the comparison to related methods, expanding the ablation studies, and providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations would significantly improve the paper.
