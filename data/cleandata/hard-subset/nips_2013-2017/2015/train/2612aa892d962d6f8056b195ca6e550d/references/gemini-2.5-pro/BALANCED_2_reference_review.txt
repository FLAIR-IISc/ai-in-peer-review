### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a method for automatically synthesizing neural network controllers that can produce stable, realistic, and interactive locomotion for a diverse set of complex 3D physically-simulated characters. The core contribution is a joint optimization framework that combines offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning. The method alternates between two main steps: 1) generating a dataset of optimal trajectories using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), where trajectories must satisfy task objectives and physical realism constraints while also remaining close to the predictions of the current neural network policy; and 2) training the neural network policy to regress onto the state-action pairs from these optimized trajectories.

The authors introduce several key technical improvements over prior work. First, to improve robustness and prevent divergence during policy execution, they inject noise into the sensory inputs during training. Crucially, the network is not trained to simply denoise the input; instead, it learns to produce the optimal corrective action for the perturbed state, which is computed using optimal feedback gains. The paper proposes a novel and efficient method for extracting these well-conditioned feedback gains directly from the CIO process. Second, noise is also added to hidden layer activations to prevent overfitting. Finally, the authors describe a distributed, asynchronous training architecture to make this computationally demanding procedure practical. This approach is demonstrated to successfully generate controllers for a wide variety of morphologies—including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer—without character-specific tuning, producing natural emergent gaits.

This work builds upon prior research that uses trajectory optimization to train neural network policies, such as Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). However, it advances the state of the art by tackling significantly more complex 3D characters and introducing a more tightly coupled joint optimization scheme with principled noise injection, which proves critical for achieving stable control in these challenging domains.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed joint optimization algorithm is well-motivated, and the rationale for each component is clearly explained. The method for extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Sec 3.2) is a clever and valuable contribution in its own right. The claims are well-supported by extensive experimental results on a variety of characters. The ablation study (Fig 2) is particularly effective, convincingly demonstrating that both the joint optimization ("no joint") and the noise injection schemes ("no noise") are essential for the method's success. The comparison against MPC, while qualitative, provides interesting insight into the regularizing effect of learning a single, unified policy.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The high-level overview of the method is clear, and the core optimization problem is stated formally. However, certain aspects could be clarified to improve reproducibility. The trajectory generation process (Sec 5) is described at a high level, but more formal details on how new goals are sampled and how the branching process is structured would be beneficial. Similarly, the exact schedule of the alternating optimization (Algorithm 1)—e.g., how many CIO iterations are performed before each NN update pass—is not specified. The role and update mechanism of the recurrent state `r` is also somewhat underexplained.

**Originality:** The work contains several novel contributions. While the general idea of "guiding" policy learning with trajectory optimization exists, this paper's formulation as a tightly-coupled, stochastic, alternating optimization is novel and appears highly effective. The most original technical piece is arguably the principled handling of sensor noise (Sec 2.1), where the network learns local linear feedback control by targeting the first-order optimal response `a + a_s * ε`. This is a significant improvement over naive denoising approaches. The associated method for efficiently computing the feedback gains `a_s` is also a novel and useful technique.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant and impactful. The ability to automatically generate high-quality, interactive controllers for such a diverse range of complex 3D characters from a single, unified algorithm is a major step forward for character animation, robotics, and computational biomechanics. The quality of the motions shown in the supplementary video is impressive, demonstrating behaviors that are far more stable and natural than what is often seen from contemporary deep reinforcement learning methods. This work provides a strong demonstration of a powerful paradigm that effectively combines the strengths of model-based optimization with the real-time performance and generalization capabilities of neural networks. Researchers and practitioners in these fields are very likely to build upon these ideas.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
1.  **Significant Advance:** The method successfully solves a very difficult problem—generating robust, interactive 3D locomotion controllers for diverse morphologies—which represents a substantial advance over prior art that was largely limited to 2D.
2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces a novel and principled method for robustifying policies via noise injection and learning optimal feedback, along with an efficient technique to compute the necessary gains.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on an impressive variety of characters, and the ablation studies provide strong evidence for the importance of the proposed components. The supplementary video is very compelling.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** The work presents a powerful and generalizable framework that could have a significant impact on fields like animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning.

**Arguments against:**
1.  **Limited Comparison to SOTA RL:** The paper contrasts its approach with direct RL methods only through a qualitative statement. A quantitative comparison on at least one of the tasks with a modern deep RL algorithm (e.g., PPO, SAC) would have made the paper's claims of producing "less chaotic" behavior much stronger.
2.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** Some key details regarding the training procedure and hyperparameter selection (e.g., `λ` for feedback gains) are sparse, which could make reproduction challenging.
3.  **Implicit Assumptions:** The method relies heavily on the availability of a high-quality, differentiable physics simulator and an effective trajectory optimizer (CIO). A more explicit discussion of these dependencies as limitations would be welcome.

### Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is an excellent paper. The following suggestions are intended to further strengthen the manuscript:
*   **Clarify Training Details:** Please provide more specific details on the training loop. For instance: how many iterations of trajectory optimization (step 2) are run per neural network update (step 3)? How are new goals for trajectory segments sampled in Section 5? Providing more concrete details would greatly aid reproducibility.
*   **Elaborate on Recurrent State:** The state includes a recurrent component `r`, but its structure and function are not detailed. Is it the output of a standard recurrent cell like an LSTM/GRU, or simply a vector that is fed back from the network's output to its input? A brief clarification would be helpful.
*   **Strengthen Comparison to RL:** While I understand that a full-scale comparison is costly, even a limited quantitative comparison on one character against a well-tuned deep RL baseline would substantially bolster the paper's claims. Metrics could include success rate, path efficiency, and a quantitative measure of gait regularity. If this is not feasible, the claims about RL-generated behaviors should be toned down and framed more speculatively.
*   **Discuss Limitations:** A short paragraph discussing the limitations would be valuable. Specifically, the strong reliance on an accurate model and a powerful trajectory optimizer is a key aspect of this framework that distinguishes it from model-free RL and is worth highlighting. Discussing how the method might handle model mismatch or transfer to the real world would also be interesting for future work.

Despite these minor points, the paper is a very strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
