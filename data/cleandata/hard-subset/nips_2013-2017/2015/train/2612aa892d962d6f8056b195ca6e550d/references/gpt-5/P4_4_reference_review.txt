Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method to train neural network policies for real-time, interactive control of complex 3D characters (flying, swimming, bipeds, quadrupeds) by combining trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning. The central idea is to: (1) generate optimal trajectories using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with soft dynamics/contact constraints; (2) learn a neural policy that maps a sensory state s = [q, r, q̇t−1, ft−1] to actions a = [q̇, ṙ, f]; and (3) alternate between updating trajectories and the policy in a distributed, stochastic optimization scheme with proximal regularization. To improve robustness and data efficiency, the authors inject Gaussian noise into the policy inputs and use a first-order correction in the action targets via local “feedback gains” computed from the trajectory optimizer. Execution uses a 1-step optimization per frame to reconcile network outputs with dynamics constraints.

This work is closely related to “guided policy search” approaches (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14) and to prior combinations of function approximation with trajectory optimization (Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14). Compared to these, the contributions claimed are: application to diverse 3D characters with contact-rich locomotion; an efficient way to compute local feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer using a soft-constraint perturbation and reusing the Gauss-Newton inverse; and an asynchronous distributed training setup. The paper contrasts with model-free deep RL (e.g., DQN, TRPO) by focusing on supervised learning from optimized trajectories rather than exploration, and with hand-crafted controllers (e.g., SIMBICON, ZMP-based) by offering a unified method across morphologies.

Strengths
- Addresses challenging 3D, contact-rich control tasks and demonstrates diverse behaviors with a single policy architecture and training framework.
- Sound alternating optimization design: decouples trajectory optimization and supervised policy fitting; proximal terms help stabilize asynchronous updates.
- Principled noise-injection scheme: targets a(s+ε) ≈ a + as ε using feedback sensitivities, encouraging closed-loop robustness and improving data efficiency.
- Efficient feedback gain computation derived from the same Gauss-Newton system used in CIO via a soft-constraint perturbation and Woodbury identity reuse.
- Practical distributed implementation with clear reporting of compute setup and training times.
- Ablations indicate that joint optimization and noise are important; architecture sweep offers some empirical guidance.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is primarily qualitative; quantitative metrics (success rates, stability, tracking error, energy/torque, constraint violation) are limited to an ablation figure/table for a single biped task. No comparison to strong baselines (e.g., GPS variants, TRPO, MPC with learned dynamics).
- Physical realism at execution is compromised: dynamics are enforced softly and “may include some root forces,” which can inject non-physical assistance. This limits claims for robotics applicability.
- The execution step (T=1 optimization to match q̇des, ṙdes, fdes) is MPC-like and requires an optimizer at runtime; the real-time budget, failure modes, and robustness under hard contacts are not quantified.
- Some conceptual ambiguity: the paper states the network learns “both optimal controls and a model of dynamics,” but dynamics are not learned; rather, the network outputs desired velocities/forces and a recurrent state, with physics handled by optimization.
- Limited analysis of the feedback-gain parameter λ, noise scales, and proximal weight η; sensitivity and stability analysis would strengthen the case.
- Related work discussion is brief; the relationship to guided policy search (including feedback gain computation and supervised target generation) is not evaluated experimentally.
- Reproducibility details (exact costs/weights, contact parameters, enforcement penalties) are insufficient for exact replication; references lack full bibliographic details.

Quality
- The method is technically reasonable. The feedback gain derivation via a soft-constraint perturbation of the CIO problem and reuse of C−1XX is well-motivated and likely stabilizes targets. The Gauss-Newton approximation is standard in CIO.
- The alternating optimization with proximal regularization is consistent with modern stochastic optimization practice. However, convergence analysis is not provided; empirical convergence is claimed.
- Experimental support for key claims (stability/robustness, generalization across changing goals, performance vs. MPC) is mostly qualitative; quantitative comparisons are sparse.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. The algorithm and training architecture are described concretely. Notation is mostly consistent.
- Areas needing clarification: exact cost terms and weights; how soft constraints are weighted during execution; magnitude and impact of root forces; details of data branching and goal sampling; runtime and latency during interactive control.

Originality
- The overall approach builds on established GPS/CIO ideas. The novel elements are: (i) feedback gain computation directly from the direct optimizer via soft-constraint perturbation; (ii) integration with CIO for diverse 3D locomotion; (iii) asynchronous alternating training with proximal regularization and layered noise. These are incremental but practically meaningful.

Significance
- For graphics/animation, having a unified training pipeline that yields interactive controllers for varied morphologies is valuable. For robotics, the reliance on soft dynamics and possible root forces limits immediate impact. Demonstrated scalability is promising, but broader significance would be stronger with quantitative evaluations and comparisons to leading baselines.

Pros
- Unified framework yielding diverse, realistic-looking 3D behaviors with a single policy architecture.
- Principled robustness via noise with locally optimal feedback targets; efficient feedback gain computation.
- Practical, scalable distributed implementation; training completes in hours.
- Ablation studies supporting core design choices.

Cons
- Limited quantitative evaluation; no strong baseline comparisons (GPS/TRPO/MPC).
- Soft dynamics at execution with potential non-physical forces; unclear applicability to real robots.
- Ambiguity around “learning dynamics”; sparse analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and convergence.
- Reproducibility and bibliographic completeness could be improved.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative metrics (task success, gait stability, energy/torque, constraint violation) across all characters; report runtime/latency.
- Compare against GPS variants and TRPO on at least one biped/quadruped task; include MPC with identical costs as a baseline.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ, σε, σγ, α, η; report robustness to perturbations and model errors.
- Enforce hard dynamics (or tightly penalize root forces) in a subset of experiments to assess physical realism.
- Clarify execution step details and computational budget; specify all cost terms and weights to aid reproducibility; complete bibliographic entries.

Overall, the paper presents a solid, practically useful integration of trajectory optimization and neural policy learning with a careful robustness mechanism and compelling demos. Stronger quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons are needed to support claims of advancing the state of the art.
