**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper presents a method for training neural network controllers for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to leverage a powerful, offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate high-quality motion data, and then distill this "expert" knowledge into a real-time neural network policy. The key contribution is that this is not a one-shot process; instead, the trajectories and the network policy are optimized jointly in an alternating fashion. The trajectories are optimized to satisfy task goals and physics while also being reproducible by the current policy, and the policy is trained on data from these continuously improving trajectories.

This work sits at the intersection of trajectory optimization and imitation learning. It builds directly upon recent work that combines these two areas, such as Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). The authors claim their primary contribution is scaling these ideas to significantly more complex, 3D characters, which has been a major challenge. They achieve this through several technical improvements, most notably a principled method for injecting noise during training. Instead of just adding noise to actions, they add noise to the sensory inputs and use optimal feedback gains—derived from the trajectory optimizer—to compute the corresponding "correct" noisy action. This serves as a sophisticated, dynamics-aware form of data augmentation that enhances policy robustness. The approach is positioned as an alternative to direct reinforcement learning (RL) methods, arguing that it produces more realistic and purposeful motions compared to the often "chaotic" behaviors learned by RL.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impressive Results:** The paper's primary strength lies in its results. Successfully creating stable, interactive controllers for a diverse set of 3D characters (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flier) using a single, unified algorithm is a significant achievement. The emergence of natural, cyclic gaits (walking, trotting, flapping) without explicit programming is a powerful demonstration of the method's effectiveness. This work represents a clear advance in the state-of-the-art for physics-based character animation and has strong potential implications for robotics and biomechanics.

2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The method for robustifying the policy is technically novel and well-motivated. The idea of extracting optimal feedback gains from a *direct* trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is a clever contribution. It allows the network to learn not just a single reference trajectory, but a local control policy around it, which is crucial for stability in a closed-loop setting. This is a more principled approach to generalization than simple noise injection or domain randomization.

3.  **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The ablation studies in Section 8.1 provide strong evidence for the authors' claims. The comparison against training on a static dataset ("no joint") clearly shows the necessity of the alternating optimization scheme. Similarly, the "no noise" experiment demonstrates that the proposed noise injection technique is critical for preventing overfitting and achieving stable rollouts. These experiments effectively isolate and validate the core components of the proposed framework. The qualitative comparison with MPC is also insightful, suggesting that the joint optimization acts as a regularizer, guiding the search towards more structured and less "chaotic" solutions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **Clarity on Policy Execution:** The policy execution step described in Section 7 is a critical and somewhat understated detail. The paper states that evolving the state involves solving a T=1 optimization problem at each timestep to match the network's desired velocities `q̇_des`. This is not a simple forward pass of the network; it's a model-based control step that uses the network's output as a target. This blurs the line between a pure neural network policy and a model-predictive controller. This seems essential for ensuring the physical constraints (Eq. 6) are met at runtime.
    *   How much does this per-step optimization contribute to the final stability of the controller?
    *   What is the computational cost of this step, and does it truly run at "real-time rates" (e.g., >30Hz) on standard hardware? A more detailed discussion of this hybrid execution model would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Comparison to Reinforcement Learning:** The paper's dismissal of direct RL methods as producing "chaotic" behavior is a strong claim that lacks contemporary evidence. While this may have been true for earlier methods, by the time of this work, policy gradient methods like TRPO were demonstrating significant success in learning locomotion. A quantitative comparison on even one of the character models against a modern RL algorithm would make the paper's claims about motion quality and performance far more convincing. Without it, the advantages of this complex trajectory-optimization-based pipeline over a simpler end-to-end RL approach are not fully substantiated.

3.  **Limited Scope of "Interaction":** The training data generation (Section 5) and the notion of interaction are limited to changing the target goal location. It is unclear how the learned policies would generalize to more complex, unexpected perturbations, such as being pushed, tripping over an obstacle, or walking on uneven terrain. While the noise injection provides local robustness, the training distribution does not seem to cover these larger deviations, potentially limiting the controller's applicability in highly dynamic environments.

4.  **Under-explained Details:** The role and update mechanism for the recurrent state `r` are never explained. It is introduced as part of the state and action vectors, but its function within the network and dynamics is opaque. Given the cyclic nature of the learned behaviors, this component seems important, and its omission is a notable gap.

**Overall Evaluation**

This is a high-quality paper with significant contributions. It tackles a very challenging problem—automated synthesis of controllers for complex 3D characters—and presents an effective, scalable solution.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed joint optimization algorithm is well-defined, and the claims are well-supported by strong empirical results, particularly the ablation studies.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, clarity could be improved regarding the real-time execution model and the function of the recurrent state.
*   **Originality:** High. While it builds on prior work combining optimization and learning, the specific formulation, the novel method for deriving and using feedback gains for robustification, and the successful application to a wide range of complex 3D morphologies constitute a highly original and valuable contribution.
*   **Significance:** Very High. This work demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in character control. The ability to generate diverse, realistic behaviors from a unified framework is likely to have a substantial impact on computer graphics, animation, and robotics, and will surely be built upon by future research.

Despite some weaknesses in clarity and the comparison to related fields, the strengths of the paper—its impressive results, technical novelty, and high potential impact—are overwhelming. This is a strong paper that should be accepted.
