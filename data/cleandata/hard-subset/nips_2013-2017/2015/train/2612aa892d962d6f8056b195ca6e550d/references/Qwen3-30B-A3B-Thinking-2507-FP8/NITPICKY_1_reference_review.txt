

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper presents a method for learning neural network policies that enable interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D characters with complex dynamics, using a combination of trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) and neural network regression. The key contribution is a distributed stochastic optimization approach that jointly optimizes trajectories and neural network parameters, with noise injection to improve robustness. The method is demonstrated on a range of characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) with a single network architecture.

## Strengths
The paper makes a compelling case for the need for more generalizable character control methods beyond specialized state machines. The approach of using trajectory optimization to generate training data for neural networks is well-motivated and represents a thoughtful combination of established techniques. The results on diverse 3D characters are impressive, particularly given the claim of avoiding 2D limitations. The ablation studies (comparing to static training and no-noise training) provide useful evidence for the importance of the joint optimization and noise injection components.

## Weaknesses

1. **Mathematical Notation Errors**: Several critical mathematical formulations contain notational inconsistencies that undermine the technical rigor. In Equation (2), the expression "a(s + ε) = a + asε" is ambiguous - it should be "a(s + ε) = a + a_s ε" where a_s is a matrix. In Equation (3), the norm should be squared: "‖(a + asε)− πθ(s + ε)‖^2" rather than "‖(a + asε)− πθ(s + ε)‖2". These are not trivial typographical errors but fundamental notational inconsistencies that could confuse readers.

2. **Algorithm Ambiguity**: Algorithm 1 contains multiple ambiguities. Line 2 states "X̄i = argminX Ci(X) + ∑ tR(s i,t,ai,t, θ̄, ε̄i,t) + η2 ∥∥X− X̄i∥∥2" where "η2" is ambiguous (η squared or η times 2?). The paper should use proper mathematical notation: "η/2" or "η^2" as appropriate. Similarly, the use of "X̄i" for the optimized trajectory and "X̄i" for the previous value is confusing.

3. **Missing Details in Experimental Setup**: The paper claims to use "a single network architecture and parameters to create all controllers" but fails to explain how the same architecture handles such diverse morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without any morphological adaptation. The paper states "3 hidden layer neural networks with 250 hidden units in each layer" but provides no justification for these specific hyperparameters beyond "other network sizes are evaluated in section 8.1" without providing the actual results.

4. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout. For example, "contact forces" is sometimes used interchangeably with "contact forces being applied on the character by the ground" without clarification. The term "CIO" is introduced without a formal definition at first use, which is problematic for readers unfamiliar with the prior work [12].

5. **Reference Format Issues**: Several references are incomplete or improperly formatted (e.g., "Hessian matrix vs. gauss-newton hessian matrix, P. Chen, SIAM J. Numerical Analysis, , , ,"). This is particularly problematic for a venue like NIPS where proper citation is expected.

6. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent mathematical notation throughout, including "10 −2" instead of "10^-2" and inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "q̇t , qt+1 − qt" has inconsistent spacing). These are minor but indicate a lack of attention to detail.

7. **Methodological Ambiguity**: The paper states "we disabled the neural network and generated optimal trajectories as according to 5" for the "no joint" ablation, but section 5 is titled "Training Trajectory Generation" rather than "Trajectory Optimization" as would be expected. This creates confusion about which method was used for the baseline comparison.

8. **Results Presentation**: The claim that "our full method performs two orders of magnitude better than static training" lacks concrete metrics. The paper provides no actual numbers or standard deviations to support this claim, making the comparison difficult to evaluate.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents an interesting combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training with promising results on diverse character control, the technical presentation contains multiple significant notational and methodological inconsistencies that undermine the paper's credibility. The mathematical formulations contain errors that would confuse readers, and the paper lacks sufficient detail about key implementation choices. These issues are not merely cosmetic but affect the technical soundness of the work.

The paper's contributions are not sufficiently novel compared to prior work [9, 11, 12], as the authors themselves acknowledge. The paper's main innovation appears to be the combination of techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach, and the claims about "substantially harder problems" and "stable and realistic locomotion in three-dimensional space" are not sufficiently supported by comparative results.

## Recommendation
The paper contains promising ideas but is undermined by numerous technical and presentation flaws that prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for a well-written, technically sound contribution. The mathematical errors and inconsistent notation are particularly concerning for a venue that values precision. The lack of sufficient detail about key implementation choices also makes it difficult to evaluate the method's true contribution.

Given the paper's technical shortcomings and the fact that the core approach is a combination of existing techniques without a compelling novelty argument, I recommend rejection. The authors would benefit from addressing these technical and presentation issues before resubmitting to a venue that values precision in mathematical formulation and presentation.
