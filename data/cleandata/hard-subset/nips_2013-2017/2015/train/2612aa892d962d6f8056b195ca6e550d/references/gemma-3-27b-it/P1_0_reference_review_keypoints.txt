- Core idea: combine contact-invariant trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised learning to train neural network controllers for physically simulated characters.
- Training data: CIO generates optimal trajectories which are used to supervise neural network control policies.
- Robustness trick: inject noise into sensory inputs using optimal feedback gains from trajectory optimization to improve closed-loop stability and generalization.
- Scalability: use a distributed optimization/training architecture to handle the computational load and scale to complex tasks.
- Key result: successfully produces stable, realistic 3D locomotion for diverse characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal).
- Unified approach: same network architecture applied across different morphologies, aiming to unify control methods for varied characters.
- Ablation study: thorough comparisons show the proposed components (noise, CIO, distributed scheme) materially improve performance versus naive baselines.
- Writing: paper is clearly written and well organized, making the complex methodology accessible.
- Limited comparative evaluation: lacks systematic quantitative comparisons to direct reinforcement learning methods (e.g., DDPG, PPO).
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: discussion of sensitivity to algorithmic constants (noise levels, penalty weights) is missing.
- Computational cost: trajectory optimization is computationally expensive and may limit practical applicability despite distributed mitigation.
- Simulation-only results: experiments are confined to simulation, leaving real-world transfer to robots or characters unaddressed.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a hard problem in 3D character control, offers a novel effective method, and provides strong ablation evidence.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited baselines/comparisons, unclear hyperparameter robustness, high computational demands, and uncertain real-world generalization.
- Recommendation: overall strong paper; accept with minor revisions to add comparative evaluations, hyperparameter discussion, and elaboration on computational costs and mitigation.
