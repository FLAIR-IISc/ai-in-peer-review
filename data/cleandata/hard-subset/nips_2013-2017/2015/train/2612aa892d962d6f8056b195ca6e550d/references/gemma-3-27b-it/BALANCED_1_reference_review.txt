This paper presents a compelling approach to learning control policies for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) with neural network regression. The core idea – leveraging the precision of trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network that can then execute the policy in real-time – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in control: scaling to high-dimensional, continuous spaces. The authors demonstrate impressive results on a variety of character morphologies, including swimming, flying, and legged locomotion, showcasing the generality of their method.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is built upon solid foundations in trajectory optimization and neural networks. The integration of CIO with neural network learning is logically sound, and the inclusion of noise injection and distributed optimization addresses key practical challenges. The ablation studies (section 8.1) provide good evidence that each component contributes to the overall performance.
* **Originality:** While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and application to complex 3D character control *is* a significant contribution.  Previous work (cited as [9, 11]) has explored similar ideas, but this work convincingly demonstrates scaling to substantially more challenging problems. The application of the perturbation method for calculating optimal feedback gains (section 3.2) is a clever and efficient solution.
* **Significance:**  The ability to learn generalizable control policies for diverse characters has broad implications for robotics, animation, and biomechanics.  The demonstration of stable and realistic locomotion in 3D is a substantial advance.  The potential to unify disparate control architectures (as mentioned in the introduction) is also a significant long-term goal. The method’s ability to avoid hand-crafted controllers, particularly for complex morphologies, is valuable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, approach, and results. The algorithmic description is concise and understandable.  The inclusion of a supplementary video is crucial for appreciating the quality of the learned behaviors.
* **Thoroughness of Experimentation:** The experiments cover a diverse range of characters and the ablation studies help understand the impact of each component of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Direct Methods:** The authors acknowledge the existence of “direct methods” (e.g., reinforcement learning) for training neural network controllers, but only offer a qualitative comparison ("somewhat chaotic behaviors"). A more systematic quantitative comparison would strengthen the paper. It would be helpful to see a head-to-head comparison on the same tasks, using comparable resources.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention specific values for the algorithmic constants, the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters isn’t fully explored.  A brief discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the high computational cost, emphasizing that more resources are spent generating data than learning. While this is honest, it’s a significant practical limitation.  Exploring methods to reduce the data generation cost (e.g., more efficient trajectory optimization) would be valuable.  The authors mention a linear scaling with cluster size, but the absolute cost remains high.
* **Recurrent State Representation:** The choice of recurrent state 'r' is rather opaque. The paper states it's "recurrent memory," but doesn't detail its purpose or how it's used within the neural network architecture. Further clarification would be helpful. It also isn’t clear why a recurrent state is necessary, as opposed to relying solely on past observations.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily highlights successes. Discussing scenarios where the method *fails* or struggles – and potential reasons for those failures – would strengthen the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a significant advance in scaling neural network control to complex, high-dimensional problems.
* Combines existing techniques in a novel and effective way.
* Achieves impressive results on a variety of challenging tasks.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Includes thorough experimentation and ablation studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Lacks a comprehensive quantitative comparison to alternative methods (e.g., direct reinforcement learning).
* Doesn't fully explore hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Remains computationally expensive.
* Some aspects of the implementation (recurrent state) lack detailed explanation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective approach. The results are compelling, and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges and limitations of their work. While the lack of a more thorough comparison to other methods is a weakness, the overall quality of the work and its potential impact justify acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above, particularly a more detailed comparison to direct reinforcement learning approaches and a clarification of the recurrent state representation.  The authors should also briefly discuss potential avenues for reducing the computational cost.
