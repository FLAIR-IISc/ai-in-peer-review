Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an alternating, distributed framework that learns interactive, real-time controllers for complex, 3D physically simulated characters by combining direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural policy learning. The key idea is to use the optimizer to generate trajectories and local feedback (gains), and train a neural network to imitate a robust, noise-perturbed policy, while jointly re-optimizing trajectories to stay consistent with the evolving policy. The method injects Gaussian noise in sensory inputs and hidden activations, and uses a first-order correction a + As ε where As are optimal feedback gains computed efficiently from the CIO Gauss-Newton system via a soft-constraint perturbation method. Training is distributed across many CPU nodes for trajectory optimization with a single GPU for NN regression, with proximal terms to stabilize asynchronous updates. At execution time, the learned policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces, and a short-horizon (T=1) inverse-dynamics-like optimization step updates the state, enabling stable real-time control at 50 ms steps.

This builds on lines of work in guided policy search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014), and contrasts with model-free RL in continuous control (e.g., TRPO). The novelty emphasized here includes: (i) robust 3D locomotion across diverse morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, bird, swimmer) learned from scratch without hand-designed controllers; (ii) principled noise-injection with first-order targets using feedback gains derived from direct trajectory optimization; (iii) a practical asynchronous, proximal-regularized alternating scheme; and (iv) a simple branching data-generation procedure to cover changing goals for interactive control.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall algorithmic design is sensible and well-motivated. The perturbation-based derivation of feedback gains that reuse the Gauss-Newton inverse via Woodbury is a neat and practically important contribution for direct methods (CIO). The alternating procedure with proximal terms is reasonable for stabilizing asynchronous updates, though no convergence guarantees are provided.
- Empirical support: The paper demonstrates diverse 3D behaviors and reports ablations: (a) static dataset vs joint alternation and (b) noise vs no-noise, showing large differences in test performance and stability. Network size sweeps are provided. However, quantitative evaluation is limited: the main claims (stability, realism, interactive robustness) rely heavily on qualitative videos and a few plots not fully described. There is no rigorous comparison to strong baselines (e.g., MPC with comparable horizons/costs, GPS/TRPO/DDPG contemporaries), no metrics on success rates, energy, gait stability, contact constraint satisfaction, or robustness to perturbations. The MPC comparison is qualitative and suggests different gait regularity but lacks quantitative analysis.
- Completeness: Some important details are missing or under-specified for reproduction: exact cost terms and weights (beyond a list and a few constants) for each morphology, how recurrent memory r is used (the network is not a recurrent NN; r is part of state and updated by the policy—an ablation on r would help), how contact force outputs are regularized to be physically feasible at execution (since forces are also optimized in the T=1 step), and the distribution of goal changes/branching. The reliance on soft dynamics constraints (allowing root forces) may undermine claims of physical realism; measuring penetration violations/root-force magnitudes would clarify realism.
- Scalability/compute: The distributed architecture and reported training time (~2.5 hours with ~30 nodes) is pragmatic and convincing.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, with a good high-level narrative. Algorithm 1 and the derivation of feedback gains are presented clearly. Notational consistency could be improved (x vs q vs X; action/velocity/forces), and the role/benefit of the memory state r deserves clearer exposition. Some referenced figures/tables are essential to trust the results; ensure they convey metrics, axes, and units.

Originality
Moderate to good. The overall paradigm (learn policies from trajectory optimization with noise) is known, but:
- The feedback-gain computation for direct methods is novel and practically valuable.
- The specific noise-injected training with first-order corrected targets is a principled twist beyond standard denoising/data augmentation.
- Demonstrating robust 3D locomotion across disparate morphologies with a single architecture and no mocap/state machines is a meaningful step forward in this line of work.
- The asynchronous proximal alternating scheme is a practical systems contribution.

Significance
High potential impact for physics-based animation and model-based RL/control. If substantiated, the approach offers a general recipe to get realistic, interactive controllers without hand-crafted architectures or mocap, and could transfer to robotics when dynamics are accurate. The feedback-gain derivation could be reused by others using direct trajectory optimization.

Strengths
- Elegant integration of CIO and neural policy learning with a principled treatment of noise and local feedback.
- Practical distributed implementation with reasonable wall-clock time.
- Diverse 3D results across morphologies without hand-engineering; emergent gaits.
- Useful systems/detail choices (proximal regularization, GPU training, branching data generation).
- Clear articulation of the training/execution pipeline.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and weak baselines; heavy reliance on qualitative video.
- No robustness/stability metrics, no comparisons to GPS/TRPO-like methods or strong MPC baselines with matched compute.
- Use of soft dynamics constraints and potential root forces; unclear physical fidelity at test time.
- Insufficient analysis of key design choices (effect of λ for gain softness, α, η, noise scales; ablation on memory r; sensitivity to dataset size).
- No convergence analysis or empirical study of the alternating scheme’s stability beyond a single plot.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative metrics: success rate to random goals, energy/torque cost, gait frequency/regularity, constraint violations, disturbance recovery, and latency.
- Compare against strong baselines: (i) MPC with equivalent horizon and compute, (ii) GPS/TRPO (to the extent feasible), and (iii) motion-primitive/state-machine baselines for bipeds/quadrupeds.
- Report robustness to external perturbations and sensor noise levels not seen in training.
- Provide ablations on r (no memory vs with memory), λ (feedback softness), α/η (penalties), and action/state representations.
- Clarify execution-time force feasibility and quantify root forces/penetrations; if possible, harden constraints or report the trade-offs.
- Expand details for reproducibility: cost terms/weights per task, model files, and training data distributions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, practical, and likely influential integration of trajectory optimization with neural policies.
- Novel and useful method to obtain feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Demonstrates diverse, realistic 3D behaviors with a single framework; promising for both graphics and control communities.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evidence and strong baselines to substantiate superiority over alternatives.
- Ambiguity about physical fidelity due to soft constraints/root forces.
- Some methodological details (memory, constraints, evaluation) need clarification to assess generality and reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline to weak accept. The methodological contributions and demonstrated scope are compelling, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by more thorough quantitative evaluation and clearer analysis of design choices and physical realism.
