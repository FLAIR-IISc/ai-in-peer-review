Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework for learning interactive, real-time control policies for complex 3D characters by alternating between trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised neural policy learning. Key ideas: (1) generate optimal trajectories for many initial conditions and goals; (2) inject sensory noise during data generation and label the corresponding optimal corrective actions using local feedback gains; (3) learn a neural policy that maps noisy sensory states to actions, including desired joint velocities, contact forces, and a recurrent memory rate; (4) at test time, execute the policy in real time using a 1-step inverse dynamics optimization (softly enforcing dynamics and contact constraints) to achieve the desired action. A technical contribution is a perturbation-based method to compute local feedback gains “for free” from CIO, by reusing the Gauss-Newton structure, rather than doing a separate LQG pass. The training is implemented asynchronously and distributed, with proximal regularization to stabilize alternating optimization. Demonstrations include 3D swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion without hand-designed gaits or motion capture. Ablations show that joint optimization and noise are important; an MPC comparison suggests the learned policies regularize to predictable cyclical behaviors.

This is closely related to “guided policy search” and prior work combining trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14). The main differences here are: use of CIO for rich contact dynamics; a derivation to obtain feedback gains directly from the direct trajectory optimizer; and demonstrations on diverse 3D characters. The paper also positions itself relative to model-free RL (TRPO, DDPG, Atari DQN) and classical animation control architectures.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The alternating optimization is reasonable and widely used in guided policy search variants. The CIO backbone, Gauss-Newton updates, and proximal terms are standard and appropriate. The perturbation-based computation of feedback gains that reuses CXX−1 is a neat and practically valuable idea; however, the derivation could use clearer presentation (dimensions, Woodbury application) and a discussion of conditioning and choice of λ. The execution step (T=1 optimization) is elegant and plausibly stable, but the use of soft dynamics/contact constraints and allowance of root forces reduces physical fidelity; this is acceptable for animation but limits claims for robotics.
- Empirical support: The qualitative scope (multiple 3D morphologies) is compelling. The ablations (joint vs static, with/without noise) support the design choices. However, quantitative evaluation is limited in the text: there are references to figures/tables, but no numbers here on success rates, energy, speeds, robustness, or real-time performance. Baseline comparisons to model-free RL or to other guided policy search methods are absent; the MPC comparison is qualitative and task-specific. Claims of two orders of magnitude improvement need concrete reported metrics. Convergence of the asynchronous alternating scheme is asserted but not characterized (e.g., objective curves, variance).
- Completeness: Important implementation specifics are missing or underspecified: the dimension and role of the recurrent state r; exact observation features; action scaling; run-time frame rates; character/contact parameters; and how contact forces output by the policy interact with the optimizer when contacts change. Reproducibility would benefit from more details.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well-organized, with a clear high-level flow (objective, alternating optimization, feedback gains, training architecture, execution). The motivation for noise injection and the linearization of labels using local gains is well explained. Some notational choices (e.g., mixing velocities at t and t−1, the role of r and ṙ) could be clarified. The derivation in Section 3.2 would benefit from a more explicit derivation and dimensional annotations. The execution-time optimization and the implications of soft enforcement (e.g., root forces) should be stated upfront in the abstract or limitations.

Originality
- The overall approach falls within the guided policy search family, but the paper contributes: (1) a practical and efficient method to obtain stabilizing feedback gains directly from CIO; (2) a coherent noise-injection scheme where labels are corrected via local gains; (3) a simple asynchronous, proximal regularized alternation that scales; and (4) 3D, contact-rich demonstrations across morphologies without specialized controllers. These are incremental but meaningful innovations. Related work is cited appropriately, though a sharper differentiation from GPS variants and a discussion of tradeoffs vs LQG-based labelers would strengthen the originality case.

Significance
- For graphics/animation and model-based control communities, the ability to synthesize diverse, realistic, interactive 3D behaviors with a single pipeline and no mocap is impactful. The feedback-gain computation is of independent practical interest. However, the absence of strong quantitative comparisons and the reliance on soft dynamics/root forces limit claims for robotics. If accompanied by more rigorous metrics and baselines, this could influence practice in learning controllers from trajectory optimization.

Pros
- Clear articulation of an end-to-end pipeline; practical and scalable.
- Clever reuse of CIO computations to obtain stabilizing gains.
- Demonstrations on varied 3D morphologies; emergent cyclic gaits without handcrafting.
- Ablations that justify joint optimization and noise-injection.

Cons
- Limited quantitative evaluation; no strong baselines (e.g., TRPO/DDPG/GPS).
- Derivation and analysis of the feedback gains need more rigor and discussion of conditioning/λ.
- Execution relies on soft dynamics with root forces; relevance to robotics is limited.
- Recurrent state and policy architecture details insufficient for reproduction.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide quantitative metrics: success rate, distance/orientation error, energy, speed, robustness to perturbations, wall-clock throughput at test time.
- Compare against a standard GPS variant and a model-free baseline on at least one task.
- Clarify r: dimension, role, and whether the network is strictly feedforward with r as part of state.
- Expand the derivation in Section 3.2 with dimensions and numerical conditioning analysis; study sensitivity to λ.
- Report how contact forces predicted by the policy are handled when contacts are absent; do you regularize to zero or constrain via complementarity?
- Discuss and, if possible, reduce root forces during execution, or quantify their magnitude.
- Release implementation details to improve reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept for NeurIPS as an applied ML/control paper). The paper proposes a solid, well-motivated combination of trajectory optimization and neural policy learning with a practical contribution (feedback gains from CIO) and convincing qualitative breadth. Stronger quantitative evaluation and clearer derivations would significantly strengthen the case.
