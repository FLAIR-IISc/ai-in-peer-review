

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents an innovative approach to training neural network controllers for interactive control of diverse complex characters in 3D environments. The authors address a significant limitation in previous work (which was restricted to 2D) by extending trajectory-optimization-based neural network control to 3D, enabling stable locomotion for a wide variety of character morphologies including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds using a single network architecture.

The core contribution is a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly optimizes trajectories and neural network parameters. The authors combine Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) for offline trajectory generation with a neural network policy that learns from these trajectories. Their key innovation lies in injecting noise in a principled way to learn optimal feedback gains, enabling robust behavior despite the closed-loop nature of the control problem. This approach overcomes the limitations of previous methods that either produced chaotic behaviors (direct training) or were restricted to 2D environments.

The paper demonstrates impressive results across multiple character types, showing that the same network architecture can successfully control vastly different morphologies without specialized initialization. The swimming and flying characters exhibit realistic cyclical motion patterns that emerge naturally from the optimization process, while bipedal and quadrupedal characters learn stable gaits without hand-crafted constraints. The authors provide strong empirical evidence for the necessity of their joint optimization approach through ablation studies showing significant performance degradation when using naive training or disabling noise injection.

The work connects to previous NIPS/ICML research on neural network control (e.g., [9, 11]) but makes a clear step forward by extending to 3D and demonstrating successful control of diverse character morphologies. It also relates to broader themes in reinforcement learning and trajectory optimization, particularly the use of CIO [12] and the connection to MPC.

**Strengths:**
- **Novelty:** The extension from 2D to 3D control with a single network architecture for diverse morphologies is a significant conceptual advance.
- **Significance:** The approach addresses a fundamental challenge in character animation and robotics: creating controllers that generate stable, realistic movements across diverse character types.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the technical challenges and their solutions.
- **Experimental rigor:** The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the necessity of their key contributions (joint optimization, noise injection).
- **Breadth of results:** The diverse character examples (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) convincingly demonstrate the method's generality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical foundation for the feedback gains calculation could be strengthened with more formal analysis.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly compare with the most recent state-of-the-art methods beyond the ablation studies.
- The computational requirements (cloud computing, GPU implementation) might limit accessibility for some researchers.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with careful experimental design and thorough ablation studies. The authors are appropriately honest about the limitations of their approach, noting the computational demands and the need for trajectory optimization to generate training data.

**Originality:** This work represents a significant extension of previous trajectory-optimization-based control methods to 3D environments with diverse character morphologies. The joint optimization framework with principled noise injection for learning feedback gains is novel and well-motivated.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a challenging problem with a practical solution that could impact character animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The ability to control diverse character types with a single network architecture represents a substantial step toward universal control policies, which is a compelling direction for the field.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The technical explanations are accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a bold, novel approach that significantly advances the state of the art in neural network control for complex character animation. While the methodological details could be strengthened in a few areas, the core contribution—the extension to 3D control of diverse character morphologies using a unified network architecture—is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance. The authors have clearly articulated the challenges in the field and demonstrated a compelling solution that overcomes the limitations of previous work.

The paper's most impressive achievement is demonstrating that a single network can control vastly different character types (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) with stable, realistic locomotion in 3D. This represents a meaningful step toward the goal of universal character control policies, which is a compelling direction for the field.

I believe this work makes a substantial contribution to the field and will be of interest to many NIPS readers. The novelty of the approach, combined with the convincing empirical results across diverse character types, outweighs the minor methodological weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
