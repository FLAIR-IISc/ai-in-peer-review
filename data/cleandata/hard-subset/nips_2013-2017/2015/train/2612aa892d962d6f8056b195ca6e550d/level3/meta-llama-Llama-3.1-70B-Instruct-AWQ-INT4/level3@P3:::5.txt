The paper "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" presents a novel approach for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems and tasks. The authors propose a method that combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate stable and realistic behaviors for characters with different body morphologies.

The main idea of the paper is to use a neural network to learn from the trajectory optimizer and eventually generate similar behaviors online. The authors introduce a neural network control policy that maps a sensory state of the character to an optimal action, which directly commands the desired velocity of the character and applied contact forces. The policy is trained using a distributed stochastic optimization algorithm that alternates between trajectory optimization and neural network regression.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by applying it to various tasks, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking. The results show that the method can learn successful controllers for these tasks, even in the presence of noise and perturbations.

However, the paper has some limitations. Firstly, the authors assume that full model knowledge is required, which may not be the case in many real-world applications. This assumption is stated in Equation 6, but it is not explicitly discussed in the paper.

Secondly, the paper has some issues with the organization and length of certain sections. Specifically, Sections 4 and 6 are disproportionate in coverage and length. Section 4, which discusses the neural network policy regression, is relatively short and lacks details on the implementation of the neural network. On the other hand, Section 6, which describes the distributed training architecture, is longer and provides more details on the implementation.

Thirdly, the paper fails to mention the recurrent units referenced in the abstract. The abstract states that the controller is a neural network with a small number of recurrent units that implement memory states beyond the physical system state. However, this is not discussed in the paper, and it is not clear how the recurrent units are implemented.

Lastly, the paper could benefit from some clarifications on certain points. For example, in Line 405, the authors describe the network as "stochastic" despite the network itself appearing deterministic. It would be helpful to clarify why the network is described as stochastic and how this relates to the stochastic training data.

Despite these limitations, the paper presents a promising approach for interactive control of complex characters. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, and the authors provide a detailed description of the algorithm and its implementation. However, the paper could benefit from some reorganization and clarification to make it more readable and understandable.

In terms of the review criteria, the paper scores well on originality, as it presents a novel approach for combining supervised learning with trajectory optimization. The paper also scores well on significance, as it demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on various tasks and provides a detailed description of the algorithm and its implementation. However, the paper could improve on clarity, as some sections are disproportionate in coverage and length, and some points could benefit from clarification.
