

This paper presents a compelling method for training neural network controllers that achieve stable, realistic locomotion across diverse 3D characters (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) without motion capture or task-specific handcrafting. It addresses a longstanding challenge in control: bridging the gap between offline trajectory optimization (which produces high-quality but non-real-time behaviors) and online policy learning. The work builds on prior NIPS contributions like Levine & Koltun (ICML 2014) and Mordatch et al. (TOG 2012), but significantly extends them by enabling 3D locomotion and achieving interactive control through a novel integration of trajectory optimization and neural networks. The key innovation lies in *embedding optimal feedback gains* (computed during trajectory optimization) into the neural policy via a principled noise injection scheme (Section 2.1), rather than treating the network as a pure policy approximator. This directly addresses the critical issue of closed-loop stability in neural controllers, where errors can amplify over time.

The paper excels in technical quality (Criterion 1). The constrained optimization problem (Equation 1) is elegantly relaxed via a quadratic penalty (Equation 3), enabling a distributed alternating optimization approach (Algorithm 1) that cleanly separates trajectory optimization (Section 3) and neural regression (Section 4). Crucially, the authors demonstrate that their method *requires* the joint optimization and noise injection: ablations show static trajectory training (no joint optimization) fails catastrophically (Fig. 2), and disabling noise leads to severe overfitting (no noise case). The feedback gain computation (Section 3.2) is a particularly elegant technical contribution, reusing trajectory optimization derivatives to compute stable gains without costly LQG passes. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) provide strong empirical validation of each component.

Clarity (Criterion 2) is exceptional. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with precise definitions (e.g., sensory state `s_t`, action `a_t`), clear algorithm descriptions, and compelling visual results (Fig. 1, supplementary video). The connection between the abstract method and concrete implementations (e.g., MuJoCo physics, EC2/GPU training architecture) is seamless, making the approach both theoretically sound and practically feasible. The reader can easily grasp how the components interact and why each design choice matters.

Originality (Criterion 3) is significant. While combining trajectory optimization with neural networks has been attempted (Levine & Koltun, Mordatch & Todorov), this work is the first to achieve *stable 3D locomotion* for *diverse morphologies* using a *single architecture* without task-specific features. The core novelty—embedding feedback gains into the policy via noise-injected regression—represents a fresh perspective on neural control, moving beyond simple imitation learning. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work (e.g., [9, 11] only achieved 2D locomotion) and adequately cites related control and graphics literature (e.g., [13, 21] on state machines).

Significance (Criterion 4) is high. The method unifies disparate control approaches (bipedal state machines, quadruped contact schedules, flying cyclic encodings) under a single neural framework. The demonstrated behaviors (e.g., cyclical fin sweeping for swimming, flapping modulation for flight, trot gaits for quadrupeds) are realistic and task-appropriate, without manual intervention. Critically, the policy learns *simpler, more robust approximations* of optimal trajectories than MPC alone (Fig. 3), which is a major practical advantage. The work opens doors for interactive character animation, biomechanical modeling, and robotics—applications where real-time, stable, and diverse control is paramount. The authors’ future work directions (e.g., muscle actuation, sensor delays) further highlight its broader impact.

Weaknesses are minor. The paper acknowledges a need for systematic comparison with direct RL methods (e.g., Mnih et al., 2013), though the authors’ intuition about chaotic behaviors from direct methods is plausible. Training time (hours on cloud) is non-trivial, but the paper justifies this by noting the heavy lifting is done by the trajectory optimizer (not the network), and the resulting policy enables real-time control. The parallelization architecture (Section 6) is technically sound but primarily an implementation detail; the core methodological contribution stands independently.

Overall, this is a high-quality, technically impressive, and significant contribution. It delivers on its ambitious goal: a universal synthesis method for interactive control across diverse dynamical systems. The paper’s clarity, rigorous validation, and clear differentiation from prior work make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The demonstrated results across such varied character morphologies with a single architecture represent a substantial methodological advance in the intersection of neural networks, control, and graphics. This work deserves a place at NIPS as a significant step towards practical, general-purpose neural control.
