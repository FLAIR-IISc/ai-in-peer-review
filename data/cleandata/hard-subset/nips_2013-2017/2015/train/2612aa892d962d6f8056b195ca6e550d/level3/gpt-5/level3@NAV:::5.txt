This paper proposes training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers by alternating trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised policy regression. Up front, the approach assumes full model knowledge and differentiable contact dynamics (Eq. 6), placing it firmly in model-based control rather than model-free RL. The method interleaves CIO-generated trajectories and optimal local feedback gains with noisy policy learning, and executes in real time via a 1-step optimization that enforces dynamics. Results span swimming, flying, and both bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion in 3D. The work connects to trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov), MPC, denoising and dropout regularization (Vincent et al.; Wager et al.), and contrasts with model-free methods such as TRPO.

Quality: The alternating optimization and efficient gain computation (Sec. 3.2) are technically sound and well-motivated; ablations (Sec. 8.1) substantiate the roles of joint optimization and noise. However, the reliance on full, accurate dynamics (Eq. 6) and soft enforcement allowing root forces limit physical fidelity and transfer. Baselines against modern policy-gradient methods (e.g., TRPO) and MPC are suggestive but not comprehensive.

Clarity: The manuscript is generally readable but uneven. Sections 4 (policy regression) and 6 (distributed training) are disproportionate; Sec. 4 is central yet brief, while Sec. 6 is operationally detailed. Critically, Sec. 4 fails to explain the “small number of recurrent units” advertised in the abstract: the architecture described appears feed-forward with r treated as I/O, not a recurrent parameterization. Please clarify whether true recurrent connections (and their training) are present. Line 405: clarify why the network is called “stochastic”—the policy at test time appears deterministic; the stochasticity arises from training-time input/activation noise and stochastic data perturbations.

Originality: The integration of CIO with supervised policy learning and the gain-perturbation method is a thoughtful synthesis that advances beyond prior 2D or simpler settings. While components are known, the combination and the breadth of behaviors in 3D are novel.

Significance: If made clearer and better positioned against strong baselines, this could influence interactive control in graphics and robotics where accurate simulators exist. Transfer to hardware will require addressing model mismatch.

Pros:
- Unified controller across diverse morphologies with convincing demos
- Clever reuse of CIO computations for feedback gains
- Robustness via principled noise injection and joint optimization

Cons:
- Requires full, differentiable dynamics; limits applicability
- Clarity issues around recurrence and “stochastic” policy
- Disproportionate section coverage; limited baseline comparisons

Overall: Promising results, but the paper should be restructured and clarifications added. I lean toward weak accept contingent on revisions.
