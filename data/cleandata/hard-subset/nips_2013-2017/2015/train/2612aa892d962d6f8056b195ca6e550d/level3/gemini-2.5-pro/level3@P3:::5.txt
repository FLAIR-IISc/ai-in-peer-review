This paper presents a method for training neural network feedback controllers for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with the real-time performance of neural networks. The proposed algorithm alternates between two main steps: 1) using a trajectory optimizer (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) to generate optimal state-action trajectories for various tasks (e.g., locomotion to a target), and 2) using supervised learning to train a neural network to reproduce the state-action mappings from these trajectories. Key contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, a technique to extract optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer to guide the learning process on perturbed states, and a distributed architecture for training. The method is successfully applied to a diverse set of 3D characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, generating stable and visually realistic gaits without task-specific engineering.

This work builds upon a recent trend of combining trajectory optimization with function approximation for control, similar to work by Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). However, it advances the state of the art by successfully tackling more complex, unstable, and diverse 3D locomotion problems than previously demonstrated with this class of methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Impressive Results:** The primary strength of the paper is the quality and diversity of the results. The ability to generate stable, interactive controllers for swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion from a single, unified framework is a significant achievement. The emergent behaviors, such as cyclical gaits and trotting, appear natural and are not explicitly programmed, highlighting the power of the optimization-based approach. The supplementary video effectively showcases these impressive results.
*   **Novel Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces several valuable technical ideas. The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is clever and useful, providing a way to generate richer training data for the neural network at little extra computational cost. The joint optimization framework, which interleaves trajectory optimization with network training, is shown in the ablation study (Figure 2) to be critical for good performance compared to training on a static dataset.
*   **Generality:** The framework is general and does not rely on character-specific state machines or features. This makes it applicable to a wide range of morphologies and tasks, as demonstrated by the experiments, which is a key goal in automated controller synthesis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity on Model Requirements:** A major weakness is that the paper does not explicitly state a critical prerequisite upfront: the method is model-based and requires full knowledge of the system dynamics. The trajectory optimization component relies on the equations of motion, contact models, friction cones, etc., as detailed in Equation (6). This is a strong assumption that distinguishes this work from model-free reinforcement learning and limits its direct applicability to real-world robotic systems where accurate models are often unavailable. This should be clearly articulated in the introduction to properly frame the paper's contributions and limitations.
*   **Paper Organization and Balance:** The manuscript's organization could be significantly improved. Section 6, detailing the distributed training architecture, is overly descriptive for a main paper, dedicating significant space to implementation details like AWS instance types and NFS servers. This information, while useful for reproducibility, would be better placed in an appendix. In stark contrast, Section 4 ("Neural Network Policy Regression") is exceptionally brief and lacks crucial details. This imbalance detracts from the conceptual clarity of the work.
*   **Inconsistency Regarding Recurrent Units:** There is a critical inconsistency between the abstract/overview and the technical description of the neural network. The abstract explicitly states the controller has "a small number of recurrent units that implement memory states". The state `r` and its derivative `r_dot` are introduced in Section 2 as part of the system state and policy output. However, Section 4 describes the network as a standard 3-layer feed-forward network and makes no mention of how recurrence is implemented. This is a confusing omission that makes it impossible to understand how the memory state is handled. Are the recurrent states simply passed through as part of the input/output vectors of a feed-forward net, or is there a genuine recurrent architecture (e.g., LSTM, GRU) that was not described? This needs to be clarified.
*   **Misleading Terminology:** On line 405 (and elsewhere), the authors refer to their model as a "stochastic neural network". This term is potentially misleading. The network architecture itself appears deterministic. The training process is made stochastic by injecting noise into the inputs and hidden activations, which acts as a form of regularization similar to dropout. However, at execution time (Section 7), the policy is queried "without noise," suggesting a deterministic controller. The authors should clarify that the network is deterministic but trained with stochasticity, to avoid confusion with policies that are inherently stochastic (i.e., output a probability distribution over actions).

**Recommendation:**

This paper demonstrates promising results on a challenging set of control problems and contains novel technical ideas. However, it is held back by significant issues with clarity, organization, and a critical inconsistency regarding the network architecture. The work shows strong potential but requires a major revision to be acceptable. The authors should restructure the paper to improve the balance between sections, clarify the model-based assumptions upfront, and, most importantly, resolve the contradiction regarding the use of recurrent units. With these revisions, this could be a strong contribution to the conference.
