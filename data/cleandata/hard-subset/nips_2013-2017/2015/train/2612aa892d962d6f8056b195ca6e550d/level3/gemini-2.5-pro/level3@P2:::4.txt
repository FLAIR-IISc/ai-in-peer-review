### **Summary**

This paper presents a method for training a single recurrent neural network architecture to serve as an interactive, real-time controller for a diverse set of physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with online policy learning. The authors propose a framework that jointly optimizes a collection of motion trajectories and the parameters of a neural network policy. This is achieved by alternating between two sub-problems: 1) using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate optimal trajectories that satisfy physical dynamics and task objectives, and 2) training the neural network via supervised regression to mimic the state-action mappings from these trajectories.

The authors introduce several key technical improvements that enable this method to succeed on complex, 3D locomotion tasks where previous similar approaches were limited to 2D. These include a principled method for injecting noise into the sensory inputs during training, which improves robustness and generalization. To complement this, the optimal feedback gains are calculated as a byproduct of the trajectory optimization and used to generate the correct target actions for the noisy inputs. The entire process is implemented in a distributed, asynchronous architecture, allowing it to scale. The method is successfully applied to a range of characters—including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds—demonstrating its generality without requiring task-specific state machines or motion capture data.

### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed optimization framework is sophisticated, and the implementation details suggest a significant and carefully executed engineering effort. The claims are well-supported by strong empirical evidence. The results, particularly as showcased in the supplementary video, are impressive and demonstrate stable, realistic, and purposeful behaviors across a variety of challenging control problems.

The ablation study presented in Section 8.1 is particularly valuable, as it clearly demonstrates the contributions of the key components of the proposed method. The comparison against training on a static dataset ("no joint") effectively validates the necessity of the joint optimization procedure. Similarly, the comparison against training without noise injection ("no noise") clearly shows how this technique is critical for avoiding overfitting and achieving good test-time performance. This is a complete and well-executed piece of work.

### **Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of explaining their complex method, breaking it down into logical components (Overview, Trajectory Optimization, NN Regression, etc.). The mathematical notation is consistent and the algorithm is presented clearly.

One area that could benefit from additional clarification is the policy execution step described in Section 7. The paper states that at each timestep, an optimization problem is solved to find the next state that best matches the policy's desired velocity output, subject to physical constraints. This is somewhat surprising, as one might expect the learned policy to be directly applicable without a per-frame optimization. It would be helpful if the authors could elaborate on the motivation for this. Is this final optimization step necessary because the policy regression is imperfect, or because there is residual cost from the trajectory optimization? Or is it a mechanism to ensure strict satisfaction of physical constraints (Eq. 6) which might be violated by the direct output of the network? While the authors note this allows for larger timesteps, a more detailed explanation of the trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

### **Originality**

The paper builds upon a foundation of prior work in combining trajectory optimization with policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch et al., 2014). The core idea of using an optimizer to generate data for a policy network is not entirely new. However, the authors make several novel technical contributions that collectively enable a significant leap in capability. The method for computing and utilizing optimal feedback gains to train on noisy inputs is a clever and effective way to improve robustness. Furthermore, the successful application of this unified framework to such a diverse range of complex, 3D characters is a novel achievement that pushes the state of the art in this area. While the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new learning theory, its originality lies in the synthesis of ideas and the technical innovations that make the approach practical for problems of this scale and complexity.

### **Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be of interest to researchers in computer graphics, robotics, and machine learning. The ability to automatically synthesize robust, real-time controllers for complex morphologies from only a high-level task description is a long-standing goal in these fields. This work represents a substantial step toward that goal. The generality of the approach is a major strength; practitioners in animation and game development could potentially use these ideas to drastically reduce the manual effort required to create character controllers. Likewise, the method could have applications in robotics for generating controllers for novel hardware platforms. The impressive results and the thoroughness of the technical exposition make it likely that other researchers will build upon this work.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Policy Execution (Sec 7):** As mentioned under "Clarity", please expand on the necessity of the per-frame optimization during execution. It would be valuable to understand what happens if the policy's output is used more directly (e.g., via Euler integration) and why the optimization-based approach is superior.
*   **Line 89:** There is a minor typo: "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches." should be "to unify".
*   **Line 153:** The text mentions that the proximal regularization terms are "Similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches". Could the authors provide one or two citations here to ground this statement?
*   This reviewer is not an expert in the specific domain of character animation, so it is difficult to assess the work's impact relative to the entire history of that field. However, from a machine learning and optimization perspective, the technical contributions are solid and the empirical results are compelling.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  Presents a powerful and general framework for learning controllers for a wide variety of complex, physically simulated characters.
2.  The results are state-of-the-art, demonstrating stable and realistic 3D locomotion for bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers from a single method.
3.  The technical contributions, particularly the principled use of noise and optimal feedback gains, are novel and shown to be critical for success through strong ablation studies.
4.  The paper is well-written, clear, and provides sufficient detail for others to understand and potentially build upon the work.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**

1.  The work builds heavily on existing ideas of combining trajectory optimization and policy learning, and its primary contribution is in scaling these methods to more complex problems rather than introducing a fundamentally new theoretical concept.
2.  The requirement of an optimization step at every frame during execution makes the "controller" a bit more complex than a simple feed-forward network pass, which could be seen as a minor weakness.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the weaknesses. It tackles a very challenging problem with a technically sophisticated and well-executed approach, achieving impressive and significant results.
