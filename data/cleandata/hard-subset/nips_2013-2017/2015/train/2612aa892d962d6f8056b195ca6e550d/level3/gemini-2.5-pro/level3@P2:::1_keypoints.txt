- Core idea: Jointly optimize trajectories (via Contact-Invariant Optimization) and regress an RNN policy onto those trajectories to produce real-time feedback controllers.  
- Key technical innovation: Extract and embed optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer into the supervised learning target so the network learns local linear feedback controllers, not just open-loop actions.  
- Training robustness: Inject noise at sensory input and hidden units during training to improve generalization and prevent divergence at execution.  
- Empirical scope: Demonstrates stable, realistic locomotion across diverse 3D morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) with impressive supplemental video results.  
- Algorithm design: Soft coupling of trajectory optimization and policy learning via a penalty term in a block-alternating scheme is well-motivated and elegant.  
- Experimental validation: Ablation studies convincingly show the importance of joint optimization and noise injection for performance.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: Paper is extremely well-written, with clear notation, detailed formulation, and sufficient detail for experts to reproduce.  
- Originality: Novel method for embedding feedback gains into learning and unified framework for varied morphologies; advances beyond prior optimizer-to-policy distillation work.  
- Significance: Major step forward for automated controller synthesis with broad impact on graphics, robotics, and biomechanics; emergent natural gaits without motion capture.  
- Comparison insight: Policy acts as a regularizer compared to MPC, yielding simpler, more predictable solutions.  
- Weakness — compute cost: Training is computationally expensive and requires hours on distributed cloud resources, limiting accessibility.  
- Weakness — missing baselines: Lacks systematic quantitative comparison to direct policy-search/RL methods (e.g., TRPO).  
- Weakness — runtime detail: Per-timestep optimization at execution is faster than full trajectory optimization but typical computation time per step is not reported.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, effective method with strong technical contribution, state-of-the-art results, excellent presentation, and high interdisciplinary impact.  
- Rejection arguments: High computational demands and absence of direct quantitative RL comparisons could hinder adoption and evaluation.
