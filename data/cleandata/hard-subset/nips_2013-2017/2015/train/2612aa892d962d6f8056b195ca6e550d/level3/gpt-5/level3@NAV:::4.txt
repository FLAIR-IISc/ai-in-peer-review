Summary: The paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that jointly learns optimal trajectories and a neural network policy for interactive control of complex characters. Each iteration alternates between direct trajectory optimization (via CIO) and supervised regression of a recurrent policy to noisy state–action pairs, augmented with locally optimal feedback gains derived efficiently from the Gauss–Newton machinery. At execution, the learned policy is queried and a one-step optimization enforces dynamics while matching desired velocities, enabling large timesteps. Compared to prior trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun ’14; Mordatch & Todorov ’14) and model-free policy optimization (e.g., TRPO), the contribution is a carefully engineered, distributed block-alternation that scales to diverse 3D morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion) with impressive stability and realism. The empirical insight is that decoupling into alternating subproblems allows reuse of mature solvers and works well in practice.

Quality: Technically strong and thoroughly implemented. The ablations convincingly demonstrate the benefits of (i) sensory noise during training and (ii) joint trajectory–policy learning. Results are visually compelling. I am less certain about the necessity and justification of the execution-time optimization (Sec. 7): is this compensating residual trajectory costs, policy regression error, or model mismatch? To what extent do soft dynamics constraints (including possible root forces) prevent physically implausible states?

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well organized, and provides useful cross-references. Minor issues: line 153, please add one or two citations for “similar to recent approaches,” and explain why proximal terms that keep updates small in parameter space help here (somewhat counterintuitive, but understandable for block alternation). Typo at line 89: “to unify.”

Originality: The system design is sophisticated, but I did not find novel theoretical insights. The main novelty lies in the particular combination and engineering of known pieces and the feedback-gain reuse trick.

Significance: Results are impressive; however, as I am not deeply familiar with this domain, I cannot confidently judge broader impact. A stronger quantitative comparison to direct RL baselines and MPC (beyond qualitative differences) would help.

Pros:
- Strong empirical performance across diverse 3D characters
- Clear demonstration of the benefits of noise and joint learning
- Practical alternating scheme reusing existing solvers; scalable distributed implementation
- Per-timestep re-optimization enabling large timesteps

Cons:
- Limited theoretical novelty
- Execution-time optimization rationale needs clarification
- Potential concerns about physical plausibility under soft constraints
- Missing citations at line 153; minor typo

Overall: Positive on quality and engineering; uncertain on novelty/significance.
