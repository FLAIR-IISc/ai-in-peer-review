- Proposes an alternating framework that interleaves Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization with supervised training of a recurrent policy using injected sensory noise and feedback gains computed via a perturbation method reusing the CIO Gauss-Newton Hessian.
- Execution uses a horizon-1 constrained optimization at each timestep to convert policy outputs (desired rates/forces) into feasible next states.
- Related to guided policy search and MPC; distinctive for using CIO for contact-rich 3D, optimizer-derived feedback gains, explicit noise in inputs/hidden units, and asynchronous distributed training.
- Technically sophisticated and well-engineered with broad 3D character demonstrations and stable gaits achieved without motion capture, state machines, or hand-crafted features.
- Clear empirical evidence from ablations that joint alternation and noise injection materially improve performance over strong baselines.
- Elegant, practical perturbation-based feedback-gain derivation that reuses CIO structure and mitigates instability of LQG on contact-rich trajectories.
- Practical asynchronous distributed training scheme with proximal regularization that appears to converge reliably.
- Writing is clear, well-organized, and includes useful implementation details.
- Novelty over guided policy search is incremental; paper should more crisply state fundamental new contributions beyond CIO, gain derivation, and distributed training.
- The horizon-1 re-optimization at runtime needs stronger motivation and analysis: is it required by output format, regression error, or contact complementarity; report per-step runtime and compare to simpler schemes.
- Clarify whether the execution step introduces non-physical “root forces,” how often constraints are violated, and how this affects realism and transfer to robotics.
- Evaluation metrics are underspecified; clarify train/test metrics, rollout horizon, success criteria, statistics across seeds, and quantify the “two orders of magnitude” claim.
- Report runtime and data efficiency (number of optimized trajectories) versus baselines.
- Generalization and robustness tests are lacking: evaluate out-of-distribution goal jumps, model mismatch/perturbations, and the role/dimensionality of recurrent state r.
- Cite and justify proximal/asynchronous optimization choices (e.g., Boyd et al. 2011 ADMM; Zhang et al. 2015 EASGD; Hogwild!-style work).
- Methodology is sound and the feedback-gain computation is a notable contribution, but quantitative evaluation, runtime, and robustness analyses are limited.
- Paper is mostly clear but should clarify execution-time optimization cost, exact evaluation protocol, and dimension/necessity of r; fix minor typos.
- Originality is moderate: framework follows GPS ideas but specific instantiation (CIO in 3D, gain derivation, async proximal training) is valuable and practical.
- Significance is high for character animation/graphics and contact-rich simulation practitioners, moderate for general RL/control due to conceptual proximity to GPS.
- Pros for acceptance: strong diverse empirical demonstrations, practical system, informative ablations, and a low-cost method to extract stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Cons for acceptance: incremental conceptual novelty, limited quantitative/runtime analysis, and concerns about the necessity and realism of the execution-time optimization step.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept; reviewer confidence 2.5/5.
