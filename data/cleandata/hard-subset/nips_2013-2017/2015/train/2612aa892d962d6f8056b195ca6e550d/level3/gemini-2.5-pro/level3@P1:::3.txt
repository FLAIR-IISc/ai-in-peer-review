### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimizer, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate expert motion data, and then train the neural network to mimic this expert. The key contribution lies in not treating this as a simple supervised learning problem. Instead, the authors propose a joint optimization framework where the expert trajectories and the neural network policy are iteratively and alternately refined. This process is further enhanced by principled noise injection at two levels: as data augmentation on the sensory inputs to make the policy robust to state perturbations, and as additive noise to the hidden layer activations during training to prevent overfitting. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by successfully creating controllers for a diverse set of 3D characters—including bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, and flying creatures—using the same general algorithm and network architecture, resulting in stable and realistic interactive locomotion.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The proposed method of jointly optimizing the expert trajectories and the student policy is a significant and novel contribution. This approach directly addresses a critical weakness of standard behavioral cloning, where the policy can fail catastrophically when it deviates from the expert's state distribution. By iteratively re-optimizing trajectories based on the current policy, the training data adapts to the network's behavior, leading to a much more robust controller.

2.  **Impressive Results:** The ability to generate stable, realistic gaits and motions for such a wide variety of character morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) from a single, unified framework is a strong demonstration of the method's power and generality. The emergent behaviors, like cyclic gaits and trotting, appear natural without being explicitly programmed, which is a significant achievement. The supplementary video effectively showcases the quality of the results.

3.  **Well-Motivated Design:** The use of noise injection is well-justified and appears crucial for the method's success. The technique for computing optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) to guide the network's response to sensory noise is clever, and the ablation study in Figure 2 provides convincing evidence for the benefits of both joint optimization and noise injection.

### Weaknesses (Arguments Against Acceptance)

1.  **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation lacks a systematic comparison to other state-of-the-art methods. The paper only includes a brief qualitative comparison to a generic Model-Predictive Control (MPC) approach, without providing a citation, implementation details, or quantitative metrics. Comparisons against other policy search methods (e.g., TRPO [14], which was concurrent work) or more advanced behavioral cloning techniques would be necessary to properly situate the work and substantiate its claimed superiority.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Details and Analysis:**
    *   The size of the training set (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments) seems quite small. This raises questions about the method's data efficiency and whether the baselines in the ablation study ("no joint" and "no noise") were given a fair chance, as they might be more prone to overfitting on such a small dataset.
    *   For a method intended for interactive applications, the paper completely omits any discussion of runtime performance. Key details such as frames-per-second, CPU/GPU usage during policy execution, and how these metrics scale with character complexity are essential for assessing its practical utility in games or robotics.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The mathematical presentation could be improved. Section 3.2 is particularly difficult to follow due to inconsistent notation (e.g., the time index `t` is dropped), making it hard to understand how the optimal feedback gains are calculated and used.
    *   The paper relies heavily on CIO but provides very little detail on its implementation (e.g., Eq. 6 is presented with undefined terms), forcing the reader to consult external references. A more self-contained description would significantly improve clarity.
    *   The description of the training architecture is confusing. Algorithm 1 describes a synchronous, alternating optimization scheme, while Section 6 describes an asynchronous, distributed implementation. The connection and potential differences in convergence properties between these two schemes are not discussed.
    *   The lack of publicly available code makes the work difficult to reproduce and build upon, which is a notable drawback for a paper with significant implementation complexity.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors

*   **Algorithmic Details:**
    *   In Algorithm 1, how are the initial trajectories `X_i` generated for the first iteration before the first network `theta` exists? Are they optimized without the regression cost term?
    *   The proximal regularization terms in both steps of Algorithm 1 use the same hyperparameter `eta`. Is there a principled reason for this, or was this an empirical choice? Intuitively, the "step sizes" for trajectory space and parameter space might need to be scaled differently.
    *   The ablation study in Figure 2 is useful. However, it would be more insightful to separate the two forms of noise injection: one ablation for sensory noise ("data augmentation") and another for hidden unit noise, to understand their relative contributions.

*   **Clarity and Terminology:**
    *   The paper uses "noise injection" to refer to both adding noise to sensory inputs (a form of data augmentation) and adding noise to hidden units (a regularization technique). Distinguishing these more clearly might improve readability. The analogy to denoising autoencoders is also a bit tenuous, as here the target `a` changes in response to the input noise `epsilon`.
    *   Please proofread for minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "unity these disparate approaches" -> "unify...", missing articles). Several notational inconsistencies should also be fixed (e.g., the star for optimum is sometimes a superscript and sometimes not).

*   **Task Definition:**
    *   Does the task goal include only a target position, or also a target orientation (i.e., facing direction)? The text mentions "target location and orientation," but it's not specified how orientation is incorporated. Is adding an orientation target straightforward?

*   **Future Work Suggestion:**
    *   An interesting question is whether the regression cost weight `alpha` could be annealed during training. One could imagine starting with a high `alpha` to bootstrap the policy and then gradually reducing it, allowing the trajectory optimizer more freedom later on. This could be framed as a form of curriculum learning.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a promising and well-executed idea. However, its claims of significance would be much stronger with a more thorough comparative evaluation, greater clarity in its technical descriptions, and more details regarding its practical performance.
