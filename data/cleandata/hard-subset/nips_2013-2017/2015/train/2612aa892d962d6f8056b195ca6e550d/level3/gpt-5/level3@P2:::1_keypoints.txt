- Presents a method alternating direct trajectory optimization (CIO) and supervised policy regression to train recurrent controllers for diverse 3D characters.  
- Key technical contribution: inject sensor noise and label noisy inputs with actions corrected by locally optimal feedback gains extracted efficiently from CIO via a Gauss-Newton perturbation.  
- Policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent updates, and contact forces and runs with a 1-step constrained optimizer enforcing dynamics softly for real-time control.  
- Builds on guided policy search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning but derives gains directly within CIO rather than via a separate LQG pass.  
- Incorporates gains into supervised targets for noisy inputs and scales to realistic behaviors across multiple morphologies without mocap or hand-designed state machines.  
- Alternating optimization with proximal terms is reasonable and empirically convergent; gain derivation using Woodbury/Gauss-Newton is elegant and cost-effective.  
- Denoising-style noise injection in inputs and hidden activations is well motivated and supported by ablation studies.  
- Empirical results show diverse, stable behaviors, ablations, a small architecture study, and practical distributed training/timings.  
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: few task-level benchmarks (success rates, robustness, energy, constraint violations) beyond losses and videos.  
- Execution enforces dynamics softly and may produce root forces; more reporting on physical fidelity is needed for robotics transfer claims.  
- Lack of direct empirical comparisons to GPS/iLQG/TRPO/DDPG-style methods on matched tasks.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well organized; objectives, alternation scheme, and gain derivation are concise and readable.  
- Suggest clarifying sensory/action units and scaling, especially choice of velocity/force commands vs. torques for real-world deployment.  
- Suggest reporting magnitudes of soft-constraint penalties and execution residuals (e.g., root forces) to assess realism.  
- Ask to expand on the role of recurrence: what temporal dependencies it captures and sensitivity to memory size.  
- Novelty: embedding locally optimal feedback gains into supervised targets with gains obtained stably from Gauss-Newton CIO is new and important.  
- The output-design choice (desired velocities + 1-step optimizer) differentiates this work from torque-policy baselines.  
- Significance: advances robust, realistic interactive controllers and bridges trajectory optimization with neural policy learning, with potential cross-domain impact.  
- Strengths: clear presentation, perturbation-based gain computation, principled noise-injection with gain labels, strong qualitative results across morphologies, practical real-time system.  
- Weaknesses: limited quantitative metrics, no head-to-head baselines, soft constraints and direct force commands may harm realism/transfer, recurrence role not deeply probed.  
- Questions: sensitivity to λ for gain computation and α/η for alternation; typical dynamics residuals/root forces; robustness to model mismatch; contribution of recurrence vs. feedforward.  
- Suggested improvements: add quantitative benchmarks (tracking errors, fall rates, energy, residuals), comparisons to GPS/iLQG/TRPO/DDPG, ablations on recurrence/noise/gain targets, and discuss enforcing hard constraints or torque policies.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong technical contribution, generality across 3D characters, practical training/runtime, and meaningful bridge between communities.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of quantitative baselines/robustness metrics and design choices that may limit hardware applicability without further analysis.
