1. Proposes a method using a small, fast recurrent neural network to control complex characters in a physics-based simulator, trained from offline trajectory optimization data.  
2. Demonstrates the approach on diverse morphologies and cyclic behaviors (bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting, swimming, flying) using the same network architecture.  
3. Primary strength: impressive experimental results showing stable, plausible, and interactive control across varied characters from a unified framework.  
4. Positions itself within prior work that combines offline optimization and neural networks, closely relating to Levine et al. [9].  
5. Main weakness: unclear novel scientific contribution—reads more like applying deep learning to existing trajectory-optimization techniques than introducing a fundamentally new method.  
6. Needs direct, quantitative comparisons to closely related methods (e.g., Abbeel, Levine) on metrics like learning speed, supported body variety, runtime cost, and robustness.  
7. Exposition issue: clarify what is meant by "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" and why that poses a problem for alternative approaches.  
8. Notation around lines 226–240 is confusing and should be disambiguated.  
9. The claim that the approach "produces softer feedback gains" is vague—define "softer" precisely and justify its benefits.  
10. Suggest reorganizing: move the discussion of why LQG was not used to after the proposed strategy is fully presented.  
11. Overall recommendation: experiments are strong but the paper requires significant revision to clarify its contribution relative to recent related work to establish originality and significance.
