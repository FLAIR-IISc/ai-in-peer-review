The paper "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" proposes a real-time control algorithm that combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to train a neural network to generate stable and realistic behaviors for various dynamical systems and tasks. The method uses a neural network to learn from the optimizer and produce similar behaviors online. The authors also introduce noise injection via data augmentation and additive noise in hidden layers to improve robustness.

The main contributions of the paper are the alternating joint training and noise injection, which improve generalization beyond a straightforward supervised fit to CIO. The experiments show that the network produces realistic and stable control policies on diverse character models. The authors also compare their method to naive neural network training on a static optimal trajectory dataset and to model-predictive control (MPC), demonstrating the benefits of their approach.

However, the paper has several limitations. The experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments), which weakens the claims about generalization and overfitting baselines. The comparative evaluation is weak, with only a brief MPC comparison and no systematic comparisons to other methods. Additionally, the mathematical presentation is mostly clear, but some parts, such as the CIO step and Equation 6, lack definitions and detail, requiring readers to consult external references.

The paper also has some minor writing and notation issues, such as dropped time index t in Section 3.2, which makes the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure unclear. Furthermore, the asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs substantially from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1 and should be discussed earlier or connected more clearly.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of some of the technical aspects, such as the CIO step and the perturbation method for producing feedback gains.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized, but some parts, such as Section 3.2, could be improved for better readability. The notation is mostly clear, but some minor issues, such as the use of "unity" instead of "unify," need to be fixed.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of supervised learning and trajectory optimization, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this approach differs from previous work and how it advances the state of the art.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to impact the field of character control and robotics, as it provides a method for generating realistic and stable behaviors for various dynamical systems and tasks. However, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussions of the practical benefits and limitations of the approach.

Overall, the paper is a promising and well-executed application of neural networks to character control, but it could benefit from more detailed explanations, clearer notation, and more systematic comparisons to other methods.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The experiments show that the network produces realistic and stable control policies on diverse character models.
