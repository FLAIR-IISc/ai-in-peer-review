

This paper presents a compelling method for training neural network controllers that combine trajectory optimization with policy learning to achieve robust, interactive control across diverse robotic systems. The authors propose embedding optimal feedback gains into neural network policies, creating a soft coupling between trajectory optimization and policy learning through a cost penalty for deviation. This approach enables the policy to learn simpler, more robust approximations of optimal trajectories than MPC alone, while maintaining real-time performance.

The paper excels in clarity and technical execution. The methodology is exceptionally well-organized, with clear explanations of the key innovation: using perturbation methods to compute optimal feedback gains during trajectory optimization, which are then embedded into the neural network policy. The authors effectively demonstrate their method's versatility across diverse systems (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) using a single architecture without task-specific features or state machines.

In terms of quality, the work is technically sound with thorough experimental validation. The ablation studies (comparing joint optimization with static training and with noise injection) provide strong evidence for the methodological contributions. The implementation details regarding distributed training are well-justified and effectively address scalability concerns.

The originality is significant, representing a novel integration of trajectory optimization, neural networks, and control theory. While previous work has combined trajectory optimization with neural networks (Levine et al., 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2012), this paper makes substantial improvements in robustness, generalization, and applicability to 3D locomotion. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work by demonstrating successful 3D locomotion where previous methods were limited to 2D.

The significance is high, as the method provides a unified framework for diverse robotic control problems, potentially reducing the need for specialized controllers across different robot morphologies. The results are impressive and demonstrate clear methodological advancement over existing approaches.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically impressive, with strong experimental validation across multiple domains. The only minor limitation is that the paper doesn't deeply explore the theoretical guarantees of the proposed optimization procedure, though this is understandable given the practical focus. Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in neural network control.
