This paper presents a novel approach to interactive control of complex characters using neural networks. The authors propose a method that combines trajectory optimization with policy learning, allowing for the generation of stable and realistic behaviors for a range of dynamical systems and tasks. The key insight is to decouple the problem into alternating subproblems, which enables the reuse of existing methods and works well in practice.

The paper demonstrates a high level of technical sophistication in both concept and implementation, with impressive results. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their approach, with appropriate detail and useful cross-references. The use of sensory noise during training and the joint learning of trajectories and policy are clearly demonstrated to be advantageous.

The reviewer appreciates the technical prowess of the paper but notes that there are no novel theoretical insights. The broader impact of the work is difficult to judge, as the reviewer is not deeply familiar with the domain. However, the results are impressive, and the approach seems to be well-suited to the problem of interactive control of complex characters.

One potential criticism is the need for an additional optimization step in Section 7. The reviewer expected the policy to run without additional optimization and questions whether residual trajectory cost or policy regression error necessitates re-optimization. Additionally, it is unclear whether dynamics would prevent physical implausibility. However, the reviewer acknowledges that re-optimizing each timestep permits the use of larger timesteps.

The paper could benefit from additional references to support the statement "similar to recent approaches" on line 153. The reviewer notes that keeping updates small in parameter space is counterintuitive but understandable for block-alternation.

There is a typo on line 89, where "to unify" should be used instead of "unity."

Overall, the reviewer has a positive impression of the results and optimization scheme but is uncertain about the novelty and significance of the work due to domain unfamiliarity.
