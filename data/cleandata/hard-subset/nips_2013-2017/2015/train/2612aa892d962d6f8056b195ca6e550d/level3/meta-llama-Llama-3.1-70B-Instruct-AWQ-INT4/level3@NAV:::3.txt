This paper proposes a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters. The approach combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate stable and realistic behaviors for various dynamical systems and tasks. The authors also introduce noise injection via data augmentation and additive noise in hidden layers to improve robustness.

The paper's main contributions are the alternating joint training and noise injection methods, which improve generalization beyond a straightforward supervised fit to CIO. The experiments demonstrate the network's ability to produce realistic and stable control policies on diverse character models, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking.

However, the experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments), which weakens claims about generalization and overfitting baselines. The comparative evaluation is also weak, with only a brief Model-Predictive Control (MPC) comparison and missing systematic comparisons to other methods.

The mathematical presentation is mostly clear, but some parts, such as the CIO step and Eq. 6, lack definitions and detail, requiring readers to consult external references. Additionally, Section 3.2 is confusing due to notational issues, making the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure unclear.

The paper's strengths include its ability to generate realistic and stable control policies for diverse character models, its use of noise injection to improve robustness, and its potential applications in robotics, animation, and gaming. However, the paper's weaknesses, including its limited experimental evaluation and weak comparative evaluation, need to be addressed in future work.

In terms of originality, the paper's approach to combining supervised learning with trajectory optimization and noise injection is novel and contributes to the field of neural network control. However, the paper's reliance on existing methods, such as CIO and MPC, means that it builds upon previous work rather than introducing entirely new concepts.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to automate the process of designing interactive real-time controllers for complex characters, which could have a significant impact on various fields, including robotics, animation, and gaming. However, the paper's limitations, including its small training set and weak comparative evaluation, need to be addressed to fully realize its potential.

Overall, the paper is well-executed, but its practical benefits, broader comparisons, clarifications, and additional experiments are needed to fully establish its contributions to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers.
* The approach combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization and noise injection to improve robustness.
* The experiments demonstrate the network's ability to produce realistic and stable control policies on diverse character models.

* The experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set.
* The comparative evaluation is weak, with only a brief MPC comparison and missing systematic comparisons to other methods.

* The paper's reliance on existing methods, such as CIO and MPC, means that it builds upon previous work rather than introducing entirely new concepts.
