## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a wide range of dynamical systems, including robots and animated characters. The core idea – embedding feedback gains learned through trajectory optimization into the neural network training process – is a clever approach to improving robustness and stability. By softly coupling trajectory optimization and policy optimization via a cost penalty, the resulting policies learn simpler, more robust approximations of optimal trajectories than those achievable through MPC alone.

The work builds upon existing methods in reinforcement learning, stochastic optimal control, and trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO). While the basic components aren’t entirely novel, the paper significantly advances the state-of-the-art by successfully applying this combined approach to complex, 3D systems – a feat previously unrealized in comparable work [9, 11]. The sophisticated parallelization strategy used to optimize tasks is a noteworthy implementation detail that enables scaling to these challenging problems. The demonstration of trained controllers on a diverse set of robots and characters – from swimmers to bipeds – further strengthens the impact of this work.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The technical details are presented meticulously, and the motivations for design choices are well explained. The experimental results are inspiring, showcasing realistic and stable behaviors across diverse morphologies. The integration of graphics, control, and neural networks is seamless and impressive. This paper represents a significant methodological contribution, effectively bridging the gap between offline trajectory optimization and online, interactive control. 

**Weaknesses:** While the paper is strong overall, a more detailed comparison to direct Reinforcement Learning methods [3, 6, 14] – beyond the brief impressionistic remark – would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the discussion of the hyperparameter choices could be expanded; providing more insight into the sensitivity of performance to these parameters would be helpful. Finally, the reliance on MuJoCo as the physics engine, while reasonable, limits potential applicability to systems not easily modeled within that framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Significant methodological advance in combining trajectory optimization with neural networks.
*   Demonstrated success on a challenging and diverse set of problems.
*   Exceptional clarity and technical quality of the paper.
*   Potential for wide-ranging impact in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Limited detailed comparison to alternative RL approaches.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis could be more thorough. 
*   Dependence on a specific physics engine.

**Overall:** Despite minor weaknesses, this is a truly excellent paper. It presents a novel, well-executed, and impactful contribution to the field. I **strongly recommend acceptance** to the conference.
