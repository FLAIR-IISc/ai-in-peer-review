## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as real-time controllers for diverse character models, leveraging Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and a novel alternating joint training scheme with noise injection. The core idea – using a NN to learn from and generalize the outputs of an offline optimizer like CIO – is a compelling approach to bridging the gap between high-quality, but computationally expensive, trajectory optimization and real-time control. The demonstrated ability to control a variety of character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network architecture is a significant achievement, and the accompanying video likely showcases impressive results. The method builds upon prior work in both trajectory optimization [12] and neural network control [3, 6, 9, 11], and represents a step forward in applying deep learning to complex motor control problems.

However, despite the promising results, several weaknesses limit the paper’s impact. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating feasibility, is hampered by a relatively small training dataset (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments). This raises concerns about generalization ability and the robustness of the reported performance. The comparative evaluation is particularly weak; the brief mention of MPC lacks a proper citation and systematic comparisons to other relevant control methods (e.g., reinforcement learning, other model-predictive control schemes) are missing. 

The mathematical presentation, while generally clear, suffers from a lack of detail in key sections. The CIO step (Eq. 6) and the explanation of the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure in Section 3.2 require readers to consult external references, hindering reproducibility. Furthermore, the asynchronous joint optimization described in Section 6 deviates substantially from the alternating scheme presented in Algorithm 1 and lacks sufficient connection or justification. 

Several smaller issues detract from the overall clarity. The distinction between “data augmentation” and “noise injection” is blurred, and the analogy to denoising autoencoders feels somewhat forced. Algorithmic choices (e.g., the use of a constant eta, initial X computation) are not adequately justified. Numerous minor writing and notation errors further complicate the reading experience. 

Finally, crucial practical considerations like runtime measurements and scaling with model complexity are absent, hindering assessment of the method’s suitability for real-time applications. Providing code would significantly improve reproducibility and facilitate adoption.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of CIO and neural networks for real-time control.
*   Demonstrated ability to control diverse character models with a single network.
*   Promising results suggesting potential for automating complex controller design.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation and concerns about generalization.
*   Weak comparative evaluation.
*   Lack of clarity in mathematical presentation and algorithmic details.
*   Missing runtime analysis and code.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a promising and well-executed application of neural networks to character control. However, the paper requires significant revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered a strong contribution to the field. I recommend **rejection with request for revisions**. Addressing the experimental limitations, providing a more thorough comparative analysis, clarifying the mathematical details, and including runtime measurements are essential for strengthening the paper’s impact.
