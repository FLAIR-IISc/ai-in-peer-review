- Core contribution: alternating trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural policy regression using sensory noise and locally optimal feedback gains to train recurrent controllers that output desired velocities, hidden states, and contact forces.  
- Implementation: distributed asynchronous training with proximal regularization for scalability; single-step re-optimization at test time to enforce dynamics consistency.  
- Relation to prior work: extends “learn-from-optimizer” approaches by folding noisy inputs and linearized feedback targets into training and using perturbation-based gains computed from CIO’s Gauss-Newton structure.  
- Technical soundness: alternating optimization and perturbation-based gain derivation are well-motivated and computationally efficient (Woodbury identity, reuse of CXX^{-1}).  
- Training pipeline: coherent and robust (sensor/hidden noise, proximal regularization, GPU SGD) with sufficient implementation detail for reproduction.  
- Empirical evidence: compelling diverse 3D results across morphologies and ablations showing noise injection and joint optimization are critical.  
- Quantitative gaps: limited quantitative evaluation (mainly objective values) and no direct comparisons to strong model-free baselines (e.g., TRPO) or LQG gains on the same trajectories.  
- Execution-time optimization concern: the T=1 re-optimization improves robustness but blurs the line between a pure reactive policy and a hybrid policy+MPC scheme and needs clearer motivation.  
- Dynamics fidelity concern: use of soft constraints and root forces may reduce physical plausibility for real-robot applications.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; gain derivation and implementation details are clearly presented.  
- Requested clarification: expand motivation for the T=1 optimization and explain failure modes if the network outputs are forward-integrated without it.  
- Reference suggestion: add proximal/stochastic optimization citations (e.g., Boyd, Xiao, Schmidt) for Line 153.  
- Typo: fix “to unify” at line 89.  
- Originality: mainly a system-level synthesis, with two notable contributions—efficient extraction of stabilizing feedback gains within direct trajectory optimization and integrating those gains into noisy regression targets.  
- Significance: strong practical impact for graphics/animation and contact-rich locomotion; real-robot impact requires harder constraint enforcement and additional comparisons.  
- Requests for additional experiments: quantify root forces during execution and effects of harder constraints; report success rates, perturbation recovery, and rollout horizon without T=1 optimization.  
- Suggested comparison: evaluate LQG gains computed around the same CIO trajectories (stability, conditioning, empirical performance).  
- Design question: justify outputting desired velocities and contact forces versus learning torques directly and discuss whether torques could be learned with similar robustness.  
- Compute details requested: report time per CIO iteration, per NN epoch, data volume per alternation, and sensitivity to number of branched segments.  
- Pros summary: strong, diverse 3D behaviors; elegant perturbation-based gains; robust noise-aware regression targets; practical distributed pipeline; clear ablations and writing.  
- Cons summary: limited quantitative baselines and model-free comparisons; execution-time re-optimization weakens “policy-only” claim; soft-contact/root-force choices challenge robotics fidelity; novelty primarily engineering integration.  
- Overall: strong, well-executed systems paper likely accept-worthy, but should strengthen quantitative evaluations, clarify the T=1 optimization rationale, and broaden comparisons.
