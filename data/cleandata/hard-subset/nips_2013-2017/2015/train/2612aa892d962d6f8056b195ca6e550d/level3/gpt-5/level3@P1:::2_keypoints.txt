- Proposes alternating learning that distills Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) into a small recurrent policy producing velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state updates for real-time control.
- Trains the policy on continually re-optimized CIO trajectories with noise injection in sensory inputs and hidden activations to promote robustness.
- Estimates optimal local feedback gains via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss–Newton machinery and the Woodbury identity for better conditioning.
- Executes with a short-horizon (T=1) optimization enforcing dynamics for stable, real-time control.
- Demonstrates a single network architecture handling diverse 3D behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without task-specific state machines or mocap.
- Builds on prior trajectory‑optimization→policy methods (e.g., Levine & Koltun, Mordatch & Todorov) and is distinct from direct deep RL/model-free approaches.
- Main contributions are an integrated training pipeline and technical refinements: noise-with-target-adjustment, a practical feedback-gain estimator in direct TO, asynchronous distributed training with proximal regularization, and goal-branching data generation.
- Methodology is technically sound and well-engineered; ablations (“no joint,” “no noise”) support joint optimization and noise injection benefits.
- The feedback-gain derivation leveraging Gauss–Newton and Woodbury is a neat, practically valuable contribution.
- Empirical evaluation is mostly qualitative; stronger quantitative baselines (especially Levine & Koltun and MPC) are needed.
- Suggested quantitative metrics missing: training time, compute budget, run-time frequency, failure rates, energy/torque use, goal-tracking error, disturbance recovery, morphology performance.
- Robustness to model mismatch and untrained perturbations at test time is not rigorously assessed.
- Clarity issues: LQG discussion appears before presenting the alternative, making the narrative harder to follow.
- Notation problems (lines 226–240) confuse nominal/perturbed states and optimizer solutions; clearer distinct symbols and evaluation points are needed.
- Statements like “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” should be quantified (norms, singular values, condition numbers) and linked to observed issues.
- “Softer feedback gains” must be defined precisely (smaller norms, better conditioning, smoother variation) and supported with evidence (e.g., gain norm vs. λ plots).
- Originality is incremental: consolidates known ideas, with the main novelty being efficient feedback-gain computation and noise-informed supervision.
- The claim of solving “substantially harder” problems than prior work requires stronger, controlled comparisons.
- Significance: could be influential for animation/robotics if quantitative evidence supports claims; the engineering recipe is practically useful.
- Specific suggested experiments: head-to-head with Levine & Koltun, quantitative MPC baseline, feedback gain statistics vs. LQG, ablation of velocity/force vs. torque outputs, and robustness/generalization tests (pushes, mass/friction changes, sensor noise).
- Pros: strong multi-morphology demonstrations, elegant feedback-gain integration, clear ablations, scalable real-time pipeline.
- Cons: limited novelty relative to recent pipelines, insufficient quantitative comparisons and robustness analysis, key clarity/notation problems, vague claims about gain conditioning.
- Overall: solid experimental work with impressive breadth, but needs clearer positioning and stronger quantitative evaluation against closely related methods to justify publication.
