

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" (NIPS 2015)

This paper presents a method for training neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems including swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion. The authors combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate stable, realistic behaviors without motion capture or task-specific features. The key innovation lies in interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains. This builds on prior work in trajectory optimization for character animation [12, 11] and neural network control [9, 3, 6, 14], but extends it to 3D environments where previous work was limited to 2D. The method represents a significant step toward unifying disparate control approaches across different character types.

**Strengths:**
- The paper demonstrates impressive empirical results, successfully controlling diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture.
- The method produces stable, realistic locomotion behaviors that are superior to naive neural network training approaches.
- The integration of trajectory optimization with neural networks for real-time control is technically sound and well-motivated.
- The ablation studies (Section 8.1) effectively demonstrate the necessity of both joint optimization and noise injection.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Full Model Knowledge Requirement:** The method fundamentally requires full model knowledge (Eq. 6), which is a significant limitation for real-world robotics applications where accurate dynamics models are often unavailable. The paper fails to adequately address this limitation or discuss potential extensions to model-free settings.

- **Structural Imbalance:** Sections 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) and 6 (Distributed Training Architecture) are disproportionately short and long, respectively. Section 4 fails to mention the recurrent units referenced in the abstract, creating a disconnect between the paper's stated approach and its implementation details.

- **Misleading "Stochastic" Description:** The paper describes the network as "stochastic" (line 405), but the network itself is deterministic. The stochasticity comes from the training data (via injected noise), which is a common practice but not the same as a stochastic policy. This mischaracterization needs clarification to avoid confusion in the community.

- **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper compares to naive neural network training and ablates noise injection, but lacks comparison to more recent reinforcement learning methods (e.g., TRPO [14], PPO) that might provide a more meaningful benchmark for performance.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-supported by experiments. However, the paper's claim of "universal synthesis methods applicable to arbitrary behaviors" is overstated due to the full model knowledge requirement. The authors are honest about limitations in their ablation studies but fail to adequately address the model knowledge constraint.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written but suffers from the structural imbalance mentioned above. The description of the recurrent units in Section 4 is particularly confusing given their prominence in the abstract. The distinction between the stochastic training process and the deterministic network needs clearer explanation.

**Originality:** The combination of trajectory optimization with neural networks for control is novel, particularly for 3D character control. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate from related work [9, 11] in terms of specific technical innovations. The noise injection technique is similar to existing approaches [4, 5, 19].

**Significance:** The results are promising for character animation and demonstrate a path toward unified control approaches across different character types. However, the full model knowledge requirement limits its broader significance for robotics applications. The method's computational demands (2.5 hours training time) also pose practical challenges.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents promising results for character animation but has significant structural and conceptual issues that need addressing. I recommend a major revision to:
1. Clarify the full model knowledge requirement and discuss limitations
2. Rebalance sections 4 and 6, with particular attention to explaining the recurrent units
3. Correct the misleading "stochastic" description
4. Include a more thorough comparison to recent RL methods

The paper's core contribution is valuable for character animation, but the current presentation doesn't fully justify its significance for the broader NIPS audience due to the model knowledge constraint and structural issues. With the suggested revisions, it could make a strong contribution to the field of machine learning for control.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
*Pro:* Strong empirical results across diverse character types; effective demonstration of noise injection and joint optimization; novel application of trajectory optimization with neural networks.
*Con:* Full model knowledge requirement limits applicability; structural imbalance in paper; misleading description of "stochastic" network; insufficient comparison to recent RL methods.
