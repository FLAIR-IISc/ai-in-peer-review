### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time, interactive feedback controller for physically simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline, high-quality trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization, or CIO) to generate a dataset of optimal state-action pairs, which is then used to train the neural network policy. The key contributions include framing the training as a joint optimization problem where the trajectories and network parameters are optimized in an alternating fashion, a principled method for injecting noise into sensory inputs and network activations to improve robustness, and a novel technique for extracting optimal feedback gains directly from the CIO solver. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach by applying the same architecture to generate stable and realistic locomotion for a diverse set of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, without task-specific engineering or motion capture data.

This work fits into a line of research that combines trajectory optimization with function approximation to learn control policies, closely related to recent work by Levine & Koltun (2014) [9] and Mordatch et al. (2014) [11]. While those papers established the viability of this general approach, this paper aims to scale it to more complex, 3D locomotion tasks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the experimental results are the primary strength. The ability to control such a wide variety of characters with different morphologies and dynamics using a single, unified framework is impressive. The supplementary video effectively showcases the quality and stability of the resulting controllers. The ablation study presented in Figure 2 provides convincing evidence for the necessity of both the joint optimization framework (as opposed to training on a static dataset) and the noise injection techniques for achieving good generalization and avoiding policy divergence at runtime.

However, some technical claims could be better supported. For instance, the authors state that using LQG to compute feedback gains can produce "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." This is a strong claim that requires more substantiation. It would be helpful to explain precisely why this occurs and what negative effects it has on the learning process, perhaps with a small illustrative example or quantitative evidence.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The overall algorithm and architecture are presented in a logical manner. However, several key sections would benefit from improved clarity.

1.  The derivation of the optimal feedback gains in Section 3.2 is difficult to follow due to confusing notation. The use of `s(X) = s̄` in the augmented cost function `C̃(X, s̄)` (line 229) while the penalty term is `||s(X) - s̄||^2` is ambiguous. Using more distinct symbols for the reference state and the variable state would greatly improve readability.
2.  The claim that the proposed approach "produces softer feedback gains" is vague. The term "softer" should be defined more concretely (e.g., in terms of the gain matrix norm). Furthermore, the intuition that weakening the initial state constraint allows the system to "better achieve the master cost function" should be explained more thoroughly.
3.  The exposition could be improved. The motivation for developing a new method for feedback gain calculation would be clearer if the discussion of the perceived drawbacks of LQG were presented before, rather than after, the proposed solution.

**Originality:** The paper's main weakness is its positioning with respect to prior work. While the specific combination of CIO, noise injection, and the proposed feedback gain calculation may be novel, the core concept of using trajectory optimization to generate "expert" data for training a neural network policy has been explored in closely related work [9, 11]. The paper claims to solve "substantially harder problems" than previous work but does not provide a clear and explicit explanation of what novel technical elements enable this leap.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should more clearly delineate their contribution from that of Levine, Abbeel, and their co-authors. This should go beyond simply stating that they handle 3D characters. A detailed comparison on metrics such as learning speed, supported character variety, final policy robustness, and runtime computational cost would help situate the contribution within the existing literature and highlight its unique advantages. As it stands, the work reads more as a successful application and scaling-up of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new method.

**Significance:** The results are significant and demonstrate a promising path toward automated synthesis of controllers for complex, interactive characters. If the method is as general as claimed, it could have a substantial impact on applications in computer animation, video games, and robotics by reducing the manual effort required to design controllers. The demonstrated success on diverse 3D locomotion tasks represents a clear advance for this class of methods. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the originality concerns mentioned above. A clearer articulation of the scientific contribution beyond the impressive engineering result would strengthen the paper's potential impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Demonstrates impressive, high-quality results on a diverse set of challenging 3D control problems using a single, unified method.
*   The approach successfully generates stable, interactive controllers from scratch, which is a significant achievement.
*   The ablation study provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the joint optimization and noise injection components.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper does not adequately differentiate its contributions from very closely related prior work, particularly from Levine, Koltun, and Mordatch.
*   The novelty of the core methodology is limited, making the work appear more incremental than foundational.
*   Key technical details, especially regarding the calculation and properties of the feedback gains, are not explained with sufficient clarity and justification.

In conclusion, this paper presents strong experimental work with impressive results. However, its contribution is undermined by a lack of clear positioning relative to prior art and some unclear technical explanations. I would be willing to reconsider my rating if the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to address these points, particularly by providing a more explicit comparison to related methods and clarifying the novelty and justification of their technical approach.
