- Proposes training RNN controllers to match CIO-generated near‑optimal feedback policies via alternating CIO trajectory optimization and supervised policy regression with noise.
- Computes local feedback gains by reusing CIO’s Gauss–Newton structure to teach the network how optimal actions change under small sensory perturbations.
- Actions are desired velocities and contact forces; a small recurrent state adds memory beyond physical state; execution uses a horizon-1 optimization each step.
- Builds on prior guided-policy/trajectory-optimization work but targets diverse 3D characters with a common architecture and introduces a practical feedback-gain computation.
- Technically coherent and plausible engineering choices (noise injection, proximal regularization, distributed GPU implementation); feedback-gain computation is useful.
- Several derivations and definitions are under-specified (notably Eq. 6 and Section 3.2 notation; missing defs for H, C, J, K, sX, aX and time indices).
- Experiments produce compelling visual results and two ablations, but dataset is small (100–200 trials × 5 branches) and comparisons to alternative controllers are limited.
- Claims of generalization and robustness need stronger evidence via larger/more varied train/test splits and stress tests (perturbations, terrain changes).
- Notational inconsistencies and missing definitions (stars/superscripts, dropped time indices) reduce clarity; asynchronous training should be tied to Algorithm 1’s alternating scheme.
- Terminology should distinguish “data augmentation” (noise during data generation) from “noise injection” (during NN training); the denoising-autoencoder analogy is partial.
- Numerous minor typos, acronym ordering issues, and equation/variable reference errors need cleaning.
- Originality: combining CIO with supervised NN learning isn’t entirely new, but reusing CIO’s Gauss–Newton for well-conditioned feedback gains and unified 3D application are noteworthy.
- Significance: method could impact character animation and robotics by turning offline trajectory optimizers into interactive policies, given robustness.
- Major weakness: lack of systematic comparisons to strong baselines (MPC with matched costs, TRPO, guided policy search) and no code release.
- Missing runtime/scaling analysis and inference cost reporting undermines claims of interactivity and practicality.
- Suggested comparisons: quantitative/qualitative baselines including MPC, TRPO, and guided-policy variants to contextualize performance.
- Suggested ablations: separate sensory vs. hidden noise effects, test without feedback-gain targets, and explore network capacity with error bars.
- Request runtime details: policy inference time, T=1 optimization cost, scaling with network size and character complexity, and feasibility for real-time systems.
- Clarify asynchrony vs. alternating training, proximal regularization behavior, convergence curves, sensitivity to η, and why the same η is used for both subproblems.
- Clarify initialization: how initial trajectories X are obtained, network weight initialization, and CIO behavior with initial penalty settings.
- Discuss role and scheduling of α (penalty weight): whether annealing helps avoid over-regularization by the current NN.
- Confirm whether orientation/heading is included in goals and evaluate abrupt/out-of-distribution goal changes.
- Quantify soft constraint/root forces at test time, their frequency/magnitude, visual impact, and implications for robotics.
- Notation fixes requested: add time indices in Sec. 3.2, define all symbols in Eq. (6), ensure consistent star/superscript usage, and fix typos.
- Pros: practical unified method, novel feedback-gain derivation, diverse convincing 3D results, and sensible robustness techniques.
- Cons: limited comparative evaluation, small training set and weak generalization analysis, missing runtime/scaling metrics, clarity issues, and no released code.
- Overall: promising and well-executed with a useful technical contribution; likely acceptable if authors add stronger baselines, clarify method/notation, and provide runtime/scaling analyses.
