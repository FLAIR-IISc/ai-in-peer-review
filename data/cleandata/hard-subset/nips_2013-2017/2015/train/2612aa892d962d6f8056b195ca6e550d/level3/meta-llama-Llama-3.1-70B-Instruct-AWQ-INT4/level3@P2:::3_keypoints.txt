- Proposes a real-time interactive character control algorithm that trains a neural network offline to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs.  
- Core techniques are alternating joint training between CIO and the network, plus noise injection (data augmentation and additive hidden-layer noise) for robustness.  
- Aims to automate controller design with a universal synthesis method for diverse behaviors, morphologies, changing objectives, and perturbations.  
- Main contribution: alternating joint training and noise injection improve generalization beyond straightforward supervised fitting to CIO.  
- Experiments show realistic, stable policies across diverse characters and tasks (swimming, flying, biped, quadruped) and allow user-controlled goals.  
- Strength: generates stable realistic behavior without motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines.  
- Strength: interaction capability and robustness gains from noise injection/data augmentation are significant.  
- Technical quality is sound with supporting theoretical analysis and experiments.  
- Weakness: small training set (100–200 trials with 5 branched segments) limits claims about generalization and overfitting baselines.  
- Weakness: comparative evaluation is weak—only a brief MPC comparison and missing systematic comparisons to other methods.  
- Presentation gap: some mathematical parts (notably the CIO step and Eq. 6) lack definitions/detail, forcing readers to consult external references.  
- Algorithmic details missing: initialization of X in Alg.1, choice and justification of hyperparameter eta, and rationale for keeping eta identical across steps.  
- Experimental design could be improved with more granular ablations (e.g., separate “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise injection” variants).  
- Clarity issues: Section 3.2 and some notation are confusing and need careful editing for readability.  
- Originality: novel combination of alternating training and noise injection, but needs clearer positioning relative to prior work and claimed advances.  
- Significance: promising and well-executed application, but needs more discussion of practical benefits and real-world impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, improved generalization over supervised CIO fitting, promising automation of controller design.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental scale, weak comparative evaluation, and incomplete mathematical/algorithmic presentation.
