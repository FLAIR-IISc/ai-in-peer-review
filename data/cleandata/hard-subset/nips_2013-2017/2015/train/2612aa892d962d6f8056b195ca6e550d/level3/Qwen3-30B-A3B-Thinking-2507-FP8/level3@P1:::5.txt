

The paper presents a method for training neural network controllers via interleaved supervised learning and trajectory optimization (CIO), enabling interactive control of diverse 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) without motion capture or task-specific features. It builds on prior trajectory optimization work [12] and recent neural control approaches [9,11], extending them to 3D continuous control problems. The core innovation lies in jointly optimizing trajectories and a neural policy using stochastic noise injection and optimal feedback gains, with the network learning implicit dynamics rather than explicit policies.

**Strengths:**  
The method demonstrates significant empirical success, achieving stable 3D locomotion across diverse morphologies (e.g., birds, swimmers, quadrupeds) using a single architecture. The ablation studies (Fig. 2, Table 1) convincingly show that joint optimization (vs. static training) and noise injection are critical for robustness—static training fails catastrophically (2 orders of magnitude worse), while noise-free training overfits. The unified architecture across character types (Section 8) is a compelling contribution, avoiding the need for task-specific state machines. The trajectory optimization approach (CIO) enables complex behaviors without manual initialization, and the distributed training framework (Section 6) scales efficiently to large problems. The work directly addresses a key challenge in embodied AI: closing the gap between offline trajectory optimization and real-time interactive control.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Critical dependency on full model knowledge (Eq. 6):** The method requires exact physical dynamics (H(q), C(q, q̇), etc.) for trajectory optimization, as stated in the constraints (Eq. 6). This severely limits applicability to real-world robotics where dynamics are often unknown or approximate. The paper does not discuss how this constraint impacts deployment on physical systems or whether dynamics can be learned. This is a major quality concern, as the method cannot be applied to scenarios lacking precise model knowledge—a common limitation in robotics.  

2. **Technical inconsistencies:**  
   - Section 4 (NN policy) fails to describe the "small number of recurrent units" referenced in the abstract and Section 2.1, despite the network’s claimed recurrent structure. This omission undermines the technical contribution.  
   - The network is described as "stochastic" (Line 405), but the policy is deterministic; the stochasticity arises only from training data injection. This terminology is misleading and should be clarified.  

3. **Structural imbalances:** Section 4 (NN policy) is disproportionately brief (3 pages) compared to Section 6 (training architecture, 5 pages), despite the former being central to the method. Key details (e.g., recurrent unit implementation, noise injection mechanics) are glossed over, while Section 6 focuses on infrastructure. The manuscript would benefit from restructuring to prioritize technical exposition over implementation logistics.  

4. **Overstated universality:** While the method works for diverse *simulated* characters, the requirement for full model knowledge (point 1) and the need for task-specific cost functions (Section 8) mean it is not truly "universal" as claimed. The paper does not address whether the same controller could handle novel tasks (e.g., climbing) without retraining.  

**Originality & Significance:** The work extends trajectory optimization to 3D control with neural policy learning, a meaningful step beyond 2D prior work [9,11]. The joint optimization framework and noise injection strategy are novel contributions to the control-ML intersection. Significance is high for animation/robotics, as it enables interactive control of complex behaviors without manual engineering. However, the reliance on exact dynamics reduces broader impact for real-world robotics.  

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound and empirically strong approach with significant potential. However, the critical dependency on full model knowledge (a major limitation for real-world deployment), the omission of recurrent units, and the structural imbalance in the manuscript weaken its contribution. The method is not "universal" as claimed, and the lack of discussion on dynamics approximation is a serious oversight. The rebuttal noted promising results but required restructuring; addressing these points would strengthen the work.  

**Pro:** Strong empirical results across diverse 3D tasks; novel joint optimization framework; unified architecture for multiple morphologies.  
**Con:** Requires full physical model knowledge (limits applicability); recurrent units unexplained; misleading "stochastic" description; structural imbalance in presentation.  

This work advances the state of the art in neural control for simulation, but the dynamics dependency and technical gaps prevent it from being a transformative contribution for NIPS. Acceptance is borderline, but the critical weakness in model dependency warrants rejection unless these issues are addressed in revision.
