This paper presents a powerful and elegant method for training recurrent neural network policies to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to softly couple offline trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning. This builds upon an established line of research in guided policy search and learning from experts (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). However, the authors introduce a key methodological innovation: they compute and embed optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer directly into the neural network's training targets. This explicitly teaches the policy to be robust to perturbations, a critical step for stable real-time execution. The framework is demonstrated to be highly effective, generating stable and plausible locomotion for an impressive diversity of morphologies, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters, without task-specific engineering.

The strengths of this paper are numerous and substantial.
*   **Quality & Significance:** The work is of exceptionally high quality. The technical contribution of using optimizer-derived feedback gains to create robust training targets is both novel and highly effective, representing a significant advance in synthesizing controllers that bridge the gap between offline planning and online execution. The experimental results, showcasing the general applicability of a single method across vastly different dynamical systems, are inspiring and demonstrably push the state of the art.
*   **Originality:** While building on familiar concepts, the specific formulation for softly coupling the optimizations and, most importantly, the method for reusing the trajectory optimizer's computations to derive well-conditioned feedback gains, is a distinct and valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is written with exceptional clarity. The method is well-motivated and described in sufficient detail, and the ablation studies provide clear evidence for the importance of the joint optimization and noise injection components.

The paper has very few weaknesses. The primary limitation may be the substantial computational cost of the distributed training architecture, which could be a barrier to reproducibility for labs with fewer resources. Furthermore, while the ablation study is strong, a direct quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art model-free reinforcement learning algorithms on one of the tasks could have further contextualized the performance benefits of this trajectory-optimization-based approach.

In conclusion, this is an outstanding paper that presents a complete and technically impressive piece of research. It successfully integrates concepts from optimization, control theory, and deep learning to solve a challenging problem. The results are compelling and the methodological contribution is significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
