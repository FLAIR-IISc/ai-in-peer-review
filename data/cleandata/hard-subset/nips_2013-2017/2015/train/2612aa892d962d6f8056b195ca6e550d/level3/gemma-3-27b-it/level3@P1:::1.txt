This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a diverse range of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and various forms of locomotion with differing body morphologies. The core idea is to bridge the gap between offline trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) and online control through neural networks. The authors achieve this by jointly optimizing trajectories and the neural network policy, with a penalty encouraging the network to accurately reproduce the optimal actions generated by the optimizer. A particularly noteworthy aspect is the incorporation of optimal feedback gains, derived from the trajectory optimization process, into the training signal for the neural network, enhancing robustness to noise and regression errors.

The work builds upon a rich history of control and learning approaches.  Early neural network control efforts are acknowledged, contrasted with the recent successes in areas like computer vision and speech recognition, where large datasets are readily available. The paper correctly positions itself within the lineage of reinforcement learning and stochastic optimal control, acknowledging the difficulty of scaling these methods to high-dimensional, continuous spaces.  It also draws connections to recent work combining trajectory optimization and neural networks [9, 11], but convincingly argues that the presented method advances the state-of-the-art by successfully tackling substantially more complex problems, specifically 3D locomotion, which was previously inaccessible to these approaches.  The historical context of physics-based controllers, utilizing state machines and simplified models for specific morphologies [21, 18, 13, 8, 7], is also well-covered, highlighting the ambition of the work to create a unified, generalizable control framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The method is rigorously described, with clear explanations of the optimization process, the incorporation of feedback gains, and the distributed training architecture. The use of CIO as a trajectory optimization method is well-justified, and the mathematical formulation of the objective function and the algorithms is precise.  The ablation studies (section 8.1) provide strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach, demonstrating the importance of joint optimization and noise injection.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanations are concise yet thorough, making the complex ideas accessible to a broad audience. The inclusion of a supplementary video is a significant asset, providing a compelling demonstration of the method’s capabilities.
* **Originality:** While building upon existing techniques, the combination of trajectory optimization, neural network control, and the integration of feedback gains represents a novel and effective approach. The ability to control diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture is a particularly significant contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are highly impactful. The demonstration of stable and realistic locomotion in 3D space, coupled with the method’s generality and scalability, represents a substantial advance in the field.  The potential applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics are significant. The emphasis on efficient data generation via trajectory optimization, rather than relying solely on direct reinforcement learning, is a pragmatic and promising direction.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the distributed architecture is presented as an implementation detail, the reliance on cloud computing and lengthy training times (hours) could be a barrier to wider adoption.  A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and potential for further optimization would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Although the authors report the values of key algorithmic constants, a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable. Understanding how the performance varies with different noise levels (σε, σγ), penalty weights (α), and regularization parameters (η) could provide further insights into the method’s robustness.
* **Limited Comparison to Direct Methods:** The authors acknowledge the need for a systematic comparison with direct methods for training neural network controllers [3, 6, 14]. While they offer an impression that direct methods yield more chaotic behaviors, a quantitative comparison would strengthen the argument for the superiority of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging problem in control with a novel and effective solution.
* The technical details are sound, and the experimental results are compelling.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
* The potential impact of the work is significant.
* The method's generality, demonstrated across diverse character morphologies, is a major strength.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and potential hyperparameter sensitivity may limit wider adoption.
* A more thorough comparison with direct training methods would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an outstanding paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of control and machine learning. The method is technically sound, clearly presented, and demonstrates impressive results. The minor weaknesses do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
