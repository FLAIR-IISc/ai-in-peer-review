

This paper presents a method for training neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse character locomotion tasks, using alternating joint training between Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and neural networks with noise injection techniques. The authors demonstrate successful application across swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion in 3D environments without requiring motion capture or task-specific features.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing an alternating joint training scheme that combines trajectory optimization with neural network regression, alongside noise injection strategies to improve robustness. The approach shows promise in generating stable, realistic control policies across diverse character morphologies, which is a significant advancement over previous work limited to 2D environments.

However, several critical issues undermine the paper's impact. The experimental evaluation suffers from a severely limited training set (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments), weakening claims about generalization and overfitting. Comparative evaluation is superficial, with only a brief MPC comparison provided without proper citation or systematic benchmarking against relevant methods. The mathematical presentation lacks clarity in key sections (particularly CIO formulation and Eq. 6), and Section 3.2 contains confusing notational issues (dropped time index t) that obscure the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure.

The paper also omits important practical details for real-time applications: runtime measurements, scaling with model complexity, and clarification about whether orientation is included in the target. The terminology requires refinement (distinguishing "data augmentation" from "noise injection" and questioning the denoising-autoencoder analogy), and numerous minor writing/notation issues need correction.

While the core idea is promising and well-executed, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive experiments, clearer mathematical exposition, and better contextualization within the literature. The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs substantially from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1 without proper explanation.

Overall, this is an interesting application of neural networks to character control with potential for impact in animation and robotics. However, the current presentation lacks sufficient depth in evaluation and technical justification to fully demonstrate the claimed advantages. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the experimental limitations, providing more comprehensive comparisons, and clarifying the mathematical foundations.
