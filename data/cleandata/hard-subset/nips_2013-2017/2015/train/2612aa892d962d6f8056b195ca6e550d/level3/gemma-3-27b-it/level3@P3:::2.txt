This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for diverse characters in a physics simulator, using trajectory optimization to generate training data. The core idea is to leverage the power of trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) to create a dataset of optimal trajectories and then train a neural network to approximate the resulting control policy. The authors demonstrate this approach on a variety of characters and tasks, including swimming, flying, and walking, claiming the same network architecture performs well across these diverse scenarios.

While the experimental results appear strong, demonstrating stable and realistic behaviors, the paper falls short in clearly positioning its contribution relative to existing work. The approach feels more like an application of deep learning to enhance existing trajectory optimization techniques rather than a fundamentally novel method. Prior work has consistently shown that trajectory optimization can produce impressive control signals, and the authors need to more thoroughly address how their work advances this established area. 

Specifically, the connection to [9] (Levine & Koltun) is unclear. The authors briefly acknowledge related work but don’t detail the differences – or, importantly, comparative metrics – like learning speed, the range of body morphologies supported, runtime computational cost, or robustness to environmental variability. A direct comparison to Abbeel & Levine's work would significantly strengthen the paper.

The discussion of “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” and why this is problematic is also inadequate. The authors state they avoid this issue with their perturbation method, but the core problem isn't explained well enough for a reader unfamiliar with control theory. Why are large, ill-conditioned gains undesirable? What are the practical consequences?

The paper’s organization could also be improved. The justification for not using LQG is presented upfront, before the proposed strategy is even detailed. It would be clearer to first explain the trajectory optimization and neural network approach, *then* discuss why alternative methods like LQG were not suitable.

Furthermore, the notation in lines 226-240 is confusing. The use of both barred symbols (s̄) and squiggly symbols (s(X)) to represent related, but distinct, quantities is visually similar and hinders readability. Clearer notation is crucial.

The claim that “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” needs clarification. What does “softer” mean in this context? Compared to what baseline are the gains softer? The authors state changing the initial state is beneficial, but the reasoning behind this benefit isn’t thoroughly explained. Why does weakening the constraint on matching the initial state lead to more robust control?

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper presents compelling demonstrations of interactive control for diverse characters and tasks. The accompanying video is essential and highlights the quality of the generated behaviors.
* **Sound Methodology:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training is a reasonable approach, and the authors appear to have implemented it carefully.
* **Scalability:**  The use of distributed computing allows the authors to tackle complex problems, demonstrating a practical approach to training these models.
* **Novel Application:** Applying this combination of methods to a wide variety of characters is a valuable contribution, even if the individual components are not entirely new.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Novelty Clarification:**  The core contribution is not clearly articulated relative to existing trajectory optimization and neural policy learning work.
* **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:**  A thorough comparative analysis with prior work, especially [9], is missing.  Metrics like learning speed and scalability should be addressed.
* **Unclear Explanation of Technical Details:** The discussion of feedback gains and the rationale behind parameter λ are insufficiently explained.
* **Organization Issues:** The placement of the discussion regarding LQG is suboptimal.
* **Confusing Notation:** The notation used around lines 226-240 hinders readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a functional and demonstrable system.
* The results are compelling, and the accompanying video is strong evidence of the approach’s success.
* The authors have addressed a challenging problem – creating universal control policies for complex characters.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks a clear articulation of its novelty and significance.
* The insufficient comparison to related work weakens the claims of contribution.
* The unclear explanations of technical details detract from the paper’s overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a sound piece of experimental work, but it needs significant revision before it can be considered a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors must more rigorously position their work within the existing literature, provide a clearer explanation of the technical details, and improve the paper’s organization and clarity. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing these concerns.
