This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time, interactive feedback controller for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core of the approach is an alternating optimization procedure where an offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) generates optimal trajectories for various tasks, and a neural network is then trained via supervised learning to approximate the optimal policy derived from these trajectories. Key aspects of the method include the tight coupling of trajectory optimization and network training, the injection of noise at both the sensory input and hidden unit level to promote robustness, and a novel method for computing optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their unified approach by applying the same architecture to generate stable and realistic locomotion for a diverse set of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers.

This work builds upon a line of research that combines trajectory optimization with learned function approximators to distill computationally expensive offline planning into a fast online policy. In particular, it is closely related to recent work by Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch et al. (2014) that also uses neural networks to learn policies from trajectory optimization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Results (Significance & Quality):** The primary strength of this paper lies in its results. The ability to use a single, general-purpose method and neural network architecture to control such a wide variety of characters with different morphologies and dynamics (walking, trotting, swimming, flying) is a significant achievement. The behaviors shown in the supplementary video are compelling, appearing stable, realistic, and responsive to interactive goals. This demonstrates a substantial step towards automating the creation of versatile character controllers.

2.  **Sound Experimental Design (Quality):** The paper provides a solid experimental evaluation of the proposed method. The ablation studies in Figure 2 compellingly illustrate the necessity of the key components: the joint optimization of trajectories and the policy ("no joint" case) and the noise injection scheme ("no noise" case). These comparisons effectively show that naively training on a static dataset or training without noise leads to poor performance and instability, validating the authors' design choices. The comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) also provides an interesting insight, suggesting the learned policy acts as a regularizer that favors more predictable, cyclic behaviors over the less structured solutions found by MPC.

3.  **Complete System (Quality & Significance):** The paper describes a complete and functional system, from trajectory generation and distributed training to real-time policy execution. This work has clear potential applications in domains like video games and animation, where creating interactive, physically-based characters is a long-standing challenge.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Contribution and Originality:** The paper's main weakness is the insufficient positioning of its contributions relative to very recent and closely related work. While the results are impressive, the specific novelties that enable these results are not clearly articulated.
    *   The core idea of distilling a trajectory optimizer into a neural network policy has been explored before, most notably by Levine & Koltun [9]. The authors claim their method solves "substantially harder problems," but this needs to be substantiated with a more direct and quantitative comparison.
    *   A more thorough discussion and comparison with the work of Levine, Abbeel, and colleagues is required. The review would be much stronger if the authors compared their approach on concrete metrics such as learning speed (e.g., wall-clock time, number of samples), supported character variety, final policy performance, runtime computation cost, and robustness to external perturbations. Without this context, it is difficult for a reader to gauge the precise scientific contribution beyond the impressive demonstration. Is the key innovation the feedback gain calculation, the noise injection scheme, or the distributed architecture? The paper should be more explicit.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** Several key technical aspects require further clarification.
    *   **Feedback Gain Calculation (Section 3.2):** The motivation for the proposed feedback gain calculation method is not fully clear. The authors state that a standard LQG pass can produce "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." This claim needs more detail. What are the practical consequences of such gains (e.g., control instability, extreme sensitivity)? Why does this happen? Providing a more concrete explanation or example would be very helpful.
    *   **Exposition in Section 3.2:** The exposition could be improved. The argument would be clearer if the proposed method for computing feedback gains was presented first, followed by a discussion of why it is preferred over alternatives like LQG. As written, the critique of LQG precedes the solution, which can be confusing.
    *   **Notation (Section 3.2):** The notation in the paragraph describing the perturbation method (around lines 226-240) is confusing. Specifically, `s(X) = \bar{s}` seems to establish an identity or a hard constraint, yet the cost is then defined using `||s(X) - \bar{s}||^2`, which implies `\bar{s}` is a target that `s(X)` should be close to. The use of visually similar symbols for different concepts should be avoided, and the mathematical formulation clarified.
    *   **"Softer" Feedback Gains:** The paper claims the proposed approach "produces softer feedback gains." The term "softer" is ambiguous and should be defined more precisely. Does it mean the gains have a smaller spectral norm? Furthermore, the intuition that allowing the initial state to be modified helps achieve the master cost without aggressive feedback is interesting and should be explained more explicitly.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This paper presents strong experimental work that successfully generates interactive controllers for a diverse range of characters. However, its significant weakness is the lack of a clear and detailed comparison to closely related prior art, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and specific contribution. Several key technical descriptions also lack the clarity needed for full comprehension.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Excellent, compelling results demonstrating a general and powerful method for character control.
*   Addresses a significant and challenging problem in computer graphics and robotics.
*   The method is well-evaluated with informative ablation studies.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The novelty and contribution are not clearly distinguished from recent, highly related work (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014).
*   Key technical claims regarding the feedback gain computation are insufficiently justified and explained.

I am currently on the fence but lean towards acceptance, provided the authors can substantially revise the paper to address the issues of contribution clarity and technical exposition. Clarifying the novelty with respect to prior work is critical for this paper to have a lasting impact.
