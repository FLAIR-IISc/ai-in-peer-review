Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an alternating framework that jointly optimizes trajectories and trains a recurrent neural network policy for interactive control of complex dynamical characters (swimmer, flyer, bipeds, quadrupeds) in 3D. The method interleaves direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of a policy that maps noisy sensory inputs to desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state updates. A key ingredient is injecting sensory noise and training the network to match not only the nominal optimal action but also the local linear feedback a + Asε, where As are optimal feedback gains computed efficiently via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss-Newton Hessian produced by CIO. The overall problem is relaxed with a quadratic penalty and solved via block-alternating updates: (i) optimize trajectories given the current policy and fixed noise; (ii) regress the policy on the noisy state-action targets. Training is implemented asynchronously on a cluster and a single GPU, with proximal regularization to stabilize updates. At execution, the policy outputs desired rates and forces and the next state is obtained by solving a horizon-1 constrained optimization (implicit step) to satisfy dynamics and contacts in real time.

This line of work is closely related to guided policy search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14) and to model-predictive control (MPC). The distinctive aspects here are: (1) using CIO as the trajectory optimizer for highly contact-rich 3D behaviors, (2) computing well-conditioned feedback gains directly from the direct optimizer’s derivatives, (3) explicit noise injection in both inputs and hidden units during policy learning, and (4) an asynchronous distributed training pipeline. The authors compare to MPC qualitatively and ablate (a) joint optimization vs static dataset and (b) noise vs no-noise.

Strengths
- Technically sophisticated and well-engineered system; the breadth of demonstrated 3D characters and stable gaits is impressive, especially without motion capture, state machines, or hand-crafted features.
- Clear empirical evidence that noise injection and joint alternation materially improve performance over strong baselines; the ablations are informative.
- The perturbation-based derivation of feedback gains that reuses the CIO Gauss-Newton inverse is elegant and practically useful; it addresses instability often seen with LQG passes on contact-rich trajectories.
- The asynchronous distributed training scheme with proximal regularization is practical and appears to converge reliably.
- Writing is clear and well organized; the paper is easy to follow and includes helpful implementation details.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Novelty relative to guided policy search: While the engineering contributions and demonstrations in 3D are strong, the high-level idea of alternating trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning is very similar to prior work. The paper would benefit from a crisper articulation of what is fundamentally new (beyond CIO, the feedback gain derivation, and distributed/asynchronous training) and how these enable 3D stability that prior work couldn’t achieve.
- Section 7 execution step: The need to re-optimize a horizon-1 problem at each timestep deserves more motivation. One expects a learned policy to be executable via fast dynamics integration; here, solving an optimization (even if small) adds structure and computational cost. Is this primarily required because (a) the policy outputs desired velocities/forces rather than torques, (b) residual policy regression error necessitates correction, or (c) enforcing contact complementarity stably at large timesteps? Please quantify runtime per step and compare to a simpler scheme (e.g., PD+Euler) on stability/realism. Clarify whether this step ever produces non-physical “root forces” (as acknowledged) and how that affects realism/transfer to robotics.
- Evaluation metrics: The reported “two orders of magnitude better” in Fig. 2 needs precise definitions of train/test metrics, rollout horizon, success criteria, and statistics across seeds. Likewise, include runtime and data efficiency (number of optimized trajectories) versus baselines.
- Generalization and robustness: The branched-goal dataset is sensible, but please report (i) generalization to out-of-distribution goal jumps, (ii) robustness to model mismatch/perturbations, and (iii) the effect of recurrent state r (its dimensionality and learned role).
- Distributed optimization references: For “similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches” (line ~153), please cite relevant proximal/asynchronous work (e.g., Boyd et al. 2011, ADMM; Zhang et al. 2015, Elastic Averaging SGD; Hogwild!-style asynchronous SGD), and briefly justify the choice of proximal regularization for stability in your block-alternating setup.

Quality
- The methodology is technically sound, and the feedback-gain computation is a notable contribution. Claims about the importance of noise and joint training are supported by ablations. However, quantitative evaluation is limited; adding metrics, runtime, and robustness tests would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Very clear and well-organized. Minor areas to clarify: execution-time optimization rationale and cost; exact evaluation protocol; dimensionality of r. Typo fixes below.

Originality
- Moderate. The overall framework follows guided policy search, but the specific instantiation with CIO in 3D, the perturbation-based feedback gains, and the asynchronous proximal training are valuable and practical.

Significance
- High for character animation/graphics and control of contact-rich simulated systems; moderate for general RL/control given conceptual proximity to prior GPS. The breadth of complex 3D results is compelling and likely to be useful to practitioners.

Specific comments and minor issues
- Line 89: “to unify” (typo: currently “to unity”).
- Line ~153: please add 1–2 references for the proximal/asynchronous remark (e.g., Boyd et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2015).
- Sec. 7: Please report per-frame solve times, tolerance, and whether warm starts are used; clarify how often constraints are violated and how large root forces get.
- Typos: “changed changed” (Sec. 8, bird), “opposing” (two-segment wings), consistency in notation for as vs a_s.
- Please report the dimension of recurrent state r and ablate its necessity.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, diverse empirical demonstrations in challenging 3D settings.
- Practical, well-engineered system; clear ablations supporting key design choices.
- Useful, low-cost method to extract stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty over guided policy search is incremental.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and runtime analysis.
- Execution-time optimization step raises questions about pure feedforward deployability and physical realism (root forces).

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Reviewer confidence: 2.5/5 (not deeply familiar with character-control literature; stronger in RL/control).
