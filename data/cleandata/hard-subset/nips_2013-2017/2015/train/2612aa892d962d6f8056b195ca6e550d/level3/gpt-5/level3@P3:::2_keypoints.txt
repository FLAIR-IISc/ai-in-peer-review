- Paper trains small recurrent nets via alternating trajectory optimization (CIO) and supervised regression to produce interactive locomotion across multiple 3D morphologies.
- Key technical contribution: perturbation-based extraction of local feedback gains from the Gauss-Newton Hessian, avoiding an LQG pass.
- Policies output desired velocities, recurrent updates, and contact forces; execution uses a T=1-step optimality condition for real-time control.
- Method is an instance of trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning, closely related to guided policy search and NeuroAnimator.
- Main claimed novelties unclear: scaling to 3D multi-contact characters, the Hessian-based gain extraction, noise-injection with gain targets, or unification across morphologies.
- Strength: solid engineering producing natural cyclic behaviors without hand-crafted state machines or motion capture.
- Strength: sensible alternating training loop with proximal regularization and noise; ablations support design choices.
- Strength: practical, tunable feedback-gain extraction reusing trajectory-optimizer machinery.
- Strength: simple real-time execution via T=1 optimization and an asynchronous distributed implementation with reasonable training time.
- Weakness: insufficient positioning vs prior work; need explicit statement of what is new and comparison to Abbeel/Levine guided policy search variants.
- Weakness: missing quantitative comparisons on sample efficiency, variety of bodies/behaviors, runtime cost, and robustness to disturbances.
- Weakness: vague claim of “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” — needs quantified norms, condition numbers, spectra, and explanations why LQG fails.
- Weakness: unclear definition of “softer” gains and missing plots showing gain norms, stability margins, and task/energy trade-offs versus λ.
- Weakness: exposition flow should present the perturbation gain method before discussing why LQG was not used.
- Weakness: confusing notation around augmented cost and sensory states (lines 226–240); propose clearer symbols and consistency.
- Weakness: ambiguous phrasing that the network learns “the dynamics under an implicit policy”; clarify policy semantics and why outputs are velocities/forces instead of torques.
- Weakness: evaluation lacks quantitative metrics for energy, gait regularity, COM variability, perturbation recovery, and ablations on recurrence size.
- Quality assessment: technically sound with reasonable gain derivation and validated training loop, but claims of generality need quantitative support.
- Clarity assessment: generally well written but hampered by ordering, notation issues, and the “implicit policy” ambiguity; recommend additional diagrams.
- Originality assessment: moderate — combines known components; most original element is the gain extraction tied to CIO.
- Significance: potentially useful to ML/graphics/robotics practitioners, but would be stronger with rigorous comparisons and robustness metrics.
- Pros: effective small recurrent policies across diverse characters; integrated gain computation; real-time control; meaningful ablations.
- Cons: limited novelty relative to guided policy search; no direct quantitative comparisons to related methods or MPC baselines; notation and gain-discussion ambiguities; reliance on soft root forces may reduce realism.
- Recommendation: borderline, leaning Weak Reject unless authors clarify positioning vs guided policy search, add quantitative comparisons/robustness metrics, and fix gain/LQG and notation issues.
