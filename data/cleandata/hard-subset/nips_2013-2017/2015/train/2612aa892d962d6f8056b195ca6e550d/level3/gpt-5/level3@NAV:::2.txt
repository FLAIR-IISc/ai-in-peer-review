This paper interleaves trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised training of a compact recurrent neural policy that outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent updates, yielding interactive controllers for swimming, flying, and legged locomotion across diverse morphologies. The method injects sensor and network noise during training, uses proximal regularization in an asynchronous, distributed setting, and introduces a perturbation-based procedure to extract local feedback gains from the direct optimizer without an LQG pass. At test time, a short-horizon optimization (T=1) tracks the policy’s desired velocities, enabling real-time control. This line sits squarely within model-based control with function approximation, closely related to trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun [9]) and CIO-based synthesis [12], and echoes recent model-free RL policy learning work [14].

Quality: The experimental suite is compelling and the engineering is careful. Ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) substantiate design choices. However, the evaluation lacks direct comparisons to the closest line (Abbeel/Levine and successors): please report standardized metrics—learning speed (wall-clock, samples), runtime cost (Hz on stated hardware, memory footprint), robustness to disturbances and model/terrain variability, and breadth of supported bodies/behaviors—against [9,11] and an MPC baseline. Quantify “interactive” (control frequency, latency) and report failure rates and recovery under perturbations.

Clarity: Move the discussion of why LQG was not used to after the proposed gain-computation method. Clarify “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”: provide norms/condition numbers and examples. Lines 226–240: the notation s(X)=s̄ while the cost uses s(X)−s̄ is confusing; disambiguate bar/tilde and reuse consistent symbols. Define “softer feedback gains according to λ” precisely (e.g., bounds on gain norms, sensitivity of a to s) and justify why allowing initial-state deviation (soft constraint) is beneficial relative to holding it fixed.

Originality: The contribution reads as a strong application of deep learning to trajectory-optimization-based control rather than a fundamentally new algorithm. The perturbation-based gain extraction reused from the CIO Hessian is potentially novel and should be isolated, analyzed, and compared to LQG (stability, gain conditioning, task performance).

Significance: If accompanied by rigorous head-to-head comparisons and robustness analyses, the demonstration that a small, fast network can unify interactive control across diverse bodies would be impactful for graphics/robotics.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid engineering and impressive breadth of behaviors with one architecture
- Sensible training pipeline with informative ablations
- Practical, potentially novel gain-computation procedure

Arguments against acceptance:
- Unclear novelty relative to [9,11]; missing direct comparisons
- Insufficient quantitative metrics (robustness, runtime, conditioning)
- Exposition/notation issues hinder reproducibility and clarity
