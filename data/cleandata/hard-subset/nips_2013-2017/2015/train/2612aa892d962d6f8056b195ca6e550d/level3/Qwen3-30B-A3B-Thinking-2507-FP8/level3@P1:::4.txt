

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped locomotion. The approach combines trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning, enabling interactive real-time control without motion capture or task-specific features. The key innovation lies in the distributed stochastic optimization framework that alternates between trajectory optimization (using Contact-Invariant Optimization) and neural network regression, with noise injection during training to improve robustness.

The paper demonstrates significant technical sophistication, with well-executed experiments across multiple character types in 3D environments. The results are impressive: the same network architecture successfully controls vastly different morphologies (e.g., a swimmer, bird, biped, and quadruped) with natural, stable locomotion. The authors clearly demonstrate the advantages of their approach through ablation studies showing that joint optimization with noise injection yields orders-of-magnitude better performance than naive neural network training on static trajectories.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the technical contributions and thorough experimental validation. The methodological details are sufficiently complete for reproducibility, and the supplementary video effectively demonstrates the results. The authors appropriately reference related work in trajectory optimization, neural network control, and character animation, though they could have more explicitly contrasted with recent RL approaches like those in [9, 11].

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with strong experimental validation. The authors carefully address both strengths (stable locomotion across diverse morphologies) and weaknesses (e.g., the need for trajectory optimization to prevent instability). The training procedure is well-justified, with noise injection and trajectory re-optimization effectively preventing overfitting and enabling robust behavior. The distributed training architecture is practical and efficiently leverages cloud resources.

For originality, the paper presents a novel combination of trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning, though it builds on established techniques. The key contribution is the distributed stochastic optimization framework that enables effective joint learning, which is significantly more scalable than previous approaches. While the core components (trajectory optimization, neural networks) are familiar, their specific integration for interactive control of diverse characters represents a meaningful advance. The paper could strengthen its claim of novelty by more explicitly contrasting with similar work in [9, 11] that addresses 2D characters.

The significance of this work is substantial for character animation and robotics. The ability to generate stable, realistic locomotion for diverse characters using a single, unified framework represents a major step toward automated character control synthesis. The approach could significantly reduce the manual effort required to create complex character behaviors in animation and robotics. However, the paper could better articulate how this advances the state of the art beyond previous trajectory optimization approaches, particularly regarding the theoretical contribution.

The main weaknesses concern the motivation for the trajectory re-optimization step (Section 7). The paper states that "physical state evolution is a result of optimization" but doesn't fully explain why the policy alone cannot generate physically plausible trajectories without this additional step. The reviewer expected the policy to produce actions that could be directly executed without re-optimization. While the paper demonstrates that re-optimization enables larger timesteps and more stable behavior, the necessity of this step isn't fully justified. It would be valuable to have a clearer explanation of whether the residual trajectory cost or policy regression error necessitates re-optimization, or whether dynamics alone would prevent physical implausibility.

Additionally, the paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how the method scales to more complex environments and whether the distributed training approach remains practical for very large-scale problems. The claim that "the heavy lifting is done by the trajectory optimizer" could be better substantiated with a quantitative comparison of computational costs.

The paper contains a few minor issues: a typo at line 89 ("to unify"), and the reference to "recent approaches" at line 153 would be strengthened with one or two specific citations. The motivation for the specific hyperparameters (σε = 10^-2, λ = 10^2) could also be better explained.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that successfully demonstrates a novel framework for interactive control of diverse characters. The method is well-executed, the results are compelling, and the approach has clear potential for practical applications. While the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited (focusing on a practical implementation of a known concept), the engineering sophistication and empirical results are impressive. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of character animation and control, particularly for its ability to handle diverse morphologies with a single unified framework. The quality of the implementation, the thorough experimental validation, and the clear presentation make this a worthy contribution to NIPS. The minor weaknesses in motivation and justification don't detract from the overall strength of the work, which represents a significant practical advance in the field.
