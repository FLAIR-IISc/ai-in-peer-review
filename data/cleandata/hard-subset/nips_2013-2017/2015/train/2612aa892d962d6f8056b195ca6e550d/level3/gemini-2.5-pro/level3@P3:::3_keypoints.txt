- Paper proposes training an RNN policy to mimic an offline trajectory optimizer (CIO) via alternating joint optimization and two-level noise injection (sensory augmentation + hidden-layer noise) for real-time controllers.  
- Key contributions: a novel co-adaptive training scheme and principled use of noise to improve robustness.  
- Results: produces stable, plausible, and emergent gaits for diverse morphologies (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) using a single framework.  
- Advances prior work by scaling trajectory-optimization + function-approximation approaches from 2D to more unstable 3D characters.  

Strengths
- Novel training scheme: alternating joint optimization lets the policy influence its training data and is crucial for stability (the "no joint" baseline fails).  
- Effective noise strategy: using the optimizer to compute feedback for perturbed states plus hidden-layer noise improves test-time robustness and reduces overfitting.  
- Generality: a unified algorithm produces natural, cyclic locomotion across multiple character types without handcrafted gait encoding.  

Weaknesses / experimental suggestions
- Comparative evaluation is limited; MPC comparison is anecdotal and lacks citations—needs systematic benchmarking against state-of-the-art continuous-control methods (e.g., RL algorithms).  
- Small training set (100–200 trials) raises questions about generalization; performance scaling with more data should be studied.  
- Ablation study lacks granularity—separate effects of data augmentation vs hidden-layer noise should be evaluated.  
- Missing runtime and scaling metrics: no policy execution time, per-character training time breakdown, or how performance scales with DOFs—important for real-time claims.  

Clarity & reproducibility issues
- Paper is not self-contained: CIO formulation, constraints in Eq.6, and derivation of feedback gains are underspecified or confusing, hindering reproducibility.  
- Inconsistency between algorithm description and implementation: distributed/asynchronous training in Sec.6 differs from synchronous Algorithm 1 and needs clarification.  
- No code release: given complexity, providing source code is important for reproducibility and adoption.  

Detailed questions / minor issues
- Clarify goal specification: is target orientation part of the cost and policy I/O?  
- Justify hyperparameter choices (e.g., same proximal regularization eta for both updates) and explain how initial trajectories X are computed before theta training.  
- Terminology: the denoising-autoencoder analogy is misleading; better distinguish "data augmentation" (noisy inputs) from "noise injection" (hidden activations).  
- Writing and notation need proofreading and fixes (typos, missing definitions, inconsistent notation and acronyms).  

Overall assessment
- Promising and well-executed approach with significant ideas (joint optimization, principled noise injection) and impressive results, but weakened by limited comparisons, clarity/reproducibility gaps, and missing performance data; addressing these would make it a strong contribution.
