

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion). The authors propose a technique that interleaves supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate training data for neural network controllers. The key insight is that the network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously, with the action defined as velocity rather than a traditional control policy. The approach demonstrates impressive results across multiple character types in 3D without requiring motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound method that produces stable, realistic behaviors across diverse character morphologies. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion in 3D environments. The use of trajectory optimization to generate training data is a clever solution to the data scarcity problem in control learning. However, the paper requires full model knowledge (equation 6), which is a significant limitation for real-world applications where complete physical models are often unavailable. The experimental results support the claims, but the method's dependency on precise dynamics models reduces its applicability.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written but suffers from structural issues. Sections 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) and 6 (Distributed Training Architecture) are disproportionately long compared to other sections, making the paper unbalanced. More critically, Section 4 fails to address the recurrent units referenced in the abstract, creating a disconnect between the stated approach and the described method. The description of the network as "stochastic" (line 405) is misleading since the network itself is deterministic despite using stochastic training data. The paper would benefit from a more focused presentation that aligns with the abstract's claims.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by combining trajectory optimization with neural network training to solve complex 3D locomotion problems. While similar approaches exist (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014, Mordatch & Todorov 2012), this work demonstrates substantially harder problems—achieving stable 3D locomotion where previous work was limited to 2D. The method unifies disparate control approaches for different character types (bipedal, quadrupedal, flying, swimming), which is a notable advance in the field.

**Significance**: The results are significant as they demonstrate a generalizable approach to character control that works across diverse morphologies without specialized controllers. The method could impact robotics, animation, and biomechanics by providing a unified framework for interactive character control. However, the requirement for full model knowledge limits the practical significance for real-world robotics applications where precise dynamics models are often unavailable. The paper's value is primarily in demonstrating the feasibility of this approach for complex animation tasks.

**Strengths**: 
- Demonstrates stable 3D locomotion across diverse character types
- Unifies disparate control approaches for different character morphologies
- Provides a principled way to inject noise for robustness
- Shows clear comparison to ablations (no joint optimization, no noise injection)

**Weaknesses**: 
- Requires full model knowledge (equation 6), limiting real-world applicability
- Structural imbalance with disproportionate coverage of Sections 4 and 6
- Fails to mention recurrent units in Section 4 despite their prominence in the abstract
- Misleading description of the network as "stochastic" when it's deterministic

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
*Pro*: Strong experimental results across multiple character types, significant methodological contribution to unifying control approaches, well-motivated combination of trajectory optimization with neural networks.

*Con*: Critical limitation of requiring full model knowledge, structural issues in the paper, misleading description of network properties, and failure to address recurrent units in the main method description.

**Recommendation**: The paper presents promising results that advance the state of the art in character control, but the structural issues and critical limitation regarding model knowledge need to be addressed. The authors should restructure the paper to properly address the recurrent units in the method description, clarify the "stochastic" network claim, and reduce the disproportionate length of Sections 4 and 6. If these issues are resolved, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As currently written, it is borderline for acceptance due to the model knowledge limitation and structural issues.

Given the promising results and significant methodological contribution, I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the clarity and structural issues. The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field if the authors refine the presentation and address the critical limitations.
