

This paper proposes a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse character locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) in physics simulators. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (via Contact-Invariant Optimization) with neural network regression, using interleaved supervised learning and trajectory optimization to generate robust controllers. The network learns state-action mappings where actions represent desired velocities (not control policies), with a small recurrent component for memory. Key innovations include noise injection during training, trajectory optimization for feedback gains, and a distributed training framework.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**  
The work builds on trajectory-optimization-based control methods like CIO [12] and prior neural policy learning [9, 11]. While [11] demonstrated 2D locomotion, this paper extends to 3D with diverse morphologies (swimmers, birds, bipeds, quadrupeds). It positions itself as "unifying disparate control approaches" (e.g., state machines for bipeds vs. cyclic encodings for flying), but the core technique—using neural networks to approximate precomputed trajectories—aligns closely with Levine & Koltun [9]. The claim of "novel combination" requires stronger differentiation from existing work, especially regarding the use of noise and feedback gains.

**Strengths**  
1. **Quality**: The method is technically sound. Ablation studies (Fig. 2, Table 1) convincingly show that joint optimization + noise injection is essential for stability (vs. static trajectory training or no noise). The distributed training architecture (Sec. 6) enables scalable execution (2.5h on 30 nodes), and the approach achieves stable, realistic behaviors across diverse tasks without task-specific features.  
2. **Significance**: The empirical results are compelling—same architecture controls swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion. This demonstrates practical utility for animators and robotics, addressing a key challenge in character control (e.g., "interactive" goal changes via trajectory branching).  
3. **Clarity**: The algorithm (Algorithm 1) and training pipeline (Sec. 5–6) are well-structured. The supplementary video effectively illustrates the diverse behaviors.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Originality**: The central contribution is under-justified. Prior work [9, 11] already uses neural networks to approximate trajectory-optimized policies for locomotion. The novelty relative to [9] (which also uses trajectory optimization + neural networks) is unclear. The authors should explicitly compare metrics (learning speed, supported body types, robustness to noise) with [9] and [11]. The claim that their method "solves substantially harder problems" (3D vs. 2D) needs quantification (e.g., success rates, computational cost).  
2. **Clarity**: Notation around optimal feedback gains (Sec. 3.2) is confusing (e.g., $\bar{s}$ vs. $s$). The justification for avoiding LQG (Sec. 3.2) should be moved *after* presenting their perturbation method for better flow. The phrase "softer feedback gains" lacks definition—*softer* relative to what? Why does weakening the initial-state constraint improve robustness?  
3. **Technical Depth**: The claim that LQG produces "very large and ill-conditioned gains" is unsubstantiated. The authors should clarify *why* this is problematic (e.g., numerical instability) and how their method avoids it. The noise injection rationale (Sec. 2.1) is underdeveloped—how does it differ from standard dropout or data augmentation?  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pros*:  
- Demonstrates a practical, scalable method for diverse locomotion tasks.  
- Strong empirical validation across 4 character types (3D, complex dynamics).  
- Ablation studies rigorously validate key design choices (noise, joint optimization).  
- Distributed training implementation is well-executed.  

*Cons*:  
- Fails to adequately distinguish contribution from [9, 11], undermining novelty.  
- Critical technical claims (ill-conditioned gains, "softer" gains) lack justification.  
- Notation and exposition (Sec. 3.2) hinder reproducibility.  
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art neural control methods (e.g., MPC, [14]).  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper presents a solid, well-executed application of neural networks to control synthesis, with strong empirical results. However, the contribution is incremental relative to recent NIPS work (e.g., [9] in ICML 2014). The core technique—learning neural policies from trajectory-optimized data—is not novel, and the paper does not sufficiently clarify *how* it advances beyond prior art. The method’s robustness and versatility are impressive, but without a clearer positioning against existing literature, the significance is diminished.  

**Recommendation**: **Weak Reject**. The work is technically sound and significant for practitioners, but the lack of novelty relative to recent trajectory-optimization-based neural control work (e.g., [9]) and insufficient justification for key claims prevent it from meeting NIPS’s bar for originality. The authors should: (1) explicitly compare with [9] on metrics like task diversity, learning speed, and robustness; (2) clarify the "softer gains" claim with a concrete comparison; (3) revise Sec. 3.2 for notation clarity and logical flow. With these revisions, the paper could be strengthened for acceptance.
