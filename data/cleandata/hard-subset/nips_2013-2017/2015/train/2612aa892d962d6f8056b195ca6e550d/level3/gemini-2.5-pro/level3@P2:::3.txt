This paper presents a method for training a single recurrent neural network architecture to serve as a real-time feedback controller for a diverse set of physically simulated characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate high-quality motion data. Crucially, instead of a simple one-way transfer of knowledge, the authors propose a joint optimization scheme where the neural network policy and the reference trajectories are iteratively and alternately refined. The system's robustness is enhanced by injecting noise at two key points: as data augmentation on the sensory inputs (requiring the computation of optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer) and directly to the hidden unit activations during network training. This approach is related to recent work on combining trajectory optimization and function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch et al., 2014), but extends it to more complex 3D locomotion tasks, yielding stable and realistic gaits without task-specific state machines or features.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Generality:** The paper tackles the challenging and important problem of creating general-purpose, real-time controllers for complex dynamical systems. The ability to use a single, unified algorithm to generate stable and natural-looking locomotion for characters with vastly different morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) is a significant strength and a step towards more automated controller design. The results, particularly in the supplementary video, are impressive.
2.  **Novelty of the Training Method:** The main contribution is the joint, alternating optimization framework. The ablation study (Figure 2) convincingly demonstrates that this approach is far superior to a naive supervised learning baseline where the network is trained on a static dataset of optimized trajectories. This highlights that simply learning to mimic an optimizer is insufficient for stable closed-loop control and that co-adapting the trajectories and the policy is essential.
3.  **Effective Use of Noise:** The principled injection of noise is another key element contributing to the method's success. The data augmentation technique, which uses optimal feedback gains to compute the correct target action for a perturbed state, is a clever way to teach the network local robustness. This, combined with noise in the hidden layers, effectively regularizes the policy and prevents the catastrophic divergence common in learned controllers.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While the overall idea is clear, several key sections lack the detail needed for full comprehension and reproducibility.
    *   **Section 3.2 (Optimal Feedback Gains):** This section is confusing. The derivation drops the time index `t`, making it unclear how the perturbation `s(X) = s̄` relates to a single timestep. A more explicit, step-by-step explanation of how a perturbation at time `t` affects the optimal action `a_t` would be very helpful.
    *   **Section 3 (Trajectory Optimization):** The paper relies heavily on CIO but provides little detail about the cost function `C(X)` and the constraints in Eq. (6). While citing the original work is appropriate, a brief, self-contained summary of the key cost terms (e.g., torque minimization, goal achievement) and constraints would greatly improve the paper's accessibility.
    *   **Algorithm vs. Implementation:** The asynchronous distributed architecture described in Section 6 appears to differ from the strictly alternating scheme of Algorithm 1. This discrepancy should be acknowledged and discussed, explaining why the asynchronous updates are stable and how they relate to the presented algorithm.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation could be stronger in several areas.
    *   **Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a systematic comparison to other state-of-the-art learning-based control methods (e.g., recent policy gradient or actor-critic methods). The qualitative comparison to an uncited MPC method is insufficient to properly position the work in the field.
    *   **Ablation Study:** The ablation is good but could be more granular. The "no noise" experiment removes both sensory and hidden unit noise. Separating these two effects would provide a clearer understanding of their individual contributions.
    *   **Training Data:** The claim of robust generalization is based on a seemingly small training set (100-200 trials with 5 segments each). It would be useful to know if the "no joint" baseline was trained on a dataset of comparable size and diversity, as its poor performance might be partially explained by overfitting to a limited static dataset.
    *   **Performance Metrics:** For a method targeting real-time interactive applications, details on runtime performance are essential. The paper should report the execution time of the policy and discuss how it scales with the complexity of the character model and network architecture.

### Questions for the Authors

*   In Algorithm 1, what is the initial state of the trajectories `X` before the first optimization step? Are they initialized randomly, or from a preliminary CIO run with the initial `θ`?
*   The regularization parameter `η` is used for both the trajectory and policy updates. What is the justification for using the same value? Have you explored tuning these separately?
*   Does the locomotion task goal include a target orientation (facing angle) in addition to position? If so, how is this handled? If not, how difficult would it be to extend the method?
*   Could you clarify your use of terminology? The injection of noise into sensory inputs is a form of data augmentation, while noise in hidden units is a regularization technique. The analogy to denoising autoencoders seems slightly inexact, as here the noise changes both the input and the target output.
*   Have you considered an annealing schedule for the penalty weight `α`? It seems plausible that one could gradually reduce the reliance on the CIO targets, allowing the network to find its own stable solutions.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and effective framework for a very difficult problem. The results are compelling and demonstrate a significant advance in generating unified controllers for diverse characters. The core idea of joint optimization is well-motivated and empirically validated through a strong ablation study.
*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The clarity of the technical exposition needs significant improvement, particularly in the description of the feedback gain calculation. The experimental evaluation is limited by a lack of comparison to other learning-based methods and missing performance metrics, which slightly weakens the claims of state-of-the-art performance and practical utility.

Overall, this is a strong paper with impressive results and a solid core contribution. The weaknesses are primarily in the clarity of the presentation and the breadth of the experimental evaluation. If the authors can address the clarity issues and strengthen the evaluation, this would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
