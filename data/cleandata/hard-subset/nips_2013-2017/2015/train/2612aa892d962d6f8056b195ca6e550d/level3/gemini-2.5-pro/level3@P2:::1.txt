This paper presents a powerful and general method for training recurrent neural network policies to serve as real-time feedback controllers for complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage the power of offline trajectory optimization to generate training data for the neural network. The authors propose a joint optimization framework that alternates between optimizing a set of trajectories (using Contact-Invariant Optimization) and regressing a neural network policy onto the data generated from these trajectories.

This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with neural network function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). However, it makes several crucial contributions that enable it to solve substantially more difficult control problems, demonstrating stable and realistic locomotion for a diverse set of 3D characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers). Key technical innovations include a novel method for extracting and incorporating optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer into the training process. This allows the network to learn a robust local policy, not just a mapping from states to open-loop actions. Furthermore, noise is injected at both the sensory input and hidden unit levels during training to improve generalization and prevent divergence during policy execution.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of exceptionally high quality and technically sound. The proposed algorithm, which softly couples trajectory optimization and policy learning via a penalty term in a block-alternating scheme, is well-motivated and elegant. The claims are strongly supported by impressive experimental results, as shown in the supplementary video, which are a clear testament to the method's power and generality. The ablation study in Figure 2 provides convincing evidence for the importance of the two main contributions: the joint optimization framework and the principled injection of noise. The work is presented as a complete and polished contribution, from the theoretical formulation to the distributed implementation and extensive evaluation.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of first presenting the high-level idea and then delving into the technical details of each component. The notation is clear and consistent. The problem formulation in Eq. (1) and its subsequent relaxation are presented with great clarity. The explanation of how optimal feedback gains are calculated as a byproduct of the direct trajectory optimization (Section 3.2) is particularly insightful. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

*   **Originality:** While the general concept of distilling an optimizer into a policy network exists, this work introduces significant novelties. The primary original contribution is the method for embedding optimal feedback gains into the supervised learning target (Eq. 2). This elegantly transforms the learning problem from simple behavioral cloning to learning a local, linear feedback controller around the optimal trajectory, which is key to achieving the demonstrated robustness. This is a clever way to extract more information from the trajectory optimizer than just the optimal state-action pairs. Furthermore, applying a unified framework to synthesize controllers for such a wide variety of morphologies and gaits in 3D is a novel and impressive achievement.

*   **Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant and represent a major step forward in automated controller synthesis. The ability to generate robust, interactive controllers for complex characters from only a physical model and a high-level task description has profound implications for computer animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The work demonstrates that it is possible to generate natural, cyclic gaits (e.g., walking, trotting) as an emergent property of the optimization, without needing motion capture data or hand-crafted state machines. The comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Figure 3, which suggests the policy network acts as a regularizer that encourages simpler, more predictable solutions, is a very interesting finding. This paper is likely to be highly influential and inspire a great deal of future work.

**Weaknesses**

The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for improvement rather than significant flaws.

*   The computational cost of the training process is substantial, requiring hours on a distributed cloud computing setup. The paper correctly notes that "the heavy lifting is done by the trajectory optimizer." While the end result is a fast real-time policy, the high upfront cost of training may limit its accessibility.
*   The paper mentions that a systematic comparison with direct policy search methods (i.e., reinforcement learning) remains to be done. While the qualitative comparison is insightful, a quantitative comparison on one of the tasks against a modern RL algorithm like TRPO would have further contextualized the contribution.
*   The policy execution step (Section 7) involves solving a small optimization problem at each timestep. While this is certainly faster than full trajectory optimization, it would be helpful to state the typical computation time per step to provide a clearer picture of the real-time performance.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and highly effective method for training robust, real-time neural network controllers for a wide array of complex 3D characters.
2.  The core technical contribution—embedding optimal feedback gains into the learning process—is a significant and original idea that greatly improves policy robustness.
3.  The experimental results are state-of-the-art, demonstrating an unprecedented level of generality and quality in automatically synthesized character animation.
4.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and the work is of very high technical quality.
5.  The work has high potential for significant impact across multiple fields, including graphics, robotics, and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The method is computationally very demanding, which could be a barrier to adoption.
2.  A direct quantitative comparison to contemporary reinforcement learning methods is missing.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that presents a significant methodological advance with truly impressive results. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It represents a landmark contribution to the problem of learning complex motor skills.
