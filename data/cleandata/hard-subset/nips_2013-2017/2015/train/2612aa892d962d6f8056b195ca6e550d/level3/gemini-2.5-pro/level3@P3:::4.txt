This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural network policies for the interactive control of complex, physically-simulated characters. The core contribution is a framework that jointly learns a set of optimal trajectories and a control policy that can reproduce those trajectories. The approach effectively combines the power of offline trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization) for generating high-quality, physically plausible motions with the real-time performance of a neural network controller. The authors formulate this as a large constrained optimization problem and propose an effective solution that decouples it into alternating, independent subproblems: optimizing the trajectories to satisfy task costs while matching the current policy, and then performing supervised regression to update the policy network based on the newly optimized trajectories. This work builds on recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with policy learning but demonstrates successful application to a significantly more challenging and diverse set of 3D characters and locomotion tasks, including swimming, flying, and bipedal/quadrupedal walking.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality and Significance:** The most significant strength of this paper is the impressive quality and diversity of the results. The method successfully synthesizes stable, realistic, and interactive controllers for a range of characters with very different morphologies and dynamics (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) from a single, unified framework. This is a substantial practical achievement that advances the state-of-the-art in character animation and control. The technical sophistication of the system, from the optimization formulation to the distributed implementation, is very high.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper's claims are well-supported by thorough empirical evaluation. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 clearly and convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the joint optimization approach compared to training on a static dataset, and they effectively show that injecting sensory noise during training is critical for achieving good generalization and avoiding the divergence of the policy at runtime.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of presenting a complex methodology in a structured and understandable manner. The problem is well-motivated, the algorithm is broken down into logical components, and the text provides appropriate detail and useful cross-references, making it relatively easy to follow the overall approach.

**Weaknesses and Questions**

*   **Originality:** While the engineering and results are top-notch, the paper's primary contribution seems to be a novel and highly effective combination of existing techniques rather than the introduction of new fundamental theory. The core idea is an empirical one: that decoupling the problem into alternating trajectory optimization and supervised learning subproblems works remarkably well and scales to complex domains. As a reviewer not deeply familiar with the specifics of the character animation literature, I cannot definitively judge the novelty against all prior work. However, from a machine learning perspective, it reads as a very strong systems and application paper rather than one offering new theoretical insights into policy learning or optimization.
*   **Motivation for Policy Execution Step:** I found the motivation for the extra optimization step during policy execution (Section 7) to be insufficiently explained. After training a policy, one would typically expect to use it directly to command the system. Instead, the authors use the policy's output as a target for a T=1 trajectory optimization at each timestep. Could the authors please elaborate on why this is necessary? Is it to correct for residual error from the policy regression, ensuring the final action is perfectly physically plausible? Or is it primarily a computational trick to enable stable simulation with larger timesteps, as briefly mentioned? A clearer explanation of this design choice would strengthen the paper.
*   **Minor Points:**
    1.  Line 89: There is a typo: "to unity" should likely be "to unify".
    2.  Line 153: The text states that adding proximal regularization terms is "similar to recent approaches". It would be helpful to include one or two specific citations here for context. While keeping updates small in parameter space can seem counterintuitive for fast optimization, it is an understandable strategy to ensure stability in a block-alternating scheme, and referencing prior work would support this.

**Overall Assessment**

Overall, this is a strong paper with impressive results and a clear presentation. The authors have developed and demonstrated a powerful system for creating interactive controllers for a wide variety of complex characters. The work is a complete and well-executed piece of research that makes a significant practical contribution. My main reservation concerns the degree of conceptual novelty, but the practical significance for researchers and practitioners in computer graphics, robotics, and reinforcement learning is likely to be high. I lean towards acceptance.
