- Presents a method to train neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers via interleaved trajectory optimization and neural policy regression for diverse physical characters.

- Key innovation: jointly optimize trajectories and a neural policy using distributed stochastic optimization, with noise injection and learned feedback gains for robust real-time control.

- Single network architecture produces stable, realistic behaviors across multiple morphologies without task-specific features or motion capture.

- Strong experiments: generates diverse cyclic 3D locomotion (bird flapping, quadruped trotting, biped walking) and outperforms naive neural training by ~100× (Fig. 2).

- Ablations show noise injection and joint trajectory-policy optimization are necessary for stability.

- Distributed training scales efficiently (30 optimization nodes per GPU) and policy execution is real-time (50 ms timesteps).

- Advances practical utility in character animation and robotics by unifying control across different character types.

- Main novelty is incremental relative to prior work (Levine & Koltun ICML 2014; Mordatch & Todorov TOG 2012) that also used trajectory optimization to train neural policies.

- Paper lacks direct comparisons to prior neural policy methods on key metrics (learning speed, supported body variety, runtime cost).

- Claim that LQG yields “very large, ill-conditioned feedback gains” is asserted without concrete justification or analysis of how that causes divergence.

- The proposed perturbation method’s mechanism for mitigating ill-conditioned gains is not clearly explained or empirically compared to LQG alternatives.

- Explanation of “softer feedback gains” and why weakening the initial-state constraint improves stability is vague and misplaced in the text.

- Notation is confusing (e.g., \bar{s} vs. \tilde{s{}}, lines 226–240), hindering reproducibility.

- Methodology does not establish a new theoretical framework; significance is mainly practical rather than conceptual.

- “Universal controller” claim overstates results — training still requires trajectory optimization per character and generalization to unseen perturbations is unproven.

- Writing is generally clear but key technical details (trajectory optimization, feedback gain derivation, noise rationale) lack sufficient motivation and control-theory context (e.g., Lyapunov).

- Ablation studies are strong evidence for components but do not contextualize novelty relative to prior art.

- MPC comparison is insightful but needs expanded discussion explaining why cyclical behaviors emerge versus non-cyclical MPC outputs.

- Recommendation: Weak reject — technically sound and experimentally strong but not novel enough for NIPS without revisions.

- Required revisions suggested: quantify improvements over prior work, clarify and justify the ill-conditioned gain critique and perturbation solution, resolve notation ambiguities, and reorganize presentation of the LQG critique.
