Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an offline-to-online pipeline for real-time character control that alternates between trajectory optimization (CIO) and neural network (NN) regression. The NN is trained to reproduce CIO solutions under noisy inputs; training alternates between re-optimizing trajectories with a penalty to match the current NN and re-fitting the NN to the (noisy) CIO outputs. A key ingredient is injecting Gaussian noise both on the inputs (data augmentation) and within hidden layers (noise injection), coupled with using local feedback gains (computed efficiently from the CIO Hessian via a perturbation method) to generate action targets a + As ε for noisy inputs. The approach yields recurrent policies that generate realistic, stable behaviors across diverse 3D systems (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds) without motion capture or state machines. This line of work builds on CIO [Mordatch et al. TOG’12], guided policy learning via trajectory optimization [Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14], and contrasts with direct deep RL (e.g., TRPO, DQN), as well as MPC.

Strengths
- Clear, compelling formulation: alternating joint optimization that ties NN training to a trajectory optimizer, with proximal regularization and stochastic perturbations.
- Technical novelty in practice: the perturbation-based computation of feedback gains from a direct optimizer is elegant, efficient, and avoids ill-conditioned LQG passes.
- Robustness via two complementary noise mechanisms: noisy inputs with corresponding action corrections (local linearization) and hidden-layer noise for regularization.
- Demonstrated breadth: a single architecture handles 3D swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion with sensible gaits.
- Practical system: asynchronous distributed implementation; fast offline training (hours) and interactive execution.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited evaluation set and size: 100–200 trials with 5 branches per trial is small for claims about generalization; baselines risk being underpowered.
- Comparative evaluation is weak: only a brief, uncited MPC comparison; no systematic comparisons to guided policy search, TRPO, or model-based RL.
- Missing runtime/scaling: no measurements of online control cost per step, CPU/GPU budgets, or scaling with DOFs/contacts—critical for real-time use (e.g., games).
- Some mathematical exposition lacks details: parts of CIO (Eq. 6) and the gain-derivation section (3.2) omit definitions and drop time indices, making reproduction harder.
- Asynchrony vs. alternation: Sec. 6’s asynchronous scheme differs substantially from Alg. 1’s alternating block updates, yet this divergence is not discussed until late.
- Ablations could be more granular: “no joint” and “no noise” are helpful, but “no sensory noise” vs. “no hidden noise” should be separated; more systematic studies of λ, α, η would strengthen claims.
- Reproducibility: no code or data release; several notational issues and typos hinder clarity.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a neat use of proximal terms and a thoughtful method for extracting stabilizing gains from CIO. Claims about generalization and robustness are plausible but not fully supported by the small dataset and limited baselines. Presentation gaps (definitions in Eq. 6; Section 3.2 notation) impede full reproducibility.

Clarity
The high-level algorithm and motivation are clear, but several details need tightening:
- Eq. 6: define all symbols (H, C, J, K, d) precisely and state how soft constraints are implemented.
- Section 3.2: keep the time index t; specify whether sX and aX are block-Jacobians per time or aggregated over the horizon; clarify the dependence of a on Xt and how As is assembled.
- Algorithm 1: state θ initialization explicitly; explain how initial Xi are obtained in the first round; clarify why the same η is used in both subproblems and whether different η’s were tried.
- Terminology: distinguish data augmentation (sensory noise + linearized target) from hidden-layer noise injection; the denoising autoencoder analogy is only partial since the target changes with ε.
- Task specification: clarify whether the target includes facing angle/orientation (later text suggests yes); discuss whether adding a target heading is straightforward.

Originality
Alternating joint training with CIO has precedents, but this work contributes: (i) an efficient gain-computation technique for direct trajectory optimizers; (ii) a combined noise-in-inputs-and-hidden-units recipe tailored to closed-loop control; and (iii) diverse 3D results without motion capture. The overall combination and the practical system are original and useful.

Significance
If broadly validated, the method could be impactful for animation and robotics where real-time, stylistically regular behaviors are needed. However, significance is tempered by limited comparisons and the absence of performance/scaling data that practitioners need.

Detailed comments and questions
- MPC comparison: please cite and detail the MPC setup, horizon, costs, and solver; include quantitative metrics (success rate, energy, stability).
- Baselines: compare against guided policy search (Levine & Koltun), TRPO, and a simple PID/state-machine baseline where applicable.
- Runtime: report average control step time, CPU threads, solver iterations, and how cost grows with DOFs/contacts; include a breakdown for the T=1 inner optimization.
- Data size and overfitting: expand the training set or show cross-character/goal generalization; report training vs. test errors and rollout success rates under perturbations.
- Ablations: separate “no sensory noise” and “no hidden noise;” study sensitivity to α (penalty weight), η (proximal), λ (softness in gain computation), and σε, σγ.
- Annealing α: does annealing the penalty weight to zero (curriculum) help or hurt? Is a permanent penalty necessary to regularize CIO toward NN-compatible solutions?
- Algorithm choices: are initial Xi computed with θ random or set to a neutral policy? Why keep η identical across subproblems; did different ηθ, ηX improve stability?
- Section 6: explain earlier how the asynchronous implementation departs from strict alternation and why proximal terms suffice for stability; include convergence diagnostics.
- Reproducibility: releasing code/data (or at least the CIO gain computation and small models) would greatly aid adoption.
- Writing/notation: fix typos (“unity”→“unify”, missing articles, misplaced stars), ensure consistent acronym order, and correct references to variables/equations.

Pros
- Practical, well-motivated approach that yields stable, realistic behaviors across diverse 3D characters.
- Elegant reuse of CIO computations to obtain feedback gains.
- Clear evidence that joint training and noise improve robustness over naïve supervised fitting.

Cons
- Limited dataset and weak baselines leave generalization claims under-supported.
- Missing runtime/scaling data and code hinder practical adoption.
- Some derivations and notation are unclear; asynchronous-versus-alternating discrepancy is under-explained.

Overall assessment
A promising and carefully engineered application of NNs to character control with thoughtful algorithmic contributions. With stronger comparisons, clearer math, runtime/scaling reporting, and more granular ablations (plus code), this could be a solid contribution. As is, I view it as borderline accept, leaning positive given the novelty and breadth of results.
