## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for characters in a physics simulator. The core idea is to leverage trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) to generate training data for a neural network policy, enabling real-time interactive control across diverse body types and behaviors like swimming, flying, and walking. The authors demonstrate successful control of several simulated characters without relying on motion capture or hand-designed state machines.

The work builds upon the established connection between trajectory optimization and control, and specifically extends recent efforts [9, 11] to more complex, 3D locomotion tasks. The authors address challenges like overfitting and instability through noise injection and a carefully designed training procedure. The distributed training architecture is also a practical contribution, allowing for efficient data generation and network training. 

However, the paper’s positioning within the existing literature is a significant weakness. While the authors acknowledge related work, they don’t sufficiently articulate the novelty of their approach. The paper feels more like an *application* of trajectory optimization to train neural network controllers, rather than a fundamentally new method. Prior work has already demonstrated the effectiveness of trajectory optimization for generating realistic control signals, and the use of neural networks to approximate step-by-step policy actions is not entirely novel (e.g., [9]). 

A crucial comparison is missing: a detailed evaluation against the work of Abbeel and Levine and their successors. The authors should provide metrics like learning speed, the range of supported body morphologies, runtime computational cost, and robustness to environmental variability. The claim regarding "softer" feedback gains (parameter λ) needs clarification – what does "softer" mean in this context, and why is changing the initial state beneficial? Furthermore, the discussion of why LQG was not used would be clearer if presented *after* the proposed strategy is introduced. 

Minor issues include confusing notation around lines 226-240 (the use of bars and squiggles is visually similar and should be disambiguated). The explanation of the “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” is also somewhat vague.

**Overall:** This is a sound experimental work demonstrating the feasibility of using trajectory optimization to train neural network controllers for complex character animation. However, the paper needs substantial revision to better clarify its contribution relative to existing literature and address the points raised above.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Demonstrates a working system for interactive control of diverse characters.
* Presents a practical distributed training architecture.
* Addresses challenges of overfitting and instability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Lacks a clear articulation of novelty.
* Insufficient comparison to related work, particularly Abbeel and Levine’s line of research.
* Unclear explanations of key concepts (e.g., “softer” feedback gains).
* Notation issues.
