Summary and relation to prior work
This paper presents a method for learning real-time controllers for diverse 3D characters (swimmer, bird, biped, quadrupeds) by alternating trajectory optimization with neural network policy regression. The approach assumes full knowledge of the system dynamics, constraints, and contacts (Eq. 6), implemented via MuJoCo, with dynamics enforced as soft constraints. The core idea is to (i) generate a large set of optimized trajectories via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), (ii) inject sensor noise and use a novel perturbation-based computation of optimal local feedback gains around each trajectory (Section 3.2), and (iii) train a neural network (with a small recurrent state) by regression to match noisy inputs and corresponding optimal actions plus linear feedback. Policy execution uses the learned network to produce desired velocities/forces and then solves a one-step constrained optimization (T=1) to advance the state, enabling stable interactive control at 20 Hz.

The work is closely related to guided policy search and learning-from-optimizer approaches [9, 11], as well as real-time control with RNNs [6], and complements model-free RL approaches such as TRPO [14] and DQN [10] (on discrete domains). In graphics/control, it builds on CIO [12] and contrasts with state-machine and template-based controllers (e.g., SIMBICON [21], ZMP/capture-point methods [18]). The technical novelty lies primarily in the coupling of CIO with a denoising-style training objective that uses analytically obtained local feedback gains, plus an asynchronous distributed training setup.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The alternating optimization is well motivated and standard; the derivation of feedback gains by reusing the Gauss-Newton system and Woodbury identity (Section 3.2) is neat and practically useful. The denoising-style training objective (Eq. 3) and proximal regularization are reasonable.
- Assumptions and scope: It should be stated upfront that the method requires full, differentiable model knowledge, contact geometry, and friction cones (Eq. 6). This is not model-free RL; performance under model mismatch is not evaluated.
- Empirical support: Qualitative results are compelling across multiple morphologies, and an ablation on noise injection/joint optimization plus a small architecture sweep are provided. However, quantitative evaluation is limited. There are no task completion rates, robustness-to-perturbation metrics, energy/efficiency measures, or systematic comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., MPC across all tasks, GPS/TRPO on comparable simulated systems). The single MPC comparison (bird) is insightful but anecdotal.
- Methodological clarity: The execution-time scheme relies on a T=1 optimization step to satisfy the dynamics and match the network’s desired velocities/forces. This is an important design choice that blurs the line between a pure reactive policy and online model-predictive control; please discuss how much the one-step optimizer “rescues” the policy versus the policy alone being stabilizing.

Clarity
- Organization: Sections 4 (policy regression) and 6 (distributed training) are disproportionate; Section 4 is underdeveloped relative to its centrality, while Section 6 devotes substantial space to system details that could be condensed or moved to supplementary material.
- Recurrent units: The abstract and Section 2 emphasize a small number of recurrent units/memory state r, but Section 4 does not explain how recurrence is implemented or trained (e.g., number of recurrent units, where recurrence sits in the network, how ṙ is produced, BPTT vs. feed-forward with augmented state, initialization of r at episode boundaries). Please expand Section 4 to cover this.
- “Stochastic policy” terminology: Line 405 (Section 2.1) refers to a stochastic policy, but the network itself appears deterministic; stochasticity comes from training-time noise injection and the noisy targets that incorporate local feedback. Please clarify the use of “stochastic” (is the policy stochastic at execution, or only trained with noise?).
- Notation and definitions: The claim “the network is not learning a control policy, but rather the dynamics under an implicit policy” (Section 1) is confusing since πθ maps state to desired velocities/forces. Please rephrase.
- Costs: Section 8 says task costs are “the same” across experiments but does not list them precisely. For reproducibility, specify all cost terms and weights (including soft constraint penalties for dynamics/contacts), and report the dimension of r.

Originality
- The general learning-from-trajectory-optimization framework is not new (cf. GPS [9,11]), but the paper introduces a practical way to compute stabilizing feedback gains within a direct trajectory optimization framework and integrates denoising-style training into a large-scale asynchronous pipeline. Applying a single method to diverse 3D morphologies without hand-crafted state machines is noteworthy. Overall, the conceptual contribution is moderate; the empirical breadth and control engineering of the pipeline are the main advances.

Significance
- If reproducible, the approach is likely to be useful to graphics and robotics practitioners who operate with known simulators and seek interactive control. The feedback-gain computation could be of independent interest. However, the heavy reliance on known, accurate models (and soft enforcement of dynamics) limits impact on settings with model error. Stronger quantitative evidence and baseline comparisons would significantly raise the paper’s significance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Demonstrates diverse, visually plausible 3D behaviors (swimming, flying, biped/quadruped locomotion) with a single training recipe and interactive control.
- Clever and efficient computation of local feedback gains within CIO; principled noise-injection objective.
- Asynchronous distributed training pipeline with convergence behavior reported; practical contribution for scaling.
- Ablations indicate joint optimization and noise are important.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Requires full differentiable model and contact knowledge; no evaluation under model mismatch.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and weak baselines; little evidence of robustness (disturbance rejection, recovery rates).
- Clarity/structure issues: under-specified recurrent architecture; Section 4 too short; “stochastic policy” terminology potentially misleading.
- Execution relies on a T=1 optimization step; not purely a standalone policy, complicating attribution of performance.
- Novelty relative to guided policy search-style methods is incremental.

Requested clarifications and suggestions
- State early that full model knowledge and differentiable contact dynamics are required (Eq. 6), and discuss implications for real robots/model error.
- Expand Section 4: architecture diagram, location and size of recurrent units, training (BPTT or otherwise), r initialization, and how ṙ is supervised.
- Clarify “stochastic policy” (Line 405) and whether any stochasticity remains at test time.
- Provide precise task/dynamics/soft-constraint cost terms and weights; release or detail hyperparameters needed for reproduction.
- Add quantitative metrics (success rate, energy, step stability, perturbation tests) and broader baseline comparisons (MPC on all tasks; GPS/TRPO where feasible).
- Consider moving much of Section 6 to supplementary and use the space to improve methodological clarity and results.

Overall
Promising, well-engineered method with compelling demonstrations and a useful feedback-gain technique, but the paper would benefit from restructuring, clearer treatment of recurrence, and stronger quantitative evaluation and baselines. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the clarity issues and add more quantitative evidence; otherwise, this is a strong submission that could be further strengthened.
