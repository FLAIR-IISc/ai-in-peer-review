

The paper proposes a novel method for training neural network controllers via alternating supervised learning and trajectory optimization, specifically using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) as the offline trajectory generator. The core innovation lies in jointly optimizing the neural network policy and the trajectory dataset through an alternating scheme, combined with strategic noise injection (sensory and hidden-layer) to enhance robustness. The method successfully generates stable, realistic locomotion across diverse 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without task-specific features or motion capture, demonstrating a significant step toward unified character control. This advances prior work (e.g., Levine et al., ICML 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, TOG 2012) by scaling to complex 3D dynamics where earlier methods were limited to 2D.

**Quality** is strong in execution: the method is technically sound, with rigorous ablation studies showing that joint training (vs. static supervised learning) and noise injection (vs. no noise) are critical for performance (Fig. 2, Table 1). However, the experimental evaluation has limitations. The training set size (100–200 trials, 5 branched segments each) is small for claims about generalization, particularly given the high-dimensional control space. The ablation for noise injection conflates sensory and hidden-layer noise, making it unclear which component is most impactful. Additionally, the paper lacks runtime measurements for real-time execution (e.g., FPS, CPU usage), which is crucial for practical adoption in animation/gaming. The trajectory optimization details (Sec. 3.2) are confusing due to dropped time indices and inconsistent notation (e.g., "as" vs. "dads"), requiring readers to consult external references for clarity.

**Clarity** is mostly adequate but hampered by minor issues. The alternating optimization framework (Alg. 1) is well-motivated, yet the asynchronous distributed training (Sec. 6) is disconnected from the algorithm description, creating confusion. The denoising autoencoder analogy (Sec. 2.1) is misleading; noise here induces optimal action changes (Eq. 2), unlike standard denoising, and this distinction should be clarified. Typos ("unity" → "unify"), inconsistent variable usage (e.g., "q̇t" vs. "q̇"), and misplaced references (e.g., citing "Eq. 6" without defining it) reduce readability. The claim about orientation handling (e.g., "goal position" vs. "goal position/orientation") remains ambiguous, though the method likely extends to orientation via cost functions.

**Originality** is the paper’s strongest aspect. While trajectory optimization for control is established (e.g., CIO), the *joint training* approach—using CIO to generate data *and* feedback gains for the neural network—is novel for character control. The noise injection strategy (sensory + hidden-layer) also differs from prior work (e.g., Hinton et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2012), as it leverages optimal feedback gains rather than generic augmentation. However, the paper inadequately distinguishes from closely related work: Levine et al. (ICML 2014) uses trajectory optimization for RL, but the authors fail to discuss how their alternating scheme differs from their dual-gradient approach. A clearer comparison to Mordatch & Todorov (2012) is needed.

**Significance** is high for animation/robotics applications. The ability to control diverse morphologies (e.g., bird vs. spider quadruped) with a single architecture eliminates labor-intensive state machines, a major practical contribution. The method’s robustness to online goal changes (Sec. 5) enables interactive control, addressing a key limitation of prior trajectory-optimization-based approaches. However, the paper overstates real-time viability without performance metrics. For robotics, the claim that "trained character models accurately reflect robot parameters" requires empirical validation. The potential for biomechanical extensions (Sec. 9) is promising but speculative.

**Pro/Con Summary**:  
*Pro*: First to demonstrate unified 3D locomotion control via joint CIO-NN training; noise injection effectively prevents divergence; diverse, realistic results across four character types; clear ablation showing necessity of core components.  
*Con*: Weak comparative evaluation (only MPC vs. their method, no RL/baseline comparisons); small training set undermines generalization claims; missing runtime data; unclear mathematical details (Sec. 3.2); ambiguous orientation handling.

The paper makes a compelling contribution to character control, but requires strengthening in experimental rigor and clarity. Key revisions should include: (1) separate ablation for sensory vs. hidden noise; (2) runtime measurements; (3) expanded comparison to RL baselines (e.g., PPO, SAC); (4) clarification of Sec. 3.2 notation; (5) explicit handling of orientation in experiments. The core idea is promising and well-executed, but the current evidence for generalization and practicality is insufficient for a strong accept. A **weak accept** is appropriate if these revisions are addressed.
