### Summary

This paper presents a method for training a single neural network architecture to act as a real-time, interactive feedback controller for a diverse range of complex, physically simulated characters. The method is demonstrated on challenging 3D locomotion tasks including bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting, swimming, and flying, without requiring motion capture data or task-specific controller design. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate a dataset of near-optimal state-action pairs, and then use this data to train a recurrent neural network policy via supervised learning. The authors propose a novel, distributed, alternating optimization scheme where the trajectory data and the network parameters are refined together. Key technical contributions include a method for extracting optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer to create robust training targets, and the principled injection of noise into both sensory inputs and hidden unit activations during training to improve generalization and prevent divergence during execution.

The work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with function approximation [9, 11], but significantly extends the complexity and dimensionality of the problems solved, moving from 2D to diverse 3D characters. It presents a unified approach that contrasts with traditional, more specialized methods for controlling bipeds [21], quadrupeds [13], or swimmers/fliers [8].

### Quality and Clarity

The paper presents a technically sound approach, and the results, particularly as shown in the supplementary video, are highly impressive. The method's ability to generate stable, natural-looking gaits for a wide variety of morphologies from a general-purpose objective function is a strong demonstration of its power. The empirical evaluation, including the ablation studies in Section 8.1, effectively supports the authors' claims about the importance of joint optimization and noise injection.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity and organizational issues that detract from the quality of the contribution.

1.  **Missing Architectural Details:** The abstract and state/action definitions (Section 2) explicitly mention recurrent units (`r` and `r_dot`) as part of the controller's state and action. However, Section 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression), which describes the network, fails to mention these recurrent units or how they are implemented and trained. This is a critical omission, leaving the reader to guess about a key component of the proposed model.
2.  **Disproportionate Coverage:** The paper's organization could be improved. For example, Section 4 is very brief on the network details, while Section 6 provides a lengthy description of the distributed training implementation on AWS. While implementation details are useful, the current balance gives undue weight to the engineering aspects (which could be in an appendix) at the expense of core model details.
3.  **Misleading Terminology:** The conclusion refers to "stochastic neural networks". This is potentially confusing. While the *training* process is stochastic (via noise injection on inputs and activations), the network itself appears to be a deterministic function. At execution time (Section 7), the policy is queried "without noise". It would be clearer to state that a deterministic policy is trained using a stochastic procedure to enhance robustness.
4.  **Undeclared Assumptions:** A major assumption of this method is the availability of a complete and accurate differentiable model of the character's dynamics and its contact interactions (as seen in Equation 6 and the use of the MuJoCo simulator). This is a strong requirement that limits the direct applicability of the method to real-world robotics and should be stated more prominently as a limitation in the introduction or discussion.

### Originality

The paper demonstrates strong originality. While the high-level idea of distilling a trajectory optimizer into a neural network has been explored [9, 11], this work makes several novel contributions:
*   The extension and successful application of this paradigm to a much more challenging and diverse set of 3D characters is a significant step forward.
*   The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is a clever technique that elegantly solves the problem of generating appropriate targets for noisy inputs. This could be a useful contribution for other researchers in the field.
*   The specific block-alternating optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) that interleaves trajectory optimization with network regression in a distributed, stochastic fashion is a novel and effective framework for this problem class.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant. The ability to automatically synthesize high-quality controllers for complex locomotion tasks without manual, character-specific engineering is a long-standing goal in computer animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning. This work represents a demonstrable advance towards that goal. The emergent behaviors (e.g., natural walking/trotting gaits) are compelling and suggest that the framework is powerful enough to discover realistic and efficient control strategies. Researchers and practitioners in animation and game development are likely to build upon these ideas. The method for extracting feedback gains could also find broader use in the optimal control community.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Impressive Results:** The method successfully generates high-quality, stable, and interactive controllers for a diverse set of very challenging 3D characters, a significant advance over prior work.
2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces a novel method for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimizers and an effective distributed optimization framework.
3.  **High Potential Impact:** The work addresses a difficult and important problem, and the unified approach is likely to influence future research in character animation, control, and reinforcement learning.

### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  **Poor Clarity:** The paper's presentation is confusing and omits critical details about the recurrent network architecture, making the work difficult to understand and reproduce.
2.  **Unstated Limitations:** The critical dependence on a perfect physics model is not sufficiently highlighted, potentially overstating the method's immediate applicability outside of simulation.

### Final Assessment

This paper presents promising and significant results on a very challenging problem. The core technical ideas are novel and powerful. However, the paper is currently hampered by major issues in clarity and presentation, particularly the omission of key details about the recurrent network. While the results are strong enough to warrant serious consideration, the paper would need substantial revision to meet the quality standards for NIPS. I lean towards acceptance on the condition that the authors thoroughly address the clarity issues raised, especially by providing a complete and unambiguous description of their network architecture and training procedure for the recurrent components.
