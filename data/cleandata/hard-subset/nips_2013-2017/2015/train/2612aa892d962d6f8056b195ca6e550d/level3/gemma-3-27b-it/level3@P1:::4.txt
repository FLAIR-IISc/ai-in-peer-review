This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for a diverse range of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and various forms of locomotion. The core idea is to leverage the strengths of both trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) and neural network learning, addressing a long-standing challenge in automating the creation of complex and stable character controllers.  This work builds on a rich history of neural network control, particularly in robotics and animation, and aims to overcome the limitations of traditional, manually-tuned approaches or methods requiring extensive motion capture data. Recent NIPS work has explored related ideas [9, 11], but this paper distinguishes itself by successfully tackling significantly more complex, three-dimensional locomotion problems.

The strength of this work lies in its technical sophistication and demonstrable success. The authors effectively combine supervised learning from a trajectory optimizer with a neural network policy, cleverly addressing the difficulties of learning in continuous, high-dimensional spaces. A key contribution is the alternating optimization scheme, which decomposes the problem into manageable sub-problems (trajectory optimization and neural network regression), allowing for efficient reuse of existing tools and implementations.  The inclusion of noise injection during training, guided by optimal feedback gains, is a particularly insightful addition that promotes robustness and generalization.  The detailed description of the distributed training architecture, utilizing cloud computing and GPU implementation, highlights the practical considerations necessary to tackle this computationally demanding problem.  Experimental results, presented through compelling visuals (and encouraged via a video submission), demonstrate the method's ability to generate stable and realistic behaviors across a diverse set of character morphologies and tasks.  The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed joint optimization and noise injection techniques.

However, while technically strong, the paper's originality and significance are somewhat difficult to assess without deeper domain expertise. The authors acknowledge this, and the paper feels more like a substantial engineering achievement than a groundbreaking theoretical advancement.  The connection to reinforcement learning and stochastic optimal control is mentioned in the introduction, but a more thorough discussion of how this work differs from, and potentially advances, those fields would strengthen the paper. The motivation for the additional optimization step performed at runtime (Section 7), despite the already learned policy, isn’t fully convincing. While the authors explain it enables larger timesteps, it's not immediately clear why the learned policy alone wouldn't suffice for stable, physically plausible behavior, or whether residual trajectory cost/policy regression error necessitate this re-optimization. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The presentation is clear and logical, with sufficient detail to allow a knowledgeable reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results. Cross-references to related work are appropriate and helpful. However, a minor typo exists on line 89 (“to unify.”).  Additionally, on line 153, the statement “similar to recent approaches” would benefit from one or two specific citations to provide context.  The explanation for keeping updates small in parameter space (due to block alternation) is somewhat counterintuitive, but ultimately understandable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical soundness:** The method is technically sound, combining established techniques in a novel and effective manner.
*   **Empirical results:** The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the method's ability to generate realistic and stable behaviors across a diverse range of characters.
*   **Clarity and organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Practical relevance:** The method addresses a practical problem in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited novelty:** While technically strong, the theoretical novelty may be incremental.
*   **Domain dependence:** Assessing the broader significance requires deeper expertise in the field of character control.
*   **Runtime optimization justification:** The necessity of runtime optimization in Section 7 isn’t fully explained.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of character control. While the theoretical novelty may be limited, the technical sophistication, compelling results, and clear presentation make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the minor concerns regarding the runtime optimization step and providing more specific citations on line 153.
