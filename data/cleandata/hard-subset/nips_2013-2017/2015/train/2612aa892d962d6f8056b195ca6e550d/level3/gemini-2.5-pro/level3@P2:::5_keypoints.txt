- Proposes training a single recurrent neural network as a real-time feedback controller for diverse, complex simulated characters.
- Uses Contact-Invariant Optimization offline to generate near-optimal state-action datasets for supervised policy learning.
- Trains the recurrent policy via a distributed, alternating optimization that refines trajectories and network parameters together.
- Introduces a method to extract optimal feedback gains from trajectory optimization to produce robust training targets.
- Injects noise into sensory inputs and hidden activations during training to improve generalization and prevent execution divergence.
- Demonstrates the approach on challenging 3D locomotion tasks (bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting, swimming, flying) without motion-capture or task-specific controllers.
- Results (including supplementary video) show stable, natural-looking gaits across diverse morphologies, supported by ablation studies.
- Empirical evaluation and ablations effectively support claims about joint optimization and noise injection benefits.
- Major clarity and organization problems detract from the paper’s quality and reproducibility.
- Critical omission: Section describing the network fails to detail the recurrent units (r and r_dot) or how they are implemented and trained.
- Disproportionate focus on engineering/implementation (AWS distributed training) at the expense of core model details.
- Misleading terminology: calling the model “stochastic neural networks” is confusing since the trained policy is deterministic at execution; stochasticity is only in training.
- Undeclared limitation: method assumes a complete, differentiable dynamics/contact model (MuJoCo), restricting applicability to real-world robotics.
- Original contributions include scaling trajectory-optimizer distillation to diverse 3D characters, the feedback-gain extraction method, and a block-alternating distributed optimization algorithm.
- Significance: advances automatic synthesis of high-quality controllers for complex locomotion, with potential impact on animation, games, and optimal control research.
- Arguments for acceptance: impressive and diverse results, novel technical ideas, and high potential impact.
- Arguments for rejection: poor clarity/omitted network details and insufficient emphasis on the method’s dependence on perfect simulation models.
- Final assessment: promising and significant work, but should be revised to provide a complete, unambiguous description of the recurrent architecture and training; reviewer leans toward conditional acceptance.
