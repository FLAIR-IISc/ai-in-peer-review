This paper proposes a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters in a physics simulator. The authors demonstrate that a small, fast neural network can represent an interactive control policy for characters, and that the same architecture can be used across diverse cyclic behaviors and body types.

The method combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to learn the neural policy. The authors claim that this approach allows for the generation of stable and realistic behaviors for a range of dynamical systems and tasks, including swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking with different body morphologies.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the authors provide a clear overview of their method and its components. The experimental results are impressive, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a range of tasks.

However, I have some concerns regarding the novelty and contribution of the paper relative to recent related work. The use of neural networks to approximate step-by-step policy actions has been developed before (e.g., [9]), and the authors need to better position their contribution relative to that literature. Additionally, prior work has already established that trajectory optimization can produce realistic control signals, and the authors need to clarify what is new and innovative about their approach.

In terms of clarity, there are a few points that need to be addressed. The notation around lines 226–240 is confusing, and the authors should disambiguate the visually similar symbols (bars and squiggles). Additionally, the claim that "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ" needs to be justified and clarified, including a definition of "softer" relative to what.

The authors also need to provide more context and comparison to recent related work, particularly the work by Abbeel and Levine. Metrics such as learning speed, supported body variety, runtime computation cost, and robustness to environmental variability should be explicitly compared.

Overall, I believe that the paper presents sound experimental work, but it needs to better clarify its contribution relative to recent related work and address some minor issues with notation and clarity.
