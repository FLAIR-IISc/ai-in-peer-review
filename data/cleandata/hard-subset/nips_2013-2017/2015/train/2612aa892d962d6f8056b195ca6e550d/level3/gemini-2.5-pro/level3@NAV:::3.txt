This paper presents a method for creating real-time, interactive controllers for diverse physical characters. The core idea is to train a recurrent neural network to reproduce the outputs of an offline trajectory optimization method, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). This approach builds upon recent work that combines trajectory optimization with supervised learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch et al., 2014) but extends it to more complex 3D characters and tasks like walking, swimming, and flying. The paper's main contributions are a novel alternating joint training scheme, where the CIO-generated trajectories and the neural network policy are iteratively refined together, and the use of noise injection in both sensory inputs and hidden layers to improve the policy's robustness and generalization beyond a standard supervised fit.

The strengths of this work are significant. The proposed joint training and noise injection techniques are well-motivated and the experimental results are visually impressive, demonstrating that a single framework can produce stable and realistic control policies for a variety of complex character morphologies. The emergence of natural gaits without task-specific engineering is a notable achievement. This demonstrates the potential of combining powerful offline optimization with neural function approximators for complex control problems, advancing the state of the art in character animation.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. The primary concern is the experimental evaluation. The claims of generalization are not fully supported, as the training set is small (100–200 trials), and the comparative evaluation is weak. The paper includes only a brief, uncited comparison to MPC and lacks a systematic comparison against other relevant policy learning methods. For a real-time application, the absence of runtime analysis and discussion of how performance scales with model complexity is a critical omission.

The clarity of the paper could also be improved. Key mathematical details, such as the CIO cost components and the derivation of Equation 6, are not self-contained, requiring readers to consult external references. Section 3.2 is particularly confusing due to inconsistent notation, making the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure difficult to follow. Furthermore, the lack of provided code hinders reproducibility.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and well-executed approach to a challenging problem. The core ideas are original and the results are compelling. However, for the work to be a strong contribution, the authors should significantly bolster the experimental validation with more extensive training data, rigorous comparisons to baselines, and performance benchmarks. Additionally, clarifying the mathematical presentation and providing implementation details would substantially increase the paper's value and reproducibility.
