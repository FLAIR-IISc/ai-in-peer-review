

This paper presents a method for training neural network controllers to enable interactive control of diverse complex characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) without motion capture or task-specific features. The key innovation is a joint learning framework that alternates between trajectory optimization and neural network policy regression, with sensory noise injection during training. The authors demonstrate that this approach enables stable, realistic behaviors across multiple character types in 3D environments where prior work was limited to 2D.

The paper excels in technical execution and clarity. The methodology is well-motivated, thoroughly validated through comprehensive ablation studies (Section 8.1), and clearly written with appropriate detail and organization. The implementation details, including distributed training architecture and use of large timesteps (50ms), are particularly impressive. The results demonstrate significant advantages over naive neural network training (two orders of magnitude better performance) and show the necessity of noise injection to prevent overfitting.

Regarding quality, the empirical validation is strong and the approach is technically sound. However, the paper lacks novel theoretical insights, and the main contribution appears to be the practical implementation and demonstration of combining existing techniques rather than fundamentally new concepts. The motivation for the extra optimization step in Section 7 could be better explained, and the reviewer questions whether residual trajectory cost necessitates re-optimization and whether dynamics would prevent physical implausibility.

The paper's significance lies in its successful application to challenging 3D locomotion tasks across diverse character types, demonstrating a practical approach to interactive character control. While the novelty claim of "unifying disparate control approaches" may be overstated, the method represents a substantial practical advance in the field. The paper is well-written with only minor issues (typo at line 89), and the results are compelling.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution with impressive empirical results. While the theoretical novelty is limited, the practical effectiveness and clear demonstration of the method's advantages make it a valuable addition to the literature on neural control. The paper meets the quality and clarity standards of NIPS, though the significance claim could be tempered to better reflect the incremental nature of the contribution.
