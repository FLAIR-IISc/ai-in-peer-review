## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as feedback controllers for characters in a physics simulator, demonstrating successful control of diverse behaviors (swimming, flying, walking) and body types. The approach cleverly combines trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with supervised learning of a neural network policy, leveraging the optimizer to generate training data and refine feedback gains. The authors demonstrate impressive results, showcasing stable and realistic locomotion across a variety of simulated characters. 

The work builds upon a strong foundation of trajectory optimization for control, a technique well-established in the literature (e.g., [12]). However, the paper’s positioning within this field feels somewhat underdeveloped. While the authors acknowledge related work (e.g., [9, 11]), they don’t thoroughly differentiate their contribution. The claim of achieving “substantially harder problems” – specifically 3D locomotion – feels unsupported without a direct comparison to existing methods on similar benchmarks.  The connection to prior work by Abbeel and Levine on learning from demonstration and control is particularly salient, and a detailed comparative analysis (learning speed, body variety, computational cost, robustness) is crucial. 

Several aspects require clarification. The description of “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” is vague and needs explanation regarding *why* this is problematic. The rationale for choosing *not* to use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) control should be presented *after* the proposed strategy, not before, for better pedagogical flow.  Furthermore, the notation around lines 226–240 is confusing due to the visual similarity between symbols (s(X) =  ̄s and cost defined via s(X) –  ̄s).  The statement that the approach produces "softer" feedback gains needs a clear definition of "softer" and justification for why perturbing the initial state improves performance.

**Strengths:**

*   Strong experimental results demonstrate the versatility of the method across diverse characters and behaviors.
*   The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a promising approach.
*   The inclusion of noise injection is a practical consideration for robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficiently contextualized within existing literature on trajectory optimization and learning from demonstration.
*   Lack of quantitative comparison to relevant prior work (e.g., Abbeel & Levine).
*   Unclear explanations regarding feedback gain properties and design choices.
*   Notational ambiguity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a solid experimental investigation and demonstrates a functional system for interactive character control. The core idea of combining trajectory optimization with neural networks is sound and the results are visually compelling.

**Arguments for Rejection:** The paper’s contribution feels incremental without a more rigorous positioning within the existing literature. Key claims lack supporting evidence and critical details require clarification.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The authors need to significantly strengthen the paper's connection to related work and address the specific points of clarification outlined above to elevate the contribution and ensure clarity for the NIPS audience.
