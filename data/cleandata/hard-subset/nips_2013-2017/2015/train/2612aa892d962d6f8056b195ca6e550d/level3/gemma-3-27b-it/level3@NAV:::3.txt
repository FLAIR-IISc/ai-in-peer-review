## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as real-time controllers for diverse character models. The core idea – leveraging Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate training data for a neural network, combined with alternating joint training and noise injection – is well-motivated and demonstrably effective in producing stable and realistic character locomotion across various morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal). The approach smartly combines the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with the online adaptability of neural networks. The emphasis on robustness through noise injection, mimicking techniques like denoising autoencoders, is a valuable contribution. 

The novelty lies in the effective integration of CIO with NN training and the specific techniques used to improve generalization. While previous work has explored similar concepts [9, 11], this paper tackles more complex 3D locomotion problems successfully, a significant step forward. The authors rightly point out the challenge of scaling deep learning to control problems requiring precise outputs and closed-loop stability, and their proposed solution is a practical approach. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating impressive visual results (encouraged by the accompanying video), relies on a limited training set (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments). This raises concerns about overfitting and weakens claims of broad generalization. The comparative evaluation is particularly weak, with only a brief, uncited comparison to MPC. A more systematic comparison to existing control methods (e.g., reinforcement learning, state machines, other trajectory optimization-based approaches) is crucial.

Several technical clarifications are needed. The description of the CIO step (Section 3.2) lacks sufficient detail, requiring readers to consult [12]. Notational inconsistencies (e.g., dropping the time index *t*) in Section 3.2 obscure the procedure for handling noisy inputs. Algorithmic choices, such as the use of a constant eta in both optimization steps, are not sufficiently justified.  Furthermore, the asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs considerably from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1 and should be addressed earlier. The distinction between “data augmentation” and “noise injection” should be clarified, and the analogy to denoising autoencoders carefully re-examined. 

Finally, reproducibility would be greatly enhanced by providing code. Runtime measurements and scaling with model complexity are missing, hindering assessment of the practical applicability for real-time applications.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and promising approach to character control with visually compelling results, successfully bridging the gap between offline trajectory optimization and online neural network control.
**Arguments for Rejection:** The limited experimental evaluation, weak comparative analysis, and lack of clarity/reproducibility hinder confidence in the robustness and broader impact of the proposed method.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution, but requires substantial revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered for acceptance. I recommend a rejection with a strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the reviewers' concerns.
