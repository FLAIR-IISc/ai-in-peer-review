This paper presents a compelling approach to interactive character control, leveraging neural networks to learn from and generalize Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The core idea – using a NN to approximate the complex mapping learned by CIO, thereby enabling real-time control – is strong and well-motivated. The method demonstrates impressive results across diverse character morphologies, suggesting a significant step towards a universal control synthesis method. However, the paper suffers from limitations in experimental validation, clarity, and detail, hindering a full assessment of its impact.

**Summary:** The authors propose a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for complex dynamical systems (characters). They achieve this by iteratively alternating between trajectory optimization (CIO) and neural network training, using noise injection to improve robustness and generalization. The network learns to predict optimal actions based on sensory inputs, allowing for real-time, interactive control without relying on motion capture or task-specific features.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of CIO and NN training, particularly the alternating optimization scheme, is a novel approach. Extending successful CIO results to 3D locomotion is a substantial achievement, overcoming limitations of prior work.
*   **Performance:** The reported ability to control diverse character models with a single network architecture is impressive. The video accompanying the submission provides convincing visual evidence of realistic and stable behaviors.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The underlying concepts are grounded in established control theory (CIO) and machine learning (NNs). The use of noise injection is a sensible approach to improve generalization.
*   **Potential Significance:** A successful universal control synthesis method has broad applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The small training set (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments) raises concerns about generalization and the risk of overfitting.  The baselines are weak; the brief MPC comparison lacks detail (no citation) and a systematic comparison to other relevant methods is missing.
*   **Lack of Clarity & Detail:** Several parts of the mathematical presentation are unclear.  The CIO step (section 3) and Eq. 6 lack sufficient definition and context, requiring external references. Section 3.2, specifically, suffers from notational inconsistencies (dropped time index 't') that make the procedure difficult to follow.
*   **Missing Implementation Details:**  The absence of code hinders reproducibility and wider adoption. Crucially, runtime measurements and scaling with model complexity are missing, essential for evaluating the practical feasibility of the approach, especially for real-time applications.
*   **Terminology & Conceptual Issues:** The reviewer's distinction between "data augmentation" and "noise injection" is valid and could improve clarity. The analogy to denoising autoencoders feels somewhat strained and could benefit from further justification.
*   **Algorithmic Choices & Justification:**  The authors do not explain why initial X's are computed with random initial weights, or why the same hyperparameter eta is used in both optimization steps. Justification for this choice is needed.
*   **Architectural Disconnect:** The asynchronous joint optimization in Sec. 6 is significantly different from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1 and should be discussed earlier, or the connection made more explicit.
*    **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The ablations are too coarse-grained. Separate evaluations of "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" are needed to understand their individual contributions.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its core methodology. However, the limited experimental validation and lack of detail weaken the confidence in the claims.

**Clarity:** The writing is generally good, but suffers from notational inconsistencies and a lack of detail in key sections. Improvements in clarity would significantly enhance the paper’s accessibility.

**Originality:** The combination of CIO and neural networks, along with the alternating optimization scheme, represents a novel contribution. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in both control and machine learning.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is high, but the current evaluation does not fully demonstrate its significance. The practical benefits and advantages over existing methods need to be more convincingly established.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and promising approach to interactive character control.
*   Demonstrated ability to control diverse character models.
*   Sound technical foundations.
*   Potential for significant impact in multiple fields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation and weak baselines.
*   Lack of clarity and detail in key sections.
*   Missing implementation details (code, runtime measurements).
*   Unjustified algorithmic choices.



**Recommendations:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

*   Expand the experimental evaluation with a larger training set and more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Provide more detailed explanations and definitions, particularly for the CIO step and Eq. 6.
*   Release the code to facilitate reproducibility and adoption.
*   Include runtime measurements and scaling analysis.
*   Clarify algorithmic choices and provide justifications for parameter settings.
*   Address the notational inconsistencies and improve the clarity of Section 3.2.
*   Conduct more granular ablation studies.
*   Explore the possibility of annealing the CIO regularization parameter (alpha).
