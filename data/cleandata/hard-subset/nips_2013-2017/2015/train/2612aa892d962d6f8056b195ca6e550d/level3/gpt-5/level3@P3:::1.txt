Summary and relation to prior work
This paper trains recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse, high-DOF dynamical systems (flying, swimming, biped and quadruped locomotion) without motion capture or task-specific state machines. The key idea is to interleave trajectory optimization (using Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with policy regression, and to “embed” local feedback into the training targets. Specifically, the authors inject Gaussian noise into policy inputs and use a first-order expansion of the optimal action around each state, a(s+ε) ≈ a + As ε, where As are locally optimal feedback gains computed efficiently from the CIO Gauss-Newton system via a perturbation method (Woodbury identity) rather than a separate LQG pass. This yields training pairs that teach the network both nominal actions and how to correct small deviations, improving robustness to compounding errors at rollout.

Trajectory optimization and policy learning are softly coupled by a quadratic penalty R that measures deviation between the network’s output at noisy inputs and the noise-linearized optimal action, and the overall problem is solved by block-alternating optimization with proximal regularization. Noise is also injected into hidden activations to encourage saturation and robustness. At test time, the policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces; the next state is obtained by solving a one-step constrained optimization that enforces dynamics softly (T=1), enabling stable 20 Hz control.

The work is closely related to Guided Policy Search (Levine & Koltun, 2013/2014), which also alternates trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning under coupling penalties, and to Mordatch & Todorov (2014) on combining function approximation and trajectory optimization. The paper advances this line by (i) using CIO to synthesize rich contact-rich behaviors in 3D, (ii) computing and using optimal feedback gains as part of the training targets, (iii) demonstrating robust recurrent policies that generalize across diverse morphologies, and (iv) showing that softly coupling trajectory and policy optimization regularizes solutions toward simpler, more predictable policies than MPC alone.

Strengths
- Methodological contribution: Elegant soft coupling between trajectory optimization and policy learning; a practical, well-motivated penalty formulation with proximal terms that works asynchronously.
- Robustness via feedback embedding: Computing and injecting local feedback gains into supervised targets is novel here and addresses the classic covariate shift/regression error amplification problem for closed-loop control.
- Engineering quality: Clear algorithmic description, GPU-accelerated training, and effective distributed/asynchronous implementation; parallelization is solid and unobtrusive.
- Breadth of results: Diverse 3D characters (swimmer, bird, multiple bipeds and quadrupeds) learned with a single architecture and no hand-crafted state machines; emergent gaits and cyclic behaviors.
- Clarity: Exceptionally well written; derivations and design choices are justified; ablations demonstrate the need for joint optimization and noise.
- Insightful comparison: Qualitative juxtaposition to MPC suggests the joint optimization regularizes solutions toward simpler, more robust trajectories/policies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited quantitative evaluation: Few metrics (success rate, robustness to perturbations, energy/effort, tracking error, stability margins). Comparisons to GPS/TRPO or model-free baselines are missing.
- Modeling assumptions and transfer: Dynamics are enforced softly (including root forces), and actions include desired velocities and contact forces. This raises questions about physical realism and transfer to hardware.
- Theoretical guarantees: No convergence/stability guarantees for the alternating optimization; the derivation of gains relies on local quadratic approximations and finite-differenced Jacobians; sensitivity to λ, α, η is not analyzed.
- Ablations: While “no joint” and “no noise” ablations are provided, the specific contribution of feedback-gain embedding (vs simple denoising targets) is not isolated quantitatively.
- Execution step: The T=1 optimization at runtime is elegant but adds per-step solve time; real-time performance beyond the reported setting and robustness under larger disturbances could be further characterized.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully executed. The feedback-gain computation that reuses the Gauss-Newton inverse is insightful and practically important. The stochastic, proximal, block-alternating optimization is reasonable and empirically convergent. Experimental evidence is strong qualitatively and includes relevant ablations; more rigorous quantitative comparisons would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
Outstanding. The paper is well-organized, explains design choices, and provides enough detail for reproduction given access to a comparable physics engine and CIO implementation. Figures and video (as stated) support the claims.

Originality
Combines known ingredients (trajectory optimization, supervised policy learning, noise injection) in a novel and effective way, with a distinctive contribution: embedding optimal feedback gains into the training targets and computing them efficiently within direct trajectory optimization. Applying this to diverse 3D contact-rich behaviors without motion capture or state machines is a noteworthy step beyond prior NIPS/ICML work.

Significance
High. The approach pushes the frontier of learning robust closed-loop policies for complex dynamical systems and offers a practical recipe others can build on. The integration across graphics, control, and neural networks will likely influence both animation and robotics communities.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, technically solid, and impactful methodology with a new twist (feedback-gain embedding).
- Strong, diverse results with a single architecture; no task-specific heuristics.
- Insightful analysis/ablations; high-quality writing and presentation.
- Bridges trajectory optimization and deep policies in a scalable, parallel framework.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited quantitative benchmarks and head-to-head comparisons (e.g., GPS/TRPO, model-predictive baselines with feedback).
- Soft dynamics and action parameterization limit claims about physical realism and hardware deployment.
- Lack of theoretical convergence/stability guarantees; sensitivity analyses are light.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Provide quantitative metrics (success rates, disturbance rejection, energy) and comparisons to GPS/TRPO and MPC with feedback.
- Isolate the effect of feedback-gain embedding with a controlled ablation (e.g., same noise, no As term).
- Report runtime (CPU/GPU) for the T=1 execution solve, and robustness to larger perturbations or goal changes at test time.
- Discuss pathways to real-robot transfer (hard dynamics constraints, torque outputs) and sensitivity to λ, α, η, σε, σγ.
- Consider releasing code or pseudocode and more details on CIO settings for reproducibility.

Overall evaluation
A high-quality, technically impressive paper that makes a significant methodological contribution. The results are compelling and the exposition is exemplary. I recommend acceptance.
