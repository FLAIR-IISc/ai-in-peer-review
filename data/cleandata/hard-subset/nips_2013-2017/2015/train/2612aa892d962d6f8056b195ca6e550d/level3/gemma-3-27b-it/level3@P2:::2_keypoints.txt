- Presents a method training RNNs as feedback controllers using trajectory optimization to generate imitation training data for diverse dynamical systems.  
- Demonstrates convincing experimental results synthesizing complex locomotion (swimming, flying, walking) across varied morphologies with a single small network.  
- Core novelty claimed is scaling trajectory-optimization-based imitation to complex 3D locomotion and a unified architecture for diverse characters.  
- Contribution feels incremental relative to strong existing trajectory-optimization literature and needs clearer justification of significance.  
- Builds on Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate training trajectories and avoids direct high-dimensional reinforcement learning.  
- Paper does not clearly articulate how it differs from or improves upon established trajectory-optimization pipelines.  
- Lacks thorough comparative analysis to related work (e.g., Abbeel & Levine): missing metrics like learning speed, supported morphologies, runtime cost, and robustness.  
- Overly downplays overlap with prior neural network policy approximation work; needs clearer novelty justification (scale vs. robustness vs. generality).  
- Introduces optimal feedback gains and a perturbation method, but the motivation for avoiding LQG is confusing and ill-placed in the narrative.  
- Recommend moving the limitations-of-LQG discussion before the perturbation method to improve clarity.  
- The claim about “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” is vague and needs concrete explanation of problems and consequences.  
- Notation (lines 226–240) is confusing—bars and tildes for perturbed variables are visually similar and must be disambiguated.  
- Explanation of how the method yields “softer” feedback gains (λ) is unclear; define “softer” and relate it to stability, robustness, or other metrics.  
- Need justification for why modifying the initial state via perturbation benefits the training or resulting gains.  
- Strength: Sound experimental validation with a video that highlights high-quality generated motions.  
- Strength: Unified approach that could generalize control architectures across biped, quadruped, flying, and swimming characters.  
- Strength: Practical focus on real-time interactive control, moving beyond offline-only optimization.  
- Weakness: Incremental contribution—doesn’t convincingly leap beyond existing trajectory optimization and NN-based control methods.  
- Weakness: Insufficient quantitative and qualitative comparisons to closely related prior work.  
- Weakness: Convoluted explanations in places (LQG avoidance, “softer” gains) that compromise clarity.  
- Weakness: Notational confusion in Section 3.2 that hinders readability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, strong experimental results, potential to simplify complex animation and robotic control workflows.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, weak comparative analysis, unclear explanations and notation.  
- Overall assessment: weak accept conditioned on substantial revisions to clarify contributions, strengthen comparisons to prior work, and improve exposition and notation.
