Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a practical scheme for interactive control of complex characters that jointly learns trajectories and a neural policy, alternating between direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised policy regression. The policy outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state updates; during training, sensory noise is injected and optimal local feedback gains are computed from the trajectory optimization via a perturbation method, so the network learns to correct deviations from nominal trajectories. At test time, the learned policy is queried each step, and a short-horizon (T=1) optimization enforces dynamics consistency; this “implicit integration” enables stable control with comparatively large timesteps.

The approach builds on and unifies several strands: trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14), stochastic regularization and proximal updates for stability (related in spirit to TRPO’s trust regions, Schulman et al., 2015), and graphics/robotics controllers (CIO for rich contact dynamics; SIMBICON and ZMP-style baselines). The main empirical insight is that decoupling the problem into alternating subproblems lets the authors reuse mature methods (CIO; batched SGD on GPU) while achieving robust, realistic behaviors across diverse morphologies.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The block-alternating scheme is clearly specified, with a relaxed penalty formulation that couples the CIO subproblems and policy regression via a quadratic imitation term and proximal regularization. The perturbation-based computation of feedback gains reuses CIO’s Gauss-Newton system and is a nice engineering contribution that appears well-motivated and numerically better conditioned than a separate LQG pass. Noise injection in inputs and hidden units is well supported and ablations indicate substantial gains in rollout stability and test performance.
- Empirics: The paper shows diverse 3D results (swimming, flying, biped, quadruped) with consistent architecture and hyperparameters. Ablations (no joint alternation; no noise) are persuasive and highlight the value of both sensory noise and joint learning. The MPC comparison is informative and hints at the regularization effect of matching policy outputs during trajectory optimization.
- Caveats: While the engineering is strong, there are no new theoretical guarantees, and convergence is reported empirically. Reproducibility would benefit from more explicit task cost definitions (Section 8 says they are “the same for all experiments”) and runtime/latency numbers at execution.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, well organized, and contains useful cross-references. The problem setup, alternating optimization, and implementation details are described at an appropriate level of detail. The motivation for input noise and local gains is particularly well communicated.
- One place where motivation could be expanded is Section 7: the need for an additional optimization step at execution (even with the learned policy outputting desired velocities/forces) deserves a more explicit rationale.

Originality
- The overall approach is a thoughtful combination of known components (direct trajectory optimization; supervised policy regression; noise injection; proximal updates), applied at scale to challenging 3D contact-rich tasks. The perturbation-based reuse of CIO computations to estimate feedback gains is a neat practical twist. Conceptually, the work advances the trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning paradigm with an asynchronous, distributed implementation and careful regularization. I do not see fundamentally new theory, but the integration is sophisticated.

Significance
- Given the impressive results and robustness across morphologies, practitioners in graphics and model-based RL are likely to find this valuable. The demonstrated advantages of sensory noise and joint alternation are clearly evidenced. That said, I am not deeply familiar with the full state of the art in this specific subdomain, so I am cautious in assessing broader impact and novelty relative to closely related contemporaneous work.

Detailed comments and questions
- Section 7 (policy execution): Please clarify the motivation for re-optimizing a T=1 step at each timestep. Is this primarily to (a) enforce dynamics consistency to avoid drift from regression error, (b) mitigate residual trajectory cost because the policy only approximates the optimizer, or (c) act as an implicit integrator allowing larger timesteps? You note soft dynamics constraints and possible root forces; could you quantify how often and how large these nonphysical forces are, and whether they could induce implausible motions that the dynamics would otherwise prevent? It would help to discuss the trade-off between relying purely on the policy versus inserting the optimization layer, and to report execution-time latency and sensitivity to ∆t.
- Feedback gains (Sec. 3.2): Nice reuse of the Gauss-Newton system. A small comparison to an LQG-derived gain (stability, condition numbers, rollout quality) would strengthen the case that the perturbation method yields “better-conditioned gains.”
- Proximal regularization and “keeping updates small”: At line 153, where you write “similar to recent approaches,” please add one or two references. Candidates include proximal/ADMM methods for stochastic block-alternation and trust-region policy methods (e.g., TRPO). Also, while keeping updates small in parameter space can be counterintuitive for exploration, it makes sense here to stabilize the alternating scheme; a brief discussion would be useful.
- Baselines: Beyond MPC, could you report results for a direct policy-gradient/trust-region baseline (e.g., TRPO) on at least one morphology, even if performance is worse, to contextualize the benefits of trajectory-optimization–guided learning?
- Costs and reproducibility: Please provide the exact task cost terms and weights used in Sec. 8 (or in the supplement), and per-task training compute (node-hours) and execution-time FPS/latency.
- Minor: Line 89 has a typo: “to unity” should read “to unify.”

Pros
- High technical sophistication in both concept and implementation; strong engineering.
- Clear, well-organized presentation with useful ablations and cross-references.
- Demonstrates compelling, diverse 3D results with a single architecture.
- Joint alternation and sensory noise clearly improve robustness and test performance.
- Practical method for computing local feedback gains from CIO.

Cons
- Limited novelty in theory; largely a strong integration of existing ideas.
- Motivation and implications of the extra per-timestep optimization (Sec. 7) could be clearer; possible reliance on soft constraints may allow nonphysical forces.
- Limited comparison to direct RL baselines; broader impact within RL/graphics not fully quantified.
- Reproducibility would benefit from fuller disclosure of cost definitions and runtime details.

Overall recommendation
I have a positive impression of the results and the optimization scheme. Despite limited theoretical novelty, the paper presents a well-executed and effective method with impressive empirical performance, and the core empirical insight—that decoupling into alternating subproblems allows reuse of existing methods and works well in practice—is convincingly demonstrated. I lean toward acceptance, while acknowledging some uncertainty about novelty and broader significance due to domain unfamiliarity.
