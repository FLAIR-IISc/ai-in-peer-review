- Proposes an alternating scheme that jointly learns trajectories (CIO) and a neural policy, with perturbation-derived local feedback gains and sensory-noise regularization; at test time a T=1 optimization enforces dynamics consistency ("implicit integration").
- Builds on and unifies trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning, proximal/trust-region style regularization, and graphics/robotics contact controllers (CIO, SIMBICON, ZMP).
- Technical soundness: block-alternating formulation, relaxed penalty coupling, and Gauss-Newton reuse for feedback gains are well-specified and numerically motivated.
- Noise injection in inputs and hidden units is well-justified and ablations show substantial gains in rollout stability and test performance.
- Empirics: diverse 3D tasks (swimming, flying, biped, quadruped) with consistent architecture and persuasive ablations (no alternation; no noise).
- MPC comparison is informative and suggests a regularization benefit from matching policy outputs during trajectory optimization.
- Main caveat: no new theoretical guarantees; convergence demonstrated empirically only.
- Reproducibility concerns: need exact task cost definitions and per-task training compute and execution-time/latency numbers.
- Clarity: paper is clearly written and organized; motivation for input noise and local gains is well communicated.
- Section 7 needs stronger motivation for the per-timestep T=1 re-optimization even when the policy outputs desired velocities/forces.
- Originality: thoughtful integration of known components applied at scale; perturbation-based reuse of CIO computations for gains is a neat practical contribution.
- Significance: likely valuable to practitioners in graphics and model-based RL given robustness across morphologies, though broader impact/novelty relative to close work is uncertain.
- Question: clarify whether T=1 re-optimization primarily enforces dynamics consistency, mitigates residual trajectory cost, or acts as an implicit integrator; quantify nonphysical/root forces and sensitivity to timestep.
- Suggestion: compare perturbation-derived feedback gains to LQG-derived gains (stability, conditioning, rollout quality).
- Suggestion: add explicit references for proximal/ADMM/trust-region methods and discuss the interplay between small parameter updates and exploration.
- Baseline request: report a direct policy-gradient/trust-region baseline (e.g., TRPO) on at least one morphology for context.
- Reproducibility request: provide exact cost terms/weights used and per-task node-hours and runtime FPS/latency in supplement.
- Minor typo: line 89 "to unity" should be "to unify."
- Pros: strong engineering and implementation, clear presentation, compelling diverse 3D results, clear benefits from joint alternation and sensory noise, practical gain computation method.
- Cons: limited theoretical novelty, unclear motivation/implications of per-timestep optimization (possible nonphysical forces), limited direct RL baseline comparisons, and incomplete reproducibility details.
- Overall recommendation: positive impression and leaning toward acceptance due to strong empirical performance and engineering, despite limited theoretical novelty and some reproducibility/clarity concerns.
