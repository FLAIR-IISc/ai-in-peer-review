This paper presents a method for synthesizing real-time feedback controllers for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to train a recurrent neural network to reproduce the behavior of a powerful offline trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The key contributions are a novel training procedure and the use of noise to improve robustness. The training involves an alternating, joint optimization of both the reference trajectories (via CIO) and the neural network policy parameters. To improve generalization and prevent divergence during execution, the method injects noise at two levels: as data augmentation on the sensory inputs and as additive noise in the network's hidden layers. The authors demonstrate impressive results, generating stable and plausible gaits for a diverse set of characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, all using the same general framework.

This work builds upon a promising line of research that combines the strengths of trajectory optimization with function approximation for control (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). While previous work showed success in 2D, this paper makes a significant step forward by tackling more complex and unstable 3D characters, demonstrating a higher degree of robustness and quality in the resulting motions.

### Strengths

*   **Novel Training Scheme:** The central contribution—the alternating joint optimization of trajectories and the network policy—is a powerful idea. Instead of treating training as a one-shot supervised learning problem on a fixed dataset, this method allows the policy to influence the data it is trained on. This co-adaptation appears crucial for learning a stable, general-purpose controller, as demonstrated by the poor performance of the "no joint" baseline.
*   **Effective Use of Noise:** The principled injection of noise is another key strength. Using the trajectory optimizer to compute optimal feedback gains for perturbed sensory states (Sec 3.2) is a clever way to teach the network how to react to deviations, going beyond simple data augmentation. This, combined with hidden layer noise, demonstrably improves test-time performance and prevents the overfitting seen in the "no noise" ablation.
*   **Impressive and General Results:** The ability of a single, unified algorithm to produce high-quality locomotion for such a diverse set of morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) is a significant achievement. The emergent gaits appear natural and cyclic without being explicitly encoded, highlighting the method's potential as a general tool for character control.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

**1. Experimental Evaluation:** While the qualitative results are strong, the quantitative evaluation could be substantially improved.
*   **Comparative Evaluation:** The comparison to other methods is very limited. The model-predictive control (MPC) comparison is brief, anecdotal, and lacks a citation or implementation details. To properly assess the significance of this work, a more systematic comparison against contemporary state-of-the-art methods in continuous control (e.g., reinforcement learning algorithms like TRPO) would be highly valuable.
*   **Training Data Size:** The experiments rely on a relatively small training set (100–200 trials with 5 branched segments). This weakens the claims about generalization and may unfairly penalize the baselines, which might be overfitting precisely because of the limited data. A study on how performance scales with more training data would strengthen the paper's conclusions.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study is informative but could be more granular. For instance, separating the "no noise" case into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" would help disentangle the specific contributions of each noise source.
*   **Missing Performance Metrics:** The paper focuses on a real-time application, yet provides no runtime measurements. Information on training time (beyond the 2.5-hour total), policy execution time, and how these scale with character complexity (e.g., number of DOFs) is essential for evaluating practical viability in games or robotics.

**2. Clarity and Reproducibility:**
*   **Lack of Detail:** The paper is not self-contained, making it difficult for an expert to reproduce the results. The CIO formulation and the constraints in Eq. 6 are presented without definition, requiring readers to consult an external reference for core details. Similarly, the derivation of optimal feedback gains in Section 3.2 is confusing due to notational issues (e.g., the time index `t` is dropped), making the procedure difficult to follow.
*   **Inconsistent Algorithm Description:** The distributed, asynchronous training architecture described in Section 6 appears to differ significantly from the synchronous, alternating optimization presented in Algorithm 1. This discrepancy should be addressed and justified much earlier, as it changes the interpretation of the algorithm.
*   **Code Availability:** Given the complexity of the physics, optimizer, and training loop, providing source code would be invaluable for reproducibility and adoption by the community.

**3. Detailed Questions and Minor Issues:**
*   **Goal Specification:** The paper states the task is to reach a goal "location and orientation." Please clarify if the target orientation (e.g., facing angle) is part of the cost function and policy input/output, and discuss the straightforwardness of adding such targets.
*   **Algorithmic Choices:** Could the authors justify some hyperparameter choices? Why is the same proximal regularization hyperparameter `eta` used for both the trajectory and policy updates, and is there a principled reason for keeping them identical? In Algorithm 1, how are the initial trajectories `X` computed in the first iteration, before `theta` is trained?
*   **Terminology:** The analogy to denoising autoencoders is slightly misleading, as the network is not trained to reconstruct the original `a` from a noisy `s`, but rather to predict the *optimal action* for the noisy state, `a + as*epsilon`. It might also be clearer to distinguish between "data augmentation" (modifying sensory inputs) and "noise injection" (modifying hidden activations).
*   **Writing and Typos:** The manuscript needs a thorough proofreading pass to fix numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "unity" → "unify" on p.2, missing articles, misplaced stars on variables, inconsistent acronym ordering).

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-executed and promising approach to a very challenging problem in character animation and control. The core ideas of joint optimization and principled noise injection are significant contributions that demonstrably improve upon a standard behavioral cloning baseline. The results are impressive and showcase a high degree of generality. However, the paper is weakened by an insufficient experimental comparison to other methods, a lack of clarity in key technical sections, and missing details on performance that are crucial for a real-time system. With a more rigorous evaluation and improved presentation, this work would represent a very strong contribution to the field.
