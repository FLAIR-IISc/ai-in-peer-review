- Presents a method that alternates Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory generation with neural-network policy regression for real-time control of diverse 3D characters, assuming full differentiable model knowledge and using MuJoCo with soft dynamics constraints.
- Core contributions: large-scale CIO trajectory dataset, perturbation-based computation of optimal local feedback gains, denoising-style regression objective with noise injection, and an asynchronous distributed training pipeline.
- Technical soundness: alternating optimization is reasonable; reuse of Gauss-Newton system and Woodbury identity to compute feedback gains is neat and useful.
- Training objective and proximal regularization are well motivated and practical.
- Major assumption/scope limitation: requires full, differentiable dynamics, contact geometry, and friction cones—this is not model-free and model mismatch is not evaluated.
- Empirical results are qualitatively strong across multiple morphologies but lack comprehensive quantitative evaluation.
- Missing quantitative metrics: no success rates, robustness/perturbation tests, energy/efficiency measures, or systematic baseline comparisons (MPC/GPS/TRPO) across tasks.
- Methodological clarity concern: execution uses a one-step (T=1) optimizer that blurs pure policy vs. online MPC—need discussion of how much the optimizer “rescues” the learned policy.
- Paper organization issues: Section 4 (policy regression) is underdeveloped while Section 6 (distributed training) is overly detailed and could be condensed or moved to supplement.
- Recurrent architecture under-specified: number/location of recurrent units, training method (BPTT or not), r dynamics, and episode initialization are not described.
- Terminology issue: calling the policy “stochastic” is misleading since stochasticity appears to come only from training-time noise, not execution-time policy stochasticity.
- Confusing phrasing: statement that “the network is not learning a control policy, but rather the dynamics under an implicit policy” contradicts that πθ maps states to desired velocities/forces—needs rewording.
- Reproducibility gaps: task costs, cost weights, soft-constraint penalties, and dimension of the recurrent state r are not fully specified.
- Originality is moderate: framework resembles guided policy search but contributes a practical feedback-gain computation within direct trajectory optimization and a denoising-style training integration.
- Significance limited by reliance on accurate models and soft enforcement of dynamics; feedback-gain technique could be independently useful for practitioners with simulators.
- Pros: diverse visually plausible behaviors without hand-crafted state machines; efficient local feedback gain computation; principled noise-injection objective; scalable asynchronous training with ablations showing importance of joint optimization and noise.
- Cons: requires full differentiable model and contact knowledge with no model-mismatch tests; limited quantitative evaluation and weak baselines; unclear recurrent details and misleading “stochastic” wording; dependence on T=1 optimizer complicates performance attribution; novelty incremental relative to GPS-like methods.
- Requested clarifications: state model-knowledge requirement early and discuss implications for real robots/model error.
- Requested clarifications: expand Section 4 with architecture diagram, recurrent unit details, training procedure, r initialization, and supervision of ṙ.
- Requested clarifications: clarify whether the policy is stochastic at test time or only trained with noise.
- Requested clarifications: provide full task/dynamics/soft-constraint cost terms and hyperparameters for reproducibility.
- Requested experiments: add quantitative metrics (success rate, energy, perturbation tests) and broader baselines (MPC on all tasks; GPS/TRPO where feasible).
- Suggested restructure: move system-level training details to supplementary material and use freed space to improve methodological clarity and additional results.
- Overall: promising and well-engineered with a useful feedback-gain technique and compelling demos; recommend acceptance if clarity issues and stronger quantitative evidence/baselines are added, otherwise a solid but improvable submission.
