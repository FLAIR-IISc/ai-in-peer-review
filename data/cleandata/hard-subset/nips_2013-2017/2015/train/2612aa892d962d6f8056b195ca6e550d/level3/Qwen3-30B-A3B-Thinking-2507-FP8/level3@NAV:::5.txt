

This paper presents a method for training neural network controllers using interleaved trajectory optimization and supervised learning, enabling interactive control of diverse 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) without task-specific features. The core innovation lies in jointly optimizing trajectories and a neural policy that learns both state-action mappings and implicit dynamics, with stochastic training data injection to enhance robustness. The approach builds on prior trajectory optimization work (Mordatch et al., 2012; Levine & Koltun, 2014) but extends it to challenging 3D continuous control tasks where prior methods were limited to 2D.  

**Strengths:** The method demonstrates compelling empirical results across diverse morphologies, achieving stable, realistic behaviors where simpler direct-RL approaches fail (Section 8.1). The integration of contact-invariant optimization (CIO) with neural networks provides a principled way to learn robust controllers, validated through ablation studies showing significant performance gains over static training or no-noise baselines. The real-time execution framework (Section 7) is elegant and avoids trajectory storage.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Critical omission:** The paper assumes full model knowledge (Eq. 6) but fails to state this upfront, which is essential for reproducibility and scope clarity.  
2. **Structural imbalance:** Section 6 (distributed training) is disproportionately lengthy (3 pages) compared to Section 4 (neural regression, 1.5 pages), despite Section 4 being central to the method.  
3. **Missing detail:** Section 4 omits the recurrent units explicitly referenced in the abstract (e.g., "small number of recurrent units"), undermining the described architecture.  
4. **Misleading terminology:** Line 405 describes the network as "stochastic" despite its deterministic nature (only training data is stochastic), requiring clarification.  

**Recommendation:** The results are promising and address a significant gap in character control, but the paper requires restructuring to fix the above issues. The core contribution is novel and impactful (originality/significance), and the technical execution is sound (quality), but clarity must improve. The authors should: (1) explicitly state the full-model requirement early, (2) rebalance Sections 4–6, (3) clarify recurrent unit integration, and (4) correct the "stochastic" terminology. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS. **Recommendation: Rebuttal + Revision.**
