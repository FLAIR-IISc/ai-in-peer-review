- Presents a method that alternates offline trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization) with supervised training of an RNN policy, adding sensory and hidden noise and a novel feedback-gain computation, demonstrated across bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers.

- Builds on prior work that distills trajectory optimization into neural policies, notably Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch et al. (2014).

- Strength: Impressive empirical results — a single architecture produces stable, realistic, interactive locomotion for diverse morphologies and tasks.

- Strength: Sound experimental design — ablation studies show the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection; comparison with MPC provides useful insight.

- Strength: Complete system — covers trajectory generation, distributed training, and real-time execution with clear application potential in games and animation.

- Weakness: Insufficient positioning against closely related recent work; novelty and specific contributions are not clearly articulated.

- Weakness: Lacks direct, quantitative comparison to prior methods on metrics like learning speed, sample complexity, policy performance, runtime cost, and robustness.

- Weakness: Unclear which component (feedback gain calc, noise scheme, distributed architecture) is the key innovation enabling results.

- Weakness: Technical exposition needs clarification — motivation and consequences of the proposed feedback-gain method versus LQG are not well explained.

- Weakness: Organization in Section 3.2 is confusing; the proposed method should be presented before critiquing alternatives.

- Weakness: Notation is confusing (e.g., s(X)=\bar{s} vs cost using ||s(X)-\bar{s}||^2) and should be clarified.

- Weakness: The term "softer" feedback gains is ambiguous and should be defined (e.g., spectral norm, sensitivity) with intuition for why allowing initial-state modification reduces aggressive feedback.

- Recommendation summary: Strong experimental contribution but needs substantial revision to clarify novelty relative to prior art and to improve technical exposition; reviewer leans toward acceptance contingent on these fixes.

- Arguments for acceptance: compelling, general results on a significant problem with informative evaluations.

- Arguments for rejection: insufficiently distinguished novelty from recent related work and under-justified technical claims about feedback gains.
