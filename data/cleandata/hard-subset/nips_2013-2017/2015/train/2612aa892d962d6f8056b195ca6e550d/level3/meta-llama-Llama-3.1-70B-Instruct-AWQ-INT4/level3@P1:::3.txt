This paper proposes a novel real-time control algorithm for physically consistent interactive character control. The method combines Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with a neural network (NN) to generate stable and realistic behaviors for a range of dynamical systems and tasks, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking. The algorithm alternates between CIO and NN training, using noise injection via data augmentation and additive noise in hidden layers to improve robustness.

The paper's main contributions are the alternating joint training and noise injection, which improve generalization beyond a straightforward supervised fit to CIO. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that the network produces realistic and stable control policies on diverse character models.

However, there are several limitations and areas for improvement. The experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set, which weakens claims about generalization and overfitting baselines. Additionally, the comparative evaluation is weak, with only a brief Model-Predictive Control (MPC) comparison provided and systematic comparisons to other methods missing.

The mathematical presentation is mostly clear, but some parts, such as the CIO step and Eq. 6, lack definitions and detail, requiring readers to consult external references. Section 3.2 is also confusing due to notational issues, making the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure unclear.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from providing code to aid reproducibility and adoption. Runtime and time measurements, as well as scaling with model complexity, are also important for real-time applications and should be included.

The paper's terminology could be improved by distinguishing between "data augmentation" and "noise injection" and questioning the denoising-autoencoder analogy. The reviewer also raises questions about algorithmic choices, such as the computation of initial X's using random initial weights in Alg. 1 and the use of the same hyperparameter eta in both steps.

Minor writing and notation issues, such as typos and missing articles, need to be fixed. The asynchronous joint optimization in Sec. 6 differs substantially from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1 and should be discussed earlier or connected more clearly.

Experimental ablations could be more granular, such as separate "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" variants. An interesting open question is whether the CIO regularization alpha can be annealed to zero and how that affects learning.

Overall, the paper presents a promising and well-executed application of NNs to character control. However, practical benefits, broader comparisons, clarifications, and additional experiments are needed to further establish the method's effectiveness.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the lack of detail in some mathematical derivations and the limited experimental evaluation weaken the paper's overall quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, but some sections, such as Section 3.2, are confusing due to notational issues. The terminology could also be improved to avoid ambiguity.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel combination of CIO and NNs for real-time character control. The alternating joint training and noise injection are the main contributions of the paper.

**Significance:** The paper has significant implications for the field of character control and animation. The proposed method could be used in various applications, including video games, robotics, and biomechanics.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective method for real-time character control.
* The experimental evaluation demonstrates the method's effectiveness on diverse character models.
* The paper has significant implications for the field of character control and animation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set.
* The comparative evaluation is weak, with only a brief MPC comparison provided.
* The mathematical presentation lacks detail in some sections.
* Minor writing and notation issues need to be fixed.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above.
