This paper presents a compelling approach to learning robust control policies for a diverse set of dynamical systems using recurrent neural networks. By cleverly interleaving trajectory optimization with neural network training, and injecting noise to encourage robustness, the authors achieve impressive results on locomotion tasks for swimming, flying, and legged characters. The work is exceptionally well-written, technically sound, and represents a significant methodological contribution to the field of control and machine learning.

The paper addresses a challenging problem – automating the design of interactive, real-time controllers capable of complex and stable movements. Traditional methods rely heavily on manual tuning, motion capture, or task-specific state machines. This work aims for a more general solution, drawing inspiration from both reinforcement learning and stochastic optimal control, but specifically tackling the difficulties of applying these techniques to high-dimensional continuous spaces. The authors rightly point out the recent successes in deep learning haven’t translated directly to control due to the unique challenges of data acquisition and the need for precise, closed-loop control. 

The core idea – using trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) to generate training data for a neural network policy – is insightful. The authors don’t simply mimic optimal trajectories, but rather learn the feedback gains that provide robustness to disturbances and modeling errors. This effectively bridges the gap between offline planning (trajectory optimization) and online execution (neural network control). The addition of stochastic policy training and the distributed optimization architecture further enhance the method’s effectiveness and scalability. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and well-motivated. The use of soft constraints, the derivation of optimal feedback gains, and the proximal regularization terms are all technically justified and contribute to the robustness of the approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is remarkably well-written and easy to follow, despite the complexity of the underlying concepts. The organization is logical, and the explanations are concise and informative. The figures and supplementary video are extremely helpful.
* **Originality:** While building on existing techniques like trajectory optimization and neural networks, the combination is novel and addresses a significant gap in the field. The method’s ability to generalize across diverse morphologies and tasks is a key contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are truly impressive. Achieving stable and realistic locomotion in 3D space, without relying on hand-crafted controllers or motion capture data, is a major accomplishment. The potential applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics are significant. The integration of graphics, control, and neural networks is particularly noteworthy.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The ablation studies (removing noise injection, comparing to static training) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed techniques. Comparing against MPC highlights the qualitative differences in the resulting behaviors.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the distributed architecture addresses the scalability issue, the training process still requires significant computational resources. This may limit accessibility for researchers without access to cloud computing infrastructure. While acknowledged, the cost is still a barrier.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lists the hyperparameters used, but a more detailed sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. Understanding how the performance varies with different noise levels, regularization weights, and network architectures would provide further insight.
* **Limited Comparison to Direct Reinforcement Learning:** While the authors mention prior work in direct reinforcement learning methods, a more systematic comparison would be valuable. A head-to-head comparison on the same tasks would help to quantify the advantages of the trajectory-optimization-based approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging and important problem in control and machine learning.
* The proposed method is technically sound, well-motivated, and achieves state-of-the-art results.
* The writing is exceptionally clear and the paper is well-organized.
* The results are inspiring and demonstrate the potential of combining trajectory optimization and neural networks.
* The work has broad implications for robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of training may be a barrier to adoption.
* A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
* A more thorough comparison to direct reinforcement learning methods would strengthen the paper.

**Overall:**

This is an outstanding paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS. The authors have presented a novel, technically sound, and well-evaluated method for learning robust control policies that demonstrates impressive results on a diverse set of tasks. This work is likely to have a lasting impact on the field and inspire further research in this area.
