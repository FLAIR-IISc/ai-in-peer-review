This paper presents a novel approach to interactive control of complex characters using neural networks. The authors propose a method that combines trajectory optimization with policy learning, allowing for the generation of stable and realistic behaviors for a range of dynamical systems and tasks. The key idea is to jointly learn trajectories and a policy, decoupling the problem into alternating subproblems that can be solved using existing methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a high level of technical sophistication in both concept and implementation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through impressive results on various tasks, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking. The use of sensory noise during training and the joint learning of trajectories and policy are clearly demonstrated to be advantageous.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to reuse existing methods, such as Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and stochastic gradient descent, to solve the subproblems. This approach allows for efficient computation and scalability, making it suitable for real-time interactive control.

The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the optimization scheme, including the use of quadratic proximal regularization terms and the introduction of noise during training. The authors demonstrate the importance of these techniques in preventing overfitting and improving the robustness of the policy.

However, the reviewer notes that the motivation for the extra optimization step in Section 7 could be better explained. The reviewer expected the policy to run without additional optimization, and it is unclear whether residual trajectory cost or policy regression error necessitates re-optimization. Furthermore, the reviewer questions whether dynamics would prevent physical implausibility.

Additionally, the reviewer requests references for the statement "similar to recent approaches" on line 153. The reviewer also notes a typo on line 89, where "to unify" should be used instead of "to unity."

Overall, the reviewer has a positive impression of the results and optimization scheme but is uncertain about the novelty and significance of the approach due to unfamiliarity with the domain.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying concepts. The approach is well-motivated, and the results are impressive.
