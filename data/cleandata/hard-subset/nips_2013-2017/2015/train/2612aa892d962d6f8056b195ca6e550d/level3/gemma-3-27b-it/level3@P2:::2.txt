This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, enabling stable and realistic behaviors like swimming, flying, and walking with varying body morphologies. The core idea is to leverage trajectory optimization to generate training data for the neural network, effectively learning a control policy through imitation and regularization. While the experimental results demonstrate a compelling ability to synthesize complex locomotion behaviors, the paper’s contribution feels incremental and requires stronger positioning within the existing literature.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of trajectory optimization techniques, particularly Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12], to generate training data. The authors then utilize this data to train a relatively small neural network to approximate the control policy. This approach circumvents the challenges of direct reinforcement learning in high-dimensional spaces. The key novelty appears to lie in the successful scaling of this approach to complex, 3D locomotion tasks and the demonstration of a unified architecture capable of controlling diverse character types. However, the paper doesn’t sufficiently articulate *why* this scaling is significant, given the established capabilities of trajectory optimization itself.

Prior work has demonstrated that trajectory optimization can produce highly realistic control signals, as evidenced by the examples cited in the introduction. The authors state the goal is to “automate this process” but need to more explicitly address how their approach differs from, and improves upon, existing trajectory optimization pipelines. The connection to the work of Abbeel and Levine [9] is acknowledged, but a more thorough comparative analysis is crucial. Specifically, the paper should compare performance metrics such as learning speed, the range of supported body morphologies, runtime computational cost, and robustness to environmental variability. 

Furthermore, the authors mention related work employing neural networks to approximate step-by-step policy actions [9] but downplay the potential overlap. A clearer justification of the novelty relative to such prior efforts is needed. For instance, is the improvement a matter of scale (handling more complex dynamics), robustness (dealing with noise and perturbations), or generality (a single architecture across diverse characters)?

The introduction of optimal feedback gains is a noteworthy addition. However, the explanation surrounding the motivation for avoiding Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) is somewhat convoluted. It is presented after the proposed perturbation method, making the reasoning less clear. Moving the discussion of the limitations of LQG *before* introducing the perturbation method would improve the flow. Furthermore, the phrase “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” requires more concrete explanation. Why is this problematic? What are the consequences of such gains?

The mathematical notation surrounding lines 226–240 is confusing. The use of both bars (e.g., s̄) and squiggles (e.g., s̃) to denote perturbed variables is visually similar and hinders readability. Disambiguation of these symbols is necessary. Similarly, the explanation of how the approach produces “softer” feedback gains (λ) needs clarification. What does “softer” mean in this context? Is it related to stability, robustness, or some other metric? Justifying *why* modifying the initial state in this manner is beneficial would strengthen the argument.

**Strengths:**

* **Sound Experimental Validation:** The authors present convincing experimental results demonstrating the ability to control a diverse range of characters with a single network architecture. The accompanying video is essential for appreciating the quality of the generated motions.
* **Unified Approach:** The method demonstrates a potential pathway towards unifying disparate control architectures for different character types (biped, quadruped, flying, swimming).
* **Practical Implementation:** The focus on real-time interactive control is a valuable contribution, moving beyond purely offline trajectory optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Contribution:** The paper does not convincingly establish a significant leap beyond existing trajectory optimization and neural network-based control techniques.
* **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to related work, particularly the work of Abbeel and Levine [9], is lacking in detail and quantitative analysis.
* **Convoluted Explanation:** Some explanations, such as the justification for avoiding LQG and the meaning of “softer” feedback gains, are unclear and require further elaboration.
* **Notational Confusion:** The notation in section 3.2 is confusing and requires simplification.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear experimental validation.
* The results demonstrate a compelling application of deep learning to a challenging control problem.
* The method has the potential to simplify the process of creating complex character animations and robotic control systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s contribution feels incremental and requires a stronger positioning within the existing literature.
* The lack of a thorough comparative analysis with related work weakens the justification for the proposed approach.
* The unclear explanations and notational confusion detract from the overall clarity of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. The experimental work is solid, and the results are impressive. However, the paper needs significant revisions to better articulate its contribution relative to recent related work and to improve the clarity of the explanations. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact. A stronger positioning of the contribution, coupled with a more detailed comparative analysis, is essential for justifying acceptance.
