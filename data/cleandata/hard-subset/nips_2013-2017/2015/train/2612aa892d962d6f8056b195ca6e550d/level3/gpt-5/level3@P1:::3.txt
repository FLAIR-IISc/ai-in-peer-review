This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural network (NN) controllers to approximate near‑optimal feedback policies generated by Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The core idea is an alternating (and in practice, asynchronous distributed) procedure: (i) optimize trajectories with CIO under a quadratic penalty that encourages agreement with a parametric policy; (ii) train the policy by supervised regression to match the optimized actions at noisy versions of the sensory state. A perturbation-based derivation reuses the Gauss–Newton structure from CIO to compute local optimal feedback gains “for free,” enabling the network to learn how optimal actions change under small sensory perturbations. Actions are defined as desired velocities and contact forces, with a small recurrent state providing memory beyond the physical state. The method is validated on diverse 3D characters (swimmer, bird, multiple bipeds, quadrupeds), yielding visually stable, cyclic behaviors without motion capture, state machines, or task-specific features; execution uses a horizon-1 optimization at each step.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on CIO [12] and is closely related to trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14 [9], Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14 [11]), as well as classic NN control (NeuroAnimator [3]) and modern RL for continuous control (e.g., TRPO [14]) and model-based control with MuJoCo [16]. Compared to prior guided policy search in 2D, the paper targets diverse 3D locomotion/flying/swimming with a common architecture, introduces a practical feedback-gain computation within a direct trajectory optimization framework, and emphasizes robustness via noise injection.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall, with a coherent pipeline and plausible engineering choices (noise injection at inputs and hidden units; proximal regularization; GPU/cluster implementation). The perturbation-based feedback-gain computation is a useful contribution.
- However, several derivations and definitions are under-specified. Equation (6) and the CIO step rely heavily on external references, and Section 3.2’s notation makes the “optimal action under noisy input” mapping difficult to follow (lack of time indices and missing definitions for sX, aX).
- Experimental evidence is compelling visually and includes two ablations (no joint optimization; no noise), but the dataset is small (100–200 trials × 5 branches), and comparisons to alternative controllers are limited. Claims about generalization and robustness would be stronger with larger and more varied training/testing splits and stress tests (e.g., perturbations, terrain changes).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-written, but has notational inconsistencies and missing definitions (H, C, J, K in Eq. 6; use of stars; dropped time indices in Section 3.2). The asynchronous training architecture in Section 6 should be connected more clearly to Algorithm 1’s alternating scheme.
- Terminology: distinguish “data augmentation” (noise on inputs during data generation) from “noise injection” (perturbations during NN training); the analogy to denoising autoencoders is partial since targets also change via feedback gains.
- Numerous minor typos/style issues (e.g., “unity” → “unify”), acronym order, and equation/variable references should be cleaned up.

Originality:
- The combination of CIO with supervised NN policy learning is not entirely new, but the paper’s specific contributions—reusing CIO’s Gauss–Newton structure to obtain well-conditioned feedback gains; unified application across diverse 3D morphologies; horizon-1 execution—are noteworthy. The design choice to learn actions as desired velocities plus contact forces under an implicit policy is also interesting.

Significance:
- If robust, the approach can impact character animation and potentially robotics by turning high-quality offline trajectory optimization into interactive policies. The breadth of characters suggests generality.
- The lack of systematic comparisons to strong model-free/model-based baselines (e.g., TRPO, guided policy search variants, MPC with carefully tuned costs/contact handling), limited runtime/scaling analysis, and absence of code hinder the community’s ability to adopt and build on the work.

Specific questions and suggestions:
- Comparisons: Provide quantitative and qualitative comparisons against MPC with matched costs and horizons (cite appropriately), TRPO or other policy-gradient methods, and guided policy search variations (e.g., [9], [11]).
- Ablations: Separate the effects of sensory-state noise vs. hidden-layer noise; evaluate without feedback-gain targets (i.e., plain denoising vs. gain‑informed targets); study network depth/width beyond Table 1 with error bars.
- Runtime and scaling: Report policy inference time, the cost of the T=1 optimization during execution (CPU budget, constraints scaling), and how runtime scales with network size and character complexity; discuss feasibility for real-time applications with limited compute.
- Asynchrony vs. alternating: Explain how the asynchronous distributed training relates to Algorithm 1’s steps and proximal regularization; discuss convergence behavior empirically (curves) and sensitivity to η; justify using the same η in both subproblems.
- Initialization: Clarify how initial trajectories X are obtained in the first iteration; are network weights random and CIO ignores the penalty initially (small α/large η)?
- Role of α: Discuss annealing α (penalty weight) over training—does letting α → 0 help avoid over-regularization by the current NN (curriculum vs. permanent constraint)?
- Targets and goals: Confirm whether orientation (heading) is part of the goal; demonstrate adding a target orientation; quantify performance when the goal changes abruptly or lies outside the training distribution.
- Execution physics: At test time, constraints are enforced softly and may include root forces. Quantify the magnitude/frequency of such forces and their visual impact; discuss how to avoid them and implications for robotics.
- Notation/clarity fixes: Add time indices in Section 3.2; define all symbols in Eq. (6); ensure consistent star/superscript use; fix typos and missing articles.

Pros:
- Clear, practical method that unifies CIO with robust NN policy learning.
- Novel, efficient computation of feedback gains within a direct trajectory optimizer.
- Diverse and visually convincing 3D results with a single architecture.
- Sensible robustness techniques (noise on inputs and activations).

Cons:
- Limited and incomplete comparative evaluation; weak MPC baseline and no modern RL baselines.
- Small training set and modest generalization analysis.
- Missing runtime and scaling metrics critical for “interactive” claims.
- Several clarity and notation issues; code not provided.

Overall assessment: A promising, well-executed application of NN controllers guided by trajectory optimization, with a useful technical contribution (feedback-gain derivation) and strong qualitative results across diverse morphologies. Strengthen the experimental comparisons, clarify the methodology/notation, and provide runtime details. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address these concerns, particularly by adding stronger baselines and runtime/scaling analyses.
