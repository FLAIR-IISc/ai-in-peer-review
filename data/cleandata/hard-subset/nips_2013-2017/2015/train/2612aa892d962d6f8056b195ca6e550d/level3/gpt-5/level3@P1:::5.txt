This paper proposes an alternating training scheme that couples direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of a neural network policy that maps a compact sensory state to desired velocities, contact forces, and an update to a small recurrent memory. The method assumes full model knowledge and differentiable dynamics with contacts (Eq. 6), enforced as soft constraints in MuJoCo. Key ingredients include: (1) interleaving trajectory re-optimization with NN regression under a proximal regularization; (2) sensory-noise injection with first-order action corrections derived from optimal feedback gains computed cheaply via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss-Newton Hessian; (3) hidden-unit noise during training; and (4) a distributed, asynchronous implementation. The policy executes in real time by repeatedly solving a short-horizon (T=1) inverse-dynamics-style optimization to match the NN outputs while satisfying dynamics. Results span swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion in 3D; ablations show the value of joint optimization and noise.

Relation to prior work: The approach is closest to learning-from-trajectory-optimization methods (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14), but scales to 3D locomotion and introduces a practical way to obtain stabilizing feedback gains directly from a direct optimization pipeline. Compared to model-free RL (e.g., TRPO), the method leverages a known simulator and avoids exploration instabilities; compared to MPC, it biases trajectories toward regular, cyclic behaviors via the NN matching term. It also contrasts with graphics controllers (SIMBICON, ZMP/capture point methods) by avoiding task-specific architectures or contact schedules.

Strengths
- Clear engineering of an alternating scheme that is simple to implement with existing CIO and NN toolchains, plus a scalable distributed training setup.
- Novel and practically important computation of feedback gains that reuse CXX−1 via a perturbation/Woodbury argument, yielding well-conditioned gains without a separate LQG pass.
- Strong qualitative results across diverse 3D morphologies; emergent cyclic gaits without hand-crafted state machines.
- Ablation studies (“no joint” and “no noise”) empirically support the training design; architecture sweep provides some grounding of model choices.
- Real-time execution with an implicit, dynamics-consistent T=1 update is elegant and robust.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Core assumption not stated prominently: the method requires full, accurate, differentiable dynamics and contact modeling (Eq. 6), with soft constraint enforcement and permitted root forces during execution. Please state this up front in the introduction and abstract, and discuss implications for robotics with model mismatch.
- Organization: Sections 4 (neural policy) and 6 (distributed training) are disproportionate. Section 6 reads like implementation details that could be shortened or moved to supplement; Section 4 should be expanded.
- Recurrent units ambiguity: The abstract promises “a small number of recurrent units,” and Sec. 2 introduces r and ṙ. However, Sec. 4 describes only feedforward layers with tanh and hidden noise, without specifying the recurrence. Is the network purely feedforward with r treated as part of the input/output (i.e., an external memory state), or does it include explicit recurrent connections trained via BPTT? How many recurrent units are used, how are they initialized, and what role do they play beyond the physical state? Please detail the architecture and training of recurrence.
- “Stochastic policy” wording: At l. 405 you call the policy stochastic, but at execution time noise is removed and the mapping is deterministic. Please clarify that stochasticity is a training device (sensory and hidden noise), and specify whether any stochastic policy is ever executed at test time.
- Limited quantitative evaluation: Beyond one figure and a small architecture table, there are few metrics. Consider reporting: robustness to disturbances; success rates; tracking error distributions; control effort; timing (Hz) for the T=1 solver; sensitivity to λ, α, η; and generalization to unseen goals.
- Physical realism caveat: Because constraints are soft and root forces are allowed, results may be less suitable for robotics without further regularization. Please quantify residual constraint violations and discuss how to harden them.
- Comparisons: A stronger baseline comparison to MPC (with identical costs and horizon) and to a modern policy-gradient baseline (e.g., TRPO, or trajectory-centric RL with fitted local linear models) would help establish significance.

Quality
- The algorithmic components are sound, and the feedback-gain derivation is a valuable technical contribution. Claims are backed by ablations and diverse demonstrations, but would benefit from more quantitative robustness and efficiency metrics. Convergence of the alternating scheme is empirical; proximal terms and asynchrony are reasonable but could use more diagnostics.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, but the recurrence design, test-time determinism, and model assumptions need to be clarified. Consolidate training details, expand Sec. 4, and provide a concise algorithm summary with all variables and dimensions (including r).

Originality
- Combining CIO with NN regression is not new per se, but the specific stochastic training with gain-corrected targets and the feedback gain computation within a direct optimization framework are novel and useful. Demonstrations in full 3D across morphologies strengthen the contribution.

Significance
- High for character animation and model-based control communities; moderate for robotics given the model assumptions and soft constraints. The feedback-gain computation could be of broad interest beyond this application.

Questions for authors
- How many recurrent units are used, and what ablation shows their necessity?
- Is any BPTT used, or is r updated solely via supervised targets ṙt?
- What are the real-time runtimes (ms per step) for the T=1 solve across tasks?
- How sensitive is performance to λ in the gain computation? Any guidelines?
- Do contact schedules truly emerge, or are there implicit biases in CIO costs?
- How does the method perform under moderate model mismatch (e.g., perturbed masses/friction)?

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong technical idea (feedback gains from direct optimization), solid engineering, and impressive breadth of behaviors in 3D.
- Clear empirical evidence that joint optimization and noise matter.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient clarity on recurrence and “stochastic” policy; organizational issues.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and comparisons; reliance on full model knowledge and soft constraints reduces generality.

Overall: Promising and practically relevant contribution; I recommend acceptance after restructuring to foreground assumptions, clarify the recurrent architecture and determinism at test time, and strengthen quantitative evaluation.
