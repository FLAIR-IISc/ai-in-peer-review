This paper presents a method for training a small, recurrent neural network to serve as an interactive, real-time feedback controller for physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning. A powerful trajectory optimizer, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), is used to generate optimal state-action trajectories for various tasks. The neural network is then trained to mimic the optimizer's behavior, effectively distilling the complex, non-real-time optimization into a fast, parametric policy. The authors demonstrate that a single, unified network architecture can learn diverse and complex locomotion behaviors—including swimming, flying, bipedal walking, and quadrupedal trotting—without task-specific features or motion capture data. Key technical aspects include a joint optimization procedure that interleaves trajectory optimization and network training, the injection of noise to improve robustness, and a novel method for extracting feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer to better train the policy's response to perturbations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Impressive and General Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its experimental results. The ability to synthesize stable, realistic, and interactive controllers for such a wide variety of character morphologies and movement types (swimming, flying, walking) using a single, general-purpose algorithm is a significant achievement. This demonstrates a high degree of generality and pushes the state-of-the-art in automated character control.
2.  **Sound Methodology:** The proposed framework of interleaving trajectory optimization with policy learning is methodologically sound. The ablation studies in Figure 2 provide convincing evidence for the necessity of both the joint optimization scheme (as opposed to training on a static dataset) and the noise injection techniques. These experiments clearly support the authors' claims about the importance of their specific training procedure.
3.  **Potential Significance:** The work addresses a challenging and important problem with applications in animation, gaming, and robotics. By creating a method to automate the generation of high-quality, interactive controllers, this research is likely to be influential and built upon by others in the field.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the experimental work is strong, the paper's contribution and novelty could be clarified significantly with respect to prior and concurrent work. The exposition could also be improved in several places.

1.  **Positioning of Contribution:** The paper presents its contribution as a novel combination of existing techniques that scales to harder problems. However, it needs to be more precise about how it advances the state of the art.
    *   The framing sometimes feels like this is a fundamentally new control method. It would be more accurate to position it as a successful application and scaling of deep learning to approximate well-established trajectory optimization techniques for real-time use. The fact that trajectory optimization can produce realistic control is known; the key contribution here is learning a fast, interactive policy that reproduces this quality.
    *   The novelty relative to prior work on learning neural network policies via trajectory optimization is not clearly articulated. For example, Levine & Koltun [9] also use trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network policy. The authors state their method solves "substantially harder problems" (3D vs. 2D), but this claim requires more rigorous support.
    *   The work is very closely related to the Guided Policy Search line of work from Levine, Abbeel, and others. A direct and explicit comparison is needed. The authors should discuss how their approach differs and compares on key metrics such as learning speed (sample efficiency), the variety of body types supported, runtime computation cost, and robustness to environmental changes or perturbations.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** Several key claims and technical descriptions would benefit from further explanation.
    *   In Section 3.2, the authors state that using LQG can produce "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." This is an important justification for their proposed method, but it is not explained. Please clarify what "ill-conditioned" means in this context and why large gains are problematic (e.g., do they lead to numerical instability, high-frequency oscillations, or unrealistic jerky motion?).
    *   The discussion justifying the decision not to use LQG currently precedes the presentation of the proposed alternative. The paper's narrative flow would be improved by first presenting the proposed strategy for computing feedback gains and then moving the discussion of why LQG was not used to follow it, serving as a direct comparison and justification.
    *   The notation in the paragraph spanning lines 226–240 is confusing. The expression `s(X) = s̄` is used to define a perturbation, but the cost is then defined via `s(X) - s̄`, which seems circular. Please clarify the meaning of these symbols. Using more visually distinct symbols (e.g., avoiding bars, squiggles, and other similar diacritics for different concepts) would enhance readability.
    *   The claim that "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ" needs to be substantiated. What is the precise definition of "softer" (e.g., smaller L2 norm?), and relative to what baseline are they softer (presumably, gains from a standard LQG pass)? Furthermore, the intuition that allowing the initial state to change is beneficial needs more justification. Why is this a desirable property for producing better-conditioned feedback?

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable and well-executed piece of research. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate a powerful, general-purpose method for creating interactive controllers for a diverse set of complex characters. The primary weakness is the paper's positioning relative to recent, closely related work in the literature. By clarifying the specific technical novelties and providing a more direct comparison to existing methods (particularly those of Levine, Koltun, and Abbeel), the authors can significantly strengthen the paper's claimed contribution. The clarity of a few technical arguments could also be improved. Overall, the work is technically sound and the results are significant, making it a solid contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the issues of clarity and positioning in a revised version.
