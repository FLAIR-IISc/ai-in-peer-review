Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method for training recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex characters. The core idea is to alternate between trajectory optimization—specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12]—and supervised neural network regression. The policy maps a sensory state to an “action” that consists of desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state updates; dynamics are then enforced at execution via a short-horizon (T=1) optimization step that respects contact constraints. To improve robustness, the method injects noise in two places: (i) additive Gaussian noise in the sensory inputs combined with local linear targets using feedback gains computed from the trajectory optimizer; and (ii) additive noise in hidden activations during NN training. The optimization is implemented in a distributed, asynchronous fashion on the cloud with a GPU for NN training.

Empirically, the approach yields interactive, realistic control for a diverse set of 3D systems—swimmers, fliers, bipeds, and quadrupeds—without motion capture, state machines, or task-specific features. Ablations suggest that the alternating joint training and noise injection are central to generalization and stability, outperforming a naive supervised fit to a static set of CIO trajectories.

This work connects to prior efforts that combine trajectory optimization with function approximation, including Mordatch & Todorov [11] and Levine & Koltun [9], as well as model-based control in MuJoCo [16]. It also relates to policy search and deep RL methods [10, 14], RNN control [6], and traditional animation/robotics controllers using state machines or simplified models [7, 8, 13, 18, 21]. Relative to [9, 11], the paper tackles more complex 3D locomotion with varied morphologies and emphasizes robustness via noise-driven training and a practical distributed pipeline.

Strengths
- Unification: A single architecture and training pipeline produce stable, visually plausible behaviors across qualitatively different morphologies and media (ground, water, air) without hand-engineered state machines or motion capture.
- Robustness mechanisms: Input noise with targets adjusted via locally optimal feedback gains, plus hidden-layer noise, are principled choices to improve closed-loop stability and reduce overfitting.
- Practical pipeline: The alternating optimization is simple to implement using existing CIO and NN toolchains, with an effective distributed/asynchronous training setup.
- Execution-time control: The T=1 implicit step to enforce dynamics and contacts provides stability and permits relatively large timesteps.
- Empirical signals: Ablations (no joint training; no noise) and an architecture sweep give useful insight into what matters.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparative evaluation: The paper lacks systematic comparisons to guided policy search (e.g., [9]), TRPO [14], and MPC baselines beyond a brief qualitative discussion. No metrics (success rate, energy, foot slip, stability margins) are reported to quantify “stable and realistic.”
- Small training set: 100–200 trials with 5 branches each limits claims about generalization. It would help to report generalization to unseen goal distributions, perturbations, and initial conditions.
- Clarity and notation: Some important details are deferred to prior work. Equation (6) lacks explicit definitions for all terms; Section 3.2’s derivation and notation (e.g., time indices, roles of sX and aX) make the feedback-gain computation hard to follow without extensive reconstruction. The asynchronous training architecture in Section 6 departs from the clean alternation in Algorithm 1 and would benefit from earlier and tighter integration in the presentation.
- Missing practical details: No execution-time latency and CPU/GPU budgets are provided, despite real-time claims. It is not clear how the approach scales with character complexity and constraints, and what makes it viable for applications with strict runtime limits (e.g., games).
- Terminology and minor issues: “Unify” vs. “unity,” missing articles, typos, and some mismatched variable references. The “denoising autoencoder” analogy is tenuous given targets change with noise; better to distinguish “data augmentation with gain-adjusted targets” from “hidden noise regularization.”
- Action representation and realism: Actions are desired velocities and contact forces, with dynamics enforced softly; the presence of root forces at execution may reduce physical plausibility and limit transfer to hardware.

Quality
Technically solid overall, with a sensible objective, practical alternating optimization, and a novel feedback-gain-based target adjustment. However, several derivations and constraints are under-specified, and the evaluation does not yet support strong claims about superiority over existing policy search methods.

Clarity
Readable and well organized at a high level, but key details (Eq. 6 constraints, Sec. 3.2 derivation, asynchronous vs. alternating optimization) should be clarified. Numerous minor typos and notation inconsistencies should be addressed. Releasing pseudocode for the feedback-gain computation would greatly aid reproducibility.

Originality
Builds meaningfully on CIO and guided policy search ideas with a practical, robust pipeline and a clever perturbation-based feedback gain computation within direct trajectory optimization. While the components are related to prior work, the combination and demonstrated scope across 3D morphologies are noteworthy.

Significance
Promising for character animation and potentially robotics, offering a unified approach that avoids hand-engineered state machines. Significance would be strengthened by quantitative metrics, stronger baselines, runtime analysis, and code/data release.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Comparisons: Include quantitative MPC and guided policy search baselines (citing the MPC setup) and report metrics (task completion, energy, slip, CoM variance, falls).
- Noise ablations: Separate “no input noise,” “no feedback-gain target adjustment,” and “no hidden noise” to isolate their effects.
- Hyperparameters and schedules: Explain initialization of X and θ; justify using the same proximal η in both subproblems; report any annealing of α (policy-matching penalty) and explore its effect (curriculum vs. permanent constraint). Sensitivity to λ in Sec. 3.2 would be informative.
- Execution-time performance: Report latency and CPU/GPU usage per control step, and scaling with number of DoFs, contacts, and constraint stiffness. Clarify whether the goal includes orientation and how to incorporate target heading.
- Dynamics realism: Quantify any root forces during execution and assess the trade-off between stability and physical plausibility. Discuss implications for robotic transfer.
- Clarity: Define all symbols in Eq. (6), add time indices in Sec. 3.2, and reconcile Algorithm 1 with the asynchronous pipeline in Sec. 6. Fix typos (“unify,” etc.).
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code (or at least detailed pseudocode and configurations) to foster adoption.

Arguments for acceptance
- Unified, practical method that produces diverse, stable 3D behaviors without bespoke engineering.
- Thoughtful robustness design (noise with gain-adjusted targets; hidden noise).
- Useful engineering contribution (distributed asynchronous pipeline) with clear potential impact in animation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation and weak baselines; unclear comparative advantage over GPS/TRPO/MPC.
- Missing runtime analysis and scalability evidence for real-time applications.
- Clarity gaps and minor writing issues; several derivations are hard to reproduce from the text alone.

Overall
A promising, well-executed application of NNs plus trajectory optimization to interactive character control, with solid ideas likely to interest the NIPS community. Strengthening the empirical comparisons, clarifying derivations and the asynchronous scheme, and adding runtime metrics would significantly improve the paper’s impact.
