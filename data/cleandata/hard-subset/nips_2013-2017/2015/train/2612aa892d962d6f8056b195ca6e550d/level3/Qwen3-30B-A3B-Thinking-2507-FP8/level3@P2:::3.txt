

**Review for "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"**

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse character locomotion tasks. The authors propose an alternating optimization procedure that jointly trains neural networks using trajectories generated by Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), with noise injection techniques to improve robustness. The method enables real-time interactive control for swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters without task-specific features or state machines, using a single neural network architecture across all character types.

**Relation to Previous Work**
The paper builds upon trajectory optimization for character control (Mordatch et al., 2012, 2015) and neural network control (Levine & Koltun, 2014; Huh & Todorov, 2012). It extends prior work by demonstrating 3D locomotion where previous methods were limited to 2D, and by integrating CIO with neural networks through a novel alternating optimization framework. The authors correctly position their work against existing approaches, noting that direct RL methods (e.g., Mnih et al., 2013) produce "chaotic" behaviors compared to their more purposeful outputs.

**Quality**
The technical approach is well-motivated and demonstrates clear empirical success across diverse character types. The alternating optimization (Algorithm 1) and noise injection techniques effectively address overfitting concerns. However, the experimental evaluation has significant limitations: the training set is small (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments), weakening claims about generalization. The comparative evaluation is inadequate—only a naive "static training" baseline is tested, and the MPC comparison lacks sufficient detail or citation. The ablation study could be more granular (e.g., separating sensory noise from hidden layer noise).

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-structured, but several sections suffer from poor exposition. Section 3.2 (optimal feedback gains) contains confusing notation (dropped time indices) and insufficient explanation of the perturbation method. The asynchronous training architecture (Section 6) isn't clearly connected to the alternating optimization in Algorithm 1. Numerous minor issues persist: "unity" instead of "unify," missing articles, misplaced stars, and inconsistent variable references. The mathematical presentation in several places requires readers to consult external references for full understanding.

**Originality**
The core contribution—joint training of neural networks with trajectory optimization and noise injection—is novel in its specific implementation. While the components (CIO, neural networks, noise injection) have been used separately before, their integration for this specific application represents a meaningful advance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by demonstrating 3D locomotion where previous methods were limited to 2D.

**Significance**
The work has clear potential applications in character animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The ability to train a single controller for diverse character morphologies is practically valuable. However, the paper fails to address key practical concerns: runtime measurements, scalability with model complexity, and whether orientation targets can be easily incorporated. The lack of detailed comparisons to established real-time controllers (beyond a brief MPC mention) limits the demonstration of practical advantages.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
- Demonstrates successful 3D locomotion across diverse character types with a single architecture.
- Provides clear evidence that joint optimization and noise injection improve generalization over simpler baselines.
- Offers a conceptually simple and potentially adoptable method for practitioners.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
- Limited training set size weakens generalization claims.
- Insufficient comparative evaluation against relevant baselines (e.g., direct RL methods).
- Key technical aspects (optimal feedback gains calculation) are poorly explained.
- Missing runtime and scalability analysis critical for real-time applications.

**Overall Assessment**
This is a promising application of neural networks to character control with clear empirical results. The method represents a meaningful extension of prior work on trajectory optimization for locomotion. However, the paper falls short in several critical areas: experimental evaluation lacks rigor, technical exposition contains significant flaws, and practical considerations for real-world deployment are inadequately addressed. The current version does not sufficiently demonstrate the method's advantages over existing approaches or address the practical concerns for real-world adoption.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**  
The paper has merit but requires substantial revision to address the weaknesses in experimental evaluation, technical clarity, and practical demonstration before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
