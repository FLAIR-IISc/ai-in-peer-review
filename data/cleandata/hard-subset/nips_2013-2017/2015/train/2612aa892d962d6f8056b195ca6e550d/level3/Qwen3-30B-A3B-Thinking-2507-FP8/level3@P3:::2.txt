

# Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for interactive character control in physics simulators. The approach combines trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) with neural network policy learning, enabling a single architecture to control diverse behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking) across different body morphologies. The authors claim their method produces stable, realistic behaviors without motion capture or task-specific features.

The work builds on trajectory optimization techniques for character control, particularly the CIO method introduced by the authors in [12]. It also relates to prior work on neural network controllers, including the trajectory-optimization-based methods by Levine and Koltun [9] and Mordatch and Todorov [11]. The paper's claim that "this is possible is due to a number of technical improvements" requires clearer justification relative to these prior works.

## Strengths

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the method's ability to control diverse 3D characters (swimmers, fliers, bipeds, quadrupeds) with a single neural network architecture. The authors successfully show the network can handle unexpected task changes and perturbations. The distributed training architecture is well-designed, and the ablation studies (Fig. 2, Table 1) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of their joint optimization approach, noise injection, and trajectory re-optimization.

The paper makes a compelling case for the practical utility of the method, showing it enables interactive control without requiring motion capture or task-specific feature engineering. The video examples (referenced but not included here) would likely demonstrate the quality of the generated behaviors.

## Weaknesses

The most significant weakness is the unclear positioning of the contribution relative to existing literature. The paper claims "the method presented here solves substantially harder problems" than prior trajectory-optimization-based methods [9, 11], but does not provide a clear comparative analysis. The authors should explicitly compare with Abbeel and Levine's work [9] on metrics like learning speed, supported body variety, runtime computation cost, and robustness to environmental variability.

The claim that "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ" lacks sufficient explanation. The paper does not define "softer" relative to what, and the rationale for changing the initial state (rather than fixing it as in LQG) needs clearer justification. The discussion of "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" should be clarified with a concrete example of why these are problematic.

The notation in lines 226-240 is confusing (s(X)=\bar s and cost defined via s(X)-\bar s), and the visual similarity of symbols (bars and squiggles) should be disambiguated. The discussion of why LQG was not used should be moved to after the presentation of the proposed strategy for better exposition.

The paper does not adequately address prior work on neural network policy approximation, specifically [9] which explicitly uses neural networks to approximate step-by-step policy actions. The novelty relative to such prior work is unclear. The paper should more thoroughly analyze how their joint optimization approach differs from and improves upon these existing methods.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents sound experimental work with compelling results on diverse character control tasks. The method is technically well-executed, and the distributed training approach is practical for large-scale problems. However, the paper fails to adequately position its contribution relative to the substantial body of prior work in trajectory-optimization-based control and neural network policy learning.

While the approach is impressive in its application, it appears to be an application of deep learning to existing trajectory-optimization techniques rather than a fundamentally new method. The paper needs to clearly articulate what makes this approach novel compared to previous work, particularly [9] and [11], and provide a systematic comparison of their method's advantages.

The authors should clarify the technical claims about feedback gains, improve notation clarity, and move the LQG discussion to a more logical position in the manuscript. Without these revisions, the paper's contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work.

## Recommendation

This paper has strong experimental results but lacks sufficient novelty and clear positioning relative to recent literature. The technical execution is sound, but the contribution needs better justification. I recommend *weak rejection* with the understanding that with substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, the paper could become a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors must clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon prior work, particularly the trajectory-optimization-based methods by Levine and Koltun [9] and Mordatch and Todorov [11].
