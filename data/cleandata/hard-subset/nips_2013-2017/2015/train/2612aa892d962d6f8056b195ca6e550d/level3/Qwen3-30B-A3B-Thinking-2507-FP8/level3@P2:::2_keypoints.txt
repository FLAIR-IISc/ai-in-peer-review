- Paper proposes training RNNs as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse simulated locomotion (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) by combining trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network regression.  
- Core method: interleaved supervised learning and trajectory optimization to generate robust controllers; network outputs desired velocities with a small recurrent memory.  
- Key innovations highlighted: noise injection during training, trajectory optimization for feedback gains, and a distributed training framework.  
- Builds on prior trajectory-optimization control work (e.g., CIO, Levine & Koltun), extending prior 2D results to 3D and multiple morphologies.  
- Reviewer questions novelty: contribution appears incremental relative to prior work that also fits neural networks to trajectory-optimized policies.  
- Request for stronger quantitative comparisons with prior work ([9], [11]) on learning speed, supported body types, robustness, and computational cost.  
- Strength — Quality: technically sound with ablation studies showing joint optimization + noise are critical for stability.  
- Strength — Significance: same architecture successfully controls diverse tasks, demonstrating practical utility for animation and robotics.  
- Strength — Clarity: algorithm and training pipeline are well-structured and video supplements are effective.  
- Weakness — Originality: insufficiently justified novelty; claim of solving substantially harder problems (3D) needs quantification.  
- Weakness — Clarity/Notation: Sec. 3.2 notation around optimal feedback gains (e.g., s vs. s̄) is confusing and explanation order should be improved.  
- Weakness — Technical depth: claims that LQG yields ill-conditioned gains and that their method produces “softer” gains are unsubstantiated and need justification.  
- Weakness — Noise rationale: the role and novelty of noise injection compared to standard regularization/data augmentation is underdeveloped.  
- Pros for acceptance: practical, scalable method; strong empirical validation across four character types; rigorous ablations; well-executed distributed training.  
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient distinction from prior work, unsupported technical claims, unclear exposition harming reproducibility, limited comparison with other neural control methods (e.g., MPC).  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed application with strong empirical results but incremental novelty; recommendation — weak reject.  
- Suggested revisions: explicitly compare with [9] on key metrics, quantify and define “softer gains,” and revise Sec. 3.2 for clearer notation and logical flow.
