This paper presents a method for training a single, general-purpose neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for a diverse range of complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimizer, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of near-optimal behaviors, and then use this data to train a recurrent neural network policy. The key contributions that enable this approach to succeed on challenging 3D locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking) are a joint optimization framework that interleaves trajectory optimization and network training, the injection of noise to improve robustness, and a novel method for extracting optimal feedback gains directly from the CIO solver. This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization and function approximation [9, 11], but demonstrates its efficacy on a substantially more complex and diverse set of 3D characters than previously shown.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of work. The proposed algorithm, which alternates between optimizing trajectories and regressing the network policy, is well-motivated. The experimental results, particularly as demonstrated in the supplementary video, are highly compelling and convincingly support the claim that the method can generate stable, realistic, and interactive controllers for various morphologies. The ablation study in Section 8.1 provides strong evidence for the necessity of the joint optimization framework and noise injection, showing that naive training on a static dataset or training without noise leads to policies that are orders of magnitude worse and quickly become unstable.

A significant strength is the method proposed in Section 3.2 for calculating optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer. This is a clever and practical contribution, as it allows the system to generate appropriate target actions for the stochastically perturbed sensory states used in training, reusing computation already performed by the optimizer. This appears more principled and efficient than running a separate LQR pass.

However, a key limitation of the method, which should be stated more prominently, is its reliance on having a complete and accurate model of the system dynamics and contact forces (as required by CIO in Equation 6). This makes the approach highly suitable for applications in simulation, such as gaming and animation, but its direct application to real-world robotics, where such models are difficult to obtain, is challenging. This assumption should be made more explicit early in the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with a clear overview and motivation. However, there are significant issues with organization and clarity in certain sections.

1.  **Imbalanced Section Coverage:** The paper's structure feels disproportionate. Section 4, "Neural Network Policy Regression," is a critical component of the method but is extremely brief. In contrast, Section 6, "Distributed Training Architecture," dedicates significant space to implementation details (e.g., AWS instance types, NFS servers) that are of secondary importance to the core scientific contribution and could be condensed or moved to an appendix. The paper would be stronger if more detail were provided on the network training procedure.

2.  **Missing Details on Recurrent Architecture:** A major point of confusion arises from the discrepancy between the abstract/overview and the network description. The abstract explicitly mentions "a small number of recurrent units that implement memory states," and the state representation `st = [ qt rt q̇t−1 f t−1 ]` includes a recurrent state `rt`. However, Section 4, which describes the network, makes no mention of recurrence. It describes a standard 3-layer feed-forward network with tanh activations. This is a crucial omission. The authors must clarify how the recurrent state is updated and integrated. Is it a standard RNN layer? Is `ṙt` simply treated as another output of the feed-forward network, which is then integrated externally? This must be explained for the work to be reproducible.

3.  **Ambiguous Terminology:** In line 405, the method is described as using "stochastic neural networks." This term is potentially misleading. As described, the final policy `πθ` appears to be deterministic at execution time (Section 7). The *training* is stochastic, due to noise added to sensory inputs (Section 2.1) and hidden unit activations (Section 4). To improve precision, the authors should clarify that the network is trained *with* stochasticity to achieve robustness, rather than being an inherently stochastic policy.

**Originality:** The work is original and makes several novel contributions. While the general concept of learning a policy from an expert optimizer exists [9, 11], this paper's specific formulation is new. The method for extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a key novelty. Furthermore, the successful application of this framework to generate robust 3D locomotion for such a diverse set of characters is a novel and significant result that pushes the state of the art. The work is well-situated within the literature and appropriately cites related contributions.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. The ability to automatically synthesize robust, interactive controllers for arbitrary character morphologies from a high-level task description is a long-standing goal in computer graphics and an important problem in control and robotics. This paper presents a general and powerful framework that makes substantial progress towards this goal. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. The resulting controllers are compelling and demonstrate emergent, realistic gaits (e.g., bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting) without being explicitly programmed, which is an important result.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Presents a powerful, general framework for synthesizing controllers for complex 3D characters.
*   The experimental results are state-of-the-art and visually impressive, demonstrating control over a wide range of morphologies.
*   Contains novel technical contributions, particularly the method for extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer.
*   The ablation study provides strong empirical validation of the method's key components.

**Arguments against:**
*   Significant clarity issues need to be addressed, most importantly the complete lack of detail regarding the recurrent network architecture mentioned in the abstract.
*   The paper's organization is imbalanced, with too much focus on implementation details and not enough on the core network model.
*   The reliance on a perfect dynamics model is a major assumption that should be framed more clearly as a limitation.

### Conclusion and Suggestions

Overall, this paper presents a very promising and significant contribution. The results are excellent, but the paper requires restructuring and clarification to meet the standards for publication. The clarity issues, particularly the missing details on the recurrent components of the network, are substantial. However, these seem addressable in a revision. I would recommend acceptance if the authors can thoroughly address the following points:

1.  **Elaborate on the Recurrent Network:** Section 4 must be expanded to fully describe the recurrent architecture. Explain how `rt` and `ṙt` are computed and used by the network. Provide a diagram if necessary.
2.  **Rebalance the Paper:** Condense Section 6 and use the space to expand Section 4. The focus should be on the model and algorithm, not the cloud computing implementation.
3.  **Clarify "Stochastic Network":** Rephrase the description to clarify that a deterministic network is trained with stochasticity for robustness.
4.  **Acknowledge Model Dependency:** Add a sentence in the introduction or discussion to explicitly state that the requirement of an accurate dynamics model is a limitation of the current approach.
