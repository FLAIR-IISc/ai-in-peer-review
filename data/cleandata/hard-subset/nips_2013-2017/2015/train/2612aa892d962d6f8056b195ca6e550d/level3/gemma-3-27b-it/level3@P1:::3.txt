This paper presents a novel approach to interactive control of complex characters using recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The core idea is to train an RNN to mimic the behavior of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimizer, effectively learning a control policy through imitation and refinement. The authors address the challenges of scaling deep learning to high-dimensional control problems by combining supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, and leveraging a distributed stochastic optimization scheme. The method demonstrates impressive results, generating stable and realistic locomotion for diverse character morphologies – swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal – without relying on motion capture or hand-designed state machines. This work builds upon and significantly extends previous efforts in neural network control, particularly those leveraging trajectory optimization [9, 11], by successfully tackling problems in three-dimensional space where prior work was limited to two dimensions.

The strengths of this paper lie in its innovative combination of established techniques and its successful application to a challenging problem. The alternating optimization framework, intelligently blending the global optimality of CIO with the real-time capabilities of a neural network, is a compelling approach.  The inclusion of noise injection is well-motivated, addressing the inherent difficulties of learning control policies in closed-loop systems and preventing overfitting. The impressive visual results, demonstrated in the accompanying video, showcase the method’s potential for creating realistic and engaging character animations and control systems. Furthermore, the authors’ claim of achieving the same results across diverse character morphologies with a unified approach is a significant step towards generalizable control solutions, contrasting with traditional methods requiring bespoke architectures [21, 18, 13, 8, 7].

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of their experimental evaluation, the relatively small training dataset (100–200 trials) raises concerns about the robustness of the learned policies and their ability to generalize to unseen scenarios.  The comparative evaluation lacks depth. A cursory mention of Model Predictive Control (MPC) without citation is insufficient, and a systematic comparison against other reinforcement learning or direct policy optimization methods [3, 6, 14] is missing. This makes it difficult to assess the true advantage of the proposed method. The mathematical presentation, while generally clear, suffers from occasional lack of detail.  Equation (6), representing the physical constraints, is particularly dense and relies heavily on the reader's familiarity with the CIO framework [12].  Section 3.2, describing the computation of optimal feedback gains, is confusing due to notational inconsistencies and a lack of clarity in the derivation. The asynchronous distributed training detailed in Section 6 deviates significantly from the alternating scheme presented in Algorithm 1, and this discrepancy requires further clarification.

Several specific points warrant attention. The distinction between “data augmentation” (used to create training data) and “noise injection” (added during training) could be made more explicit, and the analogy to denoising autoencoders [17] feels tenuous.  The algorithmic choices, such as using the same learning rate η in both the trajectory optimization and neural network regression steps, are not adequately justified. Finally, the manuscript contains numerous minor writing errors, typos, and notational inconsistencies that detract from its overall clarity and professionalism.  Providing publicly available code would significantly enhance reproducibility and facilitate adoption of the method. Moreover, runtime measurements and a discussion of the algorithm’s scalability with increasing model complexity are crucial for assessing its suitability for real-time applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks for character control.
*   Demonstrated ability to generate stable and realistic locomotion for diverse character morphologies.
*   Addresses key challenges in scaling deep learning to high-dimensional control problems.
*   Potential for significant impact in areas such as robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation and concerns about generalization.
*   Weak comparative evaluation lacking systematic comparison to alternative methods.
*   Lack of clarity in certain mathematical derivations and algorithmic details.
*   Numerous minor writing and notational issues.
*   Lack of publicly available code.



Overall, this is a promising and well-executed work that makes a valuable contribution to the field of character control. However, addressing the identified weaknesses – particularly strengthening the experimental evaluation, providing a more thorough comparative analysis, and improving the clarity of the presentation – would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and warrant a stronger recommendation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon addressing the concerns outlined above.
