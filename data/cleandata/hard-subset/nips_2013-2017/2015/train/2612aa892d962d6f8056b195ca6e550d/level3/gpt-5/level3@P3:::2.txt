Summary and relation to prior work
The paper trains small recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for physically simulated characters, producing interactive locomotion and cyclic behaviors (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking) across diverse morphologies. Training alternates between trajectory optimization (CIO) and supervised regression of the network to mimic the optimal actions and feedback produced by the optimizer, with injected input and hidden noise to improve robustness. A key technical piece is a perturbation-based method to extract local feedback gains from the Gauss-Newton Hessian of the trajectory objective, avoiding a separate LQG pass that the authors report yields ill-conditioned gains. The trained policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces; at execution, a T=1-step optimality condition enforces physics and tracks the policy output at real time.

This paper is best viewed as an instance of trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning, very close to guided policy search (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014), NeuroAnimator (Grzeszczuk et al. 1998), and related lines that learn step-by-step policies from optimal trajectories. The contribution here is primarily in applying this recipe to 3D contact-rich animation with a unified architecture across bodies/behaviors, plus engineering choices (noise-injection with gain targets, distributed asynchronous optimization, a specific gains-from-Hessian derivation).

Strengths
- Solid engineering and demonstrable breadth: one architecture drives multiple 3D characters and clearly produces natural cyclic behaviors without hand-crafted state machines or motion capture.
- Sensible training loop: alternating trajectory optimization and supervised regression with proximal regularization and noise leads to stable policies; ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) support design choices.
- Practical feedback-gain extraction that reuses the trajectory optimizer’s Gauss-Newton machinery and admits a tunable “softness.”
- Real-time execution via T=1 optimization is appealing and simple to implement in the same framework.
- Good systems work: asynchronous distributed implementation, reasonable training time.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Positioning vs prior work: The method appears as an application of existing trajectory-optimization-based control plus supervised policy learning. Prior work has already established that trajectory optimization can synthesize realistic control signals and that neural policies can imitate them (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014; Huh & Todorov 2011; NeuroAnimator 1998). The paper should more explicitly articulate what is new: is it (i) scaling CIO-guided policy learning to 3D multi-contact characters, (ii) the particular feedback-gain extraction, (iii) the specific noise-injection scheme with gain targets, or (iv) unification across morphologies? Please distinguish from [9] and closely related Abbeel/Levine guided policy search variants, and cite quantitative differences.
- Missing quantitative comparisons: The closest related work (e.g., guided policy search) should be compared on:
  - Learning speed/sample efficiency: number of optimizer iterations, trajectories, wall-clock.
  - Body/behavior variety: how many morphologies gaits succeed with; success rates over random seeds; distance/time to failure.
  - Runtime cost: policy evaluation latency, T=1 solve time, CPU/GPU usage.
  - Robustness: distributional shifts (terrain slope, friction changes), external pushes; recovery rates.
Without such metrics, it is hard to assess significance beyond a compelling demo.
- “Very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”: Please define and quantify. For example, report norms and condition numbers (or singular value spectra) of the gain matrices; show closed-loop eigenvalues/spectral radii and actuator saturations. Explain why the LQG pass yields ill-conditioned gains here (state scaling? unmodelled soft-contacts? cost design?), and whether simple regularization (state/control penalties, smoothness terms, scaling) would remedy it.
- “Softer feedback gains according to λ”: Define “softer” precisely (e.g., lower gain norms, better condition numbers, smoother control signals). Provide plots of gain norms and closed-loop stability margins versus λ, and task-tracking/energy trade-offs. Clarify the intuition that softening the state constraint allows small trajectory re-optimization instead of aggressive instantaneous correction; show that this reduces sensitivity and torque spikes in practice.
- Exposition: move the discussion of why LQG was not used until after presenting your perturbation-based gain strategy; this will improve flow.
- Notation clarity (lines 226–240): The current use of bars and similar symbols is confusing. Consider s* for the nominal sensory state, ŝ for the perturbed target, and clear typography for vectors vs functions. Ensure consistency in s(X)=s* versus costs written as ||s(X)−ŝ||².
- Policy/output semantics: “Thus the network is not learning a control policy, but rather the dynamics under an implicit policy” is confusing—mapping s→(q̇, ṙ, f) is a policy. Please rephrase and clarify why outputting desired velocities/forces is preferable to torques or direct actions, and discuss consequences for stability/realism given the soft dynamics constraints (root forces).
- Evaluation breadth: The qualitative results are strong, but please add quantitative performance on:
  - Energy/effort usage, step symmetry/regularity, gait frequency, COM variability.
  - Success under perturbations and task changes (goal jumps) with statistics and confidence intervals.
  - Ablations on recurrence size; is recurrence necessary for each task?

Quality
Technically sound overall; the gain derivation is reasonable, and the training loop is validated via ablations. However, claims about generality and superiority to alternatives are not yet supported by quantitative comparisons to closely related methods.

Clarity
Generally well written, but several presentation issues hinder clarity: ordering of the LQG discussion, notation around the augmented cost and gains, and the ambiguous “implicit policy” phrasing. Additional diagrams of the alternating optimization and the gain computation would help.

Originality
Moderate. The approach combines known components; the most original element is the practical feedback-gain extraction tied to CIO. The primary novelty claim—one architecture across diverse 3D cyclic behaviors—needs quantitative evidence and a clearer contrast to guided policy search.

Significance
Potentially useful to ML/graphics/robotics practitioners; the demonstrations are compelling. Significance would be higher with rigorous comparisons and robustness metrics.

Pros
- Demonstrates an effective, small recurrent policy across diverse 3D characters.
- Practical gain computation integrated with trajectory optimization.
- Clear engineering leading to real-time control and robust rollouts.
- Meaningful ablations.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to guided policy search and prior trajectory-optimization-driven policy learning.
- No direct comparisons to Abbeel/Levine-style methods or MPC baselines on quantitative metrics.
- Ambiguities in the feedback-gain discussion, notation, and policy semantics.
- Reliance on soft dynamics (root forces) may limit physical realism; discuss implications.

Recommendation
Borderline; I lean Weak Reject unless the camera-ready addresses positioning vs guided policy search, adds quantitative comparisons and robustness metrics, and clarifies the gain/LQG and notation issues. The experimental work is sound and promising, and with these additions the paper could make a solid NIPS contribution.
