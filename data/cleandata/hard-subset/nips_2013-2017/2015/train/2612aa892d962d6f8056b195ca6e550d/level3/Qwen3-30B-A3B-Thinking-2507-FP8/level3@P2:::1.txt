

This paper presents a compelling integration of trajectory optimization and neural networks to generate robust, real-time controllers for diverse physical characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) in 3D. The core innovation lies in a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly trains a neural policy (mapping sensory states to actions) and optimizes trajectories via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), while embedding optimal feedback gains to enhance stability. Crucially, the network learns to approximate *dynamics under an implicit policy* (not a policy itself), with actions defined as velocities to enable closed-loop control. The method avoids motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines—addressing a key limitation in prior work like [9,11], which was restricted to 2D locomotion.

**Quality.** The work is technically sound and rigorously validated. The authors provide a thorough ablation study (Fig. 2, Table 1) demonstrating that: (1) joint trajectory-policy optimization outperforms naive static training by two orders of magnitude; (2) noise injection (sensory and hidden-unit) prevents overfitting and enables robustness; and (3) the 3-layer, 250-unit architecture offers the best complexity-accuracy tradeoff. The feedback gain derivation (Sec 3.2) is novel and efficient, reusing trajectory optimization computations to avoid ill-conditioned gains from LQG. The implementation details (Sec 6) and extensive experiments across diverse morphologies (swimmers, birds, bipeds, quadrupeds) convincingly show generalizability. The only minor limitation is the lack of analysis on failure modes under extreme perturbations, though the ablations cover typical instability scenarios.

**Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and self-contained. The problem setup (Sec 2) precisely defines sensory states and actions, avoiding ambiguity. Algorithm 1 elegantly formalizes the distributed optimization, and key concepts (e.g., feedback gains, noise injection) are motivated with intuitive explanations and mathematical rigor. Figures (e.g., Fig. 1, supplementary video) vividly demonstrate results without relying on external materials. The writing consistently prioritizes accessibility for cross-disciplinary readers (graphics, control, ML), making the technical contributions highly digestible.

**Originality.** This work significantly advances beyond prior NIPS contributions. While neural controllers for control exist (e.g., [3,6,10]), they typically yield chaotic behaviors or require task-specific tuning. The fusion of CIO with neural policy training—specifically embedding feedback gains and interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization—is novel for high-dimensional 3D locomotion. The authors explicitly contrast their approach with [9,11] (2D only) and MPC (which produces non-cyclical, unstable trajectories; Fig. 3), clarifying the distinct contribution: *a unified method that learns stable, purposeful behaviors without task-specific engineering*. Related work in NIPS 2013-2015 (e.g., [12, 21]) focuses on trajectory optimization alone, not neural policy transfer.

**Significance.** The results are highly significant for both theory and practice. The method enables interactive, real-time control across *diverse morphologies* using a single architecture—a major step toward scalable character animation and robotics. The avoidance of motion capture or hand-crafted state machines lowers barriers to adoption. The demonstrated behaviors (e.g., bird flapping, dog trotting, water treading) are physically realistic and purposeful, with clear implications for biomechanics (e.g., modeling muscle-reflex interactions [2]) and robotics (e.g., robust controllers for real-world perturbations [9]). The work also opens new avenues for studying sensorimotor systems (e.g., simulating sensor delays [2]).

**Pros and Cons Summary:**  
*   **Pro:** Solves a long-standing challenge (3D locomotion without task-specific design) with a unified, scalable method; rigorous ablations prove necessity of key components; exceptional clarity; broad applicability across physics-based domains.  
*   **Con:** Training requires substantial compute (2.5h on AWS), though this is justified by the problem complexity; future work should explore manipulation tasks beyond locomotion (mentioned in Sec 9).  

**Recommendation.** This paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. It makes a substantial methodological contribution that advances the state of the art in neural control for complex physical systems. The technical depth, empirical validation, and cross-disciplinary relevance align perfectly with NIPS' focus on high-impact ML research. The paper is a model of clarity and rigor, and its results—particularly the ability to learn stable controllers for diverse 3D characters without task-specific features—represent a meaningful leap beyond prior work. While training costs are non-trivial, they are a practical trade-off for the demonstrated generality and robustness. The work is clearly within NIPS scope (control, robotics, neural networks) and addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrably better way than existing methods.
