This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for complex dynamical systems, achieving stable and realistic behaviors across diverse tasks like swimming, flying, and walking with varying morphologies. The approach combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO), injecting noise during training, and adapting to changes in task specifications. The core idea is to leverage trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network, effectively learning a control policy through mimicking optimal actions. This builds on prior work in reinforcement learning and stochastic optimal control, but importantly extends it to high-dimensional continuous spaces. The authors emphasize that the method’s success hinges on technical improvements enabling control in 3D, surpassing previous 2D limitations.

The paper rightly situates itself within a long line of research attempting to automate controller design, contrasting its approach with traditional methods reliant on motion capture or state machines, and acknowledging the challenges of applying deep learning to control problems – specifically the need for precise, real-valued outputs and dealing with closed-loop system dynamics. The authors appropriately reference relevant work, including recent trajectory optimization-based methods [9, 11] and earlier neural network control approaches [3, 6, 10, 14].  The focus on unifying disparate approaches across different character types is a laudable goal.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates impressive results across a wide variety of complex locomotion tasks and character morphologies. The video accompanying the submission (and mentioned prominently) is a crucial element of this strength, providing compelling visual evidence of the method’s efficacy.
*   **Novel Combination of Techniques:** Combining trajectory optimization with neural network training is a sensible and effective approach. Leveraging CIO to generate training data is a key contribution, and the inclusion of noise injection and training for task specification changes further enhance robustness.
*   **Scalability:** The distributed training architecture (Section 6) is well-described and demonstrates an understanding of the computational demands of this problem. Addressing the scaling challenges is important for practical application.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of learning complex controllers in high-dimensional spaces and the limitations of existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Knowledge Requirement:** The method's reliance on a full dynamical model of the character (as evidenced by Equation 6) is a significant limitation that isn’t fully discussed.  This substantially restricts applicability to scenarios where an accurate model is readily available, and doesn’t address model-free control.
*   **Disproportionate Section Lengths:** Sections 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) and 6 (Distributed Training Architecture) are overly detailed in comparison to other sections, particularly Section 2 (Overview) which lacks sufficient depth. Section 4, specifically, fails to adequately explain the role of the recurrent units mentioned in the abstract, a critical component of the architecture.
*   **Clarity of "Stochastic" Policy:**  Line 405 describes the policy as “stochastic”, which is confusing. While stochasticity is *introduced during training* (via noise injection), the policy itself – a neural network producing deterministic outputs given a state – doesn’t appear inherently stochastic. This warrants clarification.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While the ablation study in Section 8.1 is a good start, it could be more comprehensive. The impact of different noise levels, network architectures, or trajectory optimization parameters could be further explored.
*   **Lack of Comparison to MPC:** The paper mentions Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8, but doesn’t provide a direct comparative analysis. A discussion of the trade-offs between these two approaches would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a compelling and technically sound approach to learning complex controllers for diverse dynamical systems. The empirical results are impressive, and the combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a clever and effective strategy. However, the limitations regarding model knowledge, the imbalance in section coverage, and the clarity issues surrounding the “stochastic” policy warrant attention. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The method achieves state-of-the-art results in complex locomotion control.
*   The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is novel and effective.
*   The paper addresses important challenges in scaling deep learning to control problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The requirement for a full dynamical model limits the practical applicability of the method.
*   The paper lacks sufficient depth in certain key areas (e.g., the role of recurrent units).
*   Some aspects of the presentation are unclear or require clarification.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically clarifying the role of recurrent units, ensuring the clarity of the "stochastic" policy description, and providing a more balanced presentation with greater depth in the overview and relevant sections. A more thorough discussion of the model knowledge requirement and its limitations would also strengthen the paper. Restructuring the paper to emphasize the core contributions and improve flow would be beneficial.
