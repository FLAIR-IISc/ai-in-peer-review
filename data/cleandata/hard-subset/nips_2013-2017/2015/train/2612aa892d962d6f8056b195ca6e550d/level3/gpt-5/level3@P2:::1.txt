Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a method for training recurrent neural network controllers that operate as near-optimal feedback policies for complex, nonlinear characters (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) in 3D. The approach alternates between direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised policy regression. A key technical contribution is to inject sensor noise during training and label noisy inputs with actions corrected via locally optimal feedback gains, which are efficiently extracted from the trajectory optimizer through a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss-Newton machinery. The policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces, and the system executes with a 1-step constrained optimization that enforces dynamics as soft constraints, enabling real-time control with large timesteps.

This work is closely related to guided policy search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014; iLQG/LQR-style local gains). Compared to those, the paper (i) derives a practical way to obtain well-conditioned feedback gains within a direct optimization framework (CIO) rather than a separate LQG pass, (ii) incorporates these gains directly into supervised targets for noisy inputs, and (iii) demonstrates robust, realistic behaviors across diverse 3D morphologies without motion capture or handcrafted state machines. The comparison with MPC highlights how the joint learning objective regularizes towards predictable, cyclic gaits.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The alternating optimization with proximal terms is reasonable and appears to converge in practice. The derivation of feedback gains via a soft-constrained perturbation (using the Woodbury identity and the Gauss-Newton inverse already computed) is elegant and cost-effective. The denoising-style noise injection in both inputs and hidden activations is well-motivated and supported by ablations.
- Empirical support: The paper demonstrates diverse, stable behaviors and includes ablations for joint optimization and noise injection, as well as a small architecture study. The distributed training architecture and timings are practical.
- Limitations: Quantitative evaluation is limited. Aside from training/test losses and some qualitative video evidence, there is little task-level benchmarking (success rates, robustness envelopes, energy/efficiency, constraint violations). The execution step enforces dynamics softly and may include root forces; more reporting on physical fidelity would strengthen claims, especially for robotics transfer. Comparisons to GPS/iLQG/TRPO/DDPG-style methods are not provided beyond qualitative discussion.

Clarity
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The optimization objectives, alternation scheme, and implementation details are explained with care. The feedback-gain derivation is concise and readable. Minor suggestions:
- Clarify units and scaling for sensory features and actions, especially the choice to command velocities and contact forces rather than torques, and how this impacts real-world deployment.
- Report magnitudes of soft-constraint penalties and typical residuals during execution (e.g., root forces), to help assess physical realism.
- Expand on the role of recurrent units: what temporal dependencies do they capture, and how sensitive are results to the memory size?

Originality
Embedding locally optimal feedback gains into the supervised targets for noisy inputs, with gains obtained directly (and stably) from a Gauss-Newton CIO pass, is novel and practically important. The unification and scaling to diverse 3D morphologies without task-specific state machines or mocap also represent a meaningful step beyond prior trajectory-optimization-plus-policy-learning approaches. The idea of outputting desired velocities and closing the loop with a 1-step constrained optimizer is an interesting design choice that differentiates this work from torque-policy baselines.

Significance
The method advances the state of the art in learning robust, realistic, and interactive controllers for complex characters. It offers a compelling bridge between graphics-style trajectory optimization and neural policy learning, likely to be adopted and extended in both communities. The results are strong, and the training/runtime system is practical. The approach could influence robust control policy training beyond animation, though demonstrating transfer to hardware or stricter physical constraints would further increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous presentation and well-motivated design choices.
- Elegant perturbation-based computation of feedback gains within CIO.
- Principled noise-injection scheme coupled with gain-labeled targets that improves robustness.
- Strong qualitative results across diverse 3D systems with a single architecture and no task-specific state machines or mocap.
- Practical distributed implementation and real-time execution strategy.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation; few task-level metrics (robustness, energy, constraint violation).
- No direct empirical comparison to GPS/iLQG/TRPO/DDPG on matched tasks.
- Soft constraint enforcement and direct command of contact forces may limit realism for robotics and complicate transfer; more analysis is needed.
- The role and necessity of recurrence are not deeply probed (ablation on memory size/absence would be helpful).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of λ for gain computation, and to α/η in the proximal-penalized alternation?
- What are typical magnitudes of dynamics residuals/root forces during execution? Can these be reduced without degrading performance?
- Have you tested robustness to unmodeled disturbances or model mismatch (e.g., mass/inertia variations)?
- How much does the recurrent state contribute versus a purely feedforward policy under your noise-injection scheme?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative benchmarks: distance/heading tracking error distributions, fall rates, recovery from pushes, energy/torque costs, constraint residuals.
- Add comparisons to GPS/iLQG/TRPO/DDPG on at least one morphology to contextualize gains from the proposed gain-labeling and CIO coupling.
- Include ablations on recurrent units, noise levels, and removal of feedback-gain targets to isolate contributions.
- Discuss pathways to enforcing hard contact constraints or torque policies to facilitate robotics transfer.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and clear technical contribution (feedback gains within CIO + denoising labels).
- Demonstrated generality across diverse 3D characters with realistic, stable gaits.
- Practical, scalable training and real-time execution.
- Bridges important areas (trajectory optimization, neural control, graphics/robotics).

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of head-to-head baselines on common metrics.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of robustness and physical realism.
- Some choices (soft constraints, force outputs) may limit direct applicability to hardware without further work.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that, with modest additions to quantitative evaluation and comparisons, merits acceptance.
