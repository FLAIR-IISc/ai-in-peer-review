This paper presents a framework for training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse, high-DOF dynamical characters (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion). The core idea is to alternate between trajectory optimization (via Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and neural policy regression, with additive sensory noise and an explicit use of locally optimal feedback gains to shape the regression targets. The network outputs desired velocities, recurrent state updates, and contact forces, effectively learning dynamics under an implicit policy rather than direct torques. A distributed, asynchronous implementation with proximal regularization enables scaling to large datasets; at test time, the learned policy is executed in real time, with a single-step optimization enforcing dynamics consistency. Ablations demonstrate that both noise injection and joint optimization are critical. Compared to related work (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14), the method tackles harder 3D tasks and introduces a practical way to extract stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits at the intersection of trajectory optimization and policy learning. It extends the “learn from optimizer” paradigm (e.g., Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov) by (1) folding noisy inputs and corresponding linearized feedback targets into policy training, (2) using a perturbation-based method to compute feedback gains leveraging the Gauss-Newton structure already computed by CIO, and (3) an asynchronous alternating scheme with proximal terms. Compared to model-free RL in continuous control (e.g., TRPO; Huh & Todorov; and earlier Neuroanimator), the behaviors here are more stable and purposeful, with clear cyclic gaits in 3D. The comparison to MPC highlights that the joint optimization regularizes the solution space toward predictable, low-variance motions.

Quality:
- Soundness: The alternating optimization (trajectory subproblems + NN regression) is technically solid, and the perturbation-based gains derivation is well-motivated and computationally efficient via the Woodbury identity and reuse of CXX−1. The training pipeline (noise on sensors and hidden units; proximal regularization; GPU SGD) is coherent and robust.
- Evidence: Results are compelling across several morphologies; the ablation studies convincingly show the benefits of noise and joint optimization. However, quantitative evaluation is limited (primarily objective values), and no direct comparisons are provided against strong model-free baselines on identical tasks (e.g., TRPO or contemporaneous direct RL) or against LQG-based gains computed on the same trajectories.
- Execution-time optimization (Section 7): Re-optimizing a one-step objective to enforce dynamics is effective and explains robustness at 50ms timesteps, but it blurs the line between a pure reactive policy and a hybrid policy+MPC scheme. A clearer motivation and discussion of when/why the learned policy alone is insufficient (e.g., residual regression error vs. soft dynamics constraints) would strengthen the work. Also, the use of soft constraints/root forces may reduce physical plausibility for robotics scenarios.

Clarity:
- The paper is well-written and organized, with sufficient implementation detail to reproduce the approach (architecture, noise scales, distributed setup). The derivation of feedback gains is clearly presented.
- Please expand the motivation for the T=1 optimization at test time: what failure modes occur if you forward-integrate the network outputs with a stiff/implicit integrator? Is the extra step mainly correcting policy approximation error, enforcing contacts, or compensating for using velocities/forces as outputs?
- Line 153 (“Similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches…”): please add references to proximal methods in stochastic/online settings (e.g., Boyd et al., Foundations and Trends in Machine Learning 2011 for ADMM; Xiao, Regularized Dual Averaging for Stochastic Optimization, NIPS 2010; or Schmidt et al., Optimization Methods for Large-Scale Machine Learning, 2013).
- Typo at line 89: “to unify.”

Originality:
- The overall system is a thoughtful synthesis of known components (CIO, alternating optimization, denoising-like training, proximal regularization), but two contributions stand out: (i) computing stabilizing feedback gains efficiently within a direct trajectory optimization pipeline, and (ii) integrating those gains into noisy regression targets to train a robust recurrent controller. While not a new theoretical framework, the engineering is nontrivial and advances the state of practice for 3D character control.

Significance:
- The demonstrated diversity and stability of behaviors in 3D is impressive and likely of high interest to graphics/animation and to parts of the RL/control community focused on contact-rich locomotion. The approach offers a practical path to real-time controllers without motion capture or hand-crafted state machines. Broader robotics impact may require harder dynamics enforcement (hard contact constraints, no root forces) and comparisons on real-robot surrogates. The reliance on an execution-time optimization step also narrows the gap to MPC, though the policy reduces the horizon to one step and delivers real-time rates.

Questions and suggestions:
- Please quantify the magnitude of root forces during execution and discuss how results change with harder constraint enforcement or smaller soft penalties.
- Report additional metrics: success rates over random start/goal distributions, perturbation recovery tests, and rollout horizon until failure without the T=1 optimization.
- Provide a comparison to an LQG gain computation around the same CIO trajectories (stability, conditioning, and empirical performance).
- Discuss the design choice to output desired velocities and contact forces instead of torques. Could the method learn torques directly with similar robustness?
- Detail compute budgets: time per CIO iteration and per NN epoch; data volume seen per alternating step; sensitivity to the number of branched segments.

Pros:
- Strong, diverse 3D results; stable cyclic gaits without state machines or mocap.
- Elegant use of perturbation-based gains and noise-aware targets for robust policy learning.
- Practical distributed training pipeline; clear ablations showing necessity of joint optimization and noise.
- Clear writing and reproducible detail.

Cons:
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of comparisons to strong model-free baselines on identical tasks.
- Execution-time re-optimization step reduces conceptual purity of “policy-only” control and may mask policy approximation gaps.
- Use of soft dynamics constraints/root forces raises questions for physical fidelity in robotics.
- Novelty is more in system integration and engineering than in new theoretical insight.

Overall: A strong and well-executed system paper with impressive empirical results and thoughtful methodological contributions. I lean toward acceptance, while encouraging the authors to strengthen quantitative evaluations, clarify the execution-time optimization rationale, and broaden comparisons.
