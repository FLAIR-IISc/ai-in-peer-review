- Proposes an alternating optimization that jointly learns trajectories via CIO and a recurrent neural policy, using Gauss–Newton-derived feedback gains and one-step execution-time optimization to enforce dynamics and large timesteps.
- Technically strong and thoroughly implemented with convincing ablations showing benefits of sensory noise and joint trajectory–policy learning; visually compelling results.
- Unclear necessity and justification for the execution-time one-step optimization — may be compensating residual costs, policy regression error, or model mismatch.
- Raises concern whether soft dynamics constraints (and possible root forces) allow physically implausible states during execution.
- Paper is clearly written and well organized; minor clarity issues: missing citations at line 153, need to explain why proximal parameter-space terms help, and a typo at line 89.
- Main novelty is engineering and system design (combination of known methods and feedback-gain reuse) rather than new theoretical insights.
- Results are impressive across diverse 3D morphologies, but broader impact is unclear to the reviewer.
- Requests stronger quantitative comparisons to direct RL baselines and MPC beyond qualitative differences.
- Pros: strong empirical performance across characters and scalable distributed implementation.
- Pros: clear demonstration of value of training noise and joint learning.
- Pros: practical alternating scheme that reuses mature solvers and per-timestep re-optimization enabling large timesteps.
- Cons: limited theoretical novelty.
- Cons: execution-time optimization rationale needs clarification.
- Cons: potential physical plausibility issues under soft constraints.
- Overall: positive assessment on quality and engineering, but uncertain about novelty and broader significance.
