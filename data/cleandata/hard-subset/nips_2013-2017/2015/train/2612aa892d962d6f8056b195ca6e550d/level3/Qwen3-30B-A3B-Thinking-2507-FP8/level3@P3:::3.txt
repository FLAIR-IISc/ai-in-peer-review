

# Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse character control tasks, including swimming, flying, and biped/quadruped locomotion. The authors propose a novel alternating joint training scheme between Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and neural networks, combined with noise injection techniques to improve robustness. This work builds upon prior trajectory optimization methods like CIO [12] and recent neural network-based control approaches [9, 11], but extends them to three-dimensional complex character control with real-time capabilities.

**Strengths:**

The paper's main contributions are well-motivated and address significant challenges in character control: the difficulty of obtaining training data for neural network controllers, the need for real-time performance, and the requirement for robustness to perturbations. The alternating joint training approach effectively combines the precision of trajectory optimization with the generalization capabilities of neural networks. The noise injection techniques (both in sensory inputs and hidden layers) provide a principled way to improve robustness without requiring extensive additional training data, which is particularly valuable given the limited training set size (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments).

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the method's ability to generate stable, realistic behaviors across diverse character morphologies (swimming, flying, biped, quadruped) without task-specific features or state machines. The supplementary video provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness in generating complex, purposeful movements.

**Weaknesses:**

The most significant limitation is the small training set size (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments), which weakens claims about generalization and overfitting. The comparative evaluation is notably weak, with only a brief MPC comparison provided (no citation) and no systematic comparisons to other methods. The paper would be significantly strengthened by more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art neural network control approaches.

The mathematical presentation suffers from several deficiencies. Key components like the CIO step (Section 3) and Equation 6 lack sufficient detail and require readers to consult external references. Section 3.2 contains confusing notational issues (dropped time index t) that make the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure unclear. The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs substantially from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1 and should be connected more clearly.

Several practical aspects are missing: runtime measurements, scaling with model complexity, and analysis of whether the target includes orientation (facing angle) as well as position. The terminology needs clarification, particularly distinguishing "data augmentation" from "noise injection" and questioning the denoising-autoencoder analogy. The paper would benefit from more granular experimental ablations (e.g., separate "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" variants).

**Specific Suggestions:**

1. Clarify whether the target includes orientation (facing angle) as well as position, and explain how adding a target angle would be straightforward.

2. Provide runtime measurements and scaling analysis with model complexity, especially important for real-time applications like video games.

3. Expand the comparative evaluation to include more recent neural network control methods beyond MPC.

4. Improve mathematical presentation by defining all key terms and providing more detail for Equation 6 and the CIO step.

5. Address the confusing notational issues in Section 3.2 and clarify the relationship between the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1 and the asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6.

6. Provide code to improve reproducibility.

7. Conduct more granular ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different noise injection techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising and well-executed application of neural networks to character control, with a clear technical contribution in the alternating joint training scheme. The method successfully generates stable, realistic behaviors across diverse character morphologies without task-specific features or state machines. However, the paper's claims about generalization and robustness are weakened by the small training set size, and the comparative evaluation is insufficient.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural network control, particularly for complex character animation, but would be significantly stronger with more comprehensive experimental validation, clearer mathematical presentation, and better comparative evaluation. The technical approach is sound and the results are impressive, but the paper needs to address the weaknesses identified above to fully demonstrate the significance of the work.

The paper meets NIPS quality criteria with strong technical content, but would benefit from addressing the weaknesses before acceptance. The significance is high for character animation and robotics control, but the current evidence for generalization and comparison to alternatives is insufficient to fully establish its contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
