This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, enabling interactive control of complex characters – swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion – without relying on motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) with neural network training, interleaving supervised learning with optimization and injecting noise during training to improve robustness and generalization. The network learns both optimal controls and a model of the dynamics simultaneously, effectively learning the dynamics *under* an implicit policy.

The work builds upon a long line of research in control, reinforcement learning, and stochastic optimal control. Historically, neural networks have been applied to control, but recent successes have largely focused on perception tasks where large datasets are readily available. This paper tackles the challenge of limited data and the need for precise real-valued outputs in a closed-loop control setting. It connects to recent work leveraging trajectory optimization for neural network training [9, 11], but extends these approaches to substantially more complex, three-dimensional locomotion problems. The authors clearly situate their work within the context of related methods, including physics-based controllers relying on state machines and simplified models [21, 18, 13, 8, 7].

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The approach appears technically sound, combining established techniques (trajectory optimization, neural networks) in a novel way. The justification for each component – supervised learning, trajectory optimization, noise injection, joint optimization – is well-articulated.  The detailed description of the optimization procedure and the treatment of optimal feedback gains are particularly strong.
* **Impressive Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate their method’s ability to control a diverse range of characters, showcasing impressive locomotion behaviors in 3D space. The accompanying video is crucial for appreciating the quality of these results.  The comparative evaluation (Figure 2) convincingly demonstrates the benefit of the proposed joint optimization technique over naive approaches.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear and concise, and the technical details are presented with sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The use of diagrams and figures is effective.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper addresses potential issues like overfitting and instability through the use of noise injection and proximal regularization. The discussion of the distributed training architecture is also valuable, given the computational demands of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** While technically impressive, the paper’s novelty feels incremental. The core idea of combining trajectory optimization with neural network training is not entirely new [9, 11]. The significant contribution lies in scaling this approach to more complex problems and demonstrating its effectiveness in 3D locomotion.  However, the paper doesn't present fundamentally new theoretical insights.
* **Re-Optimization Justification:** The necessity of re-optimizing at each timestep during policy execution (Section 7) is not fully explained. While the benefit of larger timesteps is mentioned, the motivation for *needing* to re-optimize is less clear. Is residual trajectory cost or policy regression error the driving factor? Would the dynamics inherently prevent physically implausible states without it? This point requires further clarification.
* **Limited Broader Impact Assessment:** Given my lack of deep expertise in this specific domain, I find it challenging to fully assess the broader impact and significance of this work. While the results are impressive, it's unclear whether this approach represents a substantial advance over existing methods in terms of practical applicability or long-term research directions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a robust and scalable method for learning controllers for complex characters.
* Achieves impressive results in challenging 3D locomotion tasks.
* Provides a clear and well-documented implementation, making it accessible to other researchers.
* Represents a significant engineering achievement in combining existing techniques.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Incremental novelty – the core ideas are not entirely new.
* Limited assessment of broader impact and significance due to reviewer's domain unfamiliarity.
* The necessity of re-optimization during execution requires further justification.

**Specific Comments:**

* Line 89: "to unify" -> "to unify these disparate approaches"
* Line 153: Please provide one or two references for the statement "similar to recent approaches." The comment regarding keeping updates small in parameter space is somewhat counterintuitive for a block-alternation scheme, but understandable given the context.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with impressive empirical results. While the novelty may be incremental, the technical sophistication and successful application to challenging problems are noteworthy.  I lean towards acceptance, but recommend the authors address the reviewer's concerns regarding the re-optimization step and better articulate the broader impact of their work. I rate this as a **7/10**.
