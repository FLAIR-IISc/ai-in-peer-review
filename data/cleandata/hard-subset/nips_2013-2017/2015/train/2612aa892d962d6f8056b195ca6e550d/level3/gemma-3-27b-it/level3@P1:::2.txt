This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, achieving stable and realistic behaviors for locomotion tasks with varying body morphologies. The core idea is to leverage trajectory optimization to generate training data for the neural network, effectively learning from an optimizer to achieve online, interactive control. The authors demonstrate impressive results across swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion, using a single network architecture without task-specific features or state machines.

This work builds upon a long history of applying neural networks to control, stemming from early work in the field and gaining renewed momentum with recent advances in deep learning. It also directly relates to the substantial body of work on trajectory optimization for control, notably Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12], which the authors utilize to generate training data. While trajectory optimization has previously yielded impressive results, it is an offline method. The authors aim to bridge the gap between offline optimization and online control by learning a neural policy that mimics the optimizer’s behavior. Recent work [9, 11] has explored similar ideas, but often remained limited to two-dimensional characters. This paper extends these concepts to more complex three-dimensional systems.

The strengths of this paper lie in its impressive empirical results. The ability to control diverse character types with a single network architecture is a significant achievement. The accompanying video convincingly demonstrates the stability and realism of the generated behaviors. The authors clearly articulate the challenges of learning controllers in continuous, high-dimensional spaces and address them through careful training procedures, including noise injection and interleaved supervised learning and trajectory optimization.  The implementation details, including the distributed training architecture and GPU optimization, are valuable and demonstrate a practical approach to tackling a computationally demanding problem. 

However, the paper’s originality and significance are somewhat diminished by a lack of thorough contextualization within the broader literature. While the authors acknowledge related work, they do not adequately position their contribution relative to it.  The claim that the method solves “substantially harder problems” than previous work [9, 11] requires more concrete justification.  Simply stating the difference in dimensionality is insufficient; a quantitative comparison of performance, such as learning speed, the range of supported body morphologies, runtime computational cost, and robustness to environmental perturbations, is necessary.  The work of Abbeel and Levine, in particular, represents a strong baseline for comparison.

Furthermore, the motivation for deviating from a standard Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) approach for calculating feedback gains feels somewhat disjointed in its current presentation. The issues with "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" are not fully explained, and the rationale for the proposed perturbation method isn't immediately clear.  Reordering the discussion, presenting the limitations of LQG *after* introducing the proposed solution would improve clarity.

The notation in section 3.2 is confusing. The use of both bars and squiggles (e.g., s(X) = \bar s) to denote variations on symbols creates ambiguity and hinders understanding.  Greater visual distinction is needed. Similarly, the explanation of how the proposed method produces "softer feedback gains" is lacking. What does “softer” mean in this context?  Justifying the benefit of modifying the initial state rather than directly optimizing the feedback gains would strengthen the argument. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a robust and scalable approach to learning control policies for diverse characters.
*   Achieves impressive results in complex three-dimensional locomotion tasks.
*   Provides valuable implementation details for a distributed training architecture.
*   Addresses challenges related to data acquisition and overfitting in neural network control.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently contextualized within the broader literature, particularly regarding prior work on trajectory optimization and learning control policies.
*   Lack of quantitative comparison to related methods.
*   Confusing notation and unclear explanations of key technical details (e.g., “softer” feedback gains).
*   The contribution of the perturbation method for calculating feedback gains is not fully justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a sound piece of experimental work that demonstrates the potential of combining trajectory optimization with neural networks for interactive character control. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough and critical comparison to existing literature, clearer explanations of key technical details, and a more compelling articulation of its unique contributions.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above. The paper, as it stands, presents a valuable demonstration of a working system, but needs to be elevated to a more impactful scientific contribution through better positioning within the field.
