The paper "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" presents a novel approach to training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems and tasks, including swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking with different body morphologies. The authors propose a method that combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, and using the trajectory optimizer to obtain optimal feedback gains in addition to optimal actions.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a universal synthesis method that can be applied to arbitrary behaviors, body morphologies, online changes in task objectives, perturbations due to noise and modeling errors. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by training a single neural network controller to generate stable and realistic behaviors for a range of characters and tasks.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and its components. The use of equations and pseudocode helps to clarify the technical details of the approach. The experimental results are impressive, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including comparisons with other approaches.

However, there are some issues with the paper's structure and clarity. As noted in the key points, sections 4 and 6 are disproportionate in coverage and length, with section 4 being particularly brief. Additionally, section 4 fails to mention the recurrent units referenced in the abstract, which could be confusing for readers. Furthermore, the description of the network as "stochastic" in line 405 could be clarified, as the network itself appears deterministic despite stochastic training data.

In terms of the paper's contributions, the authors demonstrate a significant improvement over previous work in the field, particularly in terms of the ability to generate stable and realistic behaviors for a range of characters and tasks. The use of trajectory optimization and noise injection during training are key innovations that enable the authors to achieve these results.

However, the paper could be improved by providing more detail on the technical aspects of the approach, particularly in section 4. Additionally, the authors could provide more context on the relevance of their work to the broader field of robotics and control, and how their approach could be applied to real-world problems.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of robotics and control, and the authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the technical aspects of their approach. With some revisions to address the issues noted above, the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.
