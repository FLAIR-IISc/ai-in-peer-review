

This paper presents a method for training neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse character behaviors in physics simulators, using trajectory optimization combined with neural policy regression. The authors demonstrate successful application across swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion with varying body morphologies using a single network architecture.

The main contribution appears to be an application of deep learning to trajectory-optimization-based control, rather than a fundamentally new method. While the experimental results are sound and the approach does produce stable interactive controllers for diverse behaviors, the paper fails to adequately position itself relative to prior work. The closest related work by Abbeel and Levine (cited as [9]) already demonstrated neural network approximation of step-by-step policy actions, and the authors do not clearly articulate how their method improves upon this work in terms of learning speed, supported body variety, runtime computation cost, or robustness to environmental variability.

Several technical issues require clarification. The claim about "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" lacks explanation of why this is problematic. The discussion of why LQG was not used should be moved after presenting the proposed strategy for better flow. Notation around lines 226-240 is confusing with visually similar symbols (bars and squiggles) that need disambiguation. The claim that "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ" requires definition of "softer" relative to what standard, and justification for why changing the initial state is beneficial.

While the experimental validation across diverse character types is impressive, the paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison with Abbeel and Levine's work and a clearer articulation of the novelty relative to existing trajectory-optimization-based control methods. The method is technically sound and produces realistic behaviors, but the contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work. The paper would be strengthened by addressing these points to better demonstrate its significance to the field.

Overall, this is a well-executed application of known techniques that produces compelling results, but the paper must better clarify its contribution relative to recent related work to meet the originality and significance criteria expected at NIPS.
