The paper proposes an alternating learning framework that couples direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised training of a small recurrent neural network to produce interactive, near-optimal feedback controllers for physically simulated characters. The policy outputs desired generalized velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state updates, and is trained on trajectories generated (and continually re-optimized) by CIO. Robustness is promoted by injecting noise in sensory inputs and in hidden activations, while optimal local feedback gains are estimated via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss-Newton machinery of the optimizer. Execution uses a short-horizon (T=1) optimization step that enforces dynamics constraints, allowing stable real-time control. The same network architecture is reported to handle diverse 3D behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion) without task-specific state machines or motion capture.

Relation to prior work: This line closely follows recent methods that learn neural policies from trajectory optimization (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’15), and is philosophically distinct from direct deep RL (e.g., TRPO) and model-free approaches. CIO has previously produced high-quality motions offline; here, the contribution is an integrated training pipeline that distills CIO into a compact recurrent policy for online control, with technical refinements: (i) noise injection with targets adjusted by optimal feedback gains, (ii) a practical, well-conditioned feedback-gain estimator within direct TO, (iii) asynchronous distributed training with proximal regularization, and (iv) a data-generation scheme that branches trajectories at random times with changing goals to encourage interactive redirection. Compared to MPC baselines, the authors argue their joint optimization regularizes toward periodic, less chaotic behaviors.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound, well-engineered, and the ablations (“no joint,” “no noise”) support the importance of joint optimization and noise injection. The feedback gain derivation that leverages the Gauss-Newton Hessian and Woodbury identity is a neat and practically valuable contribution. However, empirical evaluation is mostly qualitative; stronger quantitative comparisons are needed, especially against the most relevant prior (Levine & Koltun, 2014) and MPC. Suggested metrics: wall-clock training time and compute budget, run-time control frequency, failure rates (falls per minute), energy/torque use, goal-tracking error, disturbance recovery, and performance across morphology diversity. Robustness to model mismatch and untrained perturbations at test time is not rigorously assessed.

Clarity: The paper is generally clearly written but has several expository issues:
- The discussion of not using LQG precedes the proposed alternative, making the narrative harder to follow. Present the perturbation-based gain computation first, then contrast to LQG with pros/cons.
- Lines 226–240: notation is confusing. Using s(X)=s̄ while defining costs via s(X)−s̄ invites confusion. Use clearly distinct symbols for “nominal sensory state” (e.g., s0), “perturbed target” (e.g., s̃), and “optimizer’s solution under soft constraint” (X̃(s̃)), and restate X*, CXX, and evaluation points explicitly.
- “Very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” should be quantified (e.g., norms, singular value spectra, condition numbers) and tied to observed issues (e.g., chatter, sensitivity to sensor noise, numerical stiffness, integration instabilities).
- Define “softer feedback gains” precisely: do you mean smaller gain norms, better conditioning, or smoother temporal variation? Provide evidence (e.g., gain norm vs. λ curves) and explain why allowing initial-state adjustment via λ reduces aggressive feedback and improves stability.

Originality: The overall framework is an application and consolidation of known ideas (trajectory optimization, policy distillation, noise injection, proximal alternation). The main novel element is the efficient, well-conditioned feedback gain computation within a direct TO pipeline and its use to generate noise-adjusted supervision targets. The 3D locomotion results across diverse morphologies with one architecture are compelling, but the claim of solving “substantially harder” problems than prior work requires stronger, controlled comparisons.

Significance: If substantiated with quantitative evidence, the approach could be influential for animation and possibly robotics by providing fast, reusable controllers distilled from expensive optimizers, reducing reliance on state machines and mocap. The engineering recipe (data generation via goal-branching, proximal asynchrony, feedback-informed noise supervision) is practically useful. Lack of rigorous baselines and robustness analysis currently limits the strength of the claims.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Add a head-to-head comparison with Levine & Koltun (ICML’14) on identical tasks and simulators: training time, sample efficiency (number of optimized trajectories), run-time speed, robustness to disturbances and sensor noise, and breadth of supported morphologies.
- Include a quantitative MPC baseline on the same cost with matched horizons and constraints; report performance and behavioral regularity metrics.
- Report feedback gain statistics (norms, condition numbers) for LQG vs. the proposed method across tasks; plot performance vs. λ to justify “softer” behavior and stability.
- Clarify the consequences of outputting velocities/forces rather than torques: how much “implicit policy” is baked into the T=1 optimization step? Compare to torque-output policies.
- Provide test-time generalization and robustness tests: untrained goal changes, external pushes, parameter perturbations (mass/friction), and sensing noise levels.
- Improve notation and exposition as noted above; move the LQG discussion after the proposed method.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong demonstrations across diverse 3D characters with a single compact policy.
- Practical and elegant feedback-gain computation integrated into direct TO.
- Clear ablations showing benefit of joint optimization and noise injection.
- Scalable, reproducible engineering pipeline with real-time execution.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to recent trajectory-optimization–to–policy pipelines.
- Insufficient quantitative comparisons to the most relevant prior art and MPC.
- Clarity issues in key derivations/notation; vague claims about gain conditioning.
- Robustness and out-of-distribution generalization not rigorously evaluated.

Overall: A solid piece of experimental work that advances practice and demonstrates impressive breadth, but it needs clearer positioning and stronger quantitative evaluation against closely related methods to justify publication.
