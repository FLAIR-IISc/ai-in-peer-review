## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a compelling method for learning controllers for complex, articulated characters by jointly optimizing trajectories and a neural network policy. The core idea – interleaving trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network regression – appears to be a successful strategy for generating stable and realistic behaviors across a diverse range of morphologies and tasks (swimming, flying, walking). The authors convincingly demonstrate that decoupling the problem into these alternating subproblems allows for the reuse of existing methods and yields significant improvements over naive approaches. 

The technical sophistication of this work is readily apparent. The authors successfully navigate the complexities of high-dimensional control, incorporating techniques like noise injection for robustness and leveraging optimal feedback gains derived from the trajectory optimization step. The results, as showcased in the accompanying video, are impressive, demonstrating locomotion and complex movements in 3D environments – a significant leap beyond previous work largely confined to 2D. The clear writing and organization, coupled with helpful cross-references, make the paper accessible despite its technical depth. The authors also appropriately acknowledge the computational cost, detailing a distributed training architecture to handle the demands.

However, while technically strong, the paper lacks substantial novelty in terms of theoretical insights. It skillfully *combines* existing techniques, but doesn't appear to introduce fundamentally new algorithms or mathematical frameworks. As someone not deeply familiar with the domain, it’s difficult to assess the broader impact of this work. While the paper convincingly outperforms baseline approaches, a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning techniques would be beneficial. 

The motivation behind the re-optimization step in Section 7 feels somewhat underexplained. While the authors point to the advantages of larger timesteps, the necessity of re-optimization given the residual trajectory cost or policy regression error isn't fully justified. It’s unclear whether the dynamics alone would inherently prevent physically implausible behavior. Additionally, line 153 would benefit from one or two concrete references for the statement "similar to recent approaches." A minor editorial note: a typo exists at line 89 ("to unify").

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Impressive empirical results demonstrating stable and realistic character control.
*   Solid technical execution and clear presentation.
*   Successful combination of existing techniques into a practical and effective solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty in terms of theoretical contribution.
*   Domain-specific nature makes assessing broader impact challenging for a non-expert reviewer.
*   Re-optimization step requires further justification.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear demonstration of technical prowess. While it may not represent a groundbreaking theoretical advance, the practical improvements and impressive results make it a valuable contribution.
