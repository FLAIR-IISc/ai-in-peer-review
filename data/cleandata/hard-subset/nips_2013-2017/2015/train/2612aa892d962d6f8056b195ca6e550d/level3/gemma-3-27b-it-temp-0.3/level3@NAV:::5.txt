## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for a variety of dynamic systems, achieving stable and realistic behaviors in locomotion tasks across different character morphologies. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) to train a neural network that learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics. The approach aims to overcome the limitations of traditional control methods and achieve universal synthesis applicable to complex behaviors and online changes. This builds upon prior work in reinforcement learning and stochastic optimal control, but claims to achieve success in higher-dimensional continuous spaces than previously demonstrated.

The strengths of this work lie in its ambition and the breadth of demonstrated results. Successfully applying a single control architecture and training procedure to swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion is a significant achievement. The combination of trajectory optimization for data generation and neural networks for real-time control is a promising approach, and the inclusion of noise injection and recurrent units appears to contribute to robustness and stability. The accompanying video (mentioned in the abstract) is crucial for appreciating the quality of the generated motions.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the authors state upfront that “full model knowledge is required (see Eq. 6),” which is a substantial limitation not fully addressed in the discussion. This dependence on a precise dynamic model restricts the applicability of the method to scenarios where such a model is available. Second, the paper’s structure feels unbalanced. Sections 4 and 6 are disproportionately detailed compared to sections 3 and 5, which describe the core trajectory optimization and training procedures. Crucially, Section 4, detailing the neural network policy regression, *fails to mention the recurrent units referenced in the abstract*. This omission is a significant oversight. Furthermore, line 405 describes the network as “stochastic,” which is misleading. While the *training data* is stochastic, the network itself appears deterministic given a specific input. This requires clarification. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper demonstrates a compelling approach to learning complex locomotion behaviors, achieving results that surpass previous work in dimensionality and diversity. The method shows promise for applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The reliance on full model knowledge, the structural imbalance, the omission regarding recurrent units, and the inaccurate description of the network’s stochasticity detract from the paper’s overall quality.

**Overall:** As per the rebuttal, the paper shows promising results but requires significant restructuring and clarification to address the identified weaknesses. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent upon major revisions addressing these points.
