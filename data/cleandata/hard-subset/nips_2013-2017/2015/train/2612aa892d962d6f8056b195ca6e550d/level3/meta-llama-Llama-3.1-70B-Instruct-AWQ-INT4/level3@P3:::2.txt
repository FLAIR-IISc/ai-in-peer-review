This paper presents a novel approach for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a wide range of dynamical systems and tasks. The authors demonstrate the ability of their method to generate stable and realistic behaviors for various characters, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking with different body morphologies.

The key idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to learn a neural network policy that can reconstruct the sense and action pairs of optimal trajectories. The authors also introduce a perturbation method to produce optimal feedback gains that stabilize the resulting optimal trajectories. This approach is shown to be effective in learning controllers that can generalize to new tasks and environments.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its components. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the ability of the learned controllers to perform complex tasks such as locomotion and flight. The use of a distributed training architecture and a custom GPU implementation of stochastic gradient descent allows for efficient training of the neural network policy.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty and significance of the paper. The authors' claim that their approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ is not clearly justified, and it is unclear what is meant by "softer" in this context. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear comparison to prior work, such as the work by Abbeel and Levine, which is closely related to their approach.

Furthermore, the notation around lines 226-240 is confusing, and the use of visually similar symbols (bars and squiggles) should be disambiguated. The discussion of why LQG was not used should be moved to after the presentation of the proposed strategy for clearer exposition.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their method and results. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work and a clearer explanation of the novelty and significance of their approach.

The originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as the authors build upon existing trajectory optimization and neural network techniques. However, the combination of these techniques and the introduction of the perturbation method for producing optimal feedback gains are novel contributions.

The significance of the paper is high, as it demonstrates the ability to learn controllers that can generalize to new tasks and environments. The potential applications of this work are numerous, including robotic control, animation, and gaming.

In conclusion, while the paper is well-written and presents impressive experimental results, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work and a clearer explanation of the novelty and significance of the approach. The authors should also address the concerns regarding the notation and the comparison to prior work.
