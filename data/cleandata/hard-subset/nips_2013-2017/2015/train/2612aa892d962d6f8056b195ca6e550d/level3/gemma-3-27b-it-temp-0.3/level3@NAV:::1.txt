## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to act as robust feedback controllers for a diverse range of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and legged locomotion. The core idea – embedding feedback gains learned through trajectory optimization into the neural network training process – is a significant contribution, effectively bridging the gap between offline planning and online control. The authors softly couple trajectory optimization and policy optimization via a cost penalty, resulting in policies that learn simpler, more robust approximations than those achievable with MPC alone. 

The work builds upon existing research in reinforcement learning and optimal control, but distinguishes itself by achieving success in high-dimensional, continuous spaces – a long-standing challenge in the field. It addresses the difficulties of obtaining training data for controllers by synthesizing it via trajectory optimization, and cleverly tackles overfitting through noise injection and stochastic optimization. The sophisticated parallelization strategy, while presented primarily as an implementation detail, is crucial for scaling to the complexity of the tasks. 

The strength of this paper lies in its impressive breadth and depth. The authors demonstrate their method on a remarkably diverse set of robots, showcasing its generality and robustness. The clarity of the writing is exceptional, making a complex topic accessible. The integration of graphics, control, and neural networks is seamless, and the results are genuinely inspiring, representing a significant methodological advance. The inclusion of comparative evaluations, demonstrating the benefits of their joint optimization and noise injection techniques, further strengthens the contribution.

However, a potential weakness lies in the limited exploration of alternative neural network architectures. While the authors briefly mention experimentation with network size, a more thorough investigation of different architectures (e.g., transformers, different recurrent units) could provide further insights. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates impressive results in simulation, the transfer of these controllers to real-world robots remains an open question. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant methodological contribution bridging trajectory optimization and neural network control.
*   Exceptional clarity and technical quality.
*   Demonstrated success on a diverse range of complex tasks and morphologies.
*   Strong empirical results and comparative evaluations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of alternative neural network architectures.
*   Lack of real-world robot validation.



**Overall:** This is a highly impactful paper that pushes the boundaries of interactive control. Despite minor areas for improvement, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.
