This paper presents a method for training neural network controllers for complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning, where a neural network learns to approximate the behavior of the optimizer, enabling real-time, interactive control. This approach builds upon recent work that combines trajectory optimization with function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but extends it to a broader range of complex, three-dimensional characters and locomotion tasks, including swimming, flying, and bipedal/quadrupedal walking, using a unified framework.

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical results. The ability to generate stable, realistic, and interactive controllers for such a diverse set of morphologies using a single, general-purpose algorithm is a significant achievement. The technique of using the trajectory optimizer to compute optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2), thereby providing richer training data for the network to handle perturbations, is a clever and valuable contribution. The reported success demonstrates a substantial advance in the state of the art for automated controller synthesis in character animation and robotics.

However, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily concerning clarity and the framing of its contributions. A critical point that should be stated upfront is that the method requires full knowledge of the model dynamics, as the trajectory optimization component relies on a differentiable physics simulator (see Eq. 6). This is a major assumption that distinguishes it from model-free reinforcement learning approaches.

Furthermore, the paper's organization is unbalanced. Section 4, which describes the neural network policy, is disproportionately brief compared to Section 6, which details the distributed training architecture. This gives the impression that the engineering is the main contribution, rather than the learning methodology. Critically, Section 4 fails to elaborate on the recurrent units mentioned in the abstract, leaving their role in the controller unclear. There is also a lack of precision in terminology; for instance, the policy is described as "stochastic," but the network itself appears deterministic, while only the training data is stochastic. This should be clarified.

In summary, the paper presents a powerful methodology with promising and significant results. However, the manuscript requires substantial restructuring to improve its clarity, balance the focus of its sections, and be more precise about its core assumptions and contributions. With these revisions, it would represent a strong addition to the conference.
