Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes an alternating training scheme that couples Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with a neural network (NN) policy: CIO produces trajectories and local feedback gains; a recurrent NN is trained offline to reproduce CIO actions under noisy sensory inputs; the process alternates, with proximal regularization, and is implemented asynchronously across a cluster. The key idea is that alternating joint training plus noise injection (sensor-space augmentation and additive hidden-layer noise) yields real-time, robust control policies. Experiments span swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds. This line of work relates closely to trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (e.g., Guided Policy Search, ADMM/dual-based variants), MPC, and model-based control (LQR/LQG/iLQR). The denoising analogy is invoked to motivate input perturbations, with the twist that CIO provides local gains to adjust targets.

Quality: The method is technically sound and well-motivated; using CIO-derived gains to define noise-consistent targets is elegant. However, the empirical foundation is thin: training data are small (100–200 trials × 5 segments), comparisons are limited (brief MPC mention without a citation), and runtime/throughput/scaling data are missing despite the real-time pitch. Some mathematical presentation is incomplete (e.g., Eq. 6 and CIO constraints lack definitions; Sec. 3.2 drops the time index t and leaves the “optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs” mapping ambiguous).

Clarity: Generally clear, but Section 3.2 needs precise notation for s, as, and their dependence on t; define all symbols in Eq. 6; connect the asynchronous joint optimization (Sec. 6) earlier to Algorithm 1. Many minor writing/notation issues and typos should be fixed (e.g., “unity”→“unify”, missing articles, misplaced stars, acronym order, inconsistent references).

Originality: Alternating joint training with CIO-derived local gains, plus explicit input and hidden-layer noise, is a worthwhile combination that advances beyond straightforward supervised fits to optimized trajectories.

Significance: Promising for interactive character control, but broader comparisons and stronger evidence are needed to convince the NIPS audience of practical benefits and generalization.

Specific concerns and suggestions:
- Provide systematic baselines: MPC with citation and settings, iLQR/LQR-LQG, Guided Policy Search, modern policy-gradient methods.
- Report wall-clock training time, policy inference cost, CPU/GPU usage, and scaling with model complexity.
- Reproducibility: release code and models.
- Clarify if targets include orientation/facing and whether adding a target angle is straightforward.
- Terminology: distinguish “data augmentation” (state perturbations) from “noise injection” (hidden activations); the denoising autoencoder analogy is imperfect since targets change.
- Algorithmic choices: How are initial trajectories X initialized? Why use the same η in both blocks, and what justifies keeping η identical? Discuss annealing of α (CIO regularization).
- Ablations: separate “no data augmentation” vs “no hidden noise” variants.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Elegant integration of trajectory optimization and NN policy learning.
- Robust policies across diverse characters; clear engineering contribution.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited and inconclusive empirical evaluation and comparisons.
- Missing runtimes/scaling; clarity gaps in key derivations and algorithmic choices.
