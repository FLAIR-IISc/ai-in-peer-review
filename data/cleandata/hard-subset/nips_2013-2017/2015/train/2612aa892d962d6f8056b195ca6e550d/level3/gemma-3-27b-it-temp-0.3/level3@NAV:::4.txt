## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for complex characters, enabling interactive control across a diverse range of morphologies and tasks. The core idea – jointly learning trajectories via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and a policy through neural network regression – is well-motivated and technically sophisticated. The authors effectively demonstrate that decoupling this problem into alternating subproblems allows for the reuse of existing methods and yields impressive results in 3D locomotion, a domain where previous work has been limited. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and appropriate detail. The inclusion of cross-references to relevant work is helpful, and the experimental setup is clearly described. The claimed advantages of using sensory noise during training and the benefits of jointly learning trajectories and policy are convincingly demonstrated through ablation studies and comparative results. The results themselves, as showcased in the supplementary video, are visually striking and demonstrate the robustness and realism of the learned controllers. The authors’ decision to leverage cloud computing for this computationally intensive task is also pragmatic and well-justified.

However, while the technical prowess is undeniable, the paper lacks significant theoretical novelty. The approach builds upon established techniques – trajectory optimization and neural network regression – and the primary contribution appears to be a successful combination and application of these methods to a challenging problem. As someone not deeply familiar with the specific domain of character control, it’s difficult to assess the broader impact of this work. While the results are impressive *within* the context presented, it’s unclear how significantly this advances the field beyond the immediate application. 

The motivation for the additional optimization step in Section 7, while yielding benefits in terms of timestep size, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The reviewer questions why the policy, once trained, necessitates re-optimization at each timestep, and whether the dynamics of the system wouldn't inherently prevent physically implausible behavior. Furthermore, line 153 could benefit from one or two specific references to support the claim of similarity to recent approaches. A minor correction is needed on line 89 ("to unify"). 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong technical execution and impressive empirical results.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Successful application of existing techniques to a challenging problem.
*   Demonstrated benefits of the proposed approach through ablation studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical novelty.
*   Uncertainty regarding broader impact due to domain unfamiliarity.
*   Motivation for the final optimization step could be clearer.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution. While lacking groundbreaking theoretical insights, the practical results and technical sophistication are commendable. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the minor points raised regarding motivation and referencing.
