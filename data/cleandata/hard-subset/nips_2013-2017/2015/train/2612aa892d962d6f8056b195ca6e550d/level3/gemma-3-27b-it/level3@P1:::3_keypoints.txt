- Proposes training RNNs to imitate and refine Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectories for interactive control of complex characters.
- Combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, noise injection, and distributed stochastic optimization to scale to high-dimensional control.
- Demonstrates realistic, stable locomotion across diverse morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without motion capture or hand-designed state machines.
- Alternating optimization framework blends global CIO optimality with real-time neural network control effectively.
- Noise injection during training is well-motivated and helps prevent closed-loop overfitting.
- Unified approach across morphologies is a significant step toward generalizable control solutions.
- Experimental dataset is small (100–200 trials), raising concerns about policy robustness and generalization.
- Comparative evaluation is shallow; lacks systematic comparison to MPC citations and RL/direct policy optimization baselines.
- Some mathematical presentation lacks detail (notably Eq. (6) and Section 3.2 derivations); notation is occasionally inconsistent and confusing.
- Asynchronous distributed training in Section 6 deviates from Algorithm 1 and needs clarification.
- Distinction between data augmentation and noise injection should be clearer; denoising autoencoder analogy is weak.
- Algorithmic choices (e.g., same learning rate η for trajectory and regression steps) are not justified.
- Paper contains minor writing errors, typos, and notational issues that reduce clarity and professionalism.
- Reproducibility would benefit from publicly released code and runtime/scalability measurements for real-time viability.
- Strong potential impact in robotics, animation, and biomechanics due to novel, effective method and impressive visuals.
- Concerns that warrant rejection without revision: limited evaluation, weak comparisons, unclear derivations, presentation issues.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing experimental robustness, comparative analysis, and clarity issues.
