### **Summary**

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural network (RNN) policies to serve as robust, real-time feedback controllers for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage the power of offline trajectory optimization to generate high-quality training data for the neural network. The authors propose a framework that softly couples these two components: a trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) generates optimal state-action sequences for various tasks, while a neural network learns to regress onto these sequences.

Crucially, the contribution goes beyond simple behavioral cloning. The method introduces several key innovations to ensure the resulting policy is robust to perturbations and can operate in a closed loop without diverging. First, it explicitly computes optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer and uses them to train the network on how to react to noisy sensory inputs. This embeds local, linear feedback control directly into the learned policy. Second, the training process is an iterative, alternating optimization where trajectories are refined based on the current policy, and the policy is updated based on the new trajectories. This joint optimization allows the policy to learn simpler, more regular, and robust solutions (e.g., cyclic gaits) than the raw, sometimes chaotic, trajectories an unregularized optimizer might produce. The authors demonstrate the power and generality of their approach by applying the exact same architecture to learn controllers for a remarkably diverse set of characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, all capable of interactive control.

This work builds upon a line of research that uses trajectory optimization to guide policy learning, such as the work by Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). However, it makes significant advances by demonstrating success on a much wider and more challenging set of 3D locomotion problems and by introducing the novel and effective technique of embedding pre-computed feedback gains to handle noise and improve stability.

### **Strengths**

*   **Quality & Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically superb. The proposed method is a complete and well-thought-out system that successfully integrates sophisticated components from optimization, control, and deep learning. The claims of generating robust, interactive controllers are thoroughly supported by extensive experimental results across an impressive range of morphologies and tasks. The ablation study (Figure 2) provides convincing evidence for the necessity of the core components: joint optimization and noise injection. The discussion of the distributed training architecture, while an implementation detail, further underscores the high level of engineering and execution.

*   **Originality & Significance:** The primary methodological contribution is the novel technique for computing optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer and using them to create richer training targets for the policy network. This is a clever and effective way to instill robustness and is a significant departure from simply cloning optimal actions. The result is a powerful framework that regularizes the search for control policies towards smoother, more predictable, and more natural-looking behaviors (as shown in the qualitative comparison with MPC). The successful application of a single, unified method to such a diverse set of complex 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal/quadrupedal walking) is a landmark achievement. It represents a significant step towards a general-purpose "solver" for character control, a long-standing goal in graphics and robotics. The results are inspiring and are highly likely to influence subsequent research in data-driven control and reinforcement learning.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. Despite the complexity of the underlying system (combining inverse dynamics, trajectory optimization, and RNNs), the authors present their method in a logical and understandable manner. The overview in Section 2 provides an excellent conceptual framework, and the subsequent sections fill in the technical details effectively. The writing is precise, and the figures are informative.

### **Weaknesses**

While this is an outstanding paper, a few points could be considered for future improvement:

*   **Computational Cost:** The method requires a substantial amount of computation, involving a distributed system of ~30 nodes and a GPU, with training times on the order of hours. While the results clearly justify this cost, it represents a significant barrier to entry for researchers without access to similar resources and could limit the method's adoption. A brief discussion of this trade-off would be welcome.

*   **Quantitative Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The paper provides an excellent internal comparison against ablations of its own method. However, a quantitative comparison to a state-of-the-art model-free reinforcement learning algorithm (e.g., a contemporary policy gradient method like TRPO) on one of the tasks could have provided valuable context. This would help elucidate the trade-offs between this "optimizer-guided" approach and "direct" RL in terms of sample efficiency, final performance, and the nature of the learned gaits. The comparison to MPC is insightful but remains qualitative.

*   **Reproducibility:** Given the complexity of the system, which involves a custom distributed architecture, a specific physics simulator (MuJoCo), and custom GPU code, reproducing these results from the paper alone would be extremely challenging. While the clarity is high, the practical implementation details are necessarily vast.

### **Recommendation**

This is an exceptional paper that presents a significant methodological advance in automated controller synthesis. It combines ideas from trajectory optimization and neural network policy learning in a novel and highly effective way. The quality of the execution is top-tier, and the results—demonstrating a single algorithm creating robust controllers for swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds—are both technically impressive and visually compelling. The paper is a clear accept and represents a standout contribution to the field.
