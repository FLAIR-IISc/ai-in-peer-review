This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning. The authors propose a block-alternating optimization scheme that jointly optimizes a set of desired trajectories and the parameters of a neural network policy that can reproduce those trajectories. This approach leverages Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate physically realistic and task-oriented behaviors, which then serve as training data for the neural network. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise into the sensory inputs during training to improve robustness, and a novel technique for extracting optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer to guide the learning process. The method is demonstrated on a diverse range of 3D characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, showing that it can produce stable, interactive locomotion without task-specific state machines or motion capture data. This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but claims to solve substantially more complex 3D locomotion problems through a number of technical improvements.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed optimization framework is sophisticated, and the implementation, involving a distributed architecture with GPU acceleration, is clearly non-trivial and impressive. The claims are well-supported by the experimental results, particularly the ablation studies in Section 8.1. These experiments convincingly demonstrate that both the joint optimization of trajectories and policy, as well as the injection of sensory and hidden unit noise, are critical to the method's success. The comparison against a naive approach of training on a static dataset (the "no joint" case) clearly shows the necessity of the alternating optimization scheme for achieving good test-time performance.

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement is well-motivated, and the proposed algorithm is described in sufficient detail, with helpful cross-references between sections. The figures and tables effectively support the text.

**Originality and Significance**

The work presents a novel and effective combination of existing techniques. The central idea of learning a policy from an "expert" trajectory optimizer is not entirely new. However, the specific formulation as a distributed, stochastic, block-alternating optimization problem, the method for computing feedback gains from a direct optimizer (Section 3.2), and the principled use of noise seem to be original contributions. The ability to successfully apply this framework to a wide variety of complex 3D characters using a single, general-purpose architecture is a significant engineering achievement and a clear step forward from previous work that was limited to 2D characters.

The significance of this work seems high, particularly for fields like character animation, gaming, and potentially robotics. The ability to automatically synthesize robust, interactive controllers for arbitrary morphologies from a high-level task description is a long-standing goal. This paper makes substantial progress towards that goal. The resulting behaviors, as described and shown in the supplementary video, appear natural and purposeful, with gaits like walking and trotting emerging automatically, which is a very strong result.

As a reviewer not deeply specialized in character animation, I cannot fully assess the novelty against all prior art in that specific community, but from a machine learning and control perspective, the paper appears to be a strong contribution. It presents a complete and well-executed piece of work, not just a position paper.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The method successfully trains controllers for a diverse set of challenging 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single algorithm, leading to the emergence of complex, natural-looking gaits. This is a significant demonstration of the method's power and generality.
2.  **Sound and Well-Validated Methodology:** The joint optimization of trajectories and the policy is a powerful concept. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 provide strong evidence for the necessity of the key components of the proposed approach (joint training and noise injection).
3.  **Technical Sophistication:** The paper demonstrates a high level of technical skill in its combination of trajectory optimization, neural network regression, and distributed systems engineering. The method for extracting feedback gains is particularly clever.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, making a complex topic accessible.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors (Arguments for Rejection/Revision)**

1.  **Motivation for Runtime Optimization:** My main point of confusion lies in the policy execution step described in Section 7. After training a policy `πθ` to output a desired action `a = [q̇_des, ṙ_des, f_des]`, the method does not seem to use this action directly. Instead, at each timestep, it solves another optimization problem to find the next state `x1` that best matches the policy's output `q̇_des` while satisfying physics constraints. This seems to defeat some of the purpose of training a fast, feed-forward policy, as it re-introduces an optimization loop at runtime. Could the authors clarify the necessity of this step? Is it because the policy's output is not always physically plausible, or because of residual regression error? If the former, wouldn't the forward dynamics simulation in a physics engine naturally correct for this? While the authors note this allows for larger timesteps, a more thorough explanation of this design choice would be beneficial.
2.  **Minor Points:**
    *   On line 153, the paper states, "Similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches, we introduce quadratic proximal regularization terms..." It would be helpful to include one or two specific citations here to ground this statement. The motivation for keeping updates small in the *parameter* space makes sense for a block-alternating scheme, but a reference would be appreciated.
    *   There is a minor typo on line 89: "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be "to unify".

Overall, this is a very strong paper. The technical execution is excellent, the results are impressive, and the method represents a significant advance in synthesizing controllers for complex characters. My primary concern regarding the runtime optimization step is more a request for clarification than a fundamental flaw. I believe this work would be of great interest to the NIPS community.
