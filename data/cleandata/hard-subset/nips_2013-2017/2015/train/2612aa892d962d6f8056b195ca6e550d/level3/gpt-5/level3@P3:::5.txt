Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that couples direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural network regression to obtain near–real-time feedback controllers for diverse 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion). Up front, the method requires full model knowledge, including equations of motion, contact geometry, and friction cone constraints (see Eq. 6), and uses those constraints both to generate training data and to evolve state at test time.

Key ingredients: (i) sensory noise injection with matched action targets via locally optimal feedback gains a_s (Eq. 2), (ii) a perturbation-based method to compute these gains that reuses Gauss–Newton structure from CIO (Sec. 3.2), (iii) proximal-regularized block-alternating optimization (Alg. 1) between CIO and NN regression, and (iv) an asynchronous distributed training architecture. The policy predicts desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state rates; execution advances the physical state by solving a one-step constrained optimization (Sec. 7).

Relative to prior policy learning from trajectory optimization (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2015) and to model-free RL (e.g., TRPO; deep Q-learning in discrete domains), the main advances are: learning controllers for harder 3D, contact-rich morphologies; integrating noise with local feedback targets derived from direct trajectory optimization; and demonstrating interactive behaviors without mocap or state machines. The work also connects to earlier neural control (Huh & Todorov, 2008) and graphics control pipelines (e.g., Neuroanimator).

Strengths
- Clear and compelling demonstrations across diverse, contact-rich 3D systems; strong qualitative results.
- Clever reuse of CIO derivatives to compute well-conditioned local feedback gains with a tunable softness parameter λ.
- Joint optimization with proximal terms is practical and stabilizes asynchronous training.
- Sensory and activation noise are used principledly, with informative ablations (no-joint, no-noise).
- Real-time execution with a simple 1-step optimization loop and no need to retain trajectories.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Assumption of full dynamics and contact model: The contribution relies critically on accurate knowledge of H, C, J, d(q), and the friction cone (Eq. 6). Please state this explicitly in the introduction and abstract as a core assumption and discuss implications for deployment to real systems (model mismatch, sim-to-real).
- Section balance: Sections 4 (Neural network policy regression) and 6 (Distributed training architecture) are disproportionate. Sec. 6 gets substantial space on engineering details, while Sec. 4—arguably central to the contribution—receives minimal treatment. Please rebalance by expanding Sec. 4 (see next points) and moving some of Sec. 6 to an appendix.
- Recurrent units ambiguity: The abstract claims “a small number of recurrent units,” but Sec. 4 describes a 3-layer tanh feed-forward network and does not specify any recurrent architecture or the dimensionality/usage of r. From the pipeline, recurrence appears to be externalized via the state r and its update ṙ, not via recurrent connections in the NN. Please clarify:
  - Is the NN strictly feed-forward in θ with r acting as part of the input/output, or are there actual recurrent connections/parameters?
  - What is the size of r, and how is it initialized and regularized?
  - Provide ablations showing the effect of r’s dimensionality on stability/performance.
- “Stochastic network” wording (Line 405): Training uses stochasticity (input and hidden-unit noise), but execution removes noise and the policy appears deterministic. Please clarify why the network is described as “stochastic,” and whether any stochasticity remains at test time. If “stochastic” refers only to training-time regularization, state so explicitly.
- Metrics and quantitative evaluation: Several claims (e.g., “two orders of magnitude better,” “less chaotic than MPC”) would benefit from precise definitions and numbers:
  - Define the evaluation metrics (tracking error, success rate, fall rate, energy/torque costs) and report mean±std across seeds/initial conditions.
  - For MPC, provide quantitative comparisons on the same tasks and horizons, not only qualitative plots.
  - Report constraint violations at execution (e.g., root force magnitudes due to soft constraint enforcement) and their impact on realism.
- Feedback gains: The perturbation method is well motivated; please (i) report sensitivity to λ, (ii) compare against an LQR/LQG pass quantitatively (conditioning, robustness, and final rollout error), and (iii) discuss stability guarantees or empirical stability margins when rolling out under noise.
- Action parameterization: The paper says the network learns “dynamics under an implicit policy” by predicting desired velocities and contact forces that are realized via a one-step optimization. This is effectively a policy; please clarify the rationale and trade-offs vs directly predicting torques. A brief comparison (even a small controlled study) would strengthen the argument.
- Generalization and robustness: The training data are generated by branching from optimal trajectories. Please characterize generalization to out-of-distribution goals/perturbations and the role of noise scales σε, σγ (sweeps and robustness plots would help).
- Reproducibility details: Hyperparameters are listed in Sec. 8; consider consolidating all training details (learning rates, batch sizes, proximal rates, spectral radius init, λ selection) in one place and releasing pseudocode for Sec. 3.2 gains.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall; the reuse of CIO structure for feedback is neat. More rigorous quantitative evaluation and clarity on recurrence/stochasticity would improve it.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but the method section is imbalanced. Clarify recurrence, action semantics, and evaluation metrics. Move cluster/engineering details to appendix.
- Originality: A thoughtful synthesis of known components with a novel feedback-gain extraction and effective noise-target coupling. Advances prior CIO+NN work to harder 3D settings.
- Significance: High for graphics/animation and potentially robotics with accurate models; less so for model-free control due to reliance on full dynamics. The practical pipeline is useful.

Suggestions for restructuring
- Unify Sections 2–4 into “Method,” with subsections: (i) problem/setup, (ii) trajectory optimization, (iii) feedback gains, (iv) NN architecture and recurrence, (v) training objective/algorithm. Move most of Sec. 6 to supplemental.
- Expand Sec. 4 to detail the recurrent state r, architecture, ablations, and training specifics.
- Add a dedicated evaluation section with quantitative metrics and MPC/LQG comparisons.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, diverse results in challenging 3D contact-rich domains without mocap/state machines.
- Principled integration of trajectory optimization, local feedback, and noise-robust regression.
- Practical, scalable training pipeline; clear ablations supporting design choices.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on full accurate models; limited discussion of model mismatch.
- Method presentation imbalanced; ambiguity about recurrence and “stochastic” terminology.
- Quantitative evaluation is thin relative to the strength of the qualitative claims.

Rebuttal
The paper shows promising results but should be restructured to clarify recurrence, justify “stochastic” terminology, foreground the full-model assumption (Eq. 6), and strengthen quantitative evaluation (including MPC/LQG comparisons and constraint-violation reporting). With these revisions, I would be positive about acceptance.
