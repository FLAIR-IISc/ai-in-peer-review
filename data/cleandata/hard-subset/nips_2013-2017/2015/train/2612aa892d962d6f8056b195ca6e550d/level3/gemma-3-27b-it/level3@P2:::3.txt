This paper presents a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, achieving stable and realistic behaviors for locomotion in swimming, flying, and legged characters. The core idea is to train a neural network to mimic the output of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimizer, interleaved with further optimization of the network itself. This approach, coupled with noise injection during training, allows the method to generalize to new situations and achieve performance exceeding naive supervised learning from static trajectories.

The work builds on a rich history of neural network control, dating back to early applications and resurging with recent deep learning advances. However, unlike many recent successes in areas like vision and speech, control presents unique challenges – the need for precise continuous outputs, closed-loop stability concerns, and limited training data. The authors correctly identify these issues and address them through a combination of trajectory optimization, neural network regression, and thoughtful regularization techniques. Related work in reinforcement learning and direct neural network control is acknowledged, with the authors highlighting the advantage of their method in tackling high-dimensional, continuous control problems. The use of CIO is also well-placed, as it provides a strong foundation for generating high-quality training data.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of trajectory optimization with neural network learning in an interleaved fashion is a significant contribution. This approach circumvents the challenges of directly learning control policies in complex environments.
* **Generality:** The ability to control a wide range of character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network architecture is impressive and demonstrates the method’s potential for broad applicability.
* **Stability & Realism:** The resulting behaviors are reported to be both stable and realistic, a crucial requirement for applications like robotics and animation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The underlying principles of trajectory optimization, neural network training, and noise injection are well-established. The proposed algorithm builds upon these foundations in a principled manner.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts, although some sections require more detail (see Weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** While the results are promising, the experiments are somewhat limited. The training dataset size (100-200 trials) raises concerns about generalization and makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the method’s robustness.
* **Weak Comparative Evaluation:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to other state-of-the-art control methods. The brief mention of Model Predictive Control (MPC) without a citation is insufficient. Systematic comparisons with other neural network control approaches (e.g., DDPG, TRPO) are needed.
* **Lack of Reproducibility:** The absence of publicly available code hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult for other researchers to build upon this work.
* **Mathematical Clarity:** Some sections, particularly those detailing the CIO process (Section 3) and Eq. 6, lack sufficient detail and require the reader to consult external references. The notation in Section 3.2 is confusing and should be clarified.
* **Algorithmic Details:** Certain algorithmic choices, such as the use of a constant η in Algorithm 1, are not sufficiently justified. Exploring different values and adaptation strategies could lead to further improvements.
* **Minor Issues:** The paper contains several minor writing and notation issues (e.g., “unity” vs. “unify”, typos, missing articles) that detract from its overall polish.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The work presents a genuinely novel approach to character control, effectively bridging the gap between trajectory optimization and neural network learning.
* The ability to generalize to diverse character morphologies is a significant achievement.
* The method produces stable and realistic behaviors, demonstrating its practical potential.
* The authors have identified and addressed key challenges in applying deep learning to control problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limited experimental evaluation and lack of thorough comparisons raise concerns about the method’s robustness and competitive advantage.
* The lack of publicly available code hinders reproducibility and adoption.
* The mathematical presentation in certain sections could be improved for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in character control. The proposed method offers a compelling combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning, resulting in stable and realistic behaviors across a range of morphologies. However, the limited experimental evaluation and lack of thorough comparisons prevent me from giving a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above—particularly by expanding the experiments, providing a more comprehensive comparative evaluation, and releasing the code—would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. I would be happy to reconsider this paper if the authors address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation with comparison to baselines and code availability are critical.
