This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a diverse range of dynamical systems – swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking with different body morphologies. The core idea is to intertwine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO), injecting noise during training, and leveraging the trajectory optimizer to derive optimal feedback gains alongside optimal actions. This approach avoids the need for motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines, offering a unified framework for control across vastly different systems.

The work builds upon a rich history of neural network control, referencing early applications alongside more recent advances in deep learning and reinforcement learning. It directly addresses limitations of previous work, particularly the difficulty of scaling control techniques to high-dimensional continuous spaces. The authors explicitly position their work relative to methods like [9, 11], highlighting the increased complexity of the systems they can successfully control – particularly achieving stable 3D locomotion, a feat previously limited to 2D. The paper also acknowledges the challenges in obtaining suitable training data for controllers, contrasting it with the abundance of data available for tasks like image recognition. The connection to related work in robust control and the handling of i.i.d. vs. closed-loop data is well articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound, with a clear and well-defined optimization problem. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network regression is thoughtfully implemented, and the inclusion of feedback gains is a significant contribution. The justification for the noise injection strategy and the use of a proximal regularization term are convincing. The algorithm is well-explained, and the supplementary material likely provides further detail.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate their motivations, methodology, and results. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are accessible without sacrificing technical rigor.  The inclusion of the distributed training architecture details enhances reproducibility.
* **Originality:** The integration of trajectory optimization, neural networks, and feedback gains represents a novel approach to control.  While the individual components are known, their synergistic combination to achieve robust, real-time control across diverse morphologies is a significant contribution. The method extends previous work to substantially more complex systems.
* **Significance:** The results are highly significant. Demonstrating a single control framework capable of generating realistic and stable behaviors for swimming, flying, and walking characters is a substantial achievement. The potential applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics are considerable. The ability to learn controllers without task-specific features or motion capture data is particularly impactful. The comparison with MPC highlighting the more cyclical and potentially more natural behaviors further strengthens the significance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the distributed training architecture is described, the computational demands of the method are substantial (hours of training, reliance on cloud computing). This may limit its accessibility to researchers without significant computational resources. This is acknowledged by the authors ("Interestingly, we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it."), but it remains a practical constraint.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper provides values for algorithmic constants, but a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.  It is unclear how robust the method is to changes in these parameters.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The comparison with more direct neural network training methods (without trajectory optimization) is brief. A more extensive comparison, perhaps with a wider range of network architectures and training algorithms, would provide a stronger justification for the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper represents a significant advancement in the field of neural network control.
* The results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
* The originality and technical soundness of the work are high.
* The potential impact on robotics, animation, and biomechanics is substantial.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost of the method may limit its practical applicability.
* A more comprehensive comparative analysis would strengthen the paper.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a truly impressive paper. It addresses a challenging problem with a novel and effective solution. The clarity of the writing, the technical soundness of the method, and the significance of the results all strongly support acceptance. While the computational cost and limited comparative analysis are minor concerns, they do not outweigh the substantial contributions of this work. I highly recommend accepting this paper.
