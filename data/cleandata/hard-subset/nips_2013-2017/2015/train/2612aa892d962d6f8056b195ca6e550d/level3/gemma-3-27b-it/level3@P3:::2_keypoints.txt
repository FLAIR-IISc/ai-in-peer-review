- Proposes training recurrent neural-network feedback controllers by using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization to generate datasets; same network architecture applied to swimming, flying, walking.  
- Experimental results are strong: controllers produce stable, realistic behaviors, and the video convincingly demonstrates performance.  
- Methodology is sound: sensible combination of trajectory optimization and neural-network training with careful implementation.  
- Scalable approach: distributed computing enables tackling complex training problems.  
- Novel application: applying this pipeline across a wide variety of characters/tasks is a valuable contribution.  
- Main critique: unclear novelty — approach reads as applying deep learning to existing trajectory-optimization methods rather than a fundamental advance.  
- Insufficient comparison to prior work: lacks clear connection and empirical comparison to Levine & Koltun / Abbeel & Levine and other related methods.  
- Missing comparative metrics: no evaluation of learning speed, supported body morphologies, runtime computational cost, or robustness to environmental variability.  
- Poor explanation of “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”: paper does not explain why such gains are problematic or the practical consequences for non-control readers.  
- Ambiguous claims about λ and “softer” gains: need definition of “softer,” baseline for comparison, and why relaxing the initial-state constraint yields robustness.  
- Organization issues: justification for not using LQG is presented before the proposed method, reducing clarity — propose explaining the method first.  
- Notation problems: use of barred (s̄) and squiggly (s(X)) symbols (lines 226–240) is visually similar and hinders readability.  
- Recommendation: sound experimental work but requires major revisions on positioning, comparisons, technical explanations, and clarity; current recommendation is rejection with encouragement to resubmit after fixes.
