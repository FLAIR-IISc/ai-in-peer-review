This paper proposes a real-time control algorithm for interactive character control using neural networks. The main idea is to train a neural network offline to reproduce the outputs of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), a trajectory optimization method. The training process involves alternating joint training between CIO and the neural network, as well as noise injection via data augmentation and additive noise in hidden layers to improve robustness.

The paper relates to previous work in the field of character control, where traditional methods rely on motion capture datasets or task-specific state machines. In contrast, the proposed method aims to automate the process of designing controllers by developing a universal synthesis method applicable to arbitrary behaviors, body morphologies, online changes in task objectives, perturbations due to noise and modeling errors.

The paper's main contributions are the alternating joint training and noise injection, which improve generalization beyond a straightforward supervised fit to CIO. The experiments show that the network produces realistic and stable control policies on diverse character models, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking tasks.

The paper's strengths include its ability to generate stable and realistic behaviors for a range of dynamical systems and tasks, without requiring motion capture or task-specific features or state machines. The method also allows for interaction, where the character's goal position is controlled by the user. The paper's use of noise injection and data augmentation is also a significant contribution, as it improves the robustness of the network and prevents overfitting.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments), which weakens the claims about generalization and overfitting baselines. The comparative evaluation is also weak, with only a brief MPC comparison given and systematic comparisons to other methods missing. Additionally, the mathematical presentation is mostly clear, but some parts, notably the CIO step and Eq. 6, lack definitions and detail, requiring readers to consult external references.

The paper could be improved by providing more details on the algorithmic choices, such as the initialization of X's in Alg. 1, the choice of hyperparameter eta, and the justification for keeping eta identical in both steps. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more granular experimental ablations, such as separate "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" variants.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the significance of the results and the potential impact of the method on the field of character control.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but some parts, notably Section 3.2, are confusing due to notational issues. The paper could benefit from more careful editing to fix minor writing and notation issues.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of techniques, including alternating joint training and noise injection, which improves generalization beyond a straightforward supervised fit to CIO. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on how the method differs from previous contributions and how it advances the state of the art in the field.

In terms of significance, the paper presents a promising and well-executed application of neural networks to character control. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical benefits of the method and how it could be used in real-world applications.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-executed application of neural networks to character control.
* The method improves generalization beyond a straightforward supervised fit to CIO.
* The paper presents a promising approach to automating the process of designing controllers for interactive character control.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set.
* The comparative evaluation is weak, with only a brief MPC comparison given.
* The mathematical presentation lacks detail in some parts, requiring readers to consult external references.

Overall, the paper presents a promising and well-executed application of neural networks to character control. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical benefits of the method, more granular experimental ablations, and more careful editing to fix minor writing and notation issues.
