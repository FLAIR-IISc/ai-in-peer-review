Summary and relation to prior work
The paper trains a small recurrent neural network (RNN) to serve as a near-optimal feedback controller for physically simulated characters. The network outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces; the authors argue this can be viewed as learning the dynamics under an implicit policy rather than a direct torque policy. Training alternates between (i) direct trajectory optimization via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to produce optimal state-action trajectories and local feedback gains, and (ii) supervised regression to fit the RNN to noisy versions of those trajectories augmented with linearized feedback corrections. Additional ingredients include input and hidden-unit noise during training, randomized changes in task goals to encourage interactive responsiveness, and an asynchronous distributed implementation that interleaves multiple trajectory optimizers with a GPU-based learner. At test time, the policy runs in real-time and applies actions by solving a one-step constrained optimization (T=1) to match network outputs while satisfying dynamics constraints. Results are shown for swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion, all with the same architecture and training recipe.

This line of work is closely related to learning policies from trajectory optimization [9,11] and to optimal control/RL methods used in character animation and robotics. The paper’s combination of CIO-generated demonstrations with supervised learning parallels Levine and Koltun [9] and Mordatch and Todorov [11]. Compared to that literature, the primary claims here are: (a) successful 3D locomotion across multiple morphologies with a single architecture; (b) a perturbation-based method to extract well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer; (c) empirical evidence that noise injection and joint optimization mitigate covariate shift and reduce divergence at rollout. The paper also contrasts its policies with MPC, noting that the joint optimization encourages more regular, less chaotic cyclic behaviors.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated integration of CIO with NN regression; sensible treatment of covariate shift via noisy inputs and linear feedback targets.
- Perturbation-based computation of local feedback gains that reuses the direct optimizer’s linear systems and avoids an extra LQG pass; computationally attractive and likely broadly useful.
- Impressive qualitative diversity: swimming, flying, bipeds, and quadrupeds controlled with a single network design and training pipeline.
- Practical distributed training architecture and real-time test-time execution via a simple T=1 optimization loop.
- Useful ablations showing joint optimization and noise injection are important (two orders of magnitude benefit in one task).

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Positioning vs prior work: The most relevant comparisons are to [9] and [11]. The paper should include quantitative comparisons on common metrics (training time/wall-clock, compute budget, sample/trajectory counts, success/fall rates, energy/torque cost, task error, robustness to disturbances, and runtime latency). At present the novelty relative to those works is not crisply established.
- Limited baselines: The MPC qualitative comparison is interesting but not sufficient. Please add direct baselines (e.g., [9], [11], and if feasible a modern policy-gradient baseline such as TRPO [14]) using the same physics, tasks, and metrics.
- Ill-conditioned gains: “Very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” needs quantification. Please report gain norms/singular value spectra and condition numbers for LQG vs your method, and show how λ trades off tracking performance vs conditioning.
- “Softer gains”: Define “softer” precisely (e.g., smaller spectral norm, reduced condition number, smoother temporal variation). Justify why allowing initial-state adjustment under the augmented cost yields better-behaved feedback and demonstrate with a sensitivity/robustness analysis.
- Exposition order: Move the LQG discussion and its drawbacks after presenting your perturbation-based strategy; this will improve flow and avoid distracting the reader before your main idea appears.
- Notation around Sec. 3.2 (lines ~226–240): The use of bars and similar symbols for s(X)=s̄ and C̃(X,s̄) makes the derivation hard to follow. Please adopt clearly distinct symbols for the nominal trajectory/state (e.g., s∗, X∗) and the perturbed targets (e.g., ŝ), and add a brief derivation appendix.
- Policy/action interpretation: The paper says the network “is not learning a control policy, but rather the dynamics under an implicit policy” because it outputs desired velocities. This is potentially confusing; from a control/RL standpoint this is still a policy mapping states to control commands (albeit in velocity space). Please clarify the intended distinction.
- Quantitative evaluation: Most results are qualitative. Please report standardized metrics per domain: goal tracking error, fall rate/time-to-fall under disturbances, energy/torque usage, contact force magnitudes, and throughput/latency at test time. Include confidence intervals over multiple seeds/trials.
- Realism constraints: Dynamics are enforced softly and may include root forces; please discuss implications for physical plausibility and whether the approach still works with stricter/no root forces.
- Generalization and robustness: You branch trajectories with changing goals during training. Please evaluate generalization to unseen goal distributions, sudden goal changes, and sensor/model noise at test time, with quantitative robustness curves.
- Implementation details: Provide timings for the T=1 optimization per step (CPU/GPU), end-to-end policy query time, and network size vs control rate. Clarify recurrent state dimensionality and its effect on performance.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical components are sound, and the perturbation-based gain computation is a neat contribution. However, empirical validation lacks strong quantitative baselines against the most relevant prior methods.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but the feedback-gain section and some notation should be revised; better figure/notation hygiene would help. Reordering the LQG discussion would improve readability.
- Originality: The overall recipe (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning + noise) is incremental over [9,11]. The gain-computation technique and the breadth of 3D demonstrations are the most original aspects.
- Significance: Potentially significant for animation and control practitioners due to practical real-time policies across diverse morphologies. Significance would be strengthened by robust quantitative comparisons and metrics.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method demonstrating real-time controllers for a wide variety of characters with a single architecture.
- Useful technical contribution for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimizers.
- Solid engineering and ablations supporting key design choices.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative comparison to the closest prior work; novelty and advantage over [9,11] are not convincingly demonstrated.
- Limited metrics and robustness analysis; heavy reliance on qualitative video.
- Some unclear exposition (notation, “soft” gains) and unquantified claims (ill-conditioning).

Overall
A strong and timely application paper with a nice technical twist on feedback gain extraction and convincing qualitative breadth. With clearer positioning and rigorous quantitative comparisons to [9,11] (and ideally a policy-gradient baseline), plus the requested clarifications, this would make a compelling NIPS contribution. As it stands, I lean toward borderline accept; my enthusiasm would increase substantially with the additional experiments and clarifications above.
