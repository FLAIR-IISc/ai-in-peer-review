## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for a diverse range of dynamical systems, encompassing swimming, flying, and various walking gaits. The approach uniquely combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) and noise injection to achieve stable and realistic behaviors without requiring motion capture or hand-designed state machines. The core idea – leveraging trajectory optimization to *teach* a neural network a control policy – is compelling, and the demonstration across diverse morphologies is notable. This work builds upon a growing body of research in learning control policies (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mnih et al., 2015) but attempts to scale these techniques to more complex, high-dimensional systems.

The paper’s strengths lie in its ambition and the breadth of its experimental validation. Achieving stable locomotion in 3D space, as claimed, is a significant step forward. The combination of techniques – interleaving supervised learning, trajectory optimization, noise injection, and feedback gain computation – seems well-reasoned. The claim of a unified approach applicable across different morphologies is appealing. However, the paper suffers from some organizational and clarity issues.

Crucially, the paper states that full model knowledge is required (see Eq. 6) but doesn't fully articulate the limitations or implications of this. The dependence on a precise, accurate dynamical model significantly restricts applicability, and a more thorough discussion of this assumption is necessary. Sections 4 and 6 are disproportionately detailed; Section 4, in particular, entirely omits any mention of the recurrent units referenced in the abstract, creating a disconnect between the stated architecture and the detailed explanation. 

Further, line 405 describes the network as "stochastic" despite the deterministic nature of the network itself; the stochasticity arises from the *training data* via noise injection. This requires clarification.  

**Arguments for acceptance:** The method shows promising results in generating complex behaviors across diverse morphologies. It offers a potential path towards automating controller design.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Organizational issues, a lack of thorough discussion on model dependence, and clarity issues regarding the network's stochasticity detract from the overall impact. 

**Recommendation:** The paper exhibits potential, but requires significant restructuring and clarification. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing the aforementioned concerns. The authors should prioritize a more balanced presentation of the method, a clearer articulation of its limitations, and improved clarity in technical descriptions.
