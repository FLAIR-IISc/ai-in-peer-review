This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks as feedback controllers for a variety of dynamic systems, including swimming, flying, and walking characters. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization) with supervised learning, leveraging the optimizer to generate training data for the neural network. The authors aim for a universal control approach, applicable across different morphologies and tasks, reducing the need for hand-tuning or motion capture. This work builds upon prior efforts in trajectory optimization [12] and neural network control [3, 6, 9, 11], notably extending successful 2D control methods to more challenging 3D scenarios.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates technical soundness, particularly in its integration of trajectory optimization and neural network training. The experimental results, while presented concisely, suggest the method is capable of generating stable and realistic behaviors across a diverse set of characters. The authors acknowledge potential overfitting and address it through noise injection, a reasonable approach. However, the level of detail regarding the trajectory optimization process (beyond referencing CIO) is limited. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to the parameters used in CIO, and a more thorough ablation study exploring these dependencies would strengthen the paper. While the authors mention limitations (e.g., the need for full model knowledge), they don’t fully address the computational cost associated with generating the training data – a crucial consideration for real-world applications.

**Clarity:** The paper’s overall structure is logical, but uneven. Sections 3 and 7 are well-explained, providing clear descriptions of the trajectory optimization and policy execution stages. However, Sections 4 and 6 are disproportionately brief given their importance. Section 4, in particular, fails to elaborate on the role of recurrent units – a key component highlighted in the abstract. The explanation of the “stochastic” nature of the network (line 405) is also unclear. The network itself appears deterministic, yet the training data is stochastic. This requires better articulation; it seems the stochasticity stems from noise injection during training to improve robustness, but this connection isn’t explicitly made. The notation is generally clear, though the use of multiple subscripts (e.g., st(Xi)) can be slightly cumbersome.

**Originality:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning isn’t entirely novel [9, 11], but the application to diverse 3D characters and the demonstrated stability of the resulting controllers represent a significant step forward. The method for deriving optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) appears to be a novel contribution, offering an efficient alternative to LQG. The universality of the approach—using the same network architecture for different characters—is also noteworthy. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other recent advances in model-based reinforcement learning, which also aim to learn controllers from simulated environments.

**Significance:** The results are promising and suggest a potentially valuable approach for automating the creation of controllers for complex characters. The ability to generate stable and realistic locomotion without relying on hand-tuning or motion capture data is a significant achievement. The method could have applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics. However, the limited scope of the experiments and the computational cost associated with data generation raise questions about the scalability and practicality of the approach. A demonstration of the method's performance on a more challenging robotics platform would greatly enhance its significance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Successfully demonstrates stable and realistic control of diverse 3D characters.
*   Presents a novel method for deriving optimal feedback gains.
*   Offers a potential path towards universal control policies, reducing the need for hand-tuning.
*   Integrates trajectory optimization and neural network learning effectively.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Section 4 lacks detail regarding the recurrent units.
*   Section 6 is disproportionately short given its importance.
*   The explanation of the network’s “stochasticity” is unclear.
*   Limited discussion of the computational cost of data generation.
*   Insufficient ablation study to assess the sensitivity to CIO parameters.
*   Requires full model knowledge (Eq. 6) which is a significant limitation.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject with request for major revision. The paper presents a promising approach, but its current form suffers from uneven coverage, lack of clarity in specific areas, and insufficient exploration of its limitations. Restructuring the paper to provide a more balanced treatment of all components, clarifying the explanations, and expanding the experimental analysis would significantly improve its quality and impact. The authors should explicitly state upfront the requirement for full model knowledge and address the computational cost of the method.
