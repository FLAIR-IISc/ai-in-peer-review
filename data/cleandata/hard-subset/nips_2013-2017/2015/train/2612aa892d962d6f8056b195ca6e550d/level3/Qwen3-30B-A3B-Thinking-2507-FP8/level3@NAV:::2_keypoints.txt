- Presents training neural nets as near-optimal feedback controllers via trajectory optimization plus neural policy regression for diverse simulated characters.  
- Demonstrates successful control across swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion with a single network architecture.  
- Main contribution is an application of deep learning to trajectory-optimization-based control rather than a fundamentally new algorithm.  
- Fails to adequately position itself relative to prior work, notably Abbeel & Levine, which already demonstrated neural network policy approximation.  
- Does not clearly explain improvements over prior work in learning speed, supported morphologies, runtime cost, or robustness to environment changes.  
- Technical claim about “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” is unexplained and needs justification.  
- The paper’s explanation for not using LQG should be moved to follow presentation of the proposed method for better narrative flow.  
- Notational confusion around lines 226–240 (similar bars and squiggles) requires disambiguation.  
- The statement that the approach produces “softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” needs a clear definition of “softer” and evidence for why changing the initial state is beneficial.  
- Experimental results are technically sound and produce realistic, stable interactive controllers, but novelty/significance is insufficiently differentiated from existing trajectory-optimization methods.  
- Overall: well-executed application with compelling results, but must better compare to related work and clarify contributions to meet NIPS originality/significance standards.
