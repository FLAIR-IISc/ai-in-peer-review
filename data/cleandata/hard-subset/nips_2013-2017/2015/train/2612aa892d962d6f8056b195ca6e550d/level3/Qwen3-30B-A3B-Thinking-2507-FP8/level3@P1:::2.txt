

The paper presents a method for training neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse physical characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) using interleaved trajectory optimization and neural policy regression. The key innovation lies in jointly optimizing trajectories and a neural policy via distributed stochastic optimization, with noise injection and feedback gain learning to enable robust real-time control. The authors demonstrate a single network architecture achieving stable, realistic behaviors across multiple morphologies without task-specific features or motion capture.

**Strengths:**  
The experimental results are compelling. The method successfully generates diverse, cyclic locomotion behaviors (e.g., bird flapping, quadruped trotting, biped walking) in 3D, outperforming naive neural training by two orders of magnitude (Fig. 2). Ablations confirm the necessity of noise injection (preventing overfitting) and joint optimization (enabling stability). The distributed training architecture scales efficiently (30 optimization nodes per GPU), and execution is real-time (50ms timesteps). The work significantly advances practical applications in character animation and robotics by unifying control strategies across disparate character types—a clear step toward "universal" controllers.

**Weaknesses:**  
The core contribution is not sufficiently novel relative to recent NIPS work. The approach builds directly on Levine & Koltun (ICML 2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (TOG 2012), who already used trajectory optimization (CIO) to train neural policies for locomotion. The paper fails to clarify *how* this work differs:  
- It does not compare metrics (learning speed, supported body variety, runtime cost) against prior neural policy methods.  
- The claim that LQG produces "very large, ill-conditioned feedback gains" lacks concrete justification—why do these cause divergence, and how does the proposed perturbation method specifically mitigate this?  
- The explanation of "softer feedback gains" (Section 3.2) is vague: "softer" relative to what? Why does weakening the initial-state constraint improve stability? This should be moved to follow the method description for clarity.  
- Notation (e.g., \(\bar{s}\), \(\tilde{s}\)) is confusingly similar (lines 226–240), hindering reproducibility.  

**Originality & Significance:**  
While the *application* to diverse 3D behaviors is valuable, the *methodology* is incremental. The paper does not establish a new theoretical framework or significantly advance trajectory-optimization-based control beyond existing techniques. The significance is limited to practical utility in animation/robotics, but the "universal controller" claim overstates the case—training still requires trajectory optimization per character type. The work does not address whether the learned policies generalize to unseen perturbations (e.g., external forces) beyond the ablation studies.

**Clarity & Quality:**  
The writing is generally clear, but key technical details are obscured. The trajectory optimization (Section 3) and feedback gain derivation (Section 3.2) lack sufficient motivation. The noise injection rationale (Section 2.1) is underdeveloped; it should explicitly link to robustness in control theory (e.g., via Lyapunov stability). The ablation studies (Section 8.1) are strong evidence for the method's components but do not contextualize the *novelty* of the approach. The comparison to MPC (Fig. 3) is insightful but should be expanded to discuss why cyclical behaviors emerge—this is a key advance over non-cyclical MPC trajectories.

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Reject.** The work is technically sound and demonstrates a useful practical application, but the contribution is not sufficiently novel for NIPS. The paper must rigorously differentiate itself from Levine & Koltun (ICML 2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (TOG 2012) by:  
1) Quantifying improvements in key metrics (e.g., training data efficiency, robustness to noise) over prior neural policy methods.  
2) Clarifying the "ill-conditioned gains" problem and justifying the perturbation method’s superiority over LQG.  
3) Resolving notation ambiguities and moving the LQG critique to after presenting the proposed solution.  
Without these revisions, the paper risks being perceived as a minor extension of existing trajectory-optimization pipelines rather than a significant contribution to the field. The experimental results are strong, but the *scientific contribution* falls short of NIPS’ standards for originality and conceptual advancement.
