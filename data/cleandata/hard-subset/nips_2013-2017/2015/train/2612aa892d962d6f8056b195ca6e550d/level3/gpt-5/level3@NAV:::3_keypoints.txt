- Proposes alternating training that couples Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with a recurrent NN policy; CIO yields trajectories and local gains, NN trained offline with noisy inputs, alternating with proximal regularization and asynchronous cluster implementation.

- Key idea: alternating joint training plus sensor-space augmentation and additive hidden-layer noise produces robust, real-time control policies.

- Experiments cover multiple morphologies: swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds.

- Related to prior work on trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (e.g., Guided Policy Search, ADMM), MPC, and model-based control (LQR/LQG/iLQR).

- Uses a denoising analogy to motivate input perturbations, but CIO-provided local gains mean targets themselves change.

- Technical strength: method is well-motivated and technically sound; using CIO-derived gains to define noise-consistent targets is elegant.

- Empirical weaknesses: small training datasets (≈100–200 trials × 5 segments) and limited comparative baselines.

- Missing performance data: no wall-clock training time, policy inference cost, CPU/GPU usage, or scaling results despite real-time claims.

- Mathematical/notation gaps: Eq. 6 and CIO constraints lack definitions; Sec. 3.2 drops time index and leaves the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs mapping ambiguous.

- Clarity issues: need precise notation for s, a_s and time dependence; define all symbols in equations; connect asynchronous optimization discussion earlier; fix numerous typos and reference inconsistencies.

- Originality: combining alternating joint training with CIO-derived local gains and explicit input/hidden noise is a meaningful advance over simple supervised fits.

- Significance: promising for interactive character control but needs broader comparisons and stronger empirical evidence to convince a broad ML audience.

- Request baseline comparisons: include MPC (with citation and settings), iLQR/LQR-LQG, Guided Policy Search, and modern policy-gradient methods.

- Request reproducibility and scaling details: release code/models and report training/inference runtimes and resource usage.

- Clarify target representation: state whether orientation/facing are included as targets and how to add target angles.

- Terminology clarification: distinguish “data augmentation” (state perturbations) from “noise injection” (hidden activations); note denoising-autoencoder analogy is imperfect.

- Algorithmic questions: how are initial trajectories X initialized; why use the same η in both optimization blocks; justify keeping η identical; discuss annealing of CIO regularization α.

- Ablation studies needed: separate effects of no data augmentation vs. no hidden-layer noise and other component-wise ablations.

- Arguments for acceptance: elegant integration of trajectory optimization and NN policies, robust performance across diverse characters, clear engineering contribution.

- Arguments against acceptance: limited/inconclusive empirical evaluation, missing runtime/scaling data, and clarity gaps in derivations and algorithmic choices.
