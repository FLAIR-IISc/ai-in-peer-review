This paper presents a method for controlling complex characters in a physics-based simulator using a small, fast recurrent neural network. The core idea is to use an offline trajectory optimization process to generate training data, which is then used to learn an interactive control policy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach across a diverse range of characters and cyclic behaviors, including bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting, swimming, and flying, all using the same network architecture. This work fits into a growing body of research that combines offline optimization with function approximators like neural networks to create real-time controllers, closely relating to prior work by Levine et al. [9].

The primary strength of this paper lies in its impressive experimental results. The ability to generate stable, plausible, and interactive control for such a wide variety of morphologies from a single, unified framework is a significant engineering achievement and demonstrates the method's potential.

However, the paper's main weakness is the lack of clarity regarding its novel scientific contribution. The submission reads more as an application of deep learning to existing trajectory-optimization techniques rather than a fundamentally new method. Trajectory optimization is already well-established for producing realistic control signals, and using a neural network to approximate an optimizer's step-by-step actions has also been previously explored [9]. The authors need to better position their work by providing a direct, quantitative comparison to the most closely related methods (e.g., from Abbeel, Levine) across metrics like learning speed, supported body variety, runtime computation cost, and robustness.

Furthermore, several points in the exposition require clarification. The authors should explain what they mean by "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" and why this is a problem for alternative approaches. The notation around lines 226–240 is confusing and should be disambiguated. The claim that the proposed approach "produces softer feedback gains" is vague; "softer" should be precisely defined and its benefits justified. For improved readability, the discussion on why LQG was not used would be better placed after the proposed strategy is fully presented.

In summary, while the experimental work is sound, the paper must be significantly revised to clarify its contribution relative to recent, closely related work. Without this, the originality and significance of the work are difficult to assess.
