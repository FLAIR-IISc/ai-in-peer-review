This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to act as feedback controllers for a variety of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and walking characters. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) to learn a control policy that maps states to actions (velocities). The authors demonstrate the ability to generate stable and realistic behaviors across diverse morphologies using a single network architecture, a significant step towards a unified control approach. This work builds upon previous efforts in reinforcement learning and optimal control, but aims to address the challenges of scaling these methods to high-dimensional, continuous spaces.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in robotics, animation, and biomechanics – automating the creation of complex controllers. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of traditional methods relying on motion capture or hand-tuned state machines. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a promising approach, as it leverages the strengths of both: CIO provides high-quality training data, while the neural network allows for real-time execution and generalization. The results, as evidenced by the supplementary video, are impressive, showcasing stable and realistic locomotion across a variety of simulated characters. The successful application to 3D locomotion is a particularly notable achievement, surpassing the capabilities of some recent related work. The inclusion of noise injection and a distributed training architecture further contribute to the robustness and scalability of the method.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses in clarity and structure. The sections detailing the neural network architecture and training (Sections 4 and 6) are disproportionately lengthy and detailed relative to other sections, particularly given the relatively standard nature of the network itself. Section 4, in particular, focuses heavily on implementation details and lacks a clear description of how the recurrent units, mentioned in the abstract as critical for implementing memory, are actually incorporated into the network and utilized during training. The authors state the action generated by the network is defined as velocity and that the network learns the dynamics under an implicit policy, which feels somewhat hand-wavy and could benefit from further elaboration.

A minor point of clarification is needed regarding the description of the network as "stochastic" (Line 405). While the *training* process utilizes stochasticity through noise injection, the network itself, as presented, appears deterministic given a specific input state. This distinction should be made clearer. Furthermore, the paper assumes full model knowledge for accurate dynamics simulation, as evident in Equation 6, and a discussion of the sensitivity of the approach to model inaccuracies would be beneficial.

**Review Summary:**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with strong experimental validation. Claims are generally well-supported by the presented results, and the authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of related work. However, the lack of detail regarding the recurrent units and the slightly unclear framing of the network's “stochasticity” detract somewhat from the technical rigor.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the disproportionate length of Sections 4 and 6, and the lack of detail regarding the recurrent network architecture, hinder clarity. The discussion surrounding the implicit policy could also be more explicit.

**Originality:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning, while building on existing ideas, represents a novel and effective approach to control synthesis. The successful application to complex 3D locomotion is a significant contribution.

**Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating the potential for a unified control framework applicable to diverse characters and tasks. This work could have a substantial impact on robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The ability to generate realistic behaviors without relying on motion capture or hand-tuning is particularly valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a novel and effective approach to complex control synthesis.
*   Achieves impressive results on challenging 3D locomotion tasks.
*   Presents a unified control framework applicable to diverse morphologies.
*   Combines the strengths of trajectory optimization and neural network learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Sections 4 and 6 are disproportionately detailed and lack focus.
*   The role of recurrent units is not sufficiently explained.
*   The term “stochastic” network is potentially misleading.
*   Sensitivity to model inaccuracies is not discussed.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a promising and significant contribution to the field. However, restructuring the paper to improve clarity and address the weaknesses outlined above is crucial. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed explanation of the recurrent network architecture and clarify the network's stochastic nature. A brief discussion of the impact of model inaccuracies would also be beneficial.
