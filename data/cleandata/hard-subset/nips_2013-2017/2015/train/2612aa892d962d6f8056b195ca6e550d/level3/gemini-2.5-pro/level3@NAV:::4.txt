This paper presents a method for creating interactive controllers for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to jointly learn a set of optimal trajectories and a neural network policy that can reproduce them. This is framed as a large optimization problem, which the authors cleverly decouple into two alternating, more tractable subproblems: trajectory optimization and supervised policy regression. This approach builds upon recent work that also combines trajectory optimization with function approximation but demonstrates its effectiveness on a more diverse and challenging set of 3D characters and locomotion tasks, including swimming, flying, and bipedal/quadrupedal walking.

The primary strength of this work lies in its technical sophistication and impressive empirical results. The proposed optimization scheme, which interleaves trajectory optimization with policy learning, is well-conceived and allows the reuse of existing methods. The results, as shown in the supplementary video, are highly compelling and demonstrate a significant level of control over diverse and complex morphologies. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and the authors provide strong ablative experiments in Section 8.1 that convincingly demonstrate the claimed advantages of both the joint optimization framework and the use of sensory noise during training.

My main questions relate to the policy execution method described in Section 7. I was surprised that the learned policy does not run on its own but instead serves as a target for a further per-timestep optimization. The motivation for this extra step could be clarified. Is it required to compensate for policy regression errors or residual costs from the offline trajectory optimization? And would the dynamics model alone not prevent physically implausible states? On the other hand, I appreciate that this approach enables the use of larger, more practical timesteps.

A few minor points:
-   Line 89 contains a typo: "to unity."
-   On line 153, the claim "similar to recent approaches" could be strengthened with one or two specific references.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a powerful and effective framework. While I am not deeply familiar with this specific domain and thus cannot fully judge the novelty or broader significance, the technical execution is excellent, and the results clearly advance the state of the art in character animation. The combination of techniques is potent, and the empirical validation is thorough.
