This paper presents a practical and elegant method for training recurrent neural networks to function as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex, contact-rich dynamical systems. The central idea is to alternate trajectory optimization (via Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO [12]) with neural network regression, while explicitly teaching the policy local feedback around the optimized trajectories by embedding feedback gains into the training targets. The network outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state updates; at execution it runs in real time with a T=1 constrained optimization step that respects dynamics and contacts. Key ingredients include (i) a perturbation-based method to compute well-conditioned optimal feedback gains using quantities already computed by CIO, (ii) injecting sensor and hidden-state noise and training on noise-perturbed inputs with gain-corrected targets, (iii) soft coupling between trajectory and policy via a penalty for deviation, and (iv) a distributed asynchronous training loop with proximal regularization for stability. The approach yields stable, realistic control across diverse morphologies and tasks (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion) using a single network architecture.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on CIO [12] for contact-rich motion synthesis and on recent work that learns policies from optimized trajectories [9,11], but advances the state of the art by (a) operating in full 3D with stable, cyclic gaits, (b) providing a principled and efficient way to compute local feedback gains within a direct trajectory-optimization pipeline, and (c) showing that jointly optimizing trajectories with a policy-consistency penalty regularizes toward predictable, non-chaotic behaviors compared to pure MPC. It complements model-free RL approaches (e.g., [10,14]) and classic optimal control (iLQG/LQG), and is aligned with computer graphics controllers (e.g., SIMBICON [21]) while reducing the need for task-specific state machines or motion capture.

Quality: The method is technically sound, with careful choices that address known failure modes of closed-loop policy learning in continuous domains. The derivation of feedback gains via a soft-constraint perturbation and Woodbury identity is insightful and computationally efficient, reusing the Gauss-Newton Hessian inverse. The stochastic, proximal alternating scheme is reasonable for a nonconvex, large-scale problem, and the ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of both components. The execution-time formulation as a one-step constrained optimization is a neat and robust alternative to explicit integration. Empirical results are diverse and compelling. Some quantitative evaluations (success rates, robustness to perturbations, sensitivity to λ and α) could be expanded.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well written and organized. The problem formulation, algorithmic components, and engineering choices are clearly explained. The roles of noise, gains, and proximal terms are made explicit. Minor suggestions: add a schematic showing data flow (optimizer ⇄ policy) and a short discussion of the recurrent state’s learned function; clarify units/scales for noise variances and provide a brief stability discussion for the chosen spectral radius > 1 initialization.

Originality: Embedding analytically computed local feedback gains into the supervised learning targets, and computing those gains efficiently within CIO, is a novel and impactful contribution. The soft coupling of trajectory and policy objectives in a distributed stochastic setting, combined with task-switching via branched trajectories, is also a thoughtful design that advances prior imitation-from-optimizer approaches.

Significance: The ability to learn a single, robust controller that generalizes across very different morphologies and tasks, without MoCap or hand-designed state machines, is a substantial contribution for both graphics and control. The approach is likely to influence subsequent work on learning-in-the-loop trajectory optimization, robust policy learning for contact dynamics, and real-time character control. The practical training recipe (noise-injection, gains-as-targets, proximal alternating updates) is likely to be reused.

Strengths:
- Principled integration of local feedback gains into policy learning; elegant perturbation-based gain computation reusing CIO artifacts.
- Joint optimization with a policy-consistency penalty regularizes trajectories toward stable, cyclic behaviors; improves robustness over pure MPC.
- Clear empirical evidence across diverse 3D systems; ablations support design choices.
- Practical, scalable training architecture and real-time execution procedure.
- Exceptionally clear and well argued.

Weaknesses:
- Limited quantitative benchmarking against strong baselines (e.g., iLQG/LQR-MPC, TRPO [14], or model-free continuous-control methods) on shared metrics.
- Fidelity concerns: soft constraint enforcement and root forces may limit transfer to hardware; action space includes direct force outputs.
- Sensitivity analysis is light: robustness to model errors, external perturbations, and hyperparameters (λ, α, noise scales) could be reported more thoroughly.
- Ablation on the role of learned recurrent state (vs. purely feedforward) is limited; memory contribution is not deeply analyzed.
- Compute costs and sample efficiency relative to alternatives are discussed qualitatively but not rigorously compared.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add quantitative comparisons on standard continuous-control benchmarks or matched tasks with iLQG/MPC and a representative policy-gradient baseline, controlling for computation time and dynamics fidelity.
- Report robustness metrics (success under push disturbances, parameter noise, or unmodeled contact friction changes); include sensitivity plots for λ and α.
- Provide an ablation that removes or randomizes as in the training targets to isolate the effect of embedded gains.
- Analyze the recurrent state (dimensionality, learned timescales, contributions to performance).
- Clarify the extent of root forces at execution and discuss pathways to stricter enforcement (e.g., harder constraints or tighter penalties) and implications for sim-to-real.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, novel methodological contribution (gain-informed policy learning within CIO).
- Broad, convincing demonstrations across challenging 3D tasks with a unified architecture.
- Clear evidence that the joint/noise/gain design improves robustness and realism.
- High clarity and relevance to NIPS communities in ML, control, and graphics.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited head-to-head quantitative baselines and robustness metrics.
- Some reliance on soft dynamics constraints and action parameterization that may limit real-world deployment.

Overall recommendation: Strong accept. The paper makes a substantial, well-validated contribution that unifies trajectory optimization and neural policy learning, enabling robust, real-time control of complex characters without task-specific engineering.
