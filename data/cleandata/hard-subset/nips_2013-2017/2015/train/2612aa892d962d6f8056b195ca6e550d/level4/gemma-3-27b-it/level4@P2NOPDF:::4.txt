This paper presents an approach to interactive control of complex characters by integrating trajectory optimization and policy learning. The core contribution lies in the joint learning of trajectories and policies, achieved through an alternating optimization scheme. This decoupling allows the authors to leverage established methods for each subproblem while demonstrating effective performance in practice. While I found the paper technically sound and well-presented, my limited expertise in this specific domain prevents me from fully assessing its potential impact. It appears to be a technically strong work, though it doesn’t introduce significant novel theoretical results.

The authors demonstrate impressive technical skill throughout, from the conceptual framework to the implementation details, accompanied by compelling results. The benefits of incorporating sensory noise during training and jointly learning trajectories with the policy are clearly substantiated by the experiments. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written, striking an appropriate balance between detail and clarity given the problem’s inherent complexity and the intricacies of its implementation. Each section focuses on a distinct aspect of the problem, and the numerous cross-references significantly enhance readability and understanding.

However, the motivation behind the additional optimization step described in Section 7 could benefit from further elaboration. Having followed the preceding sections, I initially anticipated the policy would function autonomously without requiring further optimization. Is this additional step necessary due to persistent trajectory costs or policy regression errors that would accumulate when directly applying the policy's actions to the physical simulator? I understand that the dynamics simulation incorporates physical constraints, preventing unrealistic behavior, but perhaps I’m overlooking a crucial detail. I do acknowledge the advantage of this re-optimization approach – enabling the use of larger timesteps.

On line 153, the statement "similar to recent approaches…" would be strengthened by including one or two relevant citations. The rationale for restricting updates within the parameter space initially seemed counterintuitive. However, I ultimately understood its usefulness within the context of the block-alternating optimization scheme employed in this work.

There's a minor typographical error on line 89 (“to unify”). 

Overall, the results presented are highly impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and its implementation appear sensible. However, given that this research area lies somewhat outside my core expertise, I am hesitant to offer a definitive assessment of its novelty and overall significance.
