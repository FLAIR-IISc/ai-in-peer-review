This paper proposes a method for the interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization and policy learning. The core contribution is a framework for jointly learning trajectories and a control policy. The authors find empirically that decoupling this into two alternating subproblems is effective, as it allows the use of existing, well-established methods and performs well in practice. While I am not an expert in this specific domain and cannot confidently assess the paper's full impact, it appears to be a significant technical achievement, even if it does not introduce new theoretical insights.

The authors demonstrate an impressive level of technical sophistication at all levels—from the high-level concepts to the implementation details—and the results are equally impressive. The paper clearly demonstrates the claimed advantages of its two main contributions: using sensory noise during training and jointly learning the trajectories with the policy.

The paper is also very well-written. Given the complexity of the problem and the proposed solution, the authors provide an appropriate level of detail. Each section addresses a distinct aspect of the problem, and helpful cross-references effectively connect the different components of the framework.

I have one main point of confusion regarding the motivation for the additional optimization step described in Section 7. Based on the preceding sections, I expected the learned policy to be sufficient for controlling the character at test time without requiring further optimization. Could the authors clarify why this extra step is necessary? Is it to correct for an accumulation of errors resulting from a residual trajectory cost or policy regression error? I would have imagined that the forward dynamics model already enforces physical constraints, preventing implausible states, but perhaps I am misunderstanding a key detail. I do, however, appreciate that re-optimizing at each time step enables the use of a larger time step, which is a clear practical benefit.

**Minor Points:**

*   **l153:** The paper states that keeping policy updates small is "similar to recent approaches." Could the authors provide one or two citations here? This strategy seems counterintuitive as a general principle, although I understand its utility within this work's alternating optimization scheme.
*   **l89:** Typo: "to unify" seems to be an incomplete phrase.

In summary, the paper's results are impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and its implementation seem sensible. However, as this research area is outside my core expertise, I find it difficult to assess the work's novelty and long-term significance.
