- Presents a method that alternates contact-invariant trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised training of recurrent neural policies, using noise-injected sensory inputs and adjusted targets via local linear feedback gains.  
- Efficiently computes local feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer by reusing the Gauss–Newton Hessian and Woodbury identity, avoiding a separate LQG pass.  
- Soft-couples trajectory optimization and policy learning with a quadratic penalty and proximal regularization, alternating CIO and network regression.  
- At test time the learned policy runs in real time with a one-step implicit optimization enforcing dynamics and contact consistency.  
- Demonstrated across diverse 3D tasks (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion) using a single network architecture and training pipeline.  
- Builds on prior work combining trajectory optimization and function approximation, extending to harder 3D contact-rich tasks and proposing a novel feedback-gain correction for noisy targets.  
- Strength: principled, practical mechanism to extract stabilizing gains from direct trajectory optimization that is reusable and better-conditioned.  
- Strength: coherent training loop that justifies co-optimizing data and policy for closed-loop robustness.  
- Strength: generality — same architecture produces controllers for markedly different morphologies without mocap or task-specific features.  
- Strength: elegant real-time execution via one-step implicit optimization that avoids storing trajectories.  
- Strength: useful empirical ablations (noise vs no-noise, static dataset) and a network-size sweep reported.  
- Strength: clear exposition, equations, and a pragmatic distributed training setup aiding reproducibility.  
- Weakness: limited quantitative evaluation—missing standardized metrics like success rates, time-to-fall, energy cost, and disturbance rejection.  
- Weakness: sparse comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., iLQR/TVLQR, TRPO, guided policy search) on common tasks.  
- Weakness: dynamics and contact enforced softly, risk of nonphysical root forces at execution—lack of quantification of violations.  
- Weakness: action parameterization predicts desired velocities and contact forces (not torques), relying on one-step optimization and blurring policy/dynamics boundary.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of recurrent state—recurrent dimensionality across tasks and its role are not specified or ablated.  
- Weakness: robustness tests limited to small Gaussian noise; lacking evaluations for large pushes, terrain changes, parameter errors, or sensor delays.  
- Weakness: computational cost and data requirements unclear—need accounting of trajectories/samples and sensitivity to number of trials/branches.  
- Assessment: technically sound, clear, and original in the feedback-gain derivation and integration; main gap is quantitative evaluation and baselines.  
- Significance: high potential impact for physics-based animation and model-based control by unifying trajectory optimization and policy learning in high-DOF systems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant CIO+NN integration, efficient gain computation, diverse 3D demos with a single pipeline, and practical training recipe.  
- Arguments against acceptance: inadequate quantitative baselines, soft constraint reliance and one-step optimization raise hardware portability concerns, and under-analysis of action choice and recurrence.  
- Key questions for authors: recurrent-state dimensionality and sensitivity; magnitude/frequency of dynamics/contact violations and whether constraints can be hardened; standardized metrics and iLQR+TVLQR comparison; performance under larger unmodeled perturbations.  
- Recommendation: strong accept conditional on adding more quantitative results and at least one baseline comparison on a representative task.
