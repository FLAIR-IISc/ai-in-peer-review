This paper presents an approach to interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The core idea is to jointly learn trajectories and a control policy, leveraging the practical benefit of decoupling the optimization problems into alternating subproblems. This decoupling allows the authors to readily employ existing, well-established methods for each subproblem while still achieving strong performance. While my limited expertise in this specific domain prevents a definitive assessment of the work’s broader impact, it appears to represent a significant technical achievement.

The authors demonstrate an impressive level of technical depth, evident in both the conceptual framework and its implementation. The claimed benefits of incorporating sensory noise during training, and of jointly learning trajectories and the policy, are convincingly demonstrated through the presented results. The paper is exceptionally well-written, striking an appropriate balance between detail and clarity given the inherent complexity of the problem and its solution. The organization is logical, with each section focusing on a distinct aspect of the method, and the cross-referencing between sections is particularly helpful for understanding the interplay of different components.

One area where the motivation could be strengthened is the introduction of the additional optimization step described in Section 7. Having followed the preceding sections, I initially anticipated that the learned policy would operate without requiring further optimization at runtime. Is this additional step necessary due to an inherent residual trajectory cost, or a persistent policy regression error, such that directly applying the policy’s output to the simulator would lead to accumulated errors over time? I understand that the physical simulator should enforce constraints to prevent physically implausible behavior, but perhaps there’s a nuance I’m missing. I do appreciate that re-optimization at each timestep enables the use of larger timesteps, which is a valuable practical advantage.

On line 153, the phrase “similar to recent approaches…” would benefit from one or two specific citations. The notion of keeping updates small in parameter space initially seemed counterintuitive, though the explanation relating to the block-alternating optimization structure clarified its utility within the context of this work. A minor typographical error exists on line 89 ("to unify"). 

Overall, the results presented are impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and its implementation appear sensible. However, given that this research area falls somewhat outside my primary expertise, I find it challenging to confidently evaluate the novelty and ultimate significance of this contribution.
