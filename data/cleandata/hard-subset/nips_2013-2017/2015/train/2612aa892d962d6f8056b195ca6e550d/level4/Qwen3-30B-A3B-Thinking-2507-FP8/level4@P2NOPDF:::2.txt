

This paper presents a method for using a compact neural network to represent a control policy for interactive character animation in physics simulators, trained via trajectory optimization. The key claim is that a single network architecture can generalize across diverse cyclic motion patterns and body types. While the approach demonstrates practical utility, the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its contribution from established trajectory optimization techniques and recent deep learning applications in control.  

General Comments  
The work appears to apply deep learning to well-established computer graphics methodologies rather than introducing novel algorithmic advances. Trajectory optimization for generating realistic control signals has been extensively validated in prior literature (e.g., [9]), and neural network approximations of trajectory-optimized policies have also been explored in related work. The authors must clearly articulate how their specific implementation advances beyond these existing frameworks. For instance, how does this approach compare to Abbeel and Levine’s model-based reinforcement learning methods? Is the goal reduced training time, broader body-type generalization, lower runtime computation, or improved robustness to environmental noise? Without addressing these points, the contribution remains ambiguous.  

Specific Comments  
1.  **Clarity of technical claims**: The phrase "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" requires elaboration. What metrics define "large" or "ill-conditioned"? How do these properties impact the method’s reliability?  
2.  **Logical flow**: The discussion of why LQG was not used (Section 3.3) should be moved to follow the presentation of the proposed strategy, not precede it. This placement disrupts the narrative flow.  
3.  **Notation clarity**: The notation around lines 226–240 is confusing. Introducing $s(X) = \bar{s}$ and then defining a cost as $s(X) - \bar{s}$ creates visual ambiguity due to the similar appearance of $\bar{s}$ and $s(X)$. The authors should adopt distinct notation (e.g., $s_{\text{target}}$) to avoid misinterpretation.  
4.  **"Soft feedback gains"**: The claim that "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter $\lambda$" lacks context. Softer than what? How does this property improve performance? The paper should define "soft" in relation to standard control metrics (e.g., conventional LQR gains) and justify its desirability.  
5.  **Motivation for state adjustment**: The rationale for modifying the initial state during training is unclear. How does this adjustment specifically benefit the learned policy, and why is it necessary?  

The work is technically sound and demonstrates a viable application of neural networks to character control. However, the paper’s core contribution—its distinctiveness relative to existing trajectory optimization and deep learning approaches—is inadequately justified. The authors should explicitly compare their method to Abbeel/Levine’s work (and similar recent studies) in terms of key metrics: training efficiency, generalization scope (body types/motion patterns), runtime cost, and robustness. Without this, the paper risks being perceived as an incremental application rather than a meaningful advance.  

Minor suggestions:  
- Replace "more-or-less cyclic behaviours" with "diverse cyclic motion patterns" for precision.  
- Clarify whether "soft gains" refer to reduced feedback strength, smoother transitions, or another property.  
- Ensure all cited prior work (e.g., [9]) is directly contrasted with the proposed method.  

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem but requires stronger positioning against existing literature to justify its publication at NeurIPS. The technical execution is competent, but the contribution narrative must be sharpened to highlight novel insights.
