- Paper proposes learning near–real-time feedback controllers by alternating trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised training of an RNN that outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent state updates.
- Key components: CIO for trajectories and gains, noisy-sensor training with first-order action targets, alternating distributed/asynchronous optimization with proximal regularization, and T=1 constrained online optimization.
- Demonstrations cover swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal 3D locomotion without motion capture or hand-crafted gait priors.
- Builds on prior work coupling trajectory optimization and function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov) and on CIO for physically plausible motions.
- Claimed novelties are feedback-gain extraction from direct TO, joint noisy supervision matching first-order corrections, and a unified NN across morphologies.
- Strength: A single NN architecture and training recipe yields stable, purposeful behaviors across diverse bodies and tasks.
- Strength: Practical innovations—noise-injected training, perturbation-based gain computation reusing CIO curvature, and proximal regularization—improve stability.
- Strength: Real-time execution using a short-horizon T=1 constrained step balances robustness and lightweight policy evaluation.
- Strength: Ablation studies convincingly show joint optimization and noise injection are important for performance.
- Strength: Strong engineering—scalable distributed training, GPU implementation, and broad task scope.
- Weakness: Novelty is incremental; using TO to train policies is close to prior templates and needs sharper positioning against related methods.
- Weakness: Limited quantitative evaluation—missing metrics like goal-tracking error, fall rate, energy/torque, disturbance rejection, and terrain robustness.
- Weakness: Clarity gaps in derivation of “softer” feedback gains, role of λ, and confusing notation (e.g., s(X)=s̄).
- Weakness: LQG comparison is introduced at a confusing point and interrupts flow before method is fully specified.
- Weakness: Physical realism concerns—dynamics enforced via soft constraints and possible root forces may hinder hardware transfer; runtime/latency of T=1 solve not reported.
- Weakness: Unclear generalization to out-of-distribution scenarios (large goal changes, unexpected contacts, uneven terrain).
- Quality: Technically sound alternating optimization and clever use of the Woodbury identity for gain extraction, but requires stronger empirical baselines and robustness studies.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but improve feedback-gain notation, move LQG discussion after the method, and define “softer” feedback quantitatively.
- Originality: Incremental overall; most novel pieces are gain computation from direct TO and joint noisy supervision; uniform application across morphologies is noteworthy.
- Significance: High potential impact for animation and robotics if robustness and efficiency are validated more rigorously; current demos drive perceived significance.
- Suggested experiments: Quantify gain magnitudes/conditioning vs λ, report runtime/training resources and inference latency, and compare to MPC, Levine & Koltun–style TO+policy, and TRPO baselines.
- Suggested robustness tests: Disturbance rejection (impulse pushes), sensor/model noise, goal-switching, and terrain variation with failure/recovery statistics.
- Pros for acceptance: Practical unified method with compelling demos, real-time control, and thoughtful ablations/engineering.
- Cons for acceptance: Insufficient novelty relative to close work, weak quantitative evaluation and baselines, and clarity issues in gains derivation and experimental reporting.
