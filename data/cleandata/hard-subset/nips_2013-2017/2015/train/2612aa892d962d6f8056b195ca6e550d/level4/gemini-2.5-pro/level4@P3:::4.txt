This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural network policies for the interactive control of complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimization method (Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate high-quality motion data and then use this data to train a neural network policy via supervised learning. Crucially, the authors propose a joint optimization framework where the trajectories and the policy are iteratively refined together. This is formulated as a block-alternating optimization that alternates between optimizing a set of trajectories (given the current policy) and updating the policy network via regression (given the current trajectories). The authors introduce several key technical components to make this process robust, including injecting noise into sensory inputs during training and using the trajectory optimizer to compute optimal feedback gains, which helps the network learn to correct for small perturbations. The method is demonstrated on a variety of challenging 3D locomotion tasks, including swimming, flying, and bipedal and quadrupedal walking.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical results. The method successfully generates stable and visually plausible locomotion for a diverse range of characters with different morphologies and dynamics (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) using a single, unified framework. Achieving this level of robust, interactive control for complex 3D characters without task-specific state machines or motion capture data represents a significant step forward for the field. The emergent gaits, such as trotting for quadrupeds, demonstrate the power of the optimization-based approach.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Evaluation:** The proposed method is a technically sophisticated combination of trajectory optimization and deep learning. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 provide compelling evidence for the necessity of the key components of the proposed method. The comparison against training on a static dataset ("no joint") clearly demonstrates the value of the joint optimization procedure, while the "no noise" case effectively illustrates that noise injection is critical for generalization and preventing the policy from diverging during rollouts. These experiments strongly support the authors' central claims.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, successfully explaining a complex, multi-part system. The authors do a good job of breaking down the problem into its constituent parts—trajectory optimization, feedback gain calculation, and neural network regression—and explaining how they fit together within the overall distributed optimization algorithm. The cross-referencing between sections is helpful for the reader.

**Weaknesses and Points for Clarification:**

1.  **Motivation for Policy Execution Step (Section 7):** The motivation for the optimization-based policy execution step in Section 7 could be further clarified. After training, one might expect the policy `πθ` to directly command the character by feeding its action output into the physics simulator at each step. However, the paper describes an additional optimization at every timestep to find a next state `x1` that best matches the policy's desired velocity `q̇des` while satisfying the equations of motion.

    Is this additional optimization necessary because the learned policy is not perfectly consistent with the dynamics, and simply executing its actions would lead to an accumulation of errors and instability? Or is it primarily to handle the physical constraints (Eq. 6) more robustly than a standard forward integrator, as suggested by the mention of using larger timesteps? A more explicit discussion of why a direct rollout is insufficient and what problems this per-timestep optimization solves would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insight for practitioners.

2.  **Novelty:** While the results are state-of-the-art, the paper's novelty appears to lie more in the effective synthesis and scaling of existing techniques rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new theoretical concept. The work builds effectively on recent research combining trajectory optimization and policy learning. The main contributions seem to be the specific formulation of the joint, stochastic optimization problem and the engineering required to make it work on such complex 3D tasks. This is a valuable contribution, but it's worth noting that it is primarily an empirical and systems-level one.

**Minor Points:**

*   **Line 153:** The paper states, "Similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches, we introduce quadratic proximal regularization terms..." It would be helpful to include one or two citations here to ground this statement and guide interested readers.
*   **Line 89:** There is a minor typo: "It is our aim to unity these..." should be "It is our aim to unify these..."

**Summary:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with very impressive results. The authors present a well-engineered and effective system for creating interactive controllers for a wide array of complex characters. The experimental validation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the benefits of the proposed joint optimization and noise injection techniques. While the motivation for the real-time optimization step could be clearer and the core novelty is more empirical than theoretical, the paper's demonstrated success in solving a set of very difficult control problems makes it a significant and valuable contribution to the community.
