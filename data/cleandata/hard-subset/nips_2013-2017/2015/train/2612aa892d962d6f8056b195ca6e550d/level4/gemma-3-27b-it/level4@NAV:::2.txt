“Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks” demonstrates the use of a compact and efficient neural network to encode a control policy for real-time character control within a physics simulation. 

The method employs trajectory optimization during training to develop the neural network policy. The core contribution lies in demonstrating the versatility of a single network architecture across a spectrum of largely cyclic motions and varying character morphologies.

**General Comments:**

This paper presents an application of deep learning techniques to established (and relatively recent) methods within computer graphics. The effectiveness of trajectory optimization in generating plausible control signals is well-documented in prior research, including work cited by the authors. Furthermore, the use of neural networks to approximate the sequential actions necessary to replicate trajectories derived from trajectory optimization is also present in the existing literature (e.g., [9]).  Consequently, the authors should more explicitly contextualize their work in relation to these pre-existing approaches. 

Specifically, a comparison to recent work from Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine would be valuable. The authors should clarify how their approach differs – does it prioritize faster learning, broader compatibility with body types, reduced runtime computation, or improved robustness to environmental changes?

**Specific Comments:**

The statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" requires further clarification. 

Consider postponing the justification for not using LQG until after the presentation of your implemented strategy.

The notation surrounding lines 226-240 is unclear. The introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by a cost function expressed in terms of s(X) - \bar s, is visually confusing due to the similarity between bars and squiggles.

Could you elaborate on the meaning of "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda"?  What is being compared when referring to “softer” gains, and what constitutes a “soft” versus a “not soft” gain? Additionally, please explain the rationale behind modifying the initial state as part of the procedure. While the work is sound, the novelty and distinction from other recent, related research needs to be more clearly articulated.
