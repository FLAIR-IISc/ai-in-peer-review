"Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" demonstrates the application of a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive control of a character in a physics simulator. This approach utilizes trajectory optimization in a training phase to learn a neural network policy, showcasing its effectiveness across a range of cyclic behaviors and various body types.

The authors build upon existing techniques in computer graphics, specifically leveraging the ability of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals, as established by previous work. They also employ neural networks to approximate the step-by-step actions required of a policy to replicate trajectories similar to those found by trajectory optimization, a concept developed in prior research.

To further establish the significance of this work, the authors should provide a more detailed comparison to recent approaches, such as the line of work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. They should clarify how their approach improves upon existing methods, whether in terms of learning speed, support for diverse body shapes, computational efficiency at runtime, or robustness to environmental variabilities.

Regarding specific aspects of the paper, the authors should provide more context for the statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." It would be beneficial to discuss the implications of this finding and how it relates to their proposed strategy.

The notation around lines 226-240 could be improved for clarity, as the use of bars and squiggles may cause visual confusion. The authors should consider rephrasing or reorganizing this section to enhance readability.

Furthermore, the authors should elaborate on the concept of "softer feedback gains" and how their approach achieves this. Specifically, they should define what is meant by "softer" in this context, how it compares to other methods, and why it is beneficial to modify the initial state as part of their procedure.

Overall, the paper presents sound work, but the contribution relative to other recent related work could be more clearly articulated.
