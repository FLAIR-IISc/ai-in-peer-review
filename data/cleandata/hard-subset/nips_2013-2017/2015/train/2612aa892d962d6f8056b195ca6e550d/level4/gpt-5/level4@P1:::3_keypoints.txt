- Paper proposes interleaved training of CIO trajectory optimization and a recurrent NN policy, with CIO regularized to stay near the NN and the NN trained on CIO outputs with noise.  
- Two noise mechanisms: input perturbations with first-order feedback corrections and additive noise in NN hidden activations.  
- Presents a distributed asynchronous training pipeline and a single NN controlling diverse 3D morphologies (swimming, flying, legged) without hand-crafted controllers.  
- Claims improvements over prior coupled optimization/approximation work via joint training, noise, feedback gains, and proximal regularization.

- Strength: Ambitious goal of a single, general controller architecture for varied morphologies and tasks.  
- Strength: Clear technical idea of alternating trajectory optimization and NN regression with principled noise and feedback.  
- Strength: Solid engineering and scalable distributed/GPU training that runs in a few hours.  
- Strength: Compelling emergent behaviors (e.g., cyclic gait) without hand-designed schedules.

- Weakness: Limited comparative evaluation; lacks systematic quantitative baselines against RL/policy search methods.  
- Weakness: Small training dataset (100–200 trials) may understate what static training can achieve and contributes to overfitting without noise.  
- Weakness: Key methodological details (particularly CIO and Eq. 6) are under-specified; reproducibility concerns and request for code release.  
- Weakness: No timing or scaling analysis for real-time performance; per-step solve times are missing.

- Quality: Method appears technically sound and ablations support joint training and noise, but empirical claims need stronger datasets, quantitative metrics, and broader baselines.  
- Clarity: Writing generally clear but Section 3.2 and several notational issues hinder readability; define variables and retain time indices.  
- Originality: Builds on known components but joint, noise-aware training with CIO-derived feedback gains and broad applicability is a meaningful contribution.  
- Significance: Potentially significant for animation/robotics, but broader comparisons and runtime data are needed to confirm practical impact.

- Specific question: Is target specification only position (x,y,z) without orientation, and can orientation targets be added?  
- Specific question: Clarify terminology—input perturbation acts like data augmentation, not denoising autoencoders.  
- Specific question: How are initial trajectories in Alg.1 generated—random θ or without penalty?  
- Specific question: Justify shared proximal rate η for both subproblems in Alg.1.  
- Specific question: Report per-step solve times and scaling with model complexity (ms per step on typical hardware).

- Minor issues: wording and typographical fixes suggested (locomotion, unite/unify, missing articles).  
- Minor issues: Define LQG before use and fix several notational slips (misplaced star, missing s̄ in C̃, f vs fdes).  
- Minor issues: Clarify soft enforcement of dynamics and implications for root forces in simulation.  
- Minor issues: Split “no noise” ablation into “no data augmentation” vs “no hidden noise”; check Table 1a layer count.  
- Minor issues: Reconcile asynchronous training architecture with strict alternation in Alg.1 and add MPC reference.

- Pros for acceptance: Practical, well-engineered method with strong qualitative results across diverse 3D systems; clear contributions and scalable training.  
- Cons against acceptance: Weak comparative evaluation, small datasets, clarity/notation issues (esp. Section 3.2), and missing runtime analysis.

- Overall: Compelling application with promising results; strengthen by expanding comparisons, clarifying Section 3.2/CIO details, reporting runtime, and addressing editorial issues.
