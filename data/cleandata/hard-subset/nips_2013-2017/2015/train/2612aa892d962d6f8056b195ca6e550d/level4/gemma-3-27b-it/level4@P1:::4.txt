This paper proposes a method for combining trajectory optimization with policy learning to enable interactive control of complex characters. The central idea is to jointly learn trajectories and a control policy, leveraging an iterative optimization process where these two components are refined in alternating subproblems. This approach allows for the reuse of established methods while achieving effective results in practice. While I am not deeply familiar with this specific domain, the work demonstrates considerable technical accomplishment, though it doesn't present novel theoretical insights.

The authors combine a remarkable level of technical sophistication – from the underlying concepts to their implementation – with equally impressive results. I found the claimed advantages of both injecting sensory noise during training and jointly learning trajectories with the policy to be clearly demonstrated. The experiments showcase successful control of a diverse range of character morphologies, highlighting the method’s versatility.

I also found the paper to be clearly written, with an appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section addresses a distinct aspect of the work, and the cross-references between sections are particularly helpful for understanding the overall approach.

However, the motivation for the additional optimization step described in Section 7 could be clarified. After the preceding sections, I anticipated the policy to function without requiring further optimization during execution. Is this additional step necessary due to a residual trajectory cost or policy regression error that would accumulate over time when fed into the physical simulator? I understand the forward evolution of the dynamics incorporates physical constraints, preventing implausible behaviors, but I may be missing a key detail. I appreciate that re-optimization at each timestep allows for the use of larger timesteps.

Line 153: “similar to recent approaches…” – could you provide one or two relevant references here? It initially seemed counterintuitive to me that limiting updates in parameter space would generally be beneficial. However, I came to understand its usefulness in your specific scenario of block-alternating between optimization subproblems.

Typo: Line 89: “to unify” – should be “to unify these disparate approaches”.

The paper’s results are impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and its implementation seem sensible. However, this research area is somewhat outside my core expertise, making it difficult for me to confidently assess its novelty and significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Technical Execution:** The paper demonstrates a high level of technical competence in combining trajectory optimization and neural network learning.
*   **Effective Results:** The method successfully controls a diverse range of character morphologies in complex 3D environments.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts and implementation details.
*   **Addresses a Challenging Problem:** Interactive control of complex characters is a difficult problem with applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
*   **Demonstrated Advantages:** The benefits of noise injection and joint optimization are clearly shown through experimentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The paper primarily combines existing techniques rather than presenting fundamentally new theoretical contributions.
*   **Domain Expertise Required:** Evaluating the true significance of the work requires deep knowledge of the character control domain, which I lack.
*   **Motivation for Real-time Optimization:** The need for the optimization step during execution could be more thoroughly explained.
