This paper introduces a method for training neural network-based feedback controllers for a variety of complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning in an alternating optimization scheme. This framework uses a powerful trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate optimal state-action trajectories, which then serve as training data for a recurrent neural network policy. Key contributions that enable the success on challenging 3D problems include: a principled method for injecting noise into sensory inputs and network activations to improve robustness; a novel technique to extract optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer to provide richer training signals; and a distributed, asynchronous architecture to make the computationally intensive training process feasible. This work builds upon recent efforts to distill trajectory optimizers into neural network policies [9, 11], but significantly advances the state of the art by demonstrating stable, realistic, and interactive control for a diverse range of 3D characters—including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds—using a single, unified algorithm.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically excellent. The proposed framework is well-conceived, and the individual components are carefully designed and justified. The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method is particularly clever and generally applicable. The claims are strongly supported by an impressive suite of experiments and a crucial ablation study that clearly demonstrates the necessity of both the joint optimization and noise injection components for successful test-time performance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of presenting the high-level optimization algorithm first, before delving into the details of each sub-problem (trajectory optimization and network regression). The problem formulation is precise, and the figures and tables effectively supplement the text.
*   **Originality & Significance:** While the high-level concept of learning policies from optimizers is not entirely new, this paper makes several novel contributions that collectively produce a significant breakthrough. The successful application of a single, general-purpose method to generate emergent, natural-looking gaits (e.g., walking, trotting, flying, swimming) for such a wide array of morphologies is a landmark achievement. This work dramatically pushes the boundary of what is possible in automated controller synthesis for animation and robotics, providing a powerful alternative to hand-engineered state machines or direct reinforcement learning approaches that often yield less stable or less plausible behaviors. The results are not only technically impressive but also visually compelling, and the method is likely to have a major impact on future research and applications.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The comparison with alternative approaches, particularly modern deep reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g., TRPO [14]), is largely qualitative. The authors state their impression that direct methods yield more "chaotic" behaviors. While the video evidence supports this for their method's realistic results, a more direct quantitative comparison on a common benchmark task would provide a stronger empirical grounding for this claim.
*   The method's success is heavily reliant on the availability of a powerful, albeit offline, trajectory optimizer. A brief discussion of the limitations this might impose—for instance, in domains where formulating the dynamics or cost for an optimizer like CIO is intractable—would add valuable context.
*   The policy execution step requires solving a small optimization problem at each timestep to evolve the state. While the authors state this can be done at real-time rates, it is computationally more demanding than a simple forward pass. Providing some detail on the per-frame execution time would be helpful for practitioners considering this method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a significant and impressive advance in generating robust, interactive controllers for a diverse set of complex 3D characters.
2.  Introduces novel technical contributions, particularly the method for integrating feedback gains and noise, which are key to the method's success.
3.  The quality of the experimental results, as evidenced in the paper and supplementary video, is exceptionally high and convincingly demonstrates the power and generality of the approach.
4.  The paper is a model of clarity and thoroughness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  (Minor) The empirical comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning methods is lacking, making it harder to precisely situate the performance trade-offs of the approach.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that presents a complete and powerful solution to a very challenging problem. It is technically deep, rigorously evaluated, and produces beautiful results. It represents a clear and significant contribution to the field.
