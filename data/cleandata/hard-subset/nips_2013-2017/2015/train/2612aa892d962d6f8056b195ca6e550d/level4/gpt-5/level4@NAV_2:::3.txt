This paper presents a framework for interactive, real-time control of 3D articulated characters in which a neural controller is trained offline to emulate outputs from Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Crucially, CIO and the neural policy are trained in an alternating scheme so each informs the other’s objective, and the method injects noise both via input perturbations (with action targets adjusted by local feedback gains) and through hidden-layer noise. Empirically, the learned policies produce stable, lifelike behaviors across a variety of morphologies and tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion).

The approach is compelling given the difficulty of real-time control for diverse bodies. Although the high-level recipe—fit a network to CIO—might seem simple, the two main additions (joint/alternating optimization and carefully structured noise) are substantive contributions that demonstrably improve robustness and generalization in the reported studies.

That said, the empirical support would be stronger with broader comparisons and more extensive datasets. The training corpus (roughly 100–200 trials with 5 branches each) is modest, so the overfitting observed in the “no noise” variant is not surprising. The “no joint” baseline might also benefit from greater diversity in trajectories, even if the impact is less obvious.

Overall, comparative evaluation is the weakest part (as the authors note in the introduction: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The paper includes only a brief MPC comparison and provides no citation for the MPC setup.

Most of the mathematical development is clear and well-motivated, but reproducing the work would still require consulting prior references (especially for CIO; e.g., several symbols in Eq. 6 are not defined in the text). Public code would be very valuable to the community. I also found Section 3.2 (feedback gains for noisy inputs) hard to parse: notation becomes dense and the time index t disappears; it’s not obvious where time is still implicit and what exactly is being perturbed.

It would be helpful to include runtime measurements for policy execution, since the online step involves a constrained optimization at each frame and could be a bottleneck. Practical deployment (e.g., games) often has limited CPU per character, and understanding scaling with model complexity would be important.

Additional minor remarks:
- “biped location” → locomotion?
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches” → unite/unify?
- Is the target limited to x, y, z position, with no constraint on facing direction at the goal? If so, would adding a desired orientation be straightforward? This seems practically important.
- Calling the input-perturbation procedure “noise injection” may be misleading: because targets are adjusted (via a + asε), this is more akin to data augmentation than denoising in the autoencoder sense.
- Do you actually use the initial random weights θ to compute the first X’s in Algorithm 1, or do you first solve for X without the regularizer?
- Why use the same proximal weight η for both the trajectory and network subproblems? Any justification or sensitivity analysis?
- “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have” → missing “the”.
- The acronym LQG is used one line before being defined.
- “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass” → missing “a”.
- l. 231: the star in argmin appears misplaced, and is s̄ missing from C̃?
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory” → missing an article (“the”).
- “sX and aX are subsets of φX”: why does this necessarily hold?
- “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)” → here only θ is optimized, so it is the objective in Algorithm 1.
- Section 6’s asynchronous scheme differs materially from the simple alternation in Algorithm 1; it would help to foreshadow this earlier.
- l. 315: f should likely be fdes.
- “the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation” — please clarify what this implies in practice.
- Consider separating the “no noise” ablation into “no data augmentation” vs. “no hidden activation noise.”
- An interesting question: can α be annealed to 0 (for CIO only)? Is the benefit primarily a curriculum effect early on, or is it consistently helpful to keep trajectories close to what the network can represent?
- Table 1a’s caption says 4 layers, but it appears to be 3.

In sum, this is an attractive application of neural control for physically based characters, with promising qualitative results. Its practical impact would be clearer with stronger baselines, fuller ablations, and execution-time benchmarks.
