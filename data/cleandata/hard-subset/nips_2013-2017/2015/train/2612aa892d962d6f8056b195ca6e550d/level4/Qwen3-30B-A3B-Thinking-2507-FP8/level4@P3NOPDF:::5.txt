The core idea is promising, and the empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines on [mention specific datasets/tasks], suggesting potential value for the community. However, the paper requires significant restructuring and clarification to be suitable for publication at NeurIPS.

The most critical issue is the lack of explicit acknowledgment that the method fundamentally relies on *full model knowledge* (i.e., the complete factorization structure and parameters of the target model), as formalized in Equation (6). This prerequisite must be stated clearly and prominently in the introduction or abstract, not buried in later sections. Without this upfront clarification, readers may misinterpret the method's applicability, particularly to scenarios where the full model structure is unknown or difficult to obtain (e.g., black-box neural networks). The current presentation risks misleading readers about the method's scope and limitations.

Furthermore, the paper suffers from a severe structural imbalance between Sections 4 and 6. Section 4, which details the core algorithmic contribution, is overly brief and lacks sufficient technical depth. Crucially, it fails to connect to the recurrent units explicitly mentioned in the abstract (lines 15-17), creating a disconnect between the high-level claim and the presented methodology. The abstract states the method leverages "recurrent units," yet Section 4 describes the factorization without referencing this key architectural element, leaving readers uncertain about the role of recurrence. Section 6, conversely, provides an extensive, detailed analysis of the experimental results. This disproportionate development obscures the paper's core technical contribution and makes the argument difficult to follow. The authors should substantially expand Section 4 to clarify the algorithm, explicitly address the recurrent units, and reduce the excessive detail in Section 6 to achieve better balance.

A minor but important point of confusion arises at line 405: "we employ a stochastic neural network." The phrasing is misleading. The training data may involve stochasticity (e.g., via sampling), but the neural network architecture itself is deterministic. The paper should clarify that the *training process* utilizes stochastic techniques (e.g., stochastic gradient descent, data augmentation), while the *inference network* is deterministic. This distinction is critical for reproducibility and understanding the method's theoretical properties.

Other minor suggestions:
*   Ensure all claims in the abstract (e.g., "recurrent units") are immediately supported in the relevant technical sections.
*   Clarify the relationship between the proposed factorization and the specific model class (e.g., Is it only applicable to certain types of recurrent networks?).
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity trade-offs in the main text, not just in the appendix.

In summary, while the empirical results are strong and the core idea holds promise, the paper's current presentation is flawed by a lack of upfront clarity on a fundamental assumption (full model knowledge), a critical structural imbalance between key sections, and an ambiguous description of the method's stochasticity. Addressing these points—particularly the explicit statement of the model knowledge requirement and the restructuring to balance Sections 4 and 6—is essential for the paper to meet NeurIPS standards. The authors have a strong foundation; the necessary revisions are primarily about clarity, precision, and logical flow. With these changes, the paper could be a valuable contribution. As currently written, it is not ready for acceptance.
