This paper presents a method for interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization and policy learning. The core idea is to jointly learn trajectories and a control policy, leveraging the practical benefit of decomposing this joint optimization into alternating subproblems. This decoupling allows the authors to utilize existing, well-established methods and, as demonstrated, yields strong performance. While I found the paper technically sound, my limited familiarity with this specific domain prevents me from fully assessing its potential impact, though it appears to represent a significant technical achievement without introducing novel theoretical results.

The authors demonstrate impressive technical skill throughout, from the conceptual framework to the implementation details, and support their approach with compelling results. The advantages of both incorporating sensory noise during training and jointly learning trajectories with the policy are clearly substantiated. 

The paper is well-written and provides an appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the problem and its implementation. The structure is logical, with each section focusing on a distinct aspect of the work, and helpful cross-references are included throughout. 

The motivation for the additional optimization step described in Section 7 could be clarified. Having followed the preceding sections, I anticipated the policy would operate without requiring further optimization. Is this extra step necessary due to a residual trajectory cost or policy regression error that accumulates when the policy’s actions are applied in the physical simulator? While I expect the dynamics simulation to inherently enforce physical constraints, preventing implausible behavior, I may be overlooking a key consideration. I do acknowledge, however, that re-optimizing at each timestep enables the use of larger timesteps. 

On line 153, the statement “similar to recent approaches…” would benefit from one or two relevant citations. The intuition behind limiting updates in parameter space is not immediately obvious, though I ultimately understood its utility within the context of the block-alternating subproblems.

Typo: Line 89: “to unify”. The paper’s results are impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation appear sensible. However, given this research area is somewhat outside my expertise, I am hesitant to confidently evaluate the novelty and significance of this work.
