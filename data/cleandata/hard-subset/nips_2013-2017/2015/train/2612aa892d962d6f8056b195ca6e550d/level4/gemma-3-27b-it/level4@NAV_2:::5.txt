This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for a diverse range of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and walking characters with varying morphologies. The core idea is to leverage trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) to generate training data for a neural network, effectively distilling the knowledge of the optimizer into a real-time control policy. This approach bypasses the need for motion capture data or task-specific feature engineering, offering a potentially universal synthesis method for complex behaviors. This builds upon a growing body of work aiming to automate controller design, a traditionally manual and time-consuming process, and connects to the broader goals of Reinforcement Learning and stochastic optimal control, but notably extends previous work to higher-dimensional, three-dimensional spaces. The method incorporates several key techniques: interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, training for robustness to task changes, and utilizing the optimizer to obtain feedback gains.

The strengths of this paper lie in its compelling demonstration of a unified control approach applicable to a wide array of complex characters and tasks. The ability to train a single network architecture to control such diverse systems is a significant achievement, and the accompanying video effectively showcases the realistic and stable behaviors achieved. The integration of trajectory optimization with neural network training appears to be a crucial element, enabling the network to learn from a strong, albeit offline, optimization process. The noise injection and feedback gain computation techniques are well-motivated and contribute to the robustness and generalization capabilities of the learned policies. The detailed description of the distributed training architecture provides valuable insights into the computational resources required for this type of research. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations and compare their approach to alternative methods like Model Predictive Control (MPC), providing a balanced assessment of their contribution.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the abstract mentions recurrent units, Section 4 omits any discussion of their role or contribution to the network's performance. This omission hinders understanding of how the recurrent connections beyond the system state facilitate learning. The description of the sensory state (st) is concise but lacks justification; a more thorough explanation of why these specific features were chosen would be beneficial. Although the paper emphasizes the use of full model knowledge (Eq. 6), this is only briefly mentioned and its implications for practical applications are not fully explored. Furthermore, the claim of a “stochastic” neural network in line 405 feels misleading, as the network itself appears deterministic given the input; the stochasticity lies primarily in the training data generation. The disproportionate length of sections 4 and 6 relative to the core method is also a concern, with the neural network details appearing somewhat cursory. A more in-depth analysis of the network architecture and training procedure would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a novel and effective approach to learning controllers for complex dynamical systems.
*   Achieves impressive results on a diverse set of tasks and character morphologies.
*   Offers a unified control framework, reducing the need for manual design and task-specific engineering.
*   Provides a detailed description of the training process and computational requirements.
*   Addresses a challenging problem with significant potential impact in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Omission of detail regarding the function of recurrent units.
*   Limited discussion on the implications of requiring full model knowledge.
*   Potentially misleading characterization of the neural network as “stochastic”.
*   Uneven emphasis and detail across different sections of the paper.
*   Lack of a thorough ablation study assessing the individual contributions of each component of the proposed method (beyond the results in Figure 2).



I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should: 1) expand the discussion of the recurrent units and their role in the network; 2) provide further justification for the chosen sensory state representation; and 3) clarify the meaning of "stochastic" in the context of the neural network. Addressing these points will enhance the clarity and impact of this promising work.
