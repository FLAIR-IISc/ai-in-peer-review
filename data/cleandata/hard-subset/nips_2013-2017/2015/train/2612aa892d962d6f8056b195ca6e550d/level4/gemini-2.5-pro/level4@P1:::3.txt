This paper proposes an algorithm for real-time, interactive control of 3D characters. The core idea is to train a recurrent neural network (NN) policy to mimic the outputs of an offline trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key aspect of the method is that the CIO and NN training are not performed independently but are instead interleaved in an alternating optimization scheme, where the objective of each component depends on the output of the other. The method also relies on noise injection—both through data augmentation with perturbed sensory inputs and by adding noise to the NN's hidden layers during training—to improve robustness and generalization. The experiments demonstrate that the resulting neural network can produce realistic and stable control policies for a diverse set of character models, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers.

**Strengths**

*   The paper tackles the challenging problem of synthesizing real-time controllers for arbitrary, physically simulated characters, which is a significant goal in animation, robotics, and biomechanics.
*   The approach of jointly optimizing trajectories and the neural network policy is a compelling contribution. The ablation study in Figure 2 effectively demonstrates that this joint training is critical for achieving good performance, yielding results that are orders of magnitude better than training on a static dataset.
*   The principled use of noise injection and data augmentation is another significant contribution. The experiments show that this is crucial for preventing overfitting and learning a robust policy that does not diverge during execution.
*   The ability of a single, general-purpose algorithm and network architecture to generate qualitatively different and realistic gaits (e.g., walking, trotting, swimming, flying) for various morphologies is highly impressive and speaks to the method's power and generality.

**Weaknesses**

*   The primary weakness of the paper is its limited comparative evaluation. The introduction acknowledges that a systematic comparison to other methods has not been done. The paper provides only a brief qualitative comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC), for which no reference is provided. A more rigorous comparison against other policy learning or real-time control methods would be necessary to properly situate the work and validate its significance.
*   The experimental results, while impressive, would be more convincing if a larger training dataset were used. Given the relatively small dataset size ("100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments"), it is not surprising that the "no noise" variant overfits. It is also possible that the "no joint" variant would perform better if trained on a larger and more diverse set of trajectories, which would make the benefits of joint optimization less pronounced.
*   Certain key parts of the method are not explained with sufficient clarity to allow for replication. While the mathematical exposition is generally well-motivated, some components are described superficially. For instance, the Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) step, particularly the constraints in Equation 6, relies on undefined notation. Section 3.2, which details the computation of optimal feedback gains for noisy inputs, is particularly difficult to follow; the notation is confusing, and the omission of the time index 't' obscures the meaning of the equations. Releasing the source code would be a significant service to the community and would greatly aid reproducibility.
*   The paper lacks crucial performance metrics for the real-time control phase. To assess the method's practicality for interactive applications like video games—where CPU budgets for character animation are tight—it is essential to report the computational cost of a single policy execution step (Section 7) and how this cost scales with model complexity.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   Minor typos: "biped location" should likely be "biped locomotion" (p. 2), and "unity" should be "unify" (p. 2).
*   Is the locomotion target specified only by position (x, y, z), or does it include a target orientation? The ability to specify a target orientation seems crucial for many practical applications.
*   The term "noise injection" for the process described in Section 2.1 might be misleading. Since the target action is re-computed based on the perturbed input, this process is more akin to "data augmentation" than the process used in denoising autoencoders, where the network must recover a clean signal. The authors may wish to clarify this distinction.
*   Regarding Algorithm 1, is the initial (random) network `θ` used in the very first optimization of the trajectories `X`? Or is Step 2 first performed without the regularization term `R`?
*   What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter `η` for the proximal regularization terms in both the trajectory optimization and the neural network regression steps?
*   There appears to be a discrepancy between Algorithm 1, which describes a sequential, alternating optimization, and the implementation described in Section 6, which is asynchronous and distributed. This should be clarified earlier in the paper.
*   The sentence, "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation," is unclear. Please clarify what this means and its implications.
*   The "no noise" ablation study combines two different factors (sensory input noise and hidden unit noise). It would be more informative to separate these into two distinct experiments to understand their individual contributions.
*   In Section 3.2, there are several apparent typos in the equations for `X̃(s̄)` and `X̃_s̄`. For instance, `C̃` seems to be missing `s̄` as an argument, and the `argmin*` seems misplaced.
*   The caption for Table 1a mentions "4 layers," but the table seems to describe networks with 1, 2, and 3 hidden layers.
*   A potentially interesting question for future work: Can the weight `α` on the network-matching term be annealed to zero during training? This would test whether the joint optimization's main benefit is as a form of curriculum learning (making the initial task easier) or if it is always necessary to regularize the CIO trajectories towards what the network can represent.

In summary, this paper presents a visually impressive application of neural networks to character control. The core ideas of joint optimization and stochastic training are well-motivated and shown to be effective. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparative evaluation, greater clarity in the description of the method, and the inclusion of performance metrics to establish its practical applicability.
