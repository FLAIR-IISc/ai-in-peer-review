This paper proposes a real-time controller for 3D character animation in which a neural network (NN) is trained to reproduce the outputs of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key contribution is the tight coupling between CIO and NN learning: they are optimized in alternation, with each influencing the other’s objective. Another important ingredient is robustness via noise, both through data augmentation (perturbing inputs and recomputing targets) and by injecting additive noise into NN hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that the learned controllers produce stable, realistic motions across a range of character models.

Strengths
- The overall formulation is compelling and addresses a hard and important problem: real-time control for varied characters.
- The alternating optimization between CIO and the NN is a notable contribution beyond “learn a NN to mimic CIO,” and appears central to generalization.
- The use of both data augmentation and hidden-layer noise is well-motivated and empirically valuable.
- The mathematical development is mostly clear, and the approach is practical in spirit.

Weaknesses and recommendations
- Empirical evaluation is limited. The training set appears small (100–200 trials with 5 branched segments), which likely explains why the “no noise” variant overfits; similarly, the “no joint” variant might benefit from more diverse trajectories. Stronger evidence would include:
  - Larger, more diverse training corpora and tasks.
  - Clear ablations separating “no data augmentation” from “no hidden noise” to quantify their individual effects.
  - Additional baselines, particularly modern trajectory-optimization and direct-policy methods, beyond the brief MPC comparison (which lacks a citation).
  - Quantitative metrics for stability/robustness and success rates across seeds and perturbations, not only qualitative videos.
- Clarity and reproducibility could be improved. Some derivations and notations (especially around CIO) are only sketched. In particular, Section 3.2 (“generating optimal actions for noisy inputs”) is hard to follow:
  - Notation becomes ambiguous; the time index t seems dropped—please clarify which quantities remain time-indexed.
  - Equation (6) contains undefined symbols and needs explicit definitions and dimensions.
  - A more concrete procedural description (pseudo-code) of the noisy-target generation and how it interfaces with CIO would help reproduction.
  - Providing code would significantly aid the community and address many ambiguities.
- Real-time performance is not characterized. Since deployment constraints are strict (e.g., games with multiple characters and limited CPU/GPU), please report:
  - Per-frame runtime during control (with and without noise at test time).
  - Scaling with character complexity (DoFs, contact points).
  - Hardware details and any batching/approximation used.
- The paper’s structure understates the difference between the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1 and the asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6. It would help to:
  - Introduce the asynchronous variant earlier and explain when/why it is preferable.
  - Discuss convergence behavior and any stability safeguards for both variants.

Requests for clarification
- Targets and orientation: Is the target limited to x,y,z position with no facing-angle constraint? If so, how straightforward would it be to add a desired orientation (e.g., yaw) to the objective and training targets?
- Initialization in Algorithm 1: Do you truly use the randomly initialized NN parameters θ to compute the first X values, or do you start with CIO solutions computed without the NN-regularization term?
- Hyperparameters: Why is the same η used for both alternating steps in Algorithm 1? Did you explore separate schedules?
- Dynamics constraints: “Enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation” needs clarification. Does this mean residual forces/torques are applied to the root to satisfy contacts? How large are these residuals, and do they compromise physical plausibility?
- Set relationships: Why are sX and aX subsets of φX guaranteed? Please state the assumptions that ensure this.
- Objective references: “A single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)” seems to refer to the θ-only objective from Algorithm 1 rather than (4); please reconcile.

Minor comments and typos
- “biped location” → “biped locomotion.”
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches” → “unite” or “unify.”
- Acronym LQG is used before being defined. Also “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass” → “an LQG pass.”
- “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have” → “the asynchronous and stochastic setting we have.”
- l. 231: the star in the argmin is misplaced; also check whether s̄ is missing from C̃.
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory” → “the minimizer of a quadratic approximation around the optimal trajectory.”
- Section 3.2: clarify dropped time indices and fully define all symbols; make explicit whether t is still implied for any quantities.
- Table 1a: caption says 4 layers; appears to be 3.
- Algorithm 1: clarify whether α is scheduled; an interesting question is whether α can be annealed to zero for CIO only, and whether performance gains arise from curriculum effects or persistent coupling to the NN’s representational limits.
- The “no noise” condition should be split into (i) no data augmentation and (ii) no hidden-layer noise to isolate their contributions.
- Add a citation for the MPC baseline and expand the comparison if possible.
- Eq. (6) and nearby text: provide missing notation and references to enable replication.
- l. 315: f likely should be f^{des}.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely idea with promising qualitative results. The joint CIO–NN training and robustness strategies are novel and well-motivated, and the application scope is exciting. However, the empirical evaluation and clarity fall short of what is needed to firmly establish the method’s advantages and practical readiness. With expanded baselines and datasets, runtime characterization, clearer notation (especially in Section 3.2), and code release, this work would make a compelling contribution. As it stands, it is an impressive proof of concept whose practical benefits and generality remain to be fully demonstrated.
