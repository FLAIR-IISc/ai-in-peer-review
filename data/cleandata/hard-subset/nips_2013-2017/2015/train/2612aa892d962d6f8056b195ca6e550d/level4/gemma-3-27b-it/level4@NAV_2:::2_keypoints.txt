- Presents a method to train recurrent neural networks as feedback controllers for diverse dynamical characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without motion capture or task-specific features.  
- Leverages trajectory optimization to generate training data and learns state-to-action mappings that implicitly capture dynamics.  
- Key contribution: a single network architecture and training procedure that synthesizes stable 3D locomotion controllers across multiple morphologies.  
- Effectively combines established trajectory optimization and neural network regression techniques to scale to complex 3D environments.  
- Needs clearer positioning relative to recent reinforcement learning and imitation learning work (e.g., Abbeel, Levine).  
- Should explicitly state advantages over related methods (training speed, generalization, runtime efficiency, robustness).  
- Claim about “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” requires quantitative details (magnitudes, condition numbers) and discussion of performance implications.  
- Recommend restructuring to introduce limitations of traditional methods (e.g., LQG) after presenting the proposed approach for clearer motivation.  
- Notation near lines 226–240 is confusing (mixed bars and squiggles); clarify variable definitions and differences (e.g., s(X) = \bar s vs. s(X) – \bar s).  
- Statement that the approach “produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” is unclear; define “soft” gains and explain benefits and why modifying the initial state helps.  
- Overall contribution is solid, but the paper should more explicitly articulate novelty and advantages relative to existing methods.  
- Argument for acceptance: demonstrates scalability to challenging 3D locomotion tasks not addressed by prior work.  
- Argument for acceptance: single architecture and training procedure generalize across diverse body morphologies.  
- Argument for acceptance: controllers enable interactive, real-time control in physics simulators, useful for robotics, animation, biomechanics.  
- Argument for acceptance: methodology (trajectory optimization + neural regression) is well-motivated and implemented.  
- Argument against acceptance: perceived limited novelty due to heavy reliance on existing techniques and insufficient comparison to RL/imitation learning.  
- Argument against acceptance: lacks rigorous quantitative comparisons to state-of-the-art methods to substantiate performance claims.  
- Argument against acceptance: clarity issues (notation, explanations of feedback gains) hinder understanding and should be addressed.
