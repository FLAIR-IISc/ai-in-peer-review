- Proposes joint training of a neural network to reproduce CIO outputs, alternating optimization between CIO and the NN using each other's outputs.  
- Key innovation: strategic noise injection via data augmentation (perturbed trajectories) and additive hidden-layer noise, which improves generalization.  
- Experiments claim realistic, stable control across diverse character models, addressing real-time 3D character animation.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak: small training set (100–200 trials × 5 segments) likely causes overfitting in the "no noise" variant.  
- The "no joint" variant may be underpowered due to limited and non-diverse training data.  
- Comparative evaluation is insufficient: only a brief, uncited MPC comparison; broader benchmarks against established baselines are required.  
- Mathematical exposition has gaps that hinder reproducibility (e.g., Eq.6/CIO step lacks notation, Section 3.2 drops time index t).  
- Authors should clarify ambiguous notation (when t is implied) and provide code to aid replication.  
- Missing computational-efficiency analysis: timing for control computations and scaling with model complexity is needed for real-time claims.  
- Several minor but important corrections/clarifications required (word choices, target orientation constraints, mislabeling of "noise injection" vs data augmentation).  
- Algorithm 1 needs clarification: how initial θ is used to compute X, justification for using hyperparameter η in both steps, and consistency with the asynchronous scheme in Section 6.  
- Multiple typographical/math mistakes to fix (f^{des}, misplaced argmin star, missing \bar{s} in \tilde{C}, grammar around “minimizer …”).  
- Need justification that sX and aX are subsets of φX, and correct reference to Algorithm 1’s actual objective.  
- Suggested experiments: test reducing CIO hyperparameter α to zero to probe reliance on curriculum/trajectory fidelity.  
- Distinguish “no noise” variants clearly: separate effects of no data augmentation vs no hidden-layer noise.  
- Overall assessment: promising, technically interesting approach, but requires stronger empirical validation, clearer math/notation, and efficiency metrics to establish practical impact.
