In this submission, the authors introduce an algorithm for real-time control of 3D character models, where a neural network (NN) is trained offline to reproduce the outputs of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and then used to generate animations. Crucially, CIO and NN training are interdependent: they alternate, with each stage incorporating the other’s output into its objective. Another central component is the deliberate use of noise, both via data augmentation with small input perturbations and through additive noise injected into the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments indicate that the resulting network can produce realistic, stable control policies across a variety of very different character models.

This is a compelling approach, given that real-time control of arbitrary models is a difficult problem. Although the method might initially seem simple (“train a NN to predict CIO”), the additional mechanisms that make it work—alternating/joint training and noise injection—constitute substantial contributions and are shown empirically to significantly improve generalization.

That said, the experimental evidence would be stronger with a larger dataset. Since the base training trajectories were generated from only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” it is not surprising that the “no noise” variant overfits. The “no joint” variant might also gain from a broader set of training trajectories—even if this is less immediately evident.

Overall, the comparative evaluation is the paper’s weakest aspect (as acknowledged in the introduction: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The only comparison to an alternative method is a brief one with MPC at the end, and there is no citation for that approach.

For the most part, the mathematics are clearly presented and motivated, but a reader aiming to reproduce the results will need to consult additional references, as some elements are treated only superficially (especially the CIO step—see Eq. 6, where the notation is not defined). Sharing code would be greatly appreciated by the community. One section I could not fully follow is 3.2 on generating optimal actions for noisy inputs: the notation becomes quite confusing there, and it is unclear whether the time index t, which is dropped, remains implicit for some quantities.

It would also be useful to report timing for the computations during real-time control, including how these costs scale with the complexity of the 3D model. In a video game setting, there is limited CPU budget available to animate a single character.

Additional minor remarks:
- “biped location”: did you mean locomotion?
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches”: did you mean unite/unify?
- Am I correct that the target concerns only x, y, z coordinates, with no constraint on the character’s facing direction at the target? If so, would adding a desired heading be straightforward? This seems important in practice.
- I would avoid calling the step that regenerates targets for perturbed inputs “noise injection.” Since targets are recomputed to match modified inputs, this is standard data augmentation, not the same concept as in denoising autoencoders.
- Are you truly using the initial (random) network weights θ in the very first iteration to compute the initial X’s in Alg. 1, or do you begin with X’s computed without the regularization term?
- What is the rationale for using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Alg. 1?
- “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”: missing “the”?
- The acronym LQG appears one line before it is defined.
- “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”: missing “a”?
- l. 231: the star in the argmin is misplaced, and is \bar{s} missing in \tilde{C}?
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: missing a word?
- “sX and aX are subsets of φX”: why is that guaranteed?
- “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: this seems to refer to the objective in Alg. 1, since only θ is being optimized.
- The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs substantially from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1; this should be mentioned earlier.
- l. 315, should f be f^{des}?
- “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.”: I did not understand this sentence.
- The “no noise” ablation could be split into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden-layer noise injection.”
- It would be interesting to test whether α can be annealed to zero during optimization (for CIO only). Is the benefit mostly a “curriculum learning” effect (easier at the start), or is it always helpful to keep CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent?
- The caption of Table 1a says 4 layers, but I believe it is 3.

A very polished and promising application of neural networks to character control, whose practical benefits and broader applicability still need to be established.
