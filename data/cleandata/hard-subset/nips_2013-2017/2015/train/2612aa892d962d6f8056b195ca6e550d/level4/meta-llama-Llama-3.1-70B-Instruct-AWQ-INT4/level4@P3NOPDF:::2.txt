"Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" presents a method for utilizing a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive control of a character in a physics simulator. The approach employs trajectory optimization during the training phase to learn a neural network policy. The primary contribution of this paper is demonstrating that the same architecture can be applied across a range of cyclic behaviors and various body types.

General Comments:

Upon reviewing this paper, it appears to be an application of deep learning to existing techniques in computer graphics. The effectiveness of trajectory optimization in finding realistic control signals has been established by previous work, some of which is cited in the paper. Furthermore, the use of neural networks to approximate the step-by-step actions required of a policy to replicate trajectories similar to those found by trajectory optimization has also been developed in prior research (e.g., [9]). Therefore, the authors must provide a clearer positioning of their work relative to these previously published approaches.

A notable comparison can be drawn to the recent line of work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. It would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly compare their approach to this existing work, highlighting potential advantages such as faster learning, support for a wider variety of body shapes, reduced computational requirements at runtime, or increased robustness to environmental variabilities.

Specific Comments:

The statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" requires further clarification. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed explanation of this phenomenon and its implications.

The discussion of why LQG was not used would be more effectively presented after the introduction of the proposed strategy. This would provide a clearer context for the reader to understand the reasoning behind the authors' decision.

The notation in lines 226-240 is confusing, particularly with the introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by a cost definition in terms of s(X) - \bar s. The similarity in visual appearance of the bars and squiggles may lead to reader confusion. Consider revising the notation to improve clarity.

The statement "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda" is unclear. Specifically, it would be beneficial to explain what is meant by "softer" feedback gains, how they compare to existing approaches, and why this is a desirable outcome.

Additionally, the decision to change the initial state as part of the procedure warrants further explanation. While the work presented appears to be sound, the contribution relative to other recent related work is not clearly established.
