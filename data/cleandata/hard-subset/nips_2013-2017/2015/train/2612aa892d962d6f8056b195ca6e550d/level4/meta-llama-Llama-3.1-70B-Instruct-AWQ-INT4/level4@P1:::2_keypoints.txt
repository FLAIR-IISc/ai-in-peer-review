- Demonstrates a small, fast neural network as a control policy for interactive characters in a physics simulator, trained via trajectory optimization.  
- Main contribution: same network architecture works across diverse cyclic behaviors and multiple body types.  
- Must better position the work relative to prior trajectory-optimization and NN policy approximation literature, which already show similar capabilities.  
- Recommend explicit comparison to Abbeel & Levine line of work: clarify differences in learning speed, support for body shapes, runtime computation, and robustness.  
- The claim about producing “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” is unclear and needs more context or explanation.  
- Suggest moving the LQG discussion to after the proposed method is presented for improved narrative flow.  
- Notation around lines 226–240 (s(X)=bar s and cost using similar bars/squiggles) is confusing and should be revised for clarity.  
- The phrase “softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” is vague—define “softer,” compare to other methods, and justify changing the initial state.  
- Overall contribution relative to recent related work is not made sufficiently clear; add more context and empirical or conceptual comparisons.  
- Strength: shows feasibility of learning interactive control policies with neural networks for a range of characters and behaviors.  
- Weakness: unclear novelty vs prior work, insufficient comparisons, and some unclear notation/explanations.  
- Acceptance: paper is a good candidate but should address the noted weaknesses to strengthen its contribution.  
- For acceptance: novel application of deep learning to graphics and promising generality across bodies/behaviors.  
- Against acceptance: unclear contribution relative to recent work and need for more context and comparisons.
