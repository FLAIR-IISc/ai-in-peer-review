- Proposes a novel real-time control algorithm where a neural network is trained to mimic Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs, with CIO and NN training influencing each other.  
- Introduces noise/data augmentation (minor perturbations and hidden-layer additive noise) to improve generalization.  
- Joint training of CIO and NN and noise injection are significant contributions that enhance generalization.  
- Experiments show the network produces realistic, stable control policies across diverse character models.  
- Experimental validation is limited by a small training set (100–200 base trajectories with 5 branches), which may cause overfitting in the "no noise" variant.  
- The "no joint" variant could benefit from more diverse training trajectories.  
- Comparative evaluation is weak: only a brief, unreferenced comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) is provided.  
- Mathematical explanations are generally clear but some parts (e.g., CIO step Eq. 6) are superficially addressed and need additional references for reproducibility.  
- Releasing code would significantly aid community reproducibility.  
- Section 3.2 (optimal actions for noisy inputs) is unclear due to confusing notation and omission of the time index t.  
- Runtime/time measurements for real-time control are missing and important given potential CPU bottlenecks.  
- Minor wording and terminology issues: clarify "biped location" → locomotion, correct "unity" → "unite/unify," and prefer "data augmentation" over "noise injection."  
- Clarify whether targets are limited to x,y,z coordinates and whether target angle can be added.  
- Clarify use of initial network weights theta in the first iteration of Alg. 1 and justify using the same hyperparameter eta in both steps.  
- Fix grammatical errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper.  
- Clarify the asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 and how it differs from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1.  
- Discuss whether alpha can be decreased to zero during CIO optimization and the implications for learning.  
- Overall: innovative and practically promising application of NN to character control, but requires stronger comparative evaluation, clearer exposition, and additional experimental validation.
