This paper presents a method for interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The core idea is to jointly learn a set of target trajectories and a policy that can track them. A key empirical insight is that this joint optimization can be effectively decoupled into alternating subproblems, which allows for the reuse of existing, well-documented methods and proves effective in practice.

The authors demonstrate an impressive level of technical sophistication, from the high-level concepts down to the implementation details, leading to equally impressive results. While the work appears to be a feat of technical engineering rather than a source of novel theoretical insights, its practical contributions are well-supported. The claimed advantages of using sensory noise during training and of learning trajectories jointly with the policy are clearly demonstrated in the experiments.

The paper is also very well-written and structured. It provides an appropriate level of detail for such a complex system, with each section clearly addressing a different aspect of the problem, aided by helpful cross-references.

My main point of confusion regards the motivation for the additional online optimization step described in Section 7. After the preceding sections, my expectation was that the learned policy could be used directly at runtime without further optimization. Is this online step necessary because a residual trajectory cost or policy regression error would otherwise accumulate if the policy's actions were fed directly into the simulator? While I assume the forward dynamics model would prevent physically implausible states, I may be missing a key detail. I do, however, appreciate the authors' point that this re-optimization allows for the use of larger timesteps.

A few minor points:
*   Line 153: The text mentions the update rule is "similar to recent approaches." It would be helpful to include one or two citations here. The strategy of keeping updates small in parameter space is not immediately intuitive, though I understand its utility in the context of the paper's alternating optimization scheme.
*   Line 89: There appears to be a typo ("to unify").

In summary, the paper presents an impressive system with a sensible optimization scheme and compelling results. While this research area is slightly outside my core expertise, making it difficult for me to confidently assess its novelty and long-term significance, the work appears to be a strong technical contribution.
