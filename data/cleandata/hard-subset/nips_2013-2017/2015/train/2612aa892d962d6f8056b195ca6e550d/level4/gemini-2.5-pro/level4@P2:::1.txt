This paper presents a novel and effective method for training recurrent neural network (RNN) policies to serve as real-time feedback controllers for a diverse range of complex dynamical systems. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimizer, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate high-quality motion data that is then used to train the online RNN controller.

The authors' main contribution is a joint optimization framework that alternates between optimizing a set of trajectories and training the neural network policy. This is a significant departure from a simpler approach of training a network on a static, pre-computed dataset. The coupling between these two optimization stages is achieved through a quadratic penalty term, which encourages the trajectories to remain "learnable" by the policy, and in turn, guides the policy to approximate optimal behaviors. Key technical innovations that enable the success of this framework include: 1) A principled method for injecting noise into sensory inputs and hidden unit activations to improve robustness and prevent overfitting. 2) A novel and efficient technique for extracting optimal feedback gains directly from the CIO trajectory optimizer. This allows the network to learn not just a state-action mapping, but how to react optimally to small perturbations, which is crucial for stability in a closed-loop setting.

The method is demonstrated on an impressive variety of 3D characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal morphologies. The resulting controllers produce stable, realistic, and interactive locomotion gaits that emerge naturally from the optimization process without task-specific state machines or motion capture data. This work builds upon previous research combining trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but makes substantial advances, particularly in the complexity of the 3D tasks solved and the technical formulation for robustly training the policy.

### Strengths:

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The proposed joint optimization algorithm is well-motivated and clearly described. The derivation for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a particularly clever and valuable contribution. The claims of generating robust, real-time controllers are exceptionally well-supported by extensive experiments. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 provide compelling evidence for the necessity of the key components of their method (joint optimization and noise injection), demonstrating that naive training on a static dataset or training without noise fails to produce stable policies. This is a complete and carefully executed piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of first presenting a high-level overview of their method (Section 2) before delving into the specifics of each component. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the overall algorithm is easy to follow. The text effectively communicates the intuition behind key design choices, such as the use of noise and the formulation of the feedback gains. The results are presented effectively, and the accompanying video is essential for appreciating the quality of the synthesized behaviors.

**Originality:** While the general concept of using trajectory optimization to supervise a policy network has been explored before, this paper introduces several novel and crucial refinements. The primary novelty lies in the synergistic combination of techniques: the specific block-alternating optimization scheme, the principled use of noise with corresponding optimal feedback gains, and the method for extracting those gains from a direct optimization method. Applying this unified framework to such a wide and challenging array of 3D locomotion tasks is unprecedented and demonstrates a significant leap forward from prior work, which was largely confined to 2D characters.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. It presents a powerful and generalizable framework for automating the creation of high-quality controllers for complex physical systems. The results are not merely incremental; they represent a state-of-the-art achievement in character animation and learning-based control. The method has clear and immediate applications in animation, gaming, and robotics. Researchers in these fields are highly likely to build upon these ideas. The paper effectively shows how to bridge the gap between powerful but slow offline optimization methods and fast, reactive neural network policies, a problem of central importance to the field.

### Weaknesses:

The weaknesses of the paper are minor and do not detract from its overall contribution.

1.  **Computational Cost:** The training process is computationally intensive, requiring hours of training on a distributed cluster with a GPU. While this is justified by the quality of the results, it may present a barrier to entry for researchers with more limited computational resources.
2.  **Physics Enforcement:** As the authors note in Section 7, the physics constraints are enforced as soft penalties. This can lead to small physical inaccuracies (e.g., residual root forces) in the final simulation. While this appears to have a negligible effect on the visual quality of the results, it is a minor limitation.
3.  **Comparison to MPC:** The comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 is insightful but largely qualitative. A more quantitative comparison on metrics like goal achievement, energy consumption, or stability under perturbation could have further strengthened the argument that the joint optimization regularizes the behaviors toward more predictable and natural patterns.

### Summary and Recommendation:

This is an outstanding paper that presents a complete, powerful, and novel system for generating interactive controllers for a diverse set of complex characters. It masterfully combines ideas from trajectory optimization, control theory, and deep learning. The technical contributions are solid, the results are state-of-the-art, and the paper is written with exceptional clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly effective and general method for training robust RNN controllers.
*   Introduces a novel and valuable technique for extracting and utilizing optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer.
*   Demonstrates unprecedented success in applying a single learning framework to a wide variety of challenging 3D locomotion tasks.
*   The work is of very high quality, significance, and clarity, making it a perfect fit for NIPS.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   None of significance. The minor weaknesses noted above are far outweighed by the strengths.

This paper is a clear accept and should be highlighted at the conference. It is an inspiring piece of work that will undoubtedly influence future research in learning-based control, robotics, and animation.
