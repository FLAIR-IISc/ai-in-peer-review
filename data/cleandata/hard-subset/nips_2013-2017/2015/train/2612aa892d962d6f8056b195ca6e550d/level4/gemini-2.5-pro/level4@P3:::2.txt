### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time, interactive feedback controller for physically simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimization method (Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate a dataset of near-optimal behaviors for a variety of tasks and character morphologies. A neural network policy is then trained to mimic the state-action mappings from these optimized trajectories. The key features of the proposed method include an iterative training scheme that alternates between optimizing trajectories and regressing the network policy, the injection of noise at both the sensory input and hidden unit levels to improve robustness, and a novel technique for extracting optimal feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimizer. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach by applying the same architecture to generate stable and realistic locomotion for diverse 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flier, without requiring motion capture data or task-specific engineering. This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with function approximation for control, aiming to solve more complex, 3D control problems than previously demonstrated.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive and General Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its empirical results. The ability to control a wide range of characters with very different dynamics (walking, swimming, flying) using a single, unified method is a significant achievement. The resulting behaviors, as shown in the supplementary video, appear stable, realistic, and capable of responding to interactive goals, demonstrating the effectiveness and generality of the proposed framework.
2.  **Effective Joint Optimization Framework:** The paper successfully argues for and demonstrates the importance of jointly optimizing the trajectories and the policy. The ablation study in Figure 2 provides compelling evidence that this iterative approach, where the training data is continuously refined based on the current policy, is vastly superior to a naive approach of training on a static dataset. This highlights a crucial insight for making such "distillation" methods work in practice.
3.  **Principled Use of Noise:** The injection of noise during training is well-motivated and shown to be critical for generalization and preventing the controller from diverging during online execution. The connection made to denoising autoencoders and the method for generating corresponding optimal actions for noisy states via feedback gains is a principled way to build robustness.
4.  **Novel Technical Contribution in Feedback Generation:** The proposed method for computing optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) directly from the direct trajectory optimization process is a clever contribution. By re-framing the problem as a soft-constrained optimization, the method avoids a potentially inefficient and ill-conditioned LQG pass, which could be a useful technique for other researchers in the field.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Contribution and Positioning:** The main weakness of the paper is its positioning relative to very similar, recent work. The general concept of using trajectory optimization to supervise the training of a neural network policy has been explored by others (e.g., Levine & Koltun [9], Mordatch et al. [11]). While the present work tackles more complex 3D characters, the paper needs to more explicitly articulate its conceptual novelty beyond this successful scaling. A direct comparison and discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of this method compared to the closely related work from researchers like Sergey Levine and Pieter Abbeel is necessary. For example, how does this approach compare in terms of computational cost, sample efficiency, final policy robustness, or the complexity of behaviors it can learn? A clearer discussion would help readers understand the specific advance this paper makes.
2.  **Clarity of Technical Explanations:** Several key technical details could be explained more clearly to improve the paper's impact and reproducibility.
    *   In Section 3.2, the motivation for the novel feedback gain computation method is that standard LQG can produce "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." This claim is not substantiated. Providing a more concrete explanation, or a simple example of where this occurs, would significantly strengthen the motivation for your proposed technique.
    *   The term "softer feedback gains" is used to describe the output of your method. This is qualitative and somewhat vague. It would be beneficial to define what "soft" means in this context (e.g., smaller L2 norm, smoother across the trajectory) and to provide a more rigorous justification for why such gains are preferable. The intuition that the method allows the initial state to change is interesting, but its benefits are not immediately obvious and warrant further explanation.
    *   The notation in Section 3.2 (around lines 226-240) is confusing. You first introduce `s(X) = s_bar` to denote the state on the optimal trajectory, but then immediately use `s_bar` in the cost function `||s(X) - s_bar||^2` to represent the perturbed target state. Using distinct notation, such as `s*` for the optimal state and `s_target` for the perturbed state, would greatly improve readability.

### Detailed Comments

*   A minor suggestion for organization: The discussion motivating why LQG was not used (lines 224-226) feels preemptive. It might flow better if you first present your alternative method for computing feedback gains and then follow it with a comparison to the standard LQG approach, explaining the issues you encountered with the latter.
*   The distributed training architecture is a practical and important component of making this work scale. It is well-described and adds to the engineering contribution of the paper.
*   The emergence of cyclic gaits (e.g., trotting for the quadruped) without explicit encoding is a very nice result that underscores the power of the optimization-based approach.

In summary, this is a strong piece of empirical work with impressive results that convincingly demonstrate a powerful and general method for character control. The primary areas for improvement are in clarifying the specific conceptual contributions relative to closely related prior art and improving the clarity of some of the key technical descriptions. Addressing these points would elevate the paper from a strong application paper to a more impactful scientific contribution.
