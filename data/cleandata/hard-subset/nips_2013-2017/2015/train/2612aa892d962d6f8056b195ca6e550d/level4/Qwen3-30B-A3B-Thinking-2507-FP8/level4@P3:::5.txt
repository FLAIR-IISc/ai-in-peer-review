

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents an interesting approach to learning neural network controllers for complex locomotion tasks through the combination of trajectory optimization (CIO) and neural network policy learning. The authors demonstrate their method on a diverse set of 3D locomotion tasks including swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking, achieving stable and realistic behaviors without motion capture or task-specific state machines. The work builds upon recent efforts in trajectory optimization for character control [9, 11, 12] while addressing the limitations of purely neural network-based approaches [3, 6, 10, 14] that often produce chaotic behaviors.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that the combination of trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning produces substantially more stable and realistic locomotion than naive neural network training approaches. The comparative evaluation (Figure 2, Table 1) clearly shows that their joint optimization technique outperforms static training by two orders of magnitude, and noise injection is critical for preventing overfitting.

2. **Broad Applicability:** The authors successfully apply the same neural network architecture to multiple character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without specialized initialization, demonstrating the method's generality. The results show that the controller can learn cyclical gaits (trot for quadrupeds, regular bipedal walking) without explicit user input, suggesting the method can automatically discover appropriate behaviors.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The distributed training architecture (Section 6) is well-described and demonstrates good scalability with cluster size. The authors provide clear details about their implementation choices, including the use of proximal regularization to stabilize the asynchronous training process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Model Knowledge Requirement:** The paper does not sufficiently address the requirement for full model knowledge (Eq. 6), which is a significant limitation for real-world robotics applications. While this is acceptable for character animation, it fundamentally restricts the method's applicability to scenarios where a complete physics model is unavailable. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential pathways to overcome it.

2. **Section Disproportion:** Section 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) is disproportionately short compared to Section 6 (Distributed Training Architecture), despite the former being central to the method's innovation. The section does not adequately address the recurrent units mentioned in the abstract, which are a key component of the controller's architecture.

3. **Stochasticity Clarification:** The paper claims to use "stochastic neural networks" (Section 4), but the network itself appears deterministic. The stochasticity comes from the training data (noise injection), not the network architecture. This mischaracterization should be corrected to avoid confusion.

**Quality:** The method is technically sound with strong experimental validation across multiple locomotion tasks. The authors provide a thorough ablation study showing the importance of their key innovations (joint optimization, noise injection). However, the requirement for full model knowledge is a significant limitation that should be more thoroughly addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance of technical detail and high-level explanation. The figures effectively illustrate the results. However, Section 4 would benefit from greater clarity regarding the recurrent units and the nature of the stochasticity in the method.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending trajectory optimization-based control to complex 3D locomotion tasks. While related to [9, 11, 12], it solves substantially harder problems (3D vs. 2D) and provides a more comprehensive framework for learning neural network controllers. The combination of trajectory optimization with neural networks for control represents a novel approach to the problem.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate significant potential for animation and character control applications. The unified framework could streamline the development of interactive character controllers. However, the requirement for full model knowledge limits broader significance for robotics applications where model uncertainty is common. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the future work section, but it should be more prominently discussed in the main text.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound method with promising results that advance the state of the art in character control. The main limitations (model knowledge requirement, section imbalance, stochasticity clarification) are significant but not fatal. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these points.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Demonstrates successful application to diverse 3D locomotion tasks
- Provides clear comparative results showing significant improvement over baseline approaches
- Offers a unified framework that replaces task-specific state machines
- Includes thorough ablation studies to validate key methodological choices

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Requires full model knowledge (Eq. 6), limiting applicability to robotics
- Section 4 does not adequately address recurrent units from the abstract
- Mischaracterization of the neural network as "stochastic"
- Section 4 and 6 are disproportionate in length and detail

The paper makes a valuable contribution to character control, and with minor revisions addressing the limitations noted above, it would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings. The authors have demonstrated a solid technical approach with clear experimental validation, and the method shows promise for practical applications in animation and character control.
