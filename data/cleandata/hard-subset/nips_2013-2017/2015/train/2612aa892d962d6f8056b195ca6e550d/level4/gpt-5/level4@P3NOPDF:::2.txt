Summary
This paper demonstrates that a small, fast neural network can represent control policies for interactive control of physically simulated characters. Policies are trained from trajectories generated by trajectory optimization, and the same network architecture is shown to handle a variety of largely cyclic behaviors and different body morphologies.

Strengths
- The approach emphasizes runtime efficiency and interactivity, which are crucial in character control.
- Training policies from optimized trajectories is a pragmatic way to leverage strong supervision for complex dynamics.
- The empirical results indicate the architecture transfers reasonably across behaviors and morphologies without extensive per-task redesign.

Relation to prior work and contribution
My main concern is the positioning relative to closely related literature in trajectory optimization, imitation learning, and deep policy learning in graphics and robotics. Prior work has:
- Established that trajectory optimization can produce realistic, dynamically consistent motions.
- Used function approximators, including neural networks, to learn policies that track optimized trajectories (e.g., [9] and subsequent work).
- Developed methods that integrate trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning and/or RL (e.g., the guided policy search line by Abbeel, Levine, and collaborators).

Given this context, the paper should more clearly articulate its distinctive contribution. Questions the paper should answer explicitly:
- What specific advantages does your method provide over guided policy search or similar frameworks? Is it faster to train, better at handling diverse morphologies, more robust to perturbations, or lighter at runtime?
- Are there trade-offs in sample efficiency, stability, or robustness compared to those methods?
- Do you claim novelty primarily in the architecture, the training pipeline, or the breadth of behaviors demonstrated? If the main contribution is empirical (i.e., the same compact architecture works well across many characters and tasks), please frame it as such and provide ablations to support the claim.

Technical and clarity questions
- “Very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”: Please define this precisely. Do you refer to controller gains with large condition numbers leading to numerical instability or sensitivity to sensor noise? Provide quantitative evidence (e.g., spectra/condition numbers) and examples of failure modes.
- LQG discussion placement: Consider presenting your proposed strategy first, then contrasting with why LQG was not adopted, using concrete empirical or theoretical reasons (e.g., instability under contact discontinuities, sensitivity to model mismatch).
- Notation around lines 226–240: The introduction of s(X) = \bar s followed by a cost on s(X) − \bar s is confusing because the same symbols are used both for a constraint and a penalty, and the overbars/diacritics are visually similar. Please separate symbols for the target and the current value, and avoid overloading. A simple fix: define a target s*, use s(X) for the current, and write the penalty as ||s(X) − s*|| with a clearly named weight.
- “Softer feedback gains according to parameter λ”: Softer than what baseline? By “soft,” do you mean smaller gain magnitudes, lower effective stiffness/damping, or increased regularization in the controller synthesis? Please define the metric (e.g., norm of the gain matrix, closed-loop eigenvalues) and show how λ trades off tracking accuracy and robustness.
- Changing the initial state: Why is it beneficial to alter the initial state as part of training or policy extraction? Is this a curriculum strategy to improve robustness, a means to avoid local minima, or a technique to stabilize optimization? Please justify and, if possible, quantify its effect on generalization and convergence.

Experimental evaluation
- Provide a more direct comparison to the guided policy search and related methods in terms of:
  - Runtime latency and controller size at test time.
  - Sample efficiency and wall-clock time for training.
  - Robustness to perturbations and environmental variability (e.g., pushes, terrain changes).
  - Ability to accommodate different body morphologies without task-specific tuning.
- If possible, report head-to-head performance on a common benchmark task or include a controlled ablation that isolates your claimed advantages.
- Include ablations assessing: network size vs. performance/latency; effect of λ on stability and tracking; impact of initial-state variation; and sensitivity to modeling errors in the simulator.

Writing and presentation
- Clarify the problem statement up front: goal, assumptions, and the precise scope (primarily cyclic behaviors? contact-rich dynamics?).
- Improve the organization by introducing your method fully before comparing alternatives (e.g., LQG).
- Tighten notation to avoid overload and visual confusion; reduce reliance on similar diacritics.
- Define all hyperparameters, training schedules, and optimization tolerances. Provide enough detail for reproducibility (e.g., network architecture, activation functions, dataset sizes, and trajectory optimization settings).
- Where you claim architectural generality across bodies and behaviors, specify what components are fixed vs. retuned.

Overall assessment
This is sound and practically relevant work that convincingly demonstrates efficient, interactive control with compact neural policies trained from optimized trajectories. However, the paper currently underplays its relationship to closely related methods in robotics and graphics, and it does not clearly articulate or empirically validate a distinct advantage over that prior art. Without clearer positioning and stronger comparative evaluation, the contribution feels incremental relative to recent work.

I would be inclined toward a weak reject unless the authors:
- Sharpen the claim of novelty (what is uniquely enabled here),
- Provide a principled comparison to guided policy search and similar approaches,
- Clarify the technical issues noted above (ill-conditioning, “soft” gains, notation, and the role of initial-state changes),
- And include targeted ablations that support the paper’s central claims.

That said, the approach is promising, the results are compelling from a systems perspective, and with improved framing and comparisons, the work could make a valuable contribution.
