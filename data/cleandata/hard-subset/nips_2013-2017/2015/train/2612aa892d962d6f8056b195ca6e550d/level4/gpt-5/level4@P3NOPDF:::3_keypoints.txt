- Proposes a real-time 3D character controller where a neural network is trained to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs with alternating optimization.  
- Tight coupling between CIO and NN learning (alternating optimization) is a core contribution improving generalization.  
- Robustness introduced via data augmentation (perturbed inputs and recomputed targets) and additive noise in NN hidden layers.  
- Empirical results show stable, realistic motions across varied character models.  
- Strong formulation addressing a hard, important problem (real-time control for varied characters).  
- Alternating CIO–NN optimization is novel and central to performance beyond simple imitation.  
- Use of both data augmentation and hidden-layer noise is well-motivated and empirically beneficial.  
- Mathematical development is mostly clear and the approach is practical.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited: small training set (100–200 trials) may cause overfitting and limits conclusions.  
- Need larger, more diverse training corpora and tasks to validate generality.  
- Ablations should separately quantify effects of data augmentation vs. hidden-layer noise.  
- Additional baselines (modern trajectory-optimization and direct-policy methods) and a cited MPC baseline are needed.  
- Require quantitative metrics for stability/robustness and aggregated success rates across seeds and perturbations.  
- Reproducibility issues: some derivations and notation (especially Section 3.2 and Eq. (6)) are underspecified or ambiguous.  
- Provide clearer time-indexed notation, explicit definitions/dimensions for symbols, and pseudo-code for noisy-target generation/CIO interface.  
- Release code to resolve ambiguities and aid community adoption.  
- Real-time performance not characterized—report per-frame runtime, scaling with DoFs/contact points, hardware, and batching/approximations.  
- Paper understates differences between synchronous alternating Algorithm 1 and asynchronous joint optimization; introduce asynchronous variant earlier and discuss convergence and safeguards.  
- Clarify whether targets include orientation (yaw) and how to add orientation objectives to training.  
- Clarify initialization in Algorithm 1: whether first CIO uses randomly initialized NN regularization or CIO-only solutions.  
- Explain hyperparameter choice: same η for both alternating steps and whether separate schedules were explored.  
- Clarify “enforced softly” dynamics: quantify residual root forces/torques and their impact on physical plausibility.  
- Justify set relationships (why sX and aX are subsets of φX) and state assumptions that guarantee this.  
- Reconcile references to objectives in Algorithm 1 vs. Equation (4) to avoid ambiguity.  
- List of minor typos and wording fixes (e.g., “biped location”→“biped locomotion,” define LQG before use, fix misplaced star in argmin, table/algorithm captions and schedules).  
- Overall: strong, timely idea with promising qualitative results but needs expanded experiments, clearer notation, runtime characterization, and code to firmly establish advantages and practical readiness.
