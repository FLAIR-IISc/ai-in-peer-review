This paper, "Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks," demonstrates how a small, fast neural network can serve as a control policy for the interactive control of characters within a physics simulator. The proposed approach uses trajectory optimization during a training phase to learn this neural network policy. The primary contribution appears to be showing that a single network architecture can be successfully applied to a diverse range of characters and semi-cyclic behaviors.

**General Comments**

This paper appears to be an application of deep learning to established techniques in computer graphics. The ability of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals is well-documented in prior work, as the authors acknowledge. Similarly, the use of neural networks to approximate the actions of a policy, mimicking trajectories found via optimization, has also been explored previously (e.g., [9]).

Given this context, the paper's primary weakness is its positioning relative to prior art. The authors need to more clearly articulate the novelty and specific contributions of their work. For instance, a detailed comparison to the recent line of work by Levine, Abbeel, and their colleagues is missing. The authors should clarify how their approach differs and what advantages it offers. Is the proposed method faster to train? Does it generalize to a wider variety of character morphologies? Is it more computationally efficient at runtime, or more robust to environmental perturbations? Without this comparison, the unique contribution of the paper is difficult to assess.

**Specific Comments**

*   Could the authors please clarify the statement that the "resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains"? More context on when and why this occurs would be helpful.

*   Consider moving the discussion of why LQG was not used until after the main strategy has been presented. This would allow the reader to first understand the proposed method before reading the justification for why an alternative was rejected.

*   The notation around lines 226-240 is confusing. You introduce s(X) = \bar{s} and then define a cost in terms of s(X) - \bar{s}. If this is not a typo, it implies the cost is always zero. If a different target variable was intended, the visual similarity between symbols like bars and tildes makes the equations difficult to parse. Using more distinct notation would improve clarity.

*   The paper claims the approach "produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ." This statement requires further explanation. Softer than what alternative method? What is the precise definition of "softness" in the context of feedback gains?

*   Could the authors explain the motivation for modifying the initial state as part of the procedure? What is the benefit of this step?

In summary, the work appears technically sound, but the paper's contribution is not clearly delineated from recent, related work. Strengthening the positioning and clarifying the specific points raised above would significantly improve the paper.
