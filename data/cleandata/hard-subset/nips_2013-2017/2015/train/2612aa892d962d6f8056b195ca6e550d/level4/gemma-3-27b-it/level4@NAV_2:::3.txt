In this submission, the authors propose an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, where a neural network (NN) is used to generate animations, after being trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Importantly, CIO and NN training are not independent; they are performed in an alternating fashion, with the output of one informing the criterion optimized by the other. A core element of the method is the injection of noise, through data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise in the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that such a network is able to produce realistic and stable control policies on a range of diverse character models.

This is a very interesting approach, considering that real-time control of arbitrary models is a challenging task. Although the proposed method may initially appear straightforward ("learn a NN to predict the output of CIO"), the additional steps to make it work—joint training and noise injection—are significant new contributions, and are shown experimentally to improve generalization. 

The experimental results would be strengthened by comparisons to a larger dataset. Since the base training trajectories were generated with only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” it is not surprising that the “no noise” variant overfits. The “no joint” variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories, though this is less obvious. 

Overall, the comparative evaluation is a weak point of the paper (acknowledged in the introduction: "A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with MPC at the end of the paper (and this method is not referenced). 

For the most part, the mathematics are clearly explained and motivated, although someone seeking to replicate these results will need to consult additional references, as some points are only addressed superficially (especially the CIO step – see Eq. 6, whose notations are not fully defined). Providing code would be highly beneficial to the community. I was unable to fully understand section 3.2 regarding generating optimal actions for noisy inputs; the notations became confusing, and it was unclear what was being done (in particular, the time index “t” was dropped, but is it still implied for some of the quantities?). 

It would also be useful to obtain time measurements for computations during real-time control, as this could be a major bottleneck (and knowing how these computations scale with the complexity of the 3D model). In an application like a video game, limited CPU resources are available to animate a single character on screen.

A great-looking application of neural networks to character control, whose practical benefits and applicability still need to be fully established.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks for real-time control.
*   Demonstrated ability to control diverse character morphologies with a single architecture.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
*   The method appears to produce stable and realistic behaviors.
*   Joint optimization and noise injection are significant contributions.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparative evaluation against existing methods.
*   Dataset size for training may be small, leading to potential overfitting.
*   Mathematical details in some sections are lacking, making replication difficult.
*   Lack of timing measurements for real-time performance.
*   Some unclear notation and explanations (Section 3.2).
