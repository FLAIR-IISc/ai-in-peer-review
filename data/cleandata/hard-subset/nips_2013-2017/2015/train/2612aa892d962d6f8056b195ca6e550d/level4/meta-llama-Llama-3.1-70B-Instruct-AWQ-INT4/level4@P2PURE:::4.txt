This paper proposes a novel approach to enable interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The core contribution is the joint learning of trajectories and policies, with the additional empirical insight that decoupling these two optimization problems into alternating subproblems has two advantages: it allows the reuse of existing, well-documented methods, and it is effective in practice. Although I was able to follow the paper, I am not sufficiently familiar with this domain to comment on the potential impact of this work, which appears to be a technical achievement with no novel theoretical insights.

The authors demonstrate a high level of technical sophistication throughout the paper, from the concepts to the implementation, and achieve impressive results. The claimed advantages of using sensory noise during training and learning trajectories jointly with the policy are clearly demonstrated.

The paper is well-written, with an appropriate level of detail considering the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section addresses a separate aspect of the problem, and the cross-references between sections are helpful.

However, I found the motivations for the additional optimization step in Section 7 to be unclear. Given the previous sections, I expected the policy to be executable without the need for extra optimization. Is this because there is always a residual trajectory cost (or policy regression error) that would accumulate errors when feeding the action given by the policy into the physical simulator? I would have thought that the forward evolution of the dynamics would account for physical constraints, preventing physical implausibilities. Nevertheless, I appreciate that re-optimizing at every time step allows for larger time steps.

On line 153, the authors mention that their approach is similar to recent methods; providing one or two references would be helpful. I found it counterintuitive that keeping updates small in parameter space would be beneficial in general, although I eventually understood its usefulness in the context of alternating between subproblems.

There is a typo on line 89 ("to unify"). The paper's results appear impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation seem sensible. However, as this research area is not my primary focus, I am uncertain about the novelty and significance of this work.
