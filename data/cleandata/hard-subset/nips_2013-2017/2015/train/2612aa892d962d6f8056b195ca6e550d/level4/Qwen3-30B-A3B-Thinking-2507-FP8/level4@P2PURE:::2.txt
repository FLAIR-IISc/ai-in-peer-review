

"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates how a compact, fast neural network can represent a control policy for interactive character control within a physics simulator. The approach employs trajectory optimization during training to learn a neural network policy, with the key contribution being the demonstration that the same architecture generalizes across diverse cyclic behaviors and body types.  

General Comments ----------------  
This paper applies deep learning to established (though recent) computer graphics techniques. The capability of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals is well-documented in prior work (some cited here). Similarly, using neural networks to approximate step-by-step policy actions for trajectory reproduction has been explored previously (e.g., [9]). Consequently, the authors must more clearly position their work relative to these established approaches.  
The closest related work is the recent line of research by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. How does this approach compare to theirs? Is it intended to learn faster? Support a broader range of body shapes? Reduce runtime computation? Enhance robustness to specific environmental variations?  

Specific Comments -----------------  
Clarify the meaning of "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains."  
Consider relocating the discussion of why LQG was not used until after presenting the proposed strategy.  
The notation in lines 226–240 is confusing: introducing $s(X) = \bar{s}$ and then defining cost in terms of $s(X) - \bar{s}$ creates visually indistinct notation due to the bars and squiggles.  
Explain what is meant by "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter $\lambda$." Softer than what? What defines "soft" feedback gains, and why is this beneficial? Additionally, justify why changing the initial state is part of the procedure.  
Sound work overall, but the contribution relative to recent related literature remains inadequately clarified.
