This paper proposes a novel approach to enable interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The primary contribution of the paper is the joint learning of trajectories and policies, with the additional empirical insight that decoupling the two optimization problems into alternating subproblems allows for the reuse of existing methods and achieves satisfactory results in practice.

Although I am not an expert in this domain, the paper appears to be a technical tour-de-force, showcasing the authors' impressive technical sophistication at all levels, from conceptualization to implementation. The results presented in the paper are equally impressive, and the claimed advantages of using sensory noise during training and learning trajectories jointly with the policy are clearly demonstrated.

The paper is well-written, with an appropriate level of detail considering the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section focuses on a separate aspect of the problem, and the cross-references between sections are helpful. However, I found the motivations for the extra optimization step in Section 7 to be somewhat unclear. Given the previous sections, I expected the policy to run without the need for additional optimization.

I assume that the necessity for this extra step arises from the residual trajectory cost or policy regression error, which would accumulate errors when feeding the action given by the policy into the physical simulator. However, I would appreciate further clarification on this point. The forward evolution of the dynamics presumably takes into account physical constraints, preventing physically implausible results. Nevertheless, I understand that re-optimizing at every time step allows for the use of larger time steps.

On line 153, the authors mention that their approach is similar to recent methods. Providing one or two relevant references would be helpful. I found it counterintuitive that keeping updates small in parameter space would be beneficial in general. However, I eventually understood its utility in the context of block-alternating between subproblems.

There is a minor typo on line 89 ("to unify").

In conclusion, the paper's results appear impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation seem sensible. However, as this research area is not my primary focus, I am hesitant to comment on the novelty and significance of the paper.
