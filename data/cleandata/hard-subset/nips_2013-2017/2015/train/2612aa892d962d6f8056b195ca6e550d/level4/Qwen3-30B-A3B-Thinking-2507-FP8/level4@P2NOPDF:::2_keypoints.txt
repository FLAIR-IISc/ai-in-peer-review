- Paper proposes a compact neural-network policy for interactive character animation trained by trajectory optimization, claiming one architecture generalizes across cyclic motions and body types.  
- Main critique: the contribution is not clearly differentiated from established trajectory optimization and recent deep learning control work.  
- Authors must explicitly state how their method advances beyond prior frameworks (e.g., Abbeel & Levine) — is the gain in training time, generalization, runtime cost, or robustness?  
- Request explicit comparisons to prior work on key metrics: training efficiency, generalization across body types/motions, runtime computation, and robustness to noise.  
- Clarify the phrase “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”: provide quantitative metrics and explain the impact on reliability.  
- Reorder manuscript: move the discussion of why LQG was not used to follow the proposed strategy to improve narrative flow.  
- Fix confusing notation around s(X)=s̄ and cost s(X)−s̄; use distinct symbols (e.g., s_target) to avoid ambiguity.  
- Define and quantify “soft feedback gains” (softer than what?) relative to standard control metrics and justify why this is desirable.  
- Explain the motivation and specific benefits of modifying the initial state during training for the learned policy.  
- Minor edits: replace “more-or-less cyclic behaviours” with “diverse cyclic motion patterns,” clarify what “soft gains” mean, and directly contrast all cited prior work (e.g., [9]) with the proposed method.  
- Overall: technically sound and practically useful, but currently reads as an incremental application unless its distinct contributions versus existing literature are clearly demonstrated.
