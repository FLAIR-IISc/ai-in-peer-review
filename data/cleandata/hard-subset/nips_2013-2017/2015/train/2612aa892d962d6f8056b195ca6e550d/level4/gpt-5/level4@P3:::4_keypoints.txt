- Presents an alternating pipeline: trajectory optimization (CIO) ↔ supervised RNN policy learning for diverse 3D characters.  
- Two key additions: sensory/hidden-unit noise injection for robustness and a perturbation method to extract local feedback gains from the CIO Hessian.  
- Runtime control augments the learned policy with a fast T=1 optimization step enforcing dynamics and contacts, enabling real-time interactive control.  
- Builds on "learning from an optimizer" approaches (e.g., guided policy search, trajectory-optimization-based policy learning) and extends them to challenging 3D, multi-morphology, contact-rich behaviors.  
- Compared to model-free RL, the method reduces exploration burden and yields more regular, interpretable motions by leveraging a model-based optimizer.  
- Strength: strong technical and engineering design (alternating optimization, efficient gain computation, distributed asynchronous training).  
- Strength: broad results — a single architecture produces stable, realistic behaviors across swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds in 3D.  
- Strength: convincingly demonstrates robustness benefits from sensory and hidden-unit noise via ablations.  
- Strength: paper is generally clear, well organized, and well cross-referenced.  
- Concern: the T=1 runtime optimization blurs pure policy execution and MPC — authors should clarify why the policy alone is insufficient.  
- Request: quantify how much the T=1 solve corrects the network action (e.g., average ∥a − aNN∥, distribution over timesteps).  
- Request: report magnitude and frequency of root forces from soft constraint enforcement and other constraint-violation statistics.  
- Weakness: limited quantitative evaluation — needs success rates, energy/torque usage, smoothness, contact residuals, performance under goal switches.  
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., MPC with same costs/horizon, iLQG/LQR, TRPO) for context.  
- Clarification needed: confusing phrasing that the policy is "learning dynamics under an implicit policy" — clarify action interpretation (desired velocities/forces vs torques) and relation to T=1 optimization.  
- Method detail: proximal quadratic terms for asynchronous alternating updates are reasonable, but concrete citations (proximal point, ADMM, elastic averaging SGD) are requested.  
- Generalization/data efficiency: authors should detail performance scaling with number of trials/segments, generalization to unseen goals, and analyze failure modes (falls).  
- Physical fidelity concern: soft constraints and implicit integration aid stability but may mask modeling errors and complicate transfer to hardware or stricter simulators.  
- Quality judgment: technically sound with useful ablations and demonstrations, but needs quantitative diagnostics on runtime reliance and physical plausibility.  
- Clarity note: Section 7 should better motivate and quantify the runtime optimization; clarify "action as velocity" interpretation.  
- Originality: extends known alternating optimization ideas but contributes a practical, scalable 3D instantiation, neat perturbation-based gain computation, and effective noise regimen.  
- Significance: likely impactful for graphics and model-based control communities due to unified, realistic 3D locomotion across morphologies.  
- Pros for acceptance: diverse 3D task success with one architecture, clear evidence for noise benefits, practical feedback-gain extraction, and strong engineering/writing.  
- Cons against acceptance: insufficient quantitative baselines, ambiguous necessity/extent of runtime optimization, and questions about physical plausibility from soft constraints.  
- Key suggestions: quantify T=1 impact and constraint violations, report robustness/energy metrics and baseline comparisons, clarify implicit-policy wording, add proximal-method citations (e.g., Boyd et al.), and discuss λ selection for feedback gains.
