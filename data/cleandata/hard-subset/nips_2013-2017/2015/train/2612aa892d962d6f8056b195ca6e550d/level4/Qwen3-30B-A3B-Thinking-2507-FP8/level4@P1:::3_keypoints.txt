- Main contribution: joint training where a recurrent neural network is trained to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs via alternating optimization to produce near-optimal feedback controllers for 3D character animation.  
- Training uses noise injection (sensory perturbations + hidden activation noise), which the authors show is critical for generalization to unseen conditions.  
- Extends prior work by handling diverse 3D locomotion (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network architecture, moving beyond prior 2D- and specialist-focused methods.  
- Technical quality is strong and experimental results support the importance of the joint training and noise injection components.  
- Ablation studies demonstrate that removing either joint training or noise injection causes policies to quickly diverge during execution.  
- Method shows impressive generalization across diverse character morphologies using one network architecture.  
- Comparative evaluation is weak: only a meaningful comparison with MPC is shown; broader systematic comparisons with alternative neural approaches are missing.  
- Clarity is generally good but Section 3.2 (computing optimal feedback gains) is confusing due to inconsistent time-indexing, unclear notation, and an unclear role for the perturbation parameter λ.  
- Paper lacks detailed computational complexity and real-time execution analysis, leaving scalability with character complexity unclear.  
- Originality is strong: the joint training approach with noise injection is a novel combination enabling complex 3D locomotion results.  
- Significance is promising for animation, robotics, and biomechanics, but more discussion is needed on scaling to complex environments and real-world computational limits.  
- Minor issues: grammatical errors, inconsistent terminology ("noise injection" vs "data augmentation"), some terms introduced before definition (e.g., LQG), and unclear explanation of how optimal feedback gains are used in training.  
- Overall: technically sound and innovative, an important step toward general character control, but would be strengthened by clearer technical explanations and more comprehensive comparisons.
