This submission presents an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, leveraging a neural network (NN) trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key strength of the approach lies in the alternating training of the NN and CIO, where each informs the optimization criterion of the other. The method also incorporates noise injection through both data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise within the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate the network's ability to generate realistic and stable control policies across diverse character models.

This is a compelling approach to a challenging problem – real-time control of arbitrary 3D models. While seemingly straightforward ("learn a NN to predict CIO output"), the authors’ additions of joint training and noise injection represent significant contributions, and experimental results suggest improved generalization as a result.

However, the comparative evaluation constitutes the primary weakness of the paper. The dataset used for training—between 100 and 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments—is relatively small, explaining the overfitting observed in the “no noise” variant. The “no joint” variant may also benefit from more diverse training trajectories, though this is less clear. As acknowledged by the authors (“A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”), a more extensive comparison is needed. The single comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) is brief and lacks a reference.

The mathematical formulations are generally clear and well-motivated, though replicating these results would require consulting additional references, as certain aspects (particularly the CIO step, e.g., Eq. 6 with undefined notations) are treated superficially. Providing code would greatly benefit the research community. Section 3.2, detailing the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, proved particularly difficult to follow due to confusing notation (specifically, the disappearance of the time index "t" and its implications).

Furthermore, runtime performance measurements during real-time control would be valuable, as computational cost could be a major bottleneck. In applications like video games, computational resources are limited, and the ability to animate a character efficiently is crucial.

Several minor points should be addressed: "biped location" should likely be "locomotion"; "unity" should be "unify"; the current objective appears limited to x, y, and z coordinates, without constraints on character orientation – exploring the addition of an orientation target could be beneficial; the process of re-computing targets for modified inputs during data augmentation is better described as "data augmentation" rather than "noise injection," as it differs from the principles behind denoising autoencoders; clarification is needed regarding the initialization of network weights (θ) in the first iteration of Algorithm 1 (are X's computed with or without the regularization term?); the justification for using a uniform hyperparameter (η) in both steps of Algorithm 1 should be explained; “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have” should read “it is non-trivial to adapt them to the asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”; the acronym LQG is used before its definition; “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass” should read “While we can use a Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”; in line 231, the star in the argmin is misplaced, and  \bar{s} is missing in \tilde{C}; "the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory" is incomplete; the guarantee that sX and aX are subsets of \phiX requires explanation; the objective being reduced in line 315 is from Algorithm 1, not equation (4); the asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 differs significantly from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1 and this difference should be mentioned earlier; line 315, f should be f^{des}; “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.” is unclear; the “no noise” variant should be split into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise injection”; exploring whether alpha can be reduced to zero during CIO optimization (investigating a potential curriculum learning effect) would be insightful; the caption of Table 1a indicates 4 layers when it appears to be 3.

Despite these weaknesses, this work presents a promising application of neural networks to character control. Further research is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and broader applicability.
