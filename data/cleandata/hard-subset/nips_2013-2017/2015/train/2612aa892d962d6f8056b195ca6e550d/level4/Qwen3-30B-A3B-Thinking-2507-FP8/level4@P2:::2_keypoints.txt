- Introduces a method to train RNNs as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex physics-based character behaviors.
- Demonstrates approach on diverse 3D locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) and varied morphologies without motion capture or task-specific features.
- Core innovation: joint trajectory optimization and neural network policy learning where the network learns optimal controls and a dynamics model simultaneously.
- Training uses interleaved supervised learning, noise injection, and trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains.
- Builds on Contact-Invariant Optimization and neural network control, extending prior approaches to richer 3D behaviors.
- Shows improved stability and realism in 3D locomotion compared to prior mostly 2D work.
- Same network architecture controls multiple character types without specialized initialization.
- Experiments are comprehensive with clear ablations showing the importance of joint optimization and noise injection.
- Provides sufficient implementation and parameter details for reproducibility.
- Notation issues (Section 3.2, s(X)=\bar s) make parts confusing.
- Discussion of LQG versus their approach is weakly placed and should be earlier.
- Insufficiently positioned relative to closely related prior work (Levine & Koltun 2014, Mordatch et al. 2012).
- Originality is limited since core techniques are established, with unclear technical novelty over prior methods.
- Claims of solving “substantially harder problems” need stronger comparative context with previous work.
- Significance is strong for character animation, robotics, and biomechanics due to generalizable control across morphologies.
- Key technical contribution (feedback gain calculation and conditioning) needs clearer explanation versus LQG.
- Runtime efficiency and comparison to MPC or other learned controllers are not thoroughly addressed.
- Overall: technically sound with valuable empirical results, but would be stronger with clearer relation to prior work and runtime/runtime-comparison details.
