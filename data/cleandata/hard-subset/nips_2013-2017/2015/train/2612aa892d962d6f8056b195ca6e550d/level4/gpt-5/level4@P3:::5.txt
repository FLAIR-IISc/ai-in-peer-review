Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a method for training neural-network controllers for complex, physics-based characters by alternating trajectory optimization with supervised learning. The core idea is to use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate trajectories and, crucially, local optimal feedback gains around those trajectories. A neural network with mostly feedforward units and a small recurrent state is then trained to map noisy sensory inputs to actions that include desired generalized velocities, contact forces, and updates to the recurrent memory. During training, Gaussian noise is injected into the inputs (and hidden activations) and the network is trained to match noise-adjusted targets using the optimizer-derived gains. A distributed, asynchronous system generates data (trials and branched segments with changing goals), while a GPU trains the policy. At test time, the policy runs in real-time; the next state is computed by solving a T=1 optimality problem that enforces dynamics and contact constraints (softly).

The work sits at the intersection of trajectory optimization and policy learning, and is closely related to Guided Policy Search approaches (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14) and earlier work combining function approximation with trajectory optimization (Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14). The paper targets higher-dimensional, 3D locomotion and flight/swimming characters than prior 2D demonstrations, with a focus on animation-style realism and interactive control. Compared to model-free RL (e.g., Mnih et al., 2013; Schulman et al., 2015), the method relies on full model knowledge and trajectory optimization to produce high-quality demonstrations and gains.

Strengths
- Unifies control across diverse morphologies (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) with a single method and architecture, without motion capture, state machines, or task-specific features.
- Clever use of optimizer-derived local feedback gains to construct noise-augmented supervision targets; the perturbation-based derivation that reuses the Gauss–Newton Hessian from CIO is elegant and computationally efficient.
- Noise injection at both input and hidden layers improves robustness and mitigates overfitting; ablations demonstrate large gaps without joint training and without noise.
- Practical distributed training pipeline; the asynchronous proximal regularization is a sensible engineering choice and yields stable convergence empirically.
- Real-time execution with a simple T=1 optimization step that stabilizes dynamics and contacts; learned behaviors appear stable and visually plausible.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Dependence on full and accurate model knowledge: Equation (6) (EoM, non-penetration, and friction cone) is central both during data generation and test-time execution. This critical assumption should be stated up front in the introduction and abstract as it limits applicability to poorly modeled systems or real robots with unmodeled compliance.
- Stochastic policy terminology: The paper refers to a “stochastic policy,” but the network appears deterministic at test time. Noise is injected during training (inputs and hidden units), and targets are adjusted using local gains. Please clarify the terminology and explicitly state whether the deployed policy is deterministic.
- Recurrent units: The abstract emphasizes “a small number of recurrent units,” but Section 4 does not explain how recurrence is implemented and trained. Are there explicit recurrent connections and BPTT, or is “recurrence” only via the r state that is part of the input and output? Please specify the size of r, how ṙ is trained, and whether the network includes recurrent weights.
- Execution-time optimization and “near-optimal feedback control”: At test time the controller relies on solving a T=1 optimization to enforce dynamics and contacts. This is more akin to model-predictive tracking than a pure policy rollout, and “near-optimal feedback controller” may be overstated given the soft constraints, potential root forces, and lack of formal stability guarantees. Clarify the performance guarantees and discuss when the T=1 scheme may fail.
- Evaluation depth: Results are primarily qualitative. Quantitative evaluations would strengthen claims—e.g., tracking error to changing goals, robustness to perturbations and model mismatch, energy/torque usage, contact violation rates, and timing (ms/frame) with breakdown of solve time. Comparisons to MPC beyond a single example (e.g., success rates, smoothness/periodicity metrics) and to model-free baselines on simpler instances would help.
- Generalization: Training data arises from CIO under a fixed cost. It is unclear how well the policy extrapolates to new goals, terrains, perturbations, or parameter variations not seen during trajectory optimization. A held-out test regime (e.g., terrain changes, mass/friction mismatches, stronger pushes) would be informative.
- Organization: Sections 4 (Neural Network Policy) and 6 (Distributed Training Architecture) are disproportionate. Key architectural details (recurrent state, layer sizes, initialization rationale, BPTT or not) should be consolidated and expanded in Section 4, while Section 6 could be condensed.
- Clarity of action/state definitions: The paper states that “the action is velocity” and thus “the network is not learning a control policy but rather the dynamics under an implicit policy.” This is confusing. The network outputs desired velocities/contact forces that, combined with the T=1 optimization, induce controls. Please rephrase to avoid suggesting the network learns system dynamics; it learns a policy over a particular action parameterization.

Quality
Methodologically sound overall, with a principled coupling of trajectory optimization, local gains, and supervised learning, and sensible engineering choices for robustness. However, the empirical evaluation lacks rigorous quantitative analysis and ablations beyond the two reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but would benefit from:
- Stating the full-model assumption early.
- Clarifying the “stochastic policy” vs. “stochastic training” distinction.
- Detailing the recurrent component and training.
- Tightening notation (e.g., clearly distinguishing states x, poses q, sensory s, and memory r throughout).
- Completing and standardizing references.

Originality
Moderate. The approach combines known elements (CIO, noise injection, alternating optimization) in a novel and practically useful way. The feedback-gain derivation that reuses the CIO Hessian is a noteworthy contribution.

Significance
High for computer graphics and interactive character control; moderate for general RL/control. The ability to produce stable, realistic behaviors across diverse morphologies without state machines is valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and diverse qualitative results with a unified method.
- Elegant gain computation and robust training via noise.
- Practical, scalable training pipeline; real-time execution.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on accurate models; soft dynamics at runtime.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and comparisons.
- Ambiguities around recurrence and “stochastic” policy claims.
- Organizational issues and incomplete references.

Questions for the authors
1) How many recurrent units are used; are there true recurrent connections and BPTT?
2) Is the deployed policy deterministic? If so, please avoid calling it “stochastic.”
3) What are runtime timings per frame for the T=1 solve across tasks?
4) How sensitive is performance to λ in the gain computation and to noise scales σε, σγ?
5) How does the policy handle model mismatch (e.g., changed mass/friction) and larger perturbations?

Recommendation
Promising work with strong practical impact for interactive control. With clearer exposition (especially on recurrence and model assumptions) and stronger quantitative evaluation, this paper would be a solid accept. In its current form, I lean toward acceptance conditional on improved clarity and added quantitative results.
