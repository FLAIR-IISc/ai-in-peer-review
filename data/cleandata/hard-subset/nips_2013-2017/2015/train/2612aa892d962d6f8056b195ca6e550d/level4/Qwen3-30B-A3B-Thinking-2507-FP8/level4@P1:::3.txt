

This paper introduces a method for training neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse 3D character animation. The core contribution is a joint training procedure where a recurrent neural network learns to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), a trajectory optimization method, through an alternating optimization scheme. The method also incorporates noise injection during training—both through sensory input perturbations and hidden layer activation noise—which the authors demonstrate is critical for generalization to unseen conditions.

The work builds on prior trajectory optimization approaches like CIO [12] and neural network control methods [9, 11], but makes a significant leap by addressing 3D locomotion across multiple character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network architecture. Previous work had primarily focused on 2D locomotion, while this approach unifies disparate control strategies that traditionally required specialized state machines for different character types. The authors convincingly argue that their method represents a substantial step toward universal character control systems.

The paper's technical quality is strong, with well-supported claims about the importance of the joint training and noise injection components. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 effectively demonstrate that removing either component leads to policies that quickly diverge during execution. The method's ability to handle diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture is particularly impressive and demonstrates significant generalization capabilities.

However, the comparative evaluation is a notable weakness. The authors acknowledge that "a systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done," and this limitation is reflected in the paper. The only meaningful comparison is with Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Figure 3, which is insufficient to establish the method's relative advantages. More comprehensive comparisons with alternative neural network training approaches would substantially strengthen the paper's claims.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some technical details require clarification. Section 3.2, which explains how to compute optimal feedback gains, is particularly confusing due to inconsistent treatment of time indices and unclear notation. The paper would benefit from a more careful explanation of the relationship between different variables and the role of the perturbation parameter λ. Additionally, the paper lacks detailed computational complexity analysis, particularly regarding real-time execution performance. For applications like video games, where computational resources are constrained, understanding how the method scales with character complexity would be valuable.

The originality of the work is strong. While trajectory optimization and neural network control have been explored separately, the specific joint training approach with noise injection represents a novel combination of techniques that enables previously unattainable results on complex 3D locomotion tasks. The method's ability to handle diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture further distinguishes it from previous work.

The significance of the work is promising, with potential applications in character animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The demonstrated success across multiple character types and locomotion styles suggests the method could have broad applicability. However, the paper would benefit from more concrete discussion of how the method might scale to more complex environments or larger character models, and how the computational requirements might limit real-world deployment.

Minor issues include several grammatical errors (e.g., "unity" should be "unify" or "unite"), inconsistent terminology (e.g., "noise injection" versus "data augmentation"), and some technical terms used before definition (e.g., LQG mentioned before definition). The explanation of how the optimal feedback gains are used in the training process could also be clearer.

Overall, this is a technically sound and innovative contribution that addresses a challenging problem in character animation. While the comparative evaluation could be strengthened, the core technical contributions are significant and well-validated. The method represents an important step toward more general, interactive character control systems that can be applied to diverse character morphologies without specialized engineering. The paper would benefit from more detailed comparisons with alternative approaches and clearer explanations of certain technical aspects, but as it stands, it makes a valuable contribution to the field.
