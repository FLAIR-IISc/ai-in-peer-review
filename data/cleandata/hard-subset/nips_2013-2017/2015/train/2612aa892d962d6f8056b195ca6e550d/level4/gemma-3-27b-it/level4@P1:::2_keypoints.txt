- Presents a method using trajectory optimization to generate training data for recurrent neural networks that act as real-time feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems (swimming, flying, legged locomotion).
- Demonstrates a single neural network architecture can control varied characters and behaviors without motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines.
- Builds on established trajectory optimization and policy approximation techniques, emphasizing scalability and generality across morphologies.
- Needs clearer positioning relative to recent reinforcement learning and control work (e.g., Pieter Abbeel, Sergey Levine) to highlight novel contributions.
- Suggests better articulation of concrete advantages (speed, morphology support, runtime cost, robustness) to strengthen impact.
- Requests elaboration on the statement about “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” and its practical implications.
- Recommends reordering to introduce LQG limitations after presenting the perturbation method to clarify motivation.
- Notes confusing notation (bars vs. squiggles) around state/cost expressions and asks for clearer, distinct symbols.
- Asks for a clearer explanation of “softer feedback gains” and why modifying the initial state during optimization helps.
- Strength: Generality — single architecture works across diverse characters and tasks.
- Strength: Scalability — approach handles high-dimensional, challenging control problems.
- Strength: Real-time control — learned policies enable interactive control suitable for applications.
- Strength: Robustness — noise injection during training improves policy robustness.
- Strength: Clear presentation — paper structure and methodology are well explained.
- Weakness: Limited novelty — approach is incremental over existing techniques and needs clearer unique contributions.
- Weakness: Insufficient comparative analysis with recent RL/control baselines weakens claims.
- Weakness: Some technical explanations lack clarity (e.g., soft gains) and need more detail.
- Weakness: Ablation studies need deeper discussion of performance magnitudes and causes.
- Arguments for acceptance: well-executed application, demonstrated generality, thorough experiments, relevance to robotics/animation/biomechanics.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, weak comparative analysis, unclear technical explanations.
- Overall assessment: Solid, practical method showing generality and scalability; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address clarity and comparative analysis.
