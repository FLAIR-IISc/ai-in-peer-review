

This paper introduces a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion. The approach combines trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning, using a joint optimization framework that alternates between optimizing trajectories and training the neural network policy. This work builds on prior trajectory optimization methods like Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12] and extends recent efforts in neural network control [9, 11], but addresses significantly harder three-dimensional locomotion problems where previous work was limited to two-dimensional scenarios.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through its implementation of key innovations: interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, training for unexpected task changes, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across diverse character morphologies, showing stable and realistic behaviors without motion capture or task-specific features. The method's implementation is technically sophisticated, with a distributed training architecture that scales efficiently to complex problems.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-organized sections that clearly explain the methodology and results. The writing appropriately balances technical detail with accessibility for the target audience. The authors effectively demonstrate the claimed advantages of sensory noise injection and joint trajectory-policy learning, with compelling comparative results showing significant performance improvements over naive static training approaches.

A minor concern relates to Section 7's explanation of the need for additional trajectory optimization during policy execution. The paper states that "physical state evolution is a result of optimization" (Section 7), but the rationale for why the policy cannot directly generate stable behaviors without this additional optimization step is not sufficiently detailed. The authors might clarify whether residual trajectory costs or policy regression errors necessitate this step, and why the forward evolution of dynamics doesn't inherently prevent error accumulation.

The reference to "similar to recent approaches" (line 153) would be strengthened by citing one or two specific examples of comparable methods that use proximal regularization. While the paper correctly identifies that keeping parameter updates small is useful in this alternating optimization framework, providing concrete references would better contextualize this approach within the literature.

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of character control, demonstrating how neural networks can learn to generate realistic behaviors for diverse character morphologies without task-specific features. While the authors successfully show the method's effectiveness, the significance could be further strengthened by more explicitly comparing their approach to related work in character control, such as [13, 21], to clarify how this work advances beyond previous domain-specific controllers for bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion.

The paper's technical execution is impressive, with a well-structured methodology, thorough experimental validation, and clear presentation of results. While the research area is somewhat specialized, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field of neural network-based control, particularly in demonstrating the feasibility of applying these techniques to complex three-dimensional locomotion tasks.
