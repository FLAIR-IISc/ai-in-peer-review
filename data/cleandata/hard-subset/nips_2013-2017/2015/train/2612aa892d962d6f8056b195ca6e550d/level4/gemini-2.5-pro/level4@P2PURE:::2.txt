The paper "Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates how a small, fast neural network can represent a control policy for the interactive control of characters within a physics simulator. The proposed method employs trajectory optimization during a training phase to learn this neural network policy. The primary contribution is showing that a single network architecture can generalize across a variety of characters and a range of quasi-cyclic behaviors.

**General Comments**

This paper appears to be an application of deep learning to established, albeit recent, techniques from computer graphics. The use of trajectory optimization to discover realistic control signals is well-established in prior work, including some of the cited references. Similarly, the use of neural networks to approximate a policy that reproduces trajectories found via optimization has also been explored previously (e.g., [9]).

Given this context, the authors need to more clearly articulate the novelty of their contribution in relation to these previously published approaches. For instance, a comparison to the recent work by Levine and Abbeel would be particularly insightful. How does the proposed method compare in terms of learning speed, applicability to diverse character morphologies, runtime computational cost, or robustness to environmental perturbations?

**Specific Comments**

*   Could the authors clarify the statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains"?

*   The paper's clarity could be improved by moving the discussion of why LQG was not used to a point after the proposed strategy has been fully presented.

*   The notation used between lines 226-240 is confusing. For example, you introduce `s(X) = \bar s` and then define a cost in terms of `s(X) - \tilde s`. The use of visually similar diacritics (bars and tildes) for distinct variables makes the formulation difficult to follow.

*   The paper claims that the proposed approach "produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ". This statement needs clarification. Softer compared to what baseline? What is the technical definition of "soft" in the context of feedback gains?

*   Could the authors provide a justification for modifying the initial state as part of the optimization procedure?

In summary, the work appears technically sound, but its contribution relative to recent related work is not sufficiently clear.
