

This submission presents an algorithm for real-time 3D model control using a neural network (NN) trained offline to replicate Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs. Critically, CIO and NN training are interdependent, performed iteratively where each step informs the other's optimization criterion. A key innovation involves noise injection via data augmentation through small perturbations and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate the network's ability to generate realistic, stable control policies across diverse character models.  

This approach is compelling for real-time control of arbitrary models, a significant challenge. While the core idea ("learn a NN to predict CIO output") seems straightforward, the joint training and noise injection represent substantial novel contributions, validated experimentally to significantly enhance generalization.  

However, the experimental validation would be strengthened by comparisons using a larger dataset. Given the base training trajectories were generated from only "100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments," the overfitting observed in the "no noise" variant is unsurprising. The "no joint" variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories.  

Overall, the comparative evaluation is the paper's weakest aspect (acknowledged in the introduction: "A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The sole comparison to a competing method (MPC) is brief and lacks a reference.  

The mathematical exposition is generally clear and well-motivated, though replication would require additional references, particularly for the CIO step (e.g., Eq. 6's undefined notations). Code availability would be highly appreciated. A specific confusion arises in Section 3.2 regarding optimal action generation for noisy inputs: notation becomes ambiguous (e.g., the time index "t" is dropped but unclear if implied for some quantities).  

Time measurements for real-time computation would be valuable, as CPU constraints are critical in applications like video games.
