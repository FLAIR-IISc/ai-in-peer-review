- Presents a method that alternates Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization with supervised learning of recurrent policies to produce near-optimal feedback controllers.
- Policy outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent memory updates, effectively learning feedback and an implicit local dynamics model.
- Injects noise in inputs and hidden activations to encourage robustness; extracts well-conditioned feedback gains via a perturbation-based reuse of Gauss-Newton structure.
- Training is distributed for trajectory generation and GPU-accelerated for neural regression; execution uses a one-step constrained optimization at each control step.
- Demonstrates diverse 3D behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal/quadrupedal locomotion) with a single architecture and pipeline, without motion capture or hand-tuned state machines.
- Builds on prior CIO and trajectory-to-policy learning work, differing from model-free RL by leveraging trajectory optimization and explicit feedback gains.
- Compared to MPC, the joint optimization regularizes trajectories toward predictable, cyclic behaviors suitable for interactive control.
- Formulation is clear and technically sound; the gain extraction avoids an ill-conditioned LQG pass and is computationally efficient.
- Distributed training loop with proximal terms is plausible and shows empirical convergence.
- Empirical results cover multiple morphologies and tasks; ablations show joint optimization and noise injection are important.
- Key limitation: evaluation is mainly qualitative with limited quantitative metrics (tracking error, stability, energy, task success).
- Missing systematic comparisons to strong MPC and model-free RL baselines on identical tasks and compute budgets.
- Execution-time reliance on a soft-constrained T=1 optimization that can include root forces raises questions about physical fidelity and real-robot transfer.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (λ, α, η, noise scales) and data requirements is only lightly explored.
- Paper is generally well written with clear explanations; minor typos, notation inconsistencies, and missing cost/training details affect reproducibility.
- Integration of known components at scale and efficient feedback gain extraction are novel and compelling contributions.
- Method is significant for producing robust, interactive controllers for contact-rich characters and is likely to impact animation and robotics.
- Strengths: diverse 3D results, principled coupling of CIO and neural regression, efficient gain computation, scalable training, and clear ablations.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: add quantitative metrics, head-to-head baselines, clarify constraint satisfaction/removal of nonphysical forces, analyze hyperparameter sensitivity, and provide more reproducibility details or code.
- Arguments for acceptance: robust realistic 3D control, useful technical contribution on gain extraction, practical and scalable method with compelling results.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient quantitative evaluation and baselines, and potential reliance on simulation-specific tricks limiting transferability.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept—excellent, polished work with significant contributions despite needing deeper quantitative comparisons and execution-time physics clarification.
