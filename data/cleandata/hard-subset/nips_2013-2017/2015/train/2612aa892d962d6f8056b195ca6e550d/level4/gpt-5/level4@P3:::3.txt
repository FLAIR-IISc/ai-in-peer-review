Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approach for real-time control of complex 3D characters by training a recurrent neural network (NN) to mimic the outputs of a trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The method alternates between trajectory optimization and NN regression: CIO generates trajectories and optimal feedback gains; the NN is trained to map noisy sensory inputs to corresponding actions that include linearized feedback terms. Noise is injected both in the inputs (with targets adjusted using feedback gains) and in the NN’s hidden activations to encourage robustness. At test time, the learned policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces; a one-step optimization enforces physics constraints, enabling interactive control at real-time rates. Results span swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion, with a single network architecture and training procedure.

This fits squarely within recent work on learning control policies via trajectory optimization and supervised learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014), and is complementary to model-free policy search (e.g., TRPO) and MPC. The paper’s contributions are an interleaved optimization/training scheme, principled input noise with trajectory-derived feedback gains, and a distributed asynchronous training setup. Relative to prior work, the paper targets more diverse morphologies and full 3D locomotion, and emphasizes stability and visual plausibility.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling goal: real-time, interactive controllers for diverse characters without hand-designed state machines or motion capture.
- Thoughtful integration of CIO with NN training. Using feedback gains to define targets for noisy inputs is technically appealing and addresses closed-loop stability.
- Demonstrated breadth: swimming, flying, bipeds, quadrupeds; cyclic behaviors emerge without explicit gait templates.
- Practical system engineering: cloud-based distributed CIO with GPU training; a one-step physics-consistent execution scheme.
- Ablations indicate that both the joint training and noise are important for test-time stability.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Comparative evaluation is limited. Beyond qualitative MPC comparison, there are no quantitative baselines vs. modern policy-search methods (e.g., guided policy search, TRPO) or even a robustly tuned MPC. The authors themselves note that a systematic comparison remains to be done; this leaves the performance gains and generality hard to assess.
- Dataset scale. The training set (100–200 trials with 5 branches each) is modest given task complexity. The severe overfitting of the “no noise” variant could be unsurprising under such data scarcity; stronger evidence would come from larger datasets and/or stronger regularization baselines. It would also help to separate “no data augmentation via input noise” from “no hidden-layer noise” to understand their distinct effects.
- Section 3.2 is difficult to follow. The notation for perturbed optima and the role of the time index t are unclear. The derivation of as via soft constraints is interesting, but some steps (use of Woodbury identity, definitions of sX and aX) require more explanation to be reproducible.
- Real-time performance is not quantified. Since execution requires solving a constrained T=1 optimization, runtime statistics and scaling with model complexity are essential for practitioners (e.g., games/robotics).
- Algorithmic mismatch. Algorithm 1 describes strict alternation; Section 6 implements an asynchronous scheme with proximal terms. Please reconcile these (e.g., early mention that you use an asynchronous block-coordinate method with proximal regularization).
- Clarity issues and minor inconsistencies (see editorial notes below) impede reproducibility. CIO constraints (Eq. 6) use undefined notation, and several equations contain typos.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a well-motivated combination of CIO and supervised NN training, and a neat derivation for feedback-aware data augmentation. The engineering is solid. However, the empirical support is weakened by limited baselines and dataset scale, and by missing runtime/scaling data. Section 3.2 needs clearer, fully specified notation to ensure reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized, but several sections require clarification:
- Precisely define all symbols in Eq. 6 and elsewhere (e.g., H, C, J, d, K).
- In Sec. 3.2, clarify whether quantities remain time-indexed, define C̃, ensure argmin notation is correct, and spell out sX and aX in relation to φX.
- Explain the one-step execution problem (Sec. 7) more carefully, including whether fdes is intended and how soft constraints may introduce root forces.

Originality
Combining CIO with feedback-aware noise injection in an alternating, distributed training loop is a meaningful and novel integration of existing ideas. The application to diverse 3D characters with a single architecture is ambitious and practically valuable.

Significance
If made robust and reproducible, the approach could be influential for physics-based animation and possibly robotics. Demonstrating competitive performance against strong baselines and providing runtime evidence would significantly boost impact.

Requests for clarification
- Targets and orientation: Are goals only positions, or is final orientation also targeted? If not, can an orientation objective be added easily?
- Initialization: In the very first iteration of Alg. 1, are trajectories optimized with θ initialized randomly, or without the NN-regularization term?
- Hyperparameters: Why use the same η in both subproblems? Any schedule for α (e.g., decreasing toward zero to reduce policy coupling), and is there a curriculum effect?
- Terminology: The input-noise procedure adjusts targets using feedback gains; this is closer to data augmentation than to denoising autoencoders. Please clarify intended terminology and connection.
- Sensory/action features: You state sX and aX are subsets of φX; please justify and make the mapping explicit.
- Real-time compute: Provide timing per control step, dependency on number of DoF/contacts, and hardware used.

Editorial and minor comments
- “biped location” → “biped locomotion.”
- “unity these disparate approaches” → “unify.”
- Add a reference for the MPC baseline.
- LQG is used before being defined; also “a Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass.”
- Eq. 9–Sec. 3.2: ensure C̃ includes s̄; in “argmin∗X C̃(X∗)” the star superscripts look misplaced.
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around [the] optimal trajectory.”
- Algorithm 1: the NN step minimizes the NN objective, not Eq. (4) in full; please be precise.
- Section 6’s asynchronous scheme should be foreshadowed when Alg. 1 is introduced.
- Sec. 7: in the execution optimization, f should be fdes; clarify the sentence about soft enforcement of dynamics possibly introducing root forces.
- Table 1 caption: number of layers appears inconsistent (3 vs. 4).

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, unified approach to interactive control across diverse morphologies.
- Elegant feedback-aware data augmentation and robust training recipe.
- Strong qualitative results and plausible engineering path to deployment.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited quantitative baselines; unclear advantages over GPS/TRPO/MPC.
- Small training datasets; ablations could be more granular.
- Missing runtime/scaling data; some derivations and notations are hard to follow.

Overall
A compelling and well-engineered approach with promising results. I recommend acceptance contingent on strengthening the empirical comparison (at least one strong baseline), clarifying Sec. 3.2, and reporting real-time performance and scaling.
