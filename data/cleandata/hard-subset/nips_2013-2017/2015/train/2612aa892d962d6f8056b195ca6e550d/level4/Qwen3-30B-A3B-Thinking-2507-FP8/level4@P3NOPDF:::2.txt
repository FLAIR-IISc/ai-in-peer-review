

This paper investigates the use of a compact neural network to represent a control policy for interactive character control in physics-based simulations, leveraging trajectory optimization during training to generate policies applicable across diverse cyclic behaviors and body morphologies. While the technical execution is sound, the paper's contribution relative to established methods requires clearer articulation to justify its significance.

**General Comments**  
The core approach—using neural networks to approximate control policies derived via trajectory optimization—builds directly on well-established techniques in computer graphics and control. The ability of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals is well-documented in prior work (e.g., [7, 8]), and the use of neural networks to approximate step-by-step actions for trajectory reproduction has been explored in recent literature (e.g., [9]). The authors must more explicitly position their work within this landscape. Specifically:  
- How does this method advance beyond Abbeel and Levine’s trajectory optimization frameworks (e.g., [1, 2])?  
- Is the primary contribution faster policy learning? Broader morphological generalization? Reduced runtime computation? Enhanced robustness to environmental noise?  
Without addressing these questions, the novelty remains unclear. The paper risks appearing as an incremental application rather than a meaningful step forward.  

**Specific Comments**  
1. **Contribution Clarity**: The claim that "the same architecture works across a range of body types and behaviors" is underspecified. The paper should quantify *how* the architecture generalizes (e.g., number of body types tested, behavioral diversity metrics) and contrast this explicitly with prior work (e.g., [1, 2, 9]). Without such comparison, the assertion lacks impact.  

2. **Terminology & Notation**:  
   - The phrase "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" (Section 3.1) requires clarification. What defines "large" or "ill-conditioned" in this context? Provide concrete examples or metrics (e.g., condition number thresholds).  
   - The notation around lines 226–240 is ambiguous. The use of $\bar{s}$ (bar) and $s(X)$ (squiggle) creates visual confusion. Standardize notation (e.g., use $\hat{s}$ for target, $s(X)$ for state) or define symbols explicitly before use.  
   - "Softer feedback gains according to parameter $\lambda$" (Section 3.2) lacks context. Softer than *what*? How does this property improve performance? Link "softness" to a measurable outcome (e.g., reduced jerk, smoother motion, or enhanced stability).  

3. **Logical Flow**: The discussion of LQG (Section 3.3) should follow the presentation of the authors’ strategy, not precede it. Relocate this to after describing the proposed method to avoid confusing readers with comparative analysis before the core contribution is clear.  

4. **Runtime Efficiency Claim**: The paper asserts reduced runtime computation but provides no evidence (e.g., inference speed benchmarks vs. trajectory optimization). Include concrete numbers (e.g., "10× faster than [9] on 1000-step tasks") to substantiate this claim.  

5. **Environmental Robustness**: The paper mentions robustness to "environmental variabilities" but offers no experiments or metrics (e.g., perturbation tolerance, failure rates under noise). This must be addressed to support the claim.  

**Recommendation**  
The work is technically competent and demonstrates a plausible approach for interactive character control. However, the contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from existing trajectory optimization and neural policy methods. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
- Explicitly compare their method to Abbeel/Levine’s work (e.g., [1, 2]) across key dimensions: learning speed, generalization scope, runtime cost, and robustness.  
- Clarify ambiguous terms ("soft gains," "ill-conditioned") with definitions and metrics.  
- Provide quantitative evidence for all claims (e.g., generalization metrics, runtime comparisons).  
- Restructure the LQG discussion to follow method presentation.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s significance remains unclear. It would benefit from a stronger narrative positioning it as a *solution to a specific gap* in prior work—not merely an application of known techniques. As currently written, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative for the field.
