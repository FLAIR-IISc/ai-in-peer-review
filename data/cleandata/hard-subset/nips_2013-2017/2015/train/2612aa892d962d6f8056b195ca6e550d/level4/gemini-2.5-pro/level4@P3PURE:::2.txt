This paper demonstrates how a small, fast neural network can represent a control policy for the interactive control of characters in a physics simulator. The approach uses trajectory optimization during a training phase to learn this neural network policy. The main contribution is showing that a single architecture can be successfully applied across a range of quasi-cyclic behaviors and diverse character body types.

### General Comments

The work presented appears to be an application of deep learning to established, albeit recent, techniques in computer graphics. The ability of trajectory optimization to find realistic control signals has been established by previous work, some of which is cited by the authors. Similarly, the use of neural networks to approximate a policy that reproduces trajectories found via optimization has also been explored in prior work (e.g., [9]).

Given this existing research, the authors should more clearly position their contributions relative to these previously published approaches. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed comparison to the recent line of work by Levine and Abbeel, which seems closely related. The authors should clarify the specific advantages of their method over such work. For instance, does it learn faster, support a wider variety of body shapes, require less computation at runtime, or offer greater robustness to environmental variations?

While the work appears technically sound, its contribution relative to other recent, related work is not made sufficiently clear.

### Specific Comments

*   The authors state, "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." This claim requires further explanation. Under what conditions does this occur, and what are the specific consequences?

*   The paper's structure could be improved. Consider moving the discussion of why LQG was not used to a point after the proposed strategy has been fully presented, as this would provide better context for the reader.

*   The notation around lines 226-240 is confusing. The paper introduces `s(X) = \bar{s}` and subsequently defines a cost in terms of `s(X) - \bar{s}`. The use of visually similar notation, such as bars and squiggles, is ambiguous and hinders the clarity of the mathematical formulation.

*   The paper claims the proposed approach "produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ." This statement needs to be clarified. What is the baseline for comparison (i.e., softer than what)? Furthermore, the authors should provide a precise definition of what constitutes "soft" feedback gains in this context.

*   The authors should provide a justification for modifying the initial state as part of the optimization procedure. What is the benefit of this step?
