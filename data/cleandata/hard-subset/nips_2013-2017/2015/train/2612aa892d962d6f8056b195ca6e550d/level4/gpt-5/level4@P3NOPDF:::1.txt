This paper presents a principled strategy for embedding feedback gains into neural network policies, yielding controllers that are robust to disturbances and regression error. The core idea is to softly couple trajectory optimization and policy learning via a deviation penalty: the policy is trained to track optimized trajectories while being encouraged to remain close to them, yet it retains the flexibility to deviate when doing so reduces cost or improves robustness. The result is a family of controllers that approximate optimal trajectories but are simpler and more robust than controllers derived solely from MPC-based trajectory optimization. The approach is implemented with substantial parallelization to scale training and evaluation, and it is demonstrated across a diverse set of robot morphologies and tasks.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated problem formulation. The paper articulates a compelling case for integrating model-based trajectory optimization with learned feedback policies, addressing known fragilities of open-loop or narrowly tuned MPC controllers in the presence of modeling errors and disturbances.
- Technical contribution. Embedding feedback gains within the policy architecture and coupling them to trajectory optimization through a soft penalty is elegant and practical. It balances fidelity to optimized plans with robustness and implementability, and it offers a clean path to amortizing control via learned policies.
- Empirical depth and breadth. The experimental suite spans diverse robots and tasks, convincingly demonstrating generality. Results show that the learned feedback controllers are not only competitive with but often simpler and more robust than standalone MPC products, particularly under perturbations and imperfect modeling.
- Clarity and presentation quality. The paper is exceptionally well written. The methodological choices, optimization objectives, and experimental setups are explained with care, and the figures and results are high quality. The narrative makes a strong case for the design decisions and their effect on outcomes.
- Practical relevance. The combination of trajectory optimization, structured feedback, and neural policies will be of broad interest to the NIPS community spanning control, robotics, and machine learning. The demonstrated parallelization shows an awareness of computational bottlenecks and how to address them.

Questions and suggestions
- Architectural specifics. Please clarify how feedback gains are embedded in the policy. Are they fixed (e.g., LQR-derived) and exposed as inputs, or learned jointly with the network? Is the structure equivalent to a PD/LQR correction around a nominal trajectory with a learned residual, or something else?
- Coupling strength and sensitivity. The deviation penalty is central to the approach. How sensitive are results to its weighting? An ablation varying this coefficient would help illuminate the trade-off between fidelity to the trajectory and robustness/expressivity of the policy.
- Comparisons and ablations. It would strengthen the case to include:
  - A baseline that imitates optimal trajectories without explicit feedback gains (pure supervised learning), to isolate the benefit of embedded feedback.
  - A version without soft coupling (e.g., independently trained policy with no deviation penalty) to quantify what the coupling contributes.
- Robustness analysis. While the empirical robustness is compelling, any additional analysis would be valuable. For example, do you have bounds or Lyapunov-style arguments under reasonable assumptions, or at least a discussion of why the embedded structure should improve stability margins?
- Disturbance and model mismatch. How do the policies generalize to disturbances or dynamics mismatches not seen during training? Is robustness primarily local around the nominal trajectory, or does it extend to larger deviations and unseen conditions?
- Computational cost and scalability. The paper leverages significant parallelization. Please provide a concrete accounting (wall-clock time, CPU/GPU hours, number of rollouts) to help readers assess practicality. How does performance scale with added parallel resources, and what are the main bottlenecks?
- Real-world transfer. If any results are in simulation, do you have evidence of sim-to-real transfer or a discussion of what would be required (e.g., domain randomization, dynamics identification)? If some results are on hardware, clarifying setup and safety considerations would be helpful.
- Failure modes and limitations. The paper is very strong; acknowledging where the method struggles (e.g., highly discontinuous contact dynamics, sparse rewards, long-horizon credit assignment) would further increase credibility and guide future work.
- Reproducibility. Providing code, hyperparameters, and trajectory datasets would significantly increase impact. Even high-level pseudocode for the joint optimization and a schematic of the policy architecture would benefit readers.

Overall assessment
This is an excellent paper: technically sound, clearly written, and empirically convincing. It offers a compelling integration of trajectory optimization and neural feedback policies that improves robustness while maintaining practicality and scalability. The results are beautiful and inspiring, and the work will likely influence how the community thinks about blending model-based and learned control. I strongly recommend acceptance.
