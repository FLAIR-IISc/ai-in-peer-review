

This paper presents a novel approach for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion in three-dimensional space. The authors combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate controllers that do not require motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines. Instead, the neural network learns a state-action mapping that directly commands velocity and contact forces, effectively learning the dynamics under an implicit policy. The method incorporates several key technical improvements: interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, training for unexpected task changes, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of character animation and control. Previous work in neural network controllers for animation has largely been limited to 2D environments [9, 11], with more chaotic behaviors emerging from direct reinforcement learning approaches [3, 6, 14]. The current work successfully extends these methods to 3D locomotion with realistic, stable behaviors across multiple character morphologies. The authors' approach represents a meaningful advancement over prior work that required specialized controllers for different character types (e.g., state machines for bipeds [21, 18], contact schedules for quadrupeds [13], and explicit cyclic encodings for flying/swimming [8, 7]).

**Strengths:**
- **Quality**: The method is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide thorough theoretical justification for their approach, including a principled method for computing optimal feedback gains. The experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements over baseline methods (two orders of magnitude better performance than static training), with clear ablation studies showing the importance of noise injection and joint optimization.
- **Originality**: The paper presents a novel combination of techniques that significantly extends prior work. The method's ability to handle diverse 3D character locomotion without task-specific hand-crafted controllers represents a meaningful advancement beyond previous NIPS papers in this area.
- **Significance**: The results are highly significant for both animation and robotics. The approach enables interactive real-time control for diverse character types with a single controller architecture, potentially reducing the need for manual controller design. The method could impact applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized overall, with clear descriptions of the methodology and results. The supplementary video effectively demonstrates the capabilities of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Clarity (Minor)**: Section 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) does not adequately address the role of recurrent units mentioned in the abstract, creating a disconnect between the paper's claims and technical implementation. The authors should clarify how the recurrent memory states are incorporated into the network architecture.
- **Technical Detail**: The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of the requirement for full model knowledge (Eq. 6). While the authors mention this in the draft review, it's not clearly stated in the main paper.
- **Disproportionate Coverage**: Sections 4 and 6 are disproportionately detailed compared to other sections, particularly given the paper's main contribution. The neural network training details (Section 4) could be condensed to better match the emphasis on the core methodological contribution.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*For Acceptance:*
- The paper presents a significant technical advancement in neural network controllers for complex 3D character locomotion, solving problems that previous work could not.
- The method is generalizable across diverse character morphologies without task-specific hand-crafting.
- The results are well-supported by thorough experimental validation and ablation studies.
- The approach has clear potential impact for animation, robotics, and biomechanics.

*Against Acceptance:*
- The paper would benefit from clearer explanation of the recurrent architecture's role in the neural network.
- The requirement for full model knowledge (Eq. 6) is critical but not sufficiently emphasized in the main text.
- The disproportionate detail in Sections 4 and 6 detracts from the paper's central contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of neural network controllers for character animation. The method successfully extends previous work to solve substantially harder problems in 3D locomotion with realistic, stable behaviors across diverse character types. While the paper has minor clarity issues that should be addressed, the technical contribution is significant and well-supported. The results demonstrate clear advantages over prior approaches, particularly in handling complex 3D environments where previous methods failed.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should clarify the role of recurrent units in the neural network architecture, explicitly state the requirement for full model knowledge, and slightly condense the neural network training details to better match the paper's central contribution. These revisions would strengthen an already compelling technical contribution that advances the state of the art in neural network controllers for interactive character animation.
