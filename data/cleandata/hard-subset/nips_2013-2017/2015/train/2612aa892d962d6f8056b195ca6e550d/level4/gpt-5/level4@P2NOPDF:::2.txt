“Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks” presents a method for using a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive character control in a physics simulator. Policies are trained by imitating trajectories obtained from trajectory optimization. The main claim is that a single architecture can drive a variety of largely cyclic behaviors across multiple character morphologies.

General comments
- The paper reads as an application of deep networks to a trajectory-optimization-based control pipeline that is already well established in graphics and robotics. Prior work has shown that trajectory optimization can yield realistic control signals, and that neural networks can approximate per-step policy actions to track such trajectories (e.g., [9]). The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is new beyond integrating these components.
- Please position the contribution more precisely relative to the line of work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine (e.g., guided policy search and related trajectory-optimization–to–policy-learning methods). What practical advantages does your approach offer? For example:
  - Faster training or less supervision?
  - Better generalization across body types or behaviors?
  - Lower runtime cost or latency?
  - Increased robustness to model errors or environmental variability?
  - Simpler implementation or fewer tuning parameters?
- If your key novelty is the breadth of behaviors and morphologies supported by a single compact network, quantify this advantage and compare to strong baselines from that literature.

Specific comments
- “We found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”: Please clarify which process produces them, under what conditions, and how you diagnose ill-conditioning. Provide quantitative ranges (e.g., gain norms or condition numbers), discuss the impact on stability or performance, and describe any mitigation you use.
- Consider moving the discussion of why LQG was not used until after you have presented your method. The current placement disrupts the flow, and readers will better appreciate the trade-offs once they understand your approach.
- The notation around lines 226–240 is hard to parse. You introduce s(X) = s̄, then define a cost using s(X) − s̄. Because the symbols are visually similar, this is easy to misread. Consider using a different symbol for the target (e.g., s*, ŝ) and/or for the function (e.g., f(X)) to make the objective unambiguous.
- “Our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ”: Please define “softer” precisely. Do you mean smaller gain norms, better conditioning, reduced sensitivity, or smoother control signals? Softer relative to what baseline (e.g., LQG, iLQR)? Explain how λ affects the magnitude/conditioning of gains and the stability–performance trade-off, and show this effect empirically.
- Why is altering the initial state part of your procedure, and what benefit does it provide? Clarify how initial states are chosen or adapted, whether this biases the learned policy, and how it affects reproducibility and robustness. If this is essential for stabilizing training or improving exploration/coverage, please justify and quantify it.

Overall assessment
- The system appears technically solid and the demonstrations are compelling. However, the novelty and contribution relative to closely related recent work are not yet clear enough. A sharper positioning against prior trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning (especially Abbeel/Levine’s work), clearer exposition of your design choices (LQG avoidance, gain regularization via λ, initial state handling), and quantitative comparisons would strengthen the paper.
