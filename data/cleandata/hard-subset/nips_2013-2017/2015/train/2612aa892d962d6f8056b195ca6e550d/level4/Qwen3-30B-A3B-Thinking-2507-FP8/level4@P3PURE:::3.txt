

The authors propose a novel algorithm for real-time 3D character control using a neural network (NN) trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Crucially, CIO and NN training are not independent but performed in an alternating fashion, with each step's output serving as the criterion for the other. A key innovation is the injection of noise via data augmentation (small perturbations) and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate the method's ability to generate realistic, stable control policies across diverse character models.

This approach is highly relevant for real-time control of arbitrary 3D characters, a challenging problem. While the core idea ("learn a NN to predict CIO output") appears straightforward, the joint training procedure and noise injection represent significant novel contributions, substantially improving generalization as evidenced by experiments.

However, the experimental evaluation has notable weaknesses. The base training trajectories were generated using only 100–200 trials, each with 5 branched segments. Consequently, the overfitting observed in the "no noise" variant is unsurprising. The "no joint" variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories. Critically, the comparative evaluation is the paper's weakest point (acknowledged in the introduction: "A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The sole comparison to a competing method (MPC) is brief and lacks a proper reference.

The mathematical exposition is generally clear and well-motivated, though replication would require consulting additional references, particularly for the CIO step (e.g., notations in Eq. 6 are undefined). Code availability would greatly benefit the community. A specific point of confusion is Section 3.2 on generating optimal actions for noisy inputs; the notation becomes ambiguous regarding time indices (e.g., whether "t" is implied for certain quantities).

Additional practical considerations are missing: real-time computation time measurements (critical for applications like video games with limited CPU), scaling with 3D model complexity, and whether the target specifies only x,y,z coordinates (with no facing angle constraint). Adding a target angle constraint would be valuable for practical applications.

Minor corrections and clarifications are needed:
- "biped location" → "biped locomotion"
- "unity" → "unify"
- Clarify if target includes facing angle constraints (and feasibility of adding one)
- "Noise injection" misrepresents the data augmentation step (where targets are recomputed for perturbed inputs); this is simply data augmentation, distinct from denoising autoencoder principles
- Clarify Algorithm 1: Are initial X's computed using random weights θ or without regularization?
- Justify using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Algorithm 1
- "asynchronous and stochastic setting we have" → "asynchronous and stochastic setting we have" (add "the")
- Define LQG before first use
- "use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass" → "use a Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass"
- Correct notation in line 231 (star position, missing \bar{s} in \tilde{C})
- "minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory" → "minimizer of a quadratic approximation around the optimal trajectory"
- Justify why "sX and aX are subsets of \phiX"
- "a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)" → "a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in Algorithm 1 (as only θ is optimized)"
- Note that the asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs significantly from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1; this should be mentioned earlier
- Line 315: f should be f^{des}
- "dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces" is unclear
- Split "no noise" variant into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection"
- Investigate if α can be decreased to zero during CIO optimization (to test curriculum learning vs. necessity of CIO trajectory proximity)
- Table 1a caption states "4 layers" but refers to 3 layers

Despite these concerns, the work presents a compelling application of neural networks to character control. Its practical benefits and broader applicability remain to be fully established.
