

Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a variety of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion. The authors use trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate training data, then train neural networks to approximate the control policies that produce these behaviors. The key innovation is demonstrating that a single neural network architecture can control diverse character morphologies without task-specific features, state machines, or motion capture data. This work builds on recent advances in trajectory optimization for character animation [12, 9, 11], extending them to three-dimensional complex behaviors while maintaining real-time interactive control.

The paper makes several significant contributions to the field. First, it successfully applies neural network approximation to trajectory optimization solutions, enabling interactive control without requiring motion capture or manual controller design. Second, it demonstrates generalization across diverse character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single architecture. Third, it introduces practical techniques for training robust controllers, including noise injection during training, trajectory optimization with feedback gains, and distributed training on cloud infrastructure. The experimental results are compelling, showing stable locomotion across various character types in 3D environments, which is notably more challenging than previous 2D work.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The technical approach is well-motivated and carefully executed, with thorough experimental validation across multiple character types. The authors provide clear explanations of their methodological innovations, including the distributed stochastic optimization algorithm, noise injection techniques, and trajectory optimization with feedback gains. The implementation details are sufficiently detailed to allow reproduction, and the supplementary video effectively demonstrates the quality of the generated behaviors. The paper's contribution to the field is significant in demonstrating how trajectory optimization and neural networks can be combined to solve complex control problems with diverse character morphologies.

However, the paper would benefit from a clearer positioning relative to recent related work, particularly the line of work by Levine and Abbeel [9, 11]. While the authors cite this work, they do not sufficiently articulate how their approach differs or improves upon it. Specifically, it would be valuable to clarify: (1) whether this method learns faster than direct reinforcement learning approaches; (2) how it handles a wider variety of body shapes compared to previous work; (3) whether it requires less computation at runtime; and (4) whether it is more robust to certain environmental variabilities. Without these distinctions, it's difficult to fully appreciate the paper's novelty.

The presentation of certain technical details could also be improved. For example, the discussion of feedback gains in Section 3.2 is somewhat confusing, particularly the transition from defining s(X) = s̄ to using this in the augmented cost function. The notation with bars and squiggles is visually ambiguous, and the explanation of "softer feedback gains" would benefit from a clearer comparison to the LQG approach they're trying to improve upon. The statement that "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ" is unclear—softer than what? What does "soft" mean in this context, and why is it beneficial?

The paper's contribution relative to previous trajectory optimization + neural network approaches [9, 11] needs stronger justification. While the authors note that previous work was limited to 2D characters, they don't thoroughly explain why their approach solves the 3D problem where others failed. A more detailed comparison of the specific technical improvements that enable this extension would strengthen the paper's contribution.

The experimental evaluation is strong overall, but the comparative analysis in Section 8.1 could be expanded. The ablation study comparing joint optimization to static training is convincing, but it would be valuable to include a comparison to other recent methods for real-time character control, such as model predictive control (MPC), to better position the paper's contribution. The observation that MPC produces non-cyclical behaviors while their method produces cyclic gaits is interesting but could be developed further to explain why this matters for the specific application domain.

Overall, this is a solid piece of work with significant practical value for character animation and robotics. The technical execution is strong, the results are impressive, and the method is well-motivated. With clearer positioning relative to recent related work and some refinements to the presentation of technical details, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS. The authors should address the questions raised about how their approach differs from and improves upon existing trajectory optimization + neural network methods, particularly in terms of scalability to 3D environments and generalization across character morphologies.
