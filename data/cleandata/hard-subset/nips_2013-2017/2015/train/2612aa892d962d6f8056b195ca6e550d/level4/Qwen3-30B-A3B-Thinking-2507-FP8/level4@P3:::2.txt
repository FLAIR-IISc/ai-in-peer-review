

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex character behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking) without requiring motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines. The approach combines trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) with neural network policy learning, using a principled interleaving of supervised learning with trajectory optimization, noise injection during training, and training for unexpected task changes. The core insight is that the network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously, with the action defined as velocity rather than a control policy.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions in several important ways. It builds upon the trajectory optimization approach of Mordatch et al. (2012) and Levine & Koltun (2014) while extending their work to three-dimensional locomotion across diverse character morphologies. The authors' approach differs from prior work by focusing on a unified architecture applicable to multiple character types and behaviors, whereas previous approaches typically required specialized controllers for different character types (e.g., bipedal vs. quadrupedal locomotion). The paper also connects to recent neural network control work (e.g., Huh & Todorov, 2013; Mnih et al., 2013), though it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from these approaches.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with a well-motivated optimization procedure and thorough experimental validation across multiple character types. The authors provide convincing evidence of stable, realistic behavior generation for diverse characters in 3D environments, which is significantly more challenging than previous 2D work. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) effectively demonstrate the necessity of the key components: joint optimization, noise injection, and training for unexpected changes. However, the paper would benefit from a more systematic comparison with alternative approaches like model-predictive control (MPC) and direct reinforcement learning methods (e.g., Schulman et al., 2015) to better establish the advantages of the proposed method.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though some notation (e.g., around lines 226-240) could be clarified. The explanation of optimal feedback gains and their relationship to the perturbation method would be improved with a more intuitive description of what "soft" gains mean and why they're beneficial. The discussion of how the method differs from prior work (particularly Levine & Koltun, 2014) is currently underdeveloped, making it difficult to assess the paper's true contribution.

**Originality:** The main contribution—demonstrating that a single neural network architecture can control diverse character morphologies across multiple locomotion tasks without task-specific tuning—represents a meaningful extension of prior work. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently articulate how this differs from or improves upon previous approaches. For example, how does this method specifically outperform the joint trajectory optimization and neural network approaches of Mordatch & Todorov (2014) or Levine & Koltun (2014)? The paper would benefit from a clearer "what's new" section that explicitly contrasts with these prior works.

**Significance:** The ability to generate stable, realistic locomotion for diverse character types using a single controller architecture has significant implications for animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The method enables interactive control without motion capture, which could substantially reduce development time for character animation. The results across swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion demonstrate the method's versatility, though the paper could better emphasize how this advances the state of the art beyond previous trajectory optimization approaches. The potential for broader applications in robotics (as mentioned in Section 9) is promising but not fully developed.

**Strengths:**
- Demonstrates impressive results across diverse 3D character types and behaviors
- Provides a unified controller architecture applicable to multiple locomotion tasks
- Effective ablation studies that validate key methodological components
- Clear presentation of the core technical approach
- Strong experimental results showing stable, realistic behavior

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient differentiation from prior work on trajectory optimization and neural network control
- Underdeveloped discussion of how the method compares to alternative approaches (MPC, direct RL)
- Some technical explanations (e.g., feedback gains) could be clearer
- Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to alternatives
- The significance of the method beyond animation applications could be more thoroughly developed

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of neural network-based control, demonstrating a valuable extension of trajectory optimization to diverse character types. While the core technical approach is sound and the results are compelling, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a clearer articulation of its contribution relative to recent work (particularly Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) and a more thorough comparison with alternative approaches. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, it has good technical merit but requires clearer positioning within the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better clarify the contribution and comparison to related work.
