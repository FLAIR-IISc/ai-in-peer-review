This paper presents a method for controlling physically simulated characters in an interactive setting using a small, fast neural network to represent the control policy. The approach involves an offline training phase where trajectory optimization is used to generate example motions, which are then used to train the neural network policy. The primary contribution is demonstrating that this single architecture is versatile enough to handle a range of characters with different body types and a variety of more-or-less cyclic behaviors, such as walking and running.

**General Comments**

This work appears to be a proficient application of neural networks to established techniques in character animation rather than a fundamental advance in either field. The core components of the proposed method have been explored in prior work. The ability of trajectory optimization to discover realistic control signals is well-established, as noted in several of the paper's citations. Similarly, the use of neural networks to approximate the state-action mappings of a policy, thereby mimicking trajectories found by an optimizer, has also been developed in previous research (e.g., [9]).

Given this existing foundation, the paper needs to more clearly articulate its specific contributions and position itself relative to these previously published approaches. For instance, the recent line of research from Levine, Abbeel, and colleagues seems particularly relevant, and a direct comparison would be highly beneficial for the reader. How does the proposed method improve upon this and other related work? The authors should explicitly state the advantages of their approach. Potential benefits could include, but are not limited to:
*   Faster learning or convergence.
*   Broader applicability to a more diverse set of character morphologies.
*   Lower computational cost at runtime, enabling more complex interactive scenarios.
*   Enhanced robustness to environmental perturbations or unmodeled dynamics.

Clarifying these points would significantly strengthen the paper's positioning and highlight its novel contributions more effectively.

**Specific Comments**

*   The paper states, "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." Could the authors elaborate on this finding? It would be helpful to understand the specific circumstances that lead to this issue and provide more intuition on how the proposed method mitigates it.

*   The discussion of why Linear-Quadratic-Gaussian (LQG) control was not used currently appears before the main strategy is detailed. The paper's narrative flow might be improved by presenting the proposed method first and then providing the justification for this design choice as a comparison or rationale afterward.

*   The notation used around lines 226-240 is potentially confusing. A target state `\bar s` is introduced from a reference trajectory `s(X)`, and a cost is then defined in terms of the difference between them. The use of visually similar symbols like bars and squiggles in close proximity can make the equations difficult to parse. Using more distinct notation would improve readability.

*   The authors claim that the "approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ." This statement requires further clarification. Softer than what baseline or alternative method? What is the technical definition of "soft" in this context (e.g., smaller in magnitude, smoother over the state space)? Why is this a desirable property for the resulting control policy?

*   The procedure involves modifying the initial state during training. What is the motivation for this step, and what specific benefits does it provide for the learning process or the quality of the final policy?

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound implementation of a neural network controller for character animation, and the results are compelling. However, its primary weakness is the lack of a clear articulation of its novel contributions in the context of closely related and recent work. By clarifying the specific advantages of this method over existing approaches, the authors could significantly strengthen the impact of their paper.
