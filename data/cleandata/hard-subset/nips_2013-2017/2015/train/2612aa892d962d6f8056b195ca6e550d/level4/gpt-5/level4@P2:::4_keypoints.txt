- Presents an alternating scheme that combines contact-invariant trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised training of a recurrent policy to enable real-time control of complex characters.
- Policy maps sensory inputs to desired velocities, forces, and recurrent-state updates using mostly feed-forward units and a small recurrent state.
- Training injects sensory and hidden-unit noise and computes local feedback gains from the optimizer via a perturbation method.
- Optimization solves a relaxed, block-alternating objective with proximal regularization in a distributed, asynchronous setup.
- Produces stable, realistic 3D behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion) without motion capture, state machines, or task-specific features.
- Builds on prior CIO and trajectory-optimization–based policy learning, and relates to continuous-control RL methods.
- Key practical improvements: noise-injected training tied to gains, efficient perturbation-based feedback-gain extraction, and a scalable distributed training pipeline.
- Technically sound and carefully engineered; proximal terms stabilize asynchronous updates and the perturbation method avoids ill-conditioned gains.
- Ablations support two central claims: joint trajectory-policy optimization outperforms fixed-dataset training, and noise injection improves robustness and test performance.
- Paper is well organized and readable, with helpful cross-references linking components.
- Results are significant and practical for large-scale/cloud GPU use, demonstrating realistic cyclic behaviors across morphologies.
- Contribution originality lies in the combination of techniques, feedback-gain extraction, and the asynchronous distributed training loop.
- Main weakness: run-time requires an extra per-timestep (T=1) optimization that’s not fully justified or quantified versus pure policy rollout.
- Questions remain whether the extra optimization addresses residual policy regression, cost mismatch, or constraint enforcement, and how root forces affect physical plausibility.
- Novelty relative to closely related work [9,11] could be articulated more clearly, highlighting specific gains from new components.
- Reproducibility concern: lacks guidance on sensitivity and selection of hyperparameters α, λ, η, σε, and σγ.
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical results, practical scalable pipeline, thoughtful noise/gain integration, and clear presentation.
- Cons against acceptance: dependence on per-timestep optimization reduces purity of policy execution and necessity/cost-benefit is not quantified; novelty positioning could be sharper.
- Minor requests: add references on proximal regularization in stochastic/distributed settings, fix typos (“to unity”→“to unify”, duplicated words, “patter”→“pattern”).
- Overall: technically strong, clearly written, empirically convincing paper; recommend acceptance pending clarification of run-time optimization rationale and clearer positioning relative to prior work.
