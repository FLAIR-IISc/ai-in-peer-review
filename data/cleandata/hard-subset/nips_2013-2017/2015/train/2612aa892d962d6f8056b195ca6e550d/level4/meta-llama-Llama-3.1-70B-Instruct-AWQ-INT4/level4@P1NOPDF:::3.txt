The authors propose an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models using a neural network (NN) to generate animations. The NN is trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), with CIO and NN training performed in an alternating fashion. The output of one is used in the criterion optimized by the other. Another key element of the method is the injection of noise through data augmentation by small perturbations and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that the network can produce realistic and stable control policies on a range of character models.

This approach is particularly interesting, as real-time control of arbitrary models is a challenging task. While the proposed method may seem straightforward at first ("learn a NN to predict the output of CIO"), the additional steps to make it work (joint training and noise injection) are significant new contributions, and experiments show that they help achieve better generalization.

However, the experimental results would be stronger if comparisons were made with a larger dataset. The base training trajectories were generated with only 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments, which is not sufficient to prevent overfitting in the "no noise" variant. The "no joint" variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories.

The comparative evaluation is the weak point of the paper, as acknowledged in the introduction. A systematic comparison with more direct methods remains to be done. The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with MPC at the end of the paper, and there is no reference provided for this method.

For the most part, the mathematical explanations are clear and well-motivated, although someone attempting to replicate these results will need to consult additional references, as some points are only addressed superficially (especially the CIO step, where notations in Eq. 6 are not defined). Providing code would be greatly appreciated by the community. One section that was difficult to understand is Section 3.2, which discusses generating optimal actions for noisy inputs. The notations became confusing, and it was unclear what was being done, particularly with the time index "t" being dropped.

It would also be useful to include time measurements for computations during real-time control, as this could be a major bottleneck, especially in applications like video games where CPU resources are limited. Knowing how these computations scale with the complexity of the 3D model would also be helpful.

Additional minor remarks include: "biped location" should be clarified as referring to locomotion; the phrase "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be corrected to "unite" or "unify"; the target appears to be only about x, y, z coordinates, with no constraint on the angle the character is facing when reaching it - it would be useful to know if it would be easy to add an extra target angle, which seems important in practice; the term "noise injection" is misleading, as it refers to data augmentation, which is a different concept from denoising autoencoders; it is unclear if the initial (random) network weights theta are used in the first iteration of Alg. 1; the justification for using the same hyperparameter eta in the two steps of Alg. 1 is not provided; there are several minor grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper.

The asynchronous joint optimization scheme described in Section 6 is significantly different from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1, and it would be worth mentioning this earlier. There are also several minor errors in notation and formatting, such as the incorrect placement of the star in the argmin on line 231, and the missing \bar{s} in \tilde{C}.

A question that would be interesting to investigate is whether alpha can be decreased all the way to zero during optimization (for CIO only), and whether the method is working primarily due to a "curriculum learning" effect or if it is always useful to keep the trajectories learned by CIO close to what the network can learn.

Overall, this is a great-looking application of neural networks to character control, whose practical benefits and applicability still need to be established.
