The paper "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" proposes an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models using a neural network (NN) trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The key innovation is the alternating fashion in which CIO and NN training are performed, with the output of one being used in the criterion optimized by the other. Another crucial element is the injection of noise through data augmentation and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers.

This approach is interesting, as real-time control of arbitrary models is a challenging task. Although the method may initially appear straightforward, the additional steps to make it work (joint training and noise injection) are significant new contributions, and experiments show they help achieve better generalization.

The experimental results are promising, but would be stronger with comparisons to a larger dataset. The base training trajectories were generated with only 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments, which might not be sufficient to prevent overfitting. The "no noise" variant is expected to overfit, and the "no joint" variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories.

The comparative evaluation is the weak point of the paper. A systematic comparison with more direct methods remains to be done. The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with Model-Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper.

For the most part, the math is clearly explained and motivated, although some points are only addressed superficially, such as the CIO step (Eq. 6). Providing code would be appreciated by the community. Section 3.2, which deals with generating optimal actions for noisy inputs, is confusing, and it is not clear what is being done, particularly with the time index "t".

It would be useful to include time measurements for computations during real-time control, as this could be a major bottleneck, especially in applications like video games where CPU availability is limited. 

Additional minor remarks: 
- The term "biped location" should be "locomotion".
- The sentence "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be "unite" or "unify".
- The target is only about x, y, z coordinates, with no constraint on the angle the character is facing when reaching it. Adding an extra target angle would be important in practice.
- The term "noise injection" is misleading, as it is actually data augmentation.
- The justification for using the same hyperparameter eta in the two steps of Alg. 1 is unclear.
- There are several minor errors in notation and formatting throughout the paper.

Strengths:

- The paper proposes a novel algorithm for real-time control of 3D models using a neural network trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO).
- The alternating fashion in which CIO and NN training are performed is a key innovation.
- The injection of noise through data augmentation and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers is a significant new contribution.
- The experimental results show promising performance on a range of character models.

Weaknesses:

- The comparative evaluation is limited, and a systematic comparison with more direct methods remains to be done.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of some mathematical concepts, such as the CIO step.
- Providing code would be appreciated by the community.
- Time measurements for computations during real-time control are not included.

Arguments for acceptance:

- The experimental results show promising performance on a range of character models.

Arguments against acceptance:

Overall, the paper is well-written and proposes a novel algorithm for real-time control of 3D models. While there are some limitations, the experimental results are promising, and the paper is a good contribution to the field.
