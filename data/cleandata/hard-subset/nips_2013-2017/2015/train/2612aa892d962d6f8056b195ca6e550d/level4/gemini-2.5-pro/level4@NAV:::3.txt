This paper introduces a method for real-time 3D character control, which employs a neural network (NN) to generate animations. The NN is trained offline to mimic the behavior of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) planner. A key aspect of this work is that the CIO planning and NN training are not decoupled; they are performed iteratively, with the output of each process informing the optimization criteria of the other. The method also incorporates noise injection, both through data augmentation with small input perturbations and via additive noise in the NN's hidden layers, as a central component. Experimental results demonstrate that this approach yields realistic and stable control policies across a diverse set of character models.

The proposed approach is compelling, as real-time control for diverse character models remains a significant challenge. While the core concept—training an NN to approximate a CIO planner—might seem straightforward, the novel contributions lie in the components required for its success. The joint training scheme and noise injection techniques are significant additions that are experimentally shown to be crucial for achieving strong generalization.

The experimental validation could be strengthened, however, by utilizing a more extensive training dataset. Given that the training trajectories were generated from a limited number of trials (100-200, each with 5 branches), the observed overfitting in the "no noise" ablation is not unexpected. It is also plausible, though perhaps less certain, that the "no joint" variant's performance could be improved with more varied training data.

Overall, a primary weakness of the paper is its limited comparative evaluation, a point the authors acknowledge in the introduction. The sole comparison to an alternative technique is a brief one with Model Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper, which notably lacks a citation.

For the most part, the mathematical derivations are presented with clarity and good motivation. However, any researcher aiming to replicate these results would need to consult additional references, as some aspects are covered superficially. This is particularly true for the CIO step, where notations in Equation 6 are not defined. The community would certainly benefit from the release of source code. One section I found particularly difficult to follow was 3.2, concerning the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs. The notation became confusing, and the procedure was unclear, especially regarding the implicit or explicit use of the time index "t," which was omitted.

It would also have been valuable to see performance metrics, such as computation times for the real-time control loop. Understanding how these computations scale with model complexity is critical, as applications like video games typically have a constrained CPU budget for animating a single character.

Additional minor points:
- Is "biped location" a typo for "locomotion"?
- In the phrase "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches," should "unity" be "unite" or "unify"?
- Am I correct in understanding that the target is defined only by x,y,z coordinates, with no orientation constraint? If so, how challenging would it be to include a target facing angle, which seems vital for practical use cases?
- I would suggest that calling the generation of new data from perturbed inputs "noise injection" is potentially misleading. Since the target is recomputed to correspond to the modified input, this process is more accurately described as "data augmentation" and is conceptually different from denoising autoencoders.
- In the first iteration of Algorithm 1, are the initial random network weights (theta) used to compute the first set of trajectories X'? Or do you begin with trajectories computed without the regularization term?
- What is the rationale for using the identical hyperparameter (eta) for both steps in Algorithm 1?
- In the sentence "it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have," is the article "the" missing?
- The acronym "LQG" appears one line before its definition.
- In "While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass," is the article "a" missing?
- In line 231, the star in the argmin seems misplaced, and should \bar{s} be included in \tilde{C}?
- In the phrase "the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory," a word appears to be missing.
- What guarantees that "sX and aX are subsets of \phiX"?
- The phrase "a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)" seems to refer to the objective from Algorithm 1, as only theta is being optimized.
- The asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 is quite different from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1. This distinction should probably be mentioned earlier.
- In line 315, should f be f^{des}?
- I did not understand the sentence: "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation."
- The "no noise" experimental variant could be more informative if split into two separate ablations: "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection."
- An interesting question for future investigation: can alpha be annealed to zero during optimization (for the CIO part)? This would clarify whether the term's benefit stems from a "curriculum learning" effect or from its continuous role in keeping CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent.
- The caption for Table 1a states 4 layers, but the architecture appears to have 3 hidden layers.

This is a promising application of neural networks for character control, though further work is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and applicability.
