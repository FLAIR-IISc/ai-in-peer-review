

This paper introduces a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex character behaviors in physics-based simulations. The authors demonstrate their approach across multiple locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking) with varying body morphologies, without requiring motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines. The core innovation lies in combining trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning, where the network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously. The method uses interleaved supervised learning with trajectory optimization, noise injection during training, and trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains.

The paper builds on established techniques in trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) and neural network control, but makes several key improvements. The authors demonstrate that their method produces more stable, realistic locomotion in three-dimensional spaces compared to prior work, which was largely limited to two-dimensional characters. They also show that the same neural network architecture can control diverse character types without specialized initialization.

Regarding quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach with comprehensive experimental validation across multiple character types and locomotion tasks. The method is well-motivated by the challenges of learning neural network controllers for complex dynamics, and the authors carefully address issues of overfitting through noise injection and trajectory optimization. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the approach's effectiveness, with clear comparisons against ablated versions of the method showing the importance of joint optimization and noise injection. The authors provide sufficient detail about implementation and parameters to allow for reproduction.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some technical sections could be improved. The notation in Section 3.2 (particularly around s(X) = \bar s) is confusing due to similar-looking symbols, and the discussion of LQG versus their approach could be better positioned earlier in the paper. The paper would benefit from clearer explanations of how their method differs from related work by Levine & Koltun (ICML 2014) and Mordatch et al. (ACM TOG 2012), which is crucial for establishing the paper's contribution.

Originality is somewhat limited. The paper applies established techniques (trajectory optimization combined with neural network policy learning) to a new domain (3D locomotion across diverse character morphologies), but the core approach is closely related to prior work. The authors acknowledge this by citing similar approaches, but they do not sufficiently clarify how their specific technical improvements (particularly regarding feedback gains and noise injection) advance the state of the art beyond previous publications. The claim that their method "solves substantially harder problems" than prior work is supported by the 3D locomotion results, but the paper would strengthen this by more explicitly comparing the complexity of their problems to those in previous work.

Significance is strong for applications in character animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The ability to control diverse character types with a single architecture without motion capture or task-specific features represents a meaningful step toward more generalizable character control systems. The results demonstrate robustness to changes in task specification and environmental perturbations, which is valuable for interactive applications. The paper's connection to potential robotics applications is promising but remains somewhat speculative.

Strengths:
- Demonstrates successful application across diverse 3D locomotion tasks with different body morphologies
- Provides clear empirical evidence of the importance of key methodological components (noise injection, joint optimization)
- Shows that the same architecture can control multiple character types without specialized initialization
- Presents a well-structured method that combines trajectory optimization with neural network learning
- Comprehensive evaluation with ablation studies

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient positioning of the work relative to closely related prior research (particularly Levine & Koltun, Mordatch et al.)
- Confusing notation in Section 3.2
- Limited discussion of how the method specifically differs from and improves upon prior work in neural network control
- Some claims about "substantially harder problems" lack sufficient comparative context with previous work
- The discussion of LQG versus their approach would be more effective if placed earlier

The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating the feasibility of a unified control approach across diverse character types in 3D. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more explicit comparison to closely related work, particularly regarding the technical differences that enable their method to handle more complex 3D locomotion tasks. The authors should clarify how their feedback gain calculation method differs from LQG and why it produces better-conditioned gains, as this is a key technical contribution.

The method's runtime efficiency is also not thoroughly addressed. While the paper mentions the computational cost of trajectory optimization, it does not clearly compare the runtime of their neural network policy to alternative approaches like MPC or other learned controllers. This is important for assessing the practical significance of their contribution.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound method with clear empirical validation. The contribution is valuable for character animation applications, but the paper would benefit from stronger positioning relative to prior work in neural network control. With better clarification of how the method advances beyond existing techniques, this work would make a more compelling contribution to NIPS.
