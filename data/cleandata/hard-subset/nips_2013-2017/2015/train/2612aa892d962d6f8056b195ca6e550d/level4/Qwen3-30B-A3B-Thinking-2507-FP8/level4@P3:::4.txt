

This paper presents a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) in 3D environments. The core innovation lies in a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly learns optimal trajectories and neural policy parameters through alternating trajectory optimization (using Contact-Invariant Optimization [12]) and neural network regression, with key enhancements including noise injection during training and leveraging optimal feedback gains. Unlike prior work [9, 11] limited to 2D scenarios, this approach achieves stable, realistic 3D locomotion across multiple body morphologies without motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines—addressing a significant gap in character control synthesis.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality*: The paper demonstrates exceptional technical rigor. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly show that both joint trajectory-policy optimization (vs. static trajectory regression) and sensory/noise injection are critical for robustness, with the full method outperforming baselines by two orders of magnitude in test performance. The trajectory optimization (Section 3) and feedback gain computation (Section 3.2) are theoretically sound and practically efficient, with clear empirical validation across four distinct character types (swimmer, bird, biped, quadruped). The implementation details (Section 6) and use of MuJoCo [16] provide strong reproducibility.  

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with each section addressing a distinct technical component (overview, stochastic policy, trajectory optimization, neural regression) and clear cross-references. The mathematical formulations (e.g., Eq. 1–4) are precise, and the supplementary video effectively demonstrates the claimed results. The writing avoids unnecessary jargon while maintaining technical depth.  

*Originality*: This work significantly advances prior NIPS contributions [9, 11, 12] by extending trajectory-optimization-based control to challenging 3D locomotion with diverse morphologies. The key novelty—using trajectory optimization to *generate* training data for neural policies while simultaneously learning feedback gains via perturbation (Section 3.2)—is distinct from direct RL methods [3, 6, 10] or simpler imitation learning. The unified architecture for disparate locomotion types (bipedal vs. quadrupedal gaits emerging without task-specific design) represents a meaningful step toward generalized character control.  

*Significance*: The method enables interactive, real-time control of complex characters without hand-crafted controllers, with clear potential for robotics (Section 9) and biomechanics. The ability to generalize across morphologies (e.g., same network for bird and swimmer) addresses a key limitation in prior work [21, 18, 13]. While the robotics application is still speculative, the core technique provides a robust foundation for future work in adaptive control.  

**Weaknesses:**  
The significance of the *theoretical* contribution requires more explicit contextualization against NIPS work. While the method builds on [9, 11, 12], it would strengthen the argument to clarify *how* the feedback gain computation (Section 3.2) improves upon LQG-based approaches [20] in terms of conditioning or computational efficiency—this is briefly mentioned but not quantified. Additionally, the connection to robotics applications (Section 9) remains underdeveloped; a brief discussion of how the method handles sensor noise or model mismatch (beyond "accurate character model") would better justify its broader impact.  

The rationale for the runtime optimization step (Section 7) is slightly ambiguous in the draft. The paper states that the policy’s action is fed into a physical optimizer to enforce constraints (Eq. 10), but the draft reviewer’s confusion is understandable. The paper clarifies that this step prevents error accumulation from policy rollout (which would otherwise violate dynamics constraints), but this could be emphasized earlier (e.g., in Section 2.1) to preempt questions about the need for "extra optimization."  

**Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance:**  
*Pros*: Rigorous validation across 4 complex locomotion tasks; novel joint optimization framework enabling 3D interactive control; clear ablation studies proving necessity of key components; unified architecture for diverse morphologies; exceptional technical execution and clarity.  
*Cons*: Limited theoretical novelty beyond incremental technical improvements; significance claims for robotics applications require stronger justification; minor ambiguity in runtime optimization rationale.  

**Recommendation**: **Strong Accept**. The paper makes a substantial, well-executed contribution to interactive character control, directly addressing a long-standing challenge in the field. While the theoretical advances are incremental, the empirical results are compelling, and the method’s generality (solving problems beyond prior NIPS work [9, 11]) meets NIPS’ bar for significance. The weaknesses are minor and addressable via minor revisions (e.g., clarifying the runtime optimization step and strengthening the robotics discussion). This work represents a meaningful step toward automated, physics-based character control with broad applicability.
