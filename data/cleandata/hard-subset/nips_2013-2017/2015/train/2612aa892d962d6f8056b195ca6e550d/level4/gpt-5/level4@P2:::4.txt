This paper presents a method that alternates trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning to enable interactive, real-time control of diverse, complex characters. The authors use contact-invariant optimization (CIO) to synthesize trajectories and simultaneously train a recurrent neural network (with mostly feed-forward units and a small recurrent state) to map sensory inputs to actions defined as desired velocities, forces, and recurrent-state updates. Key elements include injecting sensory and hidden-unit noise during training, computing local feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method, and solving a relaxed, block-alternating objective with proximal regularization in a distributed, asynchronous setup. The resulting controllers produce stable, realistic behaviors across swimming, flying, and both bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion in 3D without motion capture, state machines, or task-specific features.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on CIO for trajectory generation [12] and is closely related to learning policies from trajectory optimization, e.g., Levine & Koltun [9] and Mordatch & Todorov [11], as well as RL methods in continuous control [3, 6, 14]. The paper claims progress in scaling to harder 3D problems and introduces several practical improvements: noise-injected training tied to optimal feedback gains, a perturbation-based way to extract gains efficiently from a direct optimizer, and a distributed training scheme. These choices appear well motivated and empirically effective.

Strengths
- Quality: Technically sound and carefully engineered. The alternating scheme leverages mature solvers (CIO, batched SGD) and the proximal terms stabilize asynchronous updates. The perturbation-based feedback-gain computation is a neat reuse of quantities already computed by the optimizer and seems to avoid ill-conditioned gains. Ablations convincingly support two central claims: (i) joint optimization of trajectories and policy outperforms training on a fixed dataset, and (ii) noise injection improves robustness and test performance.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable given the complexity of the system. Cross-references help connect components.
- Significance: Results are impressive across multiple morphologies and tasks, achieving realistic, cyclic behaviors in 3D. The architecture and training pipeline are practical for large-scale use (cloud + GPU).
- Originality: While conceptually related to prior trajectory-optimization–based policy learning, the combination of techniques and demonstrated scope are noteworthy. The feedback-gain extraction and the concrete asynchronous training loop are useful contributions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Section 7 (Policy execution): The need for an additional per-timestep optimization (T=1) at run time was not fully justified. Given the stated goal of learning a near-optimal feedback controller, why not execute the learned policy directly with forward dynamics? Is the extra optimization required to mitigate residual policy regression error and cost mismatch, or to enforce constraints more robustly? A brief quantitative comparison (policy-only rollout vs. policy + T=1 optimization) would clarify the trade-off and the impact on stability and realism. Relatedly, since constraints are enforced softly, to what extent do root forces appear at execution, and how does this affect physical plausibility?
- Scope and novelty: The work is slightly outside my core area, so I cannot fully assess the novelty relative to [9, 11]. A clearer delineation of what is new (e.g., specific gains from the perturbation method, noise-with-gain targets, or the distributed proximal scheme) would strengthen the positioning.
- Sensitivity: Some guidance on choosing α, λ, η, σε, and σγ and their sensitivity would be helpful for reproducibility.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Strong empirical results across diverse characters and tasks in 3D, with clear ablations.
- Practical and scalable training pipeline using standard components.
- Thoughtful integration of noise and feedback gains to enhance robustness.
- Clear presentation and potential impact in graphics, animation, and model-based control.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Dependence on a per-timestep optimization at run time reduces the conceptual purity of “pure policy execution” and may complicate deployment; its necessity and cost/benefit are not fully quantified.
- Novelty over closely related work could be articulated more sharply.

Minor comments
- Please add one or two references to support “similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches” (proximal regularization in alternating or distributed settings) where noted.
- Typo: “to unity” → “to unify” (around line 89).
- Minor proofreading in Results (e.g., “changed changed”; “patter” → “pattern”).

Overall assessment
This is a technically strong and clearly written paper with impressive results and careful empirical validation of its key design choices. While I am not fully embedded in this subarea and thus cautious about judging novelty, the work appears substantial and of interest to the NIPS community working at the intersection of control, optimization, and deep learning. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the rationale and impact of the per-timestep optimization at execution time and tightening the positioning relative to prior work.
