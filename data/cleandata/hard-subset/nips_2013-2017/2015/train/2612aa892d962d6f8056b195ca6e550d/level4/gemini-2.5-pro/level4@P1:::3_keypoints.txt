- Proposes training an RNN policy to mimic an offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) for real-time 3D character control.
- Key novelty: interleaved/alternating optimization where CIO and NN training objectives depend on each other's outputs.
- Uses noise injection (perturbed sensory inputs and hidden-layer noise) and data augmentation to improve robustness and generalization.
- Demonstrates realistic, stable controllers across diverse morphologies: bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers.
- Strength: addresses the challenging problem of real-time controllers for arbitrary physically simulated characters.
- Strength: joint optimization of trajectories and network policy is a compelling contribution with strong ablation evidence.
- Strength: principled use of noise/data augmentation is crucial to prevent overfitting and ensure stability.
- Strength: single general-purpose algorithm/network can produce qualitatively different gaits, showing broad applicability.
- Weakness: limited comparative evaluation; lacks systematic benchmarks against other policy-learning or real-time control methods.
- Weakness: only brief qualitative comparison to MPC with no reference or rigorous evaluation.
- Weakness: relatively small training dataset (100–200 trials) may exaggerate benefits of joint optimization and cause overfitting in baselines.
- Weakness: some method components (e.g., CIO constraints in Eq. 6) use undefined notation, hindering reproducibility.
- Weakness: Section 3.2 (optimal feedback gains for noisy inputs) is confusing, with unclear notation and missing time indices.
- Weakness: source code not released; sharing it would greatly aid reproducibility.
- Weakness: missing runtime/performance metrics for policy execution steps and scaling with model complexity, limiting assessment of practical applicability.
- Suggestion: fix typos ("biped location"→"biped locomotion", "unity"→"unify").
- Question: does the locomotion target include orientation as well as position?
- Suggestion: clarify that the input perturbation procedure is data augmentation rather than classic denoising/noise-injection.
- Question: in Algorithm 1, is the initial random network θ used in the very first trajectory optimization, or is Step 2 done first without regularizer R?
- Question: justify using the same hyperparameter η for proximal regularization in both trajectory and network steps.
- Suggestion: resolve discrepancy between sequential alternating Algorithm 1 and the asynchronous/distributed implementation described in Section 6.
- Question: clarify the meaning and implications of enforcing dynamics constraints softly and the mention of root forces.
- Suggestion: separate the "no noise" ablation into sensory-input noise vs hidden-unit noise to measure individual contributions.
- Note: apparent typos in Section 3.2 equations (e.g., C̃ missing s̄, misplaced argmin*) need correction.
- Note: Table 1a caption mentions "4 layers" while the table lists 1–3 hidden layers; fix inconsistency.
- Suggestion for future work: experiment with annealing the network-matching weight α to test whether joint optimization acts as curriculum or is always necessary.
