This paper proposes an innovative algorithm for real-time control of 3D models using a neural network (NN) trained to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The key aspect of this approach is the alternating training of CIO and NN, where the output of one is used in the criterion optimized by the other. Additionally, the authors employ noise injection through data augmentation and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers to improve the network's performance. The experiments demonstrate that this network can produce realistic and stable control policies on a range of character models.

The proposed method is an interesting approach to tackling the challenging task of real-time control of arbitrary models. Although the idea of training a NN to predict the output of CIO may seem straightforward, the additional steps of joint training and noise injection are significant new contributions that have been shown to improve generalization in experiments.

However, the experimental results would be more convincing if they were based on a larger dataset. The current dataset, which consists of only 100 to 200 trials with 5 branched segments, may not be sufficient to prevent overfitting, particularly in the "no noise" variant. Furthermore, the "no joint" variant may also benefit from more diverse training trajectories.

The comparative evaluation is the weak point of the paper. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the introduction, stating that a systematic comparison of direct methods with trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done. The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with Model Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper. Providing a more comprehensive comparison with other methods would strengthen the paper's claims.

For the most part, the mathematical explanations are clear and well-motivated. However, some points are addressed superficially, particularly the CIO step (Eq. 6), which may require additional references to fully understand. Providing code would be highly appreciated by the community, as it would facilitate replication of the results.

One aspect of the paper that requires clarification is Section 3.2, which discusses generating optimal actions for noisy inputs. The notations become confusing, and it is unclear what is being done, particularly with regards to the time index "t." It would be beneficial to provide a clearer explanation of this section.

In addition to the above comments, it would be useful to include time measurements for computations during real-time control, as this could be a significant bottleneck in applications such as video games. Knowing how these computations scale with the complexity of the 3D model would also be valuable.

Minor remarks include:

* "Biped location" should be clarified as referring to locomotion.
* The phrase "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be corrected to "unite" or "unify."
* The target appears to be only about x, y, z coordinates, with no constraint on the angle the character is facing when reaching it. It would be useful to discuss whether it would be easy to add an extra target angle, which seems important in practice.
* The term "noise injection" is misleading, as it refers to data augmentation rather than denoising autoencoders.
* The justification for using the same hyperparameter eta in the two steps of Alg. 1 is unclear.
* There are several minor errors in notation and grammar throughout the paper.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents an innovative application of neural networks to character control. While the practical benefits and applicability of this approach still need to be established, the paper demonstrates promising results and contributes to the ongoing research in this field.
