### Summary

This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network (RNN) to serve as a real-time feedback controller for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning of a policy network. The authors propose a novel joint optimization framework where a set of optimal trajectories and the neural network policy are optimized concurrently in an alternating fashion. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, and a technique for extracting optimal feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimizer to guide the learning process. The method is demonstrated to be highly effective, successfully generating stable and realistic interactive locomotion for a diverse range of characters—including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and fliers—using a single, unified architecture without task-specific engineering.

### Quality and Significance

This is a high-quality paper presenting a technically sophisticated and well-executed methodology. The combination of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with a stochastic RNN policy, trained via a distributed, alternating optimization scheme, is impressive both in its conception and implementation.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Impressive and Generalizable Results:** The most significant strength is the demonstration of a single, unified method that can produce stable, interactive control for a wide variety of character morphologies (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flier). The emergence of natural-looking gaits (e.g., walking for bipeds, trotting for quadrupeds) without being explicitly programmed is a powerful testament to the method's effectiveness. This represents a significant advance over traditional methods that often require hand-crafted state machines or models for each specific character type.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide compelling evidence for their main technical claims through well-designed ablation studies in Section 8.1. The comparisons clearly show that both the joint optimization of trajectories and policy and the injection of sensory/hidden noise are critical for success. The "no joint" case demonstrates that simply training on a static dataset of optimal trajectories is insufficient, while the "no noise" case highlights the importance of stochasticity for generalization and avoiding overfitting.

3.  **Novel Optimization Framework:** While the constituent parts (trajectory optimization, policy learning) are known, their integration into a cohesive, alternating optimization framework appears novel and effective. The formulation allows the system to leverage powerful existing optimizers while creating a feedback loop where the policy regularizes the trajectories towards more predictable and stable solutions, and the continuously improving trajectories provide better data for the policy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, there are a few areas where additional clarification would strengthen the manuscript.

1.  **Motivation for the Policy Execution Method (Section 7):** The description of the overall method implies that the trained policy `πθ` should be capable of directly generating the next action. However, the execution step involves another optimization problem at each timestep to find the next state `x1` that best matches the policy's desired output `q̇des` while satisfying physical constraints. This was unexpected. Could the authors elaborate on why this is necessary?
    *   Is it because the policy's raw output does not perfectly satisfy the complex physics constraints (e.g., equation 6), and direct integration would lead to instabilities or physical implausibilities?
    *   Or is this primarily a practical choice to use an implicit integrator, which as noted, allows for larger timesteps?
    *   A clearer explanation of the limitations of a direct feed-forward application of the policy would be beneficial.

2.  **Missing Citations:** On line 153, the paper mentions that the use of quadratic proximal regularization terms is "similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches." It would be helpful to include one or two specific references here to ground the technique in existing literature for readers unfamiliar with it.

3.  **Minor Typo:** On line 89, "to unity these disparate approaches" should be "to unify these disparate approaches."

### Originality

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in both trajectory optimization and policy learning. It is closely related to recent work that also combines these two paradigms [9, 11]. However, this paper makes a significant original contribution by demonstrating that its specific formulation can successfully tackle substantially more complex, 3D locomotion problems than previously shown. Furthermore, the method for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) appears to be a novel and valuable technical contribution in its own right. The overall system, while composed of known components, is integrated in a novel way that achieves state-of-the-art results.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant step forward in automated controller synthesis for complex characters. The proposed method is powerful, the results are visually impressive and well-supported by empirical analysis, and the paper is clearly written. While my expertise in this specific subfield is limited, the technical quality and the significance of the results appear high. The work seems likely to be influential for researchers in computer animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning. I recommend acceptance.
