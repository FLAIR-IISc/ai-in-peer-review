The authors propose a novel algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, leveraging a neural network (NN) to generate animations. The NN is trained offline to replicate the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), with the training process alternating between CIO and NN optimization. The method also incorporates noise injection through data augmentation and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers. Experimental results demonstrate the network's ability to produce realistic and stable control policies across diverse character models.

This approach is particularly interesting, as real-time control of arbitrary models is a challenging task. Although the method may seem straightforward at first, the addition of joint training and noise injection are significant contributions that enhance generalization. The experimental results would be more convincing with a larger dataset, as the base training trajectories were generated with only 100-200 trials, each with 5 branched segments. This limited dataset may have contributed to the "no noise" variant's overfitting.

The comparative evaluation is the paper's weakest aspect, with the only comparison to a competing method being a brief one with Model Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper. The introduction acknowledges the need for a systematic comparison between direct methods and trajectory-optimization-based methods.

The mathematical explanations are generally clear and well-motivated, although some points are only superficially addressed, requiring additional references for a thorough understanding. Providing code would be beneficial for the community. However, some notations, such as those in Section 3.2, are confusing, and it is unclear what is being done, particularly with the time index "t".

Time measurements for computations during real-time control would be valuable, as this could be a significant bottleneck in applications like video games. Additional minor remarks include:

* The term "biped location" could be clarified as locomotion.
* The phrase "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be corrected to "unite" or "unify."
* The target appears to be only about x, y, z coordinates, with no constraint on the character's angle; it would be useful to consider adding an extra target angle.
* The term "noise injection" is misleading, as it refers to data augmentation, not denoising autoencoders.
* Clarification is needed on the initial network weights theta in the first iteration of Alg. 1.
* The justification for using the same hyperparameter eta in the two steps of Alg. 1 is unclear.
* Several minor errors, such as missing articles and incorrect notation, are present throughout the paper.

Overall, this is a promising application of neural networks to character control, but its practical benefits and applicability still need to be established.
