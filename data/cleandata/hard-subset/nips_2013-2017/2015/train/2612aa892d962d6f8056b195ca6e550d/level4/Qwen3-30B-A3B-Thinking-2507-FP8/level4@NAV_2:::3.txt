

This paper presents a method for real-time control of diverse 3D character models using neural networks trained to replicate outputs from Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The authors propose an alternating training scheme where CIO and neural network training inform each other, with a key innovation being strategic noise injection through both data augmentation with small perturbations and additive noise in neural network hidden layers. The approach successfully generates realistic, stable control policies across multiple character types including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds.

The method is particularly relevant given the challenge of real-time control for arbitrary character models. While the core idea ("train a neural network to predict CIO outputs") may seem straightforward, the authors' joint training framework and noise injection techniques constitute significant methodological contributions that substantially improve generalization, as demonstrated in their experiments.

The experimental validation would be strengthened by a larger training dataset. Given the limited trajectory generation (100-200 trials with 5 branched segments each), the overfitting observed in the "no noise" variant is expected. The "no joint" variant could also benefit from more diverse training trajectories.

The comparative evaluation represents the paper's weakest aspect, as acknowledged by the authors in the introduction ("A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The sole comparison to an alternative method (MPC) appears only briefly at the end without proper citation.

The mathematical exposition is generally clear, though replication would require consulting additional references due to superficial treatment of certain aspects, particularly the CIO implementation (Equation 6 lacks proper notation definitions). The community would benefit from the authors providing source code.

Section 3.2 (generating optimal actions for noisy inputs) presents significant notational confusion, especially regarding the handling of the time index "t" (which appears to be dropped but may still be implicitly present for certain quantities).

Real-time control computation time measurements would be valuable to assess practical feasibility, particularly for applications like video games where CPU resources are constrained for character animation.

Minor issues requiring attention include:
- "biped location" should be "biped locomotion"
- "unity" should be "unite" or "unify"
- The target appears to be position only (x,y,z) without orientation constraint; adding orientation would be valuable
- The term "noise injection" is misleading since the target is recomputed to match perturbed inputs; this should be described as "data augmentation"
- Clarify whether initial network weights are used in the first iteration of Algorithm 1
- Justify using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Algorithm 1
- Correct grammatical issues ("the" missing, "a" missing)
- Address notational inconsistencies (time index "t", \bar{s} in \tilde{C})
- Clarify the relationship between "sX" and "aX" as subsets of "φX"
- Explain the discrepancy between the asynchronous optimization in Section 6 and the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1
- Correct notation (f^{des})
- Clarify the statement about dynamics constraints being enforced softly
- Split "no noise" variant into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection"
- Consider whether α can be decreased to zero (curriculum learning effect)

This represents a compelling application of neural networks to character control, though the practical benefits and broader applicability of the approach require further validation.
