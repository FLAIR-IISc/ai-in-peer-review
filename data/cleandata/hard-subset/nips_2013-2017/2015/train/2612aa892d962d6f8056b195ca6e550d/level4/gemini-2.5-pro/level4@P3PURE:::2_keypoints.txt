- Demonstrates a small, fast neural network policy for interactive control of physics-simulated characters learned via trajectory optimization; main claim is one architecture works across diverse quasi-cyclic behaviors and body types.  
- Approach largely applies deep learning to established trajectory-optimization and policy-approximation techniques already shown in prior work.  
- Authors must more clearly position and contrast their contributions with prior work, especially the Levine & Abbeel line.  
- Clarify specific advantages over prior methods (e.g., faster learning, broader body support, lower runtime cost, greater robustness).  
- Although technically sound, the novelty/contribution relative to recent related work is not made sufficiently clear.  
- The statement about producing “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” needs explanation: specify conditions under which this occurs and its consequences.  
- Improve paper structure by moving the discussion of why LQG was not used to after the full presentation of the proposed strategy.  
- Notation around lines 226–240 is confusing (ambiguous use of bars/squiggles in s(X)=ȳ and cost terms) and hinders clarity.  
- The claim that the approach “produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” needs a baseline and a precise definition of “soft” feedback gains.  
- Provide justification for modifying the initial state during optimization and explain the benefits of that step.
