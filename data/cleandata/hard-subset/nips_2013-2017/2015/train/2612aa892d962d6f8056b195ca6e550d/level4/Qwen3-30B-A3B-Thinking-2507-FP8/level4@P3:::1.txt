

This paper presents a compelling approach to training neural network controllers for diverse complex characters, achieving interactive real-time control for a wide range of locomotion tasks without requiring motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines. The authors combine trajectory optimization with neural network training in a novel way that enables robust, stable behaviors across multiple character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal) in 3D environments. The key innovation lies in their distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly optimizes trajectories and neural network parameters while injecting noise to improve robustness, and their method for efficiently computing optimal feedback gains during trajectory optimization.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous experimental validation across multiple character types. The authors show that their method significantly outperforms naive neural network training on static trajectories (by two orders of magnitude in test performance) and that noise injection is critical for preventing overfitting and ensuring stability. The trajectory optimization component leverages Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12] but extends it through a novel perturbation method for computing optimal feedback gains without requiring LQG passes. This is a significant technical contribution that enables the neural network to learn policies that can handle small deviations from optimal trajectories, a critical requirement for real-world interactive control.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors clearly explain their method through well-structured sections and figures, making complex concepts accessible. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the algorithm descriptions are sufficiently detailed to allow reproduction. The supplementary video effectively demonstrates the diversity of behaviors achieved, which complements the written description well. The paper also provides thorough ablation studies showing the necessity of their key components (joint optimization, noise injection), which strengthens the technical claims.

In terms of originality, this work represents a meaningful advancement beyond previous approaches. While prior work [9, 11] has combined trajectory optimization with neural networks, the authors demonstrate substantially harder problems (3D locomotion) that previous methods could not solve. The paper also addresses a gap in the literature by unifying disparate control approaches for different character types (bipedal, quadrupedal, flying, swimming) under a single framework. The authors correctly acknowledge related work in neural network control [3, 6, 10, 14] and distinguish their approach by focusing on continuous, high-dimensional control with real-time interactive capabilities.

The significance of this work is substantial. It advances the state of the art in character control by providing a unified method for diverse locomotion tasks without task-specific engineering. The results demonstrate that the same neural network architecture can control characters with very different morphologies and movement patterns, which is a major step toward more generalizable control systems. The approach could have broader implications beyond character animation, potentially extending to robotics where controllers need to handle diverse environments and unexpected perturbations. The paper's emphasis on robustness through noise injection and feedback gain learning addresses a critical challenge in real-world control applications.

The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability. While the authors note their training requires hours of computation, they don't provide a detailed analysis of how the approach scales with character complexity or the number of degrees of freedom. A more thorough comparison with alternative methods in terms of training time versus performance would strengthen the significance argument. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of potential failure modes, particularly in highly dynamic or unpredictable environments.

The authors have made a strong contribution that advances the field of neural network control for character animation. Their method not only produces visually compelling and physically realistic behaviors but also provides a principled approach to learning robust controllers. The paper's technical depth, clear presentation, and impressive empirical results make it a significant contribution to the field. The work successfully bridges neural network learning with trajectory optimization, creating a framework that produces controllers with both high performance and robustness.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The paper meets all the conference's quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with excellent organization, represents a significant advance over previous work, and addresses a problem of considerable significance with broad applicability. The authors have demonstrated a remarkable ability to synthesize ideas from control theory, machine learning, and character animation to create a unified framework that works across diverse character types and locomotion tasks. This work provides a valuable foundation for future research on neural network controllers for physical systems.
