- Paper demonstrates a small, fast neural network policy for interactive character control learned via trajectory optimization, and shows a single architecture handling diverse cyclic behaviors across body types.
- Much of the method applies established trajectory-optimization techniques; prior work already demonstrates their efficacy and must be better acknowledged.
- Using neural networks to reproduce trajectory-optimized actions has been explored before (e.g., [9]); the novelty relative to these approaches is unclear.
- The authors should explicitly compare and position their work relative to the Abbeel & Levine line (clarify whether the goals are faster learning, broader body support, lower runtime cost, or greater robustness).
- Explain the phrase “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” — what it specifically means and how it arises in this context.
- Consider moving the discussion of why LQG was not used to after the full presentation of the proposed method for clearer exposition.
- Clarify the notation around lines 226–240 (e.g., s(X) = \bar s and cost defined in terms of s(X) - \bar s); the bar and squiggle symbols are ambiguous.
- Define what “softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” means: softer compared to which baseline, and what constitutes “soft” versus “not soft.”
- Justify why modifying the initial state within the proposed procedure is beneficial and how it affects results.
- Overall contribution relative to recent, related publications remains insufficiently clear and needs stronger differentiation and contextualization.
