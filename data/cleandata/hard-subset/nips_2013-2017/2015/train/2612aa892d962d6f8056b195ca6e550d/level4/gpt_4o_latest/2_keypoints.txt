- Paper shows a compact neural network can represent interactive control policies for diverse complex characters in a physics simulator.  
- Method trains the neural policy using trajectory optimization during training.  
- Main contribution claimed: same NN architecture works across many cyclic/non-cyclic behaviors and different body types.  
- Reviewer sees the paper as applying deep learning to recent, existing computer-graphics control methods rather than a wholly new paradigm.  
- Prior work has already shown trajectory optimization can generate realistic control signals; the authors should better contextualize this.  
- Using neural nets to mimic trajectory-optimized policies has been explored before (e.g., cited prior work); novelty relative to these needs clarification.  
- The reviewer identifies Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine’s recent work as the closest comparison and asks how this approach differs (speed of learning, body generality, runtime cost, robustness?).  
- Request to clarify the statement about producing “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains.”  
- Suggest postponing discussion of why LQG was not used until after presenting the proposed method.  
- Notation around lines 226–240 (e.g., s(X)=bar{s} and cost in terms of s(X)-bar{s}) is confusing and needs clarification.  
- Ask what is meant by “softer feedback gains according to parameter λ,” including what “softer” is compared to and why it matters.  
- Question why altering the initial state in the procedure is advantageous.  
- Overall: technically sound but insufficiently clear novelty and contribution relative to recent related research.
