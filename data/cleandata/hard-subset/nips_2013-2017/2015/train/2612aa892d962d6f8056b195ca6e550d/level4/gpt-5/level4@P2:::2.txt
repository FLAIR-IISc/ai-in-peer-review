This paper proposes an approach for learning near–real-time feedback controllers for complex articulated characters by interleaving trajectory optimization with supervised training of a recurrent neural network. The policy maps sensory inputs to desired velocities, contact forces, and updates to a small recurrent state, effectively learning both control and a local dynamical model. Key ingredients include: (1) using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate trajectories and associated optimal feedback gains; (2) training on noisy sensory inputs while matching noise-adjusted target actions via first-order feedback; (3) alternating optimization of trajectories and network parameters with proximal regularization in a distributed, asynchronous setup; and (4) executing the learned policy online with a short-horizon (T=1) optimization step that enforces dynamics constraints. The method is demonstrated on swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion in 3D without motion capture or task-specific state machines, and includes ablations showing the benefits of joint training and noise injection.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on CIO for generating physically plausible motions and on recent work that couples trajectory optimization with function approximation for policies (e.g., Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov). Compared to these, the paper emphasizes a unified neural architecture across diverse morphologies, a practical method for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization, and a distributed alternating optimization procedure. Connections to model-free deep RL (e.g., TRPO) and MPC are discussed qualitatively but not evaluated quantitatively.

Strengths
- Unified approach: A single NN architecture and training recipe produces stable, purposeful behaviors across very different bodies and tasks in 3D.
- Practical innovations: Noise-injected training with matched first-order action targets, a perturbation-based feedback gain computation that reuses CIO’s curvature, and proximal regularization for stable asynchronous training.
- Real-time execution: Online control via a T=1 constrained step yields robustness akin to implicit integration while keeping policy evaluation lightweight.
- Ablations: Clear evidence that joint optimization and noise are important (static-data training and no-noise variants degrade markedly).
- Engineering and scope: Scalable distributed training, GPU implementation, and demonstrations across swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion without mocap or hand-crafted gait priors.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior art: The overall template—use TO to train a policy—is close to prior work; the distinct contribution (feedback gain extraction and joint noisy training) needs sharper positioning and empirical validation against strong baselines (e.g., Levine & Koltun, TRPO, MPC).
- Limited quantitative evaluation: Results rely primarily on visuals and training/test loss plots. Missing are metrics such as goal-tracking error, fall rate, energy/torque, disturbance rejection, and robustness to model errors or terrain variation.
- Clarity gaps: The derivation of “softer” feedback gains and the role of λ need clearer intuition and quantitative characterization. Notation around s(X)=s̄ is easy to confuse. The LQG comparison interrupts the flow before the proposed method is fully specified.
- Physical realism at test time: Dynamics are enforced via soft constraints with possible root forces, which may limit transferability to hardware. The computational cost and latency of the T=1 optimization during execution are not reported.
- Generalization: It is unclear how policies extrapolate beyond the training distribution (e.g., large goal changes, unexpected contacts, uneven terrain).

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the alternating optimization is reasonable, and the gain extraction via Woodbury identity is clever. However, stronger empirical support is needed: quantitative robustness studies, baselines (MPC with similar costs; TO+policy methods like Levine & Koltun), and comparisons on identical tasks.

Clarity
- Generally well written with a clear system overview. Improve notation in the feedback-gain section (avoid visually similar bars/diacritics), move the LQG discussion after presenting your method, and define what “softer” feedback means (e.g., norms/condition numbers vs λ). Clarify training noise magnitudes and how feedback gains are used during NN training.

Originality
- Incremental but useful. The method for computing feedback gains from direct TO and the joint noisy supervision that matches first-order action corrections are the most novel elements. The uniform application to diverse morphologies in 3D with a single architecture is also noteworthy.

Significance
- High potential impact for physics-based animation and possibly robotics, provided the approach’s robustness and efficiency are demonstrated more rigorously. As presented, the significance is driven by compelling demos rather than comparative metrics.

Specific questions and suggestions
- “Very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”: Please quantify (e.g., gain norms, condition numbers, variance of action sensitivities) and illustrate how λ regularizes these.
- Clarify “softer feedback gains according to λ”: Softer relative to what baseline? Provide a plot of gain magnitude/conditioning vs λ and the resulting rollout stability.
- Reorder the LQG discussion: Present your gain computation strategy first, then contrast with LQG, explaining pros/cons.
- Notation: In the section deriving X̃(s̄), avoid using both bars and tildes; consider s′ for the perturbed target and keep consistent fonts/accents.
- Runtime and resources: Report wall-clock training time per task, GPU/CPU specs, and inference-time latency including the T=1 optimization. What is the control frequency achieved?
- Baselines: Include quantitative comparisons to (i) MPC with the same cost and horizon, (ii) Levine & Koltun–style policy learning on the same tasks, and (iii) a modern policy gradient method (e.g., TRPO) where feasible.
- Robustness: Evaluate disturbance rejection (impulse pushes), sensor/model noise, and goal-switching frequency; report failure rates and recovery behaviors.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, unified method with strong demonstrations across diverse 3D systems; real-time control; thoughtful engineering and ablations.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to closely related work; insufficient quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons; some clarity issues in the gains derivation and experimental reporting.

Overall: Solid and promising work with strong demos and practical contributions. Clarifying the methodological novelty and strengthening the empirical evaluation would substantially improve the paper.
