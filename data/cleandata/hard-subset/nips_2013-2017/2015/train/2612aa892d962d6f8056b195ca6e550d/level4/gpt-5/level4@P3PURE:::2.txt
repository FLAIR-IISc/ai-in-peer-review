"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" presents a method for using a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive character control in a physics simulator. The approach relies on trajectory optimization during training to learn the policy. The stated contribution is to demonstrate that the same architecture works across a range of more-or-less cyclic behaviors and across multiple body types.

General Comments
My reading is that this paper applies deep learning to established techniques in computer graphics. Prior work has already shown that trajectory optimization can produce realistic control signals, and some of that work is cited here. Similarly, prior work has used neural networks to approximate the step-by-step actions of a policy that reproduces trajectories obtained via trajectory optimization (e.g., [9]). The paper needs to more clearly position its contributions relative to these previously published approaches.

The closest related line of work, in my view, is the recent work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. How does the proposed approach compare to theirs? Is it intended to learn faster, to support a wider variety of body shapes, to require less computation at runtime, or to be more robust to environmental variability?

Specific Comments
- Please clarify the statement “we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains.” In what sense are the gains large or ill-conditioned, and under what circumstances?
- Consider moving the discussion explaining why LQG was not used to a point after you have presented your overall strategy.
- The notation around lines 226–240 is confusing. You introduce s(X) = s̄ and then define a cost in terms of s(X) − s̄. The overbars and other diacritics are visually similar and make it hard to parse. Please disambiguate the symbols.
- Please clarify “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ.” Softer than what? What does “soft” mean in this context?
- Why is it beneficial to change the initial state as part of your procedure?
- Overall, the work appears sound, but the contribution relative to other recent related work remains insufficiently clear.
