

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion. The key innovation lies in jointly optimizing trajectories and neural network policies through an alternating procedure that interleaves supervised learning with trajectory optimization, rather than relying on traditional reinforcement learning approaches. The authors demonstrate that their method can generate stable, realistic behaviors across multiple character morphologies without requiring motion capture, task-specific features, or hand-crafted state machines. Instead, the controller learns elaborate state-action mappings through a neural network with both feed-forward and recurrent units, where the action is defined as velocity rather than a control policy.

The work builds upon prior trajectory optimization methods like Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12], extending them to enable real-time interactive control through neural network policy learning. It addresses key limitations of previous approaches [9, 11] that were limited to two-dimensional characters by demonstrating successful three-dimensional locomotion. The paper makes several important technical contributions: (1) a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, (2) a technique for obtaining optimal feedback gains as a byproduct of trajectory optimization, and (3) a distributed training architecture that enables efficient scaling to complex problems. These contributions collectively enable the system to handle unexpected changes in task specifications and maintain stability despite limited training data.

The paper excels in technical quality and implementation. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of their optimization procedure, including detailed derivations of optimal feedback gains and a principled approach to noise injection. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across multiple character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) in three-dimensional space. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) clearly demonstrate the necessity of both joint optimization and noise injection for achieving stable, generalizable policies. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with alternative approaches like MPC, showing how their method produces more predictable, less chaotic behaviors through trajectory regularization.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise and well-organized, with each section building logically on the previous one. The authors effectively use cross-references between sections to maintain coherence. The technical details are presented at an appropriate level of detail, sufficient for experts to reproduce the results. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing the diverse character behaviors) are highly effective in conveying the paper's contributions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field in terms of originality. While trajectory optimization has been used previously for character animation, this work is novel in its approach to combining trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning to achieve interactive control. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work [9, 11] by demonstrating success in three-dimensional locomotion, where previous methods were limited to two-dimensional scenarios. The authors also provide a comprehensive discussion of related work, including traditional control approaches for different character types [21, 18, 13, 8, 7], and clearly articulate how their method unifies these disparate approaches.

The significance of the work is substantial. The proposed method enables interactive control of diverse complex characters without requiring task-specific engineering, which has broad applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The ability to use the same neural network architecture for different character morphologies represents a significant step toward more general character control systems. The paper also makes a strong case for the method's potential impact in robotics, where the learned controllers could be applied to real robots with appropriate physical models.

One minor weakness is that the paper could better explain why the physical state evolution (Section 7) requires optimization at each timestep rather than simply using the policy's output directly. While the authors note that this approach allows for larger timesteps and avoids instabilities, the connection between the policy output and the physical constraints could be made more explicit. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs between the trajectory optimization and neural network training phases, particularly regarding the "heavy lifting" done by the trajectory optimizer versus the neural network.

The paper's strengths clearly outweigh its weaknesses. The technical contribution is significant, the implementation is robust, and the results are compelling. The method represents a meaningful advance in the field of character control, with potential applications extending beyond animation to robotics.

Pro: 
- Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural network policy learning
- Demonstrates successful three-dimensional locomotion across diverse character types
- Provides clear theoretical justification for key technical components (noise injection, feedback gains)
- Comprehensive ablation studies showing necessity of proposed techniques
- Well-written with appropriate level of technical detail

Con:
- Could better explain the necessity of physical state evolution optimization at each timestep
- Limited discussion of computational trade-offs between trajectory optimization and neural network training
- Significance could be further emphasized by discussing specific robotics applications in more detail

Overall, this is a high-quality technical contribution that makes a meaningful advancement in character control. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates significant results across multiple challenging scenarios. I recommend acceptance.
