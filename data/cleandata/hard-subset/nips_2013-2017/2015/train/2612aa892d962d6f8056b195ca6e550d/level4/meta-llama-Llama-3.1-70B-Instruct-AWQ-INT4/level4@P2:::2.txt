"Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks" presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems and tasks, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking with different body morphologies. The authors' approach combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate stable and realistic behaviors. The method does not require motion capture or task-specific features or state machines.

The authors' contribution is to show that the same neural network architecture can be used to control a variety of complex characters, including those with different body types and morphologies. The approach uses a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive control of a character in a physics simulator.

The paper builds upon previous work in computer graphics and machine learning, including the use of trajectory optimization and neural networks to approximate control policies. However, the authors must better position their work relative to these previously-published approaches. For example, how does their approach compare to the recent line of work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine? Is it meant to learn faster, support a wider variety of body shapes, require less computation at runtime, or be more robust to certain environmental variabilities?

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its components. However, some of the notation and terminology could be clarified. For example, the discussion around lines 226-240 is confusing, and the notation around s(X) = \bar s and the cost in terms of s(X) - \bar s could be improved.

The authors' approach to generating feedback gains is also novel and could be further explained. Specifically, what is meant by "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda"? Softer than what? What does it mean for gains to be either soft or not soft? Why is it a good thing to change the initial state as part of their procedure?

Overall, the paper presents a sound piece of work, but the contribution relative to other recent related work is not clear enough. The authors should work to better position their approach and clarify the notation and terminology used in the paper.
