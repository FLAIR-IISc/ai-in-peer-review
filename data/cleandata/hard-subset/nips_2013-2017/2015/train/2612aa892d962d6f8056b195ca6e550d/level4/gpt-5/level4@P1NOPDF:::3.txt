This paper proposes a real-time control method for 3D character models in which a neural network (NN) generates animations after being trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Crucially, CIO and NN training are interleaved: each alternation uses the other’s output in its objective. Another key component is noise: the method augments data with small perturbations and injects additive noise into the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments indicate that the learned controller produces realistic and stable behaviors across diverse character models.

The approach is compelling, given the difficulty of real-time control for arbitrary models. While the high-level idea (“learn an NN to predict CIO outputs”) might seem straightforward, the alternating training and noise strategies are substantive contributions that, according to the experiments, significantly improve generalization.

However, the experimental evidence would be more convincing with a larger dataset. The base training set comprises only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” so it is unsurprising that the “no noise” variant overfits. The “no joint” variant might also benefit from more diverse trajectories, even if the effect is less obvious.

Overall, comparative evaluation is the weakest aspect of the paper (as noted in the introduction: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The only competitor is a brief MPC baseline at the end of the paper, for which no reference is provided.

The mathematical development is mostly clear and well-motivated, but reproducing the results would require consulting additional references because several points are treated only superficially—especially the CIO step (e.g., Eq. 6 uses undefined notation). Releasing code would be highly valuable. One section I could not follow is 3.2 on generating optimal actions for noisy inputs: the notation becomes confusing, and it is unclear exactly what is being done (in particular, the time index t seems to be dropped—if so, which quantities still implicitly depend on t?).

It would also help to report timing for real-time control and how it scales with model complexity. In practical applications such as games, CPU budgets per character are tight, and runtime could be a bottleneck.

Additional minor remarks:
- “biped location”: do you mean locomotion?
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches”: “unite” or “unify”?
- Is the target only x, y, z position, with no constraint on the character’s facing direction at the target? If so, would adding a target orientation be straightforward? This seems important in practice.
- I would avoid calling the step that regenerates targets from perturbed inputs “noise injection.” Because targets are recomputed to match modified inputs, this is standard data augmentation and differs from denoising autoencoders.
- Do you truly use the initial random NN weights θ to compute the first X’s in Algorithm 1, or do you start with X’s computed without the regularization term?
- What is the rationale for using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Algorithm 1?
- “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”: missing “the”?
- LQG is used one line before being defined.
- “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”: missing “a”?
- Line 231: the asterisk in the argmin is misplaced, and is  s̄ missing in  C̃?
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: missing a word?
- “sX and aX are subsets of φX”: why is this guaranteed?
- “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: this seems to refer to the Algorithm 1 objective, since only θ is optimized.
- The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs markedly from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1; this should be foreshadowed earlier.
- Line 315: should f be f^{des}?
- “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.” I did not understand this sentence—please clarify.
- The “no noise” variant in experiments could be split into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden-layer noise” to isolate their effects.
- It would be interesting to test whether α in CIO can be annealed to zero during optimization. Is the benefit primarily a curriculum effect (easier early problems), or is it always helpful to keep CIO trajectories close to what the NN can learn?
- Table 1a’s caption says 4 layers, but I believe there are 3.

In sum, this is a strong and appealing application of neural networks to character control, with promising qualitative results. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparative evaluation, clearer exposition of certain derivations (particularly in CIO and Section 3.2), explicit runtime measurements, and some clarifications and corrections noted above. The practical benefits and general applicability remain to be firmly established, but the approach is novel and potentially impactful.
