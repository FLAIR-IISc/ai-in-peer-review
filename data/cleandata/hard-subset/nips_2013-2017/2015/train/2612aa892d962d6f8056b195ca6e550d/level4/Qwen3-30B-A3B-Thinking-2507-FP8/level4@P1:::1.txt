

The paper presents a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to function as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse character locomotion tasks. The authors propose a joint optimization framework that combines trajectory optimization with neural network training, enabling the network to learn both optimal actions and the underlying dynamics of the system simultaneously. This approach successfully handles a wide range of 3D locomotion tasks—including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal walking—without requiring motion capture, task-specific features, or state machines. The key innovation lies in their technique for interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, training for unexpected task changes, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains.

This work builds upon and significantly extends previous trajectory optimization approaches for character control (Mordatch et al., ACM TOG 2012; Levine & Koltun, ICML 2014), which were largely limited to 2D scenarios. The authors demonstrate that their method successfully handles the substantially more challenging 3D locomotion tasks where characters are prone to instability, while previous work struggled with such complexity. The paper also relates to neural network controllers in reinforcement learning (Mnih et al., 2013; Schulman et al., 2015), but makes a clear distinction by using trajectory optimization to generate training data rather than direct reinforcement learning methods.

The paper excels in quality. The technical approach is sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis of the feedback gains calculation (Section 3.2), extensive experimental validation across diverse character types, and careful ablation studies demonstrating the importance of key components like noise injection and joint optimization. The authors show that their full method outperforms naive static training by two orders of magnitude (Figure 2), and that disabling noise injection leads to rapid policy divergence during testing. The experimental design is thorough, with appropriate baselines and comprehensive evaluation across multiple character morphologies.

Clarity is exceptional. The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with precise mathematical formulations, clear explanations of the methodology, and effective figures that illustrate both the approach and results. The supplementary video (referenced in the abstract) would provide valuable visual evidence of the quality of the locomotion behaviors. The writing is clear and accessible, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

Originality is strong. While combining neural networks with trajectory optimization is not entirely new, the specific approach—using trajectory optimization to obtain feedback gains, interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, and injecting noise to handle closed-loop control challenges—represents a significant technical advancement. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous work by demonstrating success in 3D locomotion where prior methods were limited to 2D. The paper's ability to use the same network architecture and parameters across diverse character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without specialized initialization is particularly novel.

Significance is high. The ability to generate controllers for diverse character morphologies with a single architecture represents a major step toward more general character control systems. The work has clear applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The authors demonstrate that their approach handles complex 3D locomotion tasks that previous methods struggled with, producing stable, realistic behaviors. The supplementary video (not included in the submission but referenced) would further highlight the impressive quality of the generated motions.

The main limitations I note are:
1. The paper doesn't provide a systematic comparison with direct reinforcement learning approaches (e.g., DDPG, TRPO), though the authors acknowledge this as future work. Such a comparison would strengthen the paper's position within the broader control literature.
2. The computational requirements (training taking hours on cloud infrastructure) might be a barrier for some applications, though the authors note this is a trade-off for quality.
3. The paper could more thoroughly discuss the limits of generalization to completely novel character morphologies not seen during training.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The technical approach is sound, the results are impressive, and the methodology is robust and generalizable. The paper's clarity, technical depth, and compelling results across diverse locomotion tasks make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The minor limitations noted do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions to the field of character control and neural network-based feedback control.

I recommend acceptance. This work represents a major advancement in the application of neural networks to character control, successfully extending previous trajectory optimization approaches to handle complex 3D locomotion with diverse character morphologies. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field with clear implications for both graphics and robotics.
