"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates how a small, fast neural network can represent a control policy for interactive character control within a physics simulator. The approach leverages trajectory optimization during training to learn this neural network policy. The core contribution is showing that a single network architecture generalizes effectively across a range of cyclic behaviors and diverse body types.

**General Comments**

My assessment of this paper is that it represents an application of deep learning to established (though recent) techniques in computer graphics. The effectiveness of trajectory optimization in generating realistic control signals is well-documented in prior work, some of which the authors cite. Similarly, using neural networks to approximate the sequential actions required to follow trajectories derived from trajectory optimization has also been explored previously (e.g., [9]). Therefore, the authors need to more clearly position their work relative to these existing approaches.

The closest related work, in my opinion, is the recent research by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. I would be interested to see the authors compare their approach to this work, outlining its advantages. Does it offer faster learning? Does it support a wider variety of body shapes? Is it designed for reduced runtime computation? Or is it more robust to environmental variations? Clarifying these distinctions would strengthen the paper’s contribution.

**Specific Comments**

Could you elaborate on the statement, "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains?" 

I suggest moving the discussion of why you opted not to use LQG until *after* you have presented your proposed strategy. This would improve the flow of the explanation.

The notation around lines 226-240 is somewhat confusing. You introduce s(X) = \bar{s}, and then define a cost function in terms of s(X) - \bar{s}. The overbars and squiggles are visually similar and can be easily misread.

What is meant by "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ?" Softer than what baseline? What does it mean for gains to be “soft” versus not? Furthermore, why is altering the initial state a beneficial part of your procedure? The work itself is sound, but the contribution relative to other recent, related research isn’t sufficiently clear.
