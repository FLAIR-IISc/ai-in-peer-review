This paper presents a method for training neural network-based feedback controllers for complex, physically simulated characters. The approach is demonstrated on a variety of challenging tasks, including swimming, flying, and legged locomotion for both bipeds and quadrupeds. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning. A trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), is used to generate optimal state-action trajectories for various tasks. A recurrent neural network is then trained to mimic this optimizer's behavior, effectively learning a control policy that can be executed in real-time.

The key contributions of the proposed training procedure include: 1) an alternating optimization scheme that interleaves trajectory optimization with network training, allowing the dataset and the policy to improve together; 2) the injection of noise into sensory inputs and hidden unit activations to improve robustness and generalization; and 3) a novel method for extracting optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer to provide richer, more informative training signals. The result is a single, general-purpose controller that can be used for real-time, interactive control of diverse characters without task-specific engineering or motion capture data.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive and diverse results. The ability to use a single, unified framework to generate stable and natural-looking locomotion for characters with vastly different morphologies (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) is a significant achievement. The fact that realistic gaits, such as trotting for quadrupeds, emerge naturally without being explicitly programmed demonstrates the power of the optimization-based approach.

The technical contributions are solid and well-motivated. The combination of trajectory optimization with a learned policy is a powerful paradigm, and the authors' specific implementation appears effective. The principled injection of noise and the method for deriving feedback gains from CIO are particularly clever, and the ablation study (Figure 2) convincingly shows that these components are crucial for the method's success, preventing the poor generalization that occurs when training on a static dataset. The work is of high significance to fields like character animation, gaming, and robotics.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the results are promising, the paper's presentation could be significantly improved in terms of clarity and organization. The main weaknesses are not with the method itself, but with how it is described.

1.  **Clarity on Model Assumptions:** The method relies on having a complete and accurate dynamics model of the character and its environment to perform the inverse dynamics calculations described in Equation 6. While this is implicit in the use of a trajectory optimizer like CIO, it is a crucial assumption that sets this work apart from model-free approaches. The paper would be clearer if this requirement for full model knowledge were stated more explicitly in the introduction.

2.  **Paper Organization and Detail:** The paper's structure could be improved for better balance. Section 4 ("Neural Network Policy Regression") is very brief, yet it describes a core component of the method. In contrast, Section 6 ("Distributed Training Architecture") provides a high level of implementation detail. While the training architecture is interesting from an engineering perspective, this detail is less critical to understanding the scientific contribution than a more thorough explanation of the neural network policy itself. Expanding Section 4 to provide more substance on the network would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Inconsistency Regarding Recurrent Units:** The abstract and Section 2 introduce a recurrent memory state `r` as part of the character's state and action representation. This is a key feature, as it allows the controller to maintain memory beyond the physical system state. However, Section 4, which details the neural network architecture, makes no further mention of these recurrent units. It is unclear how they are implemented (e.g., standard Elman-style connections, LSTM/GRU cells) or how their state evolution `ṙt` is trained as part of the action vector. Clarifying the role and implementation of the recurrent components is essential for reproducibility and a complete understanding of the model.

4.  **Terminology Clarification:** The conclusion refers to the use of "stochastic neural networks." This terminology may be slightly misleading. The *training process* is stochastic due to the injection of noise into the sensory inputs and hidden activations (a technique analogous to Dropout or denoising autoencoders). However, at execution time (Section 7), the policy appears to be deterministic as it is queried "without noise." The authors should clarify whether the network policy itself is inherently stochastic or if this term refers solely to the training procedure, in order to avoid potential confusion.

### Summary

Overall, this paper presents a powerful and general framework for creating real-time controllers for complex characters, supported by compelling experimental results. The core idea of tightly coupling trajectory optimization with neural network training is promising and well-executed. While the technical contributions are solid, the paper's presentation should be restructured for better clarity and impact. By addressing the points above—specifically by clarifying the model dependency, rebalancing the sections, detailing the recurrent network implementation, and refining terminology—the authors could produce a much stronger paper. Despite these presentational issues, the work demonstrates a significant advance in automated controller synthesis and represents a valuable contribution to the field.
