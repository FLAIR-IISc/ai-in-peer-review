### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time, interactive control policy for physically simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage offline trajectory optimization to generate a rich dataset of effective behaviors, and then use this data to train a compact neural network that can reproduce these behaviors online. The authors demonstrate that a single, unified training framework and network architecture can successfully learn control policies for a diverse range of characters and tasks, including swimming, flying, bipedal walking, and quadrupedal locomotion, all in a three-dimensional environment. Key technical components of their approach include interleaving trajectory optimization with supervised learning, injecting noise into sensory inputs and hidden unit activations to improve robustness and generalization, and a novel method for extracting stable feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimization process.

The work builds upon established techniques in both computer graphics and machine learning. The use of trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate complex, physically plausible motions has been demonstrated in prior work. Similarly, the concept of using a neural network to learn a policy that mimics an expert or an offline planner (like a trajectory optimizer) has also been explored recently, most notably in papers such as Levine & Koltun [9]. The main contribution of this work is in successfully combining and scaling these ideas to a wider and more challenging set of 3D characters than previously shown, and introducing a number of technical refinements that appear crucial for achieving stable and robust control in these complex domains.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive and General Results:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its empirical results. The ability to apply a single, general-purpose algorithm to produce stable, interactive locomotion for such a wide variety of character morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, fliers) is a significant achievement. The emergent behaviors, such as cyclical gaits and flapping motions, appear natural and purposeful without being explicitly programmed, demonstrating the power of the optimization-based approach.

2.  **Well-Motivated Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces several technical components that are well-justified and shown to be effective. The ablation study (Figure 2) clearly demonstrates that both the joint optimization (interleaving trajectory optimization and network training) and the noise injection schemes are critical for learning a successful policy. The static training and no-noise variants perform orders of magnitude worse, highlighting the importance of these contributions.

3.  **Novel Feedback Gain Calculation:** The proposed method for calculating optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) is an interesting contribution. It cleverly reuses computations from the direct trajectory optimization process to derive feedback terms, avoiding a potentially inefficient and ill-conditioned secondary optimization pass (like LQG).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** The paper's primary weakness is its positioning with respect to recent and highly related work, particularly the line of research from Sergey Levine, Pieter Abbeel, and their collaborators (e.g., [9]). While this work is cited, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more direct and detailed comparison. The current submission feels more like an application of deep learning to established graphics techniques without sufficiently clarifying its distinct advantages over other contemporary policy learning methods. The authors should work harder to articulate the specific contributions beyond scaling to 3D. For example:
    *   How does this method compare in terms of sample efficiency or training time?
    *   Does it require less computation at runtime?
    *   Is the resulting policy more robust to specific types of perturbations or environmental variations?
    *   Does the proposed method support a wider variety of body shapes or tasks than alternatives?

2.  **Clarity on Technical Details:** Several key technical claims and descriptions could be clarified, which would improve the reader's understanding and the paper's reproducibility.
    *   In Section 3.2, the authors state that using LQG "can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." This is a strong claim that warrants more explanation. Could the authors provide more intuition or a brief example of what this means in practice and why their method avoids this pitfall?
    *   The paper claims the proposed method produces "softer feedback gains according to parameter λ." The term "softer" is vague. Could the authors elaborate on what this means (e.g., smaller in L2 norm, less aggressive control actions)? Furthermore, an explanation of why it is beneficial to modify the initial state as part of this procedure would be helpful.
    *   The notation in the feedback gain derivation (Section 3.2, around lines 226-240) is somewhat confusing. A state `s(X)` is perturbed to a new target state `s̄`, but the notation `s(X) = s̄` is also used, which seems to imply they are equal. Using more distinct notation for the reference optimal state and the perturbed target state would improve clarity.
    *   From a readability perspective, the motivation for the proposed feedback gain calculation method might be clearer if the critique of the LQG-based alternative was presented first, thereby setting the stage for the authors' proposed solution.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a solid piece of work with impressive results. The method is technically sound, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate its ability to create controllers for a diverse set of complex characters. However, the paper's contribution relative to other recent, closely related work is not made sufficiently clear. Addressing the points of clarity and providing a more thorough comparison to alternative methods would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Demonstrates a single, powerful framework for learning interactive controllers for a diverse collection of complex 3D characters.
*   The empirical results are impressive and of high significance to computer animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning communities.
*   The technical components, particularly the joint optimization with noise injection and the novel feedback gain calculation, are well-motivated and empirically validated.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The novelty and specific advantages over closely related, contemporary work (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014) are not clearly articulated.
*   Several key technical descriptions lack the clarity needed for a full understanding and confident reproduction of the method.
