“Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks” demonstrates the use of a compact, efficient neural network to encode a control policy for real-time character control within a physics-based simulation. 

The method employs trajectory optimization during training to develop this neural network policy. The core contribution lies in demonstrating the adaptability of a single network architecture to a variety of periodic motions and differing character morphologies.

**General Comments**

This paper presents an application of deep learning techniques to established (and relatively recent) methods within computer graphics. The efficacy of trajectory optimization in generating plausible control signals is well-documented in prior research, including work referenced within the manuscript.  Furthermore, the use of neural networks to approximate the sequential actions needed to replicate trajectories derived from trajectory optimization is also not entirely novel (e.g., [9]). Consequently, the authors should more thoroughly contextualize their work in relation to these existing approaches. 

Specifically, a comparison to the recent work of Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine would be valuable. The authors should clarify how their approach differs – for example, does it prioritize faster learning, broader compatibility with body types, reduced runtime computational cost, or increased robustness to environmental changes?

**Specific Comments**

Could you elaborate on the statement “we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”? 

Consider postponing the discussion regarding the decision not to utilize LQG until after the presentation of your chosen strategy.

The notation surrounding lines 226-240 is unclear. The introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by a cost function defined in terms of s(X) - \bar s, is visually confusing due to the similarity of the bar and squiggle symbols.

Please clarify what is meant by “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda.” Softer relative to what baseline? What is the practical implication of “soft” versus “not soft” gains? Additionally, please explain the rationale behind modifying the initial state as part of your procedure.  The work itself is sound, but the novelty and significance of the contribution compared to related recent research require further emphasis.
