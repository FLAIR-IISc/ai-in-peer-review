“Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks” demonstrates how a small, fast neural network can represent a control policy for interactive character control in a physics simulator. The method uses trajectory optimization during training to learn a neural network policy. The claimed contribution is that the same architecture works across a range of mostly cyclic behaviors and diverse body types.

General comments
- In my view, this is primarily an application of deep learning to recent techniques in computer graphics. Prior work has already shown that trajectory optimization can produce realistic control signals (some of which is cited here).
- Prior work has also used neural networks to approximate step-by-step policy actions to track trajectories generated by trajectory optimization (e.g., [9]).
- The paper therefore needs to more clearly position itself relative to these approaches. The closest related line, to my mind, is the work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. How does your method compare? Is it intended to learn faster, handle a wider variety of body shapes, reduce runtime computation, or improve robustness to environmental variability?

Specific comments
- What do you mean by “we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”?
- Consider moving the discussion of why you did not use LQG until after you have presented your strategy.
- The notation around lines 226–240 is confusing. You introduce s(X) = \bar s, and then define a cost in terms of s(X) − \bar s. The bars and squiggles are visually similar and easy to confuse.
- What do you mean by “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ”? Softer than what? What precisely constitutes “soft” gains?
- Why is it desirable to change the initial state as part of your procedure?

Overall, this is solid work, but the contribution relative to other recent related work is not sufficiently clear.
