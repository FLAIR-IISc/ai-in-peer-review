This submission presents an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, employing a neural network (NN) trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) for animation generation. A key aspect of this method is the iterative, alternating training of the CIO and NN components, where each informs the optimization criterion of the other. Furthermore, the authors incorporate noise through data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise within the NN’s hidden layers. Experimental results demonstrate the network’s ability to generate realistic and stable control policies across diverse character models.

This is a compelling approach, addressing the challenging problem of real-time control for arbitrary models. While the core idea of learning a NN to predict CIO outputs appears straightforward, the authors’ additions – namely, joint training and noise injection – constitute significant contributions, and experiments suggest these improvements substantially enhance generalization performance. 

However, the experimental validation would be strengthened by comparison to a larger dataset. Given the training trajectories are generated from only “100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” overfitting in the “no noise” variant is unsurprising. The “no joint” variant could also benefit from more diverse training trajectories, though this is less immediately apparent.

The comparative evaluation remains the primary weakness of the paper, a limitation acknowledged by the authors (“A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The comparison to a competing method, MPC, is brief and lacks a corresponding reference. 

For the most part, the mathematical derivations are clear and well-motivated, though full replication may require consulting additional resources, as some points—particularly regarding the CIO step (e.g., Eq. 6, where notations are undefined)—are treated superficially. Providing code would greatly benefit the research community. I found section 3.2, concerning the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, difficult to follow. The notation became confusing, and the intended operation was unclear, particularly with the omission of the time index “t” from some quantities.

Including computation time measurements during real-time control would also be valuable, as this could represent a significant bottleneck. Knowing how performance scales with model complexity is also important, especially given the limited CPU resources available for animating characters in applications like video games.

Additional minor points: - “biped location”: should this be locomotion? - “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches”: should this be “unite” or “unify”? - Is the target limited to x, y, z coordinates, without constraints on the character’s facing angle? If so, would adding a target angle be straightforward and beneficial? - The step of generating additional data from noisy inputs is more accurately described as “data augmentation” rather than “noise injection,” as the target is recomputed to align with the modified inputs. This differs from the purpose of denoising autoencoders. - Does the algorithm truly use the initial (random) network weights θ in the first iteration to compute the initial X’s in Alg. 1, or are the X’s initialized using values computed without the regularization term? - What is the rationale for using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Alg. 1? - “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”: should this be “the”? - The acronym LQG is used before it is defined. - “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”: should this be “a”? - l. 231, the argmin contains an incorrectly placed star and lacks \bar{s} in \tilde{C}? - “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: is a word missing? - “sX and aX are subsets of \phiX”: what guarantees this? - “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: it would be more accurate to reference the objective from Alg. 1, as only θ is optimized in this step. - The asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 differs significantly from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1. This distinction should be mentioned earlier. - l. 315, f should be f^{des}? - “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.”: this sentence is unclear. - The “no noise” variant in experiments could be separated into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise injection” for clearer analysis. - Investigating whether α can be reduced to zero during optimization (CIO only) would be insightful. This could reveal whether the method’s success stems from a “curriculum learning” effect (starting with an easier task) or whether maintaining trajectories learned by CIO remains consistently beneficial. - The caption of Table 1a states 4 layers, but it appears to have 3. 

Overall, this is a promising application of neural networks to character control, but further work is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and applicability.
