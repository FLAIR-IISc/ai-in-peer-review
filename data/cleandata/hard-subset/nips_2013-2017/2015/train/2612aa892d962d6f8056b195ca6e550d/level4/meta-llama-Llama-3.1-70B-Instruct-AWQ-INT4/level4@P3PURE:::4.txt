This paper proposes combining trajectory optimization with policy learning to enable interactive control of complex characters. The main contribution is jointly learning trajectories and policies, with the additional empirical insight that decoupling the two optimization problems into alternating subproblems allows for the reuse of existing, well-documented methods and is sufficient in practice. Although I could follow the paper well, I am not familiar enough with this domain to comment on the paper's potential impact, which appears to be a technical achievement with no novel theoretical insights.

The authors demonstrate an impressive level of technical sophistication at all levels, from the concepts to their implementation, accompanied by equally impressive results. The claimed advantages of using sensory noise during training and learning trajectories jointly with the policy are clearly demonstrated. 

I found the paper to be clearly written, with an appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section treats a separate aspect of the problem, and there are useful cross-references between sections.

I think the motivations for the extra optimization step in Section 7 could have been better explained. At this stage, given what had been announced in previous sections, I was expecting the policy to run without the need for any extra optimization. Is this because there is always a residual trajectory cost (or policy regression error) such that feeding the action given by the policy into the physical simulator would accumulate errors? I would imagine that the forward evolution of the dynamics takes into account physical constraints so that no physical implausibility can result anyway, but I might be missing something? I appreciate, though, that re-optimizing at every time step lets you use larger time steps.

Line 153: The statement "similar to recent approaches" could be supported with one or two references. It is counterintuitive that keeping updates small in parameter space would be a sensible thing to do in general, though I eventually understood that it's useful in a scenario like yours where you're block-alternating between subproblems.

There is a typo on line 89: "to unify" should be corrected.

The paper's results look impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation seem sensible to me. However, this research area is slightly outside my expertise, making it difficult for me to be confident about the novelty and significance of this work.
