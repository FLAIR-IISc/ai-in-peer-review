

This paper presents a compelling approach to interactive character control by jointly optimizing trajectories and policies through an alternating minimization scheme. The core insight—that decoupling the problem into alternating subproblems enables reuse of established methods while achieving practical performance—represents a significant technical contribution. While the work lacks novel theoretical insights, its strong empirical results and robust implementation demonstrate clear value for the field.

The paper excels in technical execution and exposition. The authors successfully integrate sophisticated concepts (e.g., trajectory optimization, policy learning) with meticulous implementation, yielding impressive results. The claimed advantages—specifically, the use of sensory noise during training and the joint trajectory-policy learning—are convincingly demonstrated through thorough experiments. The writing is exceptionally clear, striking an ideal balance between depth and accessibility given the problem’s complexity. Each section logically addresses a distinct aspect, with thoughtful cross-references that enhance coherence. This level of clarity and structure is particularly commendable for a technical paper of this scope.

A few points warrant clarification to strengthen the contribution. Section 7 introduces an additional optimization step (re-optimizing trajectories at each timestep) after previously establishing a policy that operates without explicit trajectory planning. The motivation for this extra step is not sufficiently justified. Given the paper’s earlier emphasis on learning a policy that directly generates actions, it is unclear why residual trajectory costs or policy regression errors necessitate this re-optimization. Is the physical simulator’s forward dynamics insufficient to prevent error accumulation, or is the re-optimization primarily a practical measure to enable larger timesteps? The paper notes the latter benefit but does not explicitly link it to the residual cost issue. Clarifying this connection would better justify the design choice and align with the paper’s stated goal of minimizing computational overhead.

Minor issues also require attention. On line 153, the phrase "similar to recent approaches" lacks specificity; providing one or two representative references would strengthen the claim. While the rationale for small parameter updates (to avoid disrupting the alternating optimization) becomes clear later in the paper, the initial phrasing may mislead readers unfamiliar with the context. A brief elaboration at this point would improve accessibility.

The paper’s technical merits are robust, but its novelty and broader significance are challenging to assess fully. The alternating optimization framework builds on well-established techniques (e.g., block coordinate descent), and while the application to interactive character control is novel, the paper does not sufficiently contrast its approach with prior work on trajectory-based control or policy gradient methods. A more explicit discussion of how this method advances beyond existing state-of-the-art would better establish its impact. Given the reviewer’s limited familiarity with the domain, a stronger case for the work’s uniqueness is needed.

Despite these points, the paper’s execution is outstanding. The results are compelling, the implementation is sound, and the methodology is well-motivated within its specific context. The alternating optimization strategy proves effective in practice, and the benefits of sensory noise during training and joint learning are clearly validated. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the paper’s technical contribution but rather highlight opportunities for greater clarity in the narrative.

In summary, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in interactive character control. While it lacks theoretical novelty, its practical implementation and empirical results are significant. With minor revisions to clarify Section 7’s motivation and strengthen the literature context, this paper would make an excellent addition to NIPS. The writing and presentation are exemplary, and the work merits acceptance.
