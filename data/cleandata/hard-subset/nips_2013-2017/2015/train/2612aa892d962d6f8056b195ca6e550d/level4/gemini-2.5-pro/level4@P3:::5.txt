### **Summary**

This paper presents a method for training a single, general-purpose recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for a diverse range of complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage the power of offline trajectory optimization to generate high-quality training data for the neural network, enabling it to learn and reproduce complex behaviors online. The method jointly optimizes a set of optimal trajectories and the neural network parameters in an alternating fashion. Key technical contributions include: 1) using a direct trajectory optimization method (Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate data, 2) a novel technique to compute optimal feedback gains from this optimizer, which are then used to train the network on stochastically perturbed states, and 3) injecting noise into sensory inputs and hidden unit activations during training to improve robustness and prevent overfitting. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by successfully training controllers for various 3D characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, all using the same network architecture and training algorithm. The resulting policies produce stable, realistic, and interactive locomotion.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Significance:** The most impressive aspect of this work is its generality. The same framework is successfully applied to a wide variety of character morphologies and locomotion modes (swimming, flying, walking, trotting) without requiring task-specific state machines, features, or motion capture data. This represents a significant step towards a unified method for synthesizing complex motor skills, which is a major goal in both computer graphics and reinforcement learning. The results, as shown in the supplementary video, are visually compelling and demonstrate a high degree of stability and realism.

2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces several clever ideas that contribute to its success. The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a valuable contribution. It allows the network to learn not just a state-action mapping but also how to react to small perturbations, which is crucial for stability in a closed-loop setting. This, combined with the principled injection of noise during training, provides a robust solution to the challenge of transferring knowledge from an offline optimizer to an online policy.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The method successfully generates complex, cyclic gaits that emerge naturally from the optimization process rather than being explicitly designed. The ablation study (Figure 2) clearly demonstrates the importance of the two key components: joint optimization (vs. training on a static dataset) and noise injection. The performance improvements are dramatic (orders of magnitude), providing strong evidence for the authors' claims. The comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) is also insightful, suggesting that the neural network acts as a powerful regularizer, guiding the trajectory optimizer towards more structured and predictable (i.e., less "chaotic") solutions.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Model Assumptions:** A major limitation of the approach is its reliance on having access to a complete and accurate differentiable model of the system dynamics and contact forces (as required by Eq. 6 and the CIO method). This is a very strong assumption that limits the method's direct applicability to real-world robotics, where such models are rarely available. This point is critical for contextualizing the paper's contribution and should be stated explicitly and prominently, perhaps as early as the introduction, to avoid giving the impression of a model-free approach.

2.  **Lack of Detail on the Recurrent Network:** The abstract and introduction emphasize the role of recurrent units for implementing memory states. However, the paper provides almost no detail about this crucial component. Section 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) is disproportionately brief and does not explain the architecture of the recurrent part of the network, the dimensionality of the recurrent state `r`, or how `r` is updated and used. There is no analysis or discussion of what the learned memory states might represent. This is a significant omission, as it leaves a core part of the claimed contribution unexplained and unevaluated.

3.  **Misleading Terminology:** The paper uses the term "stochastic neural network" in the conclusion and "Stochastic Optimization" in the title of Algorithm 1. This is potentially misleading. The training process is stochastic due to the injection of noise into inputs and hidden activations. However, the final policy used at execution time appears to be fully deterministic (as stated in Section 7: "query the policy (without noise)"). The authors should be more precise with their terminology to distinguish between a stochastic training procedure and a stochastic policy.

4.  **Imbalance in Section Detail:** There is a notable imbalance in the level of detail provided for different parts of the system. Section 6 describes the distributed training architecture on AWS in significant detail, which feels more like an engineering implementation report than a core scientific contribution. In contrast, Section 4, which describes the neural network—a central component of the method—is very sparse. The paper would be significantly improved by condensing Section 6 and using the space to provide a more thorough description and analysis of the recurrent network architecture and its function.

### **Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a powerful and general framework for creating real-time controllers for complex characters. The combination of trajectory optimization with a robust neural network training scheme is effective, and the results are state-of-the-art for this type of problem. The strengths—particularly the method's generality and the novel technique for learning from a direct optimizer—are significant.

However, the paper's clarity and completeness are hampered by a lack of detail regarding the recurrent network components and an unclear statement of the strong model-based assumptions. Addressing these weaknesses by restructuring the paper to better explain the core technical components and properly contextualize its reliance on a perfect physics model would substantially strengthen the submission. Despite these shortcomings, the promising results and novel ideas make this a valuable contribution to the field.
