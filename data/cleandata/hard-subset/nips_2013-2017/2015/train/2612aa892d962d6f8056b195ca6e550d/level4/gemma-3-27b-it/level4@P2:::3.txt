This submission presents an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, leveraging a neural network (NN) trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key contribution lies in the alternating training process, where CIO and NN training are iteratively refined, with the output of one informing the optimization criterion of the other. The method also incorporates noise injection, both through data augmentation with small perturbations and by adding noise to the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate the network’s ability to generate stable and realistic control policies for a diverse range of character models.

This is a compelling approach to a challenging problem – real-time control of complex 3D models. While the core concept of learning a NN to predict CIO outputs may seem straightforward, the inclusion of joint training and noise injection represent significant advancements, enabling improved generalization. The method’s ability to control diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture is particularly noteworthy.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. The dataset size, “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” appears relatively small. This likely contributes to the overfitting observed in the “no noise” variant, and potentially limits the generalizability of the “no joint” variant as well. A larger and more diverse training dataset could yield more robust results. The comparative evaluation is a weakness; the sole comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) is brief and lacks a citation. A more thorough comparison to existing methods would bolster the claims of novelty and improvement.

The mathematical explanations are generally clear, though some sections require additional background knowledge. Replicating the results would necessitate consulting cited works, particularly regarding the CIO step (Eq. 6, where notations are not fully defined). Providing code would greatly facilitate reproducibility and adoption by the research community. Section 3.2, detailing optimal feedback gains for noisy inputs, proved particularly difficult to follow; the notation becomes confusing, and the purpose of certain steps remains unclear (e.g., the dropped time index “t”). Furthermore, reporting computation times for real-time control would be valuable, as this could be a limiting factor for applications like video games with limited computational resources.

Minor points: “biped location” should likely be “locomotion”; “unity” should be “unite” or “unify”; the paper assumes a target only in x,y,z coordinates without angle constraints, and adding an angle constraint may improve performance; the term "noise injection" is arguably misapplied to data augmentation; the initialization of network weights in Algorithm 1 requires clarification; the justification for using a single hyperparameter η throughout Algorithm 1 is unclear. Several minor typos and grammatical errors exist (e.g., missing articles, incorrect indexing, unclear phrasing – see original review for specifics).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of trajectory optimization (CIO) and neural networks for real-time control.
*   Demonstrated ability to control a diverse range of character models with a single approach.
*   Joint training and noise injection are significant contributions that improve generalization.
*   Potential for applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation and comparative analysis.
*   Small training dataset may limit generalizability.
*   Some mathematical explanations are unclear and require additional background knowledge.
*   Lack of code availability hinders reproducibility.
*   Computational cost of real-time control not fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to a challenging problem. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened and certain aspects of the methodology clarified, the novel combination of techniques and demonstrated results suggest a valuable contribution to the field. A revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly improve the paper's impact. I recommend a borderline accept, contingent on addressing the key concerns regarding experimental validation and clarity of presentation.
