"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates the potential of a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive control of a character in a physics simulator. The approach leverages trajectory optimization in a training phase to learn a neural network policy. A key contribution of this work is showing that the same architecture can be applied across a range of more-or-less cyclic behaviors and a variety of body types.

General Comments
----------------

Upon reading this paper, I interpret it as an application of deep learning to existing techniques in computer graphics, albeit recent ones. The ability of trajectory optimization to find realistic control signals has been established by previous work, some of which is cited in this paper. Similarly, the use of neural networks to approximate the step-by-step actions required of a policy to replicate trajectories found by trajectory optimization has been explored in prior research (e.g., [9]). Therefore, the authors must provide a clearer positioning of their work relative to these previously published approaches.

A relevant comparison is the recent line of work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. It would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly compare their approach to this one. Specifically, they should address whether their method is designed to learn faster, support a wider variety of body shapes, require less computation at runtime, or be more robust to environmental variabilities.

Specific Comments
-----------------

The statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" is unclear. Could the authors provide more context or explanation for this observation?

It might be helpful to move the discussion of why Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) control was not used until after presenting the proposed strategy. This would provide a clearer understanding of the motivation behind the chosen approach.

The notation around lines 226-240 is confusing, particularly the introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by the definition of a cost in terms of s(X) - \bar s. The use of bars and squiggles is visually similar, making it difficult to distinguish between the two. Consider revising the notation for clarity.

The claim "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda" requires further explanation. Specifically, the authors should clarify what they mean by "softer" gains, how they compare to other approaches, and why this is a desirable property. Additionally, it would be beneficial to explain why changing the initial state as part of the procedure is advantageous.

Overall, the work presented is sound, but the contribution relative to other recent related work is not clear enough.
