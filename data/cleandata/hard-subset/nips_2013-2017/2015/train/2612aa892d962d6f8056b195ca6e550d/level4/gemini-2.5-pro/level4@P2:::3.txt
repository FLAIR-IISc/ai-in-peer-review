This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to train a recurrent neural network policy to mimic the outputs of an offline trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The key contributions are that the policy learning and trajectory optimization are not separate steps but are performed jointly in an alternating fashion, and that noise is systematically injected during training to improve robustness and generalization. The method is demonstrated on a diverse set of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers, successfully producing stable and realistic interactive locomotion. This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with function approximation for control, such as [9, 11], but extends the application to more complex 3D locomotion tasks.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the challenging and important problem of generating real-time, interactive controllers for arbitrary physical models. The proposed solution is compelling. While using a neural network to approximate an optimizer is not entirely new, the specific technical contributions—the joint optimization framework and the principled use of noise with optimal feedback gains—are novel and appear crucial for success, as demonstrated by the ablation studies.
2.  **Generality:** A major strength is the method's ability to control a wide range of characters with very different morphologies and dynamics (walking, flying, swimming) using the same universal architecture and training algorithm, without task-specific features or initializations. This represents a significant step towards unifying disparate approaches in character control.
3.  **Quality of Results:** The behaviors shown in the supplementary video are impressive. The learned gaits appear stable, realistic, and purposeful, which is a notable achievement for policies learned without motion capture data. The emergence of cyclic gaits like trotting without being explicitly programmed is a strong result.

**Weaknesses / Arguments for Rejection:**

1.  **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The paper's primary weakness is the lack of a thorough comparison to alternative methods. The authors acknowledge that a systematic comparison with more direct reinforcement learning methods remains to be done. The only comparison provided is a brief qualitative one with Model Predictive Control (MPC), for which no reference is given. Without quantitative comparisons to other state-of-the-art policy learning techniques (e.g., [14]), it is difficult to assess the performance of this method in the broader context of control and reinforcement learning.
2.  **Ablation Study Concerns:** The experimental results would be stronger if the ablation studies were conducted on a larger training dataset. The training data consists of only 100-200 trials with 5 branches each. With such a small dataset, it is not surprising that the "no noise" variant overfits. A more compelling demonstration would show that even with a much larger and more diverse set of training trajectories, the joint optimization and noise injection schemes still provide a significant advantage over the baseline "no joint" approach.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the paper is generally well-written, certain key technical sections are difficult to follow, which could hinder reproducibility.
    *   Section 3.2, which describes the method for computing optimal feedback gains, is particularly confusing due to unclear notation (e.g., the time index `t` is dropped). This is a core component of the proposed data augmentation scheme, and its clarity is critical.
    *   The description of the CIO method and the cost function (Eq. 6) is superficial, requiring readers to consult external references to understand the implementation details.
    *   The paper lacks practical performance metrics for the real-time execution phase, such as computation time per frame and how it scales with model complexity. These details are essential for validating the claim of "interactive real-time control," especially in resource-constrained applications like video games.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions:**

*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   Pg. 2, "biped location" should likely be "biped locomotion."
    *   Pg. 2, "to unity these disparate approaches" should be "to unify these disparate approaches."
    *   Pg. 9, Table 1 caption mentions "4 layers," but the text and results suggest a maximum of 3 hidden layers.
*   **Clarifications:**
    1.  Regarding the task goal: Does the target location `g_k` only specify position, or does it also include a target orientation? Adding an orientation target seems important for many practical applications.
    2.  In Algorithm 1, is the initial set of trajectories `X_i` generated using an initial random network `θ`, or is the first step performed without the `R` term?
    3.  The asynchronous optimization described in Section 6 appears different from the strictly alternating scheme in Algorithm 1. It would be helpful to mention this discrepancy earlier.
    4.  In Section 7, the equation for evolving the physical state includes `f_des`. Should this be `f` from the policy output?
    5.  Could you elaborate on the sentence: "the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation"?
    6.  In Section 3.2, is it guaranteed that `s_X` and `a_X` are subsets of `φ_X`?
*   **Suggestions for Improvement:**
    1.  The term "noise injection" for the process in Section 2.1 could be clarified. Since the target action is re-computed for the perturbed state, this is more akin to "data augmentation" than a denoising objective. This would distinguish it more clearly from the additive noise injected into the hidden units.
    2.  The ablation study could be strengthened by splitting the "no noise" case into two separate experiments: one without the sensory input augmentation (Sec 2.1) and one without the hidden unit noise (Sec 4).
    3.  An interesting experiment would be to analyze the effect of annealing the penalty weight `α` over the course of training. Does the joint optimization primarily act as a curriculum, or is it always necessary to keep the CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective method for a difficult problem. The results are impressive and the core ideas are significant. However, the paper would be substantially stronger with a more thorough experimental comparison to related methods and improved clarity in its key technical descriptions.
