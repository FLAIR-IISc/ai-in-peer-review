This paper presents a method for training neural network policies for the interactive control of complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with online policy learning. The authors propose a joint optimization framework that alternates between optimizing a set of trajectories and fitting a neural network policy to these trajectories. This block-alternating approach allows the use of existing, powerful methods for each subproblem and is shown to be effective in practice. The authors also highlight the importance of injecting noise during training and using the trajectory optimizer to compute optimal feedback gains.

**Strengths**

*   The paper's primary strength lies in its impressive technical execution and the high quality of the results. The authors successfully control a diverse set of characters (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) with a single, unified framework, which is a significant engineering achievement. The behaviors shown in the supplementary video are stable, realistic, and purposeful.
*   The empirical validation is thorough. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 clearly demonstrate the benefits of the key components of the proposed method, namely the joint optimization of trajectories and the policy, and the injection of noise during training. The comparison against training on a static dataset is particularly convincing.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized. Given the complexity of the overall system, the authors do an excellent job of breaking down the problem into manageable components and explaining each part in sufficient detail. The cross-references between sections are helpful for following the logic of the complete system.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   The motivation for the optimization step at execution time (Section 7, Policy Execution) could be clarified. After training, one might expect the learned policy `π_θ` to be used directly to generate actions at each step. Instead, the policy's output is used as a target for a single-timestep optimization problem that also enforces the equations of motion. The authors should elaborate on why this is necessary. Is it to correct for policy regression errors and prevent the accumulation of errors over time? Or is it primarily a method to enforce physical constraints that the raw network output might violate? A clearer explanation would strengthen the paper and help readers understand the role of each component at test time. The paper does mention this allows for larger timesteps, which is a clear benefit, but the primary motivation remains somewhat obscure.
*   While the results are impressive, the paper's contribution seems to be more of a significant engineering advance than a source of new theoretical insights. The main novelty lies in the successful synthesis and careful integration of several sophisticated techniques (trajectory optimization, deep learning, stochastic optimization) to solve a very challenging control problem. This is not a major weakness, but the positioning of the work could be made clearer in this regard.

**Minor Points**

*   On line 153, the paper mentions that the use of proximal regularization terms is "similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches". It would be helpful to include one or two citations here to ground this claim.
*   There is a minor typo on line 89: "unity" should be "unify".

**Overall Assessment**

This is a compelling and well-executed paper that presents a sensible and effective optimization scheme for a difficult problem. The results are impressive and appear to advance the state of the art in interactive character animation and control. I recommend acceptance.
