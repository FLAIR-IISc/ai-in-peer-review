### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for creating real-time, interactive controllers for a diverse range of complex 3D characters. The core of the method is a recurrent neural network (RNN) policy that is trained to mimic the outputs of an offline trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The key contributions lie not just in this combination, but in the specific techniques developed to make it successful for high-dimensional, unstable systems. These include: 1) A joint, alternating optimization scheme where the neural network and the trajectory optimizer are trained together, with each influencing the other's objective function. 2) A principled injection of noise, both as perturbations to the network's sensory inputs (akin to data augmentation) and as additive noise to the hidden layer activations during training, which improves robustness and generalization. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach by successfully training a single network architecture to control various characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal models, which learn stable and realistic gaits without task-specific engineering.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** While the idea of using a neural network to learn a policy from a trajectory optimizer has been explored before (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), this work presents a compelling step forward by tackling more complex 3D characters. The proposed joint optimization algorithm and the systematic use of noise injection are significant technical contributions that, as shown in the ablations, are crucial for achieving stable and robust control.

2.  **Impressive and General Results:** The ability to apply the same architecture and training methodology to generate stable, cyclic, and visually plausible locomotion for such a wide variety of character morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) is a major strength. This demonstrates a high degree of generality, moving towards the paper's goal of a universal synthesis method and away from hand-crafted, character-specific controllers. The behaviors shown in the supplementary video are particularly convincing.

3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly motivates the need for each component of its method. The rationale for combining the optimality of trajectory optimization with the real-time performance of neural networks is clear. The explanations for why joint optimization is superior to static training and why noise injection prevents overfitting are well-argued and supported by experimental results.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The paper's primary weakness is the lack of a thorough comparison to other state-of-the-art methods. The introduction acknowledges that a "systematic comparison... remains to be done," but its absence makes it difficult to gauge the true significance of the results. The comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC) is brief and qualitative, without a proper quantitative baseline or reference. The strong claims of the paper would be more convincing if benchmarked against other policy search or reinforcement learning methods capable of continuous control.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility Issues:** While the paper is generally well-written, certain key sections lack the detail required for full understanding and replication.
    *   The description of Contact-Invariant Optimization in Section 3 is very high-level. Key notations in Equation 6 (e.g., H(q), C(q, q̇), τ, J, K) are not defined, forcing the reader to consult external references for a core component of the method.
    *   Section 3.2, which describes the computation of optimal feedback gains, is particularly difficult to follow. The notation becomes confusing (e.g., the time index `t` is dropped), and the derivation is dense. A more detailed, step-by-step explanation would significantly improve clarity.
    *   Given the complexity of the implementation, providing source code would be a substantial benefit to the community and would greatly enhance the paper's impact.

3.  **Weak Justification for Ablation Study Conditions:** The ablation studies in Section 8.1 convincingly show that the "no joint" and "no noise" variants perform poorly. However, the training dataset seems relatively small ("between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments"). On such a small dataset, it is not surprising that a high-capacity model without regularization overfits ("no noise" case). It is also plausible that the "no joint" variant might have performed better if trained on a much larger and more diverse set of pre-optimized trajectories. Stronger conclusions would require testing against a larger static dataset.

4.  **Missing Practical Details for Real-Time Application:** The paper targets interactive applications like gaming, yet it provides no performance metrics for the final policy execution. Information on the computational cost (e.g., milliseconds per frame on a specific CPU/GPU) and how it scales with model complexity is essential for assessing the practical viability of the method.

### **Detailed Questions and Suggestions**

*   **Goal Specification:** Is the goal location purely a 3D coordinate? Is it possible to also specify a target orientation for the character upon arrival? This seems important for many practical applications.
*   **Clarification on "Noise Injection":** The term "noise injection" for the sensory inputs (Section 2.1) could be misleading. Since the target action `a` is re-computed to be optimal for the perturbed state `s + ε`, this technique is more accurately described as **data augmentation**. This is distinct from the concept behind denoising autoencoders, where the network is trained to reconstruct the original, uncorrupted input.
*   **Initialization:** In Algorithm 1, is the first trajectory optimization step (Step 2) performed using a randomly initialized policy `θ̄`, or is it initialized without the policy-matching term `R`? The former seems likely to produce poor initial trajectories.
*   **Asynchronous Implementation:** Section 6 describes an asynchronous, distributed implementation that seems to differ from the strictly alternating scheme of Algorithm 1. This is an important practical detail that should be mentioned earlier and reconciled with the algorithm description.
*   **Policy Execution:** In Section 7, the action is `[ q̇des ṙdes f ]`, but the subsequent optimization seems to include `f des` (as `f` in the objective). Should this be `f des` in the action vector as well?
*   **Ablation Study:** The "no noise" experiment could be more informative if split into two separate tests: one without sensory noise/data augmentation, and one without hidden unit noise. This would isolate the contribution of each technique.
*   **Typos/Minor Corrections:**
    *   Pg. 2: "biped location" should likely be "biped locomotion".
    *   Pg. 2: "aim to unity" should be "aim to unite" or "unify".
    *   Acronym LQG is used before it is defined.
    *   The caption for Table 1a mentions "4 layers," but the table seems to show architectures with up to 3 hidden layers.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a promising and visually impressive application of neural networks for interactive character control. The core technical contributions—the joint optimization framework and the principled use of noise—are significant and well-motivated. However, the paper is held back by a lack of rigorous comparison to alternative methods and some unclear descriptions of key methodological components. Strengthening the experimental evaluation and improving the clarity of the technical sections would make this a much stronger contribution.
