The paper "Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates a method for controlling physically simulated characters interactively using a compact and efficient neural network to encode the control policy. The methodology involves an offline training phase that leverages trajectory optimization to learn this neural network policy. The principal contribution is showing that a single network architecture can be successfully applied to a variety of character morphologies and a diverse set of quasi-cyclic behaviors.

General Comments
----------------
This work appears to be an application of deep learning methodologies to established, albeit recent, techniques within computer graphics. The effectiveness of trajectory optimization for generating realistic control signals is well-documented in the existing literature, including some of the work cited by the authors. Similarly, the concept of employing neural networks to learn a policy that approximates or reproduces trajectories discovered by trajectory optimization has been explored in prior research (e.g., [9]).

Consequently, the authors need to more clearly articulate the novelty of their contribution in light of these previously published methods. A comparison to the recent work by researchers such as Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine, which seems highly relevant, is notably absent. How does the proposed method distinguish itself from theirs? For instance, does it offer advantages in terms of learning speed, applicability to a broader range of character models, lower runtime computational cost, or enhanced robustness to environmental perturbations?

Specific Comments
-----------------
*   Please clarify what is meant by the statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains."

*   The paper's narrative might be improved by repositioning the justification for not using LQG to a point after the proposed strategy has been fully introduced.

*   The notation used in lines 226-240 is potentially confusing. The introduction of s(X) = \bar s followed by a cost function involving s(X) - \bar s is difficult to parse due to the visual similarity of the bar and tilde notations.

*   The claim that the approach "produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ" requires more explanation. What is the baseline for this comparison (i.e., softer than what)? Furthermore, the definition of "soft" in the context of feedback gains should be explicitly stated.

*   The paper should provide a justification for modifying the initial state as part of the proposed procedure.

In summary, while the technical execution is sound, the paper's primary weakness is the insufficient clarity of its contribution relative to recent, related literature.
