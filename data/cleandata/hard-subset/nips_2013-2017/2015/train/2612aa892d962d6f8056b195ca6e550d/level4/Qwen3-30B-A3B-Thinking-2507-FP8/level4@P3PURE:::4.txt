

This paper proposes combining trajectory optimization with policy learning for interactive control of complex characters. The core contribution lies in jointly learning trajectories and policies, with the empirical insight that decoupling the optimization into alternating subproblems (i) enables reuse of well-documented existing methods and (ii) achieves sufficient practical performance. While the paper is technically sound and clearly written, I am not sufficiently familiar with this domain to assess its potential impact; the work demonstrates significant technical sophistication without novel theoretical insights.

The authors successfully integrate high-level conceptual rigor with meticulous implementation, yielding compelling results. The claimed advantages—specifically, (i) incorporating sensory noise during training and (ii) learning trajectories jointly with the policy—are clearly and convincingly demonstrated. The writing maintains an appropriate level of detail for the problem's complexity, with each section addressing a distinct aspect and providing useful cross-references.

I have a specific concern regarding Section 7's additional optimization step. Given the prior presentation, I expected the policy alone to suffice without further optimization. Is this due to a residual trajectory cost (or policy regression error) that accumulates when executing the policy's actions in the physical simulator? While I assume the dynamics model inherently enforces physical plausibility, preventing implausible states, I may be overlooking a key detail. I appreciate that re-optimization at each timestep enables larger timesteps, but the necessity of this step requires clearer justification.

Line 153 states "similar to recent approaches"; could the authors provide one or two representative references? The claim that constraining parameter updates in parameter space is beneficial is counterintuitive to me generally, though I understand its utility in their block-alternating framework.

Line 89 contains a typo: "to unify" should be "to unify." 

The results are impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation are sensible. However, as this research area is outside my primary expertise, I am unable to fully assess the novelty and significance of the contribution.
