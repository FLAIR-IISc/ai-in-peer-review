

This paper presents a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion. The authors address a significant challenge in character control: creating universal controllers that work across various morphologies and tasks without requiring motion capture or task-specific features. The core innovation lies in interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization), injecting noise during training, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains. While there is related work combining neural networks with trajectory optimization [9, 11], this paper demonstrates substantially more complex 3D locomotion behaviors, which previous methods could not achieve.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing a unified framework that replaces the disparate control architectures currently used for different character types (e.g., state machines for bipeds, contact schedules for quadrupeds, explicit cyclic encodings for flying/swimming). The authors demonstrate their method successfully controls a range of characters with a single network architecture and parameters, producing stable, realistic behaviors without task-specific hand-crafting. The approach is technically sound, with careful experimental validation across multiple character types and thorough ablation studies showing the necessity of their key innovations (joint optimization, noise injection).

The paper excels in several key areas. First, it demonstrates significant technical quality by solving a difficult problem in continuous high-dimensional spaces, which is rare in this domain. The method achieves interactive real-time control for complex 3D character behaviors where previous work was limited to 2D. Second, the work is highly significant as it provides a generalizable framework for character control that could impact animation, robotics, and biomechanics. Third, the paper is clear and well-organized, with a compelling presentation of results in the supplementary video. The authors demonstrate the method's robustness through extensive ablation studies showing that both joint optimization and noise injection are essential for good performance.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the requirement for full model knowledge (Eq. 6) is mentioned only in the trajectory optimization section, which is problematic as it represents a significant limitation for real-world applications where complete dynamics models may not be available. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation early in the paper and consider how their approach might be adapted for model-agnostic scenarios.

Second, the paper exhibits an imbalance in technical detail. Section 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) is disproportionately short compared to Section 6 (Distributed Training Architecture), which contains excessive detail about implementation specifics (e.g., AWS configurations, file locking mechanisms) that are not directly relevant to the scientific contribution. The authors should significantly condense the distributed training details and expand on the neural network architecture, particularly regarding the recurrent units mentioned in the abstract but not discussed in Section 4.

Third, the claim of "stochastic policy" in Section 4 is misleading. While the training data is stochastic due to noise injection, the neural network itself appears deterministic. The authors should clarify this distinction and explain how the stochasticity in training translates to robustness in the deterministic policy. This is particularly important given the paper's focus on robustness to perturbations.

Fourth, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with alternative approaches. While the authors mention Model Predictive Control (MPC) as a potential alternative, they do not provide a comprehensive analysis of how their method compares to other neural network control approaches (e.g., [3, 6, 14]) in terms of stability, computational efficiency, or the quality of generated behaviors.

The paper meets most NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with strong experimental validation (Quality), clearly written with sufficient detail for reproducibility (Clarity), presents a novel combination of known techniques that solves a challenging problem (Originality), and has significant potential impact for animation and robotics (Significance). The main shortcomings relate to clarity of limitations, balance of technical content, and precise terminology.

I recommend acceptance with the following conditions: (1) explicitly state the requirement for full model knowledge early in the paper and discuss its implications for real-world applications; (2) significantly condense the distributed training implementation details and expand the discussion of the neural network architecture, particularly regarding recurrent units; (3) clarify the relationship between stochastic training and the deterministic policy; and (4) provide a more comprehensive comparison with alternative neural network control methods.

This work represents a meaningful contribution to the field of character control, with the potential to significantly influence how we create interactive, physically plausible character behaviors across diverse applications. The authors have demonstrated a technically sophisticated approach that effectively bridges trajectory optimization and neural network learning, producing results that surpass previous methods in complexity and robustness.
