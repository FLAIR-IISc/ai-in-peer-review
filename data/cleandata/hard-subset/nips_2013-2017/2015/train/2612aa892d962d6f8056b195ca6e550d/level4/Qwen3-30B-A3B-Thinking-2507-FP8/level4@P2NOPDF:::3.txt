

The paper proposes a novel approach for real-time 3D character control using a neural network (NN) trained to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs, with joint training between CIO and the NN as a core innovation. Crucially, the authors alternate between optimizing CIO and the NN, using each other's outputs as part of the training criterion. A significant contribution is the strategic injection of noise—via data augmentation with perturbed trajectories and additive noise in hidden layers—which demonstrably enhances generalization. Experiments show the method generates realistic, stable control policies across diverse character models, addressing a challenging problem in real-time character animation.

This approach is compelling, as the apparent simplicity of "learning an NN to predict CIO output" masks substantial technical innovation in the joint training and noise injection mechanisms, which the authors rigorously validate. However, the experimental evaluation requires strengthening. The base training trajectories (100–200 trials, each with 5 branched segments) are limited, making the overfitting of the "no noise" variant unsurprising. Similarly, the "no joint" variant likely requires more diverse training data to establish robustness. The comparative evaluation is the paper's weakest aspect: the only comparison (to MPC) is brief, lacks a citation for MPC, and aligns with the authors' own acknowledgment that "systematic comparison with direct methods remains to be done." A broader benchmark against established baselines is essential.

The mathematical exposition is generally clear but contains gaps that hinder reproducibility. Key concepts like the CIO step (Eq. 6) lack sufficient notation definitions, and the explanation of optimal action generation under noisy inputs (Section 3.2) becomes confusing due to ambiguous notation (e.g., the dropped time index "t"). The authors should clarify whether "t" is implied for certain quantities. Additionally, providing code would significantly benefit the community. Computational efficiency is another critical omission: real-time applications (e.g., video games) demand timing data for control computations and scaling analysis with model complexity. Without this, the practical viability remains uncertain.

Several minor but important points require correction:  
- "biped location" → "biped locomotion"  
- "unity" → "unify"  
- Target constraints: Is the orientation of the character at the destination point unconstrained? If so, adding angle targets would be valuable for practical use.  
- "Noise injection" misrepresents the data augmentation step (where targets are re-computed for perturbed inputs); this is standard data augmentation, not denoising.  
- Algorithm 1: Clarify whether initial network weights θ are used to compute the first X (or if X is initialized without regularization).  
- Hyperparameter η: Justify its use in both steps of Algorithm 1.  
- Missing articles: "adapt them to the asynchronous and stochastic setting" and "use a Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass."  
- Typos: "f^{des}" (line 315), misplaced star in argmin (line 231), missing "\bar{s}" in \tilde{C}.  
- "The minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory" → likely missing "is" or "is the."  
- "sX and aX are subsets of φX" requires justification.  
- "A single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)" should reference Algorithm 1's objective (θ-optimized).  
- Section 6's asynchronous scheme differs significantly from Algorithm 1's alternating approach; this should be noted earlier.  
- Table 1a caption states "4 layers" but describes 3.  

The paper raises an interesting question: can the CIO hyperparameter α be reduced to zero during optimization (testing if the method relies on curriculum learning or requires trajectory fidelity)? This could clarify the method's core mechanism. Furthermore, the "no noise" variant should distinguish between "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" for clearer analysis.

Overall, the work presents a promising application of neural networks to character control, with a novel joint training framework and noise strategy. However, the experimental limitations—small training dataset, sparse comparisons, and lack of computational metrics—undermine claims of practical impact. The mathematical exposition, while mostly sound, requires greater precision to enable replication. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution to real-time character animation. The approach is compelling but requires empirical validation beyond the current scope to establish its broader applicability.
