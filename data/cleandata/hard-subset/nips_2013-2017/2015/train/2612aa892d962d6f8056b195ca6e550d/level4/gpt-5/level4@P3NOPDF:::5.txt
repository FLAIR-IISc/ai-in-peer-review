The paper presents promising results but would benefit from restructuring and several clarifications to improve readability and scientific transparency.

Major comments
- State the full-model-knowledge assumption up front. Equation 6 appears to require access to the full underlying model. This is a strong and central assumption that should be made explicit in the introduction and problem setup, not deferred to later sections. Please clarify precisely what “full model knowledge” entails (e.g., transition/observation models, gradients, likelihoods), discuss the practicality of this requirement in real-world settings, and outline how the approach behaves or degrades when only approximate or partial model information is available.
- Balance Sections 4 and 6. The exposition feels disproportionate: Section 6 is extensive, while Section 4 is comparatively thin. Consider moving some details from Section 6 into an appendix and expanding Section 4 to clearly articulate the core method and design choices. This will help readers understand the approach before diving into results and analysis.
- Ensure Section 4 covers the recurrent units mentioned in the abstract. The abstract highlights the use of recurrent units, but Section 4 does not adequately describe them. Please include:
  - The specific recurrent architecture (e.g., LSTM, GRU, custom cell), including equations and parameterization.
  - How the recurrent state is initialized, updated, and used (e.g., for filtering, memory, or prediction).
  - The training objective and gradients as they flow through time (BPTT details, truncation length, regularization).
  - Any ablations or sensitivity analyses showing the contribution of the recurrent component versus non-recurrent baselines.
- Clarify the claim of a “stochastic neural network” (line 405). As written, the network itself appears deterministic, with stochasticity arising only from the data or environment. If the model is truly stochastic, please specify where randomness enters the network (e.g., stochastic units, variational sampling, noise injection, dropout at test time, Bayesian weights) and how this affects training and inference. If no such mechanisms are present, consider revising the terminology to avoid confusion and to accurately reflect that the method is a deterministic network trained on stochastic data.

Specific suggestions
- Introduce assumptions and requirements early. In addition to highlighting the full-model-knowledge requirement, summarize all key assumptions in the introduction or a dedicated “Assumptions and Scope” subsection. Explain their motivation and implications for applicability.
- Improve the narrative flow. A suggested structure:
  1) Problem setup and assumptions (including full model knowledge and any stationarity/Markov assumptions);
  2) Method overview with a clear role for recurrent units;
  3) Detailed methodology (training objective, optimization, computational complexity);
  4) Experimental setup (datasets, baselines, metrics, implementation details);
  5) Results with ablations and sensitivity; and
  6) Limitations and applicability.
- Expand methodological clarity:
  - Provide a concise derivation or intuition for Equation 6 and its relationship to preceding equations.
  - State whether gradients require differentiating through the model components and whether any estimators (e.g., reparameterization, score-function) are used.
  - If recurrent units approximate a latent belief state, clarify how this relates to canonical filters and whether supervision or auxiliary losses are used to encourage faithful state tracking.
- Tighten Section 6. Focus on the most informative analyses and move auxiliary plots or extended comparisons to an appendix. Ensure every subsection ties back to a concrete methodological choice from Section 4.
- Terminology and consistency:
  - Use “stochastic neural network” only if there is intrinsic model stochasticity.
  - Keep notation consistent across sections, particularly for latent states, observations, and model parameters.
  - Cross-reference equations where assumptions are applied (e.g., when Equation 6 is invoked).
- Recurrent unit details:
  - Report hidden-state dimensionality, initialization scheme, and any regularization (dropout, layer norm).
  - Describe sequence lengths, truncation strategy, and handling of variable-length sequences.
  - If applicable, clarify whether teacher forcing, scheduled sampling, or filtering/smoothing objectives are used.
