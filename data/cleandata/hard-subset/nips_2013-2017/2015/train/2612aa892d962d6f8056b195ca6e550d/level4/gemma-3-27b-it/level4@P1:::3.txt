In this submission, the authors propose an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, where a neural network (NN) is used to generate animations after being trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Importantly, CIO and NN training are not independent; they are performed in an alternating fashion, with the output of one informing the criterion optimized by the other. Another core element of the method is the injection of noise, achieved through data augmentation via small perturbations, as well as additive noise in the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that such a network can produce realistic and stable control policies for a range of diverse character models.

This is a very interesting approach, given that real-time control of arbitrary models is a challenging task. While the proposed method may initially appear relatively straightforward (“learn a NN to predict the output of CIO”), the additional steps to make it work – joint training and noise injection – represent significant new contributions and demonstrably improve generalization.

The experimental results would be strengthened by comparisons with a larger dataset. Given that the base training trajectories were generated with only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” overfitting in the “no noise” variant is unsurprising. The “no joint” variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories, though this is less obvious. 

Overall, the comparative evaluation is a relative weakness of the paper (acknowledged in the introduction: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with Model Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper (and MPC is not referenced).

For the most part, the mathematics are clearly explained and motivated, though replicating these results would require consulting additional references, as some points are addressed only superficially – particularly the CIO step (see Eq. 6, whose notations are not fully defined). Providing code would be greatly appreciated by the community. There is one section I found difficult to fully understand: section 3.2 concerning the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs. The notations became confusing, and it was unclear what was being done (specifically, the time index “t” was dropped, but its implication remained for some quantities).

It would also be useful to have timing measurements for computations during real-time control, as this could be a major bottleneck. Knowing how these computations scale with the complexity of the 3D model is also important. For applications like video games, limited CPU resources are available for animating a single character on screen.

Additional minor remarks:
* “biped location”: should this be “locomotion”?
* “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches”: should this be “unite” or “unify”?
* Am I correct that the target is only the x, y, z coordinates, and there is no constraint on the character's orientation when reaching the target? If so, would it be easy to add an orientation target, which seems important in practice?
* I would not describe the step where additional data is generated from noisy inputs as “noise injection,” as the target is re-computed to match the modified inputs. This is simply “data augmentation” and does not relate to the denoising autoencoder concept.
* Are you using the initial (random) network weights θ in the very first iteration to compute the first X’s in Alg. 1? Or are you starting with X’s computed without the regularization term?
* What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter η in the two steps of Alg. 1?
* “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”: missing “the”?
* Acronym LQG is used one line before it is defined.
* “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”: missing “a”?
* l. 231 the star in the argmin is misplaced, and \bar{s} is missing in \tilde{C}?
* “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: is a word missing?
* “sX and aX are subsets of \phiX”: why is this guaranteed?
* “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: it would be more accurate to say the objective from Alg. 1, as only θ is optimized.
* The asynchronous joint optimization scheme described in Section 6 is distinct from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1. It would be useful to mention this earlier.
* l. 315, f should be f^{des}?
* “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.”: This sentence is unclear.
* The "no noise" variant in the experiments could be split into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" for clarity.
* A question I would find interesting to investigate is whether α can be decreased to zero during optimization (for CIO only). In other words, is the method working primarily due to a “curriculum learning” effect (easier task initially), or is it always beneficial to keep the trajectories learned by CIO close to what the network can learn?
* Caption of Table 1a states 4 layers, but I believe this is 3.

This is a promising application of neural networks to character control, though its practical benefits and applicability still need further validation.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks for real-time control.
* Demonstrated ability to control diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture.
* The method addresses a challenging problem in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
* The inclusion of noise injection and joint training significantly improves generalization.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Limited comparative evaluation.
* Relatively small dataset used for training.
* Some sections (e.g., 3.2) are difficult to follow.
* Lack of timing measurements for real-time control performance.
* Code is not provided.
