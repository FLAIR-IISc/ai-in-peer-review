"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates how a compact, efficient neural network can serve as a control policy for real-time character control within a physics simulator.
The method employs trajectory optimization during training to learn this neural network policy. The key contribution lies in demonstrating the versatility of a single network architecture across a spectrum of largely cyclic behaviors and diverse body morphologies.

General Comments ----------------
I view this paper as an application of deep learning to established (though recent) techniques within computer graphics.
Prior research has already established the capacity of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals, and several relevant works are cited.
Furthermore, the use of neural networks to approximate the sequential actions required to replicate trajectories identified through trajectory optimization is not novel (e.g., [9]).
Therefore, the authors should more clearly delineate the relationship between their work and these existing approaches.
Specifically, the recent work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine represents a particularly relevant comparison point. How does this approach compare? Is it designed for faster learning, broader support for body shapes, reduced runtime computation, or increased robustness to environmental variations?

Specific Comments -----------------
Could you elaborate on the statement, "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains"?
Consider postponing the discussion of why LQG was not utilized until after the proposed strategy has been fully presented.
The notation surrounding lines 226-240 is unclear. The introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by a cost function defined in terms of s(X) - \bar s, is visually confusing due to the similarity of the bars and squiggles.
What is meant by “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda”? Softer relative to what baseline? What constitutes “soft” versus “not soft” gains? Additionally, what is the rationale behind modifying the initial state as part of the procedure? The work is sound, but the novelty and contribution relative to recent related research require further clarification.
