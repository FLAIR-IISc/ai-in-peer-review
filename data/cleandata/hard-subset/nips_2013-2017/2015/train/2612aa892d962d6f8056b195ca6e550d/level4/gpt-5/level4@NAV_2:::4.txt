This paper integrates trajectory optimization with policy learning to enable interactive control of complex articulated characters. The central idea is to learn trajectories and the policy jointly, while practically alternating between the two optimization subproblems to i) leverage established, well-documented methods and ii) achieve satisfactory performance in practice. I was able to follow the paper, but I’m not sufficiently embedded in this specific subarea to fully assess the potential impact; on its face, the work is a notable technical achievement, though it does not appear to introduce new theoretical insights.

The authors demonstrate a high level of technical rigor from conceptual design through implementation, and the resulting behaviors are compelling. The benefits of i) injecting sensory noise during training and ii) co-optimizing trajectories with the policy are convincingly supported by the experiments.

The paper is also clearly presented, with an appropriate level of technical detail given the complexity of the system. Each section addresses a distinct component of the approach, and the cross-references between sections are helpful.

I think the motivation for the additional optimization step in Section 7 could be clarified. Based on earlier sections, I expected the learned policy to run without further optimization. Is the extra step needed due to residual trajectory cost or policy regression error, such that directly applying policy outputs in a forward simulator would lead to error accumulation? My expectation would be that the forward dynamics already enforce physical constraints and prevent implausible states, but I may be missing a subtlety. I do appreciate that per-timestep re-optimization enables using larger timesteps.
