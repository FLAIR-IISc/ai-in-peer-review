- Presents a framework where a neural controller is trained offline to emulate Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) for real-time control of 3D articulated characters.  
- Key technical contributions are alternating/joint optimization between CIO and the neural policy and structured noise injection (input perturbations with feedback gains and hidden-layer noise).  
- Learned policies produce stable, lifelike behaviors across diverse morphologies and tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal).  
- The approach is compelling given the difficulty of real-time control for diverse bodies, and the two additions materially improve robustness and generalization.  
- Empirical support is limited by modest training data (≈100–200 trials with 5 branches), making overfitting in ablations unsurprising.  
- Comparative evaluation is weak: only a brief, uncited MPC comparison is included and broader benchmarks are missing.  
- The “no joint” baseline might underrepresent the real gap due to limited trajectory diversity.  
- Mathematical development is mostly clear but several symbols (e.g., in Eq. 6) are undefined and reproducing results requires consulting prior work (CIO).  
- Public release of code would be valuable for reproducibility.  
- Section 3.2 (feedback gains for noisy inputs) is hard to parse: notation is dense and the time index t disappears, making perturbation timing unclear.  
- Runtime/execution-time measurements are missing, yet important because per-frame constrained optimization could be a CPU bottleneck in practical deployments (games, many agents).  
- Minor language/typo issues: “biped location” → locomotion, “unity” → unite/unify, missing articles and grammar errors throughout.  
- Clarify whether goal targets include orientation as well as x,y,z position and whether adding orientation is straightforward.  
- The term “noise injection” may be misleading for input perturbations because targets are adjusted (more like data augmentation than denoising).  
- Clarify Algorithm 1: are initial X computed using random θ or solved without the regularizer?  
- Explain choice and sensitivity of using the same proximal weight η for both trajectory and network subproblems.  
- LQG acronym and some phrasing are used before definition or missing words (several instances noted).  
- Several equation/text issues need fixes (misplaced star in argmin, missing s̄ in C̃, article omissions, possibly wrong f vs fdes).  
- Justify claims like “sX and aX are subsets of φX” or correct the statement if inaccurate.  
- The description of Algorithm 1 vs. Section 6’s asynchronous scheme should be reconciled or foreshadowed earlier, since they differ materially.  
- Clarify implication of “dynamics constraints are enforced softly” (e.g., root forces in simulation).  
- Recommend separating the “no noise” ablation into input-target data augmentation vs. hidden activation noise to isolate effects.  
- Ask whether α can be annealed to 0 (CIO-only) and whether benefits are curriculum-driven or persist throughout training.  
- Correct Table 1a caption (says 4 layers but appears to be 3).  
- Overall: attractive and promising application of neural control for physically based characters, but practical impact would be clearer with stronger baselines, fuller ablations, and execution-time benchmarks.
