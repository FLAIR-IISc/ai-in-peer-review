"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates the effectiveness of a small, fast neural network in representing a control policy for interactive character control in a physics simulator. The approach utilizes trajectory optimization during the training phase to learn a neural network policy. The key contribution lies in showing that the same architecture can be applied across various cyclic behaviors and body types.

General Comments:
Upon reviewing this paper, it appears to be an application of deep learning to existing techniques in computer graphics. The ability of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals has been established in prior work, some of which is cited in the paper. The use of neural networks to approximate step-by-step actions for tracing out trajectories similar to those found by trajectory optimization has also been explored in previous studies (e.g., [9]). Therefore, the authors need to provide a clearer positioning of their work relative to these previously published approaches.

A notable comparison can be made to the recent line of work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. The authors should discuss how their approach compares to this work. Specifically, are there any advantages in terms of learning speed, support for diverse body shapes, computational requirements at runtime, or robustness to environmental variabilities?

Specific Comments:
The statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" requires further clarification. It would be beneficial to move the discussion of why LQG was not used until after presenting the proposed strategy.

The notation between lines 226-240 is confusing. The introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by a cost definition in terms of s(X) - \bar s, is visually unclear due to the similarity between the bars and squiggles.

The claim "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda" needs more context. Specifically, softer than what? What does it mean for gains to be soft or not soft, and why is this a desirable property? Additionally, it is unclear why changing the initial state as part of the procedure is beneficial. Overall, the work is sound, but the contribution relative to other recent related work is not clearly established.
