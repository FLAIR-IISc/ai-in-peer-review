Summary
The paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers by coupling trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning. The key idea is to alternate between (i) direct trajectory optimization via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate state–action pairs and optimal local feedback gains, and (ii) neural policy regression that matches these actions under noisy sensory inputs. The policy outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and updates to a small recurrent memory; in effect, the network learns both a feedback policy and an implicit local dynamics model. Noise is injected in inputs and hidden activations to encourage robustness, while a perturbation-based procedure extracts well-conditioned feedback gains from the direct optimizer with minimal overhead. Training is distributed across a cluster (for trajectory generation) with GPU-accelerated neural regression, and the final policy is executed in real time by solving a one-step constrained optimization at each control step. The authors demonstrate diverse 3D behaviors—swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion—using a single architecture and training pipeline, without motion capture or hand-tuned state machines.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on CIO for contact-rich motion synthesis (Mordatch et al., 2012) and extends ideas of learning policies from optimized trajectories (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). Unlike model-free methods in discrete or continuous control (e.g., DQN, TRPO), the approach leverages trajectory optimization to generate high-quality supervision and explicit feedback gains. Compared to MPC, which often yields effective but non-periodic solutions, the joint optimization here regularizes trajectories toward predictable, cyclic behaviors suitable for interactive control.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear, with a relaxed objective that penalizes deviations between policy outputs and optimizer-provided actions under injected noise. The perturbation-based derivation of feedback gains cleverly reuses Gauss-Newton structure and the Woodbury identity, avoiding a separate ill-conditioned LQG pass. The distributed training loop with proximal terms is plausible and empirically convergent.
- Empirical support: Results span multiple morphologies and tasks in 3D, a challenging regime. Ablations indicate substantial value from joint optimization and noise injection, and a brief architecture sweep is provided.
- Limitations: Evaluation is primarily qualitative. Quantitative metrics (tracking error, stability margins, task completion rates, energy/torque use) are limited, and comparisons to strong model-based MPC baselines or model-free RL on the same tasks are not systematic. Execution-time control relies on a soft-constrained T=1 optimization that can include root forces; the impact on physical fidelity and transfer to real hardware is unclear. Sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., λ for gain softness, α, η, noise scales) and data requirements are only lightly explored.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The learning–optimization alternation, noise models, and gain computation are explained clearly. A few minor typos and notation inconsistencies can be fixed easily. Additional detail on cost terms and training distributions would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The components—trajectory optimization, supervised policy learning with noise, and recurrent networks—are known, but their integration at this scale, especially the efficient extraction of stabilizing feedback gains from a direct optimizer and the consistent success on 3D contact-rich tasks with a single architecture, is novel and compelling.

Significance
The method offers a practical path to robust, interactive controllers for complex characters without motion capture or hand-designed state machines. The approach is likely to influence both character animation and robotics, especially where contact dynamics and real-time responsiveness are critical.

Strengths
- Strong, diverse 3D results across multiple morphologies with a single training pipeline.
- Elegant coupling of CIO and neural policy regression, including principled noise injection.
- Perturbation-based feedback gain computation that is efficient and well conditioned.
- Asynchronous, scalable training architecture; policies run in real time at test time.
- Clear ablations demonstrating the necessity of joint optimization and noise.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Predominantly qualitative evaluation; add quantitative metrics (e.g., goal error, disturbances, energy/torque, foot slip, falls per minute).
- Limited head-to-head comparisons with MPC and leading model-free RL methods under identical tasks and compute budgets.
- Execution-time reliance on soft constraints and possible root forces may affect realism and deployment on robots; clarifying constraint satisfaction and removing nonphysical forces would strengthen the claim.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ (gain softness) and noise scales; report stability of rollouts under distribution shift.
- Provide more details or code for reproducibility: cost weights, network initialization, data generation protocols.

Arguments for acceptance
- Demonstrates robust, realistic 3D control across diverse systems, a clear advance over prior trajectory-to-policy work.
- Technical contribution of feedback gain extraction from direct optimization is useful and broadly applicable.
- Method is practical, scalable, and results are visually and behaviorally compelling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of thorough quantitative evaluation and limited comparative baselines may make it hard to assess advantages definitively.
- Some reliance on soft constraints and simulation-specific tricks could limit transferability.

Overall recommendation
An excellent, polished piece of work that thoughtfully integrates trajectory optimization with neural policy learning to produce robust, interactive controllers for complex characters. Despite the need for deeper quantitative comparisons and clarification of execution-time physics, the contribution is significant and timely. Strong accept.
