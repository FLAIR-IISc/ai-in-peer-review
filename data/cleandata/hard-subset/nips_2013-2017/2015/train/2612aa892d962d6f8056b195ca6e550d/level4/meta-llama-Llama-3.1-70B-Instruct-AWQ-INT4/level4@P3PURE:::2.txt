"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates the effectiveness of a small, fast neural network in representing a control policy for interactive control of a character in a physics simulator. The approach utilizes trajectory optimization in a training phase to learn a neural network policy. A key contribution of this work is showing that the same architecture can be applied across a range of cyclic behaviors and various body types.

General Comments:
Upon reading this paper, it appears to be an application of deep learning to existing techniques in computer graphics, albeit recent ones. The capability of trajectory optimization to find realistic control signals has been established in previous work, some of which is cited in this paper. The use of neural networks to approximate the step-by-step actions required of a policy to replicate trajectories similar to those found by trajectory optimization has also been explored in prior work, such as [9]. Therefore, the authors must provide a clearer positioning of their work relative to these previously published approaches.

A notable comparison is the recent line of work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. It would be beneficial for the authors to compare their approach to this work, highlighting potential advantages such as faster learning, support for a wider variety of body shapes, reduced computational requirements at runtime, or increased robustness to environmental variabilities.

Specific Comments:
The statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" requires clarification. Consider repositioning the discussion of why Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) control was not used until after presenting the proposed strategy. The notation between lines 226-240 is confusing, as the introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by the definition of a cost in terms of s(X) - \bar s, involves visually similar bars and squiggles.

The claim "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda" is unclear. Specifically, it is unclear what is meant by "softer" gains, how they compare to alternative approaches, and why this is a desirable property. Additionally, it is not explicitly stated why changing the initial state as part of the procedure is beneficial. Overall, the work is sound, but the contribution relative to other recent related work could be more clearly articulated.
