## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper demonstrates the use of a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive character control within a physics simulator. The core approach leverages trajectory optimization during a training phase to learn this neural network policy. The primary contribution, as presented, is the demonstration that a single network architecture effectively generalizes across a range of cyclic behaviors and diverse body types.

**General Comments:**

My assessment of this work is that it represents an application of deep learning techniques to established – though recent – methods within computer graphics and control. The efficacy of trajectory optimization in generating realistic control signals is well-documented in prior research, including several works cited by the authors. Furthermore, utilizing neural networks to approximate the sequential actions required to reproduce trajectories obtained through trajectory optimization is not entirely novel, with related approaches exemplified by, for instance, [9]. Consequently, the authors need to more clearly articulate the specific advancements offered by their work in relation to these existing approaches. 

A particularly relevant line of research is that by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. A detailed comparison to their work would be highly beneficial. The authors should explicitly address how their approach differs – for example, does it aim for faster learning, broader applicability across body morphologies, reduced runtime computational cost, or increased robustness to environmental variations? Clearly defining these distinctions is crucial for establishing the novelty and impact of this contribution. While the results are promising, the current presentation lacks sufficient justification for why this approach is preferable or complementary to existing methods.

**Specific Comments:**

1. **Feedback Gains:** The statement “we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” requires further clarification. What specifically constitutes “very large” and “ill-conditioned” in this context? Providing quantitative examples or a more precise definition would improve understanding.

2. **Motivation for Strategy:** The discussion regarding the decision not to utilize an LQG approach feels somewhat preemptive. I suggest relocating this explanation to *after* the authors have fully presented their chosen strategy, providing a clearer rationale based on the advantages of their method.

3. **Notation Clarity (Lines 226-240):** The notation used in this section is visually confusing. The introduction of s(X) = \bar s, followed by a cost function defined in terms of s(X) - \bar s, relies on visually similar symbols (bars and squiggles) which hinders readability. Consider alternative notation to avoid ambiguity.

4. **“Softer” Feedback Gains:** The phrase “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda” is ambiguous. Softer relative to *what*? A clearer definition of “soft” and “hard” gains, and the benefits of achieving softer gains in this context, are necessary.

5. **Initial State Perturbation:** The purpose of intentionally modifying the initial state as part of the procedure is unclear. Providing a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind this step would strengthen the paper. 

**Overall:**

This work presents a sound technical approach to character control, and the demonstrated generalization across diverse morphologies is noteworthy. However, the paper currently lacks a sufficiently detailed and critical comparison to related work. Strengthening this aspect of the paper, and clarifying the specific advantages of the proposed method, would significantly enhance its impact and contribution to the field. Addressing the specific points raised above regarding notation, definitions, and motivations will also improve clarity and readability.
