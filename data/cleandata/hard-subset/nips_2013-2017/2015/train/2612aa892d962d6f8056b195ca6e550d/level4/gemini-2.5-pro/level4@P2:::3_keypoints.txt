- Paper proposes jointly training an RNN policy with Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) in an alternating loop, using systematic noise injection for robustness.  
- Demonstrated on diverse 3D characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) producing stable, realistic locomotion without motion-capture.  

Strengths:
- Addresses an important problem (real-time controllers for arbitrary physical models) with novel joint optimization and principled noise/feedback usage.  
- Method is general, using one architecture and training algorithm across varied morphologies and dynamics.  
- High-quality results: emergent cyclic gaits and realistic behaviors shown in supplementary video.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparative evaluation: lacks quantitative comparisons to state-of-the-art RL or policy-learning baselines.  
- Ablation studies may be inconclusive due to small training dataset (100–200 trials with 5 branches each), risking overfitting explanations.  
- Clarity and reproducibility concerns: Section 3.2 notation is confusing, CIO and cost description are superficial, and runtime/per-frame performance metrics are missing.  

Minor corrections:
- Typos: "biped location" → "biped locomotion"; "to unity these disparate approaches" → "to unify these disparate approaches"; Table 1 caption mismatch about number of layers.  

Clarification questions:
- Does target g_k include orientation or only position?  
- How are initial trajectories X_i generated in Algorithm 1—using an initial random θ or without R term?  
- Asynchronous optimization in Sec. 6 differs from Algorithm 1’s alternating scheme—please clarify.  
- In Sec. 7, should f_des be f from the policy output?  
- Explain "dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation."  
- In Sec. 3.2, are s_X and a_X guaranteed subsets of φ_X?  

Suggestions:
- Rename "noise injection" in Sec. 2.1 to emphasize data augmentation vs additive noise distinction; separate sensory augmentation from hidden-unit noise in ablations.  
- Strengthen ablations by using a larger, more diverse trajectory dataset and split the "no noise" variant into two experiments (no sensory augmentation vs no hidden-unit noise).  
- Experiment with annealing penalty weight α to determine whether joint optimization acts as curriculum or remains necessary throughout training.  

Overall verdict:
- High-quality and significant work with convincing results, but would benefit from stronger comparative experiments and clearer, more reproducible technical descriptions.
