This paper presents a method for synthesizing real-time, interactive controllers for a diverse set of complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to train a recurrent neural network to act as a feedback controller that mimics the behavior of an offline trajectory optimizer. The training process involves an alternating optimization scheme where a trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) generates optimal state-action trajectories for various tasks, and a neural network is then trained via supervised regression on this data. The authors introduce several key technical contributions to make this approach successful for challenging 3D locomotion tasks, including: injecting noise into sensory inputs and hidden unit activations to improve robustness; using the trajectory optimizer to compute optimal feedback gains for training with these noisy inputs; and employing a distributed architecture to manage the significant computational cost. This work builds upon recent efforts that combine trajectory optimization with function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but demonstrates success on a substantially more difficult and diverse set of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Generality:** The primary strength of this work is its ability to automatically generate high-quality controllers for a wide range of character morphologies (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flyer) and tasks using a single, unified algorithm. This represents a significant advance over traditional methods that require hand-crafting task-specific state machines or simplified models for each new character. The resulting behaviors, as shown in the supplementary video, are stable, realistic, and purposeful.
*   **Technical Contributions:** The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a novel and clever way to generate richer training data for the policy. This, combined with principled noise injection during training, appears crucial for learning robust policies. The ablation study in Figure 2 compellingly demonstrates that both the joint optimization and the noise injection are essential for good performance, validating the paper's core technical claims.
*   **Quality of Results:** The empirical results are impressive. The method successfully learns stable locomotion in 3D, a challenging domain where characters can easily lose balance. The emergence of natural gaits (e.g., walking for bipeds, trotting for quadrupeds) without explicit encoding is a strong testament to the effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Assumptions:** A critical requirement of this method is access to the full dynamics model of the system and its derivatives (e.g., Equation 6 for equations of motion, contact constraints, etc.). This is a strong assumption that distinguishes the work from model-free reinforcement learning approaches. While appropriate for this problem domain, this dependency should be stated explicitly and prominently in the introduction to better frame the paper's contribution and context.
*   **Clarity of Network Architecture:** The abstract highlights the use of "recurrent units that implement memory states," but Section 4, which describes the neural network, is brief and does not explain how these recurrent states (`r` and `r_dot`) are handled. Is it a standard RNN architecture like an LSTM or GRU, or are the previous recurrent states simply fed in as part of the input vector to a feed-forward network? This is a crucial architectural detail that is currently missing and essential for reproducibility.
*   **Clarity of Terminology:** The paper uses the term "stochastic neural networks" (line 405). This is potentially misleading, as the policy appears to be deterministic at execution time (Section 7). The stochasticity is introduced during the training process through noise injection in the sensory inputs and hidden activations. The authors should clarify this distinction to avoid confusion.
*   **Paper Organization and Balance:** The paper's structure feels imbalanced. Section 3 provides a thorough and detailed account of the trajectory optimization component. In contrast, Section 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) and Section 6 (Distributed Training Architecture) are disproportionately brief. More details on the network training procedure (e.g., optimizer used, learning rates, batch sizes) would strengthen the paper and improve its clarity.

In summary, this paper presents a powerful and well-realized framework for creating controllers for complex characters, with strong results and novel technical contributions. The main weaknesses relate to the clarity of presentation rather than the method's technical soundness or significance. Addressing the points above, particularly by clarifying the network architecture and the assumption of a known model, would significantly improve the manuscript.
