Summary:
The paper proposes an alternating framework that combines trajectory optimization with policy learning to achieve interactive control of complex characters. The key idea is to jointly learn trajectories and the policy, while decoupling the optimization into alternating subproblems so that existing solvers can be reused. The paper also advocates training with sensory noise. The approach is technically sophisticated and produces impressive results across challenging control tasks.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical performance with convincing demonstrations. The claimed benefits of injecting sensory noise during training and jointly optimizing trajectories with the policy are well supported by experiments and ablations.
- Thoughtful system design: the alternating scheme effectively leverages well-established components, improving practicality without overcomplicating the pipeline.
- Clear and well-structured presentation. The paper maintains a good level of detail given the problem complexity, and the cross-references between sections make the narrative easy to follow.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Limited theoretical novelty. While the engineering is impressive, the paper does not appear to introduce new theory or guarantees. The novelty seems primarily in the integration and the empirical insights.
- Motivation for the extra optimization step in Section 7 could be strengthened. Based on earlier sections, I expected the learned policy to operate without online re-optimization. Please clarify:
  - Is the additional optimization primarily to correct residual trajectory cost or policy regression error that would otherwise compound under the simulator?
  - If the policy outputs physically feasible actions and the simulator enforces constraints, what concrete failure modes does the online optimization prevent (e.g., drift from reference, instability near contact, sensitivity to modeling error)?
  - What are the trade-offs in terms of accuracy, stability, and compute when running policy-only vs. policy+online optimization? An ablation comparing these modes across tasks and timesteps would be very helpful.
  - You note that re-optimizing at each timestep can allow larger control timesteps. Could you quantify this and discuss how it interacts with simulator integration step size and contact handling?
- Novelty and significance are somewhat difficult for me to fully gauge, as this specific subarea is slightly outside my core expertise. The method is clearly solid, but a sharper positioning relative to closely related alternating or guided policy search methods would help justify significance.

Clarity and presentation:
- Overall, the paper is clearly written and well organized. The level of implementation detail feels appropriate and actionable.
- It might help to include a brief schematic summarizing the alternating loop, data flow between policy learning and trajectory optimization, and where noise is injected.

Requests for clarification and additional references:
- Line 153: “similar to recent approaches…” Please cite 1–2 representative methods that constrain policy updates or trust regions. Candidates include TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015), Conservative Policy Iteration (Kakade & Langford, 2002), Natural Policy Gradient (Kakade, 2001), and Guided Policy Search variants (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2013/2014) or iterative LQG/LQR-based schemes (Todorov & Li, 2004).
- Please clarify how small updates in parameter space are enforced (e.g., KL/divergence constraints, penalties, trust regions), and why this is particularly beneficial in the alternating setup you use.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- Provide a focused ablation on the alternation itself: compare end-to-end policy learning without alternating trajectory optimization, pure trajectory optimization with MPC at test time, and your alternating approach. Report stability, sample efficiency, wall-clock time, and final performance.
- Discuss generalization: how well do policies extrapolate beyond the specific trajectories used in training, and how sensitive is performance to the coverage of the trajectory distribution?
- Include failure cases or sensitivity to modeling error, sensor noise levels, and timestep choices.

Minor comments:
- Typo at line 89: “to unify.”
- Please ensure all claims of similarity to prior work are supported by citations (e.g., line 153).

Overall assessment:
This is a strong empirical paper with substantial engineering contributions and high-quality results. The alternating optimization framework is compelling in practice, and the demonstrations convincingly show the benefits of noise injection and joint trajectory–policy learning. The primary weakness is the limited theoretical novelty and the somewhat incomplete motivation for the additional online optimization step. With clearer justification, stronger positioning relative to closely related methods, and a few targeted ablations, I would lean toward acceptance.
