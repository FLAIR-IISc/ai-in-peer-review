

Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks presents a method for using a compact, efficient neural network to represent a control policy for character animation in physics-based simulators. The approach leverages trajectory optimization during training to learn a neural policy, demonstrating that the same architecture generalizes across diverse cyclic behaviors and body types.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This work applies deep learning to established techniques in computer graphics, specifically extending trajectory optimization for control policy learning. The capability of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals is well-documented in prior literature (including cited works). Similarly, approximating policy actions via neural networks to replicate trajectory-optimization outputs has been explored previously (e.g., [9]). Consequently, the authors must more clearly articulate the novel contributions of this work relative to these existing approaches.  

The closest relevant work is the line of research by Abbeel and Levine. How does this approach compare to theirs? Does it aim to learn faster? Support broader body-type diversity? Reduce runtime computation? Enhance robustness to environmental variations? The paper does not sufficiently address these distinctions, leaving the incremental value ambiguous.  

**Specific Comments**  
1.  **Clarity of technical claims**: The phrase *"we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains"* requires elaboration. What specific numerical properties (e.g., near-zero eigenvalues) indicate ill-conditioning?  
2.  **Organization**: The discussion of why LQG was not used should follow the presentation of the proposed strategy, not precede it.  
3.  **Notation**: Lines 226–240 introduce confusing notation: *s(X) = \bar{s}* followed by a cost defined as *s(X) - \bar{s}*. The visual similarity between the bar (overline) and squiggle (tilde) in *s* and *s̄* is misleading and should be revised.  
4.  **"Soft" feedback gains**: The claim *"our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ"* lacks context. Softer than what? What does "soft" mean in this context (e.g., reduced gain magnitude, smoother transitions)? Clarify the benefit of this modification.  
5.  **Initial state adjustment**: The rationale for modifying the initial state during the procedure is unclear. Why is this step necessary or advantageous?  

**Conclusion**  
The core idea—using a neural network to approximate trajectory-optimized policies across diverse behaviors and body types—is plausible and technically sound. However, the contribution relative to recent work (e.g., Abbeel/Levine) is not sufficiently justified. The paper would benefit from:  
- A sharper comparison to prior methods, explicitly addressing learning speed, scalability, runtime efficiency, and robustness.  
- Clarification of technical terms (*soft gains*, *ill-conditioned gains*) and notation.  
- Improved logical flow, particularly regarding the LQG discussion.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s novelty and significance remain unclear. The method shows promise but fails to convincingly position itself within the broader landscape of policy learning for character control.
