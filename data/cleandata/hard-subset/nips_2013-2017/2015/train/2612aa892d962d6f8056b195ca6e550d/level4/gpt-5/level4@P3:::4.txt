Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse 3D characters (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds). The key idea is to alternate between trajectory optimization (using Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised policy learning, with two critical additions: (i) injecting sensory and hidden-unit noise to improve robustness and (ii) using a perturbation-based method to compute local optimal feedback gains “for free” from the CIO Hessian. Runtime control combines the learned policy with a fast T=1 optimization step that enforces dynamics and contacts, enabling real-time interactive control.

The work is closely related to “learning from an optimizer” approaches such as guided policy search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14), but extends them to a challenging 3D, multi-morphology setting with more realistic contact-rich behaviors. Compared to model-free deep RL (e.g., Mnih et al. DQN) or policy gradient methods (e.g., TRPO), this method leverages a powerful model-based optimizer to generate training data and local feedback, reducing exploration burden and yielding more regular, human-interpretable motions. It also connects to graphics/robotics controllers such as SIMBICON, ZMP-based control, CPGs, and MPC, but aims to unify diverse behaviors without hand-crafted state machines or motion capture.

Strengths:
- Technical depth and engineering: The alternating optimization framework, the efficient computation of feedback gains from the Gauss-Newton system, and the distributed asynchronous training infrastructure are well thought out and practically effective.
- Breadth of results: A single architecture and training pipeline produce stable, realistic behaviors across very different morphologies in 3D, which is substantially harder than 2D cases addressed by prior work.
- Robustness via noise: The role of sensory and hidden-unit noise is convincingly demonstrated via ablations; policies trained without noise or without joint optimization deteriorate markedly at test time.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is generally well written and well structured, with useful cross-references between sections.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Runtime “extra optimization” (Section 7): The method does a T=1 optimization at each step to enforce dynamics, contacts, and match the network’s desired velocities/forces. This is sensible and likely stabilizes rollouts, but it blurs the line between pure policy execution and MPC. Please clarify:
  - Why the learned policy alone (fed into a physics integrator) is insufficient. Is it primarily due to residual regression error and contact complementarity? How large is the discrepancy if the optimizer is disabled?
  - How much the T=1 solve actually corrects the network’s action in practice (e.g., average deviation ∥a−aNN∥, or distribution over timesteps). This would quantify reliance on the optimizer versus the policy.
  - The magnitude of “root forces” due to soft constraint enforcement. How often do these produce physically implausible corrections? Could you report constraint violation statistics?
- Quantitative evaluation: Beyond qualitative videos and ablations, more quantitative metrics would strengthen the claims: success rate to target under disturbances, energy/torque usage, trajectory smoothness, contact violation residuals, and performance under goal switches. Comparisons to baselines (e.g., MPC with similar costs, iLQG/LQR feedback, TRPO or other policy gradient methods on the same simulators) would contextualize gains.
- “Learning dynamics under an implicit policy”: The action is defined as desired velocities and forces, not torques; Section 2 suggests the policy “is not learning a control policy, but rather the dynamics under an implicit policy.” This is confusing. Please clarify the control interpretation and how it relates to the final T=1 optimization that realizes torques/forces subject to constraints.
- Asynchronicity and proximal regularization: The use of quadratic proximal terms to stabilize asynchronous alternating updates is reasonable, but the “similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches” statement would benefit from concrete citations (e.g., proximal point/proximal gradient methods, ADMM, proximal alternating minimization, elastic averaging SGD).
- Generalization and data efficiency: It would help to detail how performance scales with the number of trials/segments and to quantify generalization to unseen goal changes. An analysis of failure modes (e.g., falls) would also be valuable.
- Physical fidelity: Using soft constraints and implicit integration facilitates stability and large timesteps, but can mask modeling errors. Please discuss implications for transfer to hardware or to stricter simulators.

Quality:
- The approach is technically sound, and core claims are supported with ablations and diverse demonstrations. Some aspects (runtime reliance on the T=1 optimizer, physical plausibility under soft constraints) would benefit from quantitative diagnostics. The feedback-gain computation is elegant and practically useful.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and well organized. Section 7 could better motivate the runtime optimization step and quantify its impact. A brief clarification of the “action as velocity” control interpretation would also help.

Originality:
- The overall framework builds on known ideas (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning), but contributes a practical and scalable instantiation in 3D with a neat perturbation-based feedback-gain computation and an effective noise-injection regimen. The unification across morphologies without task-specific machinery is noteworthy.

Significance:
- Likely to be useful for both graphics and model-based control communities. The ability to produce stable, realistic 3D locomotion and flight across bodies with a single pipeline is a strong practical contribution, even if the theoretical novelty is limited.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong results across diverse 3D tasks with one architecture.
- Clear evidence for the benefits of noise injection and joint learning.
- Practical method for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Well-written and carefully engineered.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited quantitative comparisons to strong baselines; reliance on qualitative evaluation.
- Ambiguity around the necessity and extent of the runtime optimization step.
- Soft constraint enforcement raises questions about strict physical plausibility and transfer.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Please quantify how much the T=1 optimization alters the policy’s proposed action during execution, and provide constraint violation statistics (including any root forces).
- Report success rates, robustness to perturbations, and energy/effort metrics; compare against MPC on the same costs and horizons.
- Clarify the “implicit policy/dynamics” phrasing when the action is desired velocities/forces.
- Provide concrete citations for “similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches” (e.g., proximal point/ADMM/elastic averaging SGD).
- Discuss how λ for feedback-gain soft constraints is chosen and its effect on stability and conditioning.
- Minor edits: l89 “to unify” (typo). Section 8: “changed changed” duplication. Consider adding references to Boyd et al. (2011) for ADMM and standard proximal algorithms.

Overall assessment:
A strong, well-executed paper with compelling demonstrations and a practical algorithmic contribution. With added quantitative evaluation and clearer motivation of the runtime optimization step, this would be an excellent NIPS paper.
