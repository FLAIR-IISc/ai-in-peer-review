- Proposes a real-time 3D character control algorithm where a neural network is trained offline to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs with alternating joint training between CIO and the NN.
- Introduces noise injection via data augmentation (small perturbations and additive noise in hidden layers) as a key element to improve generalization.
- Experiments show the network can produce realistic, stable control policies across multiple character models.
- Novelty lies in the alternating joint training and noise injection steps, which materially improve performance over a naïve “learn-to-predict-CIO” approach.
- Experimental dataset is small (100–200 base trials with 5 branched segments each), risking overfitting—especially in the “no noise” variant.
- The “no joint” variant likely needs more diverse training trajectories to fairly evaluate its performance.
- Comparative evaluation is weak: only a brief comparison to MPC is provided, with no reference, and no systematic comparison to direct methods.
- Mathematical exposition is generally clear but omits definitions and details needed for replication (e.g., undefined notations in Eq. 6 for the CIO step).
- Section 3.2 (generating optimal actions for noisy inputs) is confusing, with unclear notation and dropping of the time index t.
- Providing code would aid reproducibility and community validation.
- Runtime measurements for real-time control and scaling with model complexity are missing but important for practical applications (e.g., games).
- Clarify terminology and wording: “biped location” (locomotion), fix “unity” → “unite/unify,” and correct several grammatical errors and typos.
- Clarify target specification: current target appears to be only x,y,z coordinates with no target facing angle—adding angle constraints may be important in practice.
- The term “noise injection” is misleading here since it mainly refers to data augmentation rather than denoising autoencoder-style injection.
- Unclear whether initial random network weights theta are used in the first iteration of Algorithm 1 and why the same hyperparameter eta is used in both algorithmic steps.
- The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs substantially from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1 and should be discussed earlier.
- Minor notation/formatting errors: misplaced star in an argmin, missing \bar{s} in \tilde{C}, and other small mistakes.
- Suggest investigating whether alpha can be annealed to zero (CIO-only) and whether benefits arise primarily from curriculum learning versus keeping CIO trajectories close to network-capable behaviors.
- Overall assessment: promising and well-executed application of NNs to character control, but practical benefits and broader applicability require stronger experimental validation and clearer presentation.
