This paper presents a novel method for training recurrent neural network (RNN) controllers capable of generating complex, stable, and realistic behaviors for a diverse set of physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with the real-time capabilities of a neural network policy. The authors formulate this as a joint optimization problem, where a set of optimal trajectories and the parameters of a neural network policy are optimized concurrently.

Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training by using optimal feedback gains derived from the trajectory optimizer. This makes the resulting policy robust to perturbations and errors. The method uses an alternating, distributed optimization scheme that interleaves trajectory optimization (using Contact-Invariant Optimization) and supervised learning (for the RNN policy). The framework is demonstrated on an impressive array of challenging 3D control tasks, including swimming, flying, and bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion, all using the same general architecture. This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization and function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but significantly advances the state-of-the-art by successfully tackling more complex 3D characters and generating stable, realistic locomotion without task-specific state machines or features.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of exceptional technical quality. The proposed method is well-founded, combining established techniques (trajectory optimization, supervised learning for NNs) with novel and insightful contributions, particularly the method for computing and utilizing optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) to guide the training process. The claims are strongly supported by an extensive set of experiments on diverse and challenging control problems. The ablation study (Section 8.1) is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating the critical importance of both the joint optimization framework and the noise injection techniques for achieving robust performance.

*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant and impactful. The ability to generate high-quality controllers for such a wide range of morphologies (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) from a single, unified algorithm is a major achievement in character animation and control. The emergent behaviors, such as natural walking gaits and efficient flight patterns, are visually stunning and demonstrate a qualitative leap in automated controller synthesis. This work provides a powerful and practical framework that researchers and practitioners in robotics, animation, and biomechanics are likely to build upon. The comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Figure 3 interestingly suggests that the neural network acts as a powerful regularizer, guiding the optimization towards solutions that are not only effective but also more structured and predictable.

*   **Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions. The primary one is the method for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method and using them to create meaningful training targets for perturbed states. This provides a more principled approach to robustness than simply augmenting data with random noise. The formulation as a large-scale, distributed, alternating optimization problem is also a novel and effective way to tackle this complex policy search problem. While it uses known components, their synthesis is novel and leads to results that are substantially better than prior work, particularly in the extension to complex 3D locomotion.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is incredibly clear and well-written. The authors do an excellent job of presenting the overall vision in the introduction, providing a concise overview of the method in Section 2, and then delving into the technical details of each component. The problem is formally stated, and the algorithm is laid out precisely. The figures and the accompanying video are highly effective at conveying the quality and diversity of the results.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is exceptionally strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for further improvement rather than significant criticisms.

1.  **Comparison to Reinforcement Learning:** The authors correctly position their work relative to direct reinforcement learning (RL) methods, noting that a systematic comparison has not yet been done. While the qualitative superiority of the generated motions is evident from the video, a quantitative comparison against a state-of-the-art policy gradient method (e.g., TRPO, which was contemporary) on one of the benchmark tasks (like the biped) could have further strengthened the paper's claims. This would help quantify the trade-offs between the two approaches in terms of sample efficiency, final performance, and the "naturalness" of the resulting policy.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires a significant computational budget (a cluster of ~30 nodes plus a GPU, with training times in the hours). This is perfectly acceptable given the complexity of the problem and the quality of the results. However, a brief discussion of the scalability and the computational bottlenecks would be beneficial for readers who wish to apply or extend the work. Is the performance limited by the trajectory optimization or the network training?

3.  **Real-Time Policy Execution:** The policy execution step (Section 7) involves solving a small, single-timestep optimization problem to enforce the dynamics constraints. This is a clever approach that avoids the instability of simple forward integration. It would be helpful to add a brief comment on the computational cost of this step to confirm that it runs comfortably in real-time for interactive applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a fantastic paper that represents a tour de force of technique and a significant advance in the field of character animation and motor control. It masterfully combines ideas from optimization, control theory, and machine learning to solve a very difficult problem. The results are beautiful, convincing, and inspiring. The paper is technically deep, rigorously evaluated, and communicated with exceptional clarity. It is a clear accept and a candidate for one of the best papers at the conference.
