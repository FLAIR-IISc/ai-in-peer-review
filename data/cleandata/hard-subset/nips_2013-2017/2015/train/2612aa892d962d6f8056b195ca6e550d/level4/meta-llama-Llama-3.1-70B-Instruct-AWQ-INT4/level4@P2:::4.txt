This paper proposes a novel approach to interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The main contribution is the joint learning of trajectories and policy, which is achieved by decoupling the two optimization problems into alternating subproblems. This approach enables the reuse of existing, well-documented methods and has been demonstrated to work well in practice.

The authors showcase an impressive level of technical sophistication at all levels, from concepts to implementation, and achieve equally impressive results. The advantages of using sensory noise during training and learning trajectories jointly with the policy are clearly demonstrated. The paper is well-written, with an appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section treats a separate aspect of the problem, and there are useful cross-references between sections.

One area for improvement is the motivation for the extra optimization step in Section 7. It would be beneficial to provide a clearer explanation for the need for this step, particularly given the previous sections' announcements. It appears that there may be a residual trajectory cost or policy regression error that necessitates this step, but further clarification would be helpful.

Additionally, it would be useful to provide one or two references to support the statement in line 153, "similar to recent approaches." It is also worth noting that keeping updates small in parameter space may not be a sensible approach in general, but it is useful in this specific scenario where block-alternating between subproblems is employed.

The paper's results are impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation appear sensible. However, as this research area is not within my primary expertise, I am unable to confidently assess the novelty and significance of the work.
