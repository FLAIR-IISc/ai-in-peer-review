- Novel contribution: trains a neural network to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs with alternating joint training and noise injection, arguing CIO and NN training are interdependent rather than independent.
- Key innovations: alternating training scheme and additive hidden-layer noise plus input perturbation data augmentation to improve generalization for real-time 3D character control.
- Empirical strength: experiments indicate alternating training and noise injection are critical—“no noise” overfits and “no joint” lacks trajectory diversity given limited training data (100–200 trials, 5 branches/trial).
- Weak comparative evaluation: only a brief, undocumented MPC comparison; lacks systematic baselines against other NN-based or optimization methods.
- Mathematical exposition problems: notation inconsistencies and missing details (undefined symbols in Eq.6, dropped time index in Section 3.2, unclear noisy input handling).
- Algorithm ambiguity: Algorithm 1 unclear about initial X (random weights vs. regularization-free computation) and lacks hyperparameter (η) justification for both steps.
- Replicability concerns: relies on external CIO references; essential details omitted and code is not provided.
- Missing practical metrics: no real-time inference timings or scaling analysis with model complexity despite relevance to CPU-constrained applications (e.g., gaming).
- Unclear claims: statement that “dynamics constraints are enforced softly” lacks context and quantification.
- Minor technical/writing issues: multiple typos and wording errors (e.g., “Biped location”→locomotion, “Unity”→unify), misplaced symbols, incorrect labels (Table 1a caption), and inconsistent terminology (noise injection vs. standard data augmentation).
- Experimental design suggestions: split the “no noise” variant into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise” to isolate effects; test whether CIO weight α can be annealed to zero to assess intrinsic utility.
- Asynchronous vs alternating schemes: asynchronous implementation in Section 6 differs substantially from Algorithm 1’s alternating scheme and should be clarified earlier.
- Main takeaway: method is novel and promising but current empirical validation and exposition are insufficient to support broad claims of practical superiority.
- Recommendation: Major Revision — required fixes include expanded experiments with stronger baselines and larger datasets, clarified mathematics/algorithms, inclusion of real-time performance metrics, and addressing the listed minor issues.
