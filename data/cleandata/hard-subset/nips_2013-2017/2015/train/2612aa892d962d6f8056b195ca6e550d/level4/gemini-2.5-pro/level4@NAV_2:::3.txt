This paper presents a method for creating real-time, interactive controllers for a diverse range of 3D characters. The core of the approach is a recurrent neural network policy that is trained to emulate the outputs of an offline trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The key contributions lie not just in this combination, but in the specific training methodology. The authors propose a joint optimization scheme where the neural network and the trajectory optimizer are trained in an alternating fashion, with the output of each influencing the objective of the other. Furthermore, noise is strategically injected during training, both as perturbations to the network's sensory inputs (with corresponding optimal actions recomputed) and as additive noise to the hidden layer activations. The experiments demonstrate that this framework can produce stable and visually realistic locomotion for various morphologies, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers, without task-specific engineering.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper tackles a challenging and important problem in animation and robotics: the automatic synthesis of robust, real-time controllers for complex, physically simulated characters.
*   The proposed method of jointly training the trajectory optimizer and the neural network policy is a significant contribution. The experimental results convincingly show that this alternating approach yields far better generalization than naively training a network on a static dataset of optimized trajectories.
*   The principled injection of noise is shown to be critical for preventing overfitting and producing stable policies. This, combined with the joint training, provides a robust solution to the challenge of learning from limited, self-generated data in a closed-loop control setting.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary weakness lies in the comparative evaluation. While the ablation studies are informative, the dataset used for training ("100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments") seems relatively small. On such a dataset, it is not surprising that variants without noise or joint training overfit. It would be more compelling to show if these ablations still fail on a much larger and more diverse set of training trajectories.
*   The paper lacks a thorough comparison to alternative state-of-the-art methods. The comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC) is brief and qualitative, and the authors acknowledge that a systematic comparison to direct policy search methods (e.g., from reinforcement learning) has not been performed. This makes it difficult to assess the full significance and advantages of the proposed approach relative to the field.
*   While generally well-written, some technical details are not explained with sufficient clarity for reproducibility. The CIO formulation in Equation 6 uses notation that is not defined in the text. Section 3.2, which describes the computation of optimal feedback gains, is particularly difficult to parse due to confusing notation and the apparent omission of time indices. The community would greatly benefit from the release of source code to accompany the paper.
*   The paper omits practical performance details. For a method aimed at interactive applications, information on the computational cost (e.g., milliseconds per frame) of the policy execution step (Section 7) is essential. It would also be valuable to know how this cost scales with the complexity of the character model.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions:**
*   There are a few minor typos: "biped location" should likely be "biped locomotion," and "unity these disparate approaches" should be "unify" or "unite."
*   Regarding the task, is the goal specified only by a target position, or does it also include a target orientation? Adding an orientation target seems important for many practical applications.
*   The term "noise injection" in Section 2.1 could be misleading. Since the optimal action is recomputed for the perturbed input, this process is more akin to data augmentation than to the concept behind denoising autoencoders, where the network must recover a clean signal.
*   In Algorithm 1, is the initial set of trajectories `X` generated without the neural network regularization term, or is a randomly initialized network `θ` used from the very first iteration?
*   What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter `η` for the proximal regularization terms in both the trajectory optimization and the neural network regression steps?
*   The paper states that adapting methods like ADMM to the asynchronous, stochastic setting is "non-trivial." Could the authors elaborate briefly on the specific challenges?
*   The acronym LQG is used before it is formally introduced.
*   In Section 3.2, the notation `argmin*X` seems to have a misplaced star, and the cost function `C̃` appears to be missing `s̄` in its argument list.
*   The asynchronous optimization described in Section 6 is a departure from the alternating scheme of Algorithm 1. It would improve clarity to mention this implementation detail earlier when the algorithm is introduced.
*   In Section 7, in the optimization objective `argmin x`, should the final term be `||f - f_des||^2`?
*   The sentence, "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation," is unclear. Could the authors rephrase this to clarify its meaning and implications?
*   To better understand the contribution of the different noise sources, the "no noise" ablation study could be split into two separate experiments: one without sensory input perturbation ("no data augmentation") and one without hidden unit noise.
*   An interesting question for future work: could the weight `α` on the policy-matching term be annealed towards zero during training? This could test whether the joint optimization's main benefit is as a form of curriculum learning (making the initial optimization easier) or if continued regularization from the policy is always necessary.
*   The caption for Table 1a mentions 4 layers, but the table seems to describe networks with up to 3 hidden layers.
