

This paper presents a novel approach for real-time 3D character control using a neural network (NN) trained to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs, with key innovations in alternating joint training and noise injection. The method leverages data augmentation via input perturbations and additive noise in hidden layers to enhance generalization, demonstrating stable, realistic control across diverse character models. The core insight—that CIO and NN training are interdependent, not independent—is a significant contribution beyond a simple "learn CIO outputs" approach.

The primary strength lies in the methodological novelty. The alternating training scheme and strategic noise injection directly address the challenge of real-time control for arbitrary models, where prior work often struggles with generalization. The experiments convincingly show that these elements are critical: the "no noise" variant overfits due to limited training data (100–200 trials with 5 branched segments per trial), and the "no joint" variant likely suffers from insufficient trajectory diversity. However, the comparative evaluation is the paper’s weakest aspect. The authors acknowledge the lack of systematic comparisons with direct methods (Introduction), yet the only competitor is a brief MPC comparison lacking a reference. A larger-scale comparison against relevant baselines (e.g., other NN-based or optimization methods) is essential to validate the claimed advantages.

The mathematical exposition is generally clear but suffers from notation inconsistencies and insufficient detail. Key issues include: (1) Undefined symbols in Equation 6 (e.g., CIO step not fully motivated); (2) Confusing notation in Section 3.2 (e.g., dropped time index *t*, unclear handling of noisy inputs); (3) Ambiguity in Algorithm 1 (e.g., whether initial *X* uses random weights or regularization-free computation). Replication would require consulting external references for CIO, which is impractical. Providing code would greatly benefit the community. Additionally, critical practical details are missing: real-time inference timings and scaling with model complexity are absent, yet CPU constraints in applications like gaming make these vital. The claim that "dynamics constraints are enforced softly" is unclear without context.

Minor issues warrant attention: 
- "Biped location" should be "locomotion."
- "Unity" → "unify" (Section 1).
- Target specification (x,y,z only?) lacks facing angle constraints—adding this would improve practicality.
- "Noise injection" misrepresents data augmentation (recomputing targets for perturbed inputs is standard augmentation, not denoising).
- Hyperparameter *η* justification for both steps in Algorithm 1 is missing.
- Typos: "asynchronous and stochastic setting we have" → "the," "LQG pass" → "a LQG pass," "f should be f^{des}" (line 315), misplaced star in argmin (line 231), and "sX and aX are subsets" (no guarantee stated).
- The asynchronous scheme (Section 6) differs substantially from the alternating scheme (Algorithm 1); this should be clarified earlier.

The paper’s most compelling question is whether *α* (in CIO) can be reduced to zero during optimization—this would distinguish between curriculum learning and inherent utility of CIO guidance. The "no noise" variant should be split into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise" to isolate effects. Table 1a caption incorrectly states "4 layers" (should be 3).

While the application is promising, the paper falls short in establishing practical viability. The limited training data and inadequate baselines prevent robust claims about generalization. Without stronger empirical validation and clearer technical exposition, the practical benefits remain unproven. The core idea is novel and well-motivated, but the evidence to support its superiority is insufficient.

**Recommendation**: **Major Revision**. The paper addresses a relevant problem with a creative method, but requires substantial improvements: (1) Expand experiments with larger datasets and more competitive baselines; (2) Clarify mathematical details (notation, Algorithm 1, Section 3.2); (3) Include real-time performance metrics; (4) Address all minor issues listed above. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution. As-is, the weak evaluation undermines the otherwise strong methodological insight.
