This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate optimal state-action data, and then use this data to train a neural network policy via supervised learning. The key contributions of the proposed method include a joint optimization framework that interleaves trajectory optimization with network training, the injection of noise into sensory inputs and hidden units to improve robustness, and a novel technique for deriving optimal feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimization process. The authors demonstrate that a single, unified architecture can learn stable and realistic locomotion behaviors for a diverse range of characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, without task-specific features or motion capture data.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its empirical results. The ability to control a wide variety of morphologically distinct characters (swimming, flying, walking) with a single, general-purpose algorithm is a significant achievement. The behaviors shown in the supplementary video appear realistic, stable, and task-directed, which is notably difficult to achieve in 3D physics-based control.

2.  **Unified Framework:** The paper successfully moves towards a unified approach for character control, eschewing the hand-crafted state machines and simplified models that have traditionally dominated character animation and robotics for specific morphologies (e.g., inverted pendulums for bipeds). This is an important and ambitious direction for the field.

3.  **Technical Contributions:** The method introduces several valuable technical ideas. The joint, block-alternating optimization of trajectories and the policy is a principled way to address the closed-loop instability problem. Furthermore, the proposed method for calculating optimal feedback gains directly from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is a clever contribution that avoids a separate, potentially inefficient LQG pass.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** The main weakness of the paper is the insufficient discussion of its relationship to closely related, concurrent work in reinforcement learning and control. The methodology is very similar in spirit to the Guided Policy Search (GPS) framework (e.g., Levine & Koltun, [9]; Levine & Abbeel). In both approaches, trajectory optimization is used to guide the learning of a complex neural network policy. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed comparison. How does the proposed distributed stochastic optimization (Algorithm 1) differ from the alternating methods used in GPS? The authors should clarify the novelty of their approach in this context. For instance, is the proposed method more sample-efficient, computationally cheaper at runtime, more robust to specific perturbations, or applicable to a broader class of problems than these existing methods?

2.  **Clarity on Technical Details (Section 3.2):** The justification for the novel feedback gain calculation needs to be clarified.
    *   The paper states that using LQG can "produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" and that the proposed method produces "softer feedback gains." These claims require more substantiation. Could the authors provide a more formal definition or empirical measure of what constitutes "ill-conditioned" or "soft" gains in this context? A quantitative comparison against a standard LQG-derived baseline would make this contribution much stronger.
    *   The intuition behind relaxing the initial state constraint is not fully explained. Why is it beneficial to allow the optimizer to modify the initial state to "better achieve the master cost function," rather than finding the optimal response from that exact state? A more detailed explanation of the trade-offs involved would be helpful.
    *   From a purely organizational standpoint, the critique of LQG might be more effective if it came after the proposed method has been fully presented, allowing the reader to first understand the new approach before considering its advantages over alternatives.
    *   The notation in this section is slightly confusing. The use of `s̄` to denote the perturbed state and `s(X)` to denote the state from the optimal trajectory can be difficult to parse, especially in the cost function `||s(X) - s̄||²`. Using more visually distinct notation could improve readability.

### Summary

Overall, this is a solid paper demonstrating an effective method for a difficult problem. The empirical results are convincing and showcase the power and generality of the approach. However, the paper's primary weakness is its positioning, as the conceptual framework is very similar to other recent work, and the specific advantages of the proposed algorithmic choices are not clearly articulated or demonstrated. The technical contribution regarding feedback gain calculation is interesting but requires better motivation and empirical validation. Addressing the clarity of the technical sections and providing a more thorough comparison to related literature would substantially improve the paper.
