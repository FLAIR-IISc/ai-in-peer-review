The authors present a novel approach to training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for a wide range of dynamical systems and tasks, including swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking with different body morphologies. The proposed method combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate stable and realistic behaviors. The neural network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously, and the action generated by the network is defined as velocity.

The authors provide a thorough overview of the method, including the introduction of noise during training, the use of trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains, and the application of a perturbation method to produce feedback gains that stabilize resulting optimal trajectories. The paper also presents a distributed stochastic optimization algorithm, which reduces the complex policy search problem to an alternating sequence of independent trajectory optimization and neural network regression problems.

The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in learning successful controllers for a diverse set of three-dimensional characters, including swimming and flying creatures, as well as biped and quadruped walking tasks. The authors also provide a comparative evaluation of their method, showing that it outperforms naive neural network training on a static optimal trajectory dataset and that the injection of noise during training is crucial for preventing overfitting.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and results. The use of figures and tables to illustrate the results is also helpful. The authors' discussion of the potential applications of their method, including robotic control, animation, and gaming, is also insightful.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in the field. While the authors provide a brief discussion of related work, a more thorough comparison with other approaches would be helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neural network control and demonstrates the potential of the proposed method for generating robust and realistic controllers for a wide range of tasks and body morphologies.
