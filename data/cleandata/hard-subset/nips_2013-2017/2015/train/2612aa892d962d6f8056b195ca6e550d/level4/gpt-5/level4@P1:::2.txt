Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper trains recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers by interleaving trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning. The policy outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent-state updates; the physical state is then advanced by solving a short-horizon (T=1) constrained optimization consistent with dynamics. Key ingredients include: injecting Gaussian noise into sensory inputs during training; deriving local optimal feedback gains as a byproduct of trajectory optimization (via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss–Newton system), and alternating between trajectory re-optimization and neural network regression with proximal regularization in a distributed, asynchronous setup. The same architecture is applied to diverse 3D characters (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds) without motion capture or state machines. Ablations suggest both joint optimization and noise injection are important; a comparison to MPC indicates that the joint optimization biases toward regular, cyclic behaviors.

This work is closely related to prior approaches that combine trajectory optimization with policy learning, notably Levine and Koltun (ICML’14) and Mordatch and Todorov (RSS’14). It also connects to work on real-time RNN controllers, model-based control with MuJoCo, and denoising-style regularization in neural nets. The claimed advance over earlier work is the breadth of 3D tasks and morphologies and the practical training recipe (noise, gain computation, distributed alternating optimization) that yields stable interactive control.

Strengths:
- Demonstrates a single NN architecture and training scheme that yields stable, visually realistic 3D behaviors across multiple morphologies without hand-crafted state machines or motion capture.
- Sensible and well-engineered training pipeline: data generation via CIO, noise injection, and a practical method for extracting feedback gains by reusing the trajectory optimizer’s linearization/Hessian.
- Real-time execution with a compact policy; no need to store trajectories at runtime.
- Ablation studies (no joint optimization; no noise) provide useful evidence for the design choices.
- Clear description of the distributed training architecture and practical timings.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Positioning versus closely related work is not sufficiently clear. Please compare directly against Levine and Koltun (and related Abbeel/Levine line of work) along the following axes: training time/data requirements, runtime efficiency, robustness to perturbations, breadth of morphologies, and final behavior quality. A quantitative comparison (e.g., task success, fall rate, goal-tracking error, energy/torque cost) would strengthen the contribution.
- Quality/metrics: Results are primarily qualitative. Provide quantitative measures of stability, robustness to disturbances, and tracking performance, as well as failure cases. Reporting energy/torque cost and distance-to-goal over long rollouts would help.
- Action definition and execution: The policy outputs desired velocities and forces, and the next state is solved via a T=1 optimization with soft dynamics constraints. Please discuss whether root forces are ever significant at execution time, how this affects physical plausibility, and how solutions differ from an explicit torque policy rolled out with an integrator.
- Feedback gains: “We found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains.” Please clarify: do you mean high gain magnitudes, poor conditioning of the gain matrices, or numerical instability in the resulting closed-loop system? Provide norms/condition numbers and examples. Also clarify why your perturbation-based method avoids these issues in practice.
- “Softer feedback gains according to parameter λ”: softer than what? Define precisely how λ affects the gain magnitude/conditioning and the closed-loop response, and justify why relaxing the state-matching constraint (versus hard constraints as in LQG) is desirable for stability/robustness in your setting. A small sensitivity plot of performance vs λ would be useful.
- Notation clarity (Sec. 3.2, lines ~226–240): s(X)=s̄ and the subsequent cost using s(X)−s̄ are easy to confuse visually. Consider using distinct symbols or typography (e.g., s*, ŝ) and reduce diacritics. Also ensure consistency between s̄ and the “noisy” s+ε used elsewhere.
- Organization: Consider presenting your perturbation-based gain computation first, then contrasting with an LQG pass and explaining why the latter led to ill-conditioned gains in your experiments. This would improve narrative flow.
- Claims of advancing from 2D to 3D: Please provide a clearer comparison against prior 3D character-control results and articulate what specifically enables 3D stability here (e.g., gain computation, noise injection, recurrent state).
- Generalization: The training data uses branched goal changes. How far can test-time goal changes deviate (magnitude/rate) before failure? Provide statistics.
- Reference formatting is incomplete; please provide full bibliographic details.

Evaluation on NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound and well-implemented. The gain derivation that reuses the Gauss–Newton system is a practical contribution. However, experimental evaluation is mostly qualitative; more quantitative comparisons and robustness metrics would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable, but some notation and terminology (“soft gains,” conditioning) need clarification. Reordering the LQG discussion and tightening notation in Sec. 3.2 would help.
- Originality: Moderate. The overall recipe—trajectory optimization plus supervised policy learning with noise—is known. The specific perturbation-based feedback gain computation within a direct trajectory optimization framework and the breadth of 3D results are the main novel aspects.
- Significance: Practical significance for animation and model-based control is promising, especially the cross-morphology controller. Scientific/algorithmic significance would be higher with stronger quantitative evidence and clearer differentiation from prior work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Unified controller across diverse 3D characters without hand-crafted structure.
- Practical, scalable training pipeline with ablation support.
- Useful technique for extracting stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited quantitative evaluation and incomplete comparison to closely related methods.
- Some methodological ambiguities (gain conditioning, “soft gains,” role of root forces at execution).
- Clarity and positioning could be improved.

Specific requests for revision:
- Add quantitative comparisons to Levine and Koltun/Mordatch and Todorov on shared tasks.
- Report robustness metrics (disturbance rejection, fall rate), tracking error, energy/torque usage, and long-horizon stability.
- Clarify and quantify the feedback gain issues and the effect of λ; improve notation in Sec. 3.2.
- Discuss action representation and runtime T=1 optimization implications for physical plausibility.
- Improve reference completeness and organization of the LQG discussion.
