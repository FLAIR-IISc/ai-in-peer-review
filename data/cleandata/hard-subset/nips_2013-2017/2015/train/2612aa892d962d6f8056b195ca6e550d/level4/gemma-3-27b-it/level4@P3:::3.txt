This submission proposes a novel algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, leveraging neural networks (NNs) trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key contribution lies in the alternating training process – CIO and NN training are interwoven, with the output of one informing the optimization criterion of the other. Further strengthening the approach is the strategic injection of noise, both through data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise within the NN's hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate the method’s ability to generate realistic and stable control policies across a diverse range of character models.

This is a compelling approach to a challenging problem: real-time control of complex, articulated models. While the core idea – learning a NN to approximate the output of CIO – may seem conceptually straightforward, the essential additions of joint training and noise injection represent significant contributions, demonstrably improving generalization performance. The ability to control diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture is particularly noteworthy.

However, the paper's experimental validation could be strengthened. The training dataset, consisting of “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” appears relatively small, potentially contributing to the overfitting observed in the “no noise” variant. Expanding the diversity of training trajectories, even if computationally expensive, could further improve robustness. The comparative evaluation represents a relative weakness. While the authors acknowledge this ("A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"), the comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) is brief and lacks a citation. A more thorough comparison to related methods, including reinforcement learning approaches, would be valuable.

For the most part, the mathematical formulations are clearly presented and motivated. However, a reader intending to reproduce these results may require familiarity with additional resources, particularly regarding the CIO step (e.g., Eq. 6, where notations are not fully defined). Releasing the code would greatly facilitate reproducibility and broader adoption. Section 3.2, concerning the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, proved difficult to follow. The notations become complex, and the omission of the time index *t* in some equations introduces ambiguity. 

Including runtime performance measurements would also be beneficial. Understanding the computational cost of real-time control – and its scaling with model complexity – is crucial for assessing practical applicability, particularly in latency-sensitive applications like video games.

Minor points: “biped location” should be “locomotion”; "unity" should be "unify"; the paper does not explicitly constrain character orientation during target reaching - exploring this would be a useful extension; the term "noise injection" is arguably misapplied to the data augmentation step – it's more accurately described as data augmentation; clarification is needed regarding the initialization of network weights in Algorithm 1; the justification for using a shared hyperparameter η in both steps of Algorithm 1 is unclear; minor grammatical errors exist (e.g., missing articles, incorrect word choice). A few typos were found, including in lines 231, 315, and the table captions. The discussion of the asynchronous optimization scheme in Section 6 feels disconnected from Algorithm 1, which describes a more straightforward alternating approach; addressing this disparity would improve clarity. The sentence about dynamics constraints being enforced softly is unclear. Finally, exploring whether α can be decreased to zero during optimization could provide insights into the importance of the CIO-learned trajectories.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network learning, and the alternating training scheme, represent a novel approach to real-time character control.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method appears technically sound, with clear mathematical formulations and encouraging experimental results.
*   **Significance:** Successful real-time control of diverse character models has the potential for significant impact in fields like robotics, animation, and game development.
*   **Generalization:** The ability to apply the same network architecture to different character morphologies is a major strength.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The lack of thorough comparison to other state-of-the-art methods weakens the claims of superiority.
*   **Small Dataset:** The relatively small size of the training dataset raises concerns about overfitting and generalization.
*   **Clarity Issues:** Some sections of the paper, particularly Section 3.2, lack clarity and require further explanation.
*   **Missing Practical Considerations:** The absence of runtime performance measurements limits the assessment of real-world applicability.

**Overall:**

This is a promising paper with a novel and potentially impactful approach. However, the limited comparative evaluation, small dataset, and occasional clarity issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend **weak accept** with the expectation that the authors will address the weaknesses identified in this review, particularly by performing more extensive comparative experiments, expanding the training dataset, and improving the clarity of the technical explanations. Providing code would also significantly enhance the value of this work.
