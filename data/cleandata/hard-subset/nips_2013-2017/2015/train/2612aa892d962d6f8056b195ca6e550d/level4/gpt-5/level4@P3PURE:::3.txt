This paper proposes an algorithm for real-time control of 3D character models, where a neural network (NN) generates animations after being trained offline to reproduce the outputs of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key design choice is that CIO optimization and NN training are interleaved: each alternation uses the other’s outputs in its objective. Another essential component is noise injection, implemented both via data augmentation with small perturbations and via additive noise in the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that the resulting policy can produce realistic and stable control across a range of distinct character models.

This is a compelling approach to a hard problem—real-time control for arbitrary characters. While the top-level idea (“train an NN to predict CIO outputs”) may seem straightforward, the alternating joint training and noise injection steps represent meaningful and nontrivial contributions that, according to the experiments, substantially improve generalization.

That said, the empirical evaluation would be stronger with a larger and more diverse dataset. Since the base training trajectories come from only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” it is unsurprising that the “no noise” variant overfits; the “no joint” variant might also benefit from more diverse trajectories, even if the effect is less obvious. Overall, the comparative evaluation is the weakest aspect of the paper (as the authors acknowledge: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with MPC at the end of the paper, and there is no citation for this MPC variant.

For the most part, the mathematics are clearly introduced and motivated, but faithful replication would still require consulting external references; certain points are treated only superficially (particularly the CIO step—see Eq. 6, where several notations are undefined). Making code available would significantly help the community. One section I could not follow is Section 3.2 on generating optimal actions for noisy inputs: the notation becomes confusing, the time index t appears to be dropped, and it is unclear which quantities still implicitly depend on time and how the optimization is actually carried out.

It would also be helpful to report run-time performance during real-time control, and how it scales with model complexity, since this can be a bottleneck in practice (for example, in games, limited CPU is available per character).

Additional minor remarks and questions:
- “biped location”: did you mean locomotion?
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches”: did you mean unite or unify?
- Is the target only x, y, z position, with no constraint on the character’s facing angle at the target? If so, how easy would it be to add an orientation target (which seems important in practice)?
- I would avoid calling the procedure that recomputes supervision for perturbed inputs “noise injection.” Because the targets are recomputed to match the modified inputs, this is standard data augmentation rather than denoising-style noise injection.
- In Algorithm 1, do you truly use the initial random network parameters θ to compute the first X’s, or do you first compute X without the regularization term?
- What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Algorithm 1?
- “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”: missing “the.”
- The acronym LQG is used one line before it is defined.
- “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”: missing “a.”
- Line 231: the star in the argmin appears misplaced, and is ȳs missing in C̃?
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: missing a word (e.g., “the optimal trajectory”)?
- “sX and aX are subsets of ϕX”: why is this guaranteed?
- “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: it seems you mean the objective from Algorithm 1, since only θ is optimized in that step.
- The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 is quite different from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1; it would help to flag this distinction earlier.
- Line 315: should f be f^{des}?
- “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.” I found this sentence unclear—please elaborate on what “root forces” arise and under what conditions.
- In the ablations, consider splitting “no noise” into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden-layer noise,” to disambiguate their effects.
- An interesting question is whether α can be annealed all the way to zero during CIO (i.e., is the benefit primarily a curriculum effect that eases optimization early on, or is it helpful to keep CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent throughout?).
- Table 1a’s caption says 4 layers, but I believe the architecture has 3.

In summary, this is an attractive and promising application of neural networks to character control. The method is well motivated and the ablations suggest real benefits from the joint training and noise mechanisms. However, broader and stronger empirical comparisons, clearer exposition of certain technical details (especially around CIO and Section 3.2), and run-time analysis are needed to fully establish the method’s practical advantages and applicability.
