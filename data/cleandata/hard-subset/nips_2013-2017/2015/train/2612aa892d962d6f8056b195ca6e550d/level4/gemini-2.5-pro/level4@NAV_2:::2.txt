This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to use an offline trajectory optimization method, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of near-optimal state-action trajectories for a variety of tasks. A neural network policy is then trained on this data. The key contributions of the training procedure include a block-alternating optimization scheme that interleaves trajectory optimization with network regression, the injection of noise at both the sensory input and hidden unit levels to improve robustness, and a novel method for computing optimal feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimizer. This approach is demonstrated to be general enough to produce stable and realistic interactive locomotion for a diverse range of 3D characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, all using the same network architecture. This builds upon previous work that combined trajectory optimization and neural networks (e.g., [9, 11]), extending the demonstrated capability to more complex 3D locomotion.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Quality:** The most significant strength of this work is its empirical demonstration of a single, unified framework that can learn controllers for a wide variety of character morphologies and locomotion styles (swimming, flying, walking, trotting). The ability to generate stable, plausible, and interactive control for such diverse 3D systems without task-specific state machines or features is a notable achievement and advances the state of the art in character animation. The quality of the resulting motions, as shown in the supplementary video, is very high.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed methodology is technically sound. The combination of a powerful offline optimizer with a compact neural network policy is well-motivated. The specific technical choices—injecting noise to prevent overfitting and improve stability, and jointly optimizing trajectories and the policy—are crucial for success.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The ablation study in Section 8.1 provides compelling evidence for the importance of the key components of the method. The comparisons clearly show that both joint optimization (as opposed to training on a static dataset) and noise injection are critical for learning a policy that performs well at execution time.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   **Originality and Positioning:** The primary weakness is the clarity of the paper's novel contributions relative to very closely related recent work. The paper acknowledges that combining trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning has been explored before [9, 11]. The main contribution appears to be the successful application to more complex 3D characters and a set of technical improvements that enable this. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed discussion and comparison with other contemporary policy search methods (e.g., the work of Levine, Abbeel, and Schulman). A clearer articulation of the specific advantages of the proposed approach—for example, in terms of computational cost, robustness, scalability, or the quality of the learned behaviors—would help to better situate the work and highlight its significance.
*   **Comparison to MPC:** The comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 is insightful but brief. The authors claim their method produces more "predictable and less chaotic behaviors." This is an interesting point that could be substantiated with a more quantitative analysis or a deeper discussion on why the regularization provided by the neural network encourages this outcome.

**Detailed Comments for Clarification**

*   In Section 3.2, you state that using an LQG pass to compute feedback gains can produce "very large and ill-conditioned" gains. This is an important motivation for your proposed method. Could you provide more intuition or a concrete example of this issue?
*   Also in Section 3.2, the discussion of why LQG was not used appears before the full presentation of your proposed alternative. The clarity of this section might be improved by first fully describing your perturbation-based method for obtaining feedback gains and then contrasting it with the LQG approach.
*   The notation in the latter part of Section 3.2 (around lines 226-240) could be clearer. The introduction of `s(X) = s̄` followed by a cost term involving `s(X) - s̄` can be confusing, especially since the bar and tilde symbols are visually similar. Using more distinct notation for the perturbed target state and the state along the optimized trajectory could enhance readability.
*   The term "softer feedback gains" is used to describe the output of your method. Could you please define what "soft" means more precisely in this context (e.g., smaller L2 norm, smoother over time)? Why is this property desirable? It would also be helpful to expand on the intuition for why allowing the initial state to be a soft constraint helps achieve this.
