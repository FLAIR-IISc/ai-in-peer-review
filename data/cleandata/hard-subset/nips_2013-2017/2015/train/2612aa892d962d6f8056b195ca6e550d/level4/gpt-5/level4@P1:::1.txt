Summary:
The paper proposes a strategy for training neural network policies that incorporate local feedback gains, making them robust to disturbances and regression error. Trajectory optimization and policy learning are softly coupled via a penalty on deviations between the network’s outputs and the optimized actions, enabling the policy to approximate optimal trajectories while yielding behaviors that are simpler and more robust than those produced by MPC-based trajectory optimization alone. Although the authors employ substantial distributed parallelization to generate training data and refine policies, this engineering component serves primarily as an enabler rather than a core contribution. The resulting controllers operate in real time and successfully drive a diverse set of characters (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds).

Strengths:
- Elegant integration of trajectory optimization with neural policy learning through a soft consistency penalty. This design lets the policy inherit structure and stability from optimized trajectories while remaining robust to execution-time perturbations.
- The explicit use of feedback gains within the learned policy is a key idea that addresses the well-known sensitivity of learned controllers to compounding errors, improving stability and realism.
- The approach produces high-quality, visually compelling locomotion and control across markedly different morphologies without task-specific state machines or motion capture.
- Clear, polished presentation and a well-motivated comparison to MPC-generated behaviors; the observation that the joint objective regularizes toward predictable, cyclical behaviors is insightful.
- Strong systems execution: the distributed, asynchronous training pipeline is thoughtfully constructed and demonstrably effective, even if not the central intellectual contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The extensive parallelization is not itself a conceptual advance; it is an implementation detail that, while useful, is secondary to the main methodological ideas.

Evaluation:
- Quality: Excellent. The method is technically sound, with a convincing formulation that leverages trajectory optimization to supervise policy learning while embedding feedback structure to handle perturbations and model mismatch.
- Clarity: Excellent. The paper is exceptionally clear and well organized. The explanations of the coupling mechanism and the role of feedback gains are easy to follow.
- Originality: High. The soft coupling of trajectory optimization with neural policy regression, together with the explicit integration of feedback gains into the learned controller, is a compelling and distinctive synthesis.
- Significance: High. Demonstrating robust, real-time control across diverse 3D characters without task-specific architectures is impactful for both ML/control and computer graphics communities.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean and well-justified method that addresses a central challenge in learning stable, realistic controllers.
- Strong empirical results across diverse morphologies, with behaviors that are more regular and robust than MPC baselines.
- Clear writing and thorough evaluation of the core idea’s practical utility.

Arguments against acceptance:
- None substantial; the emphasis on engineering scale (parallelization) is supportive rather than foundational.

Overall recommendation:
This is an outstanding paper—technically strong, clearly written, and delivering impressive results. It compellingly bridges trajectory optimization and learned feedback policies to achieve robust, real-time control across varied systems. Strong accept.
