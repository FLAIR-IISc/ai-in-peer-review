

This paper presents a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex character behaviors, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion. The authors combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization [12]) with neural network policy learning, addressing key challenges in real-time character control. The core innovation lies in interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, and training for unexpected task changes. This approach differs from prior work [9, 11] by achieving stable 3D locomotion where previous methods were limited to 2D, while maintaining a unified architecture across diverse character morphologies without motion capture or task-specific features.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The method is well-supported by thorough experimental validation across multiple character types, with clear ablation studies showing the necessity of both joint optimization and noise injection. The authors effectively demonstrate that their full method outperforms naive neural network training on static trajectories by two orders of magnitude, with noise injection preventing overfitting that leads to policy divergence. The trajectory optimization and neural network training procedures are carefully designed to address the unique challenges of learning controllers (requiring precise real-valued outputs in closed-loop systems rather than i.i.d. samples), with the authors properly acknowledging the limitations of direct learning approaches [3, 6, 10, 14].

The paper excels in clarity. The writing is precise and well-organized, with each section addressing a distinct aspect of the problem and providing useful cross-references. The technical details are presented at an appropriate level for the complexity of the domain, and the supplementary video effectively demonstrates the impressive results. The methodological descriptions (Sections 2-4) are particularly clear, making the approach accessible to readers familiar with trajectory optimization and neural networks.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a significant contribution by extending prior work [12, 9, 11] to 3D locomotion while maintaining a unified architecture across diverse character types. The specific combination of trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning, including the interleaving technique and noise injection strategy, represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches. The paper adequately references relevant work, including the foundational CIO paper [12] and related trajectory optimization methods [9, 11].

The significance of the work is substantial for character animation and control. The method enables interactive, real-time control of diverse complex characters without motion capture or task-specific features, which is a major practical advantage. The ability to learn controllers for multiple character morphologies using the same architecture represents a meaningful step toward unifying disparate control approaches [21, 18, 13, 8, 7]. However, the paper could better articulate how this advances the broader field of reinforcement learning or control theory beyond the specific application to character animation.

The primary weakness lies in Section 7's explanation of the runtime optimization step. The authors claim that "physical state evolution is a result of optimization," but the motivation for this additional optimization step at runtime is not sufficiently explained. Given the description of the policy as "a neural network that learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously," it's unclear why the policy output alone wouldn't suffice for evolution without additional optimization. The reviewer expected the policy to operate without this extra step, as the paper states the network "learns optimal controls" rather than just dynamics. This appears to be because the policy regression error creates a residual trajectory cost that accumulates without re-optimization, but this relationship needs clearer explanation. The paper mentions that "this approach is reminiscent of feature-based control" but doesn't elaborate on how it differs from or improves upon existing feature-based approaches.

The paper would benefit from additional references at line 153 ("similar to recent approaches..."). While the proximal regularization approach is standard in optimization, providing one or two specific references would strengthen the claim. The typo at line 89 ("to unify" should be "to unify") is minor but should be corrected.

Overall, this is a well-executed, technically sound paper with impressive results. The method addresses a significant challenge in character control with a clear, well-motivated approach that builds on prior work while extending it meaningfully to 3D. The paper's strengths in technical execution, clarity, and results outweigh the minor weakness in explaining the runtime optimization step. The work makes a valuable contribution to character animation and control, and its principles could potentially extend to robotics applications. For these reasons, I recommend acceptance.
