This paper proposes a novel algorithm for real-time control of 3D models using a neural network (NN) that generates animations after being trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Notably, CIO and NN training are performed in an alternating fashion, where the output of one is used in the criterion optimized by the other. Another crucial component of the method is the injection of noise through data augmentation by small perturbations and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that this network can produce realistic and stable control policies for a range of character models.

This approach is particularly interesting given the challenges of real-time control of arbitrary models. Although the proposed method may initially seem straightforward ("learn a NN to predict the output of CIO"), the additional steps required to make it work (joint training and noise injection) are significant new contributions that improve generalization, as shown in experiments.

However, the experimental results would be stronger with comparisons to a larger dataset. The base training trajectories, generated with only 100-200 trials, each with 5 branched segments, are limited, making it unsurprising that the "no noise" variant overfits. The "no joint" variant may also benefit from more diverse training trajectories.

The comparative evaluation is the paper's weak point, as acknowledged in the introduction. A more comprehensive comparison to competing methods is needed, and the only comparison provided is a brief one with MPC at the end of the paper, which lacks a reference. 

For the most part, the mathematical explanations are clear and well-motivated, although some points are addressed superficially, particularly the CIO step (see Eq. 6, whose notations are not defined). Providing code would be beneficial for the community. One section that requires clarification is 3.2, which discusses generating optimal actions for noisy inputs. The notations are confusing, and it is unclear what is being done, especially regarding the time index "t" and whether it is still implied for some quantities.

Time measurements for computations during real-time control would be useful, as this could be a major bottleneck, especially in applications like video games where CPU availability is limited. Knowing how these computations scale with the complexity of the 3D model would also be beneficial.

Minor remarks include: 
- "biped location" could be rephrased as "locomotion."
- "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be "unite" or "unify."
- The target appears to be only about x, y, z coordinates, with no constraint on the angle the character is facing when reaching it. If correct, it would be useful to discuss the possibility of adding an extra target angle, which is important in practice.
- The term "noise injection" might be misleading, as it refers to data augmentation with re-computed targets, which is unrelated to denoising autoencoders.
- Clarification is needed on whether the initial (random) network weights theta are used in the first iteration of Alg. 1 to compute the first X's.
- The justification for using the same hyperparameter eta in both steps of Alg. 1 is unclear.
- Several minor errors and inconsistencies are present throughout the paper, including missing articles, incorrect acronym usage, and typographical errors.

Overall, this is a promising application of neural networks to character control, but its practical benefits and applicability still need to be established.
