This paper presents a method for the interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The core proposal is to learn the trajectories and the policy jointly. A key empirical insight is that decoupling this problem into alternating subproblems is effective, as it i) allows for the reuse of existing, well-documented methods and ii) performs sufficiently well in practice. While I could follow the paper well, my expertise in this specific domain is limited, making it difficult for me to assess its potential impact. On the surface, the work appears to be a feat of technical engineering, albeit one without novel theoretical insights.

The authors demonstrate an impressive level of technical sophistication at all levels—from the high-level concepts down to the implementation—which produces equally impressive results. I found that the paper clearly demonstrated the claimed advantages of i) using sensory noise during training and ii) learning trajectories jointly with the policy.

The paper is also clearly written, with what I believe is an appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section treats a separate aspect of the problem, and there are very useful cross-references between sections.

However, the motivation for the additional optimization step in Section 7 could have been better explained. At that point in the paper, based on what had been presented, I expected the policy to be able to run without the need for any extra optimization at test time. Is this step necessary because there is always a residual trajectory cost (or policy regression error) that would cause errors to accumulate if the policy's action were fed directly into the physical simulator? I would imagine that the forward evolution of the dynamics already takes physical constraints into account, so no physically implausible outcomes should be possible, but I might be missing something. I do appreciate, however, that re-optimizing at every timestep allows for the use of larger time steps.

A few minor points:
*   l153: The text states the method is "similar to recent approaches..." but provides no references. Could the authors add one or two? It was initially counterintuitive to me that keeping updates small in parameter space would be a sensible strategy in general, though I eventually understood its utility in the context of the paper's block-alternating optimization.
*   l89: There is a typo: "to unify".

In conclusion, the paper's results are rather impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation seem sensible. However, as this research area is slightly outside my expertise, it is difficult for me to be confident about the work's novelty and significance.
