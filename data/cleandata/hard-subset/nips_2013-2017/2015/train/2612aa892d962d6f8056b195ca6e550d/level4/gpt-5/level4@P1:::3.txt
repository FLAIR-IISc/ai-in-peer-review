Summary
The paper proposes a method for real-time control of diverse 3D characters by training a recurrent neural network (NN) to mimic and generalize the outputs of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Crucially, CIO and NN training are interleaved: trajectory optimization is regularized to stay close to the current NN policy, while the NN is trained to match the (noise-perturbed) optimal actions and feedback gains produced by CIO. The authors inject noise in two ways: (1) input perturbations coupled with first-order action corrections using optimal feedback gains, and (2) additive noise in hidden activations during NN training. They also present a distributed, asynchronous training setup and demonstrate a single NN architecture controlling a range of systems—swimming, flying, and legged locomotion—without task-specific features, state machines, or motion capture. Compared with prior work that coupled trajectory optimization and function approximation [Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov], the present method claims more complex 3D behaviors and improved stability via several technical improvements (joint training, noise, feedback computations, and proximal regularization).

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely goal: a single method and controller architecture achieving robust, realistic control across very different morphologies and tasks.
- Clear technical idea: alternating between trajectory optimization and NN regression, with principled noise injection and use of optimal feedback gains to make training robust to rollout deviations.
- Solid engineering: distributed training, GPU-based NN optimization, and a practical pipeline that appears to scale to nontrivial problems in a few hours.
- Demonstrated behaviors are compelling; the emergence of cyclic gait without hand-crafted schedules is notable.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Comparative evaluation is limited. Aside from an ablation vs. “no joint” and “no noise,” the only external comparison is a brief qualitative contrast with MPC without a proper reference or quantitative metrics. The introduction itself acknowledges that a systematic comparison with direct RL or policy search methods is pending; this paper would benefit from a more substantial empirical comparison.
- Dataset size and diversity: the training set is relatively small (100–200 trials, each with 5 branches). It is not surprising that the “no noise” variant overfits. The “no joint” variant might also benefit from a more diverse static dataset; the current comparison may understate what static training could achieve.
- Clarity and reproducibility: while most math is well-motivated, several key details (especially CIO) are covered superficially. Eq. 6 uses undefined notation, and Section 3.2 (feedback gains for noisy inputs) is difficult to follow; dropping the time index t exacerbates ambiguity. Code release would greatly help reproducibility.
- Real-time performance: the execution phase solves a T=1 optimization at each time step. No timing results or scaling analysis are given, which is critical for real-time applications (e.g., games) with limited CPU budgets.

Quality
- The approach appears technically sound and the ablations support the need for joint training and noise. However, the empirical case would be stronger with larger datasets, quantitative metrics (tracking error, energy, stability margins), and broader baselines (e.g., TRPO, DDPG, guided policy search).

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear, but Section 3.2 is hard to parse; defining all variables, retaining time indices where relevant, and clarifying the role of λ would help. Some notational slips and missing definitions (see detailed comments) hinder readability.

Originality
- The overall framework builds on known components (CIO, supervised policy learning), but the joint, noise-aware training with explicit use of CIO-derived feedback gains is a meaningful and practical contribution. The breadth of 3D behaviors controlled by a single architecture is noteworthy.

Significance
- Potentially significant for animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The demonstrated generality across morphologies is compelling, but broader comparisons and runtime measurements are needed to establish practical impact.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Target specification: Is the goal only in x, y, z? Is there no constraint on final facing orientation? If so, would adding a target orientation be straightforward? This seems important in practice.
- “Noise injection” terminology: The input perturbation with adjusted targets is more akin to data augmentation than denoising autoencoders, since targets change with inputs.
- Algorithm initialization: Are the initial trajectories in Alg. 1 computed using random initial θ in the penalty, or are the first X’s obtained without the penalty term?
- Shared proximal rate: What justifies using the same η in both subproblems of Alg. 1?
- Real-time step: Please report per-step solve times and scaling with model complexity. How many milliseconds per step on typical hardware?

Minor issues and editorial comments
- “biped location” → “locomotion.”
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches” → “unite” or “unify.”
- Missing article: “it is non-trivial to adapt them to the asynchronous and stochastic setting we have.”
- LQG is used one line before being defined.
- “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass” → “an LQG pass.”
- Section 3.2: l. 231, the star in argmin appears misplaced; is s̄ missing in C̃?
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory” → “the minimizer of a quadratic approximation around the optimal trajectory.”
- “sX and aX are subsets of φX”: please justify why this holds.
- “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)” → strictly, this reduces the θ-subproblem objective in Alg. 1.
- The asynchronous training architecture (Section 6) differs from strict alternation in Alg. 1; mention and reconcile earlier.
- Section 7: at l. 315, should f be fdes?
- “the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation”: this is unclear—please explain how soft enforcement leads to root forces and its implications.
- Experimental ablations: split “no noise” into “no data augmentation” vs. “no hidden noise.”
- Investigate whether α (penalty weight) can be annealed to zero on the CIO side. Is the benefit primarily a curriculum effect, or is it necessary to keep trajectories close to what the NN can represent?
- Table 1a caption says 4 layers; I believe the model has 3.
- Please add an MPC reference.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and well-engineered method with strong qualitative results across diverse 3D systems.
- Clear contributions: joint trajectory–policy optimization, principled noise/feedback handling, and scalable training.
- Potentially impactful for interactive control in graphics and robotics.

Cons against acceptance
- Weak comparative evaluation and small training datasets limit the strength of claims.
- Several clarity and notation issues, especially in Section 3.2 and CIO details.
- Missing runtime analysis for real-time control.

Overall
A compelling application of NNs to physics-based character control, with promising results and thoughtful engineering. To strengthen the paper, expand the comparative study, clarify Section 3.2, report real-time performance, and address the noted editorial issues. The practical benefits and broader applicability are promising but not yet fully established.
