## Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

This paper demonstrates the use of a small, rapidly evaluated neural network to represent a control policy for interactive character control within a physics simulator. The core approach utilizes trajectory optimization during training to learn this neural network policy. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the adaptability of a single network architecture to a diverse set of cyclic behaviors and varying body morphologies.

**General Comments**

My assessment is that this work represents an application of deep learning techniques to established, recent advances in computer graphics. The effectiveness of trajectory optimization in generating realistic control signals is well-documented in prior research, including work cited by the authors. Furthermore, the use of neural networks to approximate the sequential actions required to replicate trajectories obtained through trajectory optimization has also been explored previously (e.g., [9]). Consequently, the authors need to more explicitly delineate the novelty of their approach relative to this existing body of work. 

Specifically, the recent work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine on reinforcement learning for control appears particularly relevant. A clearer comparison would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should articulate how their method differs – does it aim for faster learning, broader applicability to different body shapes, reduced runtime computational cost, or increased robustness to environmental variations? Establishing this differentiation is crucial for understanding the contribution.

**Specific Comments**

1.  The statement “we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” lacks clarity. Please elaborate on the implications of these gains being large and ill-conditioned.

2.  Consider restructuring the presentation to postpone the discussion of why Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) control was not utilized until *after* the proposed strategy has been fully presented. This would improve the narrative flow and allow for a more focused justification.

3.  The notation around lines 226-240 is visually confusing. The introduction of  s(X) = \bar s, followed by a cost function defined in terms of s(X) - \bar s, employs visually similar symbols (bars and squiggles) which hinder readability. A different notation would be beneficial.

4.  The phrase “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda” requires clarification. Softer relative to what baseline? What does it mean for feedback gains to be "soft" versus not soft in this context? Additionally, please explain the rationale behind perturbing the initial state as part of the procedure—why is this modification beneficial?

Overall, this is sound work. However, the paper’s contribution relative to the current landscape of related research is not sufficiently emphasized. A more thorough and direct comparison to recent advancements in the field, particularly reinforcement learning approaches for control, would substantially improve the paper’s impact and clarity.
