This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to act as a real-time feedback controller for physically-simulated characters. The core concept is to use an offline trajectory optimizer, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate high-quality motion data, and then train the network to replicate the optimizer's behavior online. The main contribution is a joint optimization framework where the policy and the trajectories are iteratively refined together. This approach builds upon recent work combining trajectory optimization and policy learning [9, 11], but extends it to more complex, three-dimensional locomotion tasks such as walking, swimming, and flying. The authors attribute their success on these harder problems to several technical improvements, including a principled method for injecting noise during training and a novel technique for extracting optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality and Significance:** The technical quality of the work is very high. The authors demonstrate considerable sophistication in integrating multiple complex systems—a physics simulator, a trajectory optimizer, and a neural network training pipeline—into a cohesive framework. The results are impressive, as shown in the supplementary video, showcasing stable and plausible control across a diverse set of character morphologies and movement styles (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) from a single, unified method. The ablation study in Section 8.1 clearly demonstrates the value of the key technical contributions, namely the joint optimization procedure and the injection of sensory noise.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. Despite the complexity of the overall system, the authors do a good job of breaking down the problem into its constituent parts (Trajectory Optimization, NN Regression, Distributed Training), making the methodology understandable. The cross-references between sections are helpful for following the flow of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** While the engineering and results are impressive, the work's primary contribution appears to be an effective and sophisticated combination of existing techniques rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new theoretical concept. The originality lies in the specific formulation of the joint optimization problem and the technical refinements that enable it to work on challenging 3D characters.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a powerful and well-engineered framework that successfully generates interactive controllers for a variety of complex 3D characters, which is a significant practical achievement.
*   The empirical results are visually compelling and strongly support the authors' claims.
*   The ablation studies provide clear evidence for the importance of the proposed technical components (joint optimization, noise injection).
*   The work has high potential for impact in applied fields such as computer animation, gaming, and robotics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The contribution is more of a significant engineering accomplishment that pushes the state of the art, rather than a novel theoretical insight. The novelty lies in the synthesis and scaling of existing ideas.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The motivation for the additional optimization step at execution time (Section 7) could be clarified. After training, I expected the policy network to be capable of directly controlling the character by outputting an action that could be fed to the simulator. Instead, the policy's output is used as the target for a single-step optimization problem. Is this because directly applying the policy's actions leads to an accumulation of errors and instability? I would assume the physics simulation enforces constraints, preventing physical implausibility, but perhaps I am missing a detail. Clarifying why this step is necessary would be helpful.
2.  Line 153: The paper states that using quadratic proximal regularization terms is "similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches". Could you please provide one or two key references here? It is not immediately intuitive why keeping updates small in parameter space is a generally beneficial strategy, though I understand its utility in this block-alternating optimization context.
3.  Minor typo on line 89: "to unity these disparate approaches" should likely be "to unify these disparate approaches".
