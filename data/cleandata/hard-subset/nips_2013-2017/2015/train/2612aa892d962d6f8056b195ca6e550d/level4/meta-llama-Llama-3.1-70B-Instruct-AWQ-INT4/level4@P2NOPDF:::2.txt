"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" presents a method for utilizing a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy for interactive control of a character in a physics simulator. This approach employs trajectory optimization during the training phase to learn a neural network policy. The primary contribution of this paper is demonstrating that the same architecture can be applied across a range of cyclic behaviors and various body types.

General Comments
----------------

Upon reviewing this paper, it appears to be an application of deep learning to existing techniques in computer graphics, albeit with recent advancements. The effectiveness of trajectory optimization in finding realistic control signals has been established in prior research, some of which is cited in this paper. The use of neural networks to approximate the step-by-step actions required of a policy to replicate trajectories similar to those found by trajectory optimization has also been explored in previous studies (e.g., [9]). Therefore, the authors must provide a clearer positioning of their work in relation to these previously published approaches.

A notable comparison can be made to the recent work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss how their approach differs from this line of research. Specifically, they should address whether their method is designed to learn faster, support a wider variety of body shapes, require less computation at runtime, or exhibit greater robustness to environmental variabilities.

Specific Comments
-----------------

The statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" is unclear. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed explanation of this phenomenon.

The discussion of why LQG was not used would be more effective if presented after the introduction of the authors' strategy. This would provide a clearer context for the decision.

The notation in lines 226-240 is confusing, particularly with the introduction of s(X) = \bar s and the subsequent definition of a cost in terms of s(X) - \bar s. The use of similar visual symbols (bars and squiggles) may contribute to the confusion.

The claim "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda" requires further clarification. Specifically, the authors should explain what is meant by "softer" feedback gains, how they compare to alternative approaches, and why this is a desirable outcome.

Additionally, it is unclear why changing the initial state is beneficial as part of the procedure. While the work presented is sound, the contribution of this paper relative to other recent related research is not clearly established.
