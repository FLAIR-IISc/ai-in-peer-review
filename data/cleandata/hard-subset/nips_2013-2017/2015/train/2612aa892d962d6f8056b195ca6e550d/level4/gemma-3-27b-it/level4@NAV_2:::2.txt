"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems, enabling stable and realistic behaviors for locomotion tasks across diverse body morphologies – swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking – without relying on motion capture or task-specific features. The approach leverages trajectory optimization to generate training data for the neural network, which learns to map states to actions, effectively learning the dynamics under an implicit policy. The key contribution lies in demonstrating the ability to synthesize controllers for complex 3D locomotion using a single network architecture and training procedure, a feat not previously achieved with comparable methods.

General Comments
This paper builds upon established techniques in both deep learning and computer graphics, specifically the use of trajectory optimization for generating realistic motor behaviors and neural networks for function approximation. The authors effectively combine these approaches to create a system capable of generating interactive control policies for a diverse set of characters. A core strength is the scalability of the method to complex, three-dimensional environments, surpassing previous work largely limited to two-dimensional characters. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough positioning of its contribution relative to recent advancements, particularly in the field of reinforcement learning and imitation learning for control.

The ability of trajectory optimization to produce compelling control signals is well-established, as acknowledged by the authors. Similarly, utilizing neural networks to approximate policies learned from trajectory data has been explored previously [9]. To strengthen the paper, a more detailed comparison with related work, such as the approaches developed by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine, is crucial. Specifically, clarifying the advantages of this method – whether in terms of training speed, generalization capability, computational efficiency at runtime, or robustness to environmental variations – would significantly enhance its impact.

Specific Comments
The phrase “we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” (line 226) requires further elaboration. Providing specific details about the magnitude and condition number of the gains, and explaining the implications for controller performance, would be valuable. 

Consider restructuring the presentation to introduce the limitations of traditional methods like LQG *after* outlining the proposed approach. This would create a clearer narrative flow and highlight the motivations behind the authors' chosen strategy.

The notation surrounding lines 226-240 is somewhat confusing. The use of both bars and squiggles to denote different variables can be visually distracting. Clarifying the distinction between s(X) = \bar s and the subsequent use of s(X) – \bar s would improve readability.

The statement “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” (line 241) lacks clarity. Defining what constitutes “soft” gains and explaining the benefits of this approach – specifically, why modifying the initial state is advantageous – would strengthen the explanation. 

Overall, the work presents a solid contribution to the field of character control. However, explicitly articulating the novelty and advantages relative to existing methods would further solidify its significance.

Arguments for acceptance:
* **Scalability to 3D Locomotion:** The method successfully addresses the challenging problem of generating stable and realistic locomotion in complex, three-dimensional environments, a feat that has proven difficult for previous approaches.
* **Generality:** The use of a single network architecture and training procedure across diverse body morphologies and locomotion tasks demonstrates the versatility of the approach.
* **Interactive Control:** The resulting controllers enable interactive, real-time control of characters in a physics simulator, opening up possibilities for applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
* **Sound Methodology:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network regression is well-motivated and effectively implemented.

Arguments against acceptance:
* **Limited Novelty:** The paper builds upon existing techniques and lacks a sufficiently detailed comparison to closely related work, particularly in reinforcement learning and imitation learning.
* **Lack of Quantitative Comparison:** While qualitative results are compelling, a more rigorous quantitative evaluation against state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the claims of improved performance.
* **Clarity Issues:** Certain aspects of the presentation, particularly the notation and explanations surrounding the feedback gains, could be improved for clarity.
