- Proposal: Train an RNN to mimic Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs via alternating trajectory optimization and supervised NN regression with feedback-aware noise and hidden-layer noise for robustness.
- Execution: At test time the policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces; a one-step constrained optimization enforces physics for real-time interactive control.
- Relation to prior work: Extends trajectory-optimization + supervised learning approaches (Levine & Koltun, Mordatch & Todorov) and complements model-free methods and MPC.
- Main contributions: interleaved optimization/training loop, principled input-noise targets derived from trajectory feedback gains, and a distributed asynchronous training pipeline.
- Strength: Clear and compelling goal to produce real-time controllers for diverse characters without hand-designed controllers or motion capture.
- Strength: Thoughtful integration of CIO with NN training; using feedback gains to adjust noisy-input targets addresses closed-loop stability.
- Strength: Demonstrated breadth across swimming, flying, bipeds, and quadrupeds with emergent cyclic behaviors and a single architecture.
- Strength: Solid engineering (cloud/distributed CIO, GPU training) and a pragmatic one-step physics-consistent execution scheme.
- Strength: Ablation studies show both joint training and noise are important for stability at test time.
- Weakness: Comparative evaluation is limited—no quantitative baselines against modern policy-search methods (GPS/TRPO) or a robustly tuned MPC.
- Weakness: Small dataset scale (100–200 trials with branches) raises overfitting concerns and weakens empirical claims.
- Weakness: Section 3.2 is hard to follow; notation for perturbed optima, time indexing, and derivation steps (Woodbury identity, sX/aX) need clarification for reproducibility.
- Weakness: Real-time performance is not quantified; runtime per control step and scaling with DoF/contacts are missing.
- Weakness: Algorithmic mismatch—Algorithm 1 shows strict alternation but Section 6 implements an asynchronous proximal scheme; reconcile and foreshadow this earlier.
- Weakness: Clarity and minor inconsistencies (undefined symbols in Eq. 6, typos) impede reproducibility.
- Quality assessment: Technically sound with a neat derivation and solid engineering, but empirical support is weakened by limited baselines, dataset size, and missing runtime data.
- Clarity needs: Define all symbols (H, C, J, d, K) in equations, correct Eq. 9/C̃ notation, and explicitly spell out sX and aX relative to φX.
- Execution clarification: Explain the one-step execution optimization clearly (is fdes intended, can soft constraints introduce root forces?).
- Originality: Meaningful and novel integration of CIO with feedback-aware noise and distributed alternating training; applying a single architecture to diverse 3D characters is ambitious.
- Significance: Potentially influential for physics-based animation and robotics if made robust, reproducible, and benchmarked against strong baselines with runtime evidence.
- Requests for clarification: whether goals include orientation as well as position, how Alg. 1 is initialized, why same η used in subproblems and any schedule for α, and precise mapping of sensory/action subsets.
- Terminology note: Input-noise target adjustment is closer to data augmentation than denoising autoencoders—please clarify intended framing.
- Real-time compute request: Provide timing per control step, scaling with DoF/contacts, and hardware used.
- Editorial/minor comments: Multiple wording fixes, missing MPC reference, LQG defined late, Eq./algorithm captions and layer counts inconsistent.
- Recommendation: Accept contingent on adding at least one strong quantitative baseline, clarifying Sec. 3.2 and notation, and reporting runtime/scaling performance.
