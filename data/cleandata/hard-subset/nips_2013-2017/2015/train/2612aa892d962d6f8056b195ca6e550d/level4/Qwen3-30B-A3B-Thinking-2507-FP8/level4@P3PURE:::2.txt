

Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks presents a method for using a small, fast neural network to represent a control policy enabling interactive character control within a physics simulator. The approach leverages trajectory optimization during training to learn a neural network policy, with the key contribution demonstrating that the same architecture generalizes across diverse cyclic behaviors and body types.  

General Comments  
This paper constitutes an application of deep learning to established techniques in computer graphics. The ability of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals has been validated in prior work, some of which is appropriately cited. Similarly, the use of neural networks to approximate policy actions for replicating trajectory-optimization outputs has been explored in earlier research (e.g., [9]). Consequently, the authors must more clearly position their work relative to these existing approaches. The closest related work involves the recent line of research by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. The authors should explicitly clarify how their method compares to this work: Is it intended to learn faster? Support a broader range of body shapes? Reduce runtime computation? Enhance robustness to specific environmental variabilities? The contribution relative to recent literature remains insufficiently clarified.  

Specific Comments  
1.  Clarify the statement: "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." What specific process is referenced, and why are the gains ill-conditioned?  
2.  The discussion of why LQG was not used should be moved to after the presentation of the proposed strategy, to maintain logical flow.  
3.  The notation in lines 226–240 is confusing. The introduction of $s(X) = \bar{s}$ followed by a cost defined as $s(X) - \bar{s}$ uses visually similar symbols (bars and squiggles), creating ambiguity.  
4.  Explain the claim: "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter $\lambda$." Softer than what baseline? What defines "soft" versus "hard" gains? Why is modifying the initial state part of the procedure?  
The work is sound, but the contribution relative to recent related literature requires stronger justification.
