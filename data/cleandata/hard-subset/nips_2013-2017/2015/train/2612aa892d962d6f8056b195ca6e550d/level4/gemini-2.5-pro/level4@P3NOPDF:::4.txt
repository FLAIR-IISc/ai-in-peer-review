### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for interactive control of complex, physically simulated characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The core contribution is a framework for jointly learning optimized trajectories and a corresponding policy that can generate similar behaviors in response to user commands. A key empirical insight is that decoupling this joint optimization into alternating subproblems is highly effective, allowing the authors to leverage existing, well-documented methods for each component. The approach produces impressive results on a range of complex character control tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Sophistication and Impressive Results:** The paper demonstrates a high level of technical sophistication, from the conceptual framework down to the implementation details. This technical skill translates into visually impressive and compelling results, showcasing fine-grained control over complex characters.

2.  **Clear Empirical Validation:** The authors effectively demonstrate the benefits of their key design choices. The claimed advantages of incorporating sensory noise during training and of learning the trajectories jointly with the policy are clearly supported by the experiments.

3.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-written and clear. Given the complexity of the problem and the proposed solution, the authors provide an appropriate level of detail. The document is well-structured, with each section addressing a distinct aspect of the problem, and helpful cross-references effectively guide the reader through the methodology.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification**

1.  **Motivation for Online Optimization (Section 7):** A key point that could be clarified is the motivation for the additional online optimization step described in Section 7. After reading the preceding sections, my expectation was that the learned policy could be used directly at runtime to generate actions. The necessity of this extra optimization step was not immediately apparent. Is this step required because of a residual error—either from the trajectory cost or the policy regression—that would otherwise accumulate if the policy's actions were fed directly into the physics simulator? While the simulator's forward dynamics would enforce physical plausibility, I wonder if this re-optimization is primarily a mechanism to correct for drift and keep the character's state close to the learned manifold of optimized behaviors. A more explicit discussion of the trade-offs and the root cause for needing this step would strengthen the paper. I do appreciate the authors' point that this allows for the use of larger timesteps, but the underlying necessity remains somewhat unclear.

2.  **Request for References (Line 153):** On line 153, the paper states that keeping parameter updates small is "similar to recent approaches." It would be helpful to include one or two specific citations here. While I ultimately understood the utility of this trust-region-like approach within the paper's alternating optimization scheme, the general principle can be counterintuitive, and grounding it with references would benefit readers less familiar with these methods.

**Overall Assessment**

Overall, the paper presents an impressive system with strong empirical results. The proposed optimization framework and its implementation appear sound and well-executed. On the surface, the work is a significant technical achievement.

My main reservation stems from my limited expertise in this specific subfield of character animation and reinforcement learning, which makes it difficult for me to confidently assess the novelty and potential long-term impact of the work relative to the state of the art. The contribution appears to be a powerful and effective combination and application of existing techniques rather than a breakthrough in fundamental theory. Nonetheless, the quality of the execution and the results are very high.
