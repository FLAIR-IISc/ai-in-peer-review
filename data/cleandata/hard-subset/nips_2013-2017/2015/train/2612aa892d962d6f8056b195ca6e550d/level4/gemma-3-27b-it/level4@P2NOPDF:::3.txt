This submission introduces an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, leveraging a neural network (NN) trained offline to mimic the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key aspect of the approach is the iterative, alternating training of the CIO and NN, where each informs the optimization criterion of the other. Furthermore, the method incorporates noise injection through data augmentation via small input perturbations and additive noise within the NN’s hidden layers. Experimental results demonstrate the network’s ability to generate realistic and stable control policies across a diverse set of character models.

This is a compelling approach to a challenging problem: real-time control of complex 3D models. While the core idea—learning a NN to predict CIO’s output—appears conceptually simple, the authors’ additions of joint training and noise injection represent significant contributions. Experiments suggest these elements are crucial for improved generalization performance. 

However, the strength of the experimental evaluation is a concern. The training dataset, generated from “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” is relatively limited. Consequently, overfitting in the “no noise” variant is unsurprising, and the “no joint” variant could also benefit from more varied training data. The paper acknowledges a need for a more systematic comparison to other methods (as stated in the introduction), and the current evaluation is the primary weakness. The sole comparison to a competing method, MPC, is brief and lacks a proper citation.

The mathematical presentation is generally clear and well-motivated, though further detail would be helpful for replication. Certain aspects, particularly the CIO step (e.g., Eq. 6 and its associated notation), are presented superficially and require familiarity with external resources. Providing code would significantly benefit the research community. Section 3.2, detailing the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, was particularly difficult to follow due to confusing notation and the omission of the time index “t” from some quantities. 

Including runtime measurements for real-time control would also be valuable. This information is critical for assessing the practical applicability of the method, particularly considering computational bottlenecks in applications like video games where CPU resources are constrained.

Minor points for consideration: “biped location” should likely be “locomotion”; “unity these disparate approaches” should be “unify”; the current target appears limited to x, y, z coordinates – exploring the addition of an orientation constraint (target angle) could be beneficial; the term “noise injection” may be more accurately described as “data augmentation” given the re-computation of targets; clarification is needed regarding the initial network weights (θ) used in the first iteration of Alg. 1; the justification for using a shared hyperparameter (η) in both steps of Alg. 1 is unclear; grammatical corrections are needed ("it is non-trivial to adapt them to *the* asynchronous and stochastic setting," and "While we can use *a* Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass"); several minor typographical errors exist (lines 231, 315); and explanations are needed regarding the meaning of subsets sX and aX of φX and the statement about objective minimization in relation to Alg. 1. Furthermore, the asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 differs significantly from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1, and this difference should be highlighted earlier in the paper. The sentence regarding dynamics constraints and root forces (l. 315) requires clarification. Finally, the “no noise” variant in the experiments could be further divided into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise injection” for a more nuanced analysis. An interesting avenue for future work would be to investigate whether α can be reduced to zero during optimization, exploring the contribution of CIO as a form of curriculum learning. The caption for Table 1a incorrectly states 4 layers; the network appears to have 3.

In summary, this is a promising application of neural networks to character control. However, the practical benefits and broad applicability of the method require further validation through more extensive experimentation and a more thorough comparative analysis.
