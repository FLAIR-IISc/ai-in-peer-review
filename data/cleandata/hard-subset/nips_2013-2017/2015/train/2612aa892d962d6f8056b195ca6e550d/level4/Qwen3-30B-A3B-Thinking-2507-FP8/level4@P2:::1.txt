

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural network controllers for diverse character locomotion tasks, combining trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning to achieve robust, real-time interactive control. The authors introduce a method where recurrent neural networks learn not just control policies but also implicit dynamics models, incorporating optimal feedback gains through a principled noise injection strategy. The key innovation lies in their joint optimization framework that softly couples trajectory optimization with neural network training using a quadratic penalty for deviation, allowing the network to learn stable, realistic behaviors without task-specific hand-crafting.

The work builds on recent trajectory optimization methods like Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12] and related neural network control approaches [9, 11], but makes significant advances by extending these to complex 3D locomotion scenarios where prior work was limited to 2D. The method successfully controls diverse characters including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion with a single architecture, eliminating the need for task-specific state machines or motion capture data. This represents a meaningful step toward unifying disparate control approaches across different character types.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical grounding and thorough experimental validation. The authors provide detailed ablation studies demonstrating the necessity of key components like noise injection and joint optimization. The method's robustness to perturbations and ability to generalize across diverse character morphologies are convincingly demonstrated through extensive experiments in the supplementary video. The trajectory optimization approach with optimal feedback gains calculation is well-justified and carefully implemented, with clear explanations of the mathematical derivations.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical contributions are clearly articulated with a logical flow from problem statement to method to results. The algorithmic descriptions are precise and well-illustrated, with helpful figures showing the diverse locomotion behaviors achieved. The connection between the theoretical formulation and practical implementation is particularly well-explained, making the complex methodology accessible to readers. The supplementary video effectively demonstrates the quality of the results.

Originality: This work significantly advances the state of the art in neural network control for character animation. While there is related work [9, 11], the authors extend these methods to handle substantially harder 3D locomotion problems across diverse character morphologies without task-specific tuning. The integration of optimal feedback gains within the neural network policy through principled noise injection represents a novel contribution that addresses a key challenge in neural network control for dynamic systems. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by demonstrating successful 3D locomotion where prior methods were limited to 2D.

Significance: The results represent a major step toward automatic synthesis of controllers for diverse character behaviors, with broad implications for animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The ability to control a wide range of characters with a single architecture without task-specific hand-crafting is highly significant for both research and industry applications. The method's real-time interactive capability opens new possibilities for applications in gaming, virtual reality, and robotics where responsive character control is essential. The techniques could also be extended to more complex robotic control problems with appropriate domain adaptation.

The paper's distributed training architecture, while not the central contribution, is well-designed and effectively enables the large-scale experiments required for this work. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough to allow reproduction of the results.

The main limitation is that the paper could provide more detailed analysis of computational complexity and scaling to even larger problems, though this is understandable given the paper's primary focus on methodological contribution rather than computational optimization. The connection to biological systems, mentioned in the conclusion, could also be more thoroughly explored.

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The method is technically sound, well-executed, and demonstrates impressive results across a diverse set of locomotion tasks. The work represents a tour de force of technique that successfully marries trajectory optimization, neural networks, and control theory to achieve a compelling solution to a challenging problem. The paper is clear, well-written, and makes a strong case for the significance of its contributions. I believe this work will be highly influential in the fields of character animation, robotics, and neural network control. I strongly recommend acceptance.
