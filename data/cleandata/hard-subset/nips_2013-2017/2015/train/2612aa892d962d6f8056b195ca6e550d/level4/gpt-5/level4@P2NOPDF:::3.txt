This paper presents an approach for real-time control of 3D character models in which a neural network (NN) generates animations after being trained offline to imitate Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Crucially, CIO and NN training are interleaved: each alternates optimization steps using objectives that depend on the other’s output. A second key ingredient is noise: training relies on both data augmentation via small input perturbations and additive noise in the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments suggest the method produces realistic, stable control policies across diverse character models.

The problem is important and challenging, and the overall idea is compelling. While the high-level concept (“train an NN to predict CIO outputs”) may sound straightforward, the joint training scheme and noise mechanisms are meaningful contributions that, according to the experiments, significantly improve generalization.

That said, the empirical evaluation is the weakest part. The dataset used to generate base trajectories is quite small (“100–200 trials, each with 5 branched segments”), so it is not surprising that the “no noise” variant overfits. The “no joint” variant might also benefit from broader trajectory diversity, even if the effect is less obvious. More comprehensive comparisons would strengthen the claims (as you also note: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The only baseline comparison is a brief MPC result at the end, and no citation is provided for this MPC variant.

Clarity and reproducibility are mixed. The mathematical exposition is generally well motivated, but several details would be difficult to reconstruct without consulting prior work. In particular, aspects of the CIO step are only sketched (e.g., undefined notation around Eq. 6). Section 3.2 (optimal actions for noisy inputs) is especially hard to follow: the notation becomes confusing, the time index t appears to be dropped, and it is unclear which quantities still depend on time. A public code release would be highly valuable to the community.

Runtime and scalability are not reported but are critical for real-time use. Please provide timing measurements for control at test time, and discuss how costs scale with character complexity. In practical applications (e.g., games), per-character CPU budgets are tight, and the control loop must be extremely efficient.

Specific comments and questions:
- “biped location”: do you mean locomotion?
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches”: should be “unite” or “unify.”
- Is the target defined only over (x, y, z) positions, with no constraint on facing direction at the target? If so, how difficult would it be to add a desired orientation target? This seems practically important.
- Terminology: generating additional samples from noisy inputs with recomputed targets is better described as “data augmentation,” whereas “noise injection” more aptly refers to perturbations added inside the NN (e.g., to hidden activations). Conflating them obscures the ablation.
- Initialization: In the first iteration of Algorithm 1, do you actually use random NN weights θ to compute the first X’s, or do you initialize X using CIO without the regularization term? Please clarify.
- Why is the same hyperparameter η used in both optimization steps of Algorithm 1?
- Wording: “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have” → add “the.”
- Acronym order: LQG is used one line before being defined. Also “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass” → “an LQG pass.”
- Line 231: the star in the argmin appears misplaced; is a bar over s missing in C̃? Please check notation.
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: likely missing an article/word.
- “sX and aX are subsets of φX”: why is this guaranteed? Please justify.
- “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: since only θ is optimized, this refers to the objective in Algorithm 1 rather than Eq. (4) per se—please clarify.
- The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs substantially from the alternating procedure in Algorithm 1; it would help to foreshadow this earlier.
- Line 315: should f be f^{des}?
- “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.” Please clarify what “root forces” are included and their impact on physical plausibility.
- Consider splitting the “no noise” ablation into (i) no data augmentation and (ii) no hidden-layer noise. This would isolate the contribution of each component.
- It would be informative to study annealing α to zero during CIO: is performance driven mainly by a curriculum effect (easier early optimization), or is it consistently beneficial to keep CIO trajectories close to what the NN can represent throughout training?
- Table 1a caption states 4 layers, but it appears to be 3—please verify.

Overall, this is an attractive and creative application of neural networks to character control, with promising qualitative results. The alternating training with noise appears essential and novel. To solidify the contribution, I recommend: (1) a broader and better-cited empirical comparison (including stronger baselines and larger/diverse datasets), (2) clearer and more complete notation—especially in the CIO and noisy-input sections, (3) runtime and scaling measurements, and (4) code availability. These additions would significantly improve confidence in the method’s practicality and reproducibility.
