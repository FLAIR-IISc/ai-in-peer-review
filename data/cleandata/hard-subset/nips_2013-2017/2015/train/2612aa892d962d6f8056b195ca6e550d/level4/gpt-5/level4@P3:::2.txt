Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a method for training compact recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex, physics-based characters. The approach interleaves direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised policy learning: CIO produces trajectories and local feedback gains; the policy is trained to map noisy sensory inputs to actions that track the optimized trajectories and their first-order feedback corrections. Key ingredients include: (i) injecting Gaussian noise into sensory inputs during training; (ii) using trajectory-optimizer-derived feedback gains to define target actions under noise; (iii) an alternating optimization scheme with proximal regularization to update both trajectories and policy; (iv) an efficient perturbation-based method to compute feedback gains by reusing the Gauss-Newton structure of CIO; and (v) distributed/asynchronous training. At test time, a single forward pass of the neural network yields desired velocities, forces, and recurrent-state updates, and a T=1 constrained optimization step enforces dynamics.

The authors demonstrate a single architecture working across diverse morphologies and behaviors: 3D swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion, without motion capture, state machines, or task-specific features. Ablations show joint optimization and noise are important to avoid overfitting and instability; a comparison to MPC highlights differences in gait regularity.

The work sits close to guided policy search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine and Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch and Todorov, RSS’14), which also alternate between trajectory optimization and supervised policy learning. The novelty here is the use of CIO to handle rich contact dynamics in 3D, the perturbation method for feedback gains that reuses CIO computations, and the particular noise-injection regimen that stabilizes recurrent policies. Compared to LQG-based GPS methods, CIO can discover contact sequences directly, and the gains computation avoids an additional LQG pass, potentially improving conditioning.

Strengths
- Unifies trajectory optimization and policy learning to produce real-time controllers that are robust and visually plausible across very different morphologies and media (air/water/ground).
- Uses a single network architecture and training recipe; no state machines or mocap initialization.
- Practical perturbation-based computation of local feedback gains that reuses the Gauss-Newton inverse and avoids ill-conditioned post hoc LQG passes.
- Clear ablations (no-joint, no-noise) supporting the importance of the joint optimization and noise-injection components.
- Distributed implementation with realistic training times; simple real-time execution via a one-step constrained update.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Positioning vs. closely related GPS-style methods [9,11] is underdeveloped. It remains unclear in what ways the proposed method is strictly superior or complementary (e.g., stability, sample efficiency, runtime cost, robustness to disturbances/model mismatch).
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. Most claims are qualitative (stability/realism). Metrics such as task completion rate under perturbations, recovery from pushes, energy/torque usage, and tracking error would strengthen the case. Comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., GPS/LQR-based methods, TRPO) are missing.
- The notion of “softer feedback gains” is vague; the role and effect of λ lack quantitative characterization (e.g., gain norms/condition numbers vs. λ, rollout performance).
- Some clarity issues: notation with bars/tilde in the gains section is hard to parse; the LQG discussion appears before the alternative is fully presented; phrases like “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains” need explanation.
- Runtime execution still solves a T=1 optimization with soft dynamics constraints, which may introduce root forces and could affect physical fidelity. The trade-off between stability, physical realism, and computational cost deserves more discussion.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound overall. The alternating optimization, noise-injected regression targets with first-order corrections, and reuse of Gauss-Newton structure for gains are well-motivated. However, stronger empirical validation with quantitative metrics and comparisons would better support claims of near-optimality, robustness, and generality.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, but the gains section (lines ~226–240) would benefit from cleaner notation (e.g., avoid both bars and tildes; define all Jacobians explicitly). The LQG critique would read better after the proposed gains method is presented. Clarify what “ill-conditioned gains” means (e.g., condition numbers, sensitivity) and relate to observed instability. Define “softer” gains precisely (e.g., smaller spectral norms, smoother temporal variation).

Originality
The overall framework builds on established ideas (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning + noise injection), but the application to 3D contact-rich characters with CIO, the particular stochastic alternating scheme, and the efficient gains computation are novel and practically useful. The action parametrization as desired velocities and forces is an interesting design choice that merits discussion vs. torque policies.

Significance
If further validated, the method could be impactful in animation and possibly robotics for generating robust, real-time controllers without hand-crafted architectures. The cross-morphology results are promising. The absence of quantitative benchmarks and head-to-head comparisons limits the current demonstrable advance.

Questions for the authors
- How does your method compare empirically to GPS (Levine & Koltun, 2014) or Mordatch & Todorov (2014) on similar 3D tasks in terms of data required, training time, and runtime cost?
- Can you quantify “very large and ill-conditioned gains” from the LQG pass (e.g., norms/condition numbers) and show how your λ-regularized gains differ?
- What is the sensitivity of performance to λ and α, and to the sensory/hidden noise scales? Please include gain norms and rollout metrics as λ varies.
- How robust are controllers to model mismatch or unmodeled perturbations (e.g., mass/friction changes, external pushes)?
- What is the real-time computational budget for the T=1 optimization, and how does it scale with model size?

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Reorder Section 3.2: first present your gains method, then compare to LQG and explain why the latter led to ill-conditioned gains in your setting.
- Clean up notation in the gains derivation; avoid similar-looking symbols for different objects; define all Jacobians and dimensions.
- Define “softer gains” precisely and support with plots/tables (norms, condition numbers, rollout performance vs. λ).
- Add quantitative evaluations: success rates, energy use, perturbation recovery, and comparisons to GPS/TRPO/MPC across tasks.
- Discuss the implications of soft dynamics constraints at runtime and the presence of root forces; if possible, bound or report their magnitudes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, unified method that achieves diverse, stable behaviors in 3D with a single architecture.
- Novel and efficient way to obtain feedback gains within CIO.
- Clear ablations demonstrating necessity of joint optimization and noise.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited quantitative evidence and lack of comparisons to the most relevant baselines.
- Some clarity gaps in technical exposition (gains section, notation).
- Claims about robustness/near-optimality are not yet strongly substantiated.
