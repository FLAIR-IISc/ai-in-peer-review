### Summary

This paper presents a method for training a single, general-purpose recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for a diverse range of complex, physically-simulated characters. The method successfully generates stable and realistic interactive locomotion for various morphologies, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters, without requiring motion capture data or task-specific engineering.

The core of the approach is an alternating optimization scheme that interleaves trajectory optimization with supervised learning. An offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization, or CIO) is used to generate optimal trajectories for various tasks. A neural network is then trained to mimic the state-action mappings from these trajectories. Crucially, this is not a one-off process; the network's policy is fed back into the trajectory optimization cost function, and the process iterates. This joint optimization allows the system to discover stable, cyclic, and predictable behaviors. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training for robustness and a novel technique to compute optimal feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimizer to guide the network's learning on perturbed states.

This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with neural network function approximators (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but makes a significant step forward by successfully applying these ideas to complex, unstable, high-dimensional 3D characters, a substantially harder problem than the 2D characters addressed in prior work.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality & Significance

The primary strength of this paper lies in its impressive and significant results. The ability to control such a wide array of characters with a single, unified algorithm is a noteworthy achievement in character animation and control. The behaviors shown in the supplementary video are stable, realistic, and interactive, demonstrating a clear advance in the state of the art. The method addresses the difficult problem of generating complex motor skills automatically and provides a compelling alternative to hand-engineered controllers or purely model-free reinforcement learning, which can often produce less natural behaviors. The ablation study in Section 8.1 provides strong evidence supporting the authors' claims about the importance of joint optimization and noise injection, reinforcing the technical soundness of the work. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers in robotics, animation, and control.

#### Originality

While the constituent components—trajectory optimization and supervised learning for control—are known, their integration into a synergistic, alternating optimization framework is novel. The specific technical contributions, particularly the method for deriving optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) and using them to structure the noise for training, represent a clever and original insight. The extension of this class of methods from 2D to complex 3D locomotion is a non-trivial and important contribution.

#### Clarity & Organization

While the results are strong, the paper's clarity and organization could be significantly improved. The current presentation makes it difficult to fully grasp some key aspects of the method.

1.  **Assumption of a Perfect Model:** The method relies on Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), which requires full knowledge of the system's dynamics, including the equations of motion and contact models (Equation 6). This is a critical assumption that distinguishes the work from model-free approaches. It would be beneficial to state this requirement explicitly in the introduction to properly frame the paper's contributions and limitations for the reader.

2.  **Description of the Neural Network:** The abstract and introduction highlight the use of a *recurrent* neural network, with recurrent units implementing memory states. However, Section 4 ("Neural Network Policy Regression") describes a standard regression setup on a dataset of fixed input-output pairs `{s + ε, a + asε}`. This section does not explain how the recurrent state `r` and its temporal dependencies are handled. Is the network unrolled in time during training? Or are the recurrent state and its derivative simply treated as additional features in an i.i.d. regression, ignoring the temporal structure? This is a crucial missing detail that needs to be clarified for the work to be reproducible.

3.  **Disproportionate Sectioning:** The description of the core learning method in Section 4 is very brief, while the implementation details in Section 6 ("Distributed Training Architecture") are given comparable space. Section 4 should be expanded to include the details mentioned above, as it is more central to the paper's contribution than the specific distributed computing setup used.

4.  **Imprecise Terminology:** The authors refer to using "stochastic neural networks" (e.g., in the conclusion). However, the network itself appears to be deterministic, while the training process is stochastic due to the injection of noise into inputs and activations as a form of regularization. The policy at execution time is also deterministic. Using more precise language, such as "a deterministic network trained with stochastic regularization," would improve clarity and avoid potential confusion.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper demonstrates highly impressive results on a very challenging problem, successfully generating stable and interactive control for a diverse set of 3D characters.
*   **Pro:** The proposed method is general, powerful, and represents a significant advance over prior work that was limited to simpler, 2D characters.
*   **Pro:** The core technical ideas, particularly the joint optimization of trajectories and the policy, and the use of feedback gains to structure training noise, are novel and well-motivated.
*   **Pro:** The experimental validation, including the ablation studies, is thorough and convincingly supports the main claims of the paper.

### Arguments for Rejection

*   **Con:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that obscure critical details of the methodology, particularly regarding the training of the recurrent neural network.
*   **Con:** The reliance on a perfect dynamics model is a major limitation that is not adequately highlighted upfront.
*   **Con:** The organization of the paper could be improved, with underdeveloped sections on the core method and disproportionate focus on implementation details.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a significant contribution with outstanding results. The method is powerful and the demonstrated capabilities are at the forefront of the field. However, the paper is held back by several clarity and organizational issues that need to be addressed. The work is promising and likely merits acceptance, but I would strongly urge the authors to restructure the paper and clarify the missing details regarding the network training procedure. A revised version that addresses these points would constitute a very strong NIPS paper.
