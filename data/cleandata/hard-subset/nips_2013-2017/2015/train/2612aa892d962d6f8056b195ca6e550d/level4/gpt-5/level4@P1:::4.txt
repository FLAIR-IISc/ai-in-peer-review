Summary and relation to prior work
This paper combines trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning to produce interactive, real-time controllers for complex, physically simulated characters. The key idea is to jointly optimize a set of trajectories and a recurrent neural policy by alternating between (i) direct trajectory optimization using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and (ii) supervised regression of a stochastic policy to match actions and local feedback gains along those trajectories. The policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces, and includes a small recurrent state. Noise is injected into sensory inputs and hidden units to improve robustness and generalization. The method also derives stabilizing feedback gains from the trajectory optimization via a perturbation-based approach, avoiding a separate LQG pass. Training is implemented in a distributed, asynchronous architecture with proximal regularization to stabilize the alternating optimization.

This work is closely related to learning-from-trajectory-optimization approaches (e.g., Mordatch & Todorov; Levine & Koltun) and stands in contrast to direct model-free RL methods (e.g., TRPO, DQN) and hand-crafted control architectures (e.g., state machines, ZMP/capture point). The claimed advance is scaling to diverse, high-DOF, 3D behaviors (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion) with a single architecture, and demonstrating that interleaving trajectory optimization and policy learning with noise and proximal regularization leads to stable, realistic controllers.

Strengths
- Technically sophisticated and well-engineered: from the CIO-based trajectory generation and feedback-gain computation to the distributed, asynchronous training pipeline on GPU/EC2.
- Clear empirical demonstration that (i) injecting sensory/hidden-unit noise and (ii) joint, alternating optimization substantially improve test-time robustness compared to ablations.
- Strong and diverse results: stable, cyclic locomotion for 3D bipeds and quadrupeds, and plausible swimming/flying behaviors, all with a single policy architecture and without motion capture or task-specific state machines.
- Paper organization is clear and the level of detail appropriate; sections are modular with helpful cross-references.

Weaknesses and questions
- Section 7: The need for a per-timestep optimization at execution time could be better motivated. Given the policy outputs desired velocities and forces, I expected direct forward simulation without an additional optimization step. Is the extra step primarily compensating for residual trajectory cost and/or policy regression error, preventing drift and accumulating error? Since the simulator enforces physical constraints, why is this not sufficient on its own? I appreciate that re-optimizing each step enables larger timesteps, but a more explicit discussion of the trade-offs (stability, accuracy, and computational cost) would help.
- The paper appears to deliver impressive engineering and empirical results but does not claim new theory. I am not deeply immersed in this subarea, which makes it difficult for me to confidently assess novelty and broader impact relative to closely related NIPS/ICML work.
- Line 153 (“similar to recent approaches…”): please add one or two references for the stochastic/proximal regularization viewpoint that keeps updates small between alternating subproblems. This was initially counterintuitive to me in general, though the paper made clear why it is useful in your block-alternating setup.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically sound, and claims are supported by ablations and comparative discussions (e.g., MPC vs. learned policy). The feedback-gain derivation via a perturbation method that reuses trajectory optimization computations is a nice practical contribution. The asynchronous training with proximal terms seems well motivated and empirically stable.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. One suggestion is to expand the motivation for the execution-time optimization in Section 7, as noted above.
- Originality: The combination and scaling are the main contributions. The components (CIO, supervised policy learning, noise injection, proximal regularization) are known; the novelty lies in how they are integrated and applied to challenging 3D character control with convincing results. No new theoretical insights are claimed.
- Significance: The results are strong and likely useful to researchers/practitioners in physics-based animation and possibly robotics, especially for scenarios where data must be synthesized and real-time control is required. That said, because this area is slightly outside my core expertise, I am less certain about the degree of novelty over recent related work.

Pros for acceptance
- Demonstrates a robust, general method for real-time control across diverse morphologies and tasks in 3D.
- Clear empirical evidence for the benefits of joint optimization and noise.
- Practical, well-executed system that others could build upon.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical novelty; primarily an integration and scaling effort.
- The necessity and implications of the execution-time optimization step need clearer justification.
- Novelty/significance relative to the latest trajectory-optimization-plus-learning methods is somewhat hard for me to assess definitively.

Minor comments and typos
- Line 89: “to unity” → “to unify.”
- Line 153: Please provide references for “similar to recent stochastic optimization approaches” that employ proximal regularization or trust-region–style constraints (e.g., trust-region methods; proximal/regularized SGD; you already cite TRPO, which is in a similar spirit).

Recommendation
The optimization scheme and implementation appear sensible, and the results are impressive. While the paper does not introduce new theoretical insights, it presents a compelling, well-validated systems contribution. Clarifying the execution-time optimization rationale and adding the requested references would strengthen the paper. My overall stance is positive, with some reservations about novelty and significance due to my limited familiarity with this specific subarea.
