This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems and tasks, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking with different body morphologies. The authors combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, and training for unexpected changes in the task specification.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a universal synthesis method applicable to arbitrary behaviors, body morphologies, online changes in task objectives, perturbations due to noise, and modeling errors. The authors achieve this by interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to find optimal trajectories, and then training a neural network to learn from these trajectories.

The paper is well-motivated, and the results are impressive, demonstrating stable and realistic behaviors for various characters. However, there are some issues with the presentation and clarity of the paper.

One major concern is that the paper assumes full model knowledge, which is not explicitly stated at the beginning. This assumption is crucial, as it affects the applicability of the method to real-world problems. The authors should clearly state this assumption and discuss its implications.

Additionally, Section 4 is disproportionately short and does not provide enough details about the neural network architecture, particularly the recurrent units mentioned in the abstract. The authors should expand this section to provide more information about the neural network design and training process.

Furthermore, the paper claims to use a stochastic neural network, but it is unclear how the network is stochastic, as the training data is stochastic, but the network itself seems deterministic. The authors should clarify this point and provide more details about the stochastic nature of the network.

Lastly, the paper could benefit from a clearer structure, with more distinct sections and a more logical flow of ideas. The authors should consider reorganizing the paper to improve its clarity and readability.

In terms of the NIPS criteria, the paper scores well on significance, as it addresses an important problem in control and robotics. The paper also scores well on originality, as it presents a novel combination of supervised learning and trajectory optimization. However, the paper could improve on clarity, as some sections are unclear or disproportionately short. The paper also scores well on quality, as the results are impressive and well-presented.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective method for training neural network controllers.
* The results are impressive, demonstrating stable and realistic behaviors for various characters.
* The paper addresses an important problem in control and robotics.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes full model knowledge, which is not explicitly stated.
* The paper could benefit from a clearer structure and more distinct sections.
* Some sections, such as Section 4, are disproportionately short and lack details.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
