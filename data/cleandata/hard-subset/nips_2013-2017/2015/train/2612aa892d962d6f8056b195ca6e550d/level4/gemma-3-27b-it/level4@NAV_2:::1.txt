The authors present a method for training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers, demonstrating stable and realistic behaviors across a range of dynamical systems and tasks – including swimming, flying, and both bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion with varying body morphologies. The approach eschews motion capture, task-specific features, and state machines, relying instead on a neural network architecture with a large feed-forward component for state-action mapping and a smaller recurrent component for memory. Crucially, the network learns the dynamics *under* an implicit policy by predicting velocity, and training interleaves supervised learning with trajectory optimization, incorporates noise injection, and accounts for unexpected task changes. Furthermore, the trajectory optimizer is leveraged to determine optimal feedback gains alongside optimal actions.

This paper addresses a challenging problem – automating the creation of interactive, real-time controllers capable of complex and stable movements – a goal shared with reinforcement learning and stochastic optimal control, but rarely achieved in high-dimensional, continuous spaces. The work builds upon existing methods like Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12] by utilizing a neural network to learn from the optimizer and generate online behaviors. The authors highlight recent related work [9, 11], but argue their method tackles significantly harder problems, particularly achieving stable 3D locomotion where prior work was limited to 2D characters. This improvement is attributed to a series of technical advancements detailed in the paper. The authors also contextualize their work within the historical applications of neural networks to control, noting the recent successes in areas with large datasets like computer vision, and emphasizing the unique challenges of learning controllers – requiring precise real-valued outputs, operating in closed-loop systems, and avoiding overfitting with limited data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound, carefully combining trajectory optimization with neural network training. The use of noise injection and the extraction of optimal feedback gains are particularly insightful and address key challenges in learning robust controllers.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the problem, their approach, and the experimental setup. The mathematical notation is used effectively and the explanations are concise and accessible.
*   **Originality:** The combination of trajectory optimization, neural network learning, noise injection, and feedback gain extraction is a novel and effective approach. Extending this technique to successfully control a diverse range of characters in 3D space is a significant contribution. The comparison to prior work effectively establishes the novelty of the method.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. The ability to generate stable and realistic locomotion for diverse characters using a single network architecture and training procedure is a major advance. This work has the potential to impact robotics, animation, gaming, biomechanics, and neuroscience. The demonstration of interactive control is particularly compelling.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including ablation studies (Figure 2) and comparisons to alternative approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The computational cost of the training process is significant, requiring cloud computing and hours of training time. While acknowledged, further discussion on potential methods for reducing computational burden could be beneficial.
*   The comparison to direct methods (e.g., [3, 6, 14]) is currently based on impression. A more systematic and quantitative comparison would strengthen the claims about the superior performance of the trajectory-optimization-based method.
*   While the algorithm is applied to diverse morphologies, the range of tasks is limited to locomotion. Exploring the applicability of the method to more complex manipulation or interaction tasks would be a valuable extension.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in control.
*   The results are highly significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method across a diverse range of tasks and characters.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The authors have conducted a thorough evaluation of their method.
*   The work has the potential to impact multiple fields of research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of training is high.
*   The comparison to direct methods is currently qualitative.
*   The task domain is currently limited to locomotion.

**Overall:**

This is an outstanding paper that represents a significant advance in the field of interactive control. The strengths of the work far outweigh the weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance to the conference. It’s a beautiful and compelling piece of research that will undoubtedly inspire further work in this area.
