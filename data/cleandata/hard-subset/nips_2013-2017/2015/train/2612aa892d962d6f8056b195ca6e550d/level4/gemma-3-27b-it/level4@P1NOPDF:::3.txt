In this submission, the authors propose an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, employing a neural network (NN) to generate animations trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Importantly, CIO and NN training are not independent; they are performed in an alternating fashion, with the output of one informing the criterion optimized by the other. A core element of the method is the injection of noise, achieved through data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise in the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that the resulting network can produce realistic and stable control policies across a diverse range of character models.

This is a very interesting approach, given the challenges inherent in real-time control of arbitrary models. While the proposed method might initially seem straightforward – learning an NN to predict CIO output – the additional steps required to make it effective (joint training and noise injection) represent significant contributions, and experimental results suggest these steps improve generalization considerably.

However, the experimental results would be strengthened by comparison to a larger dataset. Given that the base training trajectories were generated using only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” the overfitting observed in the “no noise” variant is not surprising. The “no joint” variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories, although this is less apparent. 

Overall, the comparative evaluation is the paper’s main weakness (acknowledged in the introduction: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The comparison to a competing method is limited to a brief analysis with MPC at the end of the paper, and this method lacks a reference.

For the most part, the mathematics are clearly explained and motivated, although replicating these results would likely require consulting additional references, as some points are addressed only superficially – particularly the CIO step (see Eq. 6, where notations are undefined). Providing code would be highly valuable to the community. I struggled to fully understand section 3.2, concerning the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs. The notation became confusing, and the specific operation was unclear (in particular, the time index "t" was dropped, but is it still implied for some of the quantities?).

Including time measurements for computations during real-time control would also be useful, as this could represent a significant bottleneck. Knowing how these computations scale with model complexity is also important; in applications like video games, limited CPU resources are available for character animation.

If so, would adding a target angle be straightforward, as it seems important in practice? – I would describe the step where additional data is generated from noisy inputs as “data augmentation” rather than “noise injection,” as the target is re-computed to match the modified inputs. This differs from the principle behind denoising autoencoders. – Are you using the initial (random) network weights θ in the very first iteration to compute the first X’s in Alg. 1, or are you starting with X’s computed without the regularization term? – What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter η in the two steps of Alg. 1? – “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”: should this be “…the asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”? – The acronym LQG is used before it is defined. – “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”: should this be “…a Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”? – l. 231 the star in the argmin is misplaced, and is \bar{s} missing in \tilde{C}? – “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: is a word missing? – “sX and aX are subsets of \phiX”: what guarantees this? – “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: this refers to the objective from Alg. 1, as only θ is optimized. – The asynchronous joint optimization scheme described in Section 6 differs significantly from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1. This difference should be mentioned earlier. – l. 315, f should be f^{des}? – “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.”: This sentence is unclear. – The “no noise” variant in the experiments could be separated into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise injection.” – An interesting question to investigate is whether α can be decreased to zero during optimization (for CIO only). In other words, does the method primarily benefit from a “curriculum learning” effect (starting with an easier task), or is it always beneficial to maintain trajectories learned by CIO close to what the network can learn? – The caption of Table 1a states 4 layers, but I believe there are 3.

Overall, this is a promising application of neural networks to character control, but further work is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and applicability.
