"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" presents how a compact, high-speed neural network can encode a control policy for interactive character control within a physics simulator. The method relies on trajectory optimization during training to learn the policy network. The claimed contribution is that a single architecture applies across a spectrum of approximately cyclic behaviors and a variety of body morphologies.

General Comments
----------------
My interpretation is that this paper applies deep learning to established (though recent) techniques in computer graphics. Prior work has already demonstrated that trajectory optimization can produce realistic control signals, and some of that is cited here. Likewise, using neural networks to approximate the stepwise policy actions that reproduce trajectories obtained via trajectory optimization has been explored in earlier work (e.g., [9]). The authors therefore need to more clearly position their contributions relative to these prior methods. The closest related line, in my view, is the recent work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. How does this approach compare? Is the goal faster learning? Support for a broader set of morphologies? Reduced runtime computation? Increased robustness to environmental variation?

Specific Comments
-----------------
What does it mean that “the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains”?

Consider moving the discussion explaining why LQG was not used to after your own strategy has been presented.

The notation around lines 226–240 is unclear. You introduce s(X) = \bar s, and then define a cost using s(X) - \bar s. The overbars and the other diacritics are visually hard to distinguish.

What is meant by “our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda”? Softer than what? What does “soft” versus “not soft” mean in this context? Why is it beneficial to alter the initial state as part of your method?

Solid work, but the contribution relative to other recent related efforts is not articulated clearly enough.
