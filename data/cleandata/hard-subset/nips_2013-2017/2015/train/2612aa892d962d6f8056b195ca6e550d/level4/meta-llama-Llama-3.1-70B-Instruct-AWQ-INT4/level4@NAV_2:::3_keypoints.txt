- Proposes a real-time control algorithm where a NN is trained offline to reproduce CIO outputs through alternating/joint optimization.  
- Core contributions are the alternating CIO–NN training scheme and noise injection (data augmentation + additive hidden noise) to improve generalization.  
- Experiments show the NN produces realistic, stable control policies across varied character models and tasks (locomotion, swimming, flying).  
- Results suggest joint training and noise injection substantially improve generalization compared to straight supervised learning.  
- Comparative evaluation is weak: small dataset (100–200 trials) likely causes overfitting and limits conclusions.  
- Only a brief comparison to MPC is provided and MPC is not properly referenced.  
- Mathematical presentation is mostly clear but some parts (especially the CIO step and Eq. 6) lack definitions and detail needed for replication.  
- Section 3.2 (generating optimal actions for noisy inputs) is confusing; notation and dropped time index "t" make the procedure unclear.  
- Providing code would greatly help reproducibility.  
- No runtime or scaling measurements for real-time control are reported — this is important for practical use (e.g., games).  
- Several minor writing/notation errors and typos throughout (wording, misplaced stars, undefined acronyms, missing words).  
- Clarification needed on whether target includes orientation (angle) or only position; adding orientation target might be important in practice.  
- Terminology: calling the perturbed-target step “noise injection” is misleading — it’s closer to data augmentation than denoising autoencoding.  
- Questions left open about algorithmic details: initial Xs in Alg.1, use of same hyperparameter eta in both steps, and justification of some algorithmic choices.  
- Suggest splitting the “no noise” experimental ablation into “no data augmentation” vs “no hidden noise injection.”  
- Interesting future investigation: whether CIO’s alpha can be annealed to zero (curriculum effect vs continual regularization).  
- Acceptance recommendation: novel method with promising results; accept with minor revisions addressing comparisons, CIO detail/notation, runtime reporting, and minor fixes.
