

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a novel approach to generating real-time controllers for diverse physical characters using neural networks trained to reproduce outputs from Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). The key innovation lies in an alternating optimization procedure where trajectory optimization and neural network training are interleaved, rather than performed sequentially. The authors demonstrate this method's effectiveness across a range of 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without task-specific features, state machines, or motion capture. The method incorporates noise injection during training to improve robustness and generalization.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it demonstrates that neural networks trained with trajectory optimization (CIO) can generate stable, realistic behaviors for complex 3D characters, solving problems substantially harder than prior work (which was limited to 2D). Second, the authors introduce a principled approach to generating optimal feedback gains for noisy inputs during training, which significantly improves robustness without requiring additional cost terms. Third, they provide a distributed training architecture that scales efficiently, allowing training on complex 3D characters.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The technical approach is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the joint optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) and the novel feedback gain computation method (Section 3.2). The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's ability to produce diverse, stable behaviors across multiple character types without task-specific tuning. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) effectively show the necessity of both joint optimization and noise injection for good generalization. The paper also makes a strong case for the method's potential applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

The paper's main weaknesses concern the comparative evaluation and some technical details. The authors acknowledge that "a systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done," and the current comparison with MPC is limited to a single figure without comprehensive analysis or timing data. This is a significant gap, as the paper's claim of superiority over direct neural network training methods rests on a single ablation study. Additional comparisons with other reinforcement learning approaches (e.g., TRPO, PPO) would strengthen the claims.

The technical presentation is mostly clear, but some aspects require clarification. The notation in Section 3.2 becomes confusing, particularly regarding the time index "t" and the relationship between the perturbation method and optimal feedback gains. The explanation of how the neural network policy is executed in real-time (Section 7) would benefit from more precise details about computational complexity and timing. The claim that "the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation" requires elaboration.

The paper is well-positioned in the NIPS literature, building on recent work in trajectory optimization (Mordatch et al., 2012; Levine & Koltun, 2014) while introducing significant technical improvements. The approach represents a meaningful advance in the field of neural network controllers for complex physical systems, moving beyond the typical classification tasks that dominate deep learning applications.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported experimental results. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach through ablation studies. The clarity is generally good, though some technical details could be better explained. The originality is strong, as the method represents a significant extension of existing trajectory optimization approaches to neural network controllers for diverse 3D characters. The significance is high, as the method enables interactive, real-time control of physically plausible character behaviors across multiple morphologies, with potential applications in animation, robotics, and biomechanics.

**Pro arguments for acceptance:**
- Demonstrates the first method capable of generating stable, realistic 3D locomotion for diverse character types without task-specific engineering
- Introduces a principled joint optimization approach that significantly outperforms naive neural network training
- Provides a clear, distributed training architecture that scales efficiently
- Shows strong generalization across different character morphologies with a single controller architecture
- Makes a compelling case for the method's broader applicability to robotics and biomechanics

**Con arguments against acceptance:**
- Comparative evaluation is limited to a single ablation study and one comparison with MPC
- Some technical details (particularly in Section 3.2) require clarification for reproducibility
- Lack of timing information for real-time execution limits assessment of practical applicability
- The paper doesn't adequately address how the method scales with increased character complexity

The authors should address these weaknesses in revision by: (1) providing a more comprehensive comparison with alternative methods (including timing data), (2) clarifying the technical details in Section 3.2, and (3) adding timing information for real-time execution. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on neural network controllers for physical systems. While the comparative evaluation could be strengthened, the method is novel, well-executed, and has clear significance for multiple application areas. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
