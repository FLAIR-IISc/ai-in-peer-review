- Paper proposes RNN policies trained to mimic an offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) using joint alternating optimization and principled noise injection for robust, real-time 3D character control.
- Joint optimization scheme: neural network and trajectory optimizer trained together, each influencing the other's objective.
- Noise injection: sensory input perturbations (data augmentation) and hidden-layer additive noise to improve robustness and generalization.
- Demonstrated generality: same architecture controls bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers producing stable, realistic gaits without task-specific engineering.
- Significant technical contribution: advances prior work by handling high-dimensional, unstable 3D characters with crucial algorithmic innovations (joint training and noise).
- Impressive visual and empirical results shown in supplementary video, indicating broad applicability toward a unified synthesis method.
- Well-motivated approach with clear rationale for combining trajectory optimality and neural-network real-time performance, supported by ablations.
- Weakness: limited comparative evaluation — lacks thorough quantitative benchmarks against state-of-the-art policy search, RL methods, and proper MPC baselines.
- Weakness: clarity and reproducibility issues — high-level CIO description, undefined notation in Equation 6, dense derivation of feedback gains, and no released source code.
- Weakness: ablation justification is weak given small training dataset (100–200 trials); overfitting in "no noise" and potential better "no joint" performance with larger datasets not ruled out.
- Weakness: missing practical real-time metrics — no ms/frame timing or CPU/GPU cost and scaling information for interactive applications.
- Question: clarify goal specification — is the target only a 3D location, and can target orientation be specified?
- Suggestion: rename sensory "noise injection" as data augmentation since actions are recomputed for perturbed states; distinguish from denoising autoencoder concept.
- Question: initialization details — was the first trajectory optimization run with a random policy or without the policy-matching term R?
- Suggestion: reconcile Algorithm 1’s alternating scheme with the described asynchronous, distributed implementation and mention this earlier.
- Question: policy execution vector inconsistency — action listed as [q̇des ṙdes f] but objective uses fdes; clarify whether action should be fdes.
- Suggestion: split "no noise" ablation into separate tests for sensory/data augmentation and hidden-unit noise to isolate effects.
- Minor corrections: typos and ordering (e.g., "biped location"→"biped locomotion", "aim to unity"→"aim to unite", define LQG before use, Table 1a caption mismatch).
- Conclusion: promising, visually impressive work with important technical ideas, but needs stronger comparative evaluation and clearer, more reproducible technical descriptions to maximize impact.
