- Proposes training compact recurrent neural-network controllers by alternating Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning.  
- Policy training uses noisy sensory inputs and trajectory-optimizer-derived first-order feedback gains as targets.  
- Key techniques: sensory Gaussian noise injection, gains-derived noisy-action targets, alternating proximal updates, Gauss-Newton-based perturbation gains, and distributed training.  
- At test time, a single forward RNN pass outputs desired velocities/forces and a T=1 constrained optimization enforces dynamics.  
- Demonstrates a single architecture across diverse 3D morphologies and media (swimming, flying, bipedal/quadrupedal locomotion) without mocap or state machines.  
- Ablations show joint optimization and noise injection are crucial to avoid overfitting and instability.  
- Novelty: applying CIO to contact-rich 3D characters, an efficient perturbation method that reuses CIO computations for gains, and a stabilizing noise regimen for recurrent policies.  
- Strength: unified, practical pipeline producing robust, visually plausible controllers with real-time execution.  
- Strength: single network/training recipe works across tasks without task-specific engineering.  
- Strength: practical gains computation avoids ill-conditioned post hoc LQG passes by reusing Gauss-Newton structure.  
- Strength: clear ablation studies and a distributed implementation with realistic training times.  
- Weakness: Insufficient positioning versus GPS/LQR-style prior work; unclear empirical advantages in stability, sample efficiency, runtime, or robustness.  
- Weakness: Limited quantitative evaluation and missing comparisons to strong baselines (GPS/LQR, TRPO); many claims are qualitative.  
- Weakness: “Softer feedback gains” and the role of λ are described vaguely without quantitative characterization.  
- Weakness: Notational and exposition issues in the gains section; LQG critique appears before the alternative is fully presented.  
- Weakness: Runtime T=1 optimization may introduce root forces and affect physical fidelity; trade-offs between stability, realism, and cost need discussion.  
- Quality: Methodologically sound but requires stronger empirical validation to support claims of near-optimality and generality.  
- Clarity: Generally clear but gains derivation needs cleaner notation, explicit Jacobians, and clearer definitions of “ill-conditioned” and “softer” gains.  
- Originality: Builds on established GPS-style ideas but contributes novel application to 3D contact dynamics, a stochastic alternating scheme, and efficient gains computation.  
- Significance: Potentially impactful for animation and robotics if quantitatively validated; cross-morphology results are promising.  
- Key questions for authors: empirical comparisons to GPS/Mordatch, quantitative measures of ill-conditioned gains and λ effects, sensitivity to λ/α/noise, robustness to model mismatch, and runtime cost of T=1 optimization.  
- Suggested improvements: reorder gains discussion, clean notation, define and quantify “softer gains,” add quantitative benchmarks (success rates, energy, perturbation recovery), and report runtime/root-force magnitudes.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practical unified method, novel in-gains computation, and clear ablations showing necessity of core components.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient quantitative evidence, unclear comparisons to baselines, and exposition gaps in the gains section.
