Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a method for learning near–optimal feedback controllers by interleaving direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised training of recurrent neural networks. The policy maps a sensory state to actions that specify desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent state updates. A key idea is to inject Gaussian noise into sensory inputs during training and, crucially, to adjust target actions using local linear feedback gains (computed efficiently from the trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss–Newton Hessian and Woodbury identity). This soft-couples trajectory optimization and policy learning through a quadratic penalty, solved by alternating between trajectory optimization and neural network regression with proximal regularization. At test time, the learned policy runs in real time, with a one-step optimization enforcing dynamics and contact consistency. The approach is demonstrated on diverse 3D tasks—swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion—using the same network architecture and training procedure.

The work builds on and extends recent lines of research that combine trajectory optimization with function approximation for policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS 2014). Compared to prior 2D results or settings with simpler contact, this paper tackles harder 3D locomotion and flight tasks and proposes a novel, efficiently computed feedback-gain correction for noise-injected supervised targets. The method is also positioned relative to model-predictive control and to robustness techniques inspired by denoising autoencoders and dropout.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A principled and practical mechanism to obtain local feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer, avoiding a separate LQG pass and yielding better-conditioned gains. This is a neat and potentially reusable contribution.
- Integration: A coherent training loop that alternates CIO and neural network regression with noise injection and proximal terms; clear rationale for why closed-loop robustness requires co-optimizing data and policy.
- Generality and breadth: A single architecture and training pipeline produces interactive controllers across markedly different morphologies (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds), without motion capture, state machines, or task-specific features.
- Real-time execution: The one-step implicit optimization to enforce dynamics/contact at test time is elegant and avoids storing trajectories.
- Empirical ablations: Comparisons to static dataset training and no-noise training show large robustness gains; network-size sweep is reported.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is well written, with equations that make the algorithmic components easy to follow. The distributed training setup is pragmatic and reproducible.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. Beyond training/test losses and qualitative videos, there is little reporting of success rates, stability margins (e.g., distance traveled before failure), energy/torque cost, or disturbance-rejection performance. Adding standardized metrics across tasks would substantiate “near-optimal” and “robust” claims.
- Comparisons to stronger baselines are sparse. Beyond ablations and an MPC qualitative comparison, the paper does not compare to alternative trajectory-optimization–based controllers (e.g., iLQR/DDP with time-varying LQR feedback), or to contemporary policy search methods (e.g., TRPO, or guided policy search variants) on the same tasks. Even if exact apples-to-apples comparisons are hard, reporting performance on a common representative task would help position the contribution.
- Physical realism and soft constraints. Dynamics and contact complementarity are enforced softly; the authors acknowledge possible nonphysical root forces during execution. It would be useful to quantify how often and how large these violations are, and whether tightening penalties or hardening constraints is feasible at test time without loss of stability.
- Action parameterization. The policy predicts desired velocities and contact forces, not torques, and relies on a one-step optimization to produce a feasible next state. This blurs the line between “policy” and “implicit dynamics under policy.” A short discussion of trade-offs versus torque policies, and whether the method extends to torque-level control without the one-step optimization, would clarify scope (especially for robotics).
- Limited analysis of the recurrent state. The paper states there are a small number of recurrent units but does not specify size across tasks or analyze what the memory captures (e.g., contact phase, goal changes). Reporting the recurrent dimensionality and ablations (with/without recurrence) would inform how critical the memory is.
- Robustness to larger perturbations. Noise injection is small and Gaussian. It would be valuable to test robustness to significant pushes, terrain variations, parameter errors, or sensor delays (which the authors mention as future work), and to report failure modes.
- Computational cost and accessibility. While the distributed setup is straightforward, the method relies on substantial offline trajectory optimization. A brief accounting of samples/trajectories per task and sensitivity to the number of trials/branches would help readers gauge data/compute requirements.

Assessment against NIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and carefully engineered. The feedback-gain derivation and its integration into training are convincing. The main gap is in comprehensive, quantitative evaluation and stronger baselines.
- Clarity: Very clear and well organized. Minor clarifications suggested above (recurrent size, action choice, constraint violations).
- Originality: A novel and practical way to extract stabilizing gains from direct trajectory optimization and to use them for target correction under noise; the joint training loop is a thoughtful consolidation of existing ideas with new twists.
- Significance: High potential impact for physics-based animation and model-based control, demonstrating diverse, interactive behaviors from a unified pipeline. The method could influence how trajectory optimization and policy learning are coupled in high-DOF systems.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant and effective integration of CIO with neural network policies, including a clever, efficient feedback-gain computation.
- Strong qualitative results across diverse 3D tasks with a single architecture, without motion capture or state machines.
- Clear presentation and practical training recipe (noise, proximal regularization, asynchronous distributed setup).
- Demonstrates real-time controllers with realistic, stable behaviors.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines (iLQR/LQR, TRPO/GPS) on common tasks.
- Reliance on soft dynamics/contact constraints and one-step optimization at execution raises questions about physical fidelity and portability to hardware.
- Action parameterization and role of recurrence are under-analyzed; generalization beyond the trained goal-perturbation setting is not deeply tested.

Questions for the authors
- What is the dimensionality of the recurrent state across tasks, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- How large are dynamics/contact violations at test time, and can constraints be hardened without instability?
- Can you report standardized metrics (success rate, time-to-fall, energy per meter) and a comparison to an iLQR+TVLQR baseline?
- How does performance degrade with larger unmodeled perturbations (e.g., push tests, terrain changes)?

Recommendation
A strong accept, contingent on adding more quantitative results and a baseline comparison on at least one task. The technical contributions and breadth of demonstrations make this a valuable and impactful paper.
