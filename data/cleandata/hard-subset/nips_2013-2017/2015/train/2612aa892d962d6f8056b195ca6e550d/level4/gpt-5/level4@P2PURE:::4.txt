This paper proposes combining trajectory optimization with policy learning to enable interactive control of complex characters. The central idea is to jointly learn the trajectories and the policy, with the empirical observation that decoupling the joint problem into alternating subproblems i) enables reuse of existing, well-documented methods and ii) works sufficiently well in practice. I was able to follow the paper, but I am not immersed enough in this domain to confidently assess its potential impact; on the surface it certainly showcases technical prowess, albeit without novel theoretical insights.

The authors demonstrate an impressive level of technical sophistication throughout—from the conceptual design to the implementation—and the results are equally compelling. The claimed benefits of i) injecting sensory noise during training and ii) learning trajectories jointly with the policy are clearly demonstrated.

The paper is clearly written, with what seems to me the appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section addresses a distinct aspect of the problem, and the cross-references are very helpful.

I think the motivation for the additional optimization step in Sec. 7 could have been explained more clearly. Based on the preceding sections, I expected the policy to run without any extra optimization. Is this step necessary because there is always a residual trajectory cost (or policy regression error) such that feeding the action produced by the policy into the physical simulator would accumulate errors? I would have thought the forward dynamics already enforce physical constraints so that no physical implausibility would result, but I may be missing something. I do appreciate, however, that re-optimizing at every timestep allows the use of a larger timestep.

Line 153: “similar to recent approaches...” Could you provide one or two references? It is counterintuitive to me that keeping updates small in parameter space would generally be advisable, although I understand why it is useful in your setting with block-alternating subproblems.

Typo: line 89: “to unify”.

Overall, the results are quite impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation appear sensible. However, this research area is slightly outside my focus, so it is difficult for me to be confident about the novelty and significance.
