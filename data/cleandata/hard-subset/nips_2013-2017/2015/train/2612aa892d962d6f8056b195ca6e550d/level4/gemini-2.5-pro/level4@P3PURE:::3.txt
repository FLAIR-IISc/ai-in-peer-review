This paper proposes an algorithm for real-time control of 3D characters, where a neural network generates animations by mimicking the output of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) planner. A key contribution is that the CIO planner and the neural network are not trained independently. Instead, they are trained jointly in an alternating fashion, where the output of each component guides the optimization of the other. Another core element is the use of noise injection, both through data augmentation with small input perturbations and via additive noise in the network's hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that the resulting network can produce realistic and stable control policies for a diverse range of character models.

The proposed approach is compelling, as real-time control of arbitrary character models is a challenging problem. While the core idea of training a network to predict a planner's output may seem straightforward, the novel additions of joint training and noise injection are significant contributions. As the experiments show, these additions are crucial for achieving good generalization.

The experimental results, however, would be more convincing if they were based on a larger training dataset. Given that the training trajectories were generated from a relatively small set of "between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments," it is not surprising that the "no noise" ablation overfits. It is also plausible that the "no joint" ablation would perform better with more diverse training data, though this connection is less direct.

A major weakness of the paper is the limited comparative evaluation, a point the authors acknowledge in the introduction ("A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The only comparison provided is a brief one against an MPC-based method at the end of the paper, for which no citation is given.

The mathematical formulation is generally clear and well-motivated. However, reproducing the results would require consulting additional references, as some components are described superficially. For instance, the notations in the CIO step (Eq. 6) are not fully defined. I also found Section 3.2, which details the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, difficult to follow. The notation becomes confusing, particularly with the omission of the time index 't', making it unclear if it is still implied for certain quantities. The community would greatly benefit from the release of the source code.

Finally, the paper would be strengthened by reporting computation times for the real-time control phase. Understanding how these computations scale with model complexity is critical, as applications like video games have tight CPU budgets for character animation.

**Additional Remarks:**
*   In the introduction, "biped location" should likely be "biped locomotion".
*   In the introduction, "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be "unite" or "unify".
*   Is the control target solely the character's final (x, y, z) position? There appears to be no constraint on the final orientation. If so, could an orientation target be easily incorporated? This seems important for practical applications.
*   The term "noise injection" for the data generation process seems imprecise. Since the target actions are re-computed for the perturbed inputs, this process is more accurately described as "data augmentation" and is distinct from the concept behind denoising autoencoders.
*   In Algorithm 1, are the initial random network weights (theta) used in the very first iteration to compute the first set of trajectories (X)? Or are the initial trajectories computed without the network regularization term?
*   What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter (eta) for both optimization steps in Algorithm 1?
*   In Section 5, "it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have" appears to be missing "the".
*   The acronym LQG is used one line before it is defined.
*   In Section 3.2, "While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass" seems to be missing "a".
*   In line 231, the star in the argmin operator appears to be misplaced. Also, is \bar{s} missing from the cost function \tilde{C}?
*   In Section 3.2, the phrase "the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory" seems incomplete.
*   The paper states that "sX and aX are subsets of \phiX". Why is this guaranteed?
*   In Section 4, the text mentions "a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)". This should likely refer to the objective from Algorithm 1, as only theta is being optimized in that step.
*   The asynchronous joint optimization scheme described in Section 6 is very different from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1. It would be beneficial to mention this distinction earlier in the paper.
*   In line 315, should 'f' be 'f^{des}'?
*   The sentence "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation" was unclear.
*   The experimental "no noise" ablation could be more informative if split into two separate variants: "no data augmentation" and "no hidden layer noise".
*   An interesting question for future work is whether alpha can be annealed to zero during CIO. This would help determine if the network regularization term primarily acts as a curriculum (making the initial task easier) or if it is always beneficial to keep the CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent.
*   The caption for Table 1a states the network has 4 layers, but the text seems to describe a 3-layer network.

In summary, this paper presents a promising application of neural networks to character control, but further work is needed to firmly establish its practical benefits and applicability.
