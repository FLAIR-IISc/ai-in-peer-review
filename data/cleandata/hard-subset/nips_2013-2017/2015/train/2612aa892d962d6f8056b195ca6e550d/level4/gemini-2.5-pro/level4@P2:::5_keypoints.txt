- Presents a method to train a single recurrent neural network as a real-time feedback controller for diverse, complex simulated characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without motion-capture or task-specific engineering.  
- Uses an alternating, iterative optimization that interleaves offline trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of the policy.  
- The policy is fed back into the trajectory optimizer so joint optimization discovers stable, cyclic, and predictable behaviors.  
- Introduces a principled noise-injection scheme during training to improve robustness to perturbations.  
- Proposes a novel technique to compute optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer and use them to guide network training on perturbed states.  
- Successfully extends prior trajectory-optimization + function-approximation approaches from 2D to challenging, unstable, high-dimensional 3D locomotion.  
- Results are impressive: stable, realistic, interactive locomotion across multiple morphologies shown in supplementary video.  
- Provides a compelling alternative to hand-engineered controllers and model-free RL, producing more natural behaviors.  
- Ablation studies strongly support the importance of joint optimization and noise injection.  
- Core idea integration is novel even if constituent methods are known; technical contributions are clever and original.  
- Major clarity issues: the paper does not explicitly state the critical assumption of a perfect dynamics/contact model required by CIO.  
- Major clarity issues: unclear how the recurrent state is trained—Section 4 reads like i.i.d. regression and omits whether the RNN is unrolled or how temporal dependencies are handled.  
- Organization issues: core learning method (Section 4) is underdeveloped while implementation/distributed training details get comparable space.  
- Terminology issue: calling the method a “stochastic neural network” is misleading—network is deterministic but trained with stochastic regularization; language should be precise.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant advance, general and powerful method, novel joint optimization and feedback-gain techniques, and thorough experimental validation.  
- Arguments for rejection: clarity problems obscuring critical methodological details, unacknowledged reliance on a perfect model, and uneven paper organization; authors should revise to clarify and restructure.
