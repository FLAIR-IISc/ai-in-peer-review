This paper proposes integrating trajectory optimization with policy learning to enable interactive control of complex characters. The core idea is to learn trajectories and the policy jointly, with the empirical observation that splitting the problem into alternating subproblems i) allows reuse of established, well-documented methods and ii) performs adequately in practice. While I could follow the paper well, I am not sufficiently embedded in this area to assess its potential impact; on the surface it appears a technical achievement without new theoretical insights.

The authors exhibit notable technical sophistication throughout—from conceptual design to implementation—backed by strong results. The claimed benefits of i) injecting sensory noise during training and ii) jointly learning trajectories with the policy are convincingly supported.

The paper is clearly written, with what seems an appropriate level of detail given the problem’s complexity and implementation demands. Each section addresses a distinct facet of the problem, and the cross-references are helpful.

I think the motivation for the additional optimization step in Sec. 7 could be clarified. At that point, based on earlier sections, I expected the policy to run without further optimization. Is the extra step needed because there remains a residual trajectory cost (or policy regression error) such that directly feeding the policy’s action into the simulator would lead to accumulating errors? I would imagine the forward dynamics already enforce physical constraints to avoid implausible behavior, but I may be missing something. I do appreciate that re-optimizing at every timestep enables the use of larger timesteps.

l153: “similar to recent approaches...” — could you provide one or two references? It is initially counterintuitive that keeping updates small in parameter space is generally desirable, though I see why it is beneficial in your block-alternating setup.
