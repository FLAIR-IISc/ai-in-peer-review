In this submission, the authors present an algorithm for real-time control of 3D character models in which a neural network (NN), trained offline to mimic the outputs of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), generates animations. Crucially, CIO and NN training are interdependent and alternate: each stage uses the other’s outputs within its optimization objective. Another key component is noise: both data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise injected into the NN’s hidden layers. The experiments indicate that the network can produce realistic and stable control policies across a wide range of character models.

This is a compelling approach, given the difficulty of real-time control for arbitrary models. Although the basic idea may sound simple (“train an NN to predict CIO outputs”), the additional mechanisms needed to make it succeed—joint training and noise injection—are substantive contributions and are shown empirically to significantly improve generalization.

That said, the experimental results would be more convincing with a larger dataset. Since the base training trajectories were generated from only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” it is not surprising that the “no noise” variant overfits. The “no joint” variant might also benefit from more diverse training trajectories, although this is less certain.

Overall, the comparative evaluation is the weakest part of the paper (as acknowledged in the introduction: “A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with MPC at the end of the paper, and there is no reference for this method.

For the most part, the mathematics are clearly explained and well motivated, but a reader attempting to replicate the results will need to consult additional references, as some aspects are treated only superficially (especially the CIO step—see Eq. 6, whose notation is not defined). Providing code would be very helpful to the community. I was also unable to fully parse Section 3.2 on generating optimal actions for noisy inputs: the notation becomes confusing, the time index t is dropped, and it is unclear which quantities retain a time dependence and what exactly is being computed.

It would also be useful to report timing measurements for real-time control and how they scale with 3D model complexity, since this could be a critical bottleneck. In a video game setting, there is limited CPU available to animate a single on-screen character.

Additional minor remarks:
- “biped location”: did you mean locomotion?
- “It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches”: unite? unify?
- Am I correct that the target is only x, y, z position, with no constraint on the character’s facing angle upon arrival? If so, would it be straightforward to add a target orientation (which seems important in practice)?
- I would avoid calling “noise injection” the step where extra data are generated from noisy inputs, since the target is recomputed to match the modified inputs. That is standard data augmentation and is unrelated to the idea behind denoising autoencoders.
- In the first iteration of Alg. 1, are you really using the initial (random) network weights θ to compute the first X’s, or do you start with X’s computed without the regularization term?
- What is the rationale for using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Alg. 1?
- “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have”: missing “the”?
- The acronym LQG is used one line before it is defined.
- “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass”: missing “a”?
- l. 231: the star in the argmin is in the wrong place, and is \bar{s} missing in \tilde{C}?
- “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory”: missing a word?
- “sX and aX are subsets of φX”: why is that guaranteed?
- “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)”: this seems to refer instead to the objective from Alg. 1, since only θ is optimized.
- The asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 is quite different from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1; it would be helpful to flag this earlier.
- l. 315, should f be f^{des}?
- “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.”: I did not understand this sentence.
- The “no noise” variant in experiments could be split into “no data augmentation” versus “no hidden-layer noise injection.”
- It would be interesting to test whether α can be annealed all the way to zero during optimization (for CIO only). Is performance driven mainly by a curriculum-learning effect (an initially easier task), or is it always beneficial to keep CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent?
- The caption of Table 1a says 4 layers, but I believe it is 3.

A strong and visually compelling application of neural networks to character control, whose practical benefits and broader applicability still need to be established.
