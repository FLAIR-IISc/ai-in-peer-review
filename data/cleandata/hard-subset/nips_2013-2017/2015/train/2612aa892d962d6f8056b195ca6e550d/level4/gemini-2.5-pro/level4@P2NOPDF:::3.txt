This paper presents a novel algorithm for the real-time control of 3D animated characters. The core of the method is a neural network (NN) trained offline to mimic the output of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) planner, enabling real-time animation generation. A key contribution is the joint, alternating optimization scheme where the CIO and NN training are coupled: the output of each process informs the optimization criterion of the other. The authors also introduce two forms of noise injection—data augmentation via input perturbations and additive noise in the network's hidden layers—to improve the robustness and generalization of the learned control policy. The experimental results demonstrate that the resulting network can generate realistic and stable control policies for a diverse set of character models.

The proposed approach is compelling, as it addresses the significant challenge of real-time control for arbitrary character models. While the core idea of training a neural network to approximate a complex planner might seem straightforward, the paper's main contributions lie in the novel techniques that make this approach viable. Specifically, the joint training scheme and the systematic use of noise injection are significant innovations, and the experiments convincingly show their necessity for achieving strong generalization.

However, the experimental validation could be strengthened. The training set, generated from only 100-200 trials with 5 branched segments each, seems relatively small. This limited data diversity may explain why the "no noise" ablation study resulted in overfitting. It is also plausible that the "no joint" variant's performance could be improved with a more varied set of training trajectories.

A more significant weakness is the limited comparative evaluation against other state-of-the-art methods. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the introduction ("A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The only comparison provided is a brief one against Model Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper, which unfortunately lacks a specific citation.

The mathematical formulation is generally clear and well-motivated. However, for full reproducibility, readers will likely need to consult external references, as some components, particularly the CIO step, are described at a high level. For instance, the notations in Equation 6 are not fully defined. The inclusion of source code would be a valuable contribution to the community and would greatly aid in the replication of these results. I found Section 3.2, which details the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, particularly difficult to follow. The notation becomes confusing, and the omission of the time index 't' makes it unclear whether certain quantities are still time-dependent.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a discussion of computational performance. Reporting inference times for real-time control and analyzing how these times scale with model complexity would be highly informative. In practical applications like video games, the CPU budget for a single character's animation is often constrained.

**Additional Points and Questions:**

1.  There appear to be minor typos: "biped location" should likely be "biped locomotion," and "unity these disparate approaches" should be "unite" or "unify."
2.  Is the control target solely the character's (x, y, z) position, with no constraint on the final facing angle? If so, how difficult would it be to incorporate a target orientation, which is often important in practice?
3.  The term "noise injection" for generating additional data from noisy inputs seems imprecise. Since the target is re-computed to match the modified inputs, this process is more accurately described as "data augmentation" and is distinct from the concept behind denoising autoencoders.
4.  In the first iteration of Algorithm 1, are the initial (random) network weights `theta` used to compute the first set of trajectories `X`, or are these initial trajectories computed without the network regularization term?
5.  What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter `eta` for both the CIO and the network training steps in Algorithm 1?
6.  There are a few grammatical errors: "it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have" appears to be missing "the." "While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass" seems to be missing "a."
7.  The acronym LQG is used one line before it is defined.
8.  In line 231, the placement of the star in the argmin seems incorrect. Also, is `\bar{s}` missing from `\tilde{C}`?
9.  The phrase "the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory" appears to be missing a word.
10. Why are `sX` and `aX` guaranteed to be subsets of `\phiX`?
11. The statement "a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)" could be clarified. It seems to refer to the objective from Algorithm 1, as only `theta` is being optimized.
12. The asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 is quite different from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1. It would be helpful to mention this discrepancy earlier.
13. In line 315, should `f` be `f^{des}`?
14. The sentence "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation" is unclear.
15. The "no noise" experiment could be more informative if split into two separate ablations: one for "no data augmentation" and another for "no hidden layer noise."
16. An interesting question for future work is whether the regularization parameter `alpha` can be annealed to zero during optimization (for the CIO step). This would help determine if the joint training provides a continuous benefit or mainly serves as a form of curriculum learning.
17. The caption for Table 1a states the network has 4 layers, but the architecture described appears to have 3 hidden layers plus an output layer. Please clarify.

In summary, this paper presents a visually impressive application of neural networks for character control. The method is novel and promising, though further work is needed to fully establish its practical benefits, robustness, and comparative performance against other state-of-the-art techniques.
