This paper proposes an algorithm for the real-time control of 3D models, in which a neural network (NN) is trained offline to generate animations by reproducing the output of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) routine. Crucially, the CIO and NN training are not performed independently but are instead interlinked in an alternating optimization scheme, where the output from each stage informs the objective function of the other. Another key component of the proposed method is noise injection, implemented both through data augmentation with small input perturbations and through the addition of noise to the NN's hidden layers. The experiments demonstrate that this approach can produce realistic and stable control policies for a diverse set of character models.

The proposed approach is compelling, as real-time control of arbitrary character models remains a challenging problem. While the core idea of training an NN to mimic a CIO controller might seem straightforward, the additional components—namely, the joint training scheme and noise injection—are significant contributions. The experiments effectively demonstrate that these additions lead to substantially better generalization.

The experimental results would be more convincing, however, if they were based on a larger training dataset. The training set, generated from only 100 to 200 trials with 5 branched segments each, appears quite limited. On such a small dataset, it is not surprising that the "no noise" ablation model overfits. The "no joint" training variant would likely also benefit from more varied training trajectories, although the impact might be less pronounced.

A significant weakness of the paper is its limited comparative evaluation, a point the authors acknowledge in the introduction: "A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done." The sole comparison to a competing approach is a brief discussion of Model Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper, for which no citation is provided.

The mathematical formulations are generally well-explained and motivated. However, replicating the results would require consulting external references, as some components are described superficially. This is particularly true for the CIO step; for instance, the notations in Equation 6 are not defined. The inclusion of source code would be a valuable contribution to the community and would greatly aid in reproducibility. I found Section 3.2, which details the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, difficult to understand. The notation becomes confusing, and the procedure is unclear. For example, the time index 't' is dropped, and it is not specified whether it is still implied for certain quantities.

It would be beneficial to include timing measurements for the real-time control computations, as inference time can be a significant bottleneck. Information on how these computation times scale with the complexity of the 3D model would also be valuable. In practical applications like video games, the computational budget for animating a single character is often limited.

Additional minor remarks:
*   In the phrase "biped location," did the authors mean "locomotion"?
*   In "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches," should "unity" be "unite" or "unify"?
*   It appears the target is defined only by (x, y, z) coordinates, without a constraint on the character's final orientation. Is this correct? If so, how difficult would it be to incorporate a target angle, which would be important for many practical applications?
*   The term "noise injection" for the process of generating new data from perturbed inputs seems imprecise. Since the target outputs are re-computed for these new inputs, this process is more accurately described as "data augmentation." It is distinct from the concept of denoising autoencoders, where the goal is to reconstruct the original, clean input.
*   In Algorithm 1, is the first set of trajectories (X) generated using the randomly initialized network weights (theta), or are they computed initially without the network-based regularization term?
*   What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter (eta) for both optimization steps in Algorithm 1?
*   In "...stochastic setting we have," is the article "the" missing?
*   The acronym LQG is used one line before its definition is provided.
*   In "While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass," is the article "a" missing?
*   In line 231, the star in the argmin operator appears misplaced. Additionally, is the term \bar{s} missing from \tilde{C}?
*   The phrase "the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory" seems incomplete.
*   What guarantees that sX and aX are subsets of \phiX?
*   The text mentions reducing the objective in (4), but since only theta is being optimized in this step, it seems the objective from Algorithm 1 would be more accurate.
*   The asynchronous joint optimization scheme described in Section 6 differs significantly from the alternating scheme presented in Algorithm 1. It would be beneficial to mention this distinction earlier in the paper.
*   In line 315, should 'f' be 'f^{des}'?
*   The sentence "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation" is unclear. Please clarify its meaning.
*   For a more thorough ablation study, the "no noise" experimental variant could be separated into two distinct cases: "no data augmentation" and "no hidden layer noise injection."
*   An interesting investigation would be to see if the hyperparameter alpha can be annealed to zero during optimization. This would help determine if the joint training acts primarily as a curriculum (making the initial optimization easier) or if the network regularization term remains beneficial throughout the entire process.
*   The caption of Table 1a states the network has 4 layers, but it appears to have 3. Please clarify.

This paper presents a promising application of neural networks to character control. However, further work is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and wider applicability.
