This submission presents an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, leveraging a neural network (NN) trained offline to mimic the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key aspect of the approach is the alternating training of CIO and the NN, where each informs the optimization criterion of the other. The method also incorporates noise injection through data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise within the NN’s hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate the network’s ability to generate realistic and stable control policies across diverse character models.

This is a compelling approach to the challenging problem of real-time control of arbitrary models. While the core idea – learning an NN to predict CIO output – appears relatively straightforward, the joint training and noise injection strategies represent significant contributions, demonstrably improving generalization in the presented experiments. 

However, the strength of the experimental results would be enhanced by comparison to a larger dataset. Given that the base training trajectories consist of only “between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments,” overfitting in the “no noise” variant is unsurprising. The “no joint” variant could also benefit from more varied training data, although this is less apparent.

The comparative evaluation constitutes the primary weakness of the paper, a limitation acknowledged by the authors (“A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done”). The comparison to a competing method is limited to a brief evaluation against MPC, lacking a formal reference.

For the most part, the mathematical derivations are clearly explained and motivated. However, replicating these results would require consulting additional references, as some points are treated superficially, particularly the CIO step (e.g., Eq. 6, where notations are undefined). Providing code would be highly valuable to the research community. Section 3.2, concerning the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, proved difficult to follow due to confusing notation and the unexplained omission of the time index “t” from certain quantities.

Including time measurements for real-time control computations would be beneficial, as this could represent a significant bottleneck, especially considering the computational demands of animating 3D models within resource-constrained environments like video games.

Additional minor points: - “biped location” should likely be “locomotion.” - “unity these disparate approaches” should be “unify.” - Is the target limited to x, y, z coordinates, without constraints on the character’s orientation? If so, would adding a target angle be straightforward and practically useful? - The step described as “noise injection” is more accurately characterized as “data augmentation,” as the target is re-computed for modified inputs, differing from the principle of denoising autoencoders. - Are the initial (random) network weights θ used in the first iteration to compute the initial X’s in Alg. 1, or are X’s initialized without the regularization term? - What is the rationale for using the same hyperparameter η in both steps of Alg. 1? - “it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have” should be “the asynchronous and stochastic setting we have.” - The acronym LQG is used before its definition. - “While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass” should be “a Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass.” - l. 231, the star in the argmin is misplaced, and \bar{s} is missing in \tilde{C}? - “the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory” is missing a word. - “sX and aX are subsets of \phiX”: what guarantees this? - “a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)” should refer to the objective from Alg. 1, as only θ is optimized. - The asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 differs significantly from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1; this distinction should be highlighted earlier. - l. 315, f should be f^{des}? - “In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation.” is unclear. - The “no noise” variant in experiments could be separated into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise injection.” - Investigating whether α can be reduced to zero during optimization (CIO only) would be insightful. Does the method’s success stem from a “curriculum learning” effect (easier initial task) or is maintaining trajectories close to those learned by CIO consistently beneficial? - The caption of Table 1a states 4 layers, but the network appears to have 3.

Overall, this is a promising application of neural networks to character control, but further investigation is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and applicability.
