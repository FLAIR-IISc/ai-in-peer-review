"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates how a small, fast neural network can represent a control policy for interactive character control within a physics simulator. The approach leverages trajectory optimization during training to learn this neural network policy. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that a single network architecture can effectively handle a variety of more-or-less cyclic behaviors across diverse body types.

**General Comments**

My assessment of this paper is that it represents an application of deep learning to established (though recent) techniques within computer graphics. The efficacy of trajectory optimization in generating realistic control signals is well-documented in prior work, some of which the authors cite. Furthermore, approximating the step-by-step actions required for a policy—to reproduce trajectories similar to those found via trajectory optimization—using neural networks has also been explored previously (e.g., [9]). Therefore, the authors need to more thoroughly contextualize their work relative to these pre-existing approaches. 

The recent work by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine feels particularly relevant. How do the authors compare their method to this line of research? Is the goal faster learning, support for a broader range of body shapes, reduced runtime computation, or increased robustness to environmental variability? Clarifying this positioning is crucial.

**Specific Comments**

What is meant by the statement "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains"? 

Consider postponing the discussion of why LQG was not used until *after* the proposed strategy has been fully presented. 

The notation around lines 226-240 is unclear. You introduce s(X) = \bar s, and then define a cost function in terms of s(X) - \bar s. The bar and squiggle notations are visually similar and create ambiguity.

What does it mean for “our approach [to] produce softer feedback gains according to parameter λ”? Softer than what baseline? What is the distinction between “soft” and “not soft” gains? Furthermore, why is modifying the initial state beneficial within the proposed procedure? This is sound work, but the contribution relative to other recent, related publications remains insufficiently clear.
