This submission presents an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models leveraging a neural network (NN) trained offline to reproduce the outputs of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). A key aspect of the method is the alternating training of CIO and the NN, where each informs the optimization criterion of the other. Furthermore, the authors incorporate noise through data augmentation via small perturbations and additive noise within the NN’s hidden layers. Experimental results demonstrate the network’s ability to generate realistic and stable control policies across diverse character models.

This is a compelling approach to the challenging problem of real-time control of arbitrary models. While the core idea – learning an NN to predict CIO outputs – may seem straightforward, the joint training and noise injection techniques represent significant contributions, demonstrably improving generalization in experiments.

However, the strength of the experimental validation would be enhanced by comparison to a larger dataset. Given that the initial training trajectories consist of only "between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments", overfitting in the "no noise" variant is unsurprising. The "no joint" variant could also benefit from more varied training data, though this is less clear. 

The comparative evaluation is the primary weakness of the paper, a limitation acknowledged by the authors ("A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) is brief, and lacks a formal citation.

The mathematical derivations are generally clear and well-motivated, though complete replication would require consulting additional references, as certain aspects – particularly the CIO step (e.g., Eq. 6, where notations are undefined) – are treated superficially.  Code release would be highly valuable to the community. Section 3.2, concerning the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, proved difficult to follow due to confusing notation and the apparent omission of the time index "t" from some quantities.

Including computation time measurements during real-time control would be beneficial, as this could represent a significant bottleneck, particularly when considering resource constraints in applications like video games. Understanding how performance scales with model complexity is crucial.

Minor comments include: replace "biped location" with "locomotion"; correct "unity" to "unite" or "unify"; clarify whether the target is limited to x,y,z coordinates, and consider the feasibility of adding an angle constraint; reclassify the noisy input data generation as "data augmentation" rather than "noise injection"; clarify the initialization of network weights (theta) in the first iteration of Alg. 1; justify the use of a single hyperparameter (eta) in both steps of Alg. 1; correct the grammatical error ("the") in "it is non-trivial..."; define LQG before its first use; correct the missing article ("a") in "While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass"; fix the argmin notation and missing term in l. 231; clarify the guarantee of "sX and aX are subsets of \phiX"; clarify the objective function in "a single pass over the data..."; highlight the difference between the asynchronous optimization in Section 6 and the alternating scheme in Alg. 1; correct "f^{des}" in l. 315; clarify the meaning of the sentence regarding dynamics constraints in simulation; further delineate the "no noise" variant into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection"; and investigate whether alpha can be reduced to zero during CIO optimization. The caption of Table 1a indicates 4 layers, though the network appears to have 3. 

Overall, this is a promising application of neural networks to character control, though further work is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and broad applicability.
