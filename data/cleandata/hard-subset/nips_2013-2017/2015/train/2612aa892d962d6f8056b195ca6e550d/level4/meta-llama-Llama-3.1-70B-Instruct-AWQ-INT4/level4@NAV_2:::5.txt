This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems and tasks, including swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking with different body morphologies. The authors propose a novel approach that combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, and using the trajectory optimizer to obtain optimal feedback gains in addition to optimal actions.

The main idea of the paper is to design a training method that scales to larger and harder control problems, even if most of the components were already known. The authors achieve this by introducing a neural network control policy that maps a sensory state of the character to an optimal action, and then using a distributed stochastic optimization algorithm to jointly find a collection of optimal trajectories and a policy that can reconstruct the sense and action pairs of all trajectories at all timesteps.

The paper presents several key contributions, including a novel approach to training neural network controllers, a distributed stochastic optimization algorithm, and a method for injecting noise during training to aid in learning policies that do not diverge when rolled out at execution time. The authors also present results on a range of tasks, including swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking, and demonstrate that their method can learn successful controllers for these tasks.

The strengths of the paper include its novelty, clarity, and thoroughness. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach, and the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The results presented in the paper are also impressive, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the authors' approach.

However, there are also some weaknesses to the paper. One major issue is that the paper assumes full model knowledge, which may not always be available in practice. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to other methods, such as model-predictive control (MPC). The paper also mentions that the neural network is stochastic, but it is not clear how the network itself is stochastic, as the training data is stochastic but the network appears to be deterministic.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to training neural network controllers, and the authors' use of distributed stochastic optimization and noise injection during training are both new and interesting contributions. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the authors' approach relates to other work in the field.

In terms of significance, the paper presents results on a range of tasks, including swimming, flying, biped and quadruped walking, and demonstrates that the authors' method can learn successful controllers for these tasks. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential applications and impact of the authors' approach.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to training neural network controllers, and the authors' results demonstrate the effectiveness of their method. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential applications of the authors' approach.
