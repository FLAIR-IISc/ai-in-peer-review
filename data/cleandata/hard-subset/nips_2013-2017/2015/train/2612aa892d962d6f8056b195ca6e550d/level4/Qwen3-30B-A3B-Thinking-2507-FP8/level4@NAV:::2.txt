

"Interactive control of diverse complex characters with neural networks" demonstrates how a compact, efficient neural network can encode a control policy for interactive character manipulation within a physics simulator. The method employs trajectory optimization during training to learn the neural network policy, with the key contribution being the demonstration that this architecture generalizes effectively across various relatively cyclic behaviors and multiple body types.

General Comments ----------------
This paper applies deep learning to established (though recent) computer graphics techniques. The capacity of trajectory optimization to generate realistic control signals is well-documented in prior work, some of which is appropriately cited. Similarly, using neural networks to approximate the step-by-step actions needed for a policy to replicate trajectories identified via trajectory optimization has been explored previously (e.g., [9]). Consequently, the authors must more clearly articulate how their specific contributions advance beyond these existing approaches.

The closest relevant work appears to be the recent research by Pieter Abbeel and Sergey Levine. The authors should explicitly compare their methodology to this line of work: Is this approach designed for faster learning? Broader support for diverse body morphologies? Reduced runtime computational demands? Enhanced robustness to specific environmental variations?

Specific Comments -----------------
Clarify the statement: "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains."
Consider relocating the discussion of why LQG was not used until after presenting your core strategy.
The notation in lines 226-240 is ambiguous: introducing `s(X) = \bar s` followed by a cost defined using `s(X) - \bar s` is visually confusing due to the similarity between bars and squiggles.
Explain what is meant by "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter \lambda." Softer than what? Define the meaning of "soft" versus "non-soft" gains. Justify why altering the initial state is part of your procedure.
Sound work, but the contribution relative to other recent related work lacks sufficient clarity.
