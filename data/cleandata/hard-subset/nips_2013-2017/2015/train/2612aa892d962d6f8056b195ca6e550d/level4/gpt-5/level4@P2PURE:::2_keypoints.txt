- Paper demonstrates a small, fast neural network can represent interactive control policies for diverse simulated characters, learned via trajectory optimization during training.  
- Claimed contribution: same network architecture works across a range of mostly cyclic behaviors and diverse body types.  
- Reviewer views this primarily as applying deep learning to existing graphics/control techniques, not a fundamentally new method.  
- Prior work already showed trajectory optimization can produce realistic control signals; this should be acknowledged more clearly.  
- Previous work has used neural networks to track trajectories from trajectory optimization (e.g., [9]); the paper must distinguish itself from these approaches.  
- The paper should explicitly compare and position itself relative to Abbeel & Levine’s line of work (e.g., learning speed, generality across body shapes, runtime computation, robustness).  
- Clarify what is meant by “very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains.”  
- Recommend moving the discussion of why LQG was not used to after the method presentation.  
- Notation around lines 226–240 is confusing (s(X)=\bar s and cost in terms of s(X)−\bar s); bars and squiggles are easily confused.  
- Define and justify “softer feedback gains according to parameter λ”: softer than what, and what constitutes “soft.”  
- Explain why changing the initial state as part of the procedure is desirable.  
- Overall assessment: solid work but the novelty and contribution relative to recent related work are not made sufficiently clear.
