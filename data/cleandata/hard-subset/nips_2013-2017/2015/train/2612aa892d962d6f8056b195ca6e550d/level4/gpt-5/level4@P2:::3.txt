Summary
This paper proposes a method for learning real-time feedback controllers for diverse 3D characters by alternating trajectory optimization with supervised neural network (NN) training. The approach uses Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate optimal trajectories and feedback gains, and trains a recurrent policy network to imitate the induced state–action mappings. Crucially, the two stages are coupled: trajectory optimization includes a penalty to keep actions close to the current NN policy, while NN training mimics the optimizer’s actions under noisy sensory inputs using first-order corrections derived from local feedback gains. Noise is injected both in the inputs (for data augmentation with action corrections) and within the NN’s hidden layers (to improve robustness). The learned policy is executed online using a short-horizon (T=1) optimization that enforces dynamics while tracking the NN’s desired velocities, memory updates, and contact forces. Results demonstrate visually plausible and stable behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion) using a single architecture and training recipe. The paper positions itself relative to prior work that learns policies from trajectory optimization [e.g., Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov], emphasizing 3D settings and technical contributions such as the perturbation-based computation of feedback gains and an asynchronous distributed training scheme.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and practical integration of trajectory optimization with policy learning; the alternating formulation regularizes both components and improves test-time robustness.
- The stochastic training scheme is well-motivated: input noise with corrected actions (via local gains) and hidden-layer noise both help resist compounding errors in closed-loop rollouts.
- The perturbation method for extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer is novel, computationally efficient, and likely of independent interest.
- Demonstrated generality across multiple morphologies and behaviors with a single network architecture and shared hyperparameters.
- Useful ablations (no joint training; no noise) illustrate the importance of the proposed components.
- The distributed, asynchronous implementation with GPU NN training is a practical contribution.

Weaknesses and questions
- Comparative evaluation is limited. Aside from a brief qualitative comparison to MPC (without a citation), there are no quantitative baselines against contemporary RL/policy optimization (e.g., TRPO) or other direct methods. This makes it hard to assess significance beyond the presented demos.
- The training dataset appears small (100–200 trials with 5 branches each). Overfitting in the “no noise” variant is therefore unsurprising. A larger or more diverse dataset would strengthen comparisons, including for the “no joint” variant.
- Section 3.2 on computing optimal actions for noisy inputs is hard to follow. Notation becomes dense, time indices disappear, and it is unclear which quantities are time-dependent. A clearer derivation and summary (with explicit assumptions and dimensions) would aid reproducibility.
- Some implementation choices lack justification or clarity: use of the same proximal weight η for both subproblems; initialization of the first trajectories (are they optimized without the NN penalty?); whether orientation is part of the task in all experiments; and details of the online T=1 optimization’s computational cost and scaling.
- The asynchronous training architecture in Section 6 differs meaningfully from the clean alternating steps of Algorithm 1. This discrepancy should be acknowledged earlier, with a brief discussion of convergence behavior under asynchrony and stale updates.

Quality
- The approach appears technically sound overall, with thoughtful engineering choices. However, key parts (CIO setup and constraints; Section 3.2 derivation) are only sketched and rely on prior work. Reproducibility would benefit from more explicit definitions, pseudocode, or code release. Quantitative metrics (tracking error, energy, stability margins) would also strengthen claims.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, but several sections (notably 3.2) suffer from overloaded notation and missing definitions (e.g., symbols in Eq. 6). The paper would be improved by reintroducing time indices where relevant, defining all terms near first use, and adding a concise high-level summary of the feedback-gain computation.

Originality
- The method combines known components (CIO, supervised policy learning, noise injection) in a novel and effective way for 3D character control. The feedback-gain extraction for direct trajectory optimizers is a notable contribution.

Significance
- The results are promising and the system appears practical, but broader significance is limited by the sparse comparisons and small training set. Demonstrating advantages over strong baselines and reporting quantitative metrics would elevate impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Elegant and effective integration of optimization and learning
- Robust behaviors across diverse 3D morphologies
- Practical contributions: stochastic training, feedback-gain extraction, distributed setup

Cons:
- Limited quantitative comparisons and baselines
- Clarity issues in derivations and definitions
- Missing runtime and scaling measurements for real-time execution

Requests for clarification and suggestions
- Provide quantitative comparisons to standard MPC (with a citation) and to at least one contemporary policy optimization method. Report metrics like task completion, energy use, and robustness to perturbations.
- Clarify Section 3.2: keep time indices; define all symbols; state the dimensionality of sX, aX, and their relationship to φX; explain the role of λ and how it affects conditioning and stability.
- Detail the initial iteration: are trajectories first optimized without the NN-matching penalty, or with the randomly initialized NN?
- Justify using the same proximal weight η in both subproblems; report sensitivity.
- Report online runtime: per-step time for the T=1 optimization, CPU/GPU usage, and scaling with model complexity and contact count.
- Specify whether orientation is part of the target in all experiments; if not, discuss how to add it.
- Separate the “no noise” ablation into (a) no input-space augmentation and (b) no hidden-layer noise to assess their individual effects.
- Explore whether α can be annealed toward zero during training (curriculum effect vs. persistent regularization to the learnable policy manifold).

Editorial and minor comments
- “biped location” → “biped locomotion”
- “aim to unity” → “aim to unite/unify”
- Use “an asynchronous and stochastic setting”
- Define LQG before first use; “an LQG pass”
- Clarify Eq. 6 symbols; in Sec. 3.2, fix argmin notation (placement of asterisks/bars) and missing terms; “minimizer of a quadratic around the optimal trajectory”
- Explain why sX and aX are subsets of φX
- When optimizing only θ, refer to the objective from Algorithm 1, not Eq. (4)
- Note earlier that Section 6 uses asynchronous updates versus strict alternation
- In Sec. 7, f should be fdes where appropriate
- Clarify “soft dynamics constraints may include some root forces”
- Table 1 caption: number of layers appears inconsistent
- Please add a citation for MPC and related choices of costs

Overall
A compelling demonstration of learning robust, real-time controllers by coupling trajectory optimization and neural networks. The approach is promising and well-motivated, but stronger quantitative comparisons, clearer derivations, and runtime reporting are needed to firmly establish significance and reproducibility.
