This paper presents a method for the interactive control of physically simulated characters using a small, fast neural network to represent the control policy. The core of the approach involves using trajectory optimization in an offline training phase to generate example motions, which are then used to train a neural network policy. The primary contribution is demonstrating that this architecture is effective across a diverse range of characters and more-or-less cyclic behaviors, such as walking, running, and swimming.

### General Comments

My assessment is that this paper describes a successful application of established deep learning and trajectory optimization techniques to the domain of character animation. The results appear to be sound and visually compelling. However, the central weakness of the manuscript is its positioning relative to prior work, which makes the novelty and significance of the contribution difficult to assess.

The two main components of the proposed pipeline are well-established in the literature. The use of trajectory optimization to discover realistic control signals for physics-based characters is a foundational technique in computer graphics, as cited by the authors. Similarly, the use of neural networks to learn a policy that approximates or "distills" the behavior from optimized trajectories has been explored in previous work (e.g., [9] from the paper's citations).

Given this context, the paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors more clearly articulated what makes their specific approach novel. Is the contribution a particular formulation of the optimization, a new network architecture, or a demonstration of scalability and generality that surpasses previous methods?

To make the contribution clearer, a direct comparison to closely related contemporary work is essential. For instance, the line of research by Levine, Abbeel, and their collaborators explores similar problems of learning control policies for complex motor skills. A discussion comparing the proposed method to theirs would be highly beneficial. Such a comparison could address several key questions:
*   **Sample Efficiency/Training Time:** Does the proposed method learn policies faster or require less offline optimization time?
*   **Generality:** Does this approach support a wider variety of character morphologies or skill types than previous work?
*   **Runtime Performance:** Does the resulting policy require less computation at runtime, making it more suitable for real-time applications like games?
*   **Robustness:** How does the policy's robustness to environmental perturbations or unmodeled dynamics compare to other methods?

Without this context and comparison, the work reads as a successful but incremental application of existing ideas. Clarifying these points would help readers understand the specific advance this paper provides to the field.

### Specific Comments and Questions for Clarification

1.  On line 191, the paper states, "we found the resulting process can produce very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains." Could you provide a more detailed explanation of what is meant by "ill-conditioned" in this context and why large feedback gains are problematic for the target application?

2.  The discussion of why Linear-Quadratic-Gaussian (LQG) control was not used (lines 190-192) feels premature. The paper's narrative might flow more logically if this justification were moved to a later section, perhaps after the proposed methodology has been fully presented.

3.  The notation used in the cost function description (around lines 226-240) is potentially confusing. The paper introduces `s(X) = \bar{s}` to define a target state and then defines a cost term based on `s(X) - \bar{s}`. The visual similarity between the variables (e.g., bars and squiggles) can make the equations difficult to parse. Using more distinct notation could improve clarity.

4.  On line 238, you state that the approach "produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ." This statement requires further clarification. Softer than what? What is the baseline for comparison? Could you elaborate on the technical meaning of "soft" versus "hard" gains in this context and explain why softer gains are advantageous?

5.  The procedure involves modifying the initial state as part of the optimization. What is the motivation for this step? Clarifying why this is a beneficial or necessary part of the process would be helpful.

In summary, this paper presents sound technical work with impressive results. However, its primary weakness is the lack of a clear articulation of its novel contribution in the context of a rapidly advancing field. By explicitly comparing the method against recent related work and clarifying the specific points listed above, the authors could significantly strengthen the manuscript.
