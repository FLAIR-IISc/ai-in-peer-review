This paper proposes a novel approach to interactive control of complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with policy learning. The core contribution is the joint learning of trajectories and policies, with the additional empirical insight that decoupling these two optimization problems into alternating sub-problems offers two advantages: i) it enables the reuse of existing, well-documented methods, and ii) it is effective in practice. Although I was able to follow the paper, I am not sufficiently familiar with this domain to comment on its potential impact, which, at first glance, appears to be a technical achievement without novel theoretical insights.

The authors demonstrate a high level of technical sophistication, from the underlying concepts to their implementation, and achieve equally impressive results. The paper clearly demonstrates the claimed advantages of i) incorporating sensory noise during training and ii) learning trajectories jointly with the policy. 

I found the paper to be well-written, with an appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the problem and its implementation. Each section addresses a separate aspect of the problem, and the cross-references between sections are very useful.

I think the motivations for the extra optimization step in Section 7 could be better explained. At this stage, given what had been established in previous sections, I was expecting the policy to run without the need for additional optimization. Is this because there is always a residual trajectory cost (or policy regression error) that accumulates errors when feeding the action given by the policy into the physical simulator? I would imagine that the forward evolution of the dynamics takes into account physical constraints, ensuring that no physical implausibility can result. However, I may be missing something. I appreciate that re-optimizing at every time step allows for the use of larger time steps.

On line 153, the authors mention that their approach is similar to recent methods. It would be helpful to provide one or two references to support this claim. I find it counterintuitive that keeping updates small in parameter space is a sensible approach in general, although I ultimately understood its utility in the context of this work, where block-alternating between sub-problems is employed.

There is a typo on line 89: "to unify" should be corrected.

The paper's results appear impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation seem sensible. However, this research area is slightly outside my expertise, making it difficult for me to confidently assess the novelty and significance of this work.
