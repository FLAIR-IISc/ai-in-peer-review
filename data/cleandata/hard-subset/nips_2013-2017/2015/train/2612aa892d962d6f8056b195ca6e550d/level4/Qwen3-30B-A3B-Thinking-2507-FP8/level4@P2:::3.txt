

This paper introduces a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse 3D characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion. The key innovation lies in the joint optimization framework where neural network training and Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) are performed in an alternating fashion, with noise injection through both data augmentation and hidden layer perturbations. The method successfully generates stable, realistic behaviors without requiring motion capture or task-specific features, representing a significant step toward unified character control.

The paper's strengths are evident in its clear motivation, well-structured methodology, and compelling empirical results. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach outperforms naive neural network training on static trajectories (by two orders of magnitude) and that noise injection is crucial for preventing overfitting. The method's ability to generate stable, purposeful behaviors across multiple character types is particularly impressive, addressing a key challenge in the field.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The comparative evaluation is the most significant concern. As acknowledged by the authors, "a systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done." The only comparison provided is with model predictive control (MPC) at the end of the paper, which is insufficient for establishing the method's relative merits. The authors should provide more comprehensive comparisons with other neural network control approaches from the literature, particularly those in [3, 6, 14].

The training dataset size is also problematic. With only "100-200 trials, each with 5 branched segments," it's unsurprising that the "no noise" variant overfits. A larger dataset would strengthen the claims about the method's generalization capabilities. The ablation study could also be improved by separating the "no noise" variant into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection" to better isolate the contributions of each component.

The mathematical exposition contains some confusing elements. Section 3.2 on optimal feedback gains is particularly challenging due to inconsistent notation (the time index "t" is dropped without explanation). The authors should clarify the relationship between the time index and the quantities being discussed. Additionally, the paper would benefit from providing time measurements for real-time execution to address computational feasibility concerns in applications like video games.

Several minor issues should be addressed:
- "biped location" should be corrected to "biped locomotion"
- "unity" should be changed to "unify" or "unite"
- The target specification (only x,y,z coordinates without orientation constraints) should be clarified
- The term "noise injection" is misleading; this is essentially data augmentation
- The justification for using the same hyperparameter η in both optimization steps needs clarification
- The asynchronous optimization scheme in Section 6 should be better connected to the alternating optimization in Algorithm 1

The paper's significance is high, as it addresses a major challenge in character animation and robotics: creating controllers that work across diverse character morphologies without extensive manual tuning. The method's potential applications in animation, robotics, and biomechanics are substantial.

Overall, this is an interesting and promising approach that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, particularly a more comprehensive evaluation and clearer mathematical exposition, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS. The current version is acceptable for publication, but the authors should address the weaknesses noted above to strengthen their claims.
