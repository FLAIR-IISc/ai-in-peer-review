This paper proposes a method for real-time control of 3D animated characters. The core idea is to train a neural network (NN) to approximate the output of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) planner. A key contribution is that the CIO planning and NN training are not performed sequentially but are instead interleaved in an alternating optimization scheme, where the output of each process informs the objective of the other. Another central element of the proposed method is the strategic injection of noise, both through data augmentation on the input and via additive noise in the network's hidden layers. The authors demonstrate that this approach can produce realistic and stable control policies across a diverse set of character models.

The proposed approach is compelling, as it addresses the challenging problem of real-time control for physically simulated characters. While the basic concept of training a network to imitate a powerful but slow planner like CIO might seem straightforward, the paper's main contributions—the joint optimization framework and the systematic use of noise—are significant and non-trivial. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that these additions are crucial for achieving robust generalization, moving the method beyond simple behavioral cloning.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation. The ablation studies, while insightful, are potentially undermined by the limited size of the training data, which consists of only 100-200 trials. With such a small dataset, it is not surprising that the "no noise" variant overfits, making it difficult to fully assess the contribution of the noise injection versus simply needing more data. Similarly, the "no joint" variant might also perform better with more diverse training trajectories. More significantly, the paper lacks a systematic comparison to competing methods, a point the authors acknowledge in the introduction. The only comparison provided is a brief, qualitative one against an unspecified Model Predictive Control (MPC) baseline at the end of the paper, without a reference. A more thorough quantitative and qualitative comparison against established techniques would be necessary to properly situate the performance and advantages of the proposed method.

Regarding clarity, the paper is mostly well-written, and the mathematical formulations are generally well-motivated. However, several sections would benefit from more detail to facilitate reproducibility. For instance, the notation in Equation 6 related to the CIO step is not fully defined within the paper. I also found Section 3.2, which describes the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, particularly difficult to follow due to confusing notation and the unclear role of the time index 't'. Given the complexity of the implementation, providing source code would be a significant benefit to the community.

Furthermore, the paper does not report any computational performance metrics. Information on the real-time inference cost (e.g., CPU/GPU time per frame) and how this cost scales with model complexity is essential for evaluating the method's practicality, especially for target applications like video games where computational budgets for character animation are often tight.

**Additional Questions and Minor Remarks:**

*   **Clarity and Typos:**
    *   "biped location": Did you mean "locomotion"?
    *   "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches": "unite" or "unify"?
    *   "it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have": Missing "the"?
    *   The acronym LQG is used one line before it is defined.
    *   "While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass": Missing "a"?
    *   l. 231: The star in the argmin appears to be misplaced. Also, is \bar{s} missing from \tilde{C}?
    *   "the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory": Seems to be missing a word, perhaps "a" or "the"?
    *   l. 315: Should f be f^{des}?
    *   The sentence "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation" was unclear to me. Could you elaborate on this point?

*   **Questions about the Method:**
    *   The target is described in terms of x,y,z coordinates. Is there any constraint on the character's final orientation? If not, would it be straightforward to add a target angle?
    *   In Algorithm 1, are the initial random network weights (theta) used in the very first iteration to compute the first set of trajectories X? Or do you begin with trajectories computed without the network-based regularization term?
    *   What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter (eta) for both optimization steps in Algorithm 1?
    *   Could you clarify the guarantee that sX and aX are subsets of \phiX?
    *   The asynchronous joint optimization scheme in Section 6 seems quite different from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1. It would be helpful to mention this discrepancy earlier.
    *   The ablation study "no noise" combines two distinct factors. It would be more informative to split this into "no data augmentation" and "no hidden layer noise" to isolate their effects.

*   **Suggestions:**
    *   I would suggest renaming "noise injection" for the data generation step to "data augmentation," as the target outputs are re-computed for the perturbed inputs. This distinguishes it from techniques like denoising autoencoders where the original clean data is the target.
    *   In the description of the optimization step for theta, "a single pass over the data to reduce the objective in (4)" seems to refer to the objective from Algorithm 1, not just Equation 4, since theta is the only variable being optimized.
    *   The caption for Table 1a mentions 4 layers, but the architecture described appears to have 3 hidden layers. Please clarify.
    *   An interesting question for future work could be whether the alpha parameter can be annealed to zero over the course of CIO. This would help determine if the network regularization term is primarily a "curriculum learning" aid or if it is always beneficial to keep the CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent.

**Summary:**

In summary, this paper presents a novel and promising method for real-time character control that cleverly combines trajectory optimization with neural network training. The core ideas of joint optimization and noise injection are significant contributions. However, the paper's claims would be substantially strengthened by a more rigorous experimental evaluation, including comparisons on a larger dataset and against alternative state-of-the-art methods, as well as a more detailed analysis of its practical performance.
