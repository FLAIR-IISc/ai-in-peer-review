This submission proposes an algorithm for real-time control of 3D models, where a neural network (NN) is used to generate animations after being trained offline to reproduce the output of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO). Notably, CIO and NN training are not independent; instead, they are performed in an alternating fashion, with the output of one being used in the criterion optimized by the other. Another core element of the method is the injection of noise, achieved through data augmentation by small perturbations and additive noise in the NN's hidden layers. Experiments demonstrate that such a network can produce realistic and stable control policies on a range of character models.

This approach is particularly interesting, given the challenges of real-time control of arbitrary models. Although the proposed method may initially appear straightforward ("learn a NN to predict the output of CIO"), the additional steps required to make it work (joint training and noise injection) are significant new contributions that have been shown in experiments to improve generalization.

However, the experimental results would be stronger if comparisons were made with a larger dataset. Since the base training trajectories were generated with only "between 100 to 200 trials, each with 5 branched segments," it is not surprising that the "no noise" variant overfits. The "no joint" variant may also benefit from more diverse training trajectories, although this is less obvious.

The comparative evaluation is the weak point of the paper, as acknowledged in the introduction: "A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done." The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one with MPC at the end of the paper, and there is no reference provided for this method.

For the most part, the mathematical explanations are clear and well-motivated, although someone attempting to replicate these results will likely need to consult additional references, as some points are only addressed superficially (especially the CIO step – see Eq. 6, whose notations are not defined). Providing code would be greatly appreciated by the community. One section that was difficult to understand is 3.2, which discusses generating optimal actions for noisy inputs. The notations became confusing, and it was unclear what was being done (in particular, the time index "t" was dropped, but is it still implied for some of the quantities?).

It would have been useful to include time measurements for computations during real-time control, as this could be a major bottleneck (and also to know how these computations scale with the complexity of the 3D model). In an application like a video game, there is limited CPU available to animate a single character on screen.

Additional minor remarks:

* "Biped location" should be clarified as referring to locomotion.
* The phrase "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches" should be corrected to "unite" or "unify."
* The target appears to be only about x, y, z coordinates, with no constraint on the angle the character is facing when reaching it. If this is the case, it would be useful to discuss the possibility of adding an extra target angle, which seems important in practice.
* The term "noise injection" is misleading when referring to the step where additional data is generated from noisy inputs, as the target is re-computed to match the modified inputs. This is more accurately described as "data augmentation," which is unrelated to the concept of denoising autoencoders.
* It is unclear whether the initial (random) network weights theta are used in the very first iteration to compute the first X's in Alg. 1, or if the X's are computed without the regularization term.
* The justification for using the same hyperparameter eta in the two steps of Alg. 1 is not provided.
* There are several minor errors in grammar and punctuation throughout the text, such as missing articles ("the") and incorrect placement of symbols (e.g., the star in the argmin is in the wrong place).
* The asynchronous joint optimization scheme described in Section 6 is different from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1, and it would be worth mentioning this earlier.
* The "no noise" variant in experiments could be split between "no data augmentation" and "no hidden noise injection."
* A question worth investigating is whether alpha can be decreased all the way to zero during optimization (for CIO only). In other words, is the method working mostly due to a "curriculum learning" effect (easier task initially), or is it always useful to keep the trajectories learned by CIO close to what the network can learn?
* The caption of Table 1a incorrectly states that the network has 4 layers, when it actually has 3.

Overall, this is a great-looking application of neural networks to character control, whose practical benefits and applicability still need to be established.
