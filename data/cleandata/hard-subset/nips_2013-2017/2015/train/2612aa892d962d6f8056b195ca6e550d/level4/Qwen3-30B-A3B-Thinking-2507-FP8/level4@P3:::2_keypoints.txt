- Presents a method that trains recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without motion capture or task-specific features.  
- Combines Contact-Invariant trajectory optimization with neural network policy learning, interleaving supervised learning and trajectory optimization with noise injection and unexpected-task training.  
- Core insight: the network simultaneously learns near-optimal controls and a dynamics model, using velocity-based actions rather than direct control policies.  
- Extends prior trajectory-optimization + NN work to 3D locomotion across multiple morphologies with a unified architecture.  
- Technical approach is sound with a well-motivated optimization procedure and thorough experimental validation.  
- Strong experimental results showing stable, realistic behavior across diverse 3D characters.  
- Ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the necessity of joint optimization, noise injection, and training for unexpected changes.  
- Paper does not sufficiently distinguish or quantify improvements over closely related prior work (e.g., Mordatch & Todorov, Levine & Koltun).  
- Lacks systematic comparisons to alternative approaches such as model-predictive control and direct reinforcement learning.  
- Some clarity issues: notation around lines 226–240 and the intuitive explanation of “soft” feedback gains need improvement.  
- Originality: meaningful extension demonstrating a single controller for diverse morphologies, but the novelty relative to prior methods is under-articulated.  
- Significance: strong implications for animation, robotics, and biomechanics, but broader applications and advantages over prior trajectory optimization need stronger emphasis.  
- Strengths: impressive multi-character 3D results, unified architecture, effective ablations, clear core technical presentation, and overall strong experimental validation.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient differentiation from prior work, limited comparisons to alternatives, unclear technical explanations (feedback gains), and limited discussion of computational complexity and broader impact.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify contributions relative to recent work and to include more thorough comparisons to alternative methods.
