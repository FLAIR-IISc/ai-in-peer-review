

The paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse physical characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) without motion capture or task-specific features. The core approach interleaves supervised learning with Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12], injects noise during training, and leverages trajectory optimization to derive optimal feedback gains. This enables interactive real-time control across multiple body morphologies in 3D, a significant extension beyond prior work limited to 2D [9, 11]. The authors demonstrate that their unified architecture—using a neural network to approximate both dynamics and control—achieves stable, realistic behaviors where direct RL methods [3, 6, 10, 14] often yield chaotic outputs. This aligns with recent NIPS work on trajectory optimization for character control [12] but advances the field by solving substantially harder 3D problems and unifying disparate control approaches (e.g., replacing task-specific state machines for bipeds/quadrupeds [13, 18, 21]).

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The method is technically sound, with rigorous ablation studies showing a 100× performance gain over static neural network training (Fig. 2) and robustness to noise injection (Table 1). The trajectory optimization and feedback gain derivation (Sec 3.2) are well-motivated and correctly implemented as a byproduct of CIO.  
*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized, with clear notation (e.g., sensory state $s_t = [q_t, r_t, \dot{q}_{t-1}, f_{t-1}]$) and thorough experimental validation across diverse characters (Fig. 1, Sec 8). The distributed training architecture (Sec 6) is adequately described.  
*Originality:* The combination of CIO with neural network regression for *3D* locomotion—without motion capture or morphology-specific tuning—represents a meaningful advance over prior work [9, 11] (which was limited to 2D). The unification of control strategies across character types is novel.  
*Significance:* The approach enables interactive, real-time control for diverse physical systems, with clear applicability to robotics (Sec 9) and animation. The method’s ability to generalize across body morphologies and handle online goal changes (Sec 5) addresses a key challenge in character control.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Originality:* The contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from [9, 11] in the main text. The authors should explicitly compare their 3D extension to Levine/Abbeel’s work [9, 11]—e.g., "Our method supports 3D locomotion with 100× better test-time performance than static NN training, whereas [9] was limited to 2D and lacked noise injection." The claim that prior work "solved substantially harder problems" requires stronger justification (e.g., quantitative comparison to [9]’s 2D results).  
*Clarity:* The derivation of optimal feedback gains (Sec 3.2) uses inconsistent notation (e.g., $\bar{s}$ vs. $s$ in Eqs. 9–10), which obscures the key insight about soft constraints. The phrase "softer feedback gains" (Sec 3.2) is vague—please clarify *compared to what* and *why* it improves stability.  
*Quality:* The discussion of LQG (Sec 3.2) should precede the explanation of the perturbation method to better contextualize the innovation. The paper states "LQG produces ill-conditioned gains" but does not quantify this or show results.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Solves a hard 3D locomotion problem unaddressed by prior NIPS work [9, 11]; demonstrates broad applicability across character types; strong empirical validation with ablation studies; unifies disparate control paradigms.  
*Con:* Insufficient differentiation from related work [9, 11]; ambiguous notation in feedback gain derivation; vague claims about "softer gains" without comparative evidence.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The technical contribution is solid and addresses a significant gap in character control. With minor revisions to clarify the novelty relative to [9, 11] and improve notation, this paper would make a valuable addition to NIPS proceedings. The results are compelling, and the method’s potential for robotics (Sec 9) elevates its significance beyond animation.
