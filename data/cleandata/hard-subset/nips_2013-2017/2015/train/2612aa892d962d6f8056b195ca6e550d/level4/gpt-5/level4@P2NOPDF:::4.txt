This paper proposes combining trajectory optimization with policy learning to enable interactive control of complex characters. The core idea is to learn trajectories and the control policy jointly, while alternating between the two optimization problems. Empirically, this decoupling lets the authors reuse established, well-documented solvers and appears to work well in practice. The contribution seems technically strong, though I do not see new theoretical insights.

The paper demonstrates a high level of technical sophistication from concepts to implementation, with equally strong empirical results. The advantages of (i) injecting sensory noise during training and (ii) learning trajectories jointly with the policy are convincingly shown. The writing is clear, with an appropriate level of detail given the complexity of the system, and the sections are well scoped with helpful cross-references.

I think the motivation for the additional optimization step in Section 7 could be explained more clearly. By that point, based on the setup in earlier sections, I expected the learned policy to execute without any further optimization. Is the extra step necessary because residual trajectory cost or policy regression error would otherwise accumulate when executing the policy actions in the simulator? One might expect the simulator’s dynamics and constraints to prevent physically implausible behavior; what specific failure mode does per-step re-optimization prevent (e.g., drift, instability, infeasible contacts)? I do appreciate the practical benefit that re-optimizing at every time step may permit larger integration steps.

I had one conceptual concern related to the update strategy. At line 153 you write “similar to recent approaches...”; please cite one or two representative references. Also, the idea of keeping updates small in parameter space is not obviously desirable in general. After reading, I see why it can be useful in your block-alternating scheme—e.g., to maintain consistency between the trajectory and policy fits—but a brief intuition or ablation would help justify this design choice.

Minor comments:
- Typo at line 89: “to unify”.
- Please clarify early on whether the policy is intended for open-loop, closed-loop, or MPC-style execution at test time, and how the Section 7 optimization fits into that taxonomy.
- If available, a short ablation quantifying the contribution of sensory noise and joint learning (versus sequential learning) would further strengthen the empirical case.

Overall, the results are impressive, and the proposed optimization scheme and implementation appear sound. However, this area is slightly outside my core expertise, so I am not fully confident about the novelty and broader significance relative to closely related recent work. On the surface, this reads as a well-executed and impactful engineering contribution without new theoretical development.
