This paper presents a compelling strategy for embedding feedback gains directly within neural network policies, resulting in remarkably robust control performance in the face of disturbances and regression error. The core idea elegantly soft-couples trajectory optimization and policy optimization via a cost penalty for deviation, enabling the policy to learn simplified, yet effective, approximations of optimal trajectories. These learned policies demonstrably outperform traditional Model Predictive Control (MPC)-based approaches in terms of robustness. While the paper leverages sophisticated parallelization techniques to manage the computational demands of optimization, this is presented as an implementation detail facilitating the primary contribution – the learned feedback controllers themselves. The method is then successfully applied to control a diverse range of robotic morphologies and tasks, showcasing its broad applicability.

This is a truly exceptional paper. The work is of the highest technical quality, exceptionally clear in its presentation, and represents a significant achievement in the intersection of graphics, control, and neural networks. The results are not merely promising, but genuinely beautiful in their demonstration of robust and adaptable control across varied robotic systems. The authors have successfully bridged a gap between planning and control, achieving a synergy where the strengths of each approach are maximized. 

The novelty lies in the approach to learning feedback gains *within* the neural network policy itself, rather than treating them as fixed parameters. This allows the policy to learn controllers that are inherently more robust to model inaccuracies and external disturbances – a crucial advantage over traditional MPC, which can be highly sensitive to these factors. The soft coupling approach is also insightful, providing a mechanism for the policy to learn effective approximations without being overly constrained by the potentially complex and brittle trajectories produced by direct optimization. 

The experimental evaluation is particularly strong. Demonstrating the method’s success across a diverse set of robots and tasks convincingly illustrates its generality and practical utility. The visual results are compelling and provide intuitive understanding of the learned behaviors. The clarity of the presentation further enhances the impact of these results.

I found no significant weaknesses in this work. The authors have clearly articulated their approach, provided a thorough experimental evaluation, and demonstrated a substantial contribution to the field. This paper is inspiring and sets a new standard for the development of robust and adaptable robot controllers. I enthusiastically recommend acceptance.
