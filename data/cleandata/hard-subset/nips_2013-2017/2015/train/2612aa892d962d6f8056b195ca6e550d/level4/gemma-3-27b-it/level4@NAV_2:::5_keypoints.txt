- Proposes training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers by distilling Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectories into a real-time policy.  
- Applies the method across diverse dynamical systems (swimming, flying, walking) and varying morphologies, extending prior work to high-dimensional 3D spaces.  
- Key techniques: interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, training for task robustness, and using the optimizer to obtain feedback gains.  
- Bypasses motion capture and task-specific feature engineering, offering a potentially general synthesis method for complex behaviors.  
- Demonstrates a single network architecture controlling varied systems, with realistic, stable behaviors shown in an effective video.  
- Integration of trajectory optimization with neural network training is crucial, enabling learning from a strong offline optimizer.  
- Noise injection and feedback gain computation improve robustness and generalization of the learned policies.  
- Provides a detailed description of the distributed training architecture and computational requirements.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and compare to alternatives like Model Predictive Control, giving a balanced assessment.  
- Weakness: Section 4 omits discussion of the role and contribution of recurrent units, hindering understanding of their benefit.  
- Weakness: Sensory state (st) is described concisely without justification for chosen features.  
- Weakness: Reliance on full model knowledge is noted but its practical implications are underexplored.  
- Weakness: Labeling the network as “stochastic” is misleading since the network is deterministic given inputs; stochasticity mainly arises from data generation.  
- Weakness: Sections 4 and 6 are disproportionately long relative to core method; network architecture and training details are somewhat cursory.  
- Weakness: Lacks thorough ablation studies isolating contributions of individual components beyond Figure 2.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and effective approach, impressive results across tasks/morphologies, unified framework reducing manual design, detailed training/process description, and high potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing recurrent-unit details, limited discussion on full model knowledge, misleading stochastic claim, uneven section emphasis, and insufficient ablation analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to expand recurrent unit discussion, justify sensory-state choices, and clarify what “stochastic” means for the network.
