This paper proposes an algorithm for real-time control of 3D animated characters. The core of the method is a neural network (NN) trained offline to reproduce the output of a Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) planner. A key contribution is that the CIO and NN training are not performed independently but are instead interleaved in an alternating fashion, with the output of each process informing the optimization objective of the other. Another central element is the use of noise, both through data augmentation with small input perturbations and via additive noise in the NN's hidden layers. The experiments demonstrate that this approach can produce realistic and stable control policies for a diverse set of character models.

This is an interesting approach to the challenging problem of real-time control for arbitrary 3D models. While the basic idea of training an NN to mimic a CIO planner may seem straightforward, the additional components—namely, the joint optimization scheme and noise injection—are significant contributions. As the experiments show, these additions are crucial for achieving good generalization.

The experimental results, however, would be more compelling if validated on a larger and more diverse dataset. The base training trajectories were generated from a relatively small set of 100 to 200 trials, each with only five branched segments. With such limited data, it is not surprising that the "no noise" ablation overfits. The "no joint" optimization variant might also see improved performance with more varied training data, though this is less certain.

The paper's primary weakness is its comparative evaluation, a limitation the authors acknowledge in the introduction ("A systematic comparison of these more direct methods with the present trajectory-optimization-based methods remains to be done"). The only comparison to a competing method is a brief one against Model Predictive Control (MPC) at the end of the paper, for which a reference is notably absent.

For the most part, the mathematical framework is clearly explained and motivated. However, replicating these results would likely require consulting additional references, as some components are described only superficially. This is particularly true for the CIO step; for instance, the notations used in Equation 6 are not defined. I found Section 3.2, which describes the generation of optimal actions for noisy inputs, especially difficult to follow. The notation becomes confusing at this point, and the procedure is unclear, partly because the time index 't' is dropped without explanation, leaving it ambiguous whether it is still implied for some quantities. To aid reproducibility, releasing the source code would be a great service to the community.

The paper would also be strengthened by a discussion of computational performance. Reporting measurements of computation time during real-time control, and how this latency scales with the complexity of the 3D model, would be very useful. This is a critical factor for practical applications like video games, where the CPU budget for animating a single character is often highly constrained.

**Additional Minor Remarks:**

*   In "biped location," was "locomotion" intended?
*   In "It is our aim to unity these disparate approaches," "unite" or "unify" would be more appropriate.
*   Is the control target defined solely by XYZ coordinates, with no constraint on the character's final orientation? If so, could an orientation target be easily incorporated? This seems important for many practical scenarios.
*   The term "noise injection" for the data generation process is potentially misleading. Since the target is recomputed to match the perturbed input, this process is more accurately described as "data augmentation" and is distinct from the concept behind denoising autoencoders.
*   Regarding Algorithm 1, is the initial (randomly initialized) network θ used to compute the first set of trajectories X', or are these initial trajectories computed without the regularization term from the network?
*   What is the justification for using the same hyperparameter η for both optimization steps in Algorithm 1?
*   In the phrase "it is non-trivial to adapt them to asynchronous and stochastic setting we have," the article "the" appears to be missing before "asynchronous."
*   The acronym LQG is used one line before it is defined.
*   In "While we can use Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) pass," the article "a" seems to be missing before "pass."
*   In line 231, the star in the argmin operator appears misplaced. Furthermore, is \bar{s} missing as an argument to \tilde{C}?
*   The phrase "the minimizer of a quadratic around optimal trajectory" seems to be missing a word, perhaps "model" or "approximation."
*   Why are sX and aX guaranteed to be subsets of \phiX? An explanation would be helpful.
*   The claim of reducing "the objective in (4)" during the NN update seems inaccurate; this step optimizes the objective from Algorithm 1, where only θ is a variable.
*   The asynchronous joint optimization scheme described in Section 6 is a significant departure from the alternating scheme in Algorithm 1. This difference should be highlighted earlier in the paper.
*   In line 315, should f be f^{des}?
*   The sentence, "In the current work, the dynamics constraints are enforced softly and thus may include some root forces in simulation," is unclear. Please clarify its meaning.
*   For a more thorough ablation study, the "no noise" variant in the experiments could be split into two separate conditions: "no data augmentation" and "no hidden layer noise."
*   An interesting question for future work is whether α can be annealed to zero during the optimization of the CIO-only objective. This would help clarify whether the network-based regularization term acts primarily as a curriculum learning mechanism or if it is always necessary to keep the CIO trajectories close to what the network can represent.
*   The caption of Table 1a states there are 4 layers, but the text seems to describe a 3-layer network. Please clarify this discrepancy.

In summary, this paper presents a visually impressive application of neural networks to character control. However, further work is needed to fully establish its practical benefits and broader applicability.
