- Proposes training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse characters (swimming, flying, biped, quadruped) using supervised learning plus Contact-Invariant Optimization, without motion capture or task-specific state machines.  
- Key contribution: same small, fast neural-network architecture controls a variety of complex body morphologies in an interactive physics simulator.  
- Builds on prior trajectory-optimization + neural-network control work but needs clearer positioning relative to recent lines of work (e.g., Abbeel & Levine).  
- Unclear what the method’s advantages are vs. related work (faster learning, broader morphology support, lower runtime cost, or better robustness?).  
- Paper is generally well-written and explains components clearly.  
- Some notation and terminology are confusing (notably lines 226–240 and the s(X)=\bar s and s(X)-\bar s expressions).  
- The method for generating feedback gains is novel but under-explained—what does “softer feedback gains according to parameter λ” mean and softer than what?  
- Unclear rationale for changing the initial state as part of their procedure and why that’s beneficial.  
- Overall the work is sound but its distinct contribution relative to recent related work is not clearly articulated.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to better position the approach and clarify notation and feedback-gain explanations.  
- Strengths: novel approach, demonstrated effectiveness across tasks and body types, clear exposition.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient comparison to related work, confusing notation/terminology, feedback-gain mechanism needs more explanation.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 7/10, Significance 8/10.
