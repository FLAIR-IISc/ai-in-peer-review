## Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for a variety of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and walking characters with different morphologies. The approach bypasses traditional methods relying on motion capture or state machines, learning directly from trajectory optimization. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12], using the optimizer to generate training data for a neural network that can then execute control online. This work builds upon recent related efforts [9, 11] but tackles more challenging problems – specifically, stable locomotion in three dimensions. The authors highlight the importance of technical improvements, including noise injection and optimized training procedures, to achieve these results.

The paper addresses a significant problem in control – automating the creation of complex, stable, and realistic movements – with potential applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics. It’s a challenging area where reinforcement learning and stochastic optimal control have previously struggled in high-dimensional, continuous spaces. This work demonstrates a compelling approach to bridge that gap by leveraging the power of trajectory optimization to generate effective training data for neural networks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Broad Applicability:** The method successfully controls a diverse range of characters and locomotion types, showcasing its generality and robustness.
*   **Novel Combination:** The combination of trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network learning is a key strength, offering a pathway to both high-quality control and real-time execution.
*   **Technical Contributions:** The improvements discussed – noise injection, stochastic optimization, and efficient feedback gain calculation – appear to be crucial for achieving stable and realistic behaviors, particularly in 3D.
*   **Clear Presentation of Challenges:** The paper clearly articulates the difficulties of learning neural network controllers, including the need for precise outputs, closed-loop operation, and overfitting.
*   **Detailed Implementation:** The authors provide a thorough description of the distributed training architecture and algorithmic details, facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Missing Detail on Recurrent Units:** Section 4 lacks a clear explanation of how the recurrent units are utilized and their role in the overall control architecture, despite being mentioned prominently in the abstract.  It would benefit from a discussion of the memory state 'r' and its impact.
*   **Stochasticity Clarification:** The description of stochasticity (l. 405) is unclear. While the training *data* is stochastic, the network itself appears deterministic during execution. A more precise explanation of how stochasticity influences the learned policy would be helpful.
*   **Model Knowledge Assumption:** The requirement of full model knowledge (Eq. 6) is not explicitly stated upfront, and its implications for real-world applications (where models are often imperfect) could be discussed.
*   **Section Imbalance:** Sections 4 (Neural Network Policy Regression) and 6 (Distributed Training Architecture) are disproportionately detailed compared to other sections, potentially disrupting the flow of the paper. The training architecture details, while thorough, may not be core to the primary contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective approach to automated control of complex characters, demonstrating significant progress in a challenging area.
*   The results are compelling, showcasing stable and realistic locomotion in diverse scenarios.
*   The technical contributions are substantial and well-justified.
*   The level of detail provided allows for a good understanding of the method and potential for reproduction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity regarding the recurrent units and the precise role of stochasticity weakens the presentation.
*   The assumption of full model knowledge may limit the applicability of the method to real-world scenarios.
*   The paper's organization and section balance could be improved for better readability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of character control. While some clarity and organizational improvements would be beneficial, the technical soundness, novelty, and demonstrated results outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the concerns regarding the recurrent units, stochasticity, and model knowledge assumption in a revised version.
