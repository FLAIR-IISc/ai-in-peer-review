- Paper proposes alternating joint training of a neural network and Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to produce real-time 3D character control policies.
- Key technical contributions are the alternating CIO–NN optimization and noise injection (data augmentation + hidden-layer additive noise) to improve generalization.
- Experiments show the network can produce realistic, stable control for diverse character morphologies using a single architecture.
- The joint training and noise techniques are non-trivial improvements beyond simply learning CIO outputs and demonstrably reduce overfitting.
- Comparative evaluation is weak: only a brief, unreferenced comparison to MPC and no broader baselines.
- Training dataset is small (100–200 trials with 5 branched segments), which likely causes overfitting in the “no noise” variant.
- The “no joint” ablation may also suffer from limited trajectory diversity; more varied data could clarify its behavior.
- Mathematical exposition is mostly clear but some parts (notably the CIO step and Eq. 6) are under-specified and require external references to replicate.
- Section 3.2 (optimal actions for noisy inputs) is confusing: notational issues (dropped time index t) obscure what is being computed.
- Algorithmic details are unclear: initial X computation in Alg.1, and justification for using the same hyperparameter η in both steps need clarification.
- The asynchronous joint optimization in Section 6 differs from the alternating scheme in Alg.1 and should be highlighted earlier.
- Timing and computational-scaling measurements for real-time control are missing but necessary for practical applications (e.g., games with limited CPU).
- Code is not provided; releasing code would aid reproducibility and community adoption.
- Terminology: calling recomputed-target data generation “noise injection” is misleading—this is data augmentation, not denoising autoencoder training.
- Suggested experiment: test annealing α to zero during CIO to see if benefits arise from curriculum learning or persistent regularization.
- Experimental variants: split the “no noise” variant into “no data augmentation” and “no hidden-layer noise” for clearer ablation.
- Minor writing/typo issues and missing definitions (e.g., LQG used before definition, several grammatical errors, misplaced symbols in equations, Table 1a caption layer count).
- Clarify modeling choices: whether targets include orientation as well as x,y,z position, and whether root forces arise from soft enforcement of dynamics constraints.
- Provide justification for design choices such as sX and aX being subsets of φX, and correct phrasing about objectives reduced in Alg.1.
- Overall assessment: promising and novel approach with strong potential, but limited validation, clarity issues, and missing implementation/timing details hinder acceptance.
