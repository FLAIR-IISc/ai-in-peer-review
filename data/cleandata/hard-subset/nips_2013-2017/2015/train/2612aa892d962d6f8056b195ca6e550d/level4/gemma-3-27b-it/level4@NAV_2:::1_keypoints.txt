- Method trains recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers producing stable, realistic behaviors for swimming, flying, and bipedal/quadrupedal locomotion across varied morphologies.
- Approach avoids motion capture, task-specific features, and state machines, using a large feed-forward mapping plus a smaller recurrent memory component.
- Network learns dynamics under an implicit policy by predicting velocity; training interleaves supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injects noise, and handles unexpected task changes.
- Trajectory optimizer is also used to extract optimal feedback gains in addition to optimal actions.
- Addresses the challenging problem of automating interactive, real-time controllers in high-dimensional continuous spaces and builds on Contact-Invariant Optimization and related work.
- Key technical advances enable stable 3D locomotion where prior work was largely limited to 2D.
- Paper situates the work within historical neural-network control efforts and highlights unique challenges of controller learning (precise real-valued outputs, closed-loop operation, limited data).
- Strength — Technical soundness: principled combination of trajectory optimization and network training, with noise injection and feedback-gain extraction improving robustness.
- Strength — Clarity: exceptionally well-written, well-organized, with effective mathematical notation and clear explanations.
- Strength — Originality: novel integration of trajectory optimization, neural learning, noise injection, and feedback-gain extraction, applied to diverse 3D characters.
- Strength — Significance: single architecture produces diverse, stable locomotion; potential impact on robotics, animation, gaming, biomechanics, and neuroscience; interactive control is compelling.
- Strength — Evaluation: comprehensive experiments including ablation studies and comparisons to alternatives.
- Weakness — High computational cost: training requires substantial cloud compute and many hours; need discussion of cost-reduction strategies.
- Weakness — Comparisons to direct methods are qualitative/impression-based rather than systematic and quantitative.
- Weakness — Limited task domain: evaluated mainly on locomotion; applicability to manipulation or complex interactions is unexplored.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound approach with significant results, clear presentation, thorough evaluation, and broad impact potential.
- Arguments against acceptance: high training cost, lack of quantitative comparisons to direct methods, and limited task scope.
- Overall: outstanding paper whose strengths far outweigh weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
