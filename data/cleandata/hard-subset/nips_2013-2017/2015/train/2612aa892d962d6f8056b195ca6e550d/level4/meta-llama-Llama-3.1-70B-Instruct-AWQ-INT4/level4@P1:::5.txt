This paper presents a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems and tasks. The authors' approach combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate stable and realistic behaviors. The method does not require motion capture or task-specific features, and the controller is a neural network that learns elaborate state-action mappings.

The paper builds upon previous work in the field, including recent studies on using neural networks for control tasks. However, the authors' approach is distinct in that it uses a combination of supervised learning and trajectory optimization to learn a control policy. The method is also notable for its ability to handle complex dynamics and high-dimensional spaces.

The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their method, including the neural network architecture, the trajectory optimization process, and the training procedure. The use of noise injection during training is also an interesting aspect of the approach, as it helps to prevent overfitting and improve generalization.

The results presented in the paper are impressive, with the authors demonstrating the ability to control a range of complex characters, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking. The supplementary video provides a clear visual representation of the characters' behaviors, and the authors' claim that their method can generate realistic and purposeful behaviors is well-supported.

One potential criticism of the paper is that it assumes full model knowledge, as noted in Equation 6. This may limit the applicability of the method to real-world scenarios where the dynamics of the system are not fully known. Additionally, the section on neural network policy regression (Section 4) is somewhat brief and could be expanded upon. The authors also mention the use of recurrent units in the abstract, but this is not discussed in detail in the paper.

In terms of the qualitative evaluation criteria, the paper scores well in terms of originality, as the authors' approach is distinct from previous work in the field. The paper also scores well in terms of significance, as the ability to generate realistic and purposeful behaviors for complex characters has many potential applications in fields such as robotics and animation. The paper is well-written and clear, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their method and results.

However, the paper could be improved in terms of clarity and organization. Some sections, such as Section 4, are somewhat brief and could be expanded upon. Additionally, the authors could provide more detail on the neural network architecture and the training procedure.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and original approach to control tasks.
* The results are impressive and demonstrate the ability to generate realistic and purposeful behaviors for complex characters.
* The paper is well-written and clear, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their method and results.

Arguments for rejection:

* The paper assumes full model knowledge, which may limit the applicability of the method to real-world scenarios.
* Some sections of the paper are somewhat brief and could be expanded upon.
* The authors could provide more detail on the neural network architecture and the training procedure.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the criticisms mentioned above.
