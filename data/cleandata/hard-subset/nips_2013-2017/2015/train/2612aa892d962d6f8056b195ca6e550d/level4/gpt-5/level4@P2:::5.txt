Post-rebuttal: Thank you for the clarifications. Overall, this is a promising paper that would benefit from restructuring and clearer exposition.

Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper presents a method for training recurrent neural network controllers via alternating trajectory optimization and supervised policy regression. Using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate trajectories and compute local feedback gains, the authors fit a policy that maps noisy sensory states to desired velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces. Noise is injected into inputs and hidden activations to promote robustness. The optimization is distributed and asynchronous, and the learned policy is executed in real time by solving a short-horizon (T=1) physical consistency problem at each step. Results are shown on diverse 3D systems (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion), with ablations indicating the benefits of joint optimization and noise.

This work is closely related to learning from trajectory optimization [Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov], model-based control with CIO [Mordatch et al.], and contrasts with model-free deep RL (e.g., TRPO, DQN) and direct continuous-control methods. Compared to prior learning-from-TO work, the paper tackles more complex 3D behaviors and introduces an efficient way to compute feedback gains by reusing the trajectory optimizer’s computations.

Strengths:
- Addresses challenging 3D control problems across multiple morphologies with a single architecture and training pipeline.
- Thoughtful integration of trajectory optimization, local feedback gains, and supervised learning; the perturbation-based gain computation is elegant and computationally efficient.
- Noise injection at both sensory and network levels is well-motivated and supported by ablations demonstrating improved robustness and generalization.
- Practical distributed implementation with clear description of asynchronous training and proximal regularization.
- Real-time execution without storing trajectories; simple T=1 optimization loop is a neat way to enforce physics consistency online.
- Ablations and an MPC comparison provide useful perspective on behavior regularization and cyclicity.

Weaknesses and suggestions for clarity:
- Assumption of full model knowledge and access to accurate dynamics/contacts (Eq. 6) is central; this prerequisite should be stated prominently in the introduction and abstract, as it affects applicability to real-world systems with model mismatch.
- Section balance: Sections 4 (policy regression) and 6 (distributed architecture) are disproportionate. Section 4 should be expanded to clearly describe the recurrent units mentioned in the abstract: their number/dimensionality, initial state, how r and ṙ are used, and whether recurrence is essential versus a purely feed-forward substitute.
- The term “stochastic neural network” is potentially misleading. The training data and inputs are stochastic due to injected noise, but the policy appears deterministic at test time. Please clarify terminology and explicitly state whether any stochasticity remains during execution.
- Execution details raise questions about physical plausibility: contact forces are part of the action, and constraints are enforced softly, sometimes allowing root forces. Discuss the implications for realism and whether hard-constraint variants or torque-based actions were attempted.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. Beyond the reported ablations, comparisons to strong model-free baselines (e.g., TRPO or contemporary policy gradient methods) and to longer-horizon MPC would strengthen claims of significance.
- Generalization and robustness: report performance under perturbations/model mismatch, sensitivity to noise scales (σε, σγ), and how well policies extrapolate to new goals or disturbances not seen in training.
- Notational/editorial issues: unify terms (e.g., “locomotion” vs “location”), fix typos (“changed changed”, “unity”→“unify”), and ensure Algorithm 1 and references to equations are easy to follow. Consider moving some system/cluster details to an appendix to free space for core method and analysis.

Quality:
Technically sound overall. The gain computation via a soft-constrained perturbation that reuses the Gauss–Newton system is a nice contribution. More detail on convergence behavior of the alternating scheme (beyond anecdotal convergence) and the conditioning/regularization of gains (choice of λ) would help. The T=1 execution optimization is reasonable but warrants discussion of stability guarantees and constraint satisfaction.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable but unevenly organized. Elevate key assumptions (full-model dynamics, soft constraints), expand the policy architecture (especially recurrence), and clarify “stochastic” vs deterministic execution. A concise schematic of the data flow (TO → gains → noisy targets → NN) would help.

Originality:
The overall pipeline builds on existing ideas (CIO, learning from TO, noise-robust training), but the combination and the gain computation method applied to diverse 3D tasks are novel and valuable.

Significance:
If access to accurate models is available, the approach is impactful for animation and potentially robotics. Demonstrations across varied morphologies with a single setup are compelling; broader quantitative comparisons would increase the community’s confidence and adoption.

Pros:
- Strong, unified method demonstrating diverse 3D behaviors
- Efficient, principled feedback gain computation from TO
- Robustness via noise with supportive ablations
- Real-time execution without storing trajectories

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on full model knowledge and soft constraints
- Limited quantitative comparisons to strong baselines
- Ambiguity around recurrence and “stochastic NN” terminology
- Clarity/organization issues; Section 4 underdeveloped relative to its importance

Recommendation:
Borderline accept. The results are promising and the technique is useful, but the paper should be restructured to highlight assumptions, clarify the policy architecture and stochasticity, and strengthen quantitative evaluation. With these revisions, the contribution would be clear and impactful.
