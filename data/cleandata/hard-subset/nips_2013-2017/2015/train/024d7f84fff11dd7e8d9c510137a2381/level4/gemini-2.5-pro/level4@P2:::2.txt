### Summary

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The key contribution is extending previous work, which has focused on specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk, which formulates risk as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability distributions. By applying the envelope theorem to this dual formulation, they derive novel, general-purpose formulas for the policy gradient.

The paper addresses two settings:
1.  **Static Risk:** For a static coherent risk applied to the total return, the authors provide a new gradient formula. They show how this generalizes known results for CVaR and derive a new formula for the mean-semideviation risk. For the general case where analytical solutions are unavailable, they propose a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient.
2.  **Dynamic Risk:** For time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one. This theorem relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and forms the basis for a proposed actor-critic algorithm.

The approach is illustrated on a simple portfolio optimization problem, demonstrating how different risk measures can lead to qualitatively different, and in some cases more rational, investment strategies.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, building upon established results in stochastic programming and coherent risk theory. The application of the envelope theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk is elegant and provides the core theoretical foundation for the proposed methods. The derived gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) appear correct and are the paper's main technical achievements. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis, and the authors are honest about the scope of their work.

The primary weakness in this area is the lack of a comprehensive empirical evaluation. While the proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is a central part of the contribution, it is not tested experimentally. An evaluation on a standard sequential decision-making benchmark would have significantly strengthened the paper's claims about the practical utility of the dynamic risk algorithm.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL is clearly articulated in the introduction. The preliminaries section provides a concise and accessible overview of coherent and dynamic risk measures, which is invaluable for readers not already expert in this area. The theoretical derivations are presented logically, progressing from the static case to the more complex dynamic case. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrate how the general framework can be specialized to concrete cases, clarifying the underlying theory. The overall structure makes a complex topic easy to follow.

### Originality

The paper makes a significant and original contribution. While policy gradient methods and coherent risk measures are established concepts, their combination in this general form is novel. Previous work in risk-sensitive RL has largely been piecemeal, developing bespoke algorithms for specific risk measures. This paper is the first to provide a unified policy gradient framework that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The derivation of the general gradient formulas and the proposal of a sampling-based algorithm that incorporates convex programming are both novel contributions. The work clearly situates itself within the literature and adequately references related work, demonstrating how it generalizes and simplifies several previous results.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are important and likely to be influential. Risk-sensitivity is a crucial consideration for deploying RL systems in real-world applications like finance, robotics, and operations research, where managing worst-case outcomes is often as important as optimizing for the average case. By providing a flexible and theoretically principled framework, this work enables researchers and practitioners to design policies that align with a wide range of risk preferences, rather than being constrained to a few pre-defined measures. The actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk, in particular, opens a path for scaling these methods to large and complex problems. This work advances the state of the art by providing both new theoretical tools and practical algorithms for a significant problem in the field.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Unification and Generality:** The paper provides a powerful and elegant framework that unifies a previously fragmented area of risk-sensitive RL. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   **Novel Theoretical Results:** The derivation of general policy gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures is a strong theoretical achievement.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a technical topic accessible and the contributions easy to appreciate.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work provides a foundation for future research and practical applications of risk-sensitive RL by offering a much more flexible set of tools for managing risk.

### Arguments for Rejection:

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments are confined to a simple, static problem. The proposed algorithm for the more challenging dynamic risk setting is not empirically validated, leaving its practical performance and limitations as open questions.
*   **Practical Concerns Not Addressed:** The paper does not discuss the potential for high variance in the gradient estimates (especially for tail-focused risks like CVaR) or the computational overhead of solving a convex program at each step of the general algorithm.
