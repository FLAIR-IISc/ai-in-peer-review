

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using coherent risk measures, extending previous work that focused on specific risk measures such as CVaR or mean-variance. The authors develop policy gradient algorithms for both static and dynamic Markov coherent risk measures, providing a general approach that encompasses and extends prior results. The work builds upon foundational contributions in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 30] for CVaR, [29] for mean-variance) while addressing the theoretical gap of generalizing to the entire class of coherent risk measures, which have been widely adopted in finance and operations research.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results. The authors provide rigorous derivations of gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk measures, supported by appropriate theorems and consistency results. The sampling-based estimation approach for the gradient, which combines convex programming with likelihood-ratio techniques, is well-justified. The numerical illustration effectively demonstrates the practical implications of using different risk criteria in a financial application. The paper correctly identifies and addresses the key challenges in risk-sensitive RL, particularly regarding the tractability of the optimization problem due to policy parametrization rather than the risk measure itself.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The technical exposition is accessible to readers familiar with RL and risk measures, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to practical implementation. The connection between the proposed framework and previous work is well-explained, and the authors effectively distinguish their contribution from prior approaches. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality: The primary contribution is the generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. While the extension from specific risk measures (like CVaR) to the general class represents a conceptual advancement, the technical approach is a natural extension of existing policy gradient methodology. The work demonstrates how previously studied specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation) emerge as special cases within their unified framework. This represents a meaningful but not revolutionary contribution to the field.

Significance: The paper addresses an important practical need in risk-sensitive decision making, where practitioners must select appropriate risk measures based on problem-specific characteristics. The numerical example clearly demonstrates how different risk criteria (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to different policy choices in financial applications, highlighting the importance of flexible risk criteria. The framework provides a principled approach to risk shaping that could help address model uncertainty in dynamic decision making, which is a significant practical challenge in real-world applications.

Strengths:
- Unified framework for coherent risk measures extends previous specific-risk approaches
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with well-supported gradient formulas
- Clear demonstration of how previous results emerge as special cases
- Practical numerical example showing the importance of risk criterion selection
- Well-organized and clearly written with appropriate technical depth

Weaknesses:
- The technical novelty is incremental rather than transformative (generalization of existing approaches)
- The dynamic risk approach requires a simulator for multiple state transitions, which may limit applicability
- The connection to model uncertainty could be explored more deeply to enhance significance

Pro acceptance: The paper makes a solid contribution to risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework that generalizes previous work. The technical approach is sound, the writing is clear, and the results have clear practical significance for practitioners who need to select appropriate risk criteria for specific applications.

Con rejection: The technical contribution is incremental, as it represents a generalization of existing policy gradient approaches rather than a fundamentally new method. The paper does not introduce substantially new theoretical insights beyond the straightforward extension of previous results to the coherent risk measure class.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a minor but meaningful contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The work provides a valuable unified framework that will be useful to researchers and practitioners seeking to apply risk-sensitive methods in their applications. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality scientific contribution to NIPS.
