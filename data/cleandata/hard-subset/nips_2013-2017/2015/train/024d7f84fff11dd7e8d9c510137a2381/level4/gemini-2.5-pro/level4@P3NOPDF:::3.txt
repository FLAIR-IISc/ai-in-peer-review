This paper addresses the problem of policy optimization in risk-averse Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). The authors consider settings with both static and dynamically consistent coherent risk measures. The primary contribution is the derivation of policy gradient theorems for both of these settings, which enables the use of gradient-based methods for optimizing parameterized policies under general risk-averse objectives. This work generalizes previous results that focused specifically on the Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) measure, extending them to a broader class of risk mappings.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and the technical arguments are easy to follow. The theoretical results appear to be sound. The main strength of the paper lies in this generalization. While the resulting policy gradient forms are a relatively straightforward extension of those in the risk-neutral setting, this is a solid and important contribution. It provides a foundational tool for a significant and growing area of research, allowing practitioners and researchers to apply policy gradient algorithms to a much wider array of risk-averse problems than was previously possible.

The contribution can be seen as an incremental but valuable step in bridging the theory of risk-averse optimization and practical reinforcement learning algorithms. By providing an explicit gradient formulation, the paper paves the way for developing more sophisticated and practical algorithms for risk-sensitive applications.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Theorem 2.1:** The notation `\xi P_\theta` may be confusing for some readers. To enhance clarity, I suggest the authors consider aligning their notation with established conventions in the literature. For example, referring to Theorem 6.6 in Shapiro et al. [26] and adopting a similar notation could make the result more immediately accessible.
*   **Line 141:** There is a typo in "enevlope," which should be corrected to "envelope."
*   **Section 3 (Clarification):** At the beginning of Section 3, it would be beneficial to add a sentence to prevent potential confusion for readers less familiar with this specific topic. The paper discusses the intractability of the problem, and it should be made explicit that this intractability stems from the policy parameterization, not from the use of risk measures themselves. It is worth noting that MDPs with Markov risk measures are generally tractable via dynamic programming. Clarifying that the challenge addressed here is specific to the parameterized policy setting would help to better frame the paper's contribution and the motivation for a policy gradient approach.
