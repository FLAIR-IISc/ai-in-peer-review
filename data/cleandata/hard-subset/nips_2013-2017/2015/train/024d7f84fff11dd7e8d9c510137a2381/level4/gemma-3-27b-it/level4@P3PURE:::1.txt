The paper proposes a novel formula for computing policy gradients for the class of static coherent risks, allowing for analytical derivation for many common risk measures. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where analytical solutions are unavailable, and provides consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper derives expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and quality:** The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is an intriguing and valuable contribution to the risk-sensitive decision making literature. However, analytical solutions of the Lagrangian saddle-points are currently limited to certain risk measures.

The proposed sample-based approximation scheme offers a promising approach to applying the derived results to a broader range of coherent risk measures by circumventing the need for analytical saddle-point derivations. Nevertheless, the paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating the viability of this estimation scheme in practice. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients, applied to various problems (e.g., CVaR), would be extremely helpful.

**Originality:** To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients – in this general form – are novel.

**Clarity:** While generally well-written, the paper’s clarity is somewhat hindered by the breadth of topics addressed within the eight-page limit. Specifically, the discussion of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is presented at a high level, and the absence of algorithmic details or experimental evaluations prevents a proper assessment of the practicality of the derived gradients. This content may be more suitable for a longer journal publication. Alternatively, focusing the paper on static risk measures would allow for a more self-contained presentation, greater detail, and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the current basic toy example. This is an interesting paper with potentially generally applicable results, but the lack of extensive experimental evaluation makes it difficult to fully assess. A narrower scope might also improve clarity given the current space constraints.
