

The paper "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures" presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives. The authors extend previous work on risk-sensitive RL, which has focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance, to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both in static and time-consistent dynamic settings. This is an important contribution as coherent risk measures have gained widespread acceptance in finance and operations research due to their desirable theoretical properties (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity).

The paper makes two key theoretical contributions: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, and (2) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that generalizes the standard policy gradient theorem to dynamic risk measures. The authors demonstrate that their approach subsumes previous results for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation, etc.) while providing a more general and theoretically grounded framework.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their theoretical results, including a consistency result for their sampling-based gradient estimator. The main theorems are well-motivated and properly contextualized within the broader literature on risk-sensitive RL. The authors correctly acknowledge related work, including previous approaches to CVaR (Chow et al., 2013; Tamar et al., 2014) and dynamic risk measures (Ruszczyński, 2010; Petrik & Subramanian, 2012). The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical utility of their framework by showing how different risk preferences (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to different policy choices in a financial trading context.

The paper excels in clarity. The writing is precise and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to practical implementation. The authors carefully define all necessary concepts (coherent risk measures, risk envelopes, Markov coherent risk) and provide clear examples to illustrate their main ideas. The supplementary material contains sufficient details for replication, including proofs and algorithm specifications. The only minor clarity issue is the notation ξPθ in Theorem 2.1, which could be made clearer by referencing Theorem 6.6 in Ruszczyński & Shapiro (2006) as suggested in the draft.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by unifying risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. While several previous papers have studied specific risk measures (CVaR, VaR, mean-variance), this is the first to provide a general framework that encompasses all coherent risk measures. The authors correctly note that previous approaches were limited to specific risk measures or specific system dynamics, while their method applies broadly to any coherent risk measure and general MDPs. This represents a significant theoretical advance that simplifies and unifies previous results.

The significance of this work is substantial. By providing a general framework for risk-sensitive RL with coherent risk measures, the authors enable practitioners to more flexibly design risk preferences tailored to specific application domains. The numerical example demonstrates how different risk measures can lead to qualitatively different policies, highlighting the importance of choosing an appropriate risk criterion. The paper also makes an important conceptual connection between coherent risk and model uncertainty, suggesting potential applications in robust decision making under model misspecification.

The paper's main weakness is that the technical contribution, while theoretically sound, represents a relatively modest extension of previous work. The authors acknowledge this by noting that their approach "generalizes and extends previous results" rather than introducing entirely new concepts. However, the significance of this generalization should not be underestimated, as it provides a principled way to handle a broad class of risk measures that practitioners commonly use. The authors do not overclaim, and their contribution is clearly positioned as a unification and generalization rather than a radical new approach.

I would also suggest a minor clarification in Section 3: the authors should explicitly state that the intractability of the risk-sensitive optimization problem stems from the policy parameterization (θ), not from the risk measure itself. This distinction is important for readers unfamiliar with the problem, as MDPs with Markov risk measures are tractable (as shown in Ruszczyński, 2010), but the policy gradient approach requires additional machinery to handle the θ-dependence.

In terms of the four review criteria:
- Quality: Strong. Theoretical results are well-supported, with proper proofs and consistency guarantees.
- Clarity: Excellent. Well-written, well-organized, with clear examples and sufficient technical detail.
- Originality: Good. Provides a unifying framework for coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work.
- Significance: Good. The framework enables more flexible risk preference design in practical applications, with clear potential for broader impact.

Pro-con acceptance list:
PROS: 
- Provides a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL with coherent risk measures
- Generalizes and simplifies previous results for specific risk measures
- Includes a detailed numerical example demonstrating practical utility
- Well-written with clear technical presentation

CONS:
- The technical contribution is a generalization rather than a novel approach
- The paper does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art beyond existing risk measures

Overall, this paper makes a solid, technically sound contribution that advances the theoretical foundations of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While the technical novelty is moderate, the unifying framework and clear exposition make this a valuable addition to the literature. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and would be of interest to researchers working on risk-sensitive decision making in reinforcement learning. I recommend acceptance.
