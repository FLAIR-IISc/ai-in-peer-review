Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for a broad class of coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. For static risk, it derives a general gradient formula based on the coherent risk representation and the envelope theorem, from which closed-form gradients for several popular measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) can be obtained. When analytic saddle points are unavailable, it proposes a sampling-based estimator that combines likelihood ratios with a convex program defined by the risk envelope, and provides consistency guarantees. For dynamic settings, the paper extends the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures by leveraging a risk-sensitive Bellman equation, and outlines an actor-critic style approach.

This work unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL results that focused on particular criteria (e.g., exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR) by operating at the level of coherent risk envelopes, a framework well-established in finance and operations research. It also connects to dynamic, time-consistent risk (Markov coherent risk) and related robust MDP formulations.

Strengths:
- A unified derivation of policy gradients for the whole class of coherent risk measures is both novel and valuable. It subsumes several known special cases and clarifies their common structure.
- The sampling-based gradient estimator with a convex-programming subroutine is broadly applicable, and the consistency result is important for practice.
- Extending the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk is a noteworthy theoretical contribution and potentially impactful for dynamic, time-consistent risk-sensitive RL.

Weaknesses:
- Analytic solutions to the Lagrangian saddle points are available only for some risk measures, limiting the immediate applicability of the closed-form formulas.
- The proposed sampling-based approximation scheme, while promising, lacks empirical validation in the main paper. In particular, an experimental comparison between the sampling-based gradients and known analytical gradients (e.g., for CVaR) on multiple problems would be very informative.
- Section 5 presents dynamic risk policy gradients at a high level. The absence of algorithmic details and experimental evaluation makes it difficult to assess practical viability.
- The breadth of topics attempted within the page limit hurts clarity. The paper would benefit from a narrower focus with deeper empirical evaluation; alternatively, a longer journal version could do justice to both static and dynamic developments.
- The current empirical illustration is a very basic toy example and does not substantiate the general claims.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears solid, and the consistency guarantees for the sampling-based estimator are carefully stated. However, the lack of empirical evidence for the general estimation scheme and for the dynamic actor-critic approach makes it hard to judge practical effectiveness.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but clarity is compromised by the amount of material packed into the main text. The dynamic-risk section, in particular, is too high level for readers to assess or reproduce the approach. A tighter scope would allow more algorithmic detail and a more informative evaluation.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, the policy-gradient results in this general coherent-risk framework are novel in the RL literature.

Significance:
Potentially high. A general, coherent-risk policy-gradient framework could influence both theory and practice of risk-sensitive RL. However, the current submission does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to establish practical significance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Unifies and generalizes multiple risk-sensitive policy-gradient results under the coherent-risk umbrella.
- Provides a broadly applicable sampling-based estimator with consistency guarantees.
- Extends the policy gradient theorem to dynamic, time-consistent risk measures.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient empirical validation, especially for the proposed sampling-based estimator and the dynamic actor-critic approach.
- Overly broad scope within the page limit leads to missing algorithmic details and reduced clarity.
- The main paper lacks comparisons between sampling-based and analytic gradients on standard benchmarks (e.g., CVaR).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide empirical comparisons between the sampling-based and analytic gradients (e.g., for CVaR) across several tasks, reporting accuracy, variance, and runtime.
- Expand algorithmic details for the dynamic setting (sampling from ξ-weighted transitions, estimation of hθ, and practical implementation choices), and include at least a modest-scale evaluation.
- Consider narrowing the main paper to static coherent risk, with a comprehensive experimental study beyond the toy example; reserve the dynamic extension and additional theory for a longer version.
- Improve the exposition by moving some technical assumptions and proofs to an appendix while giving more intuition and guidance for practitioners in the main text.

Overall assessment:
An interesting and potentially impactful contribution that unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients under coherent risk measures. However, the current submission lacks the experimental depth and algorithmic detail needed to assess practical viability, and clarity suffers from the broad scope. A narrower focus with stronger empirical support would substantially strengthen the paper.
