This paper introduces a unified framework for deriving policy gradients for the class of static coherent risk measures. The authors present a general formula that yields analytical policy gradients for several common risk measures. For cases where an analytical solution to the underlying saddle-point problem is unavailable, the paper proposes a general sampling-based algorithm and provides consistency guarantees for the resulting gradient estimates. Finally, the work extends these ideas to derive expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality**

The primary contribution of this work is the unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures, which is a valuable and novel addition to the literature on risk-sensitive decision-making. The theoretical development is elegant and provides new insights into the structure of risk-averse policy optimization. However, the practical significance of the work is difficult to assess due to several limitations.

While the unified framework is compelling, the availability of analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points is limited to a subset of risk measures. This makes the proposed sample-based approximation scheme a critical component for the general applicability of the framework. Unfortunately, the paper does not provide any empirical evidence to demonstrate that this estimation scheme is viable in practice. The lack of experiments makes it impossible to judge the performance, stability, or sample complexity of the proposed estimator. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients for a measure like CVaR, for instance, would be essential to validate the approach.

**Originality**

To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, presented in this general form, are novel and represent a significant theoretical contribution.

**Clarity and Scope**

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is considerably hindered by the breadth of topics it attempts to address within the eight-page limit. This ambitious scope leads to a high-level and sometimes superficial treatment of key concepts.

The section on dynamic risk measures (Section 5) suffers most acutely from this issue. It is presented with insufficient detail, lacking both concrete algorithmic specifications and any form of experimental evaluation. This prevents the reader from judging whether the derived gradients can lead to practical and effective algorithms. As it stands, this part of the paper raises more questions than it answers.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper presents interesting ideas with potentially broad applicability, but its current form is unconvincing due to the lack of empirical support and the issues with scope. I strongly recommend that the authors narrow the focus of this submission to significantly strengthen it.

A much stronger paper would concentrate solely on the static risk measures. This would provide the necessary space to:
1.  Offer a more self-contained and detailed presentation of the core theoretical results.
2.  Provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation that moves beyond the current basic toy example. This evaluation should rigorously validate the proposed sampling-based method, perhaps by comparing its performance and convergence against the analytical gradients on a set of well-understood problems.

The material on dynamic risk measures is interesting but seems better suited for a longer journal publication where it can be fully developed, supported with detailed algorithms, and validated with a proper experimental section.

In summary, this paper presents a promising theoretical framework, but its contribution is currently undermined by a lack of empirical validation and an overly broad scope that compromises clarity. A revised version with a narrower focus and a comprehensive experimental section would constitute a much stronger submission.
