

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using coherent risk measures, extending previous work that focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR. The authors develop policy gradient algorithms applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both in static and time-consistent dynamic settings. For static risk, they propose a sampling-based approach combined with convex programming, while for dynamic risk, they introduce an actor-critic style algorithm. The key contribution is the generalization of previous risk-sensitive RL approaches into a single coherent framework, with theorems and algorithms that subsume specific cases as special instances.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The authors provide a clear motivation by connecting risk-sensitive optimization to finance and operations research, where coherent risk measures are widely accepted. They properly contextualize their work against prior studies on risk-sensitive RL, noting that previous approaches were limited to specific risk measures or system dynamics. The mathematical derivations are rigorous, with Theorem 2.1 establishing the critical representation of coherent risk measures as worst-case expectations, which underpins the entire approach. The proofs of the main theorems (4.2 and 5.2) are well-constructed, leveraging the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio techniques, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for verification.

Clarity is excellent. The paper is well-organized, with clear exposition of both the theoretical foundations and algorithmic implementations. The notation is consistently defined, and the connection between the finance-inspired risk measures and RL is well-motivated. The authors effectively use examples (CVaR and mean-semideviation) to demonstrate how their general framework recovers and simplifies previous results. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is particularly effective in demonstrating the practical implications of different risk preferences, showing how policy selection varies with risk criteria (e.g., why a risk-averse policy might choose a heavy-tailed asset over one with higher mean returns).

Originality is strong. While risk-sensitive RL has been explored in prior work, the extension to the entire class of coherent risk measures represents a significant conceptual advance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work by emphasizing that the choice of risk measure is problem-dependent and that their framework provides a natural way to select appropriate risk measures for specific applications. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (in the conclusion) is particularly insightful and suggests broader implications beyond the immediate technical contribution.

Significance is substantial. Risk-sensitive RL is an important problem with practical applications in finance, operations research, and other domains where managing variability is as crucial as optimizing expected outcomes. The authors' framework provides a flexible tool for practitioners to design risk preferences tailored to their specific needs. The numerical example demonstrates how different risk criteria lead to different policy behaviors, highlighting the framework's practical utility. The paper also points toward future work on handling model uncertainty through risk shaping, which could have broader implications for robust decision-making under uncertainty.

The main limitation is that the experimental validation is limited to a static risk problem (Section 6), while the dynamic risk formulation (Section 5) remains largely theoretical. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more substantial empirical demonstration of the dynamic risk algorithms would strengthen the significance claim. However, given the complexity of the dynamic risk formulation and the space constraints, this limitation is understandable.

The paper is well-written and carefully referenced, with appropriate citations to both RL literature (e.g., [15, 28]) and the coherent risk measure literature (e.g., [2, 25]). The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, particularly noting that previous approaches on CVaR [8, 30] and mean-variance [18, 21] are special cases of their framework.

In summary, this paper makes a strong technical contribution by unifying risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. The theoretical developments are sound, the exposition is clear, and the significance is well-justified through both the theoretical generalization and the practical numerical example. While the experimental validation could be expanded to include dynamic risk examples, the paper's main contribution—providing a unified approach to risk-sensitive RL applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures—is significant and well-executed. The paper meets the high standards of NIPS in terms of technical quality, novelty, and potential impact. I recommend acceptance.
