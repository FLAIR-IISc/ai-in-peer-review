This paper presents a unified framework for developing policy gradient methods for the broad class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive a new, general formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure, which can be used to obtain analytical policy gradients for several common risk measures. For cases where an analytical solution is unavailable, the paper proposes a general sampling-based algorithm and proves the consistency of its gradient estimates. This framework is then extended to derive expressions for policy gradients of time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures.

**Significance and Quality**

The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a valuable and novel contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive decision-making. The general theoretical framework is intriguing and successfully unifies and generalizes several prior works that had focused on individual risk measures.

However, a key limitation is that analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points, which are needed for the main gradient formula, are only available for a subset of risk measures. The proposed sample-based approximation scheme is intended to address this for general coherent risk measures, but the paper provides no empirical evidence to show that this estimation scheme is viable in practice. An experimental comparison between the sample-based gradient estimator and the analytical one for a measure where both are available (e.g., CVaR) would be extremely helpful for validating the proposed approximation.

**Originality**

To the best of my knowledge, the policy gradient formulas derived in this general form are novel. The work provides a new perspective on risk-sensitive policy gradients by connecting them to the dual representation of coherent risk measures.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written. However, its clarity is significantly hindered by an attempt to address too many distinct topics within the strict page limit. This is particularly evident in the treatment of dynamic risk. The discussion of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is presented at a very high level, and the lack of algorithmic details or experimental results makes it impossible for a reader to judge whether the derived gradients lead to a practical approach.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

This work contains enough material for a longer journal publication. For a conference paper, I believe the submission would be much stronger if it were more focused. A paper centered exclusively on static coherent risk measures would be more self-contained and would allow space for crucial additions, such as:
*   A more comprehensive empirical evaluation that goes beyond the basic toy example.
*   A direct comparison of the analytical and sample-based gradient estimators to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed approximation method.
*   A more detailed description of the algorithms and their implementation.

**Conclusion**

This is an interesting paper with potentially significant and broadly applicable results. The theoretical contribution is strong. However, the lack of compelling experimental validation, combined with an overly ambitious scope that compromises clarity, makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the work. A narrower focus with a thorough empirical study would make for a much stronger submission.
