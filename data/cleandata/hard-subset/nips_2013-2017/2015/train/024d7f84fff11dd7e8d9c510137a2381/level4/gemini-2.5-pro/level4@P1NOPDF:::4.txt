This paper addresses the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. The authors consider a general class of coherent risk measures, defined by standard properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The proposed approach is based on the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which defines the risk of a random cost as the maximum expected cost over a convex set of probability distributions. The paper's key technical contribution relies on an assumption that the boundaries of this convex set are M-Lipschitz continuous. This assumption enables the authors to analytically characterize the optimal policy parameters and to derive a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision problems.

The paper is well-written and organized. Despite my limited familiarity with risk-sensitive optimization, I found the explanations clear and was able to follow the main arguments. The primary contribution is a unified framework for optimizing a broad class of coherent risk measures, which includes many well-known measures as special cases. This general treatment is a significant contribution. However, the paper's claims would be more compelling with a deeper empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm.

**Specific Comments and Questions:**

1.  The Preliminaries section could be improved. The distinction between the policy distribution P_{\theta} and the underlying random variable \xi was not immediately clear. Clarifying their respective roles earlier would help the reader.

2.  Equation 2 appears to be the definition of a convex envelope. Could the authors explain why this is considered more restrictive than the representation in Theorem 2.1?

3.  The analysis seems to assume discrete probability distributions. It would be beneficial to add a discussion on how the framework and results generalize to continuous random variables.

4.  The proof of Theorem 4.2 appears to rely on the smoothness of certain constraint functions. It would be helpful to explicitly state this assumption in the main text when the theorem is presented.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that provides a general and elegant approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results are compelling, though a full verification of the proofs is warranted.
