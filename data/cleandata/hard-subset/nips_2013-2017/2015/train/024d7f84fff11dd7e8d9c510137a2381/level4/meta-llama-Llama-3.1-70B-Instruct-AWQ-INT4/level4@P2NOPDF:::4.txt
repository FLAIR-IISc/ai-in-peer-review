The authors address the problem of determining policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure, focusing on a broad class of risk measures defined by coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. Their approach leverages a dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable is calculated as the maximum expected value within this hull. The paper introduces an assumption regarding the family of coherent risk measures, specifically that the maximum derivatives of the convex hull's borders are bounded (i.e., the borders are M-Lipschitz continuous). Based on this assumption, the authors analytically derive optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure and propose a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step decision) and dynamic (sequential) cases.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, allowing a non-expert in risk-sensitive optimization to follow the explanations with ease. The primary contribution of this work lies in its unified analysis of a general class of risk measures encompassing most well-known measures, which is a significant advancement in the field. However, a more in-depth empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm would further enhance the paper's impact.

Several points require clarification or consideration: 

1. The preliminaries section could be more detailed, particularly in explaining the roles of P_{\theta} and \ksi to avoid confusion.
2. Equation 2 appears to be the definition of a convex envelope; it would be beneficial to elaborate on why this formulation is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1.
3. A comment on how the analysis can be generalized to distributions on continuous variables would be a valuable addition.
4. In the short paper, it would be helpful to mention the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in proving Theorem 4.2.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that provides a general framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. While the results are compelling, the proofs require verification to ensure their validity.
