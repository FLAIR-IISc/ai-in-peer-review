This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending the scope from specific risk measures to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive a novel gradient formula for static coherent risks by leveraging their dual representation. This general formula is then used to re-derive and simplify existing results for specific measures like CVaR and to obtain new analytical gradients for others, such as mean-semideviation. For the general case where analytical solutions are unavailable, a sampling-based algorithm using convex programming is proposed, accompanied by a consistency analysis. The framework is further extended to time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures, for which a policy gradient theorem and a corresponding actor-critic algorithm are outlined.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main strength lies in its novel and elegant unification of policy gradient methods for a broad and axiomatically-justified class of risk measures. By providing a general theory, this work moves beyond the piecemeal approach of prior studies that addressed individual risk measures. This is a valuable and significant theoretical contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature.
*   **Generality:** The proposed framework, particularly the general gradient estimation algorithm, provides a principled approach for optimizing any coherent risk measure, offering great flexibility for practitioners to select or design risk criteria that are best suited for their specific problem.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The practical utility of the proposed methods, especially the general Sample Average Approximation (SAA) based gradient estimator, is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an empirical study comparing the performance of the SAA estimator against its analytical counterpart for a measure like CVaR. This would provide crucial insights into its sample complexity and viability in practice.
*   **Lack of Clarity due to Scope:** The paper attempts to cover an extensive amount of material—static risk, a general estimation algorithm, and dynamic risk—within a constrained space. This ambition unfortunately detracts from the clarity and completeness of the presentation. The treatment of dynamic risk in Section 5 is particularly high-level, lacking the algorithmic details and analysis necessary for a reader to understand its implementation or judge its practicality. The current scope feels more appropriate for a longer journal paper.
*   **Limited Experiments:** The numerical illustration is confined to a simple one-step problem. While it effectively highlights the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure, it does not evaluate the proposed policy gradient algorithms in a sequential decision-making setting, which is the primary context of this work. Evaluating the approach on standard reinforcement learning benchmarks would be necessary to demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents interesting and potentially impactful theoretical results. However, the lack of thorough empirical evaluation and the clarity issues arising from its broad scope are major concerns. I would suggest the authors narrow the focus of the paper, perhaps concentrating solely on static risk measures. This would allow for a more detailed presentation of the general gradient estimation algorithm and, crucially, space for a comprehensive experimental section that validates the proposed methods. Such a revision would make the paper's contributions clearer, more self-contained, and better substantiated.
