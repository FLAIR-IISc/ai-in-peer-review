- Introduces a unified formulation for static coherent risk measures enabling analytic policy gradients, a sampling-based estimator with consistency guarantees for intractable saddle points, and an extension to Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.  
- Novel unified treatment is valuable, offering a common framework across multiple risk-sensitive objectives.  
- The sampling-based estimator plus consistency guarantees theoretically broadens applicability beyond closed-form saddle-point cases.  
- Extension to dynamic Markov coherent risks is ambitious and potentially impactful for sequential settings.  
- Analytical saddle-point solutions appear available only for a subset of coherent risks.  
- No empirical validation is provided for the sampling-based estimator’s practical performance.  
- Lacks experimental comparison between analytic and sampled gradients (e.g., CVaR), leaving variance, bias, convergence, and sample complexity unassessed.  
- Dynamic risk section is high-level and lacks algorithmic details (pseudo-code, complexity, variance-reduction) and empirical evaluation.  
- Trying to cover many topics within eight pages compromises clarity and depth; the dynamic part feels underdeveloped.  
- The policy gradient derivations in this general form appear novel and original.  
- Theoretical contributions are sound and potentially significant if supported by convincing experiments.  
- Consistency guarantees for the sampling estimator are an important step toward practical use.  
- Absence of empirical evidence undermines confidence in estimator viability where analytical solutions don’t exist.  
- Clarity is generally good, but breadth reduces readability; a more focused exposition would help.  
- Suggested improvements: add empirical validation and analytic vs. sample comparisons, clarify which risks have closed-form saddle points, expand the dynamic section with algorithms and complexity, strengthen experiments on realistic tasks, or narrow scope to static risks for a conference version.  
- Overall: promising and theoretically strong, but incomplete empirically and in dynamic-method detail; significant potential if tightened and experimentally validated.
