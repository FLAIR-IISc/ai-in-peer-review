Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures, covering both static risk of the discounted return and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The core idea is to use the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński and Shapiro): any coherent risk can be written as the worst-case expectation over an adversarial reweighting ξ of the baseline distribution Pθ within a convex risk envelope U(Pθ). Leveraging this, the authors derive:
- A general gradient formula for static coherent risk using the envelope theorem and a likelihood-ratio trick.
- Practical estimators: (i) closed-form gradients for specific risks such as CVaR and mean-semideviation; and (ii) a sample-average approximation (SAA) scheme that combines sampling with a convex program and KKT multipliers for general coherent risks, along with a consistency result.
- A policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk that expresses the gradient in terms of a value function and an ξ*-weighted trajectory distribution, enabling an actor–critic algorithm.

This unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive RL work that targeted particular risk criteria or special dynamics: mean–variance (e.g., Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), CVaR (Tamar et al.; Chow & Ghavamzadeh), exponential utility (Borkar), and dynamic coherent risk under linear dynamics. It also connects to robust MDPs (Osogami; Ruszczyński; Petrik & Subramanian), using function approximation to scale the “critic” for dynamic risk.

Strengths
- Originality: A clean, unified treatment of policy gradients for the entire coherent risk class, with both static and dynamic (time-consistent) settings. The extension of the policy-gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk is novel and conceptually important.
- Technical quality: The derivations are principled, using the dual representation, envelope theorem, and KKT conditions. The SAA-based estimator and its consistency under mild assumptions are well motivated. The dynamic result ties rigorously to a Bellman equation for coherent risk.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is generally well written and accessible, even to readers not steeped in risk measures, and relates to a broad range of prior work.
- Significance: Provides a toolkit that can subsume CVaR/mean–variance methods and may influence how risk is handled in RL, especially in settings where time consistency is critical.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical evaluation: The numerical illustration is small-scale and mainly conceptual. Given the practical nature of the proposed estimators (inner convex programs and actor–critic with ξ*-weighted transitions), more extensive experiments would strengthen the case: comparisons to CVaR/mean–variance baselines, scaling to larger MDPs, sensitivity to tail parameters, and variance/convergence diagnostics.
- Practicality of the dynamic method: The actor requires sampling under ξ*-weighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per state. This assumes a simulator with flexible reset and transition sampling; applicability in purely offline or single-trajectory regimes is limited. A discussion of how to adapt to those regimes would be helpful.
- Assumptions on the risk envelope: The analysis assumes an explicit convex formulation of U(Pθ) with smooth dependence on Pθ (bounded derivatives of constraint functions). While this holds for several popular risks (e.g., CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation), the paper could more clearly delineate which coherent measures fall outside this template and the implications.
- Notational load in preliminaries: The roles of Pθ (baseline trajectory or transition distribution) and ξ (reweighting density) can be confusing on first read, especially when moving from static to dynamic settings where U depends on x. Minor clarifications would improve readability.

Quality
The technical approach is sound and carefully built on standard tools. The static gradient formulas recover and simplify known results (e.g., CVaR) and extend to new cases. The SAA estimator with KKT multipliers is appealing and accompanied by a consistency guarantee under reasonable conditions (Slater condition, compactness, continuity). For the dynamic setting, the connection to robust MDPs for the critic and the new gradient expression for the actor are well motivated. Proofs are in the supplement and appear plausible, though they deserve careful checking by the committee.

Clarity
Overall clear and well organized. The preliminaries could better distinguish expectations under Pθ versus Eξ[·] with Eξ[Z] = ∑ω Pθ(ω) ξ(ω) Z(ω), and explicitly note in the dynamic case that the envelope U(x, Pθ(·|x)) depends on the state. It would also help to state early that the method provides gradient estimators (not closed-form optimal policies).

Originality
High. The unification across coherent risks and the policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk appear novel and go beyond prior work that targeted specific risk measures or special dynamics.

Significance
High potential impact. A general gradient-based framework for coherent risks could become the standard way to incorporate risk in RL, especially where time consistency matters. Empirical breadth will be important to catalyze adoption.

Specific comments and questions
1) Preliminaries: Please clarify the roles of Pθ and ξ. My understanding is that Pθ is the policy-induced baseline distribution (over outcomes in the static case and over next states Pθ(·|x) in the dynamic case), while ξ is a nonnegative density that reweights Pθ within the risk envelope U(Pθ) (or U(x, Pθ(·|x))). Then Eξ[·] denotes expectation under the reweighted distribution Pθ ξ.
2) Equation (2): Is this simply a parametric description of the risk envelope U(Pθ) via affine/convex constraints to enable KKT and envelope-theorem analysis? If so, please explain how this assumption is stricter than the existence result in Theorem 2.1, and provide examples of coherent risks that do and do not admit such a representation.
3) Continuous distributions: The text mentions extensions to Lp spaces. Please sketch the needed conditions (e.g., Radon–Nikodym densities, dominated convergence/Leibniz rule for differentiating under the integral, and appropriate measurability/compactness) and any changes to the SAA procedure.
4) Theorem 4.2 assumptions: It would help to state in the main paper how the smoothness of constraint functions ge, fi with respect to p = Pθ is used (to justify envelope theorem application and to control the KKT sensitivity), and why the bounded derivative assumption is natural for CVaR, spectral, and mean-semideviation.
5) Practicalities: Please discuss computational cost of solving the inner convex program and extracting KKT multipliers per batch, and the variance of the resulting gradient estimator compared to analytic formulas (you mention a comparison for CVaR—more detail in the main text would help).
6) Dynamic algorithm: The requirement to sample under ξ*-weighted transitions is strong. Can you comment on importance sampling or off-policy corrections to avoid modifying the simulator, and on extensions to single-trajectory settings?

Pros for acceptance
- Unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients across the full coherent class (static and dynamic).
- New policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk; principled actor–critic design.
- Clear connections to robust MDPs; general SAA + KKT estimator with consistency.
- Recovers and simplifies known special cases (e.g., CVaR), enabling broader applicability.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evaluation; scalability and variance in realistic RL tasks are not demonstrated.
- Practical requirements (inner convex programs; ξ*-weighted dynamics; multi-sample simulator) may limit immediate applicability.
- Some assumptions on envelope smoothness and representation could be discussed more explicitly.

Overall assessment
A strong theory paper with a compelling and general framework for risk-sensitive RL. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifications noted above and, if space allows, modestly expanded empirical evidence.
