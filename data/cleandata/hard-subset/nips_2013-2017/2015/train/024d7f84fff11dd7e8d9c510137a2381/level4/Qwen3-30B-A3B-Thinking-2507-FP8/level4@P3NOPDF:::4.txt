

The paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive decision-making under a general class of coherent risk measures. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures as the supremum over a convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random cost is defined by the maximum expected value within this hull. A key technical contribution is the assumption that the boundary of this convex hull is M-Lipschitz continuous, enabling the derivation of closed-form optimal policy parameters and a general gradient estimation algorithm for both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) settings. The work’s primary strength lies in its broad applicability, encompassing most well-known risk measures (e.g., CVaR, entropic risk) under a single theoretical umbrella, which represents a significant advancement over prior specialized analyses.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making complex concepts accessible even to readers unfamiliar with risk-sensitive optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the dual representation approach provides a principled foundation for risk-averse policy optimization. However, several points require clarification or expansion to strengthen the contribution and ensure reproducibility.  

**Major Comments:**  
1. **Preliminaries Clarity:** The roles of $P_\theta$ (the policy-induced distribution) and $\xi$ (the random cost variable) are ambiguously defined in Section 2. Readers struggle to distinguish $P_\theta$ as a distribution over states/actions versus $\xi$ as the cost random variable. Please explicitly define all notation at first use, including the relationship between $\xi$, the policy $\theta$, and the underlying probability space.  

2. **Equation 2 and Convex Envelope:** Equation 2 states $\rho(X) = \sup_{Q \in \mathcal{Q}} \mathbb{E}_Q[X]$, which is precisely the standard dual representation of coherent risk measures (not merely a "convex envelope"). The paper claims this is "more restrictive than Theorem 2.1," but Theorem 2.1 appears to derive this representation under coherence assumptions. Clarify the distinction: Is Equation 2 a restatement of Theorem 2.1, or does it impose additional constraints? If the latter, specify these constraints.  

3. **Generalization to Continuous Distributions:** The analysis assumes discrete domains (e.g., finite state spaces in the gradient derivation). For broader applicability, the authors should discuss how the framework extends to continuous variables. For instance, would the M-Lipschitz assumption on the hull boundary require additional smoothness conditions on the cost function or policy? A brief note on this (e.g., via density assumptions or measure-theoretic adaptations) would significantly strengthen the paper.  

4. **Smoothness in Theorem 4.2:** Theorem 4.2 establishes gradient convergence under the M-Lipschitz assumption. However, the role of smoothness in the constraint functions (e.g., the policy parameterization) is not explicitly linked to the proof. Please clarify how the smoothness of these constraints interacts with the M-Lipschitz condition to ensure the gradient estimator’s validity.  

**Minor Suggestions:**  
- The empirical section is limited to theoretical analysis. While the theory is strong, adding a small-scale empirical validation (e.g., on a risk-sensitive control benchmark like the CartPole risk variant) would better demonstrate the algorithm’s practical utility and align with NIPS’ applied focus.  
- The term "borders of the hull" (Section 3) is non-standard; use "boundary" or "edges" for precision.  
- In Section 4.2, explicitly state that the M-Lipschitz assumption on the hull boundary is necessary to bound the gradient’s Lipschitz constant (a key step in the convergence proof).  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances risk-sensitive optimization by unifying diverse risk measures under a single coherent framework. The dual representation approach is elegant, and the M-Lipschitz assumption provides a tractable path to policy optimization. The paper’s clarity and organization are commendable, and its theoretical results are likely sound (though careful verification of the proofs is recommended). Addressing the clarifications above—particularly on notation, the convex envelope equivalence, and continuous-domain generalization—would elevate the work to a definitive reference in the field. The empirical gap noted is the only significant limitation, but the core theoretical contribution remains highly valuable.  

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a clear, significant advance in risk-sensitive decision theory, and the requested clarifications are straightforward to implement.
