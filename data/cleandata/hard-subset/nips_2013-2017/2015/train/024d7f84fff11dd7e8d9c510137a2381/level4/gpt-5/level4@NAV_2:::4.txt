The authors tackle the problem of selecting policy parameters that minimize a specified risk measure. They consider a broad class of risk criteria characterized by coherence properties (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, positive homogeneity). The approach leverages the dual representation of coherent risk as an expectation under an adversarially reweighted density from a risk envelope (a convex set of probability densities). Thus, the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) is the maximum expected value over this envelope. The paper imposes an additional assumption on the family of coherent risk measures, effectively bounding the sensitivity of the envelope’s boundary with respect to the underlying distribution (i.e., the envelope boundary is M-Lipschitz in Pθ). Under this assumption, the authors derive analytical expressions that enable optimization over policy parameters via gradient-based methods, and present a general gradient estimation procedure for both the static setting (single-step) and the dynamic/sequential setting.

The paper is well written and well structured. Even without deep prior exposure to risk-sensitive optimization, most derivations and intuitions are accessible. The main contribution is a unified treatment that covers a wide spectrum of coherent risk measures, subsuming many standard choices. This is a meaningful advance. That said, a more extensive empirical study of the proposed gradient estimators would strengthen the work.

Some comments/questions
1) The preliminaries could better clarify the distinct roles of Pθ and ξ. In particular, it would help to explicitly separate how Pθ encodes the policy-induced distribution over outcomes, while ξ represents the adversarial reweighting within the risk envelope, and to restate where each appears in the gradient expressions.
2) Is Equation (2) essentially the definition of a convex risk envelope in this finite setting? In what sense is it more restrictive than the general representation in Theorem 2.1?
3) Please add a brief discussion of how the analysis extends to continuous outcome spaces (e.g., required regularity, interchange of limits/integration, and any additional technical conditions on the envelope).
4) It would help to explicitly note (even in the short version) how the smoothness assumptions on the constraint functions are used in the proof of Theorem 4.2 (e.g., to justify envelope theorem application and differentiation under the max).

A strong theoretical contribution offering a general framework for risk-sensitive decision making; the results are promising, though the proofs should be carefully checked.
