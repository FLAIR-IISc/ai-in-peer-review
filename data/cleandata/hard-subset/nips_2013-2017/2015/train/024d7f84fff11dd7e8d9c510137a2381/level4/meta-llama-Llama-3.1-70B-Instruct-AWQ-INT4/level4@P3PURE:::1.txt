The paper proposes a new formula for the class of static coherent risks, from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where no analytical solution is available and shows consistency guarantees of the policy gradient estimates. Furthermore, the paper provides expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is intriguing and a valuable, novel contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, analytical solutions of the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for some risk measures. The proposed sample-based approximation scheme could enable the application of the derived results to general coherent risk measures by avoiding the need to derive analytical forms of the saddle points. Nevertheless, the paper does not provide empirical evidence that the proposed estimation scheme is viable in practice. An experimental comparison between using sample-based policy gradients and analytical ones in different problems, for instance, for Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), would be extremely helpful.

Originality: 
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients are novel in this general form.

Clarity: 
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is hindered by the number of different issues addressed within the eight pages. In particular, the dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level, and the lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluations does not allow the reader to judge whether the derived policy gradients yield approaches that are viable in practice. This content seems more suitable for a longer journal publication. A beneficial alternative would be to split this paper and focus on static risk measures, allowing for a more standalone presentation with more details and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the basic toy example in the current submission. The paper has potentially generally applicable results, but the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluations makes it difficult to assess. A narrower focus might be more appropriate to address the deficits in clarity due to space constraints.
