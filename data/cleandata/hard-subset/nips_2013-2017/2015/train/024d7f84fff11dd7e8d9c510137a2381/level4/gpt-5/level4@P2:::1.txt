Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the entire class of coherent risk measures. For static coherent risk, the authors derive a general gradient formula using the risk-envelope dual representation and the envelope theorem, recovering known results (e.g., CVaR) as special cases and providing new gradients (e.g., mean-semideviation). When the Lagrangian saddle point cannot be solved analytically, they propose a sampling-plus–convex programming approach and prove consistency of the resulting gradient estimator. For dynamic, time-consistent risk, they introduce a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, leading to an actor–critic style algorithm that relies on estimating a risk-sensitive value function (via connections to robust MDPs) and sampling under ξ-weighted transitions.

Relation to prior work
The paper unifies and extends previous risk-sensitive RL results that target specific measures such as mean-variance, exponential utility, and CVaR, and connects to dynamic coherent risk and robust MDP formulations. The contribution is to give a single framework yielding gradients across coherent risks, along with a general sampling-based estimator.

Strengths
- A unified and principled derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is novel and conceptually valuable.
- The use of the risk-envelope representation to derive gradient formulas is elegant and general; it simplifies and extends prior results (e.g., CVaR).
- The sample-based estimator with consistency guarantees is broadly applicable when analytical saddle points are unavailable.
- The dynamic extension (policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk) is theoretically interesting and potentially impactful.

Weaknesses
- Analytical saddle points are available only for select risks; thus, practical performance hinges on the sample-based estimator, which lacks convincing empirical validation in the main paper.
- The dynamic-risk section is presented at a very high level: algorithmic details (actor–critic steps, function approximation, sampling procedures, computational costs) and experiments are largely deferred, making it difficult to assess practicality.
- The empirical evaluation is minimal and does not demonstrate the effectiveness, variance, or scalability of the proposed estimators on realistic MDPs.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound and clearly leverages established tools (duality, envelope theorem, likelihood ratios). Assumptions are standard in policy-gradient literature and reasonable for many coherent risks. However, the empirical support is insufficient to judge efficiency, variance properties, and robustness of the sample-based gradient in practice, especially for risks sensitive to tail events.

Clarity
The writing is generally clear, but the breadth of material compressed into the main submission reduces readability. Section 5 in particular remains too high-level for a conference-length paper. Providing concrete algorithmic pseudocode, computational complexity, and practical guidance (e.g., how to sample ξ-weighted transitions, how many samples per state, variance-reduction techniques) would substantially improve clarity. A tighter focus on static risks with richer experiments could make the contribution more self-contained; alternatively, a longer format would better accommodate the dynamic-risk component.

Originality
To the best of my knowledge, the general gradient formulas for coherent risk measures and the policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk are new in this unified form. The work subsumes and extends several prior special-case results.

Significance
If empirically validated, the framework could influence risk-sensitive RL by enabling principled optimization across many coherent risks. Demonstrating efficacy on larger or continuous MDPs and comparing against baselines (e.g., CVaR-specific methods, mean-variance) would better establish significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical comparisons of the sample-based gradients to analytical ones (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) across multiple MDPs, including runtime, variance, and sample complexity.
- Include ablations on the number of samples and the impact of the convex program’s solution accuracy.
- For the dynamic setting, include a concrete actor–critic algorithm, implementation details, and experiments on benchmark MDPs; discuss the requirement of simulators that allow multiple next-state samples and the feasibility in standard RL settings.
- Add pseudocode and complexity analyses for both static and dynamic algorithms.

Pros
- Novel, unified gradient framework for coherent risk measures.
- General sample-based estimator with consistency guarantees.
- Extends to time-consistent dynamic risk with a policy gradient theorem.

Cons
- Insufficient empirical validation, especially for the sample-based estimator.
- Dynamic section lacks algorithmic detail and practical demonstrations.
- Breadth over depth reduces clarity in the conference format.

Overall
An interesting and potentially impactful contribution. However, the absence of thorough experiments and limited algorithmic detail, particularly for the dynamic component, make it difficult to assess practical viability. A narrower focus on static risks with comprehensive empirical evaluation, or a longer treatment including full details and experiments for the dynamic case, would strengthen the paper considerably.
