This paper introduces a novel formula for the class of static coherent risk measures, from which policy gradients for several common risk measures can be derived analytically. For cases where analytical solutions are unavailable, the authors present a general sampling-based algorithm and prove the consistency of its policy gradient estimates. The paper concludes by providing expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality**

The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a valuable and novel contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive decision-making. A key limitation, however, is that analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for a subset of risk measures.

The proposed sampling-based approximation is designed to overcome this limitation by extending the framework to general coherent risk measures without requiring analytical saddle-point solutions. However, the paper provides no empirical evidence to demonstrate that this estimation scheme is viable in practice. The submission would be substantially strengthened by an experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients on a standard benchmark (e.g., for CVaR). Such an evaluation is crucial for assessing the practical utility of the proposed methods.

**Originality**

To the best of my knowledge, the generalized form of the policy gradients derived in this work is novel.

**Clarity and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is generally well-written, its clarity is significantly hindered by the breadth of topics it attempts to address within the eight-page limit. This is particularly evident in the treatment of dynamic risk policy gradients (Section 5), which is presented at a very high level. The absence of algorithmic details and experimental results makes it impossible for the reader to judge whether these derivations lead to practical algorithms.

I believe the paper would be much stronger with a narrower focus. I recommend concentrating solely on static risk measures. This would allow for a more self-contained presentation, greater detail, and, most importantly, space for a comprehensive empirical evaluation that moves beyond the basic toy example currently included. The material on dynamic risks seems better suited for a longer journal publication where it can be developed more thoroughly.

In summary, this paper presents interesting theoretical results with potentially broad applicability. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and the clarity issues arising from its ambitious scope make it difficult to assess the practical significance of the contributions. A more focused submission with a solid empirical evaluation would be far more impactful.
