The authors tackle the problem of policy parameter optimization with the goal of minimizing a specified risk measure. Their work focuses on a broad class of coherent risk measures, characterized by properties like convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The core of their approach leverages a dual representation of coherent risk measures – expressing them as the convex hull of probability distributions, where risk is quantified as the maximum expected value within this hull. A key assumption is introduced, bounding the maximum derivative of the convex hull’s boundaries with an M-Lipschitz continuity constraint. Exploiting this assumption, the authors obtain analytical solutions for optimal policy parameters with respect to the defined risk measure, and develop a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (single-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision problems. 

The paper is clearly written and logically structured. Despite my limited background in risk-sensitive optimization, I found the explanations largely accessible. The primary strength of this work lies in its unified treatment of a general class of risk measures, encompassing many established measures, representing a valuable contribution to the field.  However, a more extensive empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the paper.

Specific comments and questions: 1- The preliminaries section would benefit from improved clarity regarding the roles of $P_{\theta}$ and $\ksi$. 2- Equation 2 appears to represent the definition of a convex envelope; clarification is needed to understand why this is presented as more restrictive than Theorem 2.1. 3- Discussion of the analysis’s potential extension to continuous variable distributions would be beneficial. 4- It would enhance the presentation to briefly acknowledge the influence of constraint function smoothness on the proof of Theorem 4.2. Overall, this is a strong theoretical work offering a general framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear promising, though a thorough verification of the proofs is recommended.
