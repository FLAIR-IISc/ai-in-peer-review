This paper presents a unified framework for applying policy gradient methods to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning problems where the objective is a coherent risk measure. The authors generalize previous work, which has largely focused on specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The core technical contribution is a novel formula for the policy gradient of a coherent risk measure, derived by leveraging the dual representation of such measures (as an expectation over a worst-case density) and applying the Envelope Theorem.

The paper develops this framework for two distinct settings:
1.  **Static Risk:** The objective is a coherent risk measure of the total discounted cost. The authors derive a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) and propose a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate this gradient. They show how this general formula elegantly recovers and simplifies known results for specific measures like CVaR and Mean-Semideviation.
2.  **Dynamic Risk:** The objective is a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measure. The authors derive a corresponding policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) in the spirit of the standard policy gradient theorem, relating the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. Based on this, they propose an actor-critic algorithm to solve for a locally optimal policy.

The work is well-situated within the literature, building upon foundational work on coherent risk measures from operations research [2, 26] and connecting to recent NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL for specific objectives [8, 21, 29, 30] and robust MDPs [19, 31].

### Strengths

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant conceptual contribution. By extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a single, principled framework that subsumes and generalizes many prior, more specialized approaches. This unification is a valuable service to the risk-sensitive RL community.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Elegance:** The theoretical development is rigorous and well-executed. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk measures combined with the Envelope Theorem to derive the gradient is a particularly elegant and powerful technique. It allows the authors to re-derive existing gradient formulas (e.g., for CVaR) with greater simplicity and under weaker assumptions, which highlights the strength of their approach.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the problem is formally defined, and the progression from the static risk case to the more complex dynamic risk case is logical and easy to follow. The notation is consistent and the key theorems are stated precisely.

4.  **Significance:** Risk-sensitive RL is an increasingly important area for deploying RL in real-world applications where average-case performance is insufficient and managing downside risk is critical (e.g., finance, autonomous systems). This paper provides a powerful and flexible toolkit that allows researchers and practitioners to design objectives using any coherent risk measure that suits their problem, rather than being limited to a few specific options. The numerical example, though simple, effectively demonstrates why this flexibility is important.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The main weakness of the paper is the empirical validation. The experiments are confined to a single, simple, static "toy" problem (an agent choosing between three assets). While this example successfully illustrates the *motivation* for using different risk measures, it does not provide insight into the performance of the proposed gradient estimation algorithms themselves. There is no analysis of the estimator's variance, sample complexity, or scalability.

2.  **No Evaluation of the Dynamic Case:** A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to developing a new policy gradient theorem and an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem. However, this algorithm is not empirically evaluated at all. The absence of any experiments for the dynamic case leaves the practical utility and performance of this major contribution completely unverified.

3.  **Practical Limitations of the Proposed Algorithms:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case relies on a simulator that can provide multiple next-state samples for any given state-action pair to estimate the function `hθ(x, a)`. This is a more demanding requirement than standard online RL algorithms that learn from single-trajectory rollouts. The authors acknowledge this limitation as a direction for future work, but it curtails the immediate applicability of the proposed method. Similarly, the static algorithm requires solving a convex optimization problem within the sampling loop, which could be computationally intensive for large sample sizes.

### Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The unification of policy gradient methods for the broad class of coherent risk measures is novel, elegant, and important for the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper is technically sound and very clearly written.

The primary drawback is the weak experimental section, which does not adequately support the algorithmic contributions, particularly for the dynamic risk setting. However, given the strength and importance of the theoretical results, this limitation is not fatal. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way from a theoretical standpoint. It provides a foundational framework upon which much future practical and theoretical work can be built.

Therefore, I recommend acceptance. I strongly encourage the authors to acknowledge the experimental limitations in the final version and, if possible, to add at least a simple proof-of-concept experiment for the dynamic actor-critic algorithm to strengthen the paper.
