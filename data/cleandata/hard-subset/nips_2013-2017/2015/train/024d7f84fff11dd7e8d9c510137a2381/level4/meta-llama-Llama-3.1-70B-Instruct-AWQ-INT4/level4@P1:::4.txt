The authors consider the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. The class of risk measures considered in the paper is general, and defined by simple coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The presented approach is based on a dual representation of any coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions. The risk of a random variable (such as a cost) is given by the maximum expected value in the hull.

The paper makes an assumption on the family of coherent risk measures, by bounding the maximum derivatives of the convex hull's borders (i.e., the borders of the hull are M-Lipschitz continuous). Using this assumption, the authors analytically derive the optimal policy parameters (w.r.t the risk measure), as well as a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static case (one-step decision), and the dynamic (or sequential) case.

The paper is well-written and organized. Although I am not familiar with risk-sensitive optimization, I was able to follow most of the explanations. The main contribution of this work is the unified analysis of a general class of risk measures that include most well-known measures. I believe that this is a significant contribution. However, a deeper empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm would be appreciated.

Some comments/questions:

1. The preliminaries section could be clarified, particularly with regards to the roles of Pθ and ξ. It would be helpful to provide more context or explanations for these variables to ensure that readers without a background in risk-sensitive optimization can follow the paper.

2. Equation 2 appears to be a definition of a convex envelope. It would be beneficial to explain why this equation is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1, and how it contributes to the overall analysis.

3. The authors may want to consider adding a comment on how the analysis generalizes to distributions on continuous variables. This would provide additional context and help readers understand the broader applicability of the results.

4. It would be nice to mention in the paper the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in proving Theorem 4.2. This would help readers understand the underlying assumptions and their impact on the results.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that provides a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results seem compelling, but the proofs need to be verified.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a unified analysis of a general class of risk measures, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors derive a general gradient estimation algorithm for both static and dynamic cases, which is a valuable contribution.
* The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to readers without a background in risk-sensitive optimization.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a deeper empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm.
* Some sections, such as the preliminaries, could be clarified to improve readability.
* The authors may want to consider addressing the generalizability of the analysis to distributions on continuous variables.
