

This paper addresses the important problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using coherent risk measures, which have gained traction in finance and operations research. The authors present a unified framework for policy gradient methods applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamically consistent. This generalization significantly extends previous work that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance (e.g., [8, 18, 21, 29, 30]) or restricted to particular system dynamics (e.g., [9, 20]). The paper's key insight is leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to develop gradient-based optimization methods that work for any coherent risk measure, rather than deriving specialized algorithms for each risk measure.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a new gradient formula for static coherent risk measures (Theorem 4.2) that enables sampling-based estimation through a combination of convex programming and likelihood-ratio techniques. Second, it derives a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2) that extends the classic policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings. Third, it proposes practical algorithms for both static and dynamic risk problems, including a two-step procedure combining sampling and convex programming for static risk, and an actor-critic approach for dynamic risk. The authors demonstrate how their framework encompasses previous results as special cases (e.g., CVaR in Section 4.1, mean-semideviation in Section 4.2), providing a more unified and general approach than prior work.

The paper excels in quality, clarity, and significance. The technical arguments are sound and well-supported by theoretical results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) and a numerical illustration in Section 6 showing the importance of flexible risk criteria for decision-making. The paper is clearly written with good organization, and the mathematical exposition is precise. The results are significant because they provide a general framework for risk-sensitive RL that can be applied to a wide range of coherent risk measures without requiring new derivations for each specific measure. This addresses a key limitation of previous work, which focused on narrow classes of risk measures.

The paper's main strength is its originality in unifying risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. While previous work has studied specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.), this paper provides a comprehensive approach applicable to all coherent risk measures. The authors correctly identify that the choice of risk measure should be problem-dependent, and their framework enables practitioners to select the most appropriate risk measure for their specific application. This represents a meaningful advancement over prior specialized approaches.

The paper has minor weaknesses. The connection between the proposed method and related work on robust MDPs (e.g., [19]) could be more explicitly discussed. While the authors mention [19] in the related work section, they could better articulate how their approach relates to the model uncertainty interpretation of coherent risk (which is highlighted in the conclusion). The numerical example in Section 6 is useful but could be strengthened by including quantitative comparisons between the different risk-averse policies.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a substantial theoretical contribution to reinforcement learning with clear practical implications. It addresses a growing interest in risk-sensitive decision making within the RL community, and the proposed framework has the potential to influence future research in this area.

Arguments for acceptance:
1. The paper provides a theoretically sound and general framework for risk-sensitive RL that unifies previous approaches.
2. The technical contributions (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are novel and significant, extending the policy gradient approach to coherent risk measures.
3. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a substantive contribution to the field.
4. The framework has practical utility for practitioners who need to select appropriate risk measures for their specific applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
1. The connection to model uncertainty and robust optimization could be more thoroughly developed, though this is a minor limitation.
2. The numerical example, while illustrative, lacks quantitative comparisons that would better demonstrate the performance advantages of the proposed framework.

The paper satisfies all NIPS quality criteria. It is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), makes a novel contribution by generalizing previous work (originality), and addresses a significant problem with practical relevance (significance). The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

Specific suggestions:
- Theorem 2.1: The notation ξPθ is indeed confusing. I recommend following the notation in Theorem 6.6 of [26] and using ξPθ to denote the re-weighted probability measure, with a clear explanation of this notation when first introduced.
- Section 3: The paper should explicitly clarify that MDPs with Markov risk measures are tractable in principle, but the problem becomes challenging due to the policy parametrization rather than the risk measure itself. This would help readers understand why the authors' approach is necessary.
- The paper could briefly discuss how their framework relates to the "risk envelope" interpretation of coherent risk (Theorem 2.1) and how this connects to model uncertainty, which is mentioned in the conclusion but not elaborated in the main text.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The proposed framework generalizes previous results while providing practical algorithms for implementation. The paper is well-written and technically sound, making a significant contribution to the field that would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
