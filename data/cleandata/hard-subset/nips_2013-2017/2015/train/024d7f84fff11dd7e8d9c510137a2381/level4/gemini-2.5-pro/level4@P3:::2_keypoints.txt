- Presents a unified framework extending policy gradient methods to the full class of coherent risk measures, generalizing beyond variance/CVaR.  
- Core technical contribution: a novel policy-gradient formula derived via the dual representation of coherent risk measures and the Envelope Theorem.  
- Static-risk contribution: Theorem 4.2 gives a general gradient formula and a practical sampling + convex-programming estimator that recovers known results (e.g., CVaR, mean-semi-deviation).  
- Dynamic-risk contribution: Theorem 5.2 provides a policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk and motivates an actor-critic algorithm.  
- Well-situated in literature, building on operations-research foundations and connecting to recent risk-sensitive RL and robust MDP work.  
- Strength — Generality/Unification: provides a single principled framework subsuming many prior specialized approaches.  
- Strength — Technical soundness and elegance: rigorous derivations that simplify and weaken assumptions of prior formulas.  
- Strength — Clarity: exceptionally well-written, well-organized, consistent notation, and precise theorem statements.  
- Strength — Significance: important for real-world risk-sensitive RL applications; numerical example shows the value of flexible risk measures.  
- Weakness — Limited empirical evaluation: experiments confined to a single simple static toy, with no analysis of estimator variance, sample complexity, or scalability.  
- Weakness — No evaluation of dynamic case: the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is not empirically tested, leaving its practical performance unverified.  
- Weakness — Practical algorithmic limitations: dynamic algorithm requires multiple next-state samples per state-action (simulator access), and the static estimator needs solving convex programs inside sampling loops, raising scalability concerns.  
- Overall: strong, novel theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance but urge acknowledgment of experimental limitations and addition of dynamic-case proof-of-concept experiments if possible.
