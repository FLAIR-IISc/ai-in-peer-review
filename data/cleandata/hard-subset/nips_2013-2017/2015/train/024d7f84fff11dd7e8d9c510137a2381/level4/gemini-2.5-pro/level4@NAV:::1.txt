This paper introduces a novel formulation for the class of static coherent risk measures, which enables the analytical derivation of policy gradients for numerous common risk measures. The authors also put forward a general sample-based algorithm for scenarios that lack an analytical solution, providing consistency guarantees for the policy gradient estimators. Finally, the work provides expressions for the policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and quality:**
The unified framework for deriving policy gradients for coherent risk measures is compelling and constitutes a valuable, novel contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive decision-making. A notable limitation, however, is that analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for a subset of risk measures.

The proposed sampling-based approximation method has the potential to make the derived results applicable to general coherent risk measures by obviating the need for analytical saddle-point derivations. However, a significant shortcoming is the paper's lack of empirical evidence to demonstrate that this estimation scheme is practically viable. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients on various problems, for instance with Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), would substantially strengthen the paper.

**Originality:**
To the best of my knowledge, the policy gradients derived in this general form are novel.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity suffers from the breadth of topics it attempts to address within the page limit. The discussion on dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is particularly high-level. The absence of algorithmic details and experimental results for this section prevents the reader from assessing the practical feasibility of the derived gradients. This material appears better suited for a longer journal publication.

A beneficial alternative would be to restructure the paper to focus exclusively on static risk measures. This would yield a more self-contained contribution, allowing for more in-depth explanations and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the rudimentary toy example provided. This is an interesting paper with potentially impactful results, but the lack of thorough experimental validation makes its contributions difficult to fully judge. A narrower scope would also likely improve the clarity deficits imposed by the space constraints.
