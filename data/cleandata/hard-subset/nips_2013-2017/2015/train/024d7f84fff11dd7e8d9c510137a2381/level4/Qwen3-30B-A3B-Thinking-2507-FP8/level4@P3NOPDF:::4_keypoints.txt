- Presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive decision-making using dual representation of coherent risk measures.  
- Leverages convex hull of probability distributions so risk = max expected cost over that hull.  
- Key technical assumption: boundary of the convex hull is M-Lipschitz, enabling closed-form optimal policy parameters.  
- Provides a general gradient estimation algorithm for both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) settings.  
- Broad applicability: encompasses many common risk measures (e.g., CVaR, entropic) under one theory.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, making complex concepts accessible.  
- Major clarity issue: ambiguous notation for P_theta (policy-induced distribution) vs. xi (cost); define all notation and relationships up front.  
- Equation 2 vs. Theorem 2.1: clarify whether Eq.2 is merely the standard dual representation or imposes extra constraints; resolve the claimed "convex envelope" vs. coherence distinction.  
- Generalization concern: analysis assumes discrete domains; discuss extension to continuous distributions and any extra smoothness/density requirements.  
- Theorem 4.2: clarify how smoothness of constraint functions/policy parameterization interacts with the M-Lipschitz assumption for gradient convergence.  
- Empirical gap: include small-scale experiments (e.g., risk-variant CartPole) to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Terminology: replace non-standard "borders of the hull" with "boundary" or "edges" for precision.  
- Explicitly state that the M-Lipschitz boundary assumption is needed to bound the gradient’s Lipschitz constant in the convergence proof.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution that unifies risk measures; sound and well-presented but needs clarification on notation, dual-representation equivalence, continuous extension, and an empirical validation.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
