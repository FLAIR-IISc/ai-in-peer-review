Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static (single-period) and time-consistent dynamic settings. The key idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk measures: any coherent risk can be written as the worst-case expectation over a convex, compact set of reweightings (the risk envelope). For static risk, the authors derive an envelope-theorem-based gradient formula that combines likelihood-ratio sampling with a convex program whose KKT multipliers appear in the gradient. This yields general estimators (including a sample-average approximation when the saddle point is not available in closed form) and recovers known special cases such as CVaR and mean-semideviation. For dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk, they establish a policy gradient theorem that relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and propose an actor-critic method: a critic estimates the robust value function using function approximation, and an actor uses the new gradient formula with reweighted dynamics to update the policy. The work unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL efforts that focused on specific objectives (e.g., variance, CVaR, exponential utility) and connects to robust MDPs.

Quality
The technical development is careful and well motivated. The static gradient formula via the Lagrangian saddle point and envelope theorem is elegant, and the examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) are convincing. The dynamic result extends classic policy-gradient theorems to Markov coherent risk and is conceptually strong. Some proofs are deferred; I did not verify all details. The empirical evaluation is limited; additional experiments would strengthen the claims about practicality and variance/accuracy trade-offs.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, even for readers not steeped in risk-sensitive optimization. However, the preliminaries could more clearly explain the roles of the nominal distribution Pθ (induced by the policy/environment) and the density reweighting ξ in the risk envelope. It would also help to explicitly state how the smoothness assumptions on the envelope constraints enter the proofs (e.g., Theorem 4.2) and to comment on continuous-state/action extensions beyond the finite Ω simplifying assumption.

Originality
The unified treatment of the entire class of coherent risks for both static and dynamic settings is novel and valuable. The new static gradient formula and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appear original and subsume several prior results as special cases.

Significance
Risk-sensitive RL is increasingly important. A general framework that supports many coherent risks and scales via sampling and function approximation is likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners. The requirement of a simulator that can sample multiple next states per state is a practical limitation in some domains, but the approach should still influence subsequent work on risk-sensitive and robust RL.

Strengths
- Unified framework covering a broad class of coherent risk measures (static and dynamic).
- Clear duality-based derivation leading to practical sampling estimators.
- Recovers known results (e.g., CVaR) and provides new general formulas.
- Actor-critic approach for dynamic risk with a principled value-function interpretation (robust MDP connection).

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation; no head-to-head comparisons of variance, bias, and sample efficiency across risks and estimators beyond small illustrations.
- Some assumptions (smoothness and bounded derivatives of constraint functions, requirement of multiple next-state samples) may restrict applicability; discussion could be expanded.
- The preliminaries and notation around Pθ versus ξ could be clearer.

Specific comments/questions
1) Preliminaries: Please clarify the conceptual roles of Pθ and ξ. Pθ is the nominal distribution induced by the policy and dynamics; ξ is an adversarial reweighting selected from the risk envelope U(Pθ). A short example showing Eξ[Z] and how ξ depends on Pθ would help.

2) Equation (2) vs. Theorem 2.1: Eq. (2) specifies U(Pθ) via affine/convex constraints with Slater-type feasibility and differentiability, which is more structured than the existence statement in Theorem 2.1. Please explain why this canonical representation is not unduly restrictive, and provide examples (e.g., CVaR, spectral risk, mean-semideviation) that fit Eq. (2).

3) Smoothness assumptions: Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 rely on differentiability and bounded sensitivity of constraint functions with respect to p. It would be useful to state explicitly where these conditions are invoked (envelope theorem applicability, interchange of limits, consistency of SAA) and to discuss failure modes when these assumptions are weakened.

4) Continuous spaces: You mention that results extend to Lp spaces. Please add a concise summary of the required modifications (e.g., densities vs. mass functions, measurability and compactness of risk envelopes, conditions for SAA consistency, handling of unbounded support).

5) Dynamic sampling requirement: The actor step needs sampling under ξ*-weighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per visited state. Please discuss practical workarounds when only single-trajectory rollouts are available (e.g., importance sampling, off-policy corrections, kernel-based estimators) and any impact on variance.

6) Empirics: A more substantial experimental section comparing (i) closed-form vs. SAA gradient estimators for CVaR and other risks, and (ii) dynamic actor-critic with different risk envelopes, would help assess variance, bias, and sample efficiency.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical framework; unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive RL results.
- Clear algorithms for both static and dynamic coherent risks.
- Potentially high impact on risk-aware RL and connections to robust MDPs.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical validation is minimal.
- Some technical assumptions and practical requirements may limit immediate applicability; more discussion would help.

Overall assessment
A strong theory paper providing a unified approach to risk-sensitive decision making with coherent risk measures. The results are compelling; I recommend acceptance provided the authors clarify the preliminaries, better articulate the role of smoothness assumptions, and add brief guidance on continuous spaces and practical sampling constraints. Proofs should be carefully checked.
