The authors analyze the risk-averse Markov decision process setting with static and dynamically consistent risk measures. The main contribution is to show the form of the gradient for both the dynamic and static setting.

The paper is a generalization of previous results, which focused specifically on Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). This extension represents a minor but useful contribution, as it provides a unified approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that generalizes and extends previous results.

The paper is well-organized, well-written, and easy to follow. The results are correct as far as I can tell. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to coherent risk measures and their application to Markov decision processes. The technical development is sound, and the paper includes several examples to illustrate the utility of the proposed approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate how their approach can be applied to various risk measures, including CVaR and mean-semideviation. The paper also includes a numerical illustration that highlights the importance of flexibility in designing risk criteria.

However, the paper's contribution is not groundbreaking, and the results are largely an extension of existing work. The policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, for example, is a natural extension of the well-known policy gradient theorem for expected return.

Minor comments:

* In Theorem 2.1, the notation ξPθ is confusing. I suggest referring to Theorem 6.6 in [26] and using the same notation.
* In line 141, there is a typo: "risk enevlop[e]" should be "risk envelope".
* In Section 3, it may be worth pointing out that Markov decision processes (MDPs) with Markov risk measures are very tractable. The reason the problem in the paper is not tractable is because of the policy parametrization and not because of the risk measure.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a useful extension of existing results on risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.
* The authors present a clear and concise introduction to coherent risk measures and their application to MDPs.
* The technical development is sound, and the paper includes several examples to illustrate the utility of the proposed approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's contribution is not groundbreaking, and the results are largely an extension of existing work.
* The policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures is a natural extension of the well-known policy gradient theorem for expected return.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, as it provides a useful contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, the authors should address the minor comments and provide additional context to help readers understand the significance of their contribution.
