This paper studies risk-sensitive policy optimization in MDPs for the broad class of coherent risk measures. The key idea is to exploit the dual representation of any coherent risk as the worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope U(Pθ) of reweighted densities ξ with respect to the nominal distribution Pθ. Building on this representation, the authors derive (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risks ρ(Z) under an explicit convex-program description of the envelope, and (ii) a policy-gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risks via a Bellman-type decomposition. For static risk, they show how to estimate gradients using sampling combined with convex programming, including special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) that recover and generalize prior results. For dynamic risk, they propose an actor-critic style algorithm that approximates the risk-sensitive value function and uses the new gradient expression to update the policy.

Relation to prior work: The paper unifies and extends several strands in risk-aware RL. It generalizes risk-specific policy-gradient methods such as mean-variance (e.g., Tamar et al., ICML 2012; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, NIPS 2013) and CVaR (e.g., Chow & Ghavamzadeh, NIPS 2014; Tamar et al., AAAI 2015), and connects to robust MDPs and dynamic coherent risk (Ruszczyński; Osogami, NIPS 2012; Petrik & Subramanian, UAI 2012). The dynamic formulation follows the time-consistent Markov coherent risk framework (Ruszczyński 2010) and yields a policy-gradient theorem analogous to the classic expected-return result (Sutton et al., NIPS 1999; Konda & Tsitsiklis, NIPS 2000).

Strengths:
- A unified gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risks, covering both static and dynamic settings, is novel and impactful.
- The static gradient formula via the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick is elegant and subsumes known results (e.g., CVaR) with weaker assumptions and simpler derivations.
- The actor-critic extension for dynamic Markov coherent risk is a substantive contribution that fills a gap beyond risk-specific treatments.
- The analysis is careful about assumptions (finite probability spaces; smoothness of envelope constraints) and provides a consistent SAA estimator when the saddle point is not analytical.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Empirical evaluation is minimal. Beyond a didactic illustration, there is no systematic assessment of estimator variance, sample complexity, or scalability (e.g., cost of solving a convex program per batch/iteration).
- Practicality for model-free settings is limited: the dynamic algorithm requires a simulator that can generate multiple next states per state and sample from ξ-weighted transitions.
- Clarity issues in the preliminaries: the interplay between Pθ (the nominal distribution induced by policy parameters) and ξ (the adversarial density within the risk envelope) is not always made explicit, which can confuse readers unfamiliar with dual representations.
- Assumptions on the envelope (affine/convex constraints with bounded derivatives in p) are reasonable for popular risks, but the paper could better motivate their necessity and discuss edge cases.
- The finite-space restriction is stated as “for ease of exposition,” but a brief sketch for the continuous case (e.g., Lp settings, absolute continuity requirements, and how SAA/convex programs translate) would strengthen the paper.

Quality:
- The technical development appears sound and builds on well-established convex duality and policy-gradient techniques. The general gradient expressions and SAA consistency proposition are valuable. As the proofs are nontrivial, careful verification by the committee is warranted.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized and mostly clear, but Section 2 could better disambiguate Pθ versus ξ and the dependence of U(Pθ) on the state in the dynamic setting. Also, explicitly stating the role of constraint smoothness in Theorem 4.2 and the gradient derivations would aid readability.

Originality:
- High. Providing a unified treatment for coherent risks (static and time-consistent dynamic) and deriving general policy-gradient formulas is a significant step beyond risk-specific approaches.

Significance:
- High. Coherent risks are widely adopted in finance/OR, and a general, sample-based gradient framework can influence risk-sensitive RL research and practice. The dynamic actor-critic result is especially relevant for large MDPs.

Specific comments and questions:
1) Preliminaries: Please clarify the roles of Pθ and ξ. Pθ is the policy-induced nominal distribution over outcomes or transitions, while ξ reweights Pθ within the risk envelope U(Pθ) to form worst-case expectations. Emphasize that ξ depends on Pθ and, in the dynamic case, on the state x via Pθ(·|x).

2) Equation (2) vs. Theorem 2.1: Eq. (2) provides an explicit, tractable convex program for the envelope U(Pθ) with affine/convex constraints and a Slater condition, whereas Theorem 2.1 guarantees existence of some convex, compact envelope. Please clarify why the structural and smoothness assumptions in Eq. (2) are stronger than existence and are needed to apply the envelope theorem and obtain consistent SAA gradients.

3) Continuous variables: A short subsection or appendix sketching the extension to continuous spaces (e.g., Lp spaces, Radon–Nikodym derivatives, absolute continuity of ξ with respect to Pθ, and how the convex program and SAA adapt) would make the contribution more broadly applicable.

4) Smoothness and Theorem 4.2: Explicitly state in the main text how bounded derivatives of the constraint maps with respect to p (Assumption 2.2) enter the application of the envelope theorem and the gradient formula.

5) Practical aspects: Discuss computational overhead of solving the convex program per batch/iteration and any warm-start strategies. For the dynamic setting, comment on feasibility with single-trajectory data and possible variance-reduction/importance-sampling strategies (which you already mention as future work).

Pros for acceptance:
- Unified, principled gradient framework for coherent risks (static and dynamic).
- Generalizes and simplifies several prior risk-sensitive RL results.
- Solid theoretical development with a clear path to algorithms.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation; no large-scale experiments or variance/sample complexity study.
- Practical constraints (requirement of simulators and multiple next-state samples) may limit immediate applicability.
- Some clarity gaps in preliminaries and assumptions.

Overall: A strong theory paper providing a general approach to risk-sensitive decision making. The results are compelling, and, pending proof verification, merit publication. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the preliminaries and assumptions would further improve the paper.
