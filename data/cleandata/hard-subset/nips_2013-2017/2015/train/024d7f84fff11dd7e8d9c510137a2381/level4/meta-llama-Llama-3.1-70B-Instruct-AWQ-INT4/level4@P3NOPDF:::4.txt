The authors address the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure, considering a general class of risk measures defined by simple coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The approach presented is based on a dual representation of any coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions. Specifically, the risk of a random variable, such as a cost, is given by the maximum expected value in the hull.

The paper makes an assumption on the family of coherent risk measures by bounding the maximum derivatives of the convex hull's borders, which implies that the borders of the hull are M-Lipschitz continuous. Using this assumption, the authors analytically derive the optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure and propose a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static case (one-step decision) and the dynamic (or sequential) case.

The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to readers unfamiliar with risk-sensitive optimization. The main contribution of this work is the unified analysis of a general class of risk measures that includes most well-known measures. This is a significant contribution, as it provides a comprehensive framework for risk-sensitive decision-making.

However, a deeper empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm would be appreciated to further demonstrate its effectiveness and robustness. Some minor clarifications and comments are also suggested to enhance the paper's clarity and completeness.

Firstly, the preliminaries section could be improved for better clarity, particularly regarding the roles of P_{\theta} and \ksi. A more detailed explanation or additional examples might help readers understand these concepts more easily.

Secondly, it is unclear why Equation 2 is considered more restrictive than Theorem 2.1, as it appears to be simply the definition of a convex envelope. A brief comment or explanation would be helpful to justify this distinction.

Thirdly, it would be beneficial to discuss how the analysis generalizes to distributions on continuous variables, as this would broaden the applicability of the proposed approach.

Lastly, mentioning the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in proving Theorem 4.2 would provide additional insight into the theoretical foundations of the paper.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that provides a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results seem compelling, but the proofs need to be verified to ensure their correctness. With some minor revisions to address the suggested comments, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
