

The paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The authors generalize previous risk-sensitive RL approaches by extending policy gradient techniques to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific instances like variance or CVaR. This is achieved through a dual representation of coherent risk measures as the maximum expectation over a convex set of probability distributions (the risk envelope), which enables the derivation of gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk measures. The key contributions include: (1) a general gradient formula for static coherent risk that facilitates sampling-based estimation, (2) a convex programming-based algorithm for gradient estimation with consistency guarantees, and (3) a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that enables actor-critic style algorithms for dynamic risk. The work unifies and generalizes several prior approaches to risk-sensitive RL, demonstrating that previous results for specific risk measures are special cases of their framework.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from theoretical foundations to practical algorithms. The authors provide thorough theoretical analysis, including consistency results for their gradient estimators, and illustrate their approach with a compelling numerical example showing how different risk preferences lead to distinct policy choices in financial applications. The technical content is sound, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a principled, unified approach to risk-sensitive RL.

However, the paper would benefit from several improvements. First, the preliminaries section could be clarified to better distinguish between the probability measure P_θ (parameterized by the policy) and the risk envelope ξ (the dual variable representing the worst-case distribution). The relationship between these two concepts is crucial for understanding the dual representation in Theorem 2.1, but the current exposition is somewhat confusing.

Second, the authors claim that their Assumption 2.2 is "more restrictive" than Theorem 2.1, but this is misleading. Theorem 2.1 establishes the dual representation for all coherent risk measures, while Assumption 2.2 specifies the form of the risk envelope that makes the optimization tractable for their gradient approach. The paper should clarify that they are not restricting the class of risk measures, but rather specifying a convenient representation for the risk envelope that enables their algorithmic development.

Third, the paper should address how their approach extends to continuous probability spaces. While the authors mention that their results can be extended to Lp-normed spaces, they do not provide sufficient details about this extension, which is important for practical applications.

Fourth, the role of constraint smoothness in Theorem 4.2 deserves more explicit discussion. The authors assume smoothness of the constraints for their consistency results, but this connection is not clearly articulated in the main text.

Finally, while the numerical example is well-chosen, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation of the gradient estimation algorithm would strengthen the paper. The authors should include comparisons with existing methods on standard RL benchmarks, particularly for dynamic risk measures, to better demonstrate the practical advantages of their approach.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical contribution that provides a valuable unification of risk-sensitive RL. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by generalizing to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific instances. The theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported, and the numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical implications of the framework.

Pro: 
- Unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL
- Generalizes previous results for specific risk measures
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with consistency guarantees
- Clear presentation of technical material
- Compelling numerical example demonstrating risk preference flexibility

Con:
- Preliminaries section needs clarification on the relationship between P_θ and ξ
- Misleading claim about Assumption 2.2 being "more restrictive" than Theorem 2.1
- Insufficient discussion of extension to continuous probability spaces
- Limited empirical evaluation beyond the illustrative example
- Inadequate explanation of constraint smoothness requirements

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a theoretically sound and practically implementable framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the theoretical advances are substantial and would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
