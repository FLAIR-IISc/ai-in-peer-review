This paper addresses the problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending policy gradient methods to the broad class of coherent risk measures, encompassing both static and time-consistent dynamic settings. The core contribution lies in deriving gradient formulas for these risk measures, generalizing previous work focused on specific instances like CVaR.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing literature in risk-sensitive RL, referencing key works such as those on mean-variance optimization [16], CVaR [18, 29, 21, 8, 30], and coherent risk measures [2]. It correctly positions its contribution as a unifying framework, allowing re-derivation of previous results within a more general context. The connection to robust MDPs [19] and the discussion of related optimization techniques [25, 26, 31] demonstrate a good understanding of the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** Extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant step towards a more flexible and broadly applicable risk-sensitive RL framework.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) appears technically sound, leveraging established results like the envelope theorem [17].
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The notation is generally clear, and the authors provide sufficient context for understanding the key concepts.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The development of sampling-based gradient estimation algorithms (Section 4.3 and 5) addresses a critical challenge in applying these methods to large-scale problems.
*   **Illustrative Example:** The numerical illustration (Section 6) effectively demonstrates the potential of the approach and highlights the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure based on the specific problem characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the generalization to coherent risk measures is valuable, the core ideas are built upon existing policy gradient techniques. The improvement, though solid, is somewhat incremental.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The algorithm relies on solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) at each iteration, which may become computationally expensive for very large state spaces. The paper acknowledges this challenge but does not provide detailed discussion of scalability or alternative approximation techniques.
*   **Assumptions:** The approach relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 and 4.1) regarding the structure of the risk envelope and the policy parametrization. The sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions is not thoroughly explored.
*  **Limited Empirical Evaluation**: The numerical illustration is helpful but limited in scope. A more extensive empirical evaluation on benchmark MDPs would strengthen the claims of the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated generalization of existing risk-sensitive RL methods.
*   The derived gradient formulas provide a valuable theoretical contribution to the field.
*   The proposed sampling-based algorithms offer a practical approach to applying these methods to large-scale problems.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to understand, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is somewhat incremental, building upon existing policy gradient techniques.
*   The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms may limit their applicability to very large problems.
*   The reliance on several assumptions requires further investigation and discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and worthwhile contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper provides a valuable generalization of existing methods and offers practical algorithms for tackling complex problems. While the contribution is not groundbreaking, it is significant enough to warrant acceptance, especially given the clear writing and theoretical soundness.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend addressing the following points in a revision:

*   Clarify the notation ξPθ (Theorem 2.1) to align with standard references [26].
*   Expand the discussion in Section 3 to explicitly state that the intractability arises from policy parametrization, not inherently from the risk measure itself.
*   Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms and potential strategies for improving scalability.
*   Consider conducting a more extensive empirical evaluation on benchmark MDPs to demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
