This paper presents novel methods for estimating policy gradients in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning problems utilizing coherent risk measures. A key contribution is extending existing techniques, previously focused on specific risk measures, to the more general class of coherent risk measures, thereby providing a unifying framework. The proposed algorithms are illustrated with a simple financial example, demonstrating the algorithm's risk-averse behavior and flexibility across different risk types.

The motivation is clearly articulated, and the problem formulation and algorithmic development logically follow from this motivation and relevant prior work. While the experimental validation is currently limited to a static risk setting, the results support the quality of the proposed approach and its ability to capture desired risk-averse behaviors.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-written. The equations are clearly presented and justified, and a comprehensive set of relevant references is provided. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of both reinforcement learning and coherent risk theory. The theoretical foundations appear solid, and the proposed gradient formulas are a valuable contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper exhibits excellent clarity. The concepts are explained in a concise and accessible manner, making it easy to follow the line of reasoning. The organization is logical, and the notation is consistent throughout. A superbly written paper provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce its results, which this paper mostly achieves, although more detail on the implementation would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel approach to risk-sensitive RL. Extending policy gradient and actor-critic methods to the general framework of coherent risk measures is a non-trivial undertaking. The proposed algorithms build upon existing techniques but offer a significant generalization and a unifying perspective. The originality is sufficient for NIPS acceptance, particularly given the breadth of the generalization. It would be helpful for the authors to more explicitly highlight how their work differs from the robust MDP approach of [19], beyond simply stating that it doesn’t scale.

**Significance:** Risk-sensitive RL is a crucial area of research with broad applicability. This paper makes a significant contribution by providing practical and theoretically grounded algorithms for addressing this problem under the coherent risk framework. The proposed approach has the potential to be used by researchers and practitioners alike to develop more robust and reliable decision-making systems. The demonstration of flexibility in risk preferences through the illustrative example further highlights the practical significance of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a unifying framework for risk-sensitive RL based on coherent risk measures.
*   Derives novel policy gradient formulas applicable to a wide class of risk measures.
*   Presents a clear and well-motivated approach.
*   Offers potential for practical applications in various domains.
*   The paper is technically sound and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental validation is limited to a simple static risk setting.
*   The scalability of the proposed algorithms to large-scale problems is not fully demonstrated.
*   The connection to robust MDPs could be further clarified.
*   The algorithm's performance compared to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms (e.g., those utilizing CVaR) isn’t shown in a direct comparison.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical results are strong, the algorithms are clearly presented, and the potential for practical impact is significant. Addressing the weaknesses identified above (particularly expanding the experimental validation) could further strengthen the paper. I believe that this work meets the criteria for NIPS and will be of interest to a broad audience within the machine learning community.
