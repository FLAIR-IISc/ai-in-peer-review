The paper proposes a new formula for the class of static coherent risks, from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. Additionally, it presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where no analytical solution is available and provides consistency guarantees for the policy gradient estimates. The paper also derives expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a valuable and novel contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, it is essential to note that analytical solutions of the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for some risk measures. The proposed sample-based approximation scheme offers a promising approach to apply the derived results to general coherent risk measures by avoiding the need to derive analytical forms of the saddle points.

A significant limitation of the paper is the lack of empirical evidence to support the viability of the proposed estimation scheme in practice. An experimental comparison between using sample-based policy gradients and analytical ones in different problems, such as Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), would be extremely helpful in assessing the practicality of the proposed approach.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients in this general form are novel and contribute to the existing literature on risk-sensitive decision-making.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is hindered by the number of different issues addressed within the limited page constraint. Specifically, the dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level, lacking algorithmic details and experimental evaluations. This makes it challenging for the reader to judge whether the derived policy gradients yield approaches that are viable in practice.

In my opinion, the content related to dynamic risk policy gradients might be more suitable for a longer journal publication. Alternatively, the authors could consider splitting the paper and focusing on static risk measures, allowing for a more standalone presentation with more comprehensive details and empirical evaluations. The current submission includes a basic toy example, but a more extensive evaluation is necessary to assess the practical implications of the proposed approach.

In conclusion, the paper presents interesting and potentially generalizable results. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluations makes it challenging to fully appreciate the significance of the contributions. A narrower focus, potentially with a more extensive evaluation, could improve the clarity and overall impact of the paper.
