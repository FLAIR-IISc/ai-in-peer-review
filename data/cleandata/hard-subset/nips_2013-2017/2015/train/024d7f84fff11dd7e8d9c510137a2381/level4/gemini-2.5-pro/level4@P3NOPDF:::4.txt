This paper addresses the problem of policy optimization for a general class of coherent risk measures, which are defined by standard axioms such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The authors' approach is founded on the dual representation of coherent risk measures, where the risk is expressed as the maximum expected value over a convex set of probability distributions. A central assumption is that the boundaries of this convex set are M-Lipschitz continuous. Leveraging this assumption, the paper derives the structure of the optimal policy and proposes a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (single-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision-making problems.

**Strengths**

The primary contribution of this work is its unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive optimization. By analyzing a broad class of coherent risk measures, the paper generalizes many existing results and provides a common lens through which to view well-known measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). This unification is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and generally easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with the nuances of risk-sensitive optimization. The theoretical results appear strong and provide a principled approach to a challenging and important problem in decision-making under uncertainty.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the theoretical contributions are significant, the empirical validation could be strengthened. A more thorough experimental analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm, perhaps comparing its performance, stability, and computational cost across different risk measures and problem domains, would substantially enhance the paper's impact.

As is common with highly theoretical papers, the proofs require careful verification by an expert in this specific subfield.

**Questions and Suggestions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarity in Preliminaries:** The distinction and relationship between the policy-induced distribution P_{\theta} and the random variable \xi were initially confusing. It would be helpful to clarify if \xi represents the underlying source of randomness (e.g., from the environment) and how P_{\theta} acts upon or transforms it.

2.  **Equation 2 and Theorem 2.1:** Equation 2 appears to be a standard representation related to the Fenchel conjugate or a convex envelope. Could the authors elaborate on how this relates to the more general dual representation in Theorem 2.1? Specifically, does the formulation in Equation 2 introduce any restrictions not present in the general theorem? A brief explanation would help readers understand the nuances of the theoretical setup.

3.  **Generalization to Continuous Variables:** The analysis seems focused on discrete distributions. It would be valuable to include a discussion on the extent to which the theoretical results, particularly the gradient estimation algorithm, generalize to scenarios involving continuous random variables. Are there additional technical challenges or assumptions required for the continuous case?

4.  **Role of Smoothness in Theorem 4.2:** The proof of Theorem 4.2 likely relies on the smoothness of the constraint functions. It would be beneficial to explicitly mention this requirement in the main body of the paper when discussing the theorem, as it is a key condition for the result to hold.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution by providing a general and unified approach to risk-sensitive policy optimization. The results are compelling and could serve as a foundation for future work in this area. While the paper would benefit from a more extensive empirical evaluation and some clarifications in the presentation, its theoretical novelty and importance are clear.
