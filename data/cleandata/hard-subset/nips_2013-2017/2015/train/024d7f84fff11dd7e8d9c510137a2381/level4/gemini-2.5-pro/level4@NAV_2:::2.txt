This paper introduces a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using policy gradient methods. The core contribution is the extension of policy gradient algorithms to the entire class of coherent risk measures, unifying and generalizing previous work that focused on specific measures such as variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors derive novel policy gradient formulas for both static coherent risk measures and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. For the static case, the proposed algorithm combines sampling with convex programming. For the dynamic case, the authors develop a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The work's utility is demonstrated on a portfolio optimization problem, showcasing the flexibility of the approach in handling different risk preferences and return distributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution by unifying risk-sensitive policy gradient methods under the umbrella of coherent risk theory. This provides a principled and general approach to a problem previously addressed with measure-specific solutions.
- The derivation of the gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) using the envelope theorem is elegant and provides a clear basis for the proposed algorithms.
- The extension to dynamic Markov coherent risk measures and the development of a corresponding risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) and actor-critic algorithm is a notable advancement for sequential decision-making problems.
- The motivation is very clear, and the paper effectively positions its contributions within the existing literature, acknowledging related work in both RL and stochastic programming.

**Weaknesses:**
- The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. While the provided financial trading example effectively illustrates the benefits of flexibility in risk-measure selection for a static problem, it does not demonstrate the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk case. An evaluation on a sequential decision-making problem, even a simple one, would substantially strengthen the paper's claims regarding the dynamic risk formulation.

**Detailed Evaluation:**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations appear correct and are built upon solid foundations in convex optimization and stochastic programming (e.g., the dual representation of coherent risk measures). The claims are well-supported by the presented theorems and proofs. The work is complete in its treatment of both static and dynamic risk settings.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear introduction to coherent risk measures before systematically building up from the static gradient formula to the more complex dynamic, actor-critic framework. The problem formulation is precise, and the notation is consistent. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrate the application of the main theoretical result.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on prior work in policy gradients and risk-sensitive control, this is the first paper to develop a general policy gradient algorithm for the entire class of coherent risk measures in an RL context. This generalization is non-trivial and represents a significant conceptual leap beyond previous works that were tailored to individual risk measures. The proposed algorithms are novel.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. Risk-sensitivity is a critical aspect of deploying RL systems in real-world applications where average-case performance is insufficient. This paper provides researchers and practitioners with a powerful and flexible toolkit for optimizing policies under a wide variety of rational risk preferences. The unified framework could become a standard approach for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods, inspiring further work in this area.
