This paper presents methods for estimating policy gradients in risk-sensitive problems with coherent risk measures, and proposes reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms based on them. The paper extends existing methods for specific risk measures to the general class of coherent risk measures, providing a unified view. The algorithm is illustrated with a toy example in the finance domain, where each asset has its own return distribution.

The motivation is very clear, and the problem formulation and algorithms follow naturally from this motivation and previous works. The experiment is relatively simple, as it is done in only a static risk problem. However, the results seem to support the quality of the algorithm in terms of its risk-averse behavior as well as the flexibility in different risk types.

Quality: The paper is well-written, and the equations are technically sound. All relevant references are given, demonstrating the authors' thorough understanding of the field.

Clarity: The clarity of this paper is well above average. The authors have done an excellent job of explaining complex concepts and providing sufficient background information for readers unfamiliar with the topic.

Originality: The paper proposes new algorithms for risk-sensitive RL problems based on related works on risk-sensitive planning and RL. The extension to the proposed approach is not straightforward, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field. The originality of this paper seems sufficient to warrant publication.

Significance: Risk-sensitive RL is an interesting and important problem, and this paper provides significant contributions. The authors present clear algorithms that solve risk-sensitive RL with coherent risk measures and demonstrate the behavior of trained policies with different risk settings on a static risk problem. The proposed approach is clearly motivated by the authors, and a natural extension to existing algorithms and problems (policy gradient, actor-critic, and coherent risk measure optimization).

The paper's main strength is its ability to provide a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL problems. The authors have successfully extended existing methods to the general class of coherent risk measures, making the paper a valuable contribution to the field. The experimental results, although simple, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

One potential area for improvement is the experiment section, which could be expanded to include more complex scenarios or comparisons with other existing methods. However, this is a minor criticism, and the paper's overall quality and significance make it a strong candidate for publication.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL problems, extending existing methods to the general class of coherent risk measures.
* The authors present clear algorithms that solve risk-sensitive RL with coherent risk measures and demonstrate the behavior of trained policies with different risk settings.
* The paper is well-written, and the equations are technically sound, demonstrating the authors' thorough understanding of the field.
* The originality of the paper seems sufficient to warrant publication, and the proposed approach is clearly motivated by the authors.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experiment section is relatively simple and could be expanded to include more complex scenarios or comparisons with other existing methods.
