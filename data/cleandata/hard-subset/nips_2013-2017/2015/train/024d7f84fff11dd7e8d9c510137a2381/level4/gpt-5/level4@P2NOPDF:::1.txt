The paper introduces a new representation for static coherent risk measures that enables closed-form derivations of policy gradients for many commonly used risks. It also proposes a general sampling-based algorithm for cases without analytic solutions and establishes consistency guarantees for the resulting gradient estimators. Finally, it provides expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is compelling and constitutes a valuable, novel contribution to risk-sensitive decision making. However, analytical solutions to the Lagrangian saddle-point problems are available only for certain risk measures. The proposed sampling-based approximation could broaden applicability by avoiding the need for explicit saddle-point solutions. That said, the paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating that the estimator is practical or competitive. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients across multiple problems—e.g., for CVaR—would be especially informative, helping to assess accuracy, sample efficiency, and computational cost.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, deriving policy gradients for coherent risk measures in this general form is novel.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but the eight-page format attempts to cover too many topics, which impairs clarity. In particular, the treatment of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is quite high level; the absence of algorithmic details and empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical viability of the proposed approaches. This material may be better suited for a longer journal article where the necessary details and experiments can be provided.

As an alternative, a narrower focus on static risk measures would allow the paper to be more self-contained, offer clearer exposition of the main technical contributions, and include a more comprehensive empirical evaluation—going beyond the simple toy example currently presented. Such an evaluation should, at minimum, compare sample-based and analytical gradients when both are available (e.g., for CVaR), and test the proposed method on a diverse set of tasks to demonstrate robustness and practical impact.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with potentially broad applicability. However, the lack of substantive experiments and limited algorithmic detail make it difficult to judge the practical effectiveness of the proposed estimators. A tighter scope or additional empirical validation would significantly strengthen the submission.
