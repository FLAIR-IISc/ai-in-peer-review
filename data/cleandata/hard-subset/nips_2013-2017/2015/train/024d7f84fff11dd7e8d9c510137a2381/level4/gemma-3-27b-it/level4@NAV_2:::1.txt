The paper proposes a new formula for policy gradients applicable to the class of static coherent risk measures, from which gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where analytical solutions are unavailable and provides consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper derives expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

This work presents a significant and novel contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature through the unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures. The ability to generalize across this class of risk measures is a valuable advancement. However, analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for a subset of risk measures. 

The proposed sample-based approximation scheme offers a promising path to applying the derived results to general coherent risk measures, circumventing the need for analytical saddle-point derivations. Nevertheless, the paper currently lacks empirical validation demonstrating the viability of this estimation scheme in practice. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients—perhaps using CVaR as a case study—would be highly beneficial.

The originality of the derived policy gradients, in this general form, appears strong. 

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is somewhat hampered by the breadth of topics addressed within the page limit. The presentation of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is particularly high-level, and the absence of algorithm details and experimental evaluations makes it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the derived gradients. The material might be better suited for a more extensive journal publication. Alternatively, a narrower focus—perhaps concentrating on static risk measures—could improve clarity and allow for a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, building on the toy example currently presented. Overall, this is a promising paper with potentially generally applicable results, but the lack of extensive experimental validation makes a full assessment challenging. A more focused presentation could also enhance its impact.
