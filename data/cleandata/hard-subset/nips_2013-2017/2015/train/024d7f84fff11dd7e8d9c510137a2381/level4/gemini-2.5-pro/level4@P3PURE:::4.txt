This paper considers the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. The class of risk measures addressed is general, defined by standard coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The proposed approach is based on the dual representation of any coherent risk measure as a convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random cost is given by the maximum expected value within this hull.

The authors' analysis relies on a key assumption: that the boundaries of this convex hull are M-Lipschitz continuous. Under this assumption, they analytically derive the optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure and present a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static (one-step) and the dynamic (sequential) decision-making settings.

The paper is well-written and organized. Despite my limited familiarity with risk-sensitive optimization, I found most of the explanations easy to follow. The main contribution of this work is the unified analysis of a broad class of risk measures, which includes many well-known examples. I believe this is a significant contribution. Overall, this is a strong theory paper that provides a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results seem compelling, although the proofs require careful verification. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm.

**Comments and Questions:**

1.  The preliminaries section could be clearer. I was confused about the distinct roles of `P_{\theta}` and `\ksi`.
2.  Equation 2 appears to be the definition of a convex envelope. Could the authors clarify why this formulation is more restrictive than the representation in Theorem 2.1?
3.  The paper would benefit from a discussion on how the analysis generalizes to distributions over continuous variables.
4.  Given its importance in proving Theorem 4.2, it would be valuable to mention the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in the main body of the paper.
