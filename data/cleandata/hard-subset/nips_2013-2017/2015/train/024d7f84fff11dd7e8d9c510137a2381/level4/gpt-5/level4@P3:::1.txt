Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the entire class of coherent risk measures, for both static and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) settings. For static risk, the authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk via risk envelopes and use the envelope theorem to derive a general gradient formula expressed at a Lagrangian saddle point. This yields simple, likelihood-ratio style estimators and recovers prior results as special cases (e.g., CVaR and mean-semideviation). When the saddle point is not available in closed form, they propose a sampling-and-convex-programming procedure and prove consistency of the resulting gradient estimator.

For dynamic risk, the paper considers Markov coherent risk measures and the associated risk-sensitive Bellman equation. It presents a policy gradient theorem that expresses the gradient as an expectation under a ξ-weighted transition kernel with a modified stage cost, enabling an actor-critic style algorithm. The critic relies on the equivalence between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs, building on approximation methods for robust MDPs.

The work unifies and generalizes strands of risk-sensitive RL: mean-variance and related criteria [21, 29], CVaR in static settings [8, 30], exponential utilities [6], and dynamic coherent risk/robust MDPs [19, 24, 31]. The proposed framework is broader than stochastic programming approaches to coherent risk [25, 26], as it addresses settings where policy parameters affect transition dynamics, not only rewards.

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: A single gradient formula for coherent static risk that recovers known special cases (e.g., CVaR) and extends to others (e.g., mean-semideviation, spectral measures).
- General estimator: A practical two-step procedure (sampling plus convex programming) with consistency guarantees for cases without closed-form saddle points.
- Dynamic risk: A policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that connects to robust MDPs and suggests an actor-critic algorithmic template.
- Theoretical clarity: Assumptions are reasonable for many coherent risks; the derivations cleanly use the dual representation and the envelope theorem; the CVaR gradient derivation is simpler and more general than earlier proofs.

Weaknesses:
- Practical viability not demonstrated: The core sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator, while theoretically appealing, lacks empirical evaluation. A comparison between the proposed SAA-based gradient and known analytical gradients (e.g., for CVaR) is critical to assess bias/variance and sample complexity in practice.
- Dynamic case is high-level: The actor-critic method is described conceptually, but important details (how to obtain ξ-weighted trajectories efficiently, how many samples per state are needed, stabilization, function approximation choices, and convergence behavior) are deferred to the supplement. No experiments are provided to evaluate the dynamic-risk gradient estimator or the actor-critic algorithm.
- Computational burden and scalability: The method requires solving a convex program to obtain primal-dual solutions (ξ, λ) for each batch (and for each state in the dynamic case). Complexity, memory footprint, and wall-clock costs are not quantified. The requirement to sample multiple next states per visited state may be restrictive for online RL.
- Assumption and scope clarity: The approach requires explicit, differentiable risk envelopes (Assumption 2.2). A clearer discussion of how to instantiate envelopes for commonly used risks (beyond CVaR and mean-semideviation), and how to handle continuous spaces and function approximation in the static case, would help practitioners.

Quality:
The theoretical development is sound and well-grounded in convex analysis and policy-gradient methodology. The consistency result for the SAA-based estimator is useful. However, the paper’s empirical support is very limited (one toy portfolio example not exercising the dynamic algorithm), making it hard to judge robustness, variance, and efficiency of the proposed estimators in realistic RL settings.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally well written, but it is dense for an 8-page conference format. Section 5’s dynamic-risk results are presented at a high level; key algorithmic details and implementation guidance appear only in the supplement. A tighter focus on static risk (with richer experiments) or more complete presentation of the dynamic method would improve readability. Minor presentation issues (e.g., a few typos and incomplete references) should be addressed.

Originality:
High. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first unified policy-gradient treatment for the full class of coherent risk measures, and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears novel.

Significance:
Potentially high. A general recipe for risk-sensitive gradients could influence a broad set of RL applications where tail behavior and time consistency matter. That said, the immediate practical impact is unclear without empirical evidence on variance, sample efficiency, and computational cost.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide experiments comparing the SAA-based gradient to analytical gradients (e.g., CVaR) across several problems, reporting bias/variance and sample complexity vs. N and problem size.
- Include dynamic-risk experiments (e.g., gridworlds, control tasks) comparing against risk-neutral baselines and CVaR/mean-variance methods, with ablations on function approximation and the number of next-state samples per state.
- Quantify per-iteration computational cost for the convex program and overall runtime, and discuss practical solver choices. Consider warm-starts or incremental updates of (ξ, λ).
- Elaborate on how to specify risk envelopes for common coherent risks (including spectral measures) and discuss extensions to continuous state/action spaces.
- Clarify conditions under which stationary Markov policies suffice for static risk, or suggest a practical augmented-state approach when needed.
- Improve exposition of the actor-critic algorithm: provide pseudocode, sampling procedure for ξ-weighted transitions, and variance reduction strategies (e.g., baselines, importance sampling).

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contribution unifying risk-sensitive policy gradients for coherent risks.
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem connecting coherent risk and robust MDPs.
- Clear generalization of known special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation).

Cons against acceptance:
- Lack of empirical validation for the key SAA estimator and the dynamic actor-critic method.
- Insufficient algorithmic detail for the dynamic case in the main paper.
- Unclear computational scalability and practical guidance.

Overall evaluation:
An interesting and potentially impactful paper with strong theoretical contributions. However, the absence of substantive experimental evaluation and the high-level treatment of the dynamic algorithm make it difficult to assess practical viability. A narrower focus on static risk with comprehensive experiments, or an expanded presentation with dynamic-risk results, would strengthen the case for acceptance.
