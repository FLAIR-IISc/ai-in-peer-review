This paper presents a unified framework for deriving policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures, moving beyond previous work that focused on specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors' main contributions are threefold. First, they derive a new, general formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure based on its dual representation and the envelope theorem. This formula allows for the analytical derivation of policy gradients for several common risk measures. Second, for cases where an analytical solution is not available, the paper proposes a general sampling-based algorithm that combines Sample Average Approximation (SAA) with convex programming and provides a proof of its consistency. Third, the authors extend their framework to dynamic settings, presenting a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures and outlining a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its unified and principled derivation of policy gradients for all coherent risk measures. This is a significant and novel contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. By leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk, the work provides a general tool that subsumes and simplifies several previous results, offering a more fundamental understanding of risk-sensitive policy gradients.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions appear sound. The derivation of the static gradient formula in Theorem 4.2 is elegant, and the subsequent application to CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively demonstrates its utility. The consistency proof for the general SAA-based gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) provides important theoretical grounding for the proposed algorithm.

3.  **Generality:** The framework's ability to handle any coherent risk measure, provided its risk envelope is known, is a powerful feature. This gives practitioners the flexibility to design or choose risk measures that are best suited to their specific problem, as convincingly argued in the numerical illustration.

**Weaknesses / Arguments for Rejection**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the lack of empirical evidence supporting the proposed general algorithm. While the paper provides a small, illustrative example in Section 6, this example serves only to motivate the use of different risk measures, not to evaluate the performance of the proposed gradient estimation algorithms. Crucially, the SAA-based gradient estimator from Section 4.3, which is a key contribution for risk measures without analytical saddle points, is never tested. This makes it impossible to judge its viability in practice concerning sample complexity, computational cost, and variance.

2.  **Clarity and Scope:** The paper attempts to cover too much material (static risk gradients, a general estimation algorithm, and dynamic risk gradients) within the page limit, which significantly hinders clarity. The treatment of dynamic risk in Section 5 is particularly superficial. It presents a high-level policy gradient theorem but omits critical details of the proposed actor-critic algorithm, its implementation, and its analysis. Without these details or any experimental results, the contribution in the dynamic setting feels preliminary and underdeveloped, making it difficult for the reader to assess its practical value.

3.  **Incomplete Contribution for Dynamic Risk:** The section on dynamic risk raises more questions than it answers. The proposed actor-critic algorithm is described as involving a complex, two-phase sampling procedure and relying on function approximation methods from other recent work. This part of the contribution feels incomplete and would be better suited for a longer publication where the algorithm can be fully specified, analyzed, and empirically evaluated. As it stands, it weakens the overall focus and completeness of the paper.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper contains the seeds of a very strong contribution, but its current form is undermined by its broad scope and lack of empirical depth. I would strongly recommend that the authors narrow the paper's focus. For instance:

*   **Option 1 (Recommended):** Focus entirely on the static risk setting. This would allow space for a thorough empirical evaluation. An essential experiment would be to compare the SAA-based gradient estimator with the analytical gradient for a measure like CVaR. This would validate the general approach and provide insight into its practical performance. The paper could also include experiments on more complex RL problems to demonstrate that the method scales beyond the one-step decision problem presented. The dynamic risk material could be moved to a future, separate submission.

*   **Option 2:** If the authors wish to retain the dynamic risk section, it needs to be substantially more detailed. At a minimum, this would require providing clear pseudocode for the full actor-critic algorithm and at least one experiment demonstrating its application, even on a simple benchmark problem. Given the space constraints, this seems challenging without sacrificing the necessary details of the static risk contribution.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a theoretically elegant and potentially impactful framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation for its core algorithmic contributions and the overly ambitious scope make it difficult to fully assess the practical significance of the work. By trying to address both static and dynamic risk in one conference paper, the authors have left the presentation of both underdeveloped, particularly the latter. A more focused paper with a solid empirical section would be a much stronger and more convincing contribution.
