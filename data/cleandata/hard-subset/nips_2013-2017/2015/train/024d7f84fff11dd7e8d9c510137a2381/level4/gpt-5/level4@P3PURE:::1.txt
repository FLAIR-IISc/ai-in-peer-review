Summary:
The paper proposes a new formula for the class of static coherent risks from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. It further presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where no analytical solution is available and establishes consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper provides expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
- The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is intriguing and a valuable, novel contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature.
- However, analytical solutions of the Lagrangian saddle points are available only for some risk measures.
- The proposed sample-based approximation scheme could enable practical application of the derived results to general coherent risk measures by avoiding the need to derive analytical saddle points. That said, the paper provides no empirical evidence that this estimation scheme is viable in practice. An experimental comparison between sample-based policy gradients and analytical ones on different problems—e.g., for CVaR—would be extremely helpful.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients are novel in this general form.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but its clarity is hindered by the number of distinct issues tackled within eight pages. In particular, the dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level, and the lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluations prevents the reader from assessing whether the derived policy gradients yield approaches that are viable in practice. This content appears better suited to a longer journal publication. Alternatively, focusing this submission on static risk measures would make the paper more self-contained and allow for additional detail and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the very basic toy example currently provided. This is an interesting paper with potentially broadly applicable results, but the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluations makes it difficult to judge its practical impact. A narrower focus might also alleviate the clarity issues caused by space constraints.
