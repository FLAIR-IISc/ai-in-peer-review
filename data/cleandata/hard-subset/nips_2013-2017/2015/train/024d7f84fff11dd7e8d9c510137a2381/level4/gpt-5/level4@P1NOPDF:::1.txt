The paper proposes a new formula for a broad class of static coherent risk measures from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases without analytical solutions and proves consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper provides expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is intriguing and represents a valuable, novel contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, analytical solutions to the Lagrangian saddle points are available only for some risk measures. The proposed sampling-based approximation scheme could extend the applicability of the results to general coherent risk measures by avoiding the need to derive analytical saddle-point solutions. That said, the paper offers no empirical evidence that this estimation scheme is practical. An experimental comparison between the sampling-based and analytical policy gradients on several problems—e.g., for CVaR—would be extremely helpful.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, the policy gradient derivations—in this general form—are novel.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but its clarity is hindered by the number of distinct issues addressed within eight pages. In particular, the dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level; the lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluations prevents the reader from assessing whether the derived gradients yield practical methods. This content seems better suited to a longer journal version. Alternatively, narrowing the focus to static risk measures would make the paper more self-contained and allow for more detailed exposition and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, beyond the very basic toy example included in the current submission. Overall, this is an interesting paper with potentially broadly applicable results, but the absence of thorough experimental evaluations makes its practical impact difficult to judge. A narrower scope could also mitigate the clarity issues caused by space constraints.
