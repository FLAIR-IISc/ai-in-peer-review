The paper introduces a novel formula for static coherent risks, enabling analytical derivation of policy gradients for a wide range of common risk measures. It further presents a general sampling-based algorithm to estimate policy gradients when analytical solutions are unavailable, accompanied by consistency guarantees. Finally, the work extends these results to provide policy gradient expressions for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality:** The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures represents a valuable and original contribution to the field of risk-sensitive decision making. While analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are currently limited to specific risk measures, the proposed sampling-based approximation offers a promising avenue for applying these results to a broader class of coherent risk measures without requiring explicit analytical forms. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed estimation scheme’s practical feasibility. Inclusion of an experimental comparison between sample-based and analytical policy gradients, perhaps using CVaR as a case study, would significantly strengthen the work.

**Originality:** To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in their presented generality, are novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity suffers from attempting to cover a substantial scope within a limited space. The presentation of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is particularly high-level, and the absence of algorithmic details and experimental evaluation hinders assessment of the practical viability of the derived gradients. This material may be better suited for a more extensive journal publication. Alternatively, focusing the paper on static risk measures would allow for a more self-contained presentation, detailed analysis, and a more thorough empirical evaluation beyond the current basic example. Overall, this is an interesting paper with potentially impactful results, but the limited experimental validation and broad scope make it difficult to fully assess. A more focused approach could address the clarity issues arising from space constraints.
