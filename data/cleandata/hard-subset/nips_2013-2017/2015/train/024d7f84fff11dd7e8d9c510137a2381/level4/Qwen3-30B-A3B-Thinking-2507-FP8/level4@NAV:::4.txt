

The authors address the problem of determining policy parameters that minimize a specified risk measure. The paper considers a broad class of risk measures defined through fundamental coherence properties—convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The proposed methodology leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures as the convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable (e.g., cost) is expressed as the maximum expected value over this hull. A key assumption imposes an upper bound on the maximum derivatives of the convex hull’s boundaries, ensuring these boundaries are M-Lipschitz continuous. Under this assumption, the authors analytically derive optimal policy parameters (with respect to the risk measure) and present a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision settings.  

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. Despite my limited familiarity with risk-sensitive optimization, I followed the majority of the exposition. The primary contribution lies in the unified analysis of a general class of risk measures encompassing most established measures, which I consider significant. A more thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the work.  

Key comments/questions:  
1. The preliminaries section lacks clarity regarding the roles of $P_{\theta}$ and $\xi$.  
2. Equation 2 appears to define a convex envelope; why is this more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?  
3. A discussion on extending the analysis to continuous distributions would be valuable.  
4. The role of constraint function smoothness in proving Theorem 4.2 should be explicitly noted.  

This is a strong theoretical contribution offering a general framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. The results are compelling, though the proofs require careful verification.
