The paper proposes a novel framework for computing policy gradients for the class of coherent risk measures. It derives a new formula for static coherent risks, enabling analytical computation of policy gradients for common risk measures. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where analytical solutions are unavailable, with consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper provides expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality:**

The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a valuable and original contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature. The ability to generalize across the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant strength. However, analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for specific risk measures. 

The proposed sample-based approximation scheme offers a path towards applying the derived results to general coherent risk measures, avoiding the need for analytical saddle-point derivations. However, the paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating the viability of this estimation scheme in practice. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients on relevant problems (e.g., for CVaR) would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Originality:** To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in this general form, are novel. The work builds upon existing research in risk-sensitive RL but offers a substantial generalization and a unified theoretical framework.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is somewhat hindered by the breadth of topics addressed within the page limit. The presentation of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is particularly high-level. The lack of detailed algorithmic descriptions and experimental evaluations makes it difficult to assess the practical viability of the derived gradients. This content might be better suited for a longer journal publication.

An alternative approach could be to narrow the scope of the paper, focusing primarily on static risk measures. This would allow for a more self-contained presentation, greater detail, and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the basic toy example provided. While the paper presents a promising and potentially generally applicable result, the absence of extensive experimental evaluations makes a thorough assessment challenging. The limited space constraints also contribute to deficits in clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The work presents a genuinely novel and general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient calculation.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations appear theoretically sound and build upon established concepts in convex optimization and reinforcement learning.
*   **Potential Impact:** A successful generalization of policy gradient methods to coherent risk measures could have a significant impact on various applications, particularly in finance and robust control.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of substantial experimental results weakens the claim of practical viability.
*   **Limited Algorithmic Detail:** The description of the dynamic risk approach is too abstract, hindering the assessment of its implementability.
*   **Breadth vs. Depth:** The attempt to cover both static and dynamic risk within a single paper results in a lack of depth in certain areas, impacting clarity.
