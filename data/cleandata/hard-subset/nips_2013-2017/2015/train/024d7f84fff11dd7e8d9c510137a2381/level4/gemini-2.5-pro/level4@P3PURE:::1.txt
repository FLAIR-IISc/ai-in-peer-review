The paper proposes a general formula for the class of static coherent risks, from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where an analytical solution is unavailable and provides consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper derives expressions for the policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality:**
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is an intriguing and valuable contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive decision-making. Nevertheless, as the authors note, analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for a subset of these risk measures.

The proposed sample-based approximation scheme is designed to extend these results to general coherent risk measures, avoiding the need to derive analytical saddle-point solutions. However, the paper lacks empirical evidence to demonstrate that this estimation scheme is viable in practice. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients for various problems (e.g., for CVaR) would be extremely helpful in assessing the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Originality:**
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in this general form, are novel.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written. However, its clarity is hindered by the number of different topics the authors attempt to address within the eight-page limit. In particular, the treatment of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is presented at a very high level. The lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluation makes it difficult for the reader to judge whether the derived gradients lead to practical approaches. This content seems better suited for a longer journal publication where it could be developed more thoroughly.

An alternative, and potentially more effective, approach would be to focus the paper exclusively on static risk measures. This would allow for a more self-contained presentation, a more detailed explanation of the core method, and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the basic toy example currently provided. In summary, this is an interesting paper with potentially applicable results, but the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation and a scope that feels too broad for the format make its practical contributions difficult to judge. A narrower focus would likely improve clarity and strengthen the paper's impact.
