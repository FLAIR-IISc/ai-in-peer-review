- Develops policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures in risk-sensitive RL.  
- Presents a unified framework that generalizes prior approaches (variance, CVaR, mean-variance).  
- Uses the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to derive gradient formulas for static and dynamic risks.  
- Connects coherent risk measures to convex optimization and policy gradient methods theoretically.  
- Provides a sampling + convex programming implementable gradient formula for static risk.  
- Extends the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures and proposes an actor-critic algorithm for sequential decision problems.  
- Demonstrates that existing risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) are special cases of the framework.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and has clear technical exposition.  
- Provides detailed proofs and establishes consistency properties for gradient estimators.  
- Theoretical analysis addresses coherence and time consistency in sequential decision settings.  
- Originality is high: offers a substantial conceptual advance by covering the whole class of coherent risks.  
- Significance: framework can influence future research and allows more flexible risk management; numerical example illustrates differing policy choices.  
- Main weakness: limited empirical validation — needs more comprehensive experiments across benchmarks.  
- Lacks detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the proposed algorithms.  
- Technical concern: finite probability space assumption is restrictive; needs extension to continuous spaces.  
- Clarification needed on the connection between Equation 2 and Theorem 2.1 (convex envelope vs. risk envelope).  
- Smoothness assumptions (Assumption 2.2) require better motivation and practical intuition.  
- Practical implementation challenges, especially the convex programming steps for gradient estimation, need more discussion.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant, sound, well-motivated theoretical contribution that generalizes prior work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation and restrictive finite-space assumption reduce immediate applicability.  
- Overall recommendation: strong theoretical paper recommended for acceptance, with the suggestion to strengthen empirical results and address applicability concerns in future work.
