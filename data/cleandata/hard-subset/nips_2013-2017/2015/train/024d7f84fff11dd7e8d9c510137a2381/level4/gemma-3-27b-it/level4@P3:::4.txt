This paper presents a compelling and theoretically sound approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending policy gradient methods to the broad class of coherent risk measures. The authors skillfully leverage the duality representation of coherent risk to derive novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk optimization problems. This unification provides a significant contribution, generalizing previous work focused on specific risk measures like CVaR and variance. The paper is well-structured and, despite the technical complexity of the topic, reasonably clear in its exposition. 

The core idea revolves around formulating risk-sensitive policy gradients within a coherent risk framework, combining sampling techniques with convex programming for static cases and an actor-critic style approach with value function approximation for dynamic cases. The resulting gradient formulas, particularly Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2, are central to the contribution and offer a pathway for applying policy gradient methods to a wide range of risk preferences. The numerical illustration, while brief, effectively demonstrates the impact of different risk criteria on policy selection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The work provides a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL, encompassing a wide class of coherent risk measures.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of gradient formulas is mathematically sound, building on established results in convex optimization and risk theory.
*   **Novelty:**  The extension of policy gradient methods to coherent risk measures, and the resulting formulas, represent a significant advancement.
*   **Clarity:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.
* **Connections to Robust MDPs:** The link between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs (mentioned briefly) is insightful and hints at broader applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Preliminaries Clarity:** The notation in the preliminaries section (particularly concerning *P<sub>θ</sub>* and *ξ*) could be made more precise and intuitive. A more explicit explanation of their roles would be beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the area.
*   **Assumption 2.2 Discussion:** While Assumption 2.2 is crucial for the derivations, its implications and the rationale behind it aren't fully discussed. The authors could elaborate on why this assumption is reasonable and how it impacts the applicability of their results. The connection to Lipschitz continuity isn't immediately obvious and could be clarified.
*   **Generalization to Continuous Variables:** The paper restricts itself to finite probability spaces.  While mentioning the possibility of extending to Lp-normed spaces, a brief discussion of the challenges and potential approaches for handling continuous distributions would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the numerical example is helpful, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the performance of the proposed algorithms on a variety of benchmark MDPs would strengthen the paper.  Specifically, comparing the performance against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms would be informative.
* **Algorithm Details:** The actor-critic algorithm is briefly mentioned, but crucial details (e.g., function approximation techniques, learning rates) are omitted. Providing more specifics, or pointing to supplementary material, would increase the reproducibility of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The generality of the framework and the derived gradient formulas represent a substantial contribution to the field.
*   The paper is well-written and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature.
*   The work opens up new avenues for research in risk-sensitive decision-making.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed algorithms.
*   The lack of detailed algorithmic specifications hinders reproducibility.
*   While generally clear, certain sections (particularly the preliminaries) could benefit from further clarification.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong submission with the potential to significantly impact the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While some areas could be improved with further elaboration and empirical validation, the theoretical contributions are substantial. I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more details about the actor-critic algorithm and expanding the empirical evaluation. Specifically, a detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the smoothness parameter *M* would be helpful.
