The authors address the problem of policy optimization under coherent risk measures, extending recent work on risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. They present a unified framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, leveraging a dual representation based on risk envelopes. This approach combines sampling techniques with convex programming to derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk settings, generalizing previous results focused on specific risk measures like CVaR. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, providing a solid theoretical foundation for their approach. The primary contribution lies in this generalization and the development of a tractable gradient estimation algorithm applicable to a broad class of risk-sensitive problems.

The strengths of the paper are its theoretical rigor and generality. The authors successfully extend existing policy gradient methods to a more general class of risk measures, providing a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers. The derivation of the gradient formulas, particularly for the dynamic case, is a significant achievement. The connection to robust MDPs, as highlighted in the discussion of dynamic risk, provides additional insight and potential avenues for future research.

However, there are areas for improvement. While the theoretical results are strong, the empirical evaluation is limited. A more comprehensive experimental analysis, demonstrating the performance of the proposed algorithms on benchmark problems and comparing them to existing methods, would significantly strengthen the paper. The preliminary section could benefit from clearer notation and explanation of key concepts, particularly the roles of Pθ and ξ. Specifically, clarifying how ξ relates to a weighting of the probability distribution Pθ would be helpful. The connection between Equation 2 and Theorem 2.1 could be more explicitly explained; elaborating on the assumptions underlying the tighter formulation would be valuable. Finally, acknowledging the implications of the smoothness assumptions on the constraint functions for the validity of Theorem 4.2, even briefly in the main body, would enhance clarity. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty and Generality:** The paper presents a novel and general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods, extending existing work to the full class of coherent risk measures.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations are rigorous and well-motivated, building upon established results in optimization and reinforcement learning.
*   **Potential Impact:** The results have the potential to be widely applicable in various domains, including finance, robotics, and control.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The lack of a comprehensive empirical analysis weakens the claims regarding the practical performance of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Clarity of Preliminaries:** The preliminary section requires further clarification to enhance readability and understanding for a broader audience.
*   **Missing Discussion:** The role of constraint smoothness is not mentioned.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper with significant potential. Addressing the concerns regarding the empirical evaluation and clarifying certain aspects of the presentation would substantially improve its impact and make it a compelling contribution to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
