This paper introduces a unified framework for policy gradient estimation within the realm of coherent risk measures. The core contribution lies in deriving a novel formula applicable to the entire class of static coherent risks, enabling analytical gradient computation for common risk measures. Furthermore, the authors present a general sampling-based algorithm to overcome limitations when analytical solutions are unavailable, accompanied by consistency guarantees for the gradient estimates. Finally, they extend these results to policy gradients for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality:**

The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a significant and novel contribution to risk-sensitive decision making. It provides a valuable theoretical foundation for applying reinforcement learning to problems where managing risk, beyond simply minimizing expected cost, is crucial. However, the practical impact hinges on the viability of the proposed sampling-based approximation scheme, as analytical solutions for Lagrangian saddle-points are limited. 

Currently, the paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of this approximation in practice. Including an experimental comparison – even a focused one – between the sample-based policy gradients and their analytical counterparts (e.g., for CVaR) would substantially strengthen the submission. Demonstrating performance on benchmark problems would be highly valuable. The theoretical results are solid, but an empirical validation is necessary to assess their practical applicability.

**Originality:**

To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients in their generalized form represent a novel contribution. The work effectively bridges the gap between the well-established field of coherent risk measures and the increasingly popular techniques of policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is somewhat hampered by the breadth of topics addressed within the page limit. The dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a high level of abstraction, and the absence of detailed algorithmic descriptions and experimental results makes it difficult to assess their practicality. This content might be better suited for a longer, more comprehensive journal publication. 

An alternative approach would be to narrow the scope of the paper, focusing primarily on static risk measures. This would allow for a more self-contained presentation, deeper technical details, and a more thorough empirical evaluation—beyond the current basic example. While the work shows promise, the lack of extensive experimental validation makes a comprehensive assessment challenging. A more focused scope could improve clarity given the space constraints.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a significant theoretical advancement.
*   **Generality:** The approach generalizes existing methods and provides a framework for handling a wide class of risk measures.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The consistency guarantees for the sampling-based approximation offer a strong theoretical foundation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experimental results limits the ability to assess the practical viability of the proposed methods.
*   **Limited Detail for Dynamic Risk:** The presentation of dynamic risk policy gradients is too high-level and lacks sufficient algorithmic detail and empirical support.
*   **Breadth vs. Depth:** The paper attempts to cover a wide range of topics, potentially sacrificing clarity and depth in certain areas.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak reject**. The theoretical contributions are valuable and the paper presents a promising direction for research. However, the lack of empirical validation and the breadth of the work significantly hinder its impact. If the authors can address the empirical concerns and potentially narrow the scope to provide a more focused and detailed analysis, I would be more inclined to support acceptance.
