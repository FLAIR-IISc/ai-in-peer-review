The authors study the problem of selecting policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. They consider a broad class of coherent risk measures characterized by standard axioms (convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance). The approach builds on the dual representation of coherent risk, expressing the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) as the supremum of its expected value over a convex risk envelope—effectively, the convex hull of a set of probability distributions.

A key assumption is that the boundary of this risk envelope has bounded derivatives; specifically, the boundary is M-Lipschitz. Under this assumption, the authors derive optimality conditions for policy parameters with respect to the risk measure and develop a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static (one-shot) and dynamic (sequential) settings.

Overall, the paper is well written and well organized. Although I am not deeply familiar with risk-sensitive optimization, I could follow most of the arguments. The principal contribution is a unified analysis covering a broad family of risk measures that subsumes many well-known special cases. This generality is significant. That said, the empirical section could be strengthened: a more thorough evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would substantially improve the paper.

Comments and questions:
1) The preliminaries need clarification. In particular, the roles of P_theta and xi are not sufficiently clear. Is P_theta the policy-induced distribution over trajectories/outcomes, and is xi a latent random variable, noise source, or an index of the distributions in the risk envelope? A brief, explicit statement would help.
2) Is Equation 2 simply the definition of the convex envelope (or convex hull) of the relevant quantities? If so, in what sense is it more restrictive than Theorem 2.1? Please clarify the additional assumptions or structural constraints that make Equation 2 stronger.
3) Please comment on how the analysis extends to continuous outcome spaces. What modifications are required for the dual representation and the gradient estimator under continuous distributions (e.g., measurability, integrability, or regularity conditions)?
4) In the short version, it would be helpful to state explicitly how the smoothness of the constraint functions is used in proving Theorem 4.2 (e.g., where Lipschitz or differentiability assumptions enter the argument and how they affect stability of the gradient).

In summary, this is a strong theory paper that offers a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear compelling, though the proofs should be carefully checked, and a deeper empirical study would strengthen the submission.
