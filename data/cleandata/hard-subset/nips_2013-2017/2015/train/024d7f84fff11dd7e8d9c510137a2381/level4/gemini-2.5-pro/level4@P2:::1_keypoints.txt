- Presents a unified framework to derive policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures, extending beyond specific measures like variance or CVaR.  
- Derives a general static gradient formula using the dual representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem, enabling analytic gradients for many common risk measures.  
- Proposes a general sampling-based algorithm combining Sample Average Approximation (SAA) with convex programming for cases without analytic solutions, and proves its consistency.  
- Extends the framework to dynamic settings with a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures and outlines an actor-critic algorithm.  

- Strength: Original and significant—provides a principled, unifying derivation that subsumes prior risk-sensitive policy-gradient results.  
- Strength: Strong technical quality—static gradient derivation (Theorem 4.2) is elegant and SAA estimator consistency (Proposition 4.4) is theoretically grounded.  
- Strength: Generality—framework applies to any coherent risk measure with a known risk envelope, giving practical flexibility.  

- Weakness: Lacks empirical validation—no experiments test the SAA-based gradient estimator, leaving questions about sample complexity, cost, and variance.  
- Weakness: Overambitious scope and reduced clarity—covering static gradients, a general estimator, and dynamic gradients within page limits makes presentation superficial.  
- Weakness: Dynamic-risk contribution is incomplete—actor-critic algorithm is under-specified, relies on complex sampling and external approximation methods, and lacks empirical or theoretical detail.  

- Suggestion (recommended): Narrow focus to the static risk setting and include thorough empirical evaluation (e.g., compare SAA estimator vs analytic CVaR, test on larger RL problems).  
- Suggestion (alternative): If retaining dynamic risk, provide full pseudocode, implementation details, and at least one experiment demonstrating the actor-critic method.  

- Conclusion: The paper is theoretically elegant and potentially impactful but undermined by insufficient experiments and an overbroad scope; a more focused paper with solid empirical results would be stronger.
