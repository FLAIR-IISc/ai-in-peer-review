

The paper introduces a novel formula for static coherent risk measures, enabling analytical derivation of policy gradients for numerous common risk measures. It also proposes a general sampling-based algorithm for cases lacking analytical solutions, establishing consistency guarantees for the policy gradient estimates, and provides expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.  

Significance and quality:  
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures represents a significant and novel contribution to risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for specific risk measures. While the proposed sampling-based approximation could enable application to general coherent risk measures by circumventing the need for analytical saddle-point derivations, the paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating the viability of this estimation scheme in practice. An experimental comparison between sample-based and analytical policy gradients across diverse problems (e.g., for CVaR) would be highly valuable.  

Originality:  
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients are novel in this general form.  

Clarity:  
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is substantially hindered by attempting to address too many distinct issues within the eight-page limit. Specifically, Section 5’s treatment of dynamic risk policy gradients is presented at a high level, with insufficient algorithmic details and experimental evaluations to assess practical viability. This content would be more appropriately suited for a journal publication. I recommend splitting the paper to focus solely on static risk measures, allowing for a more self-contained presentation, deeper technical details, and a comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the current submission’s basic toy example. While the paper presents potentially broadly applicable results, the absence of thorough experimental validation prevents a definitive assessment of its practical utility. A narrower focus would also better address the clarity deficits imposed by space constraints.
