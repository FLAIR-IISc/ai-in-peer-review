This paper introduces a policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). The authors' approach applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, covering both static risk evaluations of a trajectory's total cost and time-consistent dynamic risk measures. The core contributions are novel policy gradient formulas for both settings. For static risk, the method combines sampling with convex programming by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures. For dynamic risk, the authors derive a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem and propose a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. This work successfully generalizes several recent papers that developed risk-sensitive policy gradient algorithms for specific risk measures, such as Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) or mean-variance criteria ([8], [30], [29]), placing them within a single, unified framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Generality:** The primary strength of this paper is its unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods under the axiomatically-sound framework of coherent risk measures. By providing a general method applicable to any risk measure within this class, the work offers researchers and practitioners significant flexibility. It allows for the selection of a risk measure that is most appropriate for a given problem's characteristics, a point well-illustrated by the numerical example involving heavy-tailed distributions.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results appear to be sound. The derivation of the static risk gradient using the envelope theorem (Theorem 4.2) is elegant and insightful. The subsequent extension to the dynamic setting, which results in a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem analogous to the classic one for expected returns, is a logical and well-executed development.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the necessary background on coherent risk measures before presenting their main results.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Contribution Framing:** While the generalization is valuable, the contribution could be seen as an incremental extension of existing policy gradient theory. The core techniques—the dual representation of coherent risk and the likelihood-ratio trick—are well-established. The paper would be strengthened if the authors could more explicitly discuss the novel technical challenges encountered when moving from a specific measure like CVaR to the general class, and how their formulation overcomes them.
*   **Clarity in Technical Details:** A few points could be clarified to improve the manuscript further:
    *   In Section 3, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the intractability being addressed arises from the policy parameterization in large or continuous state-action spaces, which necessitates the proposed reinforcement learning approach. This is an important distinction, as planning with Markov coherent risk measures in tabular MDPs is known to be tractable [24]. This clarification would prevent potential confusion for readers less familiar with this specific subfield.
    *   The notation `ξP_θ` used in Theorem 2.1 is somewhat unconventional and could be confusing. The authors might consider adopting the notation used in the primary reference [26] or providing a more explicit definition of this term.
    *   There is a minor typo on line 141: "risk enevlop[e]".

In summary, this paper presents a solid, well-executed, and important extension of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. Its main contribution is providing a unified and general framework for a wide class of coherent risk measures, which is a valuable step forward for the field.
