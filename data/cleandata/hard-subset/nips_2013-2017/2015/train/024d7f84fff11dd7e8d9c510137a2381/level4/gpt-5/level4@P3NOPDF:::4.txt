Summary
The paper studies the problem of optimizing policy parameters to minimize a general coherent risk measure. Leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures as worst-case expectations over a convex risk envelope (a convex set of probability distributions), the authors derive a unified analysis and gradient-based optimization method. A key assumption is an M-Lipschitz regularity condition on the boundary of the risk envelope, which enables sensitivity analysis and gradient computation. The paper presents algorithms and theory for both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision problems.

Strengths
- Generality and unification: The framework covers a broad class of coherent risk measures, including many widely used ones, under a common analytical umbrella.
- Clear theoretical development: The use of the dual representation leads to clean characterizations of the risk gradient and to principled gradient estimation procedures for both static and sequential settings.
- Accessibility: The paper is well written and organized. Even without deep prior exposure to risk-sensitive optimization, the main ideas are understandable.
- Potential impact: Providing a general-purpose approach to risk-sensitive decision-making in policy optimization is timely and valuable.

Concerns and limitations
- Strength of assumptions: The M-Lipschitz condition on the risk envelope boundary (or equivalently on the support function/subgradients) is central but may be restrictive. More discussion of when it holds for common risk measures and model classes would be helpful, as would guidance on how to verify or approximate M in practice.
- Practical implementability: The approach appears to require access to oracles for the risk envelope (e.g., computing worst-case distributions or subgradients). The paper would benefit from a clearer account of computational costs, scalability with the dimension of the outcome space, and how the adversarial distribution is represented and updated in practice.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are limited relative to the breadth of the theory. A deeper empirical study—comparing against baselines (e.g., CVaR optimization, entropic risk, robust MDPs), measuring sample complexity and variance of gradient estimates, and exploring sensitivity to M—would strengthen the paper.
- Technical subtleties: The exchange of gradient and max (Danskin-like arguments), existence/uniqueness of saddle points, and conditions for unbiased/low-variance gradient estimators in the sequential case deserve careful verification and clearer statements of assumptions (e.g., ergodicity, mixing, boundedness).

Specific questions and requests for clarification
1) Preliminaries (roles of Pθ and ξ): Please clarify early on that Pθ denotes the nominal distribution induced by the policy parameters (e.g., over trajectories or outcomes), while ξ parameterizes an adversarial/reweighted distribution within the risk envelope used in the dual representation. A small running example would help disentangle these roles.

2) Equation (2) vs. Theorem 2.1: Equation (2) appears to define the risk as the supremum of expected values over a convex set of distributions—essentially the dual representation of a coherent risk measure. If you intend Eq. (2) to be a convex envelope or a more restrictive representation (e.g., a particular polyhedral envelope), please explain precisely how it tightens or differs from Theorem 2.1, and under what conditions this specialization holds.

3) Continuous distributions: The current exposition seems oriented to discrete outcomes. Please comment on extensions to continuous spaces (state, action, or outcome): required measurability and compactness assumptions; existence of maximizers in the risk envelope; use of Radon–Nikodym derivatives; and how the gradient estimators are implemented with sampling/importance weighting in continuous settings.

4) Theorem 4.2 and smoothness: In the short version, please explicitly state the smoothness/regularity assumptions on the constraint functions defining the risk envelope and describe how they are used in the proof (e.g., to invoke envelope/Danskin theorems, guarantee Lipschitz subgradients, or ensure stability of the argmax mapping).

5) Gradient estimation details: For the sequential case, specify the conditions under which the policy gradient theorem extends to the risk-sensitive objective (e.g., interchange of expectation and differentiation, ergodicity, boundedness of likelihood ratios). Clarify whether the estimator is unbiased or asymptotically consistent and discuss variance reduction techniques.

6) Verification and computation of M: Provide examples (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation, spectral risk measures) where the Lipschitz constant can be bounded, and discuss practical ways to estimate or upper bound M.

7) Complexity and scalability: Outline the computational complexity per gradient step, including solving the inner adversarial problem. If a convex program is required at each step, discuss how this scales and whether approximate solutions preserve convergence guarantees.

Minor comments and suggestions
- Notation: Define the risk envelope (set of distributions) as early as possible and use consistent notation for adversarial distributions (ξ) and nominal ones (Pθ).
- Examples: A simple illustrative example (e.g., CVaR in a finite-outcome setting) tracing the dual variables, the envelope, and the resulting gradient would greatly aid intuition.
- Related work: Briefly situate the method relative to robust MDPs, distributionally robust optimization, and known policy-gradient methods for CVaR/entropic risk. Cite standard dual representations (e.g., Artzner et al., Shapiro et al.) and practical algorithms (e.g., Rockafellar–Uryasev for CVaR).
- Assumption statements: Collect all regularity assumptions (boundedness, convexity, compactness, differentiability) in one place, and restate them before the main theorems.

Overall assessment
This is a strong theory paper that proposes a general and principled approach to risk-sensitive decision-making via the dual representation of coherent risk measures. The results are compelling and, if the proofs check out, likely to be influential. To maximize impact, I encourage the authors to clarify the preliminaries and assumptions, tighten the connection between Eq. (2) and Theorem 2.1, discuss extensions to continuous settings, and substantially expand the empirical evaluation to demonstrate practicality and robustness. I lean positively toward acceptance, contingent on these clarifications and a careful verification of the proofs.
