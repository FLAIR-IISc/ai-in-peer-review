- Develops policy gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures, covering static and time-consistent dynamic settings.
- Key contribution (i): general gradient formula for static coherent risks via envelope-theorem on the dual risk representation, yielding likelihood-ratio estimators with convex programming.
- Key contribution (ii): a sample-average approximation (SAA) estimator for static risk with a consistency result.
- Key contribution (iii): a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk and an actor–critic scheme leveraging robust-MDP connections for function approximation.
- Shows well-known special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) arise as corollaries of the general framework.
- Provides a toy financial example illustrating that different risk criteria produce distinct policies.
- Situates and unifies prior work on specific risk measures, extending results and connecting to robust MDP literature (Ruszczyński–Shapiro; Chow–Pavone; Petrik–Subramanian).
- Strength: unified, principled treatment subsuming several existing methods as special cases.
- Strength: clean gradient identities for static risk and a novel dynamic policy gradient theorem.
- Strength: practical estimators combining sampling with convex optimization and consistency for the SAA estimator.
- Strength: clear exposition, strong convex-analytic duality links, and useful worked examples.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation limited to a simple static, single-step portfolio example; dynamic actor–critic method lacks experiments.
- Weakness: potentially heavy computational burden (per-batch convex solves, KKT multiplier extraction, ξ-weighted transition simulation) with no runtime/sample-complexity quantification.
- Weakness: strong/possibly restrictive assumptions (explicit smooth risk envelope, finite probability spaces, bounded likelihood ratios, simulator access, Slater condition) and brief discussion on extending to continuous/data-driven settings.
- Weakness: key algorithmic details (critic approximation, two-phase sampling, convergence guarantees) and ablations are mostly in the supplement, hindering assessment of practicality.
- Weakness: limited empirical comparison to strong baselines on dynamic tasks (no head-to-head with CVaR-PG or robust RL planners).
- Quality: technical development appears sound with correct use of convex duality, envelope theorems, and likelihood-ratio methods; proofs in supplement.
- Clarity: well organized and clearly written; suggests summarizing computational complexity and moving essential algorithmic steps to main text.
- Originality: meaningful advance by extending policy gradients to general coherent risks and deriving a dynamic policy gradient theorem.
- Significance: potentially broad impact in safety-critical control, finance, and operations, stronger with empirical dynamic demonstrations.
- Suggested improvements: add dynamic actor–critic experiments on standard benchmarks and compare to CVaR-PG/robust MDP baselines.
- Suggested improvements: report computational overhead, sample counts, gradient variance, sample efficiency, and include ablations (analytic vs SAA for CVaR).
- Suggested improvements: discuss continuous state/action extensions, approximate/data-driven envelopes, and robustness to misspecification.
- Suggested improvements: consider variance-reduction/importance sampling and clarify when single-trajectory rollouts suffice.
- Suggested improvements: state conditions for existence/uniqueness of saddle points and consequences if violated.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong, general theoretical framework that unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive RL results with sound derivations and consistency guarantees.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation for the dynamic component, uncharacterized computational/practical aspects, and reliance on strong assumptions.
- Overall recommendation: leaning toward acceptance for the theoretical/unifying contribution, but empirical strengthening of the dynamic method and practical analysis is needed.
