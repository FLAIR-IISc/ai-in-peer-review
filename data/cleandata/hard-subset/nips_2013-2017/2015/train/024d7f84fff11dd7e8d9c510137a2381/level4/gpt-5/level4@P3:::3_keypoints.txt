- Paper proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for the full class of coherent risk measures, covering static and time-consistent dynamic risks.  
- For static risk, derives a likelihood-ratio style gradient via the dual (risk-envelope) representation and the envelope theorem, plus an SAA estimator with consistency guarantees.  
- For dynamic risk, derives a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, relates gradients to ξ-weighted transitions, and proposes an actor-critic algorithm leveraging robust MDP equivalence.  
- Generalizes prior work on specific risk criteria (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR) by extending dual-representation ideas to policy-gradient RL.  

- Technical development is sound; static gradient derivation and SAA consistency are appropriate under stated assumptions.  
- Dynamic policy-gradient theorem is plausible and reduces to standard policy gradient in the risk-neutral case.  
- Main empirical weakness: only a single toy example and no quantitative study of variance, bias, or sample complexity.  
- Assumptions (bounded likelihood ratios, differentiable risk envelopes, finiteness) are clearly stated but may limit applicability.  

- Paper is well organized and readable; links coherent risk, robust MDPs, and actor-critic design effectively.  
- Notation could be streamlined and roles of policy parameterization vs. risk measure should be more explicitly separated.  

- Originality is moderate: natural extension of dual representation to policy gradients; dynamic theorem and actor-critic instantiation are novel within RL but conceptually aligned with robust MDPs.  
- Significance is moderate: useful unified recipe for practitioners, but practical impact limited by computational demands and sparse experiments.  

- Strength: unified treatment subsuming prior specific-risk results.  
- Strength: clear derivations using duality and envelope theorem.  
- Strength: SAA-based estimator with consistency proof.  
- Strength: dynamic theorem and actor-critic grounded in robust MDP theory.  
- Strength: good exposition and motivation for time consistency.  

- Weakness: minimal empirical evaluation, no realistic or dynamic experiments.  
- Weakness: computational practicality concerns (computing ξ*, KKT multipliers, ξ-weighted transitions per state).  
- Weakness: requires explicit, differentiable risk envelopes which may not exist for all coherent risks.  
- Weakness: exposition focuses on finite outcome spaces; continuous extensions are deferred.  
- Weakness: no analysis of variance control or sample complexity; rare-event sensitivity may need variance reduction.  

- Suggestion: add at least one substantive dynamic-risk experiment showing actor-critic performance, runtime, and variance of gradients.  
- Suggestion: discuss computational cost and how often ξ*/KKT multipliers must be recomputed; report wall-clock or iteration cost.  
- Suggestion: elaborate on constructing U(Pθ) for common coherent risks and computing required derivatives in practice.  
- Suggestion: clarify tractability: DP is tractable in tabular settings but parameterization/large state spaces cause difficulty.  
- Suggestion: report variance/bias comparisons between analytic gradients and SAA, and include variance-reduction techniques.  
- Suggestion: outline extensions to continuous state/action spaces and function approximation for the static estimator.  

- Minor: clarify notation ξ Pθ and align with existing references; define ξ as a density multiplier.  
- Minor: fix typo “risk enevlop[e]” → “risk envelope.”  
- Minor: be consistent about overloading ρ for static vs. dynamic measures; add a notational reminder.  
- Minor: clarify Assumption 2.2’s smoothness in p and its applicability to examples.  

- Arguments for acceptance: solid, clean generalization unifying multiple prior risk-sensitive PG results; provides a reusable SAA recipe; introduces a dynamic policy-gradient theorem with actor-critic design.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical evidence and scalability concerns; dependence on explicit differentiable risk envelopes and KKT computations; incremental given standard dual-representation ingredients.  

- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on strengthening empirical validation and clarifying computational aspects.
