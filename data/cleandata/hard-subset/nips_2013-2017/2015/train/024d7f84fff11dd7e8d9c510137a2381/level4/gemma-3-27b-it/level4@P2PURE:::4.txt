The authors address the problem of policy parameter optimization with respect to a given risk measure. They consider a general class of coherent risk measures, characterized by properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. Their approach leverages a dual representation of coherent risk measures as the convex hull of probability distributions, defining risk as the maximum expected value within this hull. The paper introduces an assumption regarding the smoothness of the convex hull’s boundaries – specifically, that they are M-Lipschitz continuous – and utilizes this assumption to analytically derive optimal policy parameters for both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision problems. They also present a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both scenarios.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. Despite my limited background in risk-sensitive optimization, I found the explanations largely accessible. The primary contribution of this work lies in its unified analysis of a broad class of risk measures encompassing many commonly used measures, which I consider a significant advancement. However, a more extensive empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the paper. 

Several points warrant further clarification: 1- The preliminaries section lacks sufficient clarity; the roles of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ are unclear. 2- Equation 2 appears to simply state the definition of a convex envelope. Could the authors explain why this is presented as more restrictive than Theorem 2.1? 3- A discussion of how the analysis extends to distributions over continuous variables would be valuable. 4- The connection between the smoothness of the constraint functions and the proof of Theorem 4.2 could be explicitly stated in the paper. Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution presenting a general framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear promising, though a careful verification of the proofs is recommended.
