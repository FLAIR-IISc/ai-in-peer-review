The paper introduces a novel formula for computing policy gradients for static coherent risks, enabling analytical derivation for many common risk measures. It further presents a general sampling-based algorithm applicable when analytical solutions are unavailable, accompanied by consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the authors derive policy gradients for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality: The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a compelling and valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive decision making. While analytical solutions to the Lagrangian saddle-points are limited to specific risk measures, the proposed sample-based approximation offers a potential pathway to apply the derived results to a broader class of coherent risk measures by circumventing the need for analytical saddle-point solutions. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed estimation scheme’s practical viability. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients—for example, applied to CVaR in various problem settings—would be highly beneficial.

Originality: To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in this general form, represent a novel result.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written; however, its clarity is diminished by the scope of topics covered within the page limit. Specifically, the dynamic risk policy gradients presented in Section 5 are high-level, and the absence of algorithmic details and experimental results prevents a thorough assessment of their practical applicability. This content might be better suited for a more extensive journal publication. Alternatively, focusing on static risk measures would allow for a more self-contained paper, greater detail, and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation than the current, basic toy example provides. This is an interesting paper with potentially broadly applicable results, but the limited experimental evaluation makes a robust assessment difficult. A narrower scope could also alleviate the clarity issues arising from space constraints.
