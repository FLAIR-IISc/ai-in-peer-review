This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending the scope from specific risk measures to the general class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive a novel gradient formula for static coherent risk measures by leveraging their dual representation as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability distributions. This formulation leads to analytical gradient expressions for certain known risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and a general, sampling-based algorithm for cases where no analytical solution exists. The paper also extends this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, proposing a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths (Originality and Significance)**

The primary strength of this work is its elegant unification of policy gradients for a broad and axiomatically justified class of risk measures. By grounding the approach in the theory of coherent risk, the paper provides a principled foundation that generalizes and connects several disparate results in the risk-sensitive RL literature, which have previously focused on individual risk measures like variance or CVaR. The derivation of the main gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) using the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope Theorem is novel and insightful. This theoretical contribution is significant, as it offers a single, powerful tool for a wide range of risk-sensitive optimization problems.

**Weaknesses (Quality and Clarity)**

Despite its theoretical appeal, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its impact, primarily concerning its empirical validation and scope.

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation for the General Algorithm:** The paper proposes a general gradient estimation algorithm based on Sample Average Approximation (SAA) for cases where an analytical saddle-point of the Lagrangian is unavailable. This is a key practical contribution, as it promises to make the framework applicable to any coherent risk measure with a known risk envelope. However, this general method is not empirically validated. The only numerical experiment (Section 6) serves to motivate the choice of different risk measures, but it does not evaluate the performance or viability of the proposed learning algorithms. A significant concern is the lack of evidence showing that the SAA-based gradient estimator is effective in practice. It would be highly beneficial to include an experimental comparison between the SAA-based estimator and the analytical gradient (e.g., for CVaR) to demonstrate the practical viability, sample efficiency, and convergence properties of the proposed general estimator.

2.  **Overly Ambitious Scope and Lack of Detail:** The paper attempts to cover both static and dynamic risk measures, which compromises the clarity and completeness of the presentation within the 8-page limit. Section 5, which introduces the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk, is particularly high-level. It omits crucial details of the algorithm, its implementation, and its analysis, referring the reader to supplementary material for the "full paper version." This makes the second half of the paper's contribution difficult to assess from the main text alone. The work feels incomplete, and the reader cannot judge whether the proposed approach for the dynamic case is practical or scalable.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the density of the material, combined with the lack of algorithmic and experimental detail, hinders overall clarity. The connection between the static and dynamic cases is clear in principle, but the presentation of the dynamic case is too compressed to be fully convincing or reproducible.

**Constructive Suggestions**

To address these issues, I would strongly recommend that the authors consider narrowing the scope of the paper. A version focused solely on static coherent risk measures would allow for a more detailed theoretical presentation and, crucially, a comprehensive empirical evaluation of the proposed SAA-based gradient estimator. This would make the paper a more self-contained and impactful contribution. The work on dynamic risk is substantial and interesting, but it is not given sufficient space to be properly developed and seems better suited for a separate, more extensive publication, such as a journal article.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and theoretically elegant framework that unifies policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures.
    *   Successfully generalizes and simplifies several previous results in the risk-sensitive RL literature.
    *   The core ideas are original and represent a significant conceptual advance for the field.

*   **Arguments against Acceptance:**
    *   The main practical contribution—the general SAA-based gradient estimator—lacks any empirical validation, making its utility difficult to judge.
    *   The paper's scope is too broad for a conference paper, leading to a superficial treatment of the dynamic risk setting.
    *   The lack of detail, particularly for the proposed actor-critic algorithm, makes the work feel incomplete and not readily reproducible.

Overall, this paper introduces interesting and potentially important theoretical results. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation for its central algorithmic proposal and the clarity issues arising from its over-ambitious scope are significant drawbacks.
