Summary
The paper studies risk-averse Markov decision processes with both static risk measures and dynamically consistent (Markov) risk measures. Its main contribution is to derive policy gradient formulas in these two settings, generalizing earlier results that focused specifically on CVaR. Conceptually, the results extend the standard policy gradient theorem from the risk-neutral case to a broad class of risk measures.

Strengths
- Clear and well-organized presentation; the exposition is easy to follow.
- Theoretical results appear correct and technically sound.
- The generalization beyond CVaR is useful, offering a unified view of policy gradients for a broader class of risk measures.
- The results should be of practical interest for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, where policy gradients are a primary tool.

Limitations and novelty
- The contribution is incremental: it largely adapts known risk-neutral policy gradient techniques and earlier CVaR-specific arguments to more general static and dynamically consistent risk measures.
- The paper could better emphasize the scope and boundaries of the generalization (e.g., precise assumptions on the risk measures and on the MDP needed for the gradient forms to hold).

Clarity and suggestions
- Please clarify that MDPs with Markov (time-consistent) risk measures are tractable in principle. The main source of intractability in this paper’s optimization problem is the policy parameterization rather than the choice of risk measure itself. Making this explicit in Section 3 would help readers unfamiliar with the literature.
- When presenting the gradient results, it would help to articulate explicitly how they relate to the classical policy gradient theorem (e.g., which terms change due to the risk measure, and which steps remain identical). A brief, high-level comparison would highlight the simplicity of the extension.
- If space permits, a short illustrative example contrasting the gradients for a static risk measure versus a dynamic (Markov, time-consistent) one would clarify the practical implications of the two settings.
- It would be helpful to state clearly any assumptions on differentiability, integrability, and the structure of the risk measures (e.g., coherent/convex properties, representation via risk envelopes) that are essential for the gradient derivations.

Minor comments and typos
- Theorem 2.1: The notation ξ P_θ is confusing. I suggest aligning with the notation used in Theorem 6.6 of [26] to avoid ambiguity.
- Line 141: “risk enevlop[e]” should be “risk envelope.”
- Section 3: Consider adding an explicit remark that the computational challenges stem from the policy parameterization, not from the (Markov) risk measures themselves.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-written paper that extends policy gradient methodology to a broader class of risk measures in both static and time-consistent dynamic settings. While the novelty is modest, the generalization is clean and likely to be useful to the community, especially given the importance of risk-sensitive objectives in reinforcement learning. With the small clarifications above—particularly around notation and the source of intractability—the paper would make a clear and valuable addition to the literature.
