

The authors address the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. They consider a general class of risk measures defined by standard coherence properties—convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance—and leverage their dual representation as a convex hull of probability distributions. Specifically, the risk of a random cost variable is computed as the maximum expected value over this hull. The paper introduces a key assumption: the boundaries of the convex hull are M-Lipschitz continuous, bounding the maximum derivatives of these boundaries. Under this assumption, the authors explicitly derive optimal policy parameters for the risk measure and develop a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision settings.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. Despite my limited familiarity with risk-sensitive optimization, I found the explanations accessible and logically presented. The primary contribution lies in the unified analysis of a broad class of risk measures encompassing most well-known instances, representing a significant theoretical advance. However, a more thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the work.

**Specific comments:**  
1. The preliminaries section requires clarification. The roles of $P_{\theta}$ and $\xi$ are unclear; explicitly defining their relationship would improve readability.  
2. Equation (2) appears to be a direct restatement of the convex envelope definition. Could the authors clarify why this formulation is more restrictive than the setting in Theorem 2.1?  
3. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion on how the analysis extends to continuous distributions, particularly regarding the dual representation and Lipschitz assumptions.  
4. For Theorem 4.2, a note on the necessity of smoothness in the constraint functions for the proof would enhance clarity.  

This is a strong theoretical contribution advancing risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear compelling, though the proofs warrant careful verification. The unified framework offers a promising foundation for future work in risk-averse reinforcement learning.
