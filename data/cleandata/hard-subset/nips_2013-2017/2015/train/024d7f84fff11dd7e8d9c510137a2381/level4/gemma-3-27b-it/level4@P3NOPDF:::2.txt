This paper introduces methods for estimating policy gradients in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (RL) problems utilizing coherent risk measures, and proposes corresponding RL algorithms. A key contribution lies in generalizing existing methods, previously limited to specific risk measures, to the broader class of coherent risk measures, thereby offering a unified theoretical framework. The efficacy of the proposed algorithms is demonstrated through a toy example in a finance application, where each asset is characterized by its own return distribution.

The motivation for this work is exceptionally clear. The problem formulation and algorithmic developments flow logically from the stated motivation and build effectively upon existing literature. While the experimental evaluation focuses on a static risk problem, limiting the complexity of the environment, the results convincingly demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to achieve risk-averse behavior and its flexibility in accommodating different types of risk aversion as defined by the coherent risk measure. 

**Quality:** The paper is well-written and technically sound. The mathematical derivations are clear and correct, and the authors provide comprehensive references to relevant prior work.

**Clarity:** The clarity of this paper is notably high, exceeding that of many submissions. Concepts are explained in a concise and accessible manner, facilitating understanding of the proposed methods.

**Originality:** The work presents novel algorithms for risk-sensitive RL grounded in, and extending, research on risk-sensitive planning and RL. The generalization to coherent risk measures is not a trivial extension; it requires careful theoretical consideration and algorithmic development. The originality appears sufficient for publication.

**Significance:** Risk-sensitive RL represents an important and increasingly relevant research area. This paper makes a significant contribution by providing clear and well-defined algorithms for tackling risk-sensitive RL problems using coherent risk measures. The demonstration of trained policies operating with varying risk preferences on the static risk example effectively illustrates the practical implications of the theoretical development. The proposed approach is well-motivated and represents a natural progression from existing algorithms and theoretical foundations in policy gradient methods, actor-critic frameworks, and coherent risk measure optimization. 

**Potential Weaknesses/Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper demonstrates a solid theoretical foundation and promising results, the limited scope of the experimental evaluation is a concern. A more complex environment, perhaps involving a dynamic risk landscape or a higher-dimensional state/action space, would strengthen the claims regarding the algorithm’s scalability and generalizability. Additionally, while the finance example provides a concrete illustration, expanding the empirical evaluation to include standard RL benchmarks (even with a risk-sensitive adaptation) would broaden the paper’s impact.

Consider adding a brief discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly concerning the estimation of policy gradients with coherent risk measures. Comparing the computational cost to existing risk-sensitive RL methods would be valuable.

Finally, while the clarity is excellent, a short, intuitive explanation of *why* coherent risk measures are a suitable choice for modeling risk aversion in RL could further enhance the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The work is theoretically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem in reinforcement learning. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the current results are sufficient to demonstrate the validity and potential of the proposed approach. The contribution to the field is significant, and the paper is likely to be of interest to researchers in RL, finance, and related areas.
