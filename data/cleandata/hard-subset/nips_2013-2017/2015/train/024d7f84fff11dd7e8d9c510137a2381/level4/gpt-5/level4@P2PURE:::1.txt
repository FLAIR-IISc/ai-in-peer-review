The paper introduces a unified formulation for static coherent risk measures that yields closed-form policy gradients for many common risks. For cases without analytical solutions, it proposes a general sampling-based algorithm and establishes consistency guarantees for the resulting gradient estimates. The paper also derives policy-gradient expressions for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is compelling and represents a valuable, novel contribution to risk-sensitive decision making. That said, analytical solutions to the Lagrangian saddle-points exist only for a subset of risk measures. The proposed sample-based approximation scheme could make the results broadly applicable by avoiding the need to derive closed-form saddle points. However, the paper provides no empirical evidence that the estimator is practical. An experimental comparison between the sampling-based and analytical policy gradients on multiple problems—e.g., for CVaR—would be very informative.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, the policy-gradient derivations, in this general form, are novel.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but its clarity suffers from the number of distinct topics addressed within eight pages. In particular, the dynamic-risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level, and the absence of algorithmic details and experimental evaluation prevents the reader from assessing whether the derived gradients lead to practical methods. This material seems better suited to a longer journal article. Alternatively, narrowing the scope to static risk measures would make the paper more self-contained and allow for more detail and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the current basic toy example. Overall, this is an interesting paper with potentially broadly applicable results, but the lack of comprehensive experiments makes it difficult to judge. A tighter focus would also help alleviate the clarity issues caused by space constraints.
