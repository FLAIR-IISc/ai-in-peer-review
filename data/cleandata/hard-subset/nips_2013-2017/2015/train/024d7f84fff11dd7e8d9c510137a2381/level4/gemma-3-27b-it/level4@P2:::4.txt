This paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to risk-sensitive policy gradient methods, extending existing work to the broader class of coherent risk measures. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk, expressing it as a maximization over a convex hull of probability distributions, and derive analytical gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk settings. The key contribution lies in unifying the analysis of various risk measures under a common framework, offering a significant generalization of previous research. The paper is well-structured and generally clearly written, making it accessible even to readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of risk-sensitive optimization.

The authors build upon recent advancements in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by moving beyond specific risk measures (like variance or CVaR) to a more general and theoretically appealing class—coherent risk measures. This is a valuable step, as the choice of risk measure is often problem-dependent. The core technical contribution is deriving policy gradient theorems and associated algorithms applicable to this broad class. The approach hinges on assumptions regarding the smoothness of the risk envelope (Assumption 2.2), enabling the development of sampling-based gradient estimation techniques. The resulting algorithms combine sampling with convex programming, providing a practical pathway to optimization. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The work provides a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL, encompassing a wide range of coherent risk measures.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of gradient formulas and the accompanying theoretical results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are strong points.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed sampling-based gradient estimation algorithm offers a viable approach to tackling complex problems.
*   **Clear Structure:** The paper is well-organized and logically presented, making it relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Preliminaries Clarity:** The notation in the preliminaries section could be clearer. Specifically, the roles of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ are initially unclear and warrant further explanation.
*   **Connection to Theorem 2.1:** The authors should elaborate on why Assumption 2.2—regarding the smoothness of the risk envelope—is more restrictive than the more general Theorem 2.1.
*   **Continuous Variable Extension:** A brief discussion about how the analysis extends to continuous variable distributions would strengthen the paper.
*   **Theoretical Verification:** While the theoretical contributions appear compelling, a more rigorous verification of the proofs would increase confidence in the results.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The current empirical evaluation is limited. A more thorough investigation of the proposed algorithm's performance, comparing it to existing methods on benchmark tasks, would be highly beneficial. While the example in Section 6 is insightful, it's not a comprehensive empirical validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning – risk-sensitive decision making – with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The generality of the framework and the derivation of applicable gradient formulas are substantial contributions.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limited empirical evaluation leaves some questions about the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   Minor clarity issues in the preliminaries and regarding the relationship between Assumption 2.2 and Theorem 2.1.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical results are strong, and the proposed framework is both general and practical. While the limited empirical evaluation is a concern, the potential impact of this work justifies acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the minor clarity issues and potentially expanding the empirical analysis in a future version. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
