The paper proposes a new formula for the class of static coherent risks, from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. Additionally, it presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where no analytical solution is available and provides consistency guarantees of the policy gradient estimates. The paper also derives expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a valuable and novel contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, it is essential to note that analytical solutions of the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for some risk measures. The proposed sample-based approximation scheme has the potential to allow the application of the derived results to general coherent risk measures by avoiding the need to derive analytical forms of the saddle points. Nevertheless, the paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating the viability of the proposed estimation scheme in practice. A comparative experimental study between the sample-based policy gradients and the analytical ones in different problems, such as CVaR, would be highly beneficial in assessing the practicality of the proposed method.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in their general form, are novel.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is hindered by the attempt to address multiple issues within the eight-page limit. The presentation of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is particularly high-level, and the lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluations makes it challenging for the reader to assess the practical viability of the derived policy gradients. The content presented in this section may be more suitable for a longer journal publication. Alternatively, focusing solely on static risk measures could make the paper more standalone, allowing for a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the basic toy example provided. While the paper presents potentially generally applicable results, the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluations makes it difficult to fully judge its merits. A narrower focus could help address the clarity issues arising from space constraints.
