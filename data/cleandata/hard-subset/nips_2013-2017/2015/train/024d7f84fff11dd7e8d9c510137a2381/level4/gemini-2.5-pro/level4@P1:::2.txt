This paper presents a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using policy gradient methods. The key contribution is the extension of existing techniques, which have typically focused on specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This provides a unified approach that addresses both static risk over a total discounted return and time-consistent dynamic risk in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). For the static case, the authors derive a new policy gradient formula based on the dual representation of coherent risk measures, leading to an algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. For the dynamic case, they present a novel policy gradient theorem and a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

The paper's primary strengths are its originality, technical quality, and potential significance. By generalizing policy gradient methods to the broad and axiomatically justified class of coherent risk measures, the work unifies several disparate lines of previous research into a single, principled framework. This generalization is non-trivial and provides researchers with a powerful and flexible toolkit for a wide range of risk-sensitive problems. The theoretical development, which leverages the dual representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem, is elegant and rigorous. The paper is also exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the complex subject matter accessible. The derived algorithms are natural extensions of standard policy gradient and actor-critic methods, which increases the likelihood that they will be adopted and built upon by the community. Given the importance of managing risk in sequential decision-making, this paper represents a substantial advance.

The main weakness of the paper lies in its empirical validation. The numerical illustration, a simple portfolio selection problem, effectively demonstrates the flexibility of the framework for static risk and makes a compelling case for choosing appropriate, problem-specific risk measures (e.g., mean-semideviation over mean-variance for heavy-tailed distributions). However, the experiment is limited to this static setting. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an empirical evaluation of the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem. Demonstrating the method on a more complex, sequential decision-making task would provide more convincing evidence of its practical utility.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to an important area of reinforcement learning. The motivation is clear, the technical work is sound, and the proposed framework is a valuable generalization of prior art. While the experimental results could be more comprehensive, the theoretical contributions are novel, significant, and stand on their own.
