### Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using policy gradient methods. The authors move beyond specific risk measures (like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk, CVaR) and develop algorithms applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The key technical insight is to leverage the dual representation of any coherent risk measure, which defines it as an expectation with respect to a worst-case probability distribution chosen from a convex set known as the "risk envelope."

The paper presents two main contributions. First, for the static case (where risk is evaluated over a single cost distribution), the authors derive a novel formula for the policy gradient. This formula is particularly amenable to estimation via sampling and involves solving a convex optimization problem to identify the worst-case distribution and its corresponding Lagrange multipliers. Second, for the dynamic, sequential decision-making case, the paper extends this result to time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. This leads to a new policy gradient theorem and a corresponding actor-critic algorithm, generalizing the standard policy gradient theorem to this broad class of risk objectives. The work successfully unifies and generalizes several previous results in risk-sensitive RL, deriving them as special cases within a simpler, more powerful framework.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. By developing a policy gradient method for the entire class of coherent risk measures, the authors provide a unified theoretical framework for a problem that has previously been addressed with methods tailored to specific risk functions (e.g., mean-variance, CVaR). This is a significant contribution that could consolidate and guide future research in risk-sensitive decision-making.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is elegant and appears sound. The application of the Envelope Theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk is a clever technique that yields a clean and interpretable gradient formula. The connection established between policy gradients, convex optimization, and risk theory is compelling. The extension to the dynamic case via an actor-critic algorithm is a natural and powerful progression.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. It effectively builds the reader's understanding by first introducing the static risk problem and then extending the core ideas to the more complex dynamic setting. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively demonstrate how the general framework recovers and simplifies known results, which helps build intuition.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The main weakness is the lack of a thorough empirical analysis of the proposed algorithms. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is motivational and effectively highlights the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure. However, it does not evaluate the performance of the proposed gradient estimation algorithm itself. An experimental evaluation demonstrating the convergence properties, sample efficiency, or variance of the general gradient estimator (Section 4.3) on a benchmark problem would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, comparing the SAA-based estimator to the analytical version for CVaR (as mentioned in the text) would have been a valuable addition to the main paper.

2.  **Clarity of Preliminaries:** While generally clear, parts of the preliminary section could be improved for readers less familiar with risk theory. The interplay between the underlying probability measure $P_{\theta}$ (induced by the policy) and the worst-case density $\xi$ (chosen from the risk envelope) is central to the paper's method and could be explained more explicitly at the outset.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

1.  In Section 2, the distinction between the underlying probability measure $P_{\theta}$ and the test density $\xi$ could be further clarified. It would be helpful to explicitly state that $P_{\theta}$ is determined by the policy parameters and environment dynamics, while $\xi$ is an adversarial re-weighting of $P_{\theta}$ chosen from the risk envelope $U$ to define the risk.

2.  Assumption 2.2 posits a specific functional form for the risk envelope $U(P_{\theta})$. Could the authors comment on how this is more specific than the general existence of a convex set $U$ stated in Theorem 2.1? It seems this assumption is key as it enables the use of convex programming, but it would be good to clarify that Theorem 2.1 guarantees existence while Assumption 2.2 requires a known, tractable representation.

3.  The paper focuses on finite probability spaces and mentions that the results can be extended to Lp-normed spaces. A brief discussion in the main text on the key challenges or modifications required for such an extension (e.g., for continuous state/action spaces) would significantly enhance the paper's scope and impact.

4.  The proof of Theorem 4.2 relies on the Envelope Theorem. To improve clarity, it would be helpful if the main text briefly mentioned how the smoothness assumptions on the constraint functions (from Assumption 2.2) are necessary for this theorem to hold, providing more intuition behind the derivation.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong theoretical paper that provides an elegant and general approach to risk-sensitive policy gradient optimization. The unified framework is a significant contribution that advances the state of the art by connecting reinforcement learning with the well-established theory of coherent risk measures. While the empirical results are illustrative rather than exhaustive, the theoretical contribution is substantial and compelling. The results appear sound and the paper is well-presented. I recommend acceptance and encourage the authors to consider the suggestions above to further improve the manuscript's clarity and impact.
