

This paper develops policy gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the framework of coherent risk measures, providing a unified approach that generalizes previous work on specific risk measures like variance, CVaR, and mean-semideviation. The authors demonstrate how their framework naturally extends existing results while offering greater flexibility in risk preference specification.

The paper begins by establishing the theoretical foundation of coherent risk measures and their relevance to risk-sensitive decision making in Markov decision processes. The main technical contribution is the derivation of new gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures. For static risk, they present a novel formula combining sampling with convex programming, while for dynamic risk, they develop a policy gradient theorem that extends the actor-critic framework to risk-sensitive settings. The authors prove consistency of their gradient estimators and demonstrate how their approach recovers and simplifies previous results for specific risk measures.

The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and logical flow. The mathematical derivations are rigorous, and the authors provide thorough motivation for each technical step. They carefully reference relevant prior work, including recent NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL. The experimental section, while limited to a static risk problem, effectively demonstrates the practical utility of their framework through a trading agent example with three assets having different return distributions. The results clearly show how different risk preferences (risk-neutral, mean-semideviation, and mean-standard-deviation) lead to distinct investment strategies, highlighting the framework's flexibility.

Strengths:
- The unified coherent risk framework represents a significant theoretical advance over previous work on specific risk measures.
- The algorithms are well-motivated and extend naturally from the theoretical results.
- The paper provides rigorous mathematical proofs and consistency results.
- The experimental results effectively illustrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach.
- The writing is exceptionally clear and accessible to the RL community.

Weaknesses:
- The experiments are limited to static risk problems, and it would be valuable to see the algorithm applied to dynamic risk problems as well.
- The computational complexity analysis of the proposed method could be expanded to better compare with existing approaches.
- The paper does not thoroughly address how the approach scales to large state spaces, though this is partially addressed in the supplementary material.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations and well-supported claims. The authors carefully address the strengths and limitations of their approach, and the experiments provide relevant validation.

Clarity: The paper is outstanding in clarity, with a logical structure, consistent notation, and sufficient detail for expert reproducibility. The theoretical results are presented in an accessible manner.

Originality: The paper's main contribution is the unified framework for coherent risk measures, which generalizes previous specific-risk methods. While individual components build on prior work, the synthesis represents a substantial conceptual advance that is clearly differentiated from previous NIPS publications.

Significance: Risk-sensitive RL is increasingly important for real-world applications where managing cost variability is critical. This paper provides a general framework that can be applied to a wide range of risk-sensitive problems, offering practitioners the flexibility to choose risk preferences based on problem-specific characteristics. The ability to handle both static and dynamic risk measures within a single framework represents a meaningful advancement.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a theoretically grounded, unified framework for risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures
- Generalizes and extends previous results on specific risk measures
- Offers practical flexibility in risk preference specification
- Clear, well-written paper with strong technical content
- Demonstrates the approach's utility through a meaningful example

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental evaluation (only static risk problem)
- Computational complexity analysis could be more comprehensive
- The paper does not directly compare with the most recent risk-sensitive RL methods

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical framework is elegant, the technical content is rigorous, and the practical implications are clear. While the experimental evaluation could be expanded, the theoretical contribution is significant and the paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
