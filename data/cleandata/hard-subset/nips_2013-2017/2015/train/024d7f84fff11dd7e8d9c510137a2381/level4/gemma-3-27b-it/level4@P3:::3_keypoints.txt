- Extends policy gradient methods to the full class of coherent risk measures, unifying and generalizing prior work like CVaR.
- Builds on and correctly situates itself within existing literature on risk-sensitive RL, mean-variance, CVaR, coherent risk measures, and robust MDPs.
- Main technical contribution: derivation of gradient formulas for coherent risk measures (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2).
- Provides sampling-based gradient estimation algorithms to make the approach practical for large-scale problems.
- Numerical illustration shows the approach's potential and the importance of choosing appropriate risk measures.
- Strength: Generality — offers a broadly applicable risk-sensitive RL framework beyond specific risk measures.
- Strength: Theoretical soundness — gradient derivations leverage established results and appear technically correct.
- Strength: Clarity and organization — paper is well-written with clear notation and context.
- Strength: Practical relevance — addresses sampling-based estimation, crucial for real-world use.
- Weakness: Contribution is somewhat incremental, extending existing policy gradient ideas rather than introducing radically new techniques.
- Weakness: Computational complexity — requires solving a convex optimization at each iteration, raising scalability concerns for large state spaces.
- Weakness: Reliance on assumptions (e.g., Assumptions 2.2 and 4.1); sensitivity to assumption violations is not explored.
- Weakness: Limited empirical evaluation — only a small numerical example; needs broader benchmarking.
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound generalization, useful gradient formulas, practical algorithms, and clear exposition.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental nature, potential scalability limits, and reliance on unexamined assumptions.
- Overall assessment: solid and worthwhile contribution that merits acceptance though not groundbreaking.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Requested revision: Clarify notation ξPθ in Theorem 2.1 to align with standard references.
- Requested revision: Expand Section 3 to state that intractability stems from policy parametrization rather than from the risk measure itself.
- Requested revision: Provide more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability-improvement strategies.
- Requested revision: Conduct a more extensive empirical evaluation on benchmark MDPs.
