

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under coherent risk measures, extending previous work that focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR. The authors develop policy gradient algorithms for both static and time-consistent dynamic coherent risk measures, providing a theoretical foundation that generalizes and unifies prior results. For static coherent risk, they derive a gradient formula using the risk envelope representation from convex optimization, enabling sampling-based gradient estimation through convex programming. For dynamic risk, they extend the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures, proposing an actor-critic style algorithm that combines value function approximation with the static risk gradient framework.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a theoretically sound connection between coherent risk measures and reinforcement learning through the risk envelope representation (Theorem 2.1). Second, it develops a consistent gradient estimator for static coherent risk using sample average approximation, with rigorous convergence guarantees (Proposition 4.4). Third, it generalizes the policy gradient theorem to dynamic Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2), extending the applicability of policy gradient methods to a broader class of risk-sensitive objectives. The authors demonstrate how previous approaches for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) emerge as special cases of their framework, providing a more elegant and unified perspective.

Regarding quality, the theoretical development is sound and well-supported by rigorous proofs in the supplementary material. The paper carefully addresses the mathematical foundations of coherent risk measures and their application to reinforcement learning, with appropriate attention to technical details. However, the lack of empirical validation for the proposed sampling-based gradient estimators is a significant weakness. While the authors provide a toy example in Section 6, it only compares three risk-averse policies for a simple portfolio selection problem, without demonstrating the practical efficacy of their proposed algorithms. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, particularly comparing the sampling-based approach with analytical solutions for specific risk measures like CVaR, would substantially strengthen the paper.

The paper's originality is strong. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a unified framework for policy gradient methods under the entire class of coherent risk measures. The approach generalizes and simplifies previous results for specific risk measures, demonstrating the value of the coherent risk framework. The paper properly contextualizes its contributions by relating them to recent work on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 9, 18, 21, 29, 30]) and properly references related work on coherent risk measures [2, 25, 26].

The significance of this work is substantial for the risk-sensitive RL community. By providing a general framework that applies to all coherent risk measures, the paper enables researchers to more flexibly design risk preferences for specific applications. The authors correctly note that the choice of risk measure should be problem-dependent, and their framework allows practitioners to select the most appropriate measure for their specific context. This flexibility is particularly valuable in applications where managing tail risks or variability is critical, such as finance and operations research.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written but suffers from attempting to cover both static and dynamic risk measures within a limited page count. The dynamic risk section (Section 5) is presented at a high level, with insufficient detail about the actor-critic algorithm and its implementation. The authors mention "space limitation" as a reason for omitting full algorithm details, but this makes it difficult for readers to assess the practical viability of their approach. The current presentation would be more suitable for a journal publication than a conference paper. A more focused submission that concentrates on static coherent risk measures would allow for deeper theoretical development and more comprehensive empirical validation, addressing the current lack of experimental evidence.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of empirical validation for the proposed sampling-based gradient estimation method. While the theoretical consistency guarantees are valuable, the absence of practical demonstrations makes it difficult to assess the approach's real-world utility. The authors should provide a more thorough experimental comparison between their sampling-based approach and analytical gradient methods for specific risk measures, particularly for CVaR where the analytical solution is known. Additionally, the numerical example in Section 6 is too simplistic to demonstrate the framework's practical value.

In conclusion, this paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that unifies previous work on specific risk measures. The theoretical contributions are significant and well-supported, but the lack of empirical validation for the proposed algorithms is a substantial weakness. The paper would be significantly stronger if it focused on static coherent risk measures, allowing for more detailed theoretical development and comprehensive experimental evaluation. As currently written, the paper has strong theoretical appeal but lacks sufficient practical validation to fully demonstrate its value for the NIPS community. I recommend rejection on the grounds that the empirical evidence is insufficient to support the significance claim, though the theoretical contribution is valuable.
