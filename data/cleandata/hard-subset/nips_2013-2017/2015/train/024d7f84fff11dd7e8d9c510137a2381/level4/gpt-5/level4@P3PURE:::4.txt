The authors study the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. They consider a broad class of risk measures defined by standard coherence properties, including convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. Their approach relies on a dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) is given by the maximum expected value over this hull. The paper assumes that the family of coherent risk measures satisfies a bound on the maximum derivatives of the hull’s boundary (i.e., the boundary is M-Lipschitz continuous). Under this assumption, the authors analytically derive optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure and propose a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) settings.

The paper is well written and well organized. Although I am not deeply familiar with risk-sensitive optimization, I could follow most of the exposition. The main contribution is a unified analysis covering a general class of risk measures that includes most well-known ones; this strikes me as a significant theoretical contribution. However, a deeper empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the work.

Comments and questions:
- The preliminaries section is not clear enough. I was confused about the respective roles of P_θ and ξ.
- Isn’t Equation (2) simply the definition of a convex envelope? Why is this more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?
- Please add a comment on how the analysis generalizes to distributions over continuous variables.
- It would be helpful to mention in the short paper the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in the proof of Theorem 4.2.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that offers a general approach to risk-sensitive decision making. The results appear compelling, but the proofs need to be carefully verified.
