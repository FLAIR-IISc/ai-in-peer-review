The authors address the problem of policy parameter optimization with respect to a given risk measure. Their work focuses on a broad class of coherent risk measures, characterized by properties including convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The core of their approach leverages a dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions, defining risk as the maximum expected value within this hull. A key assumption – bounding the maximum derivatives of the convex hull’s boundaries with an M-Lipschitz continuity constraint – allows for the analytical derivation of optimal policy parameters. Furthermore, the authors present a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (single-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision problems.

This is a well-structured and clearly written paper. Despite my limited background in risk-sensitive optimization, I found the explanations largely accessible. The primary strength of this work lies in its unified analysis of a general class of risk measures encompassing many commonly used measures, representing a significant contribution to the field. However, a more extensive empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the results.

Several points warrant further consideration: 1- The preliminaries section could benefit from clarification regarding the roles of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ. 2- Equation 2 appears to represent the definition of a convex envelope; it’s unclear why this is presented as more restrictive than Theorem 2.1. 3- A discussion of how the analysis extends to distributions over continuous variables would be valuable. 4- The connection between the smoothness of constraint functions and the proof of Theorem 4.2 could be explicitly mentioned in the paper. Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution offering a general framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear promising, though a thorough verification of the proofs is recommended.
