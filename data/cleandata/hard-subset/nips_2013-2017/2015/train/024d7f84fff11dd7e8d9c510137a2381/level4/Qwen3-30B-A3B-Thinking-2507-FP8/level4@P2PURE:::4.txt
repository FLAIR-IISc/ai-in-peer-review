

The authors address the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. The paper considers a general class of risk measures defined via fundamental coherence properties—convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The approach leverages a dual representation of coherent risk measures as the convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) is expressed as the maximum expected value over this hull. A key assumption bounds the maximum derivatives of the convex hull boundaries (i.e., the hull boundaries are M-Lipschitz continuous). Under this assumption, the authors analytically derive the optimal policy parameters (with respect to the risk measure) and propose a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static (one-step decision) and dynamic (sequential) cases.  

The paper is well-written and logically organized. Despite my limited familiarity with risk-sensitive optimization, I found the exposition largely accessible. The primary contribution lies in the unified analysis of a broad class of risk measures encompassing most well-known examples, which constitutes a significant advancement. However, a more thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the work.  

Specific comments:  
1. The preliminaries section lacks clarity regarding the roles of $P_{\theta}$ and $\xi$.  
2. Equation 2 appears to be the standard definition of a convex envelope; why is this more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?  
3. It would be valuable to comment on the generalization of the analysis to distributions over continuous variables.  
4. The paper should explicitly discuss the role of constraint function smoothness in establishing Theorem 4.2.  

This is a strong theoretical contribution offering a general framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. The results are compelling, though the proofs require careful verification.
