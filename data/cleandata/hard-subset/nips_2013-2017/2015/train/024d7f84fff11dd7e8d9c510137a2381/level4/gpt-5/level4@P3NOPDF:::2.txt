Summary
The paper develops methods for estimating policy gradients in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using the general class of coherent risk measures, and builds corresponding RL algorithms (including policy gradient and actor-critic variants). This unifies and extends prior work that targeted specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR) by providing a framework that applies broadly to coherent risks. The approach is demonstrated on a finance-inspired static portfolio selection problem in which each asset has its own return distribution, illustrating how different risk preferences shape learned policies.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem formulation: The need for risk-sensitive optimization beyond expectation-based objectives is well motivated, and the transition from problem setup to algorithm design follows naturally from prior work.
- Generality and unification: Extending gradient estimation from particular risk measures to the full class of coherent risks is conceptually appealing and practically useful. The paper offers a coherent view that can accommodate multiple risk types within a single framework.
- Technical soundness: The derivations appear correct, and the connections to the literature on risk-sensitive planning and RL are accurate and complete. Notation and assumptions are well specified.
- Clarity and readability: The writing is clear and well organized. Key concepts and algorithms are easy to follow, and the presentation quality is above average.
- Empirical illustration: Although simple, the experiment convincingly shows risk-averse behavior and demonstrates flexibility across different coherent risk choices.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited empirical scope: The evaluation is restricted to a static risk problem. To support claims of broad applicability in RL, it would be valuable to include dynamic MDPs or sequential decision-making tasks. Even one or two standard benchmarks (e.g., gridworld with stochastic costs, inventory control, or simple continuous-control settings) would strengthen the case.
- Baselines and comparisons: The empirical study would benefit from comparisons to established risk-sensitive baselines (e.g., CVaR policy gradient methods or risk-neutral policy gradients) to contextualize performance and sample efficiency.
- Sensitivity analysis: Because coherent risks are parameterized (e.g., by a confidence level), it would be informative to report sensitivity of performance to risk parameters and to policy/critic hyperparameters. This would clarify the practical robustness of the proposed estimators.
- Computational aspects: A discussion of computational cost, variance of the gradient estimates, and any variance-reduction techniques would help practitioners. Reporting runtime and sample complexity trends across risk measures would make the contribution more actionable.
- Practical guidance: Since practitioners may face a choice among multiple coherent risks, it would help to include brief guidance on when to prefer one over another, or observations from the experiments that highlight trade-offs.

Quality
The paper is technically sound, with careful derivations and appropriate use of assumptions. The algorithms are clearly specified, and the theoretical framing aligns with modern RL practice. References are comprehensive and accurately positioned relative to this work.

Clarity
Exposition is strong. The paper is easy to read, with clear notation, intuitive explanations of key steps, and a logical flow from motivation to algorithms to experiments. Figures and examples effectively illustrate the main points.

Originality
The extension from specific risk functionals to the broader class of coherent risk measures is nontrivial and novel within the RL policy gradient setting. The unifying treatment and associated algorithms constitute a meaningful advance over existing methods targeted at particular risks.

Significance
Risk-sensitive RL is important for safety-critical and finance applications alike. By providing a general framework for coherent risk measures along with implementable policy gradient and actor-critic methods, the paper makes a significant contribution. While the current empirical evaluation is limited, the conceptual advance and clarity of the algorithms suggest strong potential impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can the authors comment on the variance properties of their gradient estimators and any control variates used?
- How does the approach scale with the size of the state/action space and with function approximation (e.g., deep policies/critics)?
- Are there conditions under which the proposed estimators reduce to known formulas for specific risks (e.g., CVaR), and do the empirical results match those specialized methods?
- In dynamic settings, do the authors assume time-consistent coherent risk measures, and how does this affect the theoretical guarantees?

Minor comments
- Providing pseudocode for both the policy gradient and actor-critic variants would increase reproducibility.
- A brief summary table of coherent risks considered, their defining properties, and parameterizations would aid readers.

Overall assessment
A well-written and technically solid paper that advances risk-sensitive RL by unifying policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. The contribution is original and significant, and the empirical illustration, while simple, supports the claims about risk-aware behavior and flexibility. The main limitation is the narrow empirical scope; adding dynamic benchmarks and stronger baselines would substantially strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance if the authors can address the empirical limitations and provide additional guidance on computational and variance considerations.
