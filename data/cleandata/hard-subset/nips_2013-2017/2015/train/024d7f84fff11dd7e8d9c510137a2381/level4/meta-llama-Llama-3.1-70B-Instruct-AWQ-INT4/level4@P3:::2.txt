Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures presents a novel approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (RL) by extending the policy gradient method to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This work is well-motivated, as risk-sensitive objectives have gained popularity in RL as a means to regularize the variability of the total cost/reward in a Markov decision process (MDP).

The authors provide a clear and concise problem formulation, which naturally follows from their motivation and previous works. They consider both static and time-consistent dynamic risk measures, and their approach applies to the whole class of coherent risk measures, thereby generalizing and unifying previous approaches that have focused on individual risk measures.

The technical soundness of the paper is evident in the equations and proofs presented. The authors have made a significant effort to provide all relevant references, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the existing literature.

The clarity of the paper is well above average, with the authors presenting complex concepts in a clear and concise manner. The organization of the paper is logical, and the writing is free of errors.

The originality of the paper is sufficient, as the authors propose new algorithms for risk-sensitive RL problems based on related works on risk-sensitive planning and RL. The extension of the proposed approach is not straightforward, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field.

The significance of the paper lies in its clear algorithms for solving risk-sensitive RL with coherent risk measures. The authors demonstrate the behavior of trained policies with different risk settings on a static risk problem, providing a natural extension to existing algorithms and problems (policy gradient, actor-critic, and coherent risk measure optimization).

The experiment, although simple, supports the quality of the algorithm in terms of its risk-aversive behavior and flexibility in different risk types. However, it would be beneficial to see more comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the applicability of the proposed approach to more complex problems.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL. The proposed approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of problems, and the authors have demonstrated its effectiveness in a simple yet illustrative example.
