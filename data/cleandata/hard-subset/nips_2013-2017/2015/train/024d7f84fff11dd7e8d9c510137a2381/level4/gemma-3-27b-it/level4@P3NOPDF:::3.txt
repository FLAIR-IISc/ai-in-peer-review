## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission: [Paper Title – *Please Replace with Actual Title*]

This paper analyzes the risk-averse Markov Decision Process (MDP) setting, considering both static and dynamically consistent risk measures (mappings). The primary contribution lies in deriving the gradient for policy optimization in both settings, generalizing previous work that primarily focused on Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). This extension, while incremental, represents a useful addition to the literature. The derived results demonstrate a natural and straightforward extension of policy gradient methods commonly employed in risk-neutral MDPs.

The paper is exceptionally well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The authors present a technically sound argument, and the results appear correct based on my understanding. The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the paper maintains a consistent level of rigor throughout. 

The strength of this work resides in its generality. By moving beyond the specific case of CVaR, the authors broaden the applicability of risk-averse policy gradient methods. While the extension isn’t transformative, it is a solid and important step towards a more general understanding of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors correctly identify the challenges inherent in optimizing policies under general risk measures and offer a tractable gradient-based approach.

I have a few minor comments that could further improve the clarity and impact of the paper:

* **Theorem 2.1:** The notation “ξ P<sub>θ</sub>” is somewhat confusing. I recommend referring to Theorem 6.6 in [26] and adopting the notation used there for improved consistency and readability. This would benefit readers familiar with that earlier work and reduce potential ambiguity.

* **Line 141:** Typographical error: “risk enevlop[e]” should be corrected to "risk envelope".

* **Section 3:** To aid readers less familiar with the field, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that MDPs equipped with Markov risk measures are generally tractable. The primary source of computational difficulty in *this* work stems from the policy parametrization, and not from the choice of the risk measure itself. Clarifying this distinction will help readers understand the specific challenges addressed by the authors and the significance of their contribution. Emphasizing this point reinforces the importance of the proposed gradient-based optimization approach, as it tackles the difficulty arising from the policy representation.

In summary, this paper presents a well-executed and theoretically sound extension of existing policy gradient methods to a more general class of risk measures in MDPs. The paper is clearly written and easily accessible. While the contribution is incremental, it is a valuable addition to the field, offering a practical approach to risk-averse reinforcement learning. I recommend acceptance.
