- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures, covering static and Markov dynamic settings.  
- For static risk, uses the dual risk-envelope and envelope theorem to derive a Lagrangian saddle-point gradient formula and likelihood-ratio style estimators.  
- When saddle points lack closed form, proposes a sampling-plus-convex-programming (SAA) procedure and proves consistency of the gradient estimator.  
- For dynamic risk, derives a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk expressed as expectations under a ξ-weighted transition kernel with modified stage costs.  
- Connects Markov coherent risk to robust MDPs and proposes an actor-critic algorithmic template using this equivalence.  
- Unifies and generalizes prior work (mean-variance, CVaR, exponential utilities, dynamic coherent risk / robust MDPs).  
- Strength: conceptual unification via a single coherent static risk gradient that recovers known special cases.  
- Strength: provides a general, consistent two-step estimator (sampling + convex program) for cases without closed-form solutions.  
- Strength: novel dynamic policy-gradient theorem enabling actor-critic approaches.  
- Strength: solid theoretical development leveraging convex analysis and the envelope theorem; simpler CVaR gradient derivation.  
- Weakness: no empirical evaluation of the core SAA estimator or comparison to analytical gradients to assess bias, variance, and sample complexity.  
- Weakness: dynamic actor-critic method is high-level with key implementation and sampling details deferred to the supplement and no experiments.  
- Weakness: potential computational burden and scalability concerns—requires solving convex programs per batch/state and multiple next-state samples, with no cost quantification.  
- Weakness: relies on explicit, differentiable risk envelopes (Assumption 2.2); more guidance needed for instantiating envelopes, continuous spaces, and function approximation.  
- Quality: theoretically sound and useful consistency result, but empirical support is very limited (only one toy example).  
- Clarity: generally well written but dense; dynamic section and implementation details are supplement-heavy and could be clearer.  
- Originality: high — first unified policy-gradient treatment for full coherent risk class and novel dynamic theorem.  
- Significance: potentially high impact for risk-sensitive RL, but immediate practical effect is unclear without empirical evidence on variance, efficiency, and cost.  
- Suggestions: run experiments comparing SAA vs analytical gradients, add dynamic-risk experiments and ablations, quantify per-iteration computational cost, and discuss solver/warm-start strategies.  
- Suggestions: provide constructions of risk envelopes for common coherent risks, address continuous state/action extensions, clarify stationary-policy conditions, and supply actor-critic pseudocode and variance-reduction techniques.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, novel dynamic theorem, clear generalization of known special cases.  
- Cons against acceptance: lack of empirical validation, insufficient algorithmic detail for dynamic case, and unclear computational scalability.  
- Overall: interesting and potentially impactful but needs substantially more empirical evaluation or a narrowed focus to strengthen the practical case for acceptance.
