The paper studies how to choose policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. It considers a broad class of coherent risk measures defined by standard axioms such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The approach leverages the dual representation of coherent risk, expressing the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) as the supremum of its expected value over a convex set (convex hull) of probability distributions. The authors assume a regularity condition on this family of risk measures: the boundary of the convex hull is M-Lipschitz (i.e., the boundary derivatives are uniformly bounded). Under this assumption, they derive analytical characterizations of optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk, and present a general gradient-estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision problems.

The paper is clearly written and well organized. Although I am not an expert in risk-sensitive optimization, I was able to follow the main arguments. The unifying treatment of a wide class of risk measures—encompassing most standard ones—is, in my view, the key contribution and a valuable one. That said, a more extensive empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would strengthen the paper.

Comments and questions:
1) The preliminaries could be clearer. In particular, the respective roles of P_θ and ξ were confusing. Please clarify what each represents (e.g., P_θ as the parameterized data-generating distribution or transition kernel, and ξ as the adversarial or reweighted distribution in the risk envelope), and how they interact.
2) Is Equation (2) essentially the definition of the convex envelope (or support function) representation of coherent risk? If so, in what sense is it more restrictive than Theorem 2.1? A brief explanation of the additional assumptions or constraints imposed by Eq. (2) would help.
3) Please comment on how the analysis extends to continuous state/action spaces and continuous distributions. What additional technical conditions are needed (e.g., measurability, compactness, differentiability of density functions), and do the Lipschitz assumptions on the risk envelope carry over?
4) It would be helpful to mention explicitly in the short paper how smoothness of the constraint functions is used in the proof of Theorem 4.2. For example, where do differentiability and Lipschitz or curvature bounds enter, and are there points where weaker conditions would suffice?

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that offers a general and elegant framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. The results are compelling; however, the proofs should be carefully checked, and a deeper empirical study would improve the paper’s practical impact.
