Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with both static and time-consistent dynamic risk objectives. The central contribution is a unified policy-gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. For static risk, the authors derive a gradient formula via the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem, yielding a likelihood-ratio style estimator that combines sampling with convex programming. They illustrate the approach on CVaR and mean-semideviation and propose a general sample-average approximation (SAA) estimator with a consistency guarantee. For dynamic risk, they consider Markov coherent risk measures [24], derive a policy gradient theorem that relates the gradient to a suitable value function under ξ-weighted transitions, and propose an actor-critic algorithm that uses value-function approximation (leveraging the equivalence to robust MDPs [19] and techniques from [31]).

The work generalizes several strands of prior art that target specific risks: exponential utility [6], mean-variance and variance-related criteria [29, 21, 28, 14], CVaR in static MDPs [30, 8], and dynamic coherent risk for linear systems [20, 9]. It relies on the coherent risk representation of Artzner et al. and the convex-analytic development of Ruszczyński and Shapiro [2, 26], extending these ideas to policy-gradient RL where the policy impacts trajectory distributions. The dynamic result connects to robust MDPs [19] and approximate planning in large state spaces [31]. Overall, the paper provides a clean unification and modest extension of known policy-gradient ideas to the coherent-risk setting.

Evaluation
- Quality: The technical development is sound as far as I can assess. The static gradient formula follows cleanly from the dual representation and an envelope-theorem argument. The SAA-based estimator is natural, and the consistency statement (under mild compactness and smoothness conditions on the risk envelope) is appropriate. The dynamic policy-gradient theorem is plausible and reduces to the standard risk-neutral policy gradient when U collapses to the singleton density. Assumptions (bounded likelihood ratios, known and differentiable risk-envelopes, finiteness) are clearly stated. The main weakness is limited empirical validation (a single toy example) and no quantitative study of variance, bias, or sample complexity, especially for the dynamic actor-critic.

- Clarity: The paper is well organized and mostly easy to follow. The connection between coherent risk, robust MDPs, and the proposed actor-critic is helpful. Some notation could be streamlined (see minor comments), and it would help to more explicitly separate the roles of policy parameterization vs. risk measure in tractability.

- Originality: The work primarily generalizes existing policy-gradient results (including CVaR) to the broad class of coherent risks. The static development is a natural extension of the risk-envelope representation. The dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and its actor-critic instantiation are, to my knowledge, new within RL, though conceptually aligned with known robust MDP equivalences. I view the originality as moderate.

- Significance: Providing a unified recipe to handle many coherent risks is useful, particularly for practitioners who may want to choose a risk that matches application needs (sensitivity to tails, computational tractability). The practical impact is tempered by reliance on explicit risk envelopes and KKT computations, the need for a simulator that supports multiple next-state samples, and the limited experiments. Significance is moderate.

Strengths
- Unified policy-gradient treatment of coherent risk (static and dynamic), subsuming prior results for specific risks.
- Clear derivations using standard tools (dual representation, envelope theorem, likelihood ratios).
- General SAA-based gradient estimator with a consistency result.
- Dynamic policy-gradient theorem and actor-critic architecture grounded in robust MDP theory.
- Good exposition and motivation for time consistency.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is minimal; no results on realistic RL domains or dynamic settings.
- Practicality concerns: computing ξ*, KKT multipliers, and ξ-weighted transitions per state may be costly; reliance on a simulator capable of multiple next-state samples.
- Assumes explicit, differentiable risk envelopes U(Pθ); not all coherent risks used in practice are provided in this canonical form.
- Finite outcome-space exposition; extensions to continuous spaces are deferred and not demonstrated.
- Variance control and sample complexity are not analyzed; rare-event sensitivity (e.g., CVaR) may demand variance reduction.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide at least one substantive dynamic-risk experiment (even small-scale) demonstrating the actor-critic, including runtime, variance of gradient estimates, and comparison to risk-neutral baselines.
- Discuss computational cost: how often ξ* and KKT multipliers must be recomputed, and complexity per gradient step. Include wall-clock or iteration cost in experiments.
- Elaborate on constructing U(Pθ) for common coherent risks (e.g., spectral risk [1]) and how to obtain ∂fi/∂p and ∂ge/∂p in practice when Pθ depends on θ via the policy.
- Clarify the tractability point in Section 3: dynamic programming for Markov coherent risks is tractable in tabular settings [24]; the difficulty here arises from policy parameterization and large/continuous state spaces.
- Report variance/bias comparisons between the analytic (e.g., CVaR) gradient and the SAA estimator in the main text, and include variance-reduction techniques (e.g., baselines, control variates, importance sampling for tails [3]).
- Outline how the approach extends to continuous state/action spaces (function approximation in the static estimator, sampling of ξ-weighted transitions).

Minor comments
- Theorem 2.1: The notation ξ Pθ is potentially confusing. Consider aligning with the notation in [26, Thm. 6.6] and clearly defining ξ as a density multiplier and ξPθ as the induced measure.
- Typo around line 141: “risk enevlop[e]” → “risk envelope.”
- Be consistent about overloading ρ for static vs. dynamic measures; a brief notational reminder when transitioning to (3) would help.
- Clarify Assumption 2.2’s smoothness in p and how it holds for the examples considered.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, clean generalization that unifies multiple prior risk-sensitive PG results.
- Provides a reusable recipe (including SAA) for a broad class of coherent risks.
- Introduces a dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with an actor-critic design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical evidence and no dynamic experiments; unclear practical scalability.
- Dependence on explicit, differentiable risk envelopes and KKT computations may limit applicability.
- Incremental in spirit; many ingredients are standard once the dual representation is adopted.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is well written and technically sound, and the unification it offers is useful, but the authors should strengthen the empirical section and clarify computational aspects to maximize impact.
