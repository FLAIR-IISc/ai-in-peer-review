

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending prior work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance) to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic Markov coherent risk, leveraging the representation theorem (Theorem 2.1) to formulate sampling-based estimators. For static risk, they propose a convex programming-based approach; for dynamic risk, they develop a novel actor-critic algorithm. The work generalizes and subsumes prior results (e.g., [8, 30] for CVaR; [29, 21] for mean-variance), demonstrating how these special cases emerge as instances of their framework. The numerical example in Section 6 illustrates the flexibility of their approach in selecting risk-averse policies for financial asset allocation, though it remains limited to static risk.

**Quality**: The technical contributions are sound and well-justified. The derivation of Theorem 4.2 (static risk gradient) and Theorem 5.2 (dynamic risk gradient) is rigorous, with clear connections to convex optimization and the envelope theorem. The consistency proof (Proposition 4.4) addresses sampling-based estimation rigorously. However, the experimental validation is limited: the numerical example only tests the static risk algorithm (Section 4) and does not evaluate the dynamic risk actor-critic approach (Section 5), which is a notable gap given the paper’s focus on time-consistent risk. While the finance example supports the *intuition* of risk-shaping, it lacks quantitative comparisons against prior methods on dynamic tasks or larger-scale environments.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical exposition—particularly the risk envelope formulation (Assumption 2.2) and the derivation of gradient formulas—avoids unnecessary complexity. The unifying perspective (e.g., showing CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases) significantly enhances readability. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the prose is precise without being verbose. Minor improvements could include a brief discussion of computational complexity for the convex programming step (Section 4.3) and clearer motivation for the risk envelope assumptions.

**Originality**: The paper’s core contribution—unifying risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework—is highly original. Prior work focused narrowly on individual risk measures (e.g., [8, 30] for CVaR; [29, 21] for mean-variance), while this paper identifies a *common structure* (Theorem 2.1) enabling a single algorithmic approach. This is not merely an extension but a conceptual shift: the authors demonstrate that all coherent risk measures share the same gradient estimation structure, which had not been recognized before. The dynamic risk extension (Section 5) further advances the field by resolving time-consistency concerns absent in earlier methods ([20, 9]). The related work section properly contextualizes this contribution against [24, 25, 31], highlighting the novelty.

**Significance**: The significance is strong. Risk-sensitive RL is increasingly important in finance, operations, and safety-critical systems, where managing cost variability is as crucial as minimizing expectation. By providing a scalable, unified framework—enabling risk-shaping for *any* coherent measure—the paper directly addresses a key practical need. The demonstration of how different risk measures (e.g., mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to distinct policy behaviors (Section 6) underscores its utility for domain-specific risk preferences. While the current experiments are limited, the framework’s generality and theoretical grounding suggest broad applicability to future work on dynamic risk optimization (e.g., in robotic control or energy systems).

**Pro/Con for Acceptance**:
*Pros*:  
- Unifies disparate risk-sensitive RL methods under a theoretically sound framework.  
- Provides novel gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2, 5.2) with practical sampling-based estimators.  
- Demonstrates clear, domain-relevant benefits of risk-shaping (Section 6).  
- Rigorous technical foundation with well-motivated assumptions.  

*Cons*:  
- Limited empirical validation (static risk only; dynamic risk algorithm untested).  
- No comparison to state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL baselines (e.g., [20, 29, 30]).  
- Computational scalability of convex programming step (Section 4.3) not addressed.  

**Overall**: This paper makes a significant, high-quality contribution to risk-sensitive RL. The theoretical unification is elegant and impactful, and the algorithms are well-motivated. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the work’s conceptual novelty and technical soundness strongly support acceptance. It advances the state of the art by providing a generalizable foundation for risk-aware decision making, which is essential for real-world applications where risk management is critical. The clarity and precision of the presentation further elevate its contribution.
