This paper presents a framework for estimating policy gradients in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning problems, specifically addressing the general class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive policy gradient theorems for these measures and propose practical actor-critic algorithms based on these theoretical foundations. In doing so, the work extends existing methods that have typically focused on specific, individual risk measures, thereby offering a more unified and general approach to the problem. The effectiveness of the proposed algorithms is illustrated with a financial portfolio optimization task, demonstrating the ability to train policies that exhibit different types of risk-averse behavior.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is of high quality, both in its presentation and technical content. It is well-written, with a logical flow that clearly articulates the motivation, problem formulation, and proposed solutions. The mathematical derivations are rigorous and technically sound. The authors have done an excellent job of situating their work within the existing literature on risk-sensitive planning and reinforcement learning, providing all relevant citations. The clarity is well above average; complex concepts related to coherent risk measures and their corresponding policy gradients are explained in an accessible manner, which significantly benefits the reader.

**Originality and Significance**

The originality of this work lies in its successful generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the broad and axiomatic class of coherent risk measures. While building on prior work in risk-sensitive RL, the extension from specific measures (like CVaR) to a general class is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. This generalization provides a unified theoretical lens through which to view and tackle risk-sensitive problems.

The significance of this research is substantial. Risk-sensitive decision-making is a critical and increasingly important area of reinforcement learning, with direct applications in fields where worst-case performance is a major concern, such as finance, robotics, and autonomous systems. This paper provides two key contributions:
1.  A principled theoretical framework for optimizing coherent risk measures via policy gradients.
2.  Clear, practical algorithms that make these theoretical insights actionable.

By presenting concrete algorithms and demonstrating their behavior, this paper provides a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners interested in developing more robust and risk-aware agents. The proposed approach is a natural and important extension to the standard policy gradient and actor-critic literature.

**Evaluation**

The empirical evaluation, though conducted on a relatively simple static risk problem, effectively validates the paper's central claims. The experiments clearly show that the trained policies exhibit the expected risk-averse behavior and successfully demonstrate the flexibility of the framework to accommodate different risk preferences.

A potential avenue for strengthening the work in the future would be to apply the proposed algorithms to a more complex, dynamic, and sequential decision-making problem (e.g., a standard benchmark control task). This would more conclusively demonstrate the algorithms' effectiveness in the full reinforcement learning setting. Nonetheless, this does not diminish the core contribution of the present work, which is primarily the development of the underlying theory and algorithms.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the important field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It is well-executed, clearly presented, and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.
