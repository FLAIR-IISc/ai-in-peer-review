This paper addresses the problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient methods for the general class of coherent risk measures. The authors present a unified framework that covers both static and time-consistent dynamic risk measures. The core contribution is the derivation of novel gradient formulas that leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, where risk is expressed as an expectation over a worst-case probability distribution. For static risk measures, this leads to an algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient. For dynamic risk measures, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem and propose an actor-critic style algorithm. This work generalizes and unifies several previous results in the literature, which have focused on specific risk measures such as Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) or mean-variance objectives.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of the paper is its generality. By developing a policy gradient method applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a powerful and unified framework. This consolidates and extends a significant body of prior work that had previously addressed risk-sensitivity for individual risk measures in a piecemeal fashion. This is a solid and important contribution to the field.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The theoretical development is presented logically, starting from the static case and building up to the more complex dynamic case.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical results appear to be correct. The derivations, which rely on the envelope theorem and the likelihood-ratio trick, are elegant and provide a simpler way to re-derive some previously known results (e.g., for CVaR).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** While the generalization is valuable, the contribution could be viewed as an incremental, albeit non-trivial, extension of existing policy gradient theory. The core techniques—leveraging a dual representation of the objective and applying the likelihood ratio method—are well-established. The novelty lies in the specific application and derivation for this broad class of risk measures, rather than in the development of a fundamentally new methodology.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper provides a significant generalization of prior work, presenting a unified and elegant framework for policy gradients with coherent risk. It is well-written, technically sound, and the results are of clear importance to the risk-sensitive reinforcement learning community.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The methodological contribution could be considered an incremental extension of existing techniques.

Overall, this paper presents a solid, well-executed, and valuable contribution. The strengths, particularly the successful generalization to all coherent risk measures, outweigh the limited methodological novelty.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   In Theorem 2.1, the notation `ξPθ` may be confusing to readers. To improve clarity, I suggest explicitly defining this notation (e.g., as a re-weighting of the measure `Pθ`) or adopting the notation used in the cited source [26], specifically Theorem 6.6.
*   In Section 3 (Problem Formulation), it would be beneficial to clarify for a broader audience why the problem is considered intractable. You could state that MDPs with dynamic Markov coherent risk measures are often tractable to solve exactly via dynamic programming. The intractability in this paper's setting arises from the introduction of a parameterized policy and the use of function approximation, not from the risk measure itself. Making this distinction would strengthen the motivation for a policy gradient approach.
