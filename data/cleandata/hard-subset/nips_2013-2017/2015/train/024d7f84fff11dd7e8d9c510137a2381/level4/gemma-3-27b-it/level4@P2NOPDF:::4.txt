This paper addresses the problem of policy optimization under a general class of coherent risk measures. The authors leverage a dual representation of coherent risk measures – as the convex hull of probability distributions, where risk is defined as the maximum expected value within that hull – to develop an analytical solution for optimal policy parameters. A key contribution lies in imposing a smoothness constraint (specifically, M-Lipschitz continuity of the convex hull’s boundaries) on the family of considered risk measures, which enables the derivation of both optimal policies and a gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision problems.

The paper is clearly written and well-structured. Despite my limited prior knowledge of risk-sensitive optimization, I found the core ideas relatively accessible. The primary strength of this work is its unified treatment of a broad class of coherent risk measures encompassing many commonly used measures, representing a significant contribution to the field. While the theoretical results appear compelling, further empirical validation of the proposed gradient estimation algorithm would strengthen the paper. 

I have several comments and questions for the authors:

1. The preliminaries section requires clarification. The roles of $P_{\theta}$ and $\ksi$ were initially unclear and could benefit from more explicit explanation.

2. Equation 2 appears to simply state the definition of a convex envelope. The authors should clarify why this is presented as a more restrictive condition than Theorem 2.1. A more detailed explanation of the relationship between these two concepts would be helpful.

3. The analysis primarily focuses on discrete variable distributions. It would be valuable to include a discussion regarding the generalizability of the results to continuous distributions. 

4. Theorem 4.2’s proof appears to rely on the smoothness of the constraint functions. Mentioning this connection explicitly within the paper would improve understanding and provide further insight into the theorem’s conditions.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper presenting a general framework for risk-sensitive decision-making. The results are promising and have the potential to be highly impactful, but thorough verification of the proofs is recommended. A more extensive empirical evaluation, particularly of the gradient estimation algorithm, would significantly enhance the paper’s contribution.
