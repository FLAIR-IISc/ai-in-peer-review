The authors study the problem of selecting policy parameters that minimize a specified risk measure. They consider a broad class of risk measures defined by standard coherence properties, namely convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. Their approach builds on the dual representation of coherent risk as the convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) equals the maximum expected value over that hull. They assume the family of coherent risks satisfies a bound on the maximal derivatives of the hull’s boundary (i.e., the boundary is M-Lipschitz). Under this assumption, they derive analytical expressions for risk-optimal policy parameters and present a general gradient estimation procedure for both the static (single-step) and dynamic (sequential) settings.

The paper is clearly written and well organized. Despite not being an expert in risk-sensitive optimization, I could follow most of the exposition. The main contribution is a unified analysis covering a general class of risk measures that encompasses most commonly used ones, which I view as a significant contribution. However, a more thorough empirical study of the proposed gradient algorithm would be valuable.

Some comments/questions 1- The preliminaries are not sufficiently clear; the roles of P_{\theta} and \ksi were confusing. 2- Isn’t Equation 2 essentially the definition of a convex envelope? In what way is it more restrictive than Theorem 2.1? 3- Please add a comment on how the analysis extends to distributions over continuous variables. 4- It would be helpful, even in the short version, to note the role of constraint-function smoothness in proving Theorem 4.2.

A strong theoretical paper offering a general framework for risk-sensitive decision making. The results appear compelling, but the proofs should be carefully verified.
