The authors address the problem of selecting policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. The paper considers a broad class of risk measures characterized by coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The proposed approach relies on the dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) is defined as the maximum expected value over that hull. The paper assumes the family of coherent risk measures satisfies a bound on the maximal derivatives of the hull’s boundary (i.e., the boundary is M-Lipschitz continuous). Under this assumption, the authors analytically derive the optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure and present a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static (one-step) and dynamic (sequential) settings.

The paper is well written and well organized. Although I am not deeply familiar with risk-sensitive optimization, I was able to follow most of the exposition. The main contribution is a unified analysis of a general class of risk measures that encompasses most well-known measures, which I consider a significant contribution. That said, a more extensive empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would be valuable.

Comments/questions:
1- The preliminaries section is not sufficiently clear. I was confused about the respective roles of P_{θ} and ξ.
2- Isn’t Equation 2 simply the definition of the convex envelope? In what sense is it more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?
3- Please add a comment on how the analysis extends to distributions over continuous variables.
4- In the short paper, it would be helpful to mention the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in the proof of Theorem 4.2.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that offers a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear compelling, though the proofs should be carefully verified.
