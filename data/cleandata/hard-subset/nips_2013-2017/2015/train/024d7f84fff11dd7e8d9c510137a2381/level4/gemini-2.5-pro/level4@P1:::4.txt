This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient optimization in reinforcement learning under the objective of a coherent risk measure. Coherent risk measures are a general class of functions satisfying a set of desirable axioms (e.g., convexity, monotonicity), and this work extends previous risk-sensitive RL methods that focused on specific measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The key technical insight is to leverage the dual representation of any coherent risk measure, which expresses it as the maximum expected cost over a convex set of probability densities, known as the risk envelope. The authors derive novel policy gradient formulas for both static risk measures (applied to the total cost of a trajectory) and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. For the static case, they propose an algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. For the dynamic case, they develop an actor-critic style algorithm.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its generality and theoretical contribution. It provides a principled and unified approach to risk-sensitive policy gradient methods that is applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This significantly generalizes and subsumes several prior works that were tailored to specific risk objectives. The theoretical development, which connects the dual representation of coherent risk to the Envelope Theorem for gradient derivation, is elegant and powerful. The paper is well-written, organized, and provides a compelling theoretical foundation for a broad range of risk-sensitive problems.

**Weaknesses:**
A minor weakness is the limited empirical evaluation. While the numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of flexibility in choosing an appropriate risk measure, it does not provide a deeper analysis of the proposed gradient estimation algorithms themselves. An evaluation of their convergence properties, sample efficiency, or variance, perhaps in comparison to specialized algorithms for measures like CVaR, would strengthen the paper's practical claims.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
1.  **Clarity in Preliminaries:** In Section 2.1, the relationship between the original probability measure P_θ and the test density function ξ could be made more explicit. While it is stated later, clarifying early on that ξ represents a re-weighting of P_θ chosen adversarially from the risk envelope might help readers less familiar with this area.

2.  **Assumption 2.2:** This assumption specifies the risk envelope U(P_θ) using a particular convex programming formulation. It would be helpful if the authors could briefly elaborate on how this formulation relates to the general representation in Theorem 2.1. Specifically, does assuming this structure restrict the class of coherent risk measures that the framework can handle, or is it sufficiently general for all such measures on finite spaces?

3.  **Extension to Continuous Spaces:** The paper restricts its analysis to finite probability spaces for clarity. While this is a reasonable simplification, a brief discussion on the potential challenges or necessary modifications for extending the results to continuous outcome spaces would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Role of Smoothness Assumptions:** The proof of the main gradient formula in Theorem 4.2 is deferred to the supplementary material. It would be beneficial for the reader if the main text briefly mentioned how the smoothness conditions in Assumption 2.2 are leveraged in the proof (e.g., by enabling a clean application of the Envelope Theorem).

**Summary:**
This is a strong theory paper that provides a significant and elegant generalization of policy gradient methods to a broad class of risk-sensitive objectives. The results appear sound and provide a solid foundation for future work in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. Despite the limited empirical results, the theoretical contribution is substantial and makes the paper a clear candidate for acceptance.
