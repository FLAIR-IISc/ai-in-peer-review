

This paper presents a unified approach to policy gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, extending previous work on specific risk-sensitive objectives such as variance, CVaR, and mean-variance. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to develop a general framework that encompasses both static and dynamic risk measures. For static coherent risks, they derive a gradient formula using convex programming and sampling, while for dynamic Markov coherent risks, they establish a policy gradient theorem that extends the standard policy gradient framework to risk-sensitive settings. The approach generalizes and unifies previous results that focused on specific risk measures, providing a more comprehensive theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a unified theoretical framework that encompasses most well-known risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation, spectral risk measures) under a single coherent risk framework. Second, it develops a practical gradient estimation algorithm for both static and dynamic risk settings that combines sampling with convex programming. Third, it establishes theoretical consistency results for the proposed gradient estimators under mild assumptions. The authors demonstrate the practical utility of their framework through numerical experiments showing how different risk preferences lead to different policy choices in a financial trading scenario.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear exposition of technical concepts. The theoretical development is rigorous, and the connection between coherent risk measures and their dual representation is well-motivated. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the practical significance of the framework, showing how different risk preferences lead to different investment strategies.

Regarding the evaluation criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors provide complete theoretical derivations and consistency proofs for their gradient estimators. The assumptions (particularly Assumption 2.2) are reasonable and well-justified. The experimental results provide empirical validation of the theoretical claims. The paper carefully acknowledges limitations, particularly regarding the computational complexity of the convex programming steps.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with good organization. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The connection between the dual representation of coherent risk measures and the gradient estimation approach is particularly well-articulated. The supplementary material provides additional detail for interested readers.

Originality: This work significantly advances the field by presenting the first unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures. While previous work has studied specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance), the current paper demonstrates how these can be seen as special cases of a more general framework. The authors cite relevant prior work (including recent NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL) and clearly articulate how their approach differs from and generalizes previous contributions.

Significance: The paper addresses an important problem in reinforcement learning where risk management is crucial (e.g., finance, robotics). By providing a unified framework that encompasses various risk measures, the authors enable practitioners to select risk criteria that best match their specific application requirements. The framework is particularly valuable for applications requiring time-consistent risk management. The approach could potentially influence future work on risk-sensitive decision making in sequential settings.

Strengths:
- Provides a unified theoretical framework for coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning
- Develops practical gradient estimation algorithms combining sampling with convex programming
- Establishes theoretical consistency results for the proposed estimators
- Demonstrates the practical utility of the framework through a compelling numerical example
- Clearly articulates connections to previous work and shows how their approach generalizes it

Weaknesses:
- The assumption of a finite probability space (Section 2) may limit applicability to continuous domains, though the authors acknowledge this limitation
- The computational complexity of the convex programming steps in the gradient estimation algorithm is not thoroughly analyzed
- The experimental section, while illustrative, could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods
- The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) is promising but requires more concrete development

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a significant problem in reinforcement learning with a theoretically sound and practically relevant contribution. The unified framework represents an important step toward more flexible and principled risk management in sequential decision-making.

The draft review's comments about the preliminaries being unclear could be more constructive. The roles of P_θ and ξ are clarified in the paper: P_θ represents the probability distribution of outcomes parameterized by the policy θ, while ξ represents the re-weighting of this distribution in the dual representation (risk envelope). Equation 2 is not simply the definition of a convex envelope but specifies the general form of the risk envelope under Assumption 2.2, which is necessary for the gradient derivation. The paper correctly acknowledges the finite probability space assumption but notes that extensions to continuous domains are possible with additional technical machinery.

The suggestion to add a comment on generalizing to continuous variables is valid, though the paper appropriately notes this limitation in the text. The smoothness of constraint functions is indeed crucial for Theorem 4.2, and the paper carefully establishes this in Assumption 2.2 and the subsequent discussion.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions that advance the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The proposed framework is both theoretically elegant and practically useful, providing a unified approach that generalizes previous work. The authors have made an important contribution to the field that should be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in reinforcement learning.
