- Presents a novel, theoretically grounded extension of policy gradient methods to the broad class of coherent risk measures.  
- Uses the dual representation of coherent risk (maximization over a convex hull of distributions) to derive analytical gradient formulas for static and dynamic settings.  
- Unifies analysis of various risk measures under a single framework, significantly generalizing prior work.  
- Paper is well-structured and generally clearly written, accessible to readers unfamiliar with risk-sensitive optimization.  
- Core technical contribution: policy gradient theorems and algorithms applicable to many coherent risk measures.  
- Relies on Assumption 2.2 (smoothness of the risk envelope) to enable sampling-based gradient estimation techniques.  
- Proposed algorithms combine sampling with convex programming, offering a practical optimization pathway.  
- Strength: high generality—framework covers a wide range of coherent risk measures.  
- Strength: strong theoretical rigor, including Theorems 4.2 and 5.2.  
- Strength: practical sampling-based gradient estimation algorithm.  
- Weakness: preliminaries notation unclear—roles of Pθ and ξ need clearer explanation.  
- Weakness: authors should clarify why Assumption 2.2 is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1.  
- Weakness: paper lacks discussion on extending analysis to continuous variable distributions.  
- Weakness: proofs need more rigorous verification to increase confidence.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited; Section 6 example is insightful but not comprehensive or benchmarked.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a novel, sound, broadly applicable approach and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation and minor clarity issues around preliminaries and assumptions.  
- Overall: valuable contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions and suggested expansion of empirical analysis.
