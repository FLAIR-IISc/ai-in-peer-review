The authors address the problem of determining policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure, considering a broad class of risk measures defined by coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. Their approach leverages a dual representation of any coherent risk measure as a convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable is given by the maximum expected value within the hull. The paper introduces an assumption on the family of coherent risk measures by bounding the maximum derivatives of the convex hull's borders, specifically that the borders are M-Lipschitz continuous. Under this assumption, the authors analytically derive the optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure and propose a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step decision) and dynamic (sequential) cases.

The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to readers unfamiliar with risk-sensitive optimization. The main contribution of this work lies in its unified analysis of a general class of risk measures, encompassing most well-known measures, which is a significant contribution. However, a more in-depth empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm would be beneficial.

Some comments and questions are as follows:

1. The preliminaries section requires clarification, particularly regarding the roles of P_{\theta} and \ksi.
2. It appears that Equation 2 is essentially the definition of a convex envelope. Could the authors explain why this formulation is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?
3. A comment on how the analysis extends to distributions on continuous variables would be a valuable addition.
4. It would be helpful to mention in the paper the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in proving Theorem 4.2.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that provides a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. While the results are compelling, it is essential to verify the proofs.
