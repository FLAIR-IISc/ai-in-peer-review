The paper proposes a general formula for policy gradients for the class of static coherent risks, from which gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. The authors also present a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where an analytical solution is unavailable, providing consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper extends this framework by providing expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

### Significance and Quality
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is an intriguing, valuable, and novel contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive decision-making. A key limitation, however, is that analytical solutions for the required Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for a subset of risk measures.

The proposed sample-based approximation scheme is promising, as it could enable the application of these results to general coherent risk measures without requiring the derivation of analytical saddle points. However, the paper provides no empirical evidence to demonstrate that this estimation scheme is viable in practice. An experimental comparison between using the sample-based policy gradients and the analytical ones (e.g., for CVaR) across different problems would be extremely helpful for validating the approach.

### Originality
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in this general form, are novel.

### Clarity
The paper is generally well written. However, its clarity is considerably hindered by the number of different topics it attempts to address within the eight-page limit. The treatment of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5, in particular, is presented at a very high level. The lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluation for this section makes it impossible for the reader to judge whether the derived gradients yield practical approaches. This content seems better suited for a longer journal publication.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by splitting the content. Focusing solely on static risk measures would allow for a more self-contained contribution, with space for greater detail and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the basic toy example currently provided.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with potentially valuable results, but the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation makes its practical impact difficult to assess. A narrower focus would also help address the clarity issues that arise from the current space constraints.
