- Proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for coherent risk measures, deriving a general static gradient formula via the coherent-risk representation and envelope theorem.  
- Provides closed-form gradients for several popular measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) when analytic saddle points exist.  
- Introduces a sampling-based gradient estimator that combines likelihood ratios with a convex program over the risk envelope and proves consistency.  
- Extends the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk using a risk-sensitive Bellman equation and outlines an actor-critic style approach.  
- Unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL work (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR) under a coherent-risk framework from finance/OR.  
- Strength: novel, unified derivation that subsumes known special cases and clarifies common structure.  
- Strength: broadly applicable sampling estimator with theoretical consistency guarantees.  
- Strength: notable theoretical contribution extending policy gradients to dynamic, time-consistent risk measures.  
- Weakness: analytic Lagrangian saddle points exist only for some risk measures, limiting closed-form applicability.  
- Weakness: sampling-based approximation lacks empirical validation and comparisons to analytic gradients (e.g., for CVaR).  
- Weakness: dynamic-risk policy-gradient section is high-level and missing algorithmic details and experimental evaluation.  
- Weakness: attempting broad scope within page limits reduces clarity and depth.  
- Weakness: empirical illustration is a very basic toy example that does not substantiate general claims.  
- Quality: theoretical development appears solid, but practical effectiveness is uncertain due to limited experiments.  
- Clarity: generally well written but overloaded; dynamic section is too high-level for reproduction.  
- Originality: policy-gradient results for the general coherent-risk framework appear novel in RL literature.  
- Significance: potentially high impact, but practical significance is unproven without stronger empirical evidence.  
- Arguments for acceptance: unification of risk-sensitive gradients, sampling estimator with guarantees, and dynamic extension.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation, missing algorithmic details, and lack of benchmarking comparisons.  
- Suggestions: add empirical comparisons between sampling and analytic gradients, expand dynamic algorithmic details and experiments, or narrow focus to static coherent risk with deeper evaluation.  
- Overall: interesting and potentially impactful work that needs stronger empirical support and/or a narrower scope to improve clarity and practical assessment.
