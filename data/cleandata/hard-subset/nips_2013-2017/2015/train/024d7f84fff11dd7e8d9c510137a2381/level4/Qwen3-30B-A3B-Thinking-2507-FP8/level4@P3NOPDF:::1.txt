

The paper introduces a unified framework for deriving policy gradients across static coherent risk measures, enabling analytical solutions for common risk metrics and proposing a general sampling-based algorithm for cases lacking closed-form solutions. It further extends this to Markov coherent dynamic risk measures. The theoretical contribution is significant, offering a coherent derivation of policy gradients that generalizes prior work in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, the paper's impact is limited by critical omissions in empirical validation and scope management.

**Significance and Quality**  
The unified derivation of policy gradients for static coherent risks represents a valuable and novel contribution to the literature. The analytical approach for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR) is well-motivated, and the sampling-based method for general cases addresses a key practical gap. Nevertheless, the absence of empirical evidence for the sampling scheme’s viability severely undermines the practical relevance of this contribution. The authors should provide a comparative evaluation—such as a controlled experiment demonstrating the convergence and performance of the sampling-based gradients versus analytical solutions for CVaR or other risks—across diverse environments. Without this, the utility of the proposed algorithm remains speculative. The extension to dynamic risk measures (Section 5) is conceptually interesting but lacks sufficient detail to assess its practical applicability. The paper claims "consistency guarantees" for the sampling method, yet offers no empirical support for these claims, leaving readers unable to judge whether the approach scales to real-world problems.

**Originality**  
The derivation of policy gradients in this general form for static coherent risks appears novel to the best of my knowledge. The paper successfully synthesizes existing risk-sensitive RL approaches under a unified theoretical lens, which is a meaningful advance. However, the novelty hinges on the completeness of the framework; the inability to derive analytical solutions for most risk measures (beyond a few special cases) slightly diminishes the perceived novelty of the core contribution.

**Clarity and Scope**  
The paper is well-written but suffers from overambition within its 8-page limit. The scope—spanning static analytical solutions, general sampling methods, and dynamic risk measures—creates a disjointed narrative. Section 5 (dynamic risk measures) is particularly underdeveloped: it presents the theoretical extension at a high level but omits critical algorithmic details, implementation considerations, and empirical validation. This makes it difficult to evaluate whether the dynamic risk formulation leads to tractable algorithms. The current submission’s attempt to cover all three aspects in a single paper results in superficial treatment of each, especially the dynamic case. For instance, the "consistency guarantees" for the sampling method are stated without proof sketches or experimental backing, and the dynamic risk section lacks concrete examples or pseudocode.  

**Recommendation**  
I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the lack of empirical validation for the core algorithmic proposal (the sampling-based method) and the underdeveloped dynamic risk extension prevent a clear assessment of the paper’s practical impact. To salvage the work, the authors should:  
1. **Focus exclusively on static coherent risk measures**, removing the dynamic risk section entirely. This would allow a more in-depth treatment of the analytical and sampling-based methods, including comprehensive empirical evaluations (e.g., comparisons on standard RL benchmarks with risk-sensitive objectives like CVaR).  
2. **Provide a thorough experimental analysis** demonstrating the sampling method’s convergence, computational efficiency, and performance relative to analytical approaches for at least two risk measures (e.g., CVaR and entropic risk).  
3. **Expand the theoretical treatment** of the sampling scheme, including a clear algorithm description and a proof sketch for the consistency guarantees.  

The current manuscript reads like a preliminary version of a broader contribution that would better suit a journal submission. The dynamic risk extension, in particular, requires substantial elaboration—algorithmic details, experimental validation, and a clearer justification for its necessity—to be included in a conference paper. As it stands, the paper’s ambition exceeds its execution, leaving key questions unanswered. A narrower focus on static risks would yield a more cohesive, rigorous, and impactful contribution that aligns with NeurIPS’ emphasis on both theoretical and empirical rigor. The current version, while containing promising ideas, fails to convincingly demonstrate their practical utility or completeness.
