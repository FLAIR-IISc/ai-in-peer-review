This paper presents a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for deriving policy gradients for the broad class of coherent risk measures. The authors develop novel formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risks, offering a generalization of existing methods focused on specific risk measures like CVaR. A key strength lies in the development of a sampling-based algorithm for cases where analytical solutions are intractable, accompanied by consistency guarantees for the gradient estimates.

**Summary of Contributions & Relation to Prior Work:** The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature on risk-sensitive RL, referencing key works on mean-variance optimization, VaR, CVaR, and dynamic coherent risk. It correctly identifies the limitations of prior approaches, which often focus on individual risk measures, and proposes a more general, unifying framework grounded in coherent risk theory from finance and operations research. The connection to robust MDPs [19] is also appropriately noted. The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2, build upon existing policy gradient theorems [28, 14] and envelope theorems [17], extending them to the context of coherent risk.

**Strengths:** The most compelling strength of this work is its generality. By working with the broader class of coherent risk measures, the authors offer a more flexible and potentially powerful approach to risk management in RL. The derivation of the gradient formulas is mathematically sound and the proposed sampling-based algorithm offers a practical route to implementation when analytical solutions are unavailable. The theoretical consistency guarantees are also valuable, providing a foundation for reliable gradient estimation. The discussion of dynamic risk measures and the extension to time-consistent policies address an important aspect of sequential decision-making under risk.

**Weaknesses:** While theoretically strong, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation. The numerical illustration in Section 6, though insightful in highlighting the impact of different risk criteria, is quite limited in scope. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing the performance of the proposed algorithms with existing methods (e.g., those based on CVaR) on a range of benchmark RL environments, would significantly strengthen the paper. Specifically, demonstrating the effectiveness of the sampling-based approach (Section 4.3) is crucial. The presentation of the dynamic risk portion (Section 5) feels particularly high-level. The lack of algorithmic details and experimental results for the dynamic case makes it difficult to assess the practical viability of the proposed approach. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but a more focused presentation—perhaps concentrating solely on the static risk formulation with thorough empirical results—could improve clarity and impact.

**Originality:** The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures appears to be novel, representing a significant contribution to the field. While individual components may build upon existing work, the combination and generalization offered here are genuinely original.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but, as the authors themselves acknowledge, attempts to cover a considerable amount of material within the page limit. The mathematical notation is generally clear, but the rapid introduction of several concepts and assumptions can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with coherent risk theory. Section 5, in particular, could benefit from more detailed explanations and a more structured presentation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a unifying theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL.
*   Derives novel and general policy gradient formulas.
*   Proposes a practical sampling-based algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
*   Addresses the important problem of dynamic risk and time consistency.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks a comprehensive experimental evaluation, particularly for the sampling-based algorithm.
*   The presentation of the dynamic risk portion is too high-level and lacks concrete results.
*   The breadth of the paper potentially compromises clarity and depth.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, the lack of extensive empirical validation raises concerns about its practical impact. While the ideas are promising, the absence of strong experimental results makes it difficult to fully assess the benefits of the proposed approach.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should prioritize conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation, particularly focusing on the sampling-based algorithm. Consideration should be given to either significantly expanding the empirical section or narrowing the scope of the paper to focus on the static risk formulation with a comprehensive evaluation. Greater detail in the explanation of the dynamic risk approach would also be beneficial. A clearer presentation, potentially achieved by streamlining the content, would further enhance the paper’s impact.
