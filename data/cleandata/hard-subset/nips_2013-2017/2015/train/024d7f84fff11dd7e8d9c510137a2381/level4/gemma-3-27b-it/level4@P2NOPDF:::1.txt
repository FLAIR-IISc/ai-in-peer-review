This paper introduces a novel framework for computing policy gradients for a broad class of static coherent risks, offering analytical derivations for many common risk measures. It further proposes a general sampling-based algorithm to estimate policy gradients when analytical solutions are unavailable, accompanied by consistency guarantees. Finally, the authors present policy gradient expressions for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality:**

The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures represents a valuable and original contribution to the field of risk-sensitive decision making. While the analytical solutions are currently limited to specific risk measures due to the inherent complexity of solving the Lagrangian saddle-points, the proposed sampling-based approximation offers a promising avenue for applying the theoretical results to a wider range of coherent risk measures. However, a significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical validation demonstrating the practical viability of this sampling scheme. An experimental comparison between the sample-based policy gradients and existing analytical methods, particularly for well-studied risk measures like CVaR, would substantially strengthen the paper. 

**Originality:**

To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, particularly in their general form, constitute a novel contribution to the literature. 

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is hampered by the ambitious scope of topics covered within the page limit. The discussion of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5, in particular, feels underdeveloped. The high-level presentation, coupled with the lack of algorithmic details and experimental results, makes it difficult to assess the practicality and potential benefits of the derived gradients. This aspect of the work feels more appropriate for a longer, more detailed journal publication. 

I suggest the authors consider narrowing the focus of this paper. Concentrating on the static risk measures would allow for a more self-contained presentation and facilitate a more thorough empirical evaluation, extending beyond the current limited toy example. Alternatively, splitting the work into two publications – one focusing on static risks with comprehensive experiments, and another on dynamic risks – could improve clarity and allow each topic to receive the attention it deserves. 

Overall, this is an interesting paper with potentially broadly applicable results. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and the breadth of the covered material make it difficult to fully assess its impact. A more focused approach and robust empirical support are needed to elevate this work further.
