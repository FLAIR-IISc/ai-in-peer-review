

The paper introduces a novel unified framework for deriving policy gradients for static coherent risk measures, enabling analytical solutions for many common risk metrics. It further proposes a general sampling-based algorithm for cases lacking closed-form solutions, accompanied by consistency guarantees for the gradient estimates, and extends the approach to Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality:**  
The unified derivation of policy gradients represents a significant and novel contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, the analytical approach is limited to specific risk measures where Lagrangian saddle-points can be solved explicitly. While the sampling-based scheme offers a promising path to apply the framework broadly, the paper lacks empirical validation of its practical viability. Crucially, no experiments compare the sample-based gradients against analytical solutions (e.g., for CVaR) across diverse problems. Such comparisons—particularly for well-studied risk measures like CVaR—would be essential to demonstrate the method's effectiveness and computational efficiency, making this omission a major weakness.

**Originality:**  
To the best of my knowledge, the general form of the derived policy gradients for coherent risk measures is novel. This theoretical advancement provides a cohesive foundation previously absent in the literature.

**Clarity and Presentation:**  
The paper is generally well-written but suffers from severe scope inflation within the tight eight-page limit. The inclusion of static risk analysis (Sections 3–4), a sampling algorithm (Section 4), and dynamic risk extensions (Section 5) creates a disjointed narrative. Section 5, covering dynamic risk policy gradients, is presented at an excessively high level, omitting critical algorithmic details and experimental validation. Without these, readers cannot assess whether the derived gradients translate into practical, implementable methods. The current submission’s scope—especially the dynamic risk component—appears better suited for a journal-length paper.  

The sole empirical evaluation (a "toy example" in Section 6) is insufficient to support the paper’s claims. A focused, standalone paper centered *only* on static risk measures would allow for deeper theoretical exposition, comprehensive empirical validation (e.g., multiple risk measures, problem domains, and ablation studies comparing analytical vs. sampling-based gradients), and clearer algorithmic specifications. This narrower focus would directly address the clarity deficits caused by attempting to cover too much in a short format. The current structure forces trade-offs: either sacrificing empirical rigor to include dynamic risks or omitting critical validation for static risks. The latter significantly undermines the paper’s credibility, as the core contribution (static risk gradients) lacks concrete evidence of utility.

**Recommendation:**  
The work has strong theoretical potential but requires substantial revision to be suitable for publication. The most impactful path forward is to **focus exclusively on static coherent risk measures**, removing all dynamic risk content. This would enable:  
1. A thorough empirical evaluation (beyond a toy example), including comparisons of analytical vs. sampling-based gradients for key risks (e.g., CVaR, entropic risk).  
2. Detailed algorithmic descriptions and implementation insights for the sampling scheme.  
3. A clearer, more cohesive narrative that allows readers to fully evaluate the method’s practical value.  

Without this focus, the paper’s theoretical contribution remains unverified in practice, and the dynamic risk extension lacks sufficient depth for meaningful assessment. The current version, while conceptually interesting, fails to demonstrate that the proposed framework delivers actionable improvements over existing risk-sensitive RL methods. A revised, focused submission would substantially strengthen the work’s contribution to the field.
