Summary
This paper develops policy gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning across the full class of coherent risk measures. The authors address both static risk (risk of the total discounted return under a fixed policy) and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key technical idea is to exploit the dual (risk envelope) representation of coherent risk measures and apply the envelope theorem to derive convenient gradient formulas amenable to sampling.

For static coherent risk, the paper provides:
- A gradient formula that reduces to an expectation under a worst-case reweighting of the sampling distribution, involving Lagrange multipliers from the dual problem.
- A sample-average approximation (SAA) procedure that combines Monte Carlo sampling with a convex program to estimate the risk envelope and corresponding gradient consistently.
- Recoveries of known special cases (e.g., CVaR gradient, mean-semideviation) and a unifying view that covers many popular risk criteria.

For dynamic risk, the paper derives a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures by relating the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function that solves a robust Bellman equation. This leads to an actor-critic algorithm: the critic approximates the risk-sensitive value function (leveraging robust MDP techniques), and the actor estimates the gradient using trajectories generated under ξ-weighted transitions induced by the risk envelope. A small portfolio selection experiment illustrates how different risk criteria (e.g., mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard deviation) lead to distinct, interpretable policies, especially in heavy-tailed settings.

Relation to prior work
The paper generalizes prior RL work that focused on specific risk measures—mean-variance, CVaR, and exponential utilities—to the entire coherent class. It connects to robust MDPs (dynamic coherent risk as robustness), and builds on recent function-approximation methods for robust planning. The CVaR gradient formula is recovered in a simpler and more general way (including discrete distributions), and the dynamic result extends policy gradient theorems to time-consistent coherent risks. The approach complements prior risk-sensitive actor-critic methods and robust value function approximation work.

Strengths
- A unified, principled framework: The dual representation of coherent risk measures is used to derive gradient estimators that cleanly generalize several known results.
- Technical soundness: The gradient formulas for static and dynamic cases are well presented, with clear conditions (e.g., likelihood-ratio assumptions, Slater-type feasibility), and consistency for the SAA estimator.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is clearly written and well structured; the derivations and examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) are helpful.
- Practical algorithms: The combination of sampling with convex programming for static risk is concrete, and the actor-critic design for dynamic risk is well motivated by the new policy gradient theorem.
- Insightful demonstration: The toy financial example highlights how different coherent risk choices can drive qualitatively different (and more reasonable) decisions in heavy-tailed environments.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical scope: The experiment is limited to a static risk setting and a simple portfolio example. Including dynamic MDP experiments—especially demonstrating the actor-critic algorithm with function approximation—would better validate the dynamic theory.
- Computational considerations: The static estimator requires solving a convex program per batch. A discussion of runtime, scaling (number of constraints vs. sample size), and practical solver choices would help assess feasibility on larger problems.
- Sampling requirements: The dynamic algorithm assumes access to a simulator enabling multiple next-state samples per state and sampling under ξ-weighted dynamics. Clarify how to proceed when only a single trajectory is available (e.g., importance sampling/off-policy corrections) and discuss conditions ensuring ergodicity and absolute continuity of ξ-weighted transitions.
- Assumptions and regularity: The main text defers several assumptions (finite spaces, smoothness of constraints, existence/uniqueness of saddle points) and convergence analyses to the supplement. Briefly restating key conditions (e.g., Slater condition, boundedness of multipliers, stability of the ξ-reweighted chain) would improve readability and reproducibility.
- Variance and rare events: While the paper mentions importance sampling as future work, some empirical or theoretical discussion of estimator variance under tail-sensitive risks (e.g., CVaR at small α) would be valuable. A comparison of SAA vs. closed-form estimators (when available) is briefly referenced; more details in the main text would help practitioners.
- Comparisons and baselines: For the static case, a more systematic comparison against existing CVaR/mean-variance algorithms on benchmark problems (beyond the illustrative finance example) would strengthen the empirical case.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with appropriate references and clear derivations. The static and dynamic gradient results are well grounded. Some practical details (computational and statistical properties) could be expanded.
- Clarity: Above average. The exposition is careful and the examples are instructive. Minor suggestions include making key assumptions more prominent in the main text and clarifying sampling requirements for the dynamic algorithm.
- Originality: The extension from specific risks to the full coherent class, the SAA-based gradient estimator tied to the risk envelope, and the dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk are substantial and nontrivial contributions.
- Significance: High. Coherent risk measures are widely used, and unifying risk-sensitive RL under this umbrella is important. The methods should be useful to researchers and practitioners interested in robust and risk-averse RL.

Pros for acceptance
- Unified gradient framework covering the broad class of coherent risks (static and dynamic).
- New policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and an associated actor-critic algorithm.
- Clear derivations that subsume prior results (e.g., CVaR) and provide practical estimators.
- Strong conceptual link to robust MDPs, supporting scalable approximation techniques.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evaluation, with no dynamic MDP experiments in the main text.
- Practical computational and sampling requirements (convex programs, ξ-weighted sampling) need further discussion and demonstration at scale.
- Variance control and sample complexity are not empirically characterized for tail-heavy risks.

Overall recommendation
This is a well-motivated, technically solid paper that offers a unified and general approach to risk-sensitive RL with coherent risk measures, together with practical gradient estimators and an actor-critic scheme for dynamic risk. While the experiments are modest and practical considerations deserve more attention, the contribution is significant and merits acceptance. I encourage the authors to include at least one dynamic MDP experiment, clarify computational and sampling requirements, and discuss variance reduction in the main text.
