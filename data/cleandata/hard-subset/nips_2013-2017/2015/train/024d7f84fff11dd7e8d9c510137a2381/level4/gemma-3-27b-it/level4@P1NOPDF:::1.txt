The paper proposes a new formulation for the class of static coherent risks, from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where an analytical solution is unavailable and provides consistency guarantees for the resulting policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper derives expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and quality:**

The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is an intriguing and valuable, novel contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, analytical solutions to the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for a limited number of risk measures. 

The proposed sample-based approximation scheme could enable the application of these results to a broader range of coherent risk measures by avoiding the need to derive analytical forms of the saddle points. Nevertheless, the paper does not provide any empirical evidence demonstrating the practical viability of this estimation scheme. An experimental comparison between the sample-based policy gradients and analytical solutions, applied to various problems (e.g., CVaR), would be extremely helpful.

**Originality:** To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients – in this general form – are novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is somewhat hindered by the number of different topics addressed within the eight-page limit. In particular, the dynamic risk policy gradients presented in Section 5 are described at a very high level. The lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluation prevents a proper assessment of whether the derived policy gradients lead to practical and viable approaches. This content feels more suited for a longer journal publication. 

Alternatively, focusing the paper on static risk measures would be beneficial. This narrower scope would allow for a more self-contained presentation, as well as more detailed analysis and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the current, very basic toy example. This is an interesting paper with potentially broadly applicable results, but the limited experimental evaluation makes it difficult to fully assess. A tighter focus may also help to overcome the current clarity issues imposed by space constraints.
