

This paper addresses the important problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors present a unified framework that generalizes and extends previous approaches focusing on specific risk measures like variance, CVaR, and mean-variance. They leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk measures, enabling policy optimization across the entire class of coherent risks.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field of risk-sensitive RL. The authors establish a theoretically sound approach by connecting coherent risk measures to convex optimization and policy gradient methods. For static risk, they derive a general gradient formula that can be implemented using sampling combined with convex programming. For dynamic risk, they extend the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures, developing an actor-critic algorithm for sequential decision-making. The paper demonstrates how several previously studied risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation) emerge as special cases of their framework, providing a more general and unified perspective.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear technical exposition that effectively conveys the mathematical contributions. The authors carefully establish the necessary theoretical foundations, including the dual representation of coherent risk measures and their extension to dynamic settings. The connection to previous work is well-motivated, and the paper appropriately cites relevant literature on risk-sensitive optimization and reinforcement learning.

In terms of quality, the paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide detailed proofs for their main results (presented in supplementary material) and establish consistency properties for their gradient estimators. The theoretical framework is comprehensive and addresses the important properties of coherence and time consistency for risk measures in sequential decision problems.

The originality of this work is substantial. While risk-sensitive RL has been studied for specific risk measures, this paper provides a general framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work that focused on isolated risk measures. The authors effectively demonstrate how their framework unifies and generalizes existing approaches.

The significance of this work is evident in its potential to influence future research in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. By providing a general framework for coherent risk measures, the paper opens the door to more flexible risk management in sequential decision-making under uncertainty. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the practical importance of choosing appropriate risk measures for specific applications, showing how different risk criteria lead to different policy choices even when optimizing for similar objectives.

The main weakness of the paper is the lack of extensive empirical validation. While the numerical example in Section 6 is useful for demonstrating conceptual differences between risk measures, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive experiments comparing the performance of the proposed gradient methods against existing approaches across multiple benchmark problems. The authors should also provide more details about the computational complexity of their algorithms and their scalability to larger problems.

Some specific technical points that could be clarified:

1. The assumption of a finite probability space (Section 2) is somewhat restrictive for practical RL applications. The authors should address how their approach generalizes to continuous spaces, which would strengthen the paper's applicability.

2. The connection between Equation 2 and Theorem 2.1 needs clearer exposition. The authors should explicitly explain how the convex envelope representation in Theorem 2.1 relates to the specific form of the risk envelope in Equation 2.

3. The smoothness assumptions in Assumption 2.2 are critical for the theoretical results but not sufficiently motivated. The authors should provide more intuition about why these assumptions are reasonable for practical risk measures.

4. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation challenges of the proposed algorithms, particularly regarding the convex programming steps required for gradient estimation.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for coherent risk measures represents a significant step forward in the field. While the empirical validation could be more extensive, the theoretical foundations are sound and the paper provides a valuable generalization of previous work.

The authors have made an important contribution by developing a unified approach to risk-sensitive RL that encompasses the entire class of coherent risk measures. This work has the potential to influence future research in risk-sensitive decision-making and provides a more principled approach to incorporating risk considerations in reinforcement learning algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance: This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The theoretical development is sound, well-motivated, and generalizes previous approaches. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance: The empirical validation is somewhat limited, and the paper would benefit from more comprehensive experiments demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed methods across multiple domains. The finite probability space assumption also limits the immediate applicability to many real-world RL problems.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While additional empirical validation would strengthen the paper, the theoretical framework is compelling and represents a significant step forward in the area. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors could further strengthen the paper with more extensive empirical results in future work.
