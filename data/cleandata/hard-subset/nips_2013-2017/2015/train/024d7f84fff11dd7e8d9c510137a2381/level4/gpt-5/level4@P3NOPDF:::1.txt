Summary
The paper introduces a unified formulation for static coherent risk measures that enables analytic derivations of policy gradients for many widely used risks. For cases where the associated Lagrangian saddle points are not analytically tractable, the authors propose a sampling-based estimator and provide consistency guarantees. The paper also extends the analysis to Markov coherent dynamic risk measures, deriving corresponding policy gradient expressions.

Strengths
- The unified treatment of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is novel and valuable. It offers a common lens through which multiple risk-sensitive objectives can be handled within the same framework.
- The sampling-based approach, together with consistency guarantees, is theoretically appealing and, in principle, broadens applicability beyond cases with closed-form saddle points.
- The extension to dynamic, Markov coherent risk measures is ambitious and potentially impactful, suggesting a pathway to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning in more realistic sequential settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Analytical saddle-point solutions appear to be available only for a subset of coherent risk measures. While the sampling-based alternative is promising, the paper provides no empirical validation that it works well in practice.
- There is no experimental comparison between gradients obtained analytically and those estimated via sampling, even for canonical risks such as CVaR. This makes it difficult to assess estimator variance, bias, convergence behavior, and practical sample complexity.
- The dynamic risk section is presented at a very high level. The lack of algorithmic details (e.g., pseudo-code, computational complexity, variance-reduction strategies) and empirical evaluation prevents the reader from judging whether the dynamic risk gradients lead to viable methods.
- The current submission attempts to cover many topics within eight pages, which compromises clarity and depth. Important components—particularly the dynamic part—feel underdeveloped relative to their potential importance.

Originality
To the best of my knowledge, the policy gradient derivations for coherent risk measures in this general form are novel. The unification across multiple risk measures and the extension to dynamic Markov coherent risks appear original and timely.

Quality and significance
The theoretical contributions are sound and, if supported by convincing experiments, could significantly influence risk-sensitive decision making. The consistency guarantees for the sampling-based estimator are an important step toward practical applicability. However, the absence of empirical evidence leaves the reader uncertain about the viability of the proposed estimators, especially in settings where analytical saddle points do not exist. As a result, while the paper’s potential significance is high, the current evidence base is insufficient to fully support its claims.

Clarity and presentation
The paper is generally well written, but the breadth of goals within the page limit hinders clarity. The dynamic risk section reads as a sketch rather than a concrete algorithmic contribution. A more focused exposition—either limited to static risks with thorough empirical evaluation, or a longer version that does justice to both static and dynamic settings—would improve readability and impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical validation of the sampling-based estimator:
  - Compare sample-based and analytic gradients on benchmarks (e.g., CVaR) across multiple environments.
  - Report estimator variance, bias, convergence speed, and sensitivity to sample size.
  - Include ablation studies (e.g., effect of baseline/variance reduction).
- Clarify analytical tractability:
  - Specify which coherent risk measures admit closed-form saddle points and under what conditions.
  - Summarize these cases in a concise figure or table (possibly in the appendix).
- Flesh out the dynamic risk section:
  - Present algorithmic details (pseudo-code), assumptions, and computational complexity.
  - Include at least a small-scale empirical demonstration to establish feasibility.
- Strengthen the experimental section:
  - Go beyond the current toy example and evaluate on more realistic tasks (e.g., continuous control or portfolio-style problems) to demonstrate generality.
- Consider narrowing scope:
  - A conference version focused on static coherent risks with a comprehensive empirical study could make the paper more self-contained and persuasive. The dynamic extension might be better suited for a follow-up or journal version with space for full details and experiments.

Overall assessment
An interesting and potentially impactful paper with a strong and novel theoretical contribution. The unified derivations and consistency guarantees are compelling, but the lack of substantive empirical evaluation—and the high-level treatment of the dynamic case—makes it difficult to assess practical utility. With a tighter focus and solid experiments validating the sampling-based estimator and comparing it to analytic gradients, the paper could make a strong contribution to the risk-sensitive RL literature. As it stands, the work is promising but incomplete on the empirical side.
