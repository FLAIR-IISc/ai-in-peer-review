Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the entire class of coherent risk measures. For static risk, the authors exploit the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) to express ρ(Z) as a maximization over a risk envelope, derive a new gradient formula using the Envelope Theorem and the likelihood-ratio trick, and propose two estimators: (i) closed-form for certain measures (e.g., CVaR and mean-semideviation), and (ii) a general sample-average approximation (SAA) that combines sampling with a convex program whose KKT multipliers enter the gradient. For dynamic settings, they consider time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński, 2010), show that the value function solves a risk-sensitive Bellman equation related to robust MDP planning, and prove a policy gradient theorem that expresses ∇θVθ(x) as an expectation under ξ-weighted transitions with a stage-wise cost hθ. They then outline an actor-critic algorithm that uses function approximation for the critic (building on robust MDP approximation results, e.g., Petrik & Subramanian) and a two-phase sampling scheme for the actor. A small static portfolio selection example illustrates how different coherent risk preferences yield different policies. The work unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive RL for specific criteria such as variance-related measures (e.g., mean-variance), exponential utility, and CVaR (e.g., Tamar et al.; Chow et al.; Rockafellar & Uryasev).

Strengths
- Unifying framework: A single gradient machinery covers the broad class of coherent risk measures, subsuming prior results for CVaR, mean-semideviation, and beyond.
- Technical contribution: Clear use of dual representations and envelope theorems to derive practically estimable gradients; consistency of the SAA-based gradient estimator is established under mild assumptions.
- Dynamic extension: A policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is novel and conceptually important; the connection to robust MDPs clarifies the role of time consistency.
- Practicality: The proposed estimators use standard components (likelihood-ratio gradients, convex programming) and suggest feasible implementation paths.
- Clarity and writing: The paper is generally well organized and cites relevant literature in risk-sensitive RL, robust MDPs, and stochastic programming.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental scope: The evaluation is limited to a static toy example. The dynamic methods (value-function approximation under Markov coherent risk and the actor-critic update) are not empirically demonstrated. Including at least one dynamic benchmark (e.g., gridworld, inventory control, or continuous control with simulator access) would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Computational cost: The general static estimator requires solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers at each iteration/batch. A discussion of runtime, scaling with sample size and constraint count, and practical solver choices would help practitioners.
- Assumptions and applicability: The main text assumes finite probability spaces and known, differentiable envelopes with a Slater point. While extensions are claimed, guidance on how to specify and verify U(Pθ) for common coherent measures in RL (e.g., spectral risk) and for large/continuous spaces would be valuable. Clarify how the assumptions translate when Z depends on θ through long trajectories and when function approximation is used.
- Sampling requirements: The actor requires sampling from ξ-weighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per state. Many RL settings provide only single on-policy trajectories. Discuss feasibility, off-policy variants, or importance sampling alternatives, and any stability guarantees when reweighting transitions.
- Variance reduction: The LR estimators can have high variance. While λ* acts as a baseline in the formula, discussion of additional baselines/control variates and their effect on variance (especially for tail-focused risks like CVaR) would be useful.
- Theoretical details in supplement: Key proofs and the full actor-critic algorithm are deferred. Summarizing main conditions (ergodicity, step-size assumptions, function-approximation error bounds, bias from using approximate Vθ to compute ξ*) in the main text would improve readability.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The derivations are technically sound, with appropriate use of convex duality and policy-gradient methods. The static gradient results (including consistency of SAA) are convincing. The dynamic result is well motivated and appears correct under the stated assumptions, though empirical validation is missing.
- Clarity: Above average. Notation is heavy in places (e.g., Pθ used for both trajectory and per-state transitions). Minor suggestions: include a concise pseudocode block for the general static estimator and the dynamic actor-critic; give concrete envelope forms for several measures; provide a brief complexity discussion for the convex program.
- Originality: Strong. Extending policy gradient methods to general coherent risk measures (static and dynamic) and deriving a unified gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is a nontrivial and novel advance relative to prior work focused on specific risks.
- Significance: High potential impact. Coherent risks are widely used in finance and operations research; unifying them under RL policy gradients broadens the toolbox for risk-sensitive control. Demonstrated dynamic experiments and practical guidance would increase immediate applicability.

Pros for acceptance
- Unified, principled treatment of coherent risk in RL with new gradient theorems.
- General, implementable static estimator with consistency guarantees.
- Novel dynamic policy gradient theorem tying risk-sensitive RL to robust MDPs.
- Clear exposition and relevant connections to prior literature.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation (no dynamic experiments; no comparisons on standard RL benchmarks).
- Computational and sampling requirements not fully characterized.
- Several technical details and algorithmic components deferred to the supplement.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide runtime and variance comparisons between the analytical (closed-form) and SAA-based static estimators across several coherent risks?
- How sensitive is the actor-critic performance to errors in the critic’s value approximation when computing ξ*? Do you have bounds or empirical evidence?
- Can the dynamic method be adapted to single-trajectory data (e.g., via importance sampling or off-policy corrections)?
- For continuous state/action spaces, how do you represent and solve the per-state convex problem to obtain ξ* and multipliers efficiently?

Overall recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper with a clear, general contribution and sound methodology. If space permits, adding at least one dynamic experiment and brief complexity/variance analyses would make it a clear accept. As is, I lean toward acceptance based on novelty and potential impact, with the caveat of limited empirical evaluation.
