

The paper investigates risk-averse Markov decision processes under static and dynamically consistent risk measures (mappings), with its primary contribution being the derivation of gradient forms for both dynamic and static settings. While the work generalizes prior results focused exclusively on CVaR, this extension constitutes a minor but useful advancement. The results represent a straightforward extension of policy gradient methods from risk-neutral to risk-averse settings, maintaining conceptual simplicity without introducing novel technical machinery.  

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and logically organized, making it accessible to readers familiar with MDPs and risk measures. The technical content appears correct upon review, with all proofs and derivations aligning with established principles in the field. The authors successfully demonstrate that the risk-averse policy gradient framework reduces to standard policy gradient in the risk-neutral limit, reinforcing the coherence of their approach.  

The contribution, though incremental, addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by formalizing gradient computation for a broader class of risk measures. While the extension from CVaR to general risk measures does not yield surprising insights, it provides a valuable foundation for future work on risk-averse reinforcement learning with diverse risk criteria. The paper’s clarity and precision elevate this modest extension beyond a mere technical footnote, positioning it as a useful reference for practitioners seeking to implement risk-averse policies beyond CVaR.  

Minor suggestions for improvement:  
1. **Theorem 2.1**: The notation $\xi P_\theta$ is ambiguous and potentially confusing. To enhance consistency, the authors should adopt the notation used in Theorem 6.6 of [26], which explicitly defines the transition dynamics under policy $\theta$.  
2. **Line 141 (Section 3)**: The phrase "risk enevlop" appears to be a typo (intended: "risk envelope"). More importantly, the section would benefit from a brief clarification: *MDPs with Markov risk measures are generally tractable, but the intractability in this paper arises solely from the policy parametrization, not from the risk measure itself.* This distinction is crucial for readers unfamiliar with the literature, as it prevents misattribution of computational challenges to the risk framework rather than the optimization setup.  

The paper presents a minor yet solid and important extension of existing results. It successfully bridges a gap between theoretical risk-averse MDPs and practical policy optimization, offering a clear pathway for extending risk-averse RL algorithms beyond CVaR. The authors’ emphasis on gradient-based methods aligns well with contemporary reinforcement learning practice, ensuring immediate applicability. While the core ideas are not revolutionary, their precise articulation and rigorous treatment lend the work significant value for the community.  

In summary, this is a well-executed technical contribution that advances the state of the art in risk-averse MDPs with minimal but meaningful novelty. The writing is polished, the mathematics is sound, and the extension to general risk measures is both justified and carefully executed. The minor revisions suggested above would further enhance the paper’s clarity and impact. The work merits acceptance as a solid, incremental advance in a growing area of research.
