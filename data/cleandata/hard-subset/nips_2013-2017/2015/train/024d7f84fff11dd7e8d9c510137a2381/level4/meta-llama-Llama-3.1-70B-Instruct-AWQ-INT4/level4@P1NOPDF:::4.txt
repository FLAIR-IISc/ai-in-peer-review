The authors consider the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure, focusing on a general class of risk measures defined by simple coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The presented approach is based on a dual representation of any coherent risk measure as a convex hull of probability distributions. The risk of a random variable, such as a cost, is given by the maximum expected value in the hull. The paper makes an assumption on the family of coherent risk measures by bounding the maximum derivatives of the convex hull's borders, assuming the borders of the hull are M-Lipschitz continuous. Using this assumption, the authors analytically derive the optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure, as well as a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static case (one-step decision) and the dynamic (or sequential) case.

The paper is well-written and organized. Although I am not familiar with risk-sensitive optimization, I was able to follow most of the explanations. The main contribution of this work is the unified analysis of a general class of risk measures that includes most well-known measures. I believe this is a significant contribution. However, a deeper empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm would be appreciated.

Some comments and questions:

1. The preliminaries section could be clarified. I was initially confused about the roles of P_{\theta} and \ksi.

2. Isn't Equation 2 essentially the definition of a convex envelope? Why is this more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?

3. It would be beneficial to include a comment on how the analysis generalizes to distributions on continuous variables.

4. It would be nice to mention in the paper the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in proving Theorem 4.2.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that provides a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results seem compelling, but the proofs need to be verified.
