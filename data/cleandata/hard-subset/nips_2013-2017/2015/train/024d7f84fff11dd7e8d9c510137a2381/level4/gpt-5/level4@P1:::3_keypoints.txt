- Studies risk-averse RL for static and time-consistent dynamic settings under the class of coherent risk measures.  
- Main technical contributions: new static coherent-risk gradient combining the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio method.  
- Proposes a sampling-based estimator that solves a convex program defined by the risk envelope and proves SAA consistency.  
- Derives a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk and an actor-critic algorithm using ξ-weighted trajectory distributions.  
- Unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive policy-gradient methods (variance, mean-variance, exponential utility, CVaR).  
- Recovers known special cases (e.g., CVaR) and provides a general recipe when the risk envelope is given in convex form.  
- Numerical example shows different risk criteria (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard deviation) yield qualitatively different policies, especially with heavy-tailed returns.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized exposition and apparently correct technical development.  
- Strength: provides a unifying treatment covering both static and Markov dynamic coherent risks.  
- Strength: static gradient formula is simple/practical and the SAA-based estimator is natural and consistent.  
- Strength: dynamic extension cleanly extends the policy gradient theorem and the actor-critic design is sensible via robust MDP equivalence.  
- Weakness: contribution is incremental—largely a straightforward extension of risk-neutral and CVaR-specific results.  
- Weakness: limited empirical validation—only a small illustrative example and no study of computational overhead or scalability.  
- Weakness: approach requires explicit convex form of the risk envelope and simulator access for ξ-weighted transitions, limiting applicability.  
- Quality: technically sound; envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio use are appropriate and SAA assumptions are reasonable.  
- Clarity: well written with clear assumptions; linking the critic to robust MDPs is helpful.  
- Originality: mainly a generalization of existing results; the dynamic theorem is useful but follows from prior robust MDP/risk-sensitive DP work.  
- Significance: a minor but useful unification that may ease future work; practical impact hinges on computational scalability and envelope availability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear, correct unification; broadly applicable gradient estimators with consistency guarantees; useful theoretical extension.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, limited experiments, and restrictive assumptions that may limit practical use.  
- Minor suggestions: clarify ξ Pθ notation, fix typo “risk enevlop[e]”, explicitly note DP tractability of Markov coherent risks, discuss computational costs and scaling, and expand experiments to larger tasks or CVaR baselines.
