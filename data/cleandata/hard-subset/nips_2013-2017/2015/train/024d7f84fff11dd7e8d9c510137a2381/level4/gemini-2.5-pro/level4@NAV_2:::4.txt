This paper presents a novel and general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning. The central idea is to extend existing policy gradient methods, which have previously been developed for specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This class is axiomatically defined by desirable properties such as convexity and monotonicity and is widely used in finance and operations research. The authors' approach is founded on the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which frames the risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities, known as the risk envelope.

The paper's main contributions are twofold. First, for static risk problems, it derives a new, general formula for the policy gradient by applying the envelope theorem to the dual formulation. This leads to a practical algorithm that estimates the gradient by combining sampling with convex programming. Second, for dynamic, sequential decision-making problems, the paper introduces a policy gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. This theorem connects the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and serves as the foundation for a proposed actor-critic algorithm. This work successfully unifies and generalizes several previous results in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [18, 21, 30]), providing a single, principled framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods under the umbrella of coherent risk theory. Moving from problem-specific solutions to a general, axiomatic framework is a significant conceptual advance for the field. The derived gradient formulas are novel and provide a clear theoretical basis for developing new risk-sensitive algorithms.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical development is elegant and appears sound, skillfully integrating concepts from convex optimization (dual representation, envelope theorem) with established reinforcement learning techniques (policy gradient theorem, actor-critic methods). The formulation is general and applies to any coherent risk measure whose risk envelope can be described by a set of convex constraints.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. It provides the necessary background on coherent risk measures before developing its main results, and the examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrate how the general framework recovers and simplifies previous, specific results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is a simple bandit-style problem. While it effectively demonstrates why flexibility in choosing a risk measure is important (especially for heavy-tailed distributions), it does not evaluate the performance of the proposed gradient estimation algorithms. Key practical questions regarding the sample complexity, variance of the gradient estimates, and scalability of the proposed SAA and actor-critic methods on standard RL benchmarks remain unanswered. This makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the algorithms beyond their theoretical appeal.
*   **Clarity on Some Technical Points:** Certain technical assumptions and their implications could be explained more clearly to benefit a broader audience. The step from the general theory to a practical algorithm relies on several assumptions whose importance could be better highlighted (see detailed comments below).

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  In the preliminaries, the relationship between the base probability measure Pθ (governed by the policy) and the density ξ (chosen adversarially from the risk envelope U) is crucial. For readers less familiar with this duality, it would be helpful to state more explicitly that ξ functions as a re-weighting of Pθ to find a "worst-case" distribution within the set defined by U, and the risk is the expectation under this worst-case distribution.

2.  Assumption 2.2 provides a concrete, convex-programming formulation for the risk envelope U. It would improve clarity to explicitly state that this assumption operationalizes the abstract representation in Theorem 2.1, making it possible to solve for the worst-case density ξ and its associated Lagrange multipliers, which are essential for the gradient formula in Theorem 4.2.

3.  The paper claims that the analysis can be extended from finite probability spaces to L_p-normed spaces. While omitting the full proof is understandable given space limitations, a brief discussion of the required technical machinery (e.g., use of Radon-Nikodym derivatives instead of simple re-weighting) and any additional challenges would make this claim more concrete and strengthen the paper.

4.  The application of the envelope theorem is central to the derivation of Theorem 4.2. In the main text, it would be beneficial to briefly connect the smoothness conditions in Assumption 2.2 to the prerequisites of the envelope theorem, explaining that these conditions ensure the value function of the optimization problem (Eq. 1) is differentiable with respect to the policy parameters θ.

**Summary:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution by providing a general and unified approach to policy optimization for coherent risk measures. The results are compelling and elegantly connect two mature fields. While the practical performance of the proposed algorithms needs to be demonstrated in more challenging domains, the theoretical framework itself is a valuable addition to the reinforcement learning literature.
