This paper considers the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. The authors focus on a general class of coherent risk measures, defined by properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The proposed approach is based on the dual representation of a coherent risk measure as the convex hull of a set of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable (e.g., a cost) is the maximum expected value over the distributions in that hull. A key assumption is made that the boundaries of this convex hull are M-Lipschitz continuous. Using this assumption, the authors derive a general gradient estimation algorithm for optimizing policy parameters with respect to the risk measure. The algorithm is developed for both static (single-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision-making settings.

The paper is well-written and organized, and I was able to follow most of the explanations despite not being an expert in risk-sensitive optimization. The primary contribution is the unified analysis of a general class of risk measures that includes many well-known examples, which I believe is a significant contribution. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm.

Comments and Questions:

1.  The Preliminaries section lacks some clarity. I was confused about the respective roles of P_{\theta} and \ksi.
2.  Is Equation 2 not simply the definition of a convex envelope? Could the authors clarify why this is more restrictive than the representation in Theorem 2.1?
3.  I would suggest adding a comment on how the analysis generalizes to distributions over continuous variables.
4.  It would be beneficial to mention the role that the smoothness of the constraint functions plays in the proof of Theorem 4.2.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper that provides a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results seem compelling, though the proofs require careful verification.
