This paper presents methods for estimating policy gradients in risk-sensitive problems with coherent risk measures, and proposes RL algorithms based on them. The paper particularly extends existing methods, focused on specific risk measures, to the general class of coherent risk measures, thus providing a unified framework. The approach is illustrated with a toy example in a financial setting, where each asset has its own return distribution.

The motivation is clear, and the problem formulation and algorithms follow naturally from this motivation and prior work. The experimental evaluation is relatively simple, focusing on a static risk problem. However, the results appear to support the quality of the algorithm, demonstrating its risk-averse behavior and flexibility across different risk types.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-written, with equations presented clearly and all relevant references provided. The theoretical contributions appear solid, and the connections to existing literature are appropriately made.

**Clarity:** The clarity of this paper is above average. The authors do a good job of explaining complex concepts in a digestible manner. The organization is logical, and the notation is consistent. 

**Originality:** The paper proposes novel algorithms for risk-sensitive RL problems, building upon related work in risk-sensitive planning and RL. Extending existing approaches to this more general setting of coherent risk measures is not trivial. The originality of the paper appears sufficient for NIPS.

**Significance:** Risk-sensitive RL is an important and interesting problem, and this paper provides significant contributions to the field. The paper presents clear algorithms for solving risk-sensitive RL problems with coherent risk measures and demonstrates the behavior of trained policies with different risk settings in a static risk problem. The proposed approach is well-motivated by the authors and represents a natural extension to existing algorithms and problems—including policy gradient methods, actor-critic techniques, and coherent risk measure optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL using coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work.
*   Presents novel gradient estimation algorithms and actor-critic methods for this setting.
*   Offers a theoretically sound approach, with clear connections to existing literature.
*   Demonstrates the potential of the approach through a well-motivated example.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited to a simple static risk problem, lacking a more comprehensive assessment of performance on complex dynamic environments.
*   The convergence analysis of the proposed actor-critic algorithm, while mentioned, is detailed only in the supplementary material. A more concise summary within the main text would be beneficial.
