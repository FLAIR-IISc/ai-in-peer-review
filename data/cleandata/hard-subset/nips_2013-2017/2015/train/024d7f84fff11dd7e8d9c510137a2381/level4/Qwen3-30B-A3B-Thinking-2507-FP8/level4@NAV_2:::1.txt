

The paper introduces a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, extending previous work on specific risk measures such as CVaR and mean-variance. It addresses both static coherent risk measures (using sampling combined with convex programming) and dynamic Markov coherent risk measures (using an actor-critic approach). The authors demonstrate that several previously studied risk-sensitive RL methods are special cases of their general formulation, providing a novel theoretical foundation that encompasses and generalizes existing approaches.

Quality: The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with well-supported claims. The theoretical derivations of the gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk measures are rigorous, and the consistency guarantees for the sampling-based estimators are well-established. However, the lack of empirical validation for the proposed methods significantly weakens the quality assessment. The authors do not provide any practical demonstration of the algorithm's performance, which is particularly problematic given the complexity of the proposed methods. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the absence of experimental evidence prevents a full assessment of the practical utility.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, but the attempt to cover both static and dynamic risk measures within an 8-page limit results in significant clarity issues. The dynamic risk section (Section 5) is presented at a very high level without sufficient algorithm details or implementation considerations. The treatment of the actor-critic approach lacks sufficient depth to allow readers to assess its practical viability. The paper would benefit from being split into two separate works—one focusing on static risk measures with more detailed analysis and empirical evaluation, and another on dynamic risk measures.

Originality: The unified treatment of coherent risk measures in policy gradient methods represents a significant theoretical advance. To the best of my knowledge, this general formulation of policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures is novel, extending beyond previous work that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance.

Significance: The work provides a valuable theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that could be broadly applicable across different domains. The framework offers flexibility in risk preference design, which is particularly important for real-world applications. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of empirical validation and the high level of abstraction in the dynamic risk section. Without practical demonstration of the approach's effectiveness, it is difficult to assess whether this framework will lead to meaningful improvements in real-world decision-making scenarios.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods that generalizes several previous approaches. The theoretical contribution is substantial and could provide a foundation for future research in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.

Arguments against acceptance: The lack of empirical validation for the proposed methods significantly undermines the quality and significance of the work. The paper attempts to cover both static and dynamic risk measures within an 8-page limit, resulting in insufficient detail for the dynamic risk portion and a lack of practical demonstration of the approach's viability. The paper would be stronger with a narrower focus on static risk measures, allowing for more comprehensive empirical evaluation.
