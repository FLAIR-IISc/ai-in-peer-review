

The paper introduces a novel formula for static coherent risk measures, enabling analytical derivation of policy gradients for numerous common risk measures. It further proposes a general sampling-based algorithm for scenarios lacking analytical solutions and establishes consistency guarantees for the policy gradient estimates. The work also derives policy gradients for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.  

Significance and quality:  
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures represents a valuable and novel contribution to risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are restricted to specific risk measures. While the proposed sampling-based approximation scheme could extend applicability to general coherent risk measures by circumventing analytical saddle-point derivations, the paper lacks empirical validation of the estimation scheme’s practical viability. An experimental comparison between sample-based and analytical policy gradients—particularly for risk measures like CVaR—would be highly beneficial.  

Originality:  
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients in this general form are novel.  

Clarity:  
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is significantly impaired by attempting to address multiple distinct issues within the eight-page limit. Section 5’s treatment of dynamic risk policy gradients is presented at a notably high level, with insufficient algorithmic details and no experimental evaluations. This prevents readers from assessing the practical viability of the derived approaches. The content in Section 5 is better suited for a journal publication. I recommend splitting the paper to focus exclusively on static risk measures, thereby enabling a more self-contained contribution with deeper technical details and comprehensive empirical validation beyond the current submission’s rudimentary toy example. While the paper presents potentially broadly applicable results, the absence of thorough experimental evaluations hinders assessment. A narrower scope would also address clarity deficits imposed by space constraints.
