This paper presents a novel and unified framework for deriving policy gradients for a broad class of static coherent risks, and extends this to dynamic risk measures. The core contribution lies in establishing a general formula from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be obtained analytically. For cases where analytical solutions are intractable, the authors propose a sampling-based algorithm with consistency guarantees. This work represents a valuable addition to the literature on risk-sensitive decision making.

**Significance and Quality:**

The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a particularly strong aspect of this work. This is a significant and novel contribution, offering a more general and theoretically grounded approach to policy optimization under risk. However, the practical impact is currently limited by the availability of analytical solutions. While the paper demonstrates the theoretical possibility of deriving gradients analytically for some risk measures, this is not universally applicable. 

The proposed sampling-based approximation is a crucial step towards broadening the applicability of the derived results. It allows for the use of the framework with general coherent risk measures that lack closed-form analytical solutions. However, a major weakness of the submission is the complete absence of empirical validation demonstrating the viability of this sampling scheme. An experimental comparison of the sample-based policy gradients against analytical solutions (where available), perhaps focusing on a benchmark like CVaR, would be exceptionally helpful in establishing the practical effectiveness and computational cost of the proposed method.

**Originality:**

To the best of my knowledge, the presented policy gradients, in this level of generality, are novel. The work successfully synthesizes concepts from coherent risk measures and policy gradient methods, resulting in a unique and potentially impactful framework. 

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written, but suffers from attempting to cover a substantial amount of material – static and dynamic risk measures, analytical and sampling-based methods – within a limited page count. This breadth hinders clarity, particularly in Section 5 concerning dynamic risk policy gradients. The presentation of these dynamic gradients is highly abstract, lacking concrete algorithmic details and crucially, experimental evaluation. This makes it difficult to assess whether the derived gradients lead to practical and effective algorithms.

The scope of the paper feels ambitious for its length. A more focused approach, perhaps concentrating solely on the static risk measures, would allow for a more thorough development of the ideas, a more detailed presentation of the sampling-based algorithm, and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the single, basic toy example currently provided. Splitting the content into two papers – one focusing on static risks with substantial empirical work, and another detailing the dynamic risk extensions – would be a beneficial alternative. 

In summary, this is an interesting paper with potentially significant and generally applicable results. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation makes it difficult to fully assess its practical value. A narrower focus, combined with more extensive experimentation, would significantly strengthen the contribution and improve the paper’s clarity.
