The paper introduces a general gradient formula for static coherent risk measures that enables deriving analytic policy gradients for many commonly used risks. It further provides a generic sampling-based procedure, coupled with convex optimization, to handle cases where closed-form saddle points are unavailable, and establishes consistency of the resulting gradient estimates. Finally, it derives expressions for policy gradients under Markov, time-consistent coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risks is compelling and constitutes a valuable, novel contribution to risk-sensitive decision making. However, explicit Lagrangian saddle-point solutions are provided only for certain risk measures.
The proposed sampling-based approximation could broaden applicability by removing the need to derive analytic saddle points, but the paper does not present empirical evidence that this estimator is practical. An experimental comparison between sampling-based and analytic gradients on multiple problems (e.g., for CVaR) would substantially strengthen the claims.

Originality: To the best of my knowledge, presenting policy gradients in this general form is novel.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but the breadth of topics attempted within eight pages hurts clarity. In particular, the dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level, and the absence of algorithmic details and empirical evaluation prevents the reader from assessing practical viability. This content seems better suited for a longer journal article. Alternatively, a tighter focus on static risk measures would make the paper more self-contained and allow for more detail and a more thorough empirical study beyond the simple toy example provided. Overall, this is an interesting submission with potentially broadly useful results, but the limited experimental evidence makes this difficult to evaluate; a narrower scope could also alleviate the clarity issues imposed by space constraints.
