

This paper proposes a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, extending previous work that focused on specific risk measures like variance, CVaR, and mean-variance. The authors derive a general formula for the gradient of static coherent risk measures, develop a sampling-based algorithm for gradient estimation, and extend the approach to dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. The work builds on the theoretical foundation of coherent risk measures [2] and Markov coherent risk [24], while generalizing and unifying previous results on risk-sensitive reinforcement learning [8, 18, 21, 29, 30].

Quality: The paper makes a technically sound contribution by establishing a unified framework for coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The theoretical development is rigorous, with well-motivated assumptions and clear proofs of key results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2). However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed sampling-based gradient estimation approach. While the authors provide a consistency guarantee (Proposition 4.4), they do not demonstrate how the algorithm performs in practice, especially for non-trivial risk measures where analytical solutions are unavailable. The numerical example in Section 6 is limited to a toy trading problem with only three assets, which does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written but suffers from being too ambitious for its eight-page format. The authors attempt to cover both static and dynamic risk results at a high level, making it difficult to assess the practical viability of the dynamic risk approach. Section 5 on dynamic risk measures is particularly superficial, lacking algorithm details and implementation considerations that would be necessary for replication. The connection between theoretical results and practical implementation is not sufficiently developed, and the paper would benefit from a more focused presentation.

Originality: The unified treatment of coherent risk measures in policy gradient methods is novel and represents a significant contribution to the literature. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution relative to previous work on risk-sensitive RL [8, 18, 21, 29, 30] and coherent risk measures [2, 24, 25, 26].

Significance: The potential significance of this work is high, as it provides a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that could be applied to various domains. However, without empirical validation of the proposed sampling-based approach, the significance is difficult to fully assess. The paper's claim that "our approach extends risk-sensitive RL to the whole class of coherent risk measures" is theoretically compelling but lacks practical demonstration.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a theoretically sound unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL
- Generalizes previous work on specific risk measures
- Theoretical guarantees for gradient estimation are well-established
- The work has clear potential applications in risk-sensitive decision making

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks empirical validation of the sampling-based approach
- The dynamic risk section is too superficial for a conference paper
- The numerical example is too limited to demonstrate practical utility
- The paper attempts to cover too much in eight pages, sacrificing depth

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation of the sampling-based gradient estimator across multiple risk measures and problem domains
2. Focusing on either static or dynamic risk measures (rather than both) to allow for greater depth and practical details
3. Including algorithm pseudocode and implementation specifics for the proposed methods

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the lack of empirical validation and the superficial treatment of the dynamic risk approach significantly limit the paper's impact for a conference venue like NIPS. The authors' claim that their method "generalizes and extends previous results" is theoretically valid but remains unverified in practice. The current submission does not provide sufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of the proposed sampling-based approach, which is a critical component of the paper's contribution.

Given the current state of the paper, I recommend rejection due to insufficient empirical validation and the paper's overly ambitious scope for an eight-page format. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the lack of practical demonstration significantly limits the paper's impact for a conference venue. The authors would benefit from focusing on a narrower scope (e.g., static risk measures) and providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation before resubmitting to a conference venue.
