- Summary: Proposes unified gradient-based policy optimization for general coherent risk measures via dual representation and an M-Lipschitz boundary assumption, covering static and sequential settings.

- Strength: Generality/unification: Framework covers a broad class of coherent risk measures under a single analytical approach.

- Strength: Theoretical clarity: Dual representation yields clean gradient characterizations and principled estimation methods for static and sequential cases.

- Strength: Accessibility: Well-written and organized; main ideas understandable without deep prior risk-optimization background.

- Strength: Potential impact: Offers a timely, general-purpose approach to risk-sensitive policy optimization.

- Concern: Strength of assumptions: The M-Lipschitz condition on the risk envelope boundary may be restrictive and needs clarification on when it holds.

- Concern: Practical implementability: Method seems to require oracles for the risk envelope; costs, scalability, and representation/updating of adversarial distributions are unclear.

- Concern: Empirical evaluation: Experiments are limited; more comparisons, sample-complexity/variance analyses, and sensitivity studies (e.g., to M) are needed.

- Concern: Technical subtleties: Danskin-like exchanges, saddle-point existence/uniqueness, and conditions for unbiased/low-variance sequential estimators require clearer assumptions and verification.

- Request 1: Clarify roles of Pθ (nominal policy-induced distribution) and ξ (adversarial/reweighting within risk envelope), ideally with a running example.

- Request 2: Explain precisely how Equation (2) relates to Theorem 2.1—whether Eq.(2) is a specialization (e.g., polyhedral envelope) and under what conditions.

- Request 3: Discuss extensions to continuous spaces: measurability/compactness, existence of maximizers, Radon–Nikodym use, and sampling/importance-weighting implementation.

- Request 4: State smoothness/regularity assumptions used in Theorem 4.2 and how they enable envelope/Danskin arguments and argmax stability.

- Request 5: For sequential gradient estimation, specify conditions (ergodicity, boundedness, interchange of expectation/differentiation) and clarify unbiasedness/consistency and variance-reduction options.

- Request 6: Give examples (CVaR, mean-semi-deviation, spectral risks) where M can be bounded and practical ways to estimate or upper-bound M.

- Request 7: Detail computational complexity per gradient step, especially cost of solving the inner adversarial convex program and effects of approximate solutions on convergence.

- Minor: Define the risk envelope and use consistent notation for ξ and Pθ early.

- Minor: Include a simple illustrative example (e.g., finite-outcome CVaR) tracing dual variables, envelope, and gradient.

- Minor: Better situate work relative to robust MDPs, DRO, and known policy-gradient methods; cite standard dual representations and practical algorithms.

- Minor: Collect and restate all regularity assumptions (boundedness, convexity, compactness, differentiability) in one place before main theorems.

- Overall: Strong theoretical paper with potential influence; recommend acceptance conditional on clarifications, extended empirical validation, and careful proof verification.
