- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures in both static and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) settings.  
- Static case: derives gradient via dual (risk-envelope) representation and envelope theorem, yielding a sampling estimator combining likelihood ratios with a convex program over the risk envelope.  
- Proposes a sample-average-approximation (SAA) scheme for static gradients and proves consistency of the estimator under mild conditions.  
- Dynamic case: provides a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk linking gradients to a ξ-weighted transition value function, enabling an actor–critic style method.  
- Recovers known special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation) and unifies prior risk-sensitive policy-gradient results.  
- Leverages the connection to robust MDPs to inform the critic and handle function approximation.  
- Main technical ideas extend standard likelihood-ratio policy gradients using the dual form of coherent risk.  
- Strength: unified framework that clarifies how previous CVaR/variance methods fit as special cases.  
- Strength: clear, sampling-friendly gradient formulas for static and dynamic settings.  
- Strength: theoretical consistency result for the SAA-based static estimator.  
- Strength: conceptually appealing dynamic actor–critic with ξ-weighted transitions and robust-MDP linkage.  
- Strength: well written and well organized.  
- Weakness: incremental novelty—primarily a generalization/unification rather than a new algorithmic paradigm.  
- Weakness: practical burden—static estimator needs solving a convex program per batch and dynamic method requires multiple next-state samples; computational/sample complexity not analyzed.  
- Weakness: restrictive assumptions—finite probability spaces, availability of likelihood ratios, and smooth/explicit risk envelopes.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation—only a simple static trading example, no dynamic-risk experiments, and minimal baseline comparisons.  
- Quality: theoretical development appears sound; gradient identities, Lagrangian formulation, and consistency arguments are reasonable.  
- Clarity: generally clear and well written, with minor notational/expository issues.  
- Originality: moderate—generalizes known ideas (policy gradients, dual coherent risk, actor–critic) to a broader class.  
- Significance: useful unified framework that eases plugging in different coherent risks, though conceptual step is incremental.  
- Suggestions: clarify ξPθ notation, fix typos, state tractability of Markov coherent risk via dynamic programming, discuss computational cost and sample requirements, and expand experiments to dynamic-risk settings and stronger baselines.  
- Acceptance arguments: clear unified gradient framework, consistency guarantees, robust-MDP connection, and practical value to the community.  
- Rejection arguments: limited conceptual novelty, minimal empirical validation (no dynamic experiments), and unaddressed practical computational requirements.
