### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel and general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning. The authors extend previous work, which has largely focused on specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The core technical contribution relies on the dual representation of any coherent risk measure, which frames it as a maximization of the expected cost over a convex set of probability densities, known as the risk envelope.

The paper addresses two distinct settings:
1.  **Static Risk:** For a random variable representing a total cost (e.g., discounted return of a finite trajectory), the authors derive a new policy gradient formula. This leads to a general gradient estimation algorithm that cleverly combines sampling with convex programming to find the worst-case density within the risk envelope.
2.  **Dynamic Risk:** For sequential decision-making problems, the paper considers time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. It introduces a corresponding risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem and proposes an actor-critic algorithm to solve the problem, extending the standard policy gradient theorem to this risk-sensitive setting.

The work successfully unifies and generalizes several prior results, re-deriving formulas for specific risk measures like CVaR as special cases of their general framework.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. By developing a policy gradient method for the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a unified framework that subsumes many disparate, specialized results in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. This is a significant conceptual and theoretical contribution that could guide future research in this area.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is elegant and appears sound. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk and the application of the Envelope Theorem to derive the main gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) is a powerful and novel approach in this context. The subsequent extension to the dynamic, time-consistent case via a new policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) and an actor-critic framework is a solid and complete piece of work.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. Despite the technical nature of the material, the authors do a commendable job of explaining the core concepts, making the paper accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with risk theory. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrate the power and generality of the main theoretical result.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the conceptual benefit of having a flexible choice of risk measures, it is based on a very simple one-step problem. A more comprehensive empirical analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. For example, it would be valuable to see:
    *   A comparison of the general sample-based gradient estimator from Section 4.3 with the specialized analytical estimator for CVaR (Section 4.1) on a benchmark task. This would provide practical insight into the sample efficiency and computational overhead of the general approach.
    *   An evaluation of the proposed actor-critic algorithm on a more complex sequential decision-making problem.

2.  **Clarity in Preliminaries:** The Preliminaries section could be refined for readers less familiar with this area. Specifically, the relationship and distinct roles of the underlying probability measure Pθ (parameterized by the policy) and the density functions ξ (which define the risk envelope) could be explained more explicitly. A simple illustrative example at this stage might help build intuition.

3.  **Clarification on Assumptions:**
    *   It would be helpful to briefly discuss how restrictive Assumption 2.2 is. Theorem 2.1 states that any coherent risk measure corresponds to a convex and closed risk envelope `U`. Assumption 2.2 further requires this set to be described by specific affine and convex constraints that are differentiable in `p`. Does this exclude any common or interesting coherent risk measures? A short comment on this would be useful.
    *   The paper focuses on finite probability spaces. While the authors state that the results can be extended, a brief discussion in the main paper on the key challenges or modifications needed for continuous state/action spaces would be valuable.

4.  **Connecting Theory to Proof:** The proof of the main gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) relies on the Envelope Theorem. It would be beneficial to explicitly mention in the main text how the smoothness of the constraint functions, assumed in Assumption 2.2, is critical for this theorem to apply. This would better connect the assumptions to the main results for the reader.

### **Conclusion**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant and elegant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It provides a principled and unified approach to policy optimization for a broad and important class of risk measures. The results appear compelling and generalize several previous studies in a non-trivial way. While the empirical validation is currently limited, the strength and novelty of the theoretical framework make the paper a clear candidate for acceptance. The authors are encouraged to incorporate the suggestions for improving clarity and to consider expanding the experimental section for the final camera-ready version.
