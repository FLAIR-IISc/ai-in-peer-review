- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures, deriving static and dynamic gradient formulas and proposing closed-form and SAA estimators.  
- Static method: uses dual representation + Envelope Theorem + likelihood-ratio trick to obtain gradients; offers closed-form for some risks and a general SAA-based estimator.  
- Dynamic method: defines time-consistent Markov coherent risk, proves a policy-gradient theorem with ξ-weighted transitions, links to robust MDPs, and outlines an actor-critic algorithm.  
- Strength: Provides a unified framework that subsumes prior risk-specific RL methods (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation).  
- Strength: Solid technical derivations using convex duality; SAA-based gradient estimator shown consistent under mild assumptions.  
- Strength: The dynamic policy-gradient theorem is novel and clarifies time consistency via robust MDP connections.  
- Strength: Proposed estimators use standard components (likelihood ratios, convex solvers), making implementation feasible.  
- Strength: Paper is generally well organized and cites relevant literature.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is limited to a static toy example; no dynamic experiments or benchmarks demonstrated.  
- Weakness: The SAA estimator requires solving convex programs and extracting KKT multipliers each iteration—runtime, scaling, and solver guidance are missing.  
- Weakness: Main-text assumptions (finite probability spaces, known differentiable envelopes, Slater point) need clearer guidance for common measures and large/continuous spaces.  
- Weakness: Actor requires sampling from ξ-weighted transitions and multiple next-state samples, which is impractical in single-trajectory on-policy settings; off-policy/importance-sampling alternatives are not discussed.  
- Weakness: LR estimators can have high variance; more discussion on baselines/control variates (especially for tail risks) is needed.  
- Weakness: Key proofs and full actor-critic algorithm are deferred to supplement; main text should summarize core conditions (ergodicity, step-sizes, approximation error bounds).  
- Evaluation (quality): Derivations are technically sound; static results convincing; dynamic theory plausible but empirically unvalidated.  
- Evaluation (clarity): Above average but notation-heavy; requests for pseudocode, envelope examples, and convex-program complexity discussion.  
- Evaluation (originality/significance): Highly original with potentially high impact for risk-sensitive RL and control.  
- Pros for acceptance: Unified coherent-risk treatment, implementable estimator with consistency guarantees, novel dynamic theorem, clear exposition.  
- Cons for acceptance: Limited empirical validation, unclear computational/sampling demands, and deferred technical details.  
- Questions for authors: runtime/variance comparisons between analytic and SAA estimators; sensitivity/bounds for critic approximation error when computing ξ*; adaptation to single-trajectory data (importance sampling/off-policy); efficient per-state convex-solve methods for continuous spaces.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong theoretical contribution leaning toward acceptance, contingent on added dynamic experiments and brief complexity/variance analyses.
