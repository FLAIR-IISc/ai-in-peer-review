

The paper introduces a novel unified framework for deriving policy gradients for static coherent risk measures, enabling analytical solutions for many common risk metrics. It further proposes a general sampling-based algorithm for cases lacking analytical solutions and establishes consistency guarantees for the policy gradient estimates. Finally, it derives policy gradients for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and Quality: The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures represents a valuable and novel contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, analytical solutions for the Lagrangian saddle-points are limited to specific risk measures. While the proposed sampling-based approximation scheme offers a pathway to apply the results to general coherent risk measures by circumventing the need for analytical saddle-point derivations, the paper lacks empirical validation of the scheme's practical viability. An experimental comparison between analytical and sample-based policy gradients—particularly for risk measures like CVaR across diverse problems—would significantly strengthen the claims.

Originality: To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients in this general form are novel.

Clarity: The paper is well-written but suffers from excessive scope within the eight-page limit. The dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level, with insufficient algorithmic details and no experimental evaluations. This prevents readers from assessing whether the derived gradients yield practically viable methods. The content of Section 5 appears more suitable for a journal publication. A more focused approach—centering the paper on static risk measures—would allow for greater depth, comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the current toy example, and improved clarity. While the paper presents potentially broadly applicable results, the absence of thorough experimental validation makes it difficult to evaluate the practical impact. A narrower focus would also better address the clarity deficits imposed by the tight page constraints.
