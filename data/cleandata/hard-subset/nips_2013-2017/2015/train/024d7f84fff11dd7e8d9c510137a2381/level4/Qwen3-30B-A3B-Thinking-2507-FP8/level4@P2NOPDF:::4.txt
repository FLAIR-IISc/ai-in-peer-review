

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing policy parameters to minimize a broad class of coherent risk measures, defined by standard properties (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance). The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures as the supremum of expectations over a convex set of probability measures. Their key assumption imposes an M-Lipschitz continuity constraint on the boundary of this dual set, enabling analytical derivation of optimal policy parameters and a unified gradient estimation algorithm for both single-step (static) and sequential (dynamic) decision settings.  

The paper is well-structured and accessible, with a clear exposition that facilitates understanding even for readers unfamiliar with risk-sensitive optimization. The primary contribution—unifying the analysis of a general class of risk measures encompassing most well-known examples—constitutes a significant theoretical advance. This framework provides a cohesive foundation for risk-sensitive decision-making, and the results appear compelling. However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed gradient algorithm to substantiate its practical utility.  

**Specific comments:**  
1. **Preliminaries clarity:** The roles of $P_{\theta}$ (policy parameterization) and $\xi$ (random variable) require explicit definition in Section 2. Current notation causes confusion regarding their interplay in the risk formulation.  
2. **Equation 2 vs. Theorem 2.1:** Equation 2 appears to directly state the convex envelope definition. The paper claims it is "more restrictive" than Theorem 2.1; this requires justification, as the convex envelope definition typically establishes the dual representation without additional constraints.  
3. **Continuous distributions:** The analysis currently focuses on discrete settings. A discussion of generalization to continuous distributions (e.g., via measure-theoretic extensions or practical approximations) would strengthen the framework’s applicability.  
4. **Smoothness in Theorem 4.2:** The role of constraint function smoothness in the proof of Theorem 4.2 merits explicit mention. This detail is critical for understanding the technical conditions underpinning the dynamic case.  

The theoretical foundation is robust, with careful attention to the coherence properties of risk measures. The Lipschitz assumption, while necessary for the gradient analysis, should be contextualized with respect to its practical feasibility in real-world risk-averse settings. The proofs appear sound, though verification of technical steps (particularly in Theorems 3.1 and 4.2) is recommended.  

In summary, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances risk-sensitive optimization through a unifying lens. The work’s generality and rigor are its greatest strengths. Addressing the clarifications above—especially the empirical validation and notation precision—would elevate the paper to an excellent NIPS contribution. The results are promising, and the framework offers a valuable tool for future risk-averse reinforcement learning research.
