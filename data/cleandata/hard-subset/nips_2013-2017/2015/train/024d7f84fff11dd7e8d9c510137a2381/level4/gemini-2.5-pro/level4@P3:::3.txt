### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel and unified framework for developing policy gradient algorithms for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The key contribution is the extension of policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, a widely accepted standard for risk assessment in fields like finance and operations research. The authors address two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk measure of the total discounted cost, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measure.

For the static case, the authors derive a new formula for the policy gradient by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures and applying the envelope theorem. This leads to a general, two-step gradient estimation algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. For the dynamic case, they derive a new policy gradient theorem, analogous to the standard one for expected returns, which relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. This result forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm. The paper successfully generalizes and unifies several previous works at NIPS and elsewhere that have focused on specific risk measures such as variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivations, particularly the application of the envelope theorem to the dual formulation of coherent risk in Theorem 4.2, are elegant and appear sound. The theoretical results are well-supported and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. The inclusion of a consistency proof (Proposition 4.4) for the general gradient estimator strengthens the paper's theoretical contribution. The work is a complete piece of research, presenting theoretical derivations, corresponding algorithms, and an illustrative example.

A minor weakness is that the numerical illustration, while effective at making its conceptual point, is not a comprehensive empirical evaluation. It successfully demonstrates the importance of flexibility in choosing a risk measure but does not benchmark the performance (e.g., sample efficiency, convergence speed, computational overhead) of the proposed algorithms against prior, more specialized methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The authors provide the necessary background on coherent risk measures, making the work accessible to a broader RL audience. The progression from the static risk case to the more complex dynamic risk case is natural and builds intuition effectively.

One point that could be clarified for readers less familiar with this specific subfield is the source of intractability. In Section 3, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the problem's difficulty (and thus the need for sampling-based policy gradient methods) stems from the large state spaces and/or complex policy parameterizations typical in RL, rather than from the use of coherent risk measures themselves. In a tabular planning setting, MDPs with Markov coherent risk are often tractable.

**Originality:** The work is original and makes a significant conceptual advance. While it builds upon the well-established policy gradient framework and recent work on risk-sensitive RL, the generalization to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a novel and important contribution. Previous works have provided bespoke solutions for individual risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). This paper provides a single, principled framework from which many of these previous results can be re-derived, often with simpler proofs. This unification represents a clear and original step forward for the field.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant. By providing a general-purpose tool for optimizing any coherent risk measure, the authors empower researchers and practitioners to design risk-sensitive agents tailored to specific problem domains, rather than being confined to a few standard measures. The numerical example powerfully illustrates why this flexibility is crucial: standard measures like variance can lead to counter-intuitive behavior (e.g., averting high returns) when dealing with heavy-tailed distributions, a common scenario in finance. This work provides the tools to address such issues in a principled manner. The unified theoretical perspective is also likely to simplify and guide future research in risk-sensitive RL.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Provides a novel and highly general framework for policy gradient optimization over the entire class of coherent risk measures, unifying and extending a significant body of prior work.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically sound, with elegant theoretical derivations and clear, well-written exposition.
*   **Pro:** The work has high potential significance, enabling more nuanced and problem-specific approaches to risk-sensitive decision-making in complex environments.
*   **Pro:** The framework simplifies the derivation of policy gradients for specific risk measures, as demonstrated for CVaR and mean-semideviation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The contribution could be viewed as an incremental, though non-trivial, extension of the standard policy gradient theorem to a new objective class.
*   **Con:** The experimental validation is limited to a simple illustrative example and lacks a quantitative performance comparison with existing methods on benchmark tasks.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a solid and important contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The generalization of policy gradient methods to all coherent risk measures is a valuable achievement that provides both theoretical insight and practical tools. The clarity of the presentation is commendable. Despite the limited empirical evaluation, the theoretical strength and potential impact of the work are substantial. I strongly recommend acceptance.

*   There is a typo on Line 141: "risk enevlop[e]".
*   As mentioned under "Clarity," I suggest adding a sentence in Section 3 to clarify that the intractability you are addressing is due to the scale of the MDP/policy class, not the risk measure itself, to avoid potential confusion.
