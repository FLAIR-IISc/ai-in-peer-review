The authors address the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. They consider a general class of risk measures defined by standard coherence properties – convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. Their approach leverages a dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions, where the risk of a random variable (such as a cost) is defined as the maximum expected value within this hull. The paper introduces an assumption bounding the maximum derivatives of the convex hull’s boundaries (i.e., assuming M-Lipschitz continuity of the hull’s borders). Under this assumption, the authors analytically derive optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure, and present a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (one-step decision) and dynamic (sequential) settings.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. Despite my limited background in risk-sensitive optimization, I was able to follow the majority of the explanations. The primary contribution of this work is a unified analysis of a broad class of risk measures encompassing many well-known examples, which I believe represents a significant advance. However, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm would be beneficial.

Here are some specific comments and questions:

1. The preliminaries section could be clarified. I found the roles of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ unclear.
2. Is Equation 2 simply the definition of a convex envelope? If so, why is this considered more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?
3. It would be helpful to include a discussion of how the analysis generalizes to distributions over continuous variables.
4. In the short paper, mentioning the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in the proof of Theorem 4.2 would be valuable.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that offers a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear promising, though the proofs require verification.
