This paper addresses risk-sensitive Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), extending policy gradient methods to the broader class of coherent risk measures, encompassing both static and dynamically consistent formulations. The core contribution lies in deriving gradient formulas for these risk measures, generalizing previous work focused primarily on Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The approach leverages the duality representation of coherent risk, connecting it to stochastic programming and enabling a sampling-based gradient estimation technique.

The paper builds upon and generalizes established results in risk-sensitive RL. While the extension to the full class of coherent risk measures is incremental, it provides valuable flexibility and a unifying framework. The derived gradient formulas are presented clearly and are a natural extension of standard policy gradient methods used in risk-neutral settings. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers in risk management and reinforcement learning.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers familiar with reinforcement learning and optimization. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the presentation is logically structured. The authors provide sufficient detail to understand the core concepts and proposed methodology. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** Extending risk-sensitive RL to the entire class of coherent risk measures expands the applicability of these techniques.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The gradient formulas are derived rigorously and build upon established results in convex optimization and stochastic programming.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, facilitating understanding of the key ideas.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors effectively position their contribution within the existing literature on risk-sensitive RL and coherent risk measures.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While valuable, the generalization to all coherent risk measures is not a radical departure from existing work. The core idea of extending policy gradients is intuitive.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The paper acknowledges the computational challenges associated with dynamic programming and large state spaces, relying on function approximation. A more detailed discussion of the practical limitations and scalability of the proposed approach would be beneficial.
*   **Implementation Details:** The actor-critic algorithm is briefly mentioned but lacks detailed implementation specifics, particularly regarding the function approximation techniques and sampling procedures.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound extension of existing risk-sensitive RL methods.
*   The derived gradient formulas provide a valuable tool for researchers working with coherent risk measures.
*   The clear writing and organization enhance the accessibility of the work.
*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in reinforcement learning and decision-making under uncertainty.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is somewhat incremental, and the extension to all coherent risk measures may not be transformative.
*   The scalability limitations of the approach are not fully addressed.
*   The lack of detailed implementation information hinders reproducibility and practical application.

**Specific Comments:**

*   Theorem 2.1: The notation ξPθ is potentially confusing. Referring to Theorem 6.6 in [26] and adopting the corresponding notation would improve clarity.
*   Line 141: "risk enevlop[e]" - Typo.
*   Section 3: It could be helpful to emphasize that the intractability of the problem arises primarily from the policy parametrization, rather than from the use of Markov coherent risk measures themselves. MDPs with Markov risk measures are inherently tractable.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid and valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While the contribution is incremental, the generalization to coherent risk measures, the derivation of gradient formulas, and the clear presentation justify acceptance. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a relevant problem. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the aforementioned comments.
