This paper introduces a unified framework for deriving policy gradients for the class of static coherent risk measures, which yields analytical expressions for several common risk measures. For cases where analytical solutions are unavailable, the authors propose a general sampling-based algorithm and provide consistency guarantees for its policy gradient estimates. Finally, the paper extends these results to derive policy gradient expressions for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and Quality:**
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is a significant and novel contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive decision-making. A key limitation, however, is that analytical solutions for the required Lagrangian saddle-points are available for only a subset of these measures.

The proposed sample-based approximation scheme is designed to overcome this limitation by enabling the application of these results to general coherent risk measures without requiring analytical saddle-point solutions. However, the paper provides no empirical evidence to demonstrate the practical viability of this estimation scheme. An experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical policy gradients on relevant problems (e.g., for CVaR) would be extremely helpful and would greatly strengthen the paper.

**Originality:**
To the best of my knowledge, the derivation of policy gradients in this general form for coherent risk measures is novel.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written. However, its clarity is significantly hampered by the attempt to address too many topics within the eight-page limit. The treatment of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 is particularly affected; it is presented at a very high level, lacking the algorithmic details and experimental evaluation necessary for a reader to assess the practical viability of the derived gradients.

This content seems better suited for a longer journal publication. For this conference submission, a more effective approach would be to focus exclusively on static risk measures. This would make the paper more self-contained and provide space for greater detail and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation that moves beyond the basic toy example currently presented.

In summary, the paper presents interesting ideas with potentially broad applicability, but the lack of thorough experimental evaluation makes it difficult to judge the practical impact of the contributions. A narrower focus would improve clarity and allow for the inclusion of the necessary empirical support.
