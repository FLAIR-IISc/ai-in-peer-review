### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a general framework for applying policy gradient methods to reinforcement learning problems with risk-sensitive objectives. The key contribution is the extension of these methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, moving beyond prior work that focused on specific measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) or variance. The authors tackle two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted cost, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

For the static case, the paper leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities. By applying the Envelope Theorem to this formulation, the authors derive a novel, general formula for the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2). This leads to a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient.

For the dynamic case, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This theorem connects the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm. The work successfully unifies and generalizes several previous results, providing a single, principled approach for a wide variety of risk-sensitive optimization problems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generalization and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant generalization. By developing a policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures, the authors provide a unified theoretical foundation and a set of practical algorithms that subsume and extend many previous ad-hoc approaches. This is a valuable and important contribution to the risk-sensitive reinforcement learning community.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound and elegant. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk in combination with the Envelope Theorem to derive the gradient is a clever and effective approach. The resulting gradient formulas are insightful and provide a clear path to estimation. The paper appears technically correct.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors provide the necessary background on coherent risk measures, making the work accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with the topic. The progression from the static to the dynamic case is logical and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Nature of Contribution:** While the generalization is valuable, the contribution can be viewed as a skillful synthesis and extension of existing techniques rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new method. The core components—the dual representation of risk, the Envelope Theorem, and likelihood-ratio policy gradients—are all well-established. The novelty lies in their combination to address this broader class of risk measures.

2.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration in Section 6, while effective at highlighting the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure, is quite simple (a contextual bandit problem). It does not sufficiently validate the performance or sample complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly the actor-critic method for the dynamic risk problem. A more thorough empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmark problems would strengthen the paper by demonstrating the practical effectiveness and scalability of the proposed methods compared to existing risk-specific algorithms.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Theorem 2.1:** The notation `ξP_θ` for the re-weighted probability measure is slightly unconventional and could be confusing. It would improve clarity to either define it more explicitly or, as the authors cite [26], to align the notation with that of the source material (e.g., Theorem 6.6 in [26]), which would aid readers familiar with that work.

*   **Section 3 (Problem Formulation):** To avoid potential confusion for readers new to this specific area, it would be helpful to explicitly clarify that the intractability being addressed arises from the policy parametrization and potentially large state spaces. MDPs with dynamic Markov risk measures are, in principle, tractable and can be solved with dynamic programming in tabular cases [24]. The challenge this paper tackles is scaling these solutions to complex problems using function approximation, which is an important distinction to make upfront.

*   **Typo:** There is a minor typo on line 141: "risk enevlop[e]".

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed extension of policy gradient methods to the general class of coherent risk measures. The theoretical contributions are sound, and the resulting framework unifies a significant body of prior work in risk-sensitive RL. While the work is more of a generalization than a fundamentally new paradigm, it is an important and useful contribution that advances the field. The paper is well-written and the results are clearly presented. Stronger empirical validation would further enhance its impact, but the theoretical contribution alone makes it a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
