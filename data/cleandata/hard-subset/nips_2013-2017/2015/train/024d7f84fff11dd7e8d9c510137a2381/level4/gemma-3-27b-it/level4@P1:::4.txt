The authors consider the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given risk measure. The class of risk measures considered is general, defined by coherence properties such as convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The presented approach is based on a dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions. The risk of a random variable (such as a cost) is given by the maximum expected value within this hull. The paper makes an assumption on the family of coherent risk measures, requiring bounded derivatives of the convex hull’s boundaries (i.e., the boundaries are M-Lipschitz continuous). Using this assumption, the authors derive analytical expressions for the policy gradient and a general gradient estimation algorithm for both the static (one-step decision) and dynamic (sequential) cases.

The paper is well-written and organized. Despite my limited familiarity with risk-sensitive optimization, I was able to follow most of the explanations. The main contribution of this work is a unified analysis of a general class of risk measures encompassing many well-known measures. This is a significant contribution. However, a more in-depth empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm would be valuable.

Here are some comments and questions:

1. The preliminaries section could be clearer. I found the roles of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ somewhat confusing.
2. Isn’t Equation 2 simply the definition of a convex envelope? If so, why is this considered more restrictive than Theorem 2.1?
3. It would be helpful to comment on how the analysis generalizes to continuous variables.
4. Briefly mentioning the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in proving Theorem 4.2 would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper providing a general approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results seem compelling, though the proofs warrant careful verification.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Generality:** The paper provides a unifying framework for a broad class of coherent risk measures.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas appears rigorous and builds upon established results in convex optimization and reinforcement learning.
*   **Potential Impact:** A general framework for risk-sensitive RL could have a significant impact on applications in finance, robotics, and control.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration is limited, and more extensive experiments are needed to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Technical Details:** The paper contains some technical details that may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the area. Greater clarity in the preliminaries section would be helpful.
