The paper proposes a new formula for the class of static coherent risks, from which policy gradients for many common risk measures can be derived analytically. It also presents a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where no analytical solution is available and shows consistency guarantees of the policy gradient estimates. Additionally, the paper provides expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

Significance and quality:
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is an intriguing and valuable contribution to the risk-sensitive decision-making literature. However, it is worth noting that analytical solutions of the Lagrangian saddle-points are only available for some risk measures. The proposed sample-based approximation scheme could potentially allow us to apply the derived results to general coherent risk measures by avoiding the need to derive analytical forms of the saddle points. Nevertheless, the paper does not provide any empirical evidence that the proposed estimation scheme is viable in practice. An experimental comparison between using sample-based policy gradients and analytical ones in different problems, for instance, for Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), would be extremely helpful in assessing the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Originality:
To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in their general form, are novel.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is hindered by attempting to address multiple issues within the eight-page limit. Specifically, the dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 are presented at a very high level, and the lack of algorithmic details and experimental evaluations makes it difficult for the reader to judge whether the derived policy gradients yield practical approaches. The content seems more suitable for a longer journal publication. Alternatively, focusing solely on static risk measures could make the paper more standalone, allowing for more detailed explanations and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation beyond the basic toy example in the current submission. The paper has potentially generally applicable results, but the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluations makes it challenging to assess its value. A narrower focus might be more appropriate to address the clarity issues arising from space constraints.
