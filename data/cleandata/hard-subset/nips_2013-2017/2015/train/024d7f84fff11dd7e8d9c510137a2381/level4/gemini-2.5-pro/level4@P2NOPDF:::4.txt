This paper addresses the problem of finding policy parameters that minimize a given coherent risk measure. The authors focus on a general class of risk measures defined by standard coherence properties: convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The proposed approach leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures, where the risk of a random cost is defined as the maximum expected value over a convex set of probability distributions.

A key assumption introduced in the paper is that the functions defining the boundary of this convex set are M-Lipschitz continuous. This assumption enables the authors to analytically derive the optimal policy parameters with respect to the risk measure. Furthermore, they develop a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both static (single-step) and dynamic (sequential) decision-making problems.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents a significant theoretical contribution. Its primary strength is the unified analysis of a broad class of risk measures, which encompasses many well-known examples. This general framework for risk-sensitive optimization is a valuable contribution to the field. While the theoretical results are compelling, the work would be further strengthened by a more comprehensive empirical analysis of the proposed gradient algorithm to demonstrate its practical performance.

**Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Clarity of Preliminaries:** The preliminaries section could be improved for clarity. Specifically, the relationship and distinct roles of the policy distribution, $P_{\theta}$, and the random outcome, $\xi$, were not immediately clear and could be explained more explicitly.

2.  **Equation 2 and Convexity:** Regarding Equation 2, it appears to be the definition of a convex envelope. If this is the case, it would be helpful to state it explicitly. Furthermore, could the authors clarify how this representation relates to the more general one in Theorem 2.1 and whether it imposes any additional restrictions?

3.  **Generalization to Continuous Variables:** The current analysis appears to focus on distributions over finite sets. I would suggest adding a brief comment on how the theoretical results might generalize to scenarios involving continuous random variables.

4.  **Assumption for Theorem 4.2:** The proof of Theorem 4.2 relies on the smoothness of certain constraint functions. To provide a more complete understanding of the theoretical underpinnings, it would be beneficial to mention the importance of this smoothness assumption in the main body of the paper, rather than confining this detail solely to the appendix.

In summary, this is a strong theory paper that provides a general and principled approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results are compelling, though a thorough verification of the proofs is necessary.
