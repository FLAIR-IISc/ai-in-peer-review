Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that handle the entire class of coherent risk measures, both in the static setting and in the time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) setting. For static coherent risk, the authors derive a gradient expression using the dual (risk-envelope) representation and the envelope theorem, leading to a sampling-based estimator that combines likelihood ratios with a convex program over the risk envelope. They further propose a sample average approximation (SAA) scheme and prove consistency of the resulting gradient estimator. For dynamic risk, they present a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that relates the gradient to a value function under ξ-weighted transitions, yielding an actor–critic style method. The approach recovers several known special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and unifies them under a single framework. The connection to robust MDPs is used to inform the critic.

Relative to prior work, this generalizes results that focused on specific risks such as CVaR [23, 8, 30] or mean-variance [18, 29, 21], and connects to dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs [24, 19, 31]. The core technical ideas—likelihood-ratio gradients and dual representations—are natural extensions of standard policy gradients [28, 14] to coherent risk.

Strengths:
- Unified treatment: A single, coherent framework covers many static and dynamic risk measures, clarifying how prior CVaR- and variance-based methods fit as special cases.
- Clear gradient formulas: The static gradient via the Lagrangian saddle point and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem are clean and sampling-friendly.
- Consistency: The SAA-based estimator for general static coherent risk includes a consistency result under mild conditions.
- Dynamic setting: The actor–critic approach with ξ-weighted transitions and the robust-MDP connection is conceptually appealing.
- Writing and organization: The paper is well-structured and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:
- Incremental novelty: Conceptually, the main results are natural extensions of risk-neutral policy gradients using the dual form of coherent risk; the contribution is primarily in generalization and unification rather than a new algorithmic paradigm.
- Practical burden: The static estimator requires solving a convex program per batch/sample set, and the dynamic method needs a simulator capable of multiple next-state samples per state; computational and sample complexity are not discussed.
- Assumptions: Finite probability spaces, availability of likelihood ratios, and smooth, explicit risk envelopes may be restrictive in some applications.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are limited to a simple static trading example; there is no dynamic-risk empirical validation and minimal comparison to strong baselines.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears sound. The gradient identities, Lagrangian formulation, and consistency arguments are reasonable under the stated assumptions. The connection to robust MDPs is correctly leveraged for the critic. I did not find technical errors.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well written. A few notational and expository tweaks (see minor comments) would further improve readability.

Originality:
The work primarily generalizes known ideas (policy gradients, dual representation of coherent risk, actor–critic) to a broad class of risk measures, with new gradient formulas that unify and simplify prior special cases. The degree of novelty is moderate.

Significance:
As a unified framework, this is a useful contribution for researchers and practitioners who wish to plug in different coherent risk measures without redesigning algorithms. While the conceptual step is incremental, the generality and coherence of the treatment make it practically valuable.

Suggestions and minor comments:
- Theorem 2.1/notation: The ξPθ notation is confusing. Consider aligning notation with Theorem 6.6 in [26], and make explicit that ξ reweights Pθ within the risk envelope U(Pθ).
- Typo at line 141: “risk enevlop[e]” → “risk envelope.”
- Section 3 (scope/tractability): It would help to state explicitly that MDPs with Markov coherent risk are tractable via dynamic programming; the primary source of intractability here stems from policy parameterization and the need for sampling-based gradients, not from the risk measure per se.
- Assumptions and computation: Discuss the computational cost of solving the convex program in the static estimator and the need for multiple next-state samples in the dynamic actor–critic. Some guidance on practical settings (e.g., CVaR, spectral risks) and complexity scaling would be valuable.
- Empirics: Consider adding a dynamic-risk experiment and comparisons to robust MDP baselines and risk-averse actor–critic methods (e.g., [21], [31]). Reporting variance and convergence behavior of the SAA estimator vs. analytic special-case estimators would strengthen the case.
- Notation hygiene: Ensure consistent use of indices and clarify dependence on θ in the risk envelope constraints; briefly remind the reader when Pθ(·|x) replaces Pθ in the dynamic case.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a clear, unified gradient framework for coherent risk in both static and dynamic settings.
- Includes consistency guarantees and connects to robust MDPs, enabling function approximation.
- Well written and likely to be useful to the community.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Conceptual novelty is limited; methods are natural extensions of known policy-gradient techniques.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal and lacks dynamic-risk validation.
- Practical requirements (convex programming per iteration, simulator access) may limit applicability, and computational trade-offs are not analyzed.

Overall: A minor but solid and useful generalization that improves clarity and accessibility of risk-sensitive policy gradients for coherent risks.
