This paper develops policy gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures, covering both static and time-consistent dynamic settings. The key technical contributions are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risks using an envelope-theorem argument on the dual representation of coherent risk (risk envelope), which yields likelihood-ratio estimators coupled with convex programming; (ii) a sample-average approximation (SAA) based estimator with a consistency result; (iii) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk that expresses the gradient in terms of a value function under ξ-weighted transitions, and an actor–critic scheme that leverages robust-MDP connections for function approximation. The paper shows how well-known special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) fall out as corollaries of the general framework. A toy financial example illustrates how different risk criteria lead to distinct policies.

Relation to prior work: The paper unifies earlier risk-sensitive RL approaches that targeted specific risk measures, such as mean–variance, exponential utility, and CVaR (e.g., works by Tamar, Rockafellar–Uryasev, Prashanth–Ghavamzadeh, Borkar). For dynamic risk, it builds on Markov coherent measures and their equivalence to robust MDPs (Ruszczyński–Shapiro; Chow–Pavone; Petrik–Subramanian), extending policy-gradient results to a broader class. This situates the contribution clearly within and beyond recent NIPS literature on CVaR optimization and robust RL.

Strengths:
- A unified, principled treatment of coherent risk in RL, subsuming several existing methods as special cases.
- Clean gradient identities for static risk and a new policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk.
- Practical estimators that combine sampling with convex optimization; consistency for the SAA-based estimator.
- Clear exposition, good connections to convex-analytic duality and robust MDPs, and useful worked examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation).

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited to a simple static, single-step portfolio example; the dynamic actor–critic method lacks experimental validation.
- Computational burden may be nontrivial: solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers per batch/sample set, and simulating ξ-weighted transitions. The paper does not quantify runtime, sample complexity, or variance properties.
- Assumptions may limit applicability: known explicit risk envelope with smooth constraints; finite probability spaces; bounded likelihood ratios; simulator access enabling multiple next-state samples and modified dynamics; Slater condition. Discussion of how these extend to continuous spaces and data-driven envelope estimation is brief.
- Some important algorithmic details (critic approximation, two-phase sampling, convergence guarantees) and ablations are deferred to the supplement, making it hard to assess end-to-end practicality.
- Limited comparison to strong baselines beyond recovering known special cases; no head-to-head with CVaR-specific policy-gradient or robust RL planners on dynamic tasks.

Quality: The technical development appears sound, with correct use of convex duality, envelope theorems, and likelihood-ratio methods. The static estimator’s consistency under reasonable assumptions is a plus. The dynamic gradient theorem is carefully stated. Proof details are in the supplement.

Clarity: Well organized and clearly written. The risk-envelope formulation and its role in the gradient are explained lucidly. Minor suggestions: summarize computational complexity (per-iteration costs, constraint scaling), and move some essential algorithmic steps from the supplement into the main text.

Originality: Extending policy gradients to the general class of coherent risks and deriving a dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is a meaningful advance beyond prior work focused on specific measures or particular dynamics. The unification aspect is nontrivial.

Significance: Risk-sensitive RL is important in safety-critical control, finance, and operations. A general, coherent-risk framework could influence how risk is modeled and optimized in RL. The impact will be stronger with empirical demonstrations on dynamic benchmarks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide experiments for the dynamic actor–critic method on standard RL tasks (e.g., gridworlds with rare-event costs, queueing control, dynamic portfolio with switching costs), including comparisons to CVaR-PG and robust MDP baselines.
- Report computational overhead (solve times for the convex subproblems, sample counts), gradient variance, and sample efficiency; include ablations comparing analytic vs SAA gradients for CVaR.
- Discuss extensions to continuous state/action spaces and approximate envelopes; address robustness to misspecified envelopes and data-driven estimation.
- Consider variance-reduction and importance sampling for tail-sensitive risks; clarify when single-trajectory rollouts suffice.
- State conditions ensuring existence/uniqueness of saddle points and how violations affect estimators.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, general theoretical framework that unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive RL results.
- Clear, technically sound derivations and consistency guarantees.
- Potentially broad impact across multiple risk measures and applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation, especially for the dynamic component.
- Computational and practical aspects not thoroughly characterized.
- Reliance on assumptions (explicit, smooth envelopes; simulator with controllable transitions) that may restrict immediate applicability.

Overall recommendation: Leaning toward acceptance for the theoretical contribution and unifying perspective. Strengthening the empirical section with dynamic experiments and practical performance analysis would make the case more compelling.
