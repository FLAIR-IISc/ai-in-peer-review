

The paper introduces a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, extending prior work on specific risk metrics (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance) to the entire class of coherent risk measures. It develops two key contributions: (1) a sampling-based gradient estimator for static coherent risk using convex programming, with consistency guarantees, and (2) a dynamic programming-based policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, enabling actor-critic style algorithms for dynamic settings. The theoretical results generalize prior approaches and provide a coherent foundation for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.

**Significance and Quality:**  
The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures represents a significant theoretical advance, offering a principled extension of risk-sensitive RL beyond ad hoc methods. While analytical saddle-point solutions are only available for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), the proposed sampling-based approximation scheme avoids this limitation. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed estimation scheme’s practical viability. An experimental comparison between analytical and sample-based gradient estimators (e.g., for CVaR in non-trivial domains) would substantially strengthen the claims. The current toy example (Section 6) is insufficient to assess scalability or real-world applicability, particularly for dynamic risk where the actor-critic algorithm’s complexity is not demonstrated.

**Originality:**  
The derivation of policy gradients for *general* coherent risk measures—unifying prior results under a single framework—is novel. The paper explicitly shows how existing methods (e.g., for CVaR [8, 30], mean-semideviation [26]) emerge as special cases, confirming the theoretical novelty.

**Clarity:**  
The paper is technically sound but suffers from significant clarity limitations due to its ambitious scope. Covering both static and dynamic risk in eight pages forces excessive abstraction in the dynamic section (Section 5), where algorithmic details, convergence analysis, and empirical validation are omitted. The dynamic risk formulation (Theorem 5.2) and actor-critic procedure are presented at a high level, making it difficult to assess practical feasibility. For instance, the two-phase sampling procedure for gradient estimation is described only in passing, with no discussion of computational costs or implementation challenges. Given the complexity, the paper would benefit from a narrower focus—such as prioritizing static risk measures (which have a more complete theoretical and empirical foundation)—to allow deeper treatment of the sampling-based estimator, rigorous experiments, and clearer exposition. The current scope appears better suited for a journal publication.

**Recommendation:**  
The theoretical contribution is strong and original, but the lack of empirical validation and excessive scope compromise the paper’s readiness for NIPS. The work would be significantly improved by: (1) removing the dynamic risk section to focus on static risk, enabling a comprehensive empirical evaluation (e.g., comparing analytical vs. sample-based gradients across risk measures), and (2) providing concrete implementation details for the sampling-based estimator. As submitted, the paper’s claims about practical utility remain unverified, and the dynamic risk component lacks sufficient detail to assess its novelty or applicability. A revised version with this narrower focus and empirical validation would be a compelling contribution. **Weak reject** due to insufficient evidence of practical viability and clarity limitations from overambition.
