This paper addresses the challenge of optimizing policy parameters to minimize a specified risk measure. The authors work with a broad class of coherent risk measures, characterized by fundamental properties like convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance. The proposed methodology leverages the dual representation of these measures, which defines them as a convex hull of probability distributions. Consequently, the risk associated with a random cost is the maximum expected value over this set of distributions. A central assumption is made on this family of risk measures: the boundaries of the convex hull are constrained to be M-Lipschitz continuous. Based on this assumption, the paper presents an analytical characterization of the optimal policy parameters for the given risk measure. Furthermore, it introduces a general gradient estimation algorithm applicable to both single-step (static) and sequential (dynamic) decision-making problems.

The manuscript is well-structured and clearly written. Despite my limited background in risk-sensitive optimization, I found the explanations largely accessible. The principal contribution of this work is the development of a unified analysis for a general class of risk measures, encompassing most commonly used ones. I consider this to be a significant theoretical advance. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive empirical investigation of the proposed gradient algorithm.

Specific comments and questions:
1.  The preliminaries section could be clearer. I found the respective roles of the policy-induced distribution, P_{\theta}, and the random variable, \ksi, to be somewhat ambiguous.
2.  Regarding Equation 2, it appears to be the standard definition of a convex envelope. Could the authors elaborate on why this is considered more restrictive than the statement in Theorem 2.1?
3.  I recommend including a brief discussion on how the presented analysis can be extended to handle distributions over continuous variables.
4.  It would be beneficial to highlight the role of the smoothness of the constraint functions in the proof of Theorem 4.2 within the main text.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that offers a general and principled approach to risk-sensitive decision-making. The results appear compelling, although the proofs require careful verification.
