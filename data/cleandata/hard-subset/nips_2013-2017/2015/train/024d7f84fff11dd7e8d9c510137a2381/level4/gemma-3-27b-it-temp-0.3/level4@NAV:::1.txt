The paper introduces a novel formula for static coherent risks, enabling analytical derivation of policy gradients for a wide range of common risk measures. It further proposes a general sampling-based algorithm to estimate policy gradients when analytical solutions are unavailable, accompanied by consistency guarantees. Finally, the work extends these results to provide policy gradient expressions for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

**Significance and quality:** The unified approach to deriving policy gradients for coherent risk measures represents a significant and original contribution to the field of risk-sensitive decision making. While analytical solutions are currently limited to specific risk measures, the proposed sampling-based approximation offers a pathway to apply these results to a broader class of coherent risk measures without requiring explicit saddle-point solutions. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed estimation scheme’s practical viability. An experimental comparison of the sample-based and analytical policy gradients – perhaps demonstrated with CVaR on representative problems – would substantially strengthen the work.

**Originality:** To the best of my knowledge, the derived policy gradients, in their presented generality, are novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is somewhat compromised by the breadth of topics covered within the page limit. Specifically, the discussion of dynamic risk policy gradients in Section 5 remains at a high level of abstraction. The absence of algorithmic details and experimental results hinders a proper assessment of the practical utility of these gradients. This material might be better suited for a more extensive journal publication. Alternatively, focusing the paper on static risk measures would allow for a more self-contained presentation, greater detail, and a more thorough empirical evaluation beyond the current basic example. This is a promising paper with potentially broad applicability, but the limited experimental evaluation makes it difficult to fully assess. A more focused scope could also improve clarity given the existing space constraints.
