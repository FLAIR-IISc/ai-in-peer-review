

This paper investigates risk-averse Markov decision processes (MDPs) under both static and dynamically consistent risk measures. The primary contribution lies in deriving the gradient forms for policy optimization in both settings, generalizing prior work that focused exclusively on CVaR. While the extension from CVaR to general risk measures is technically straightforward, it represents a minor yet useful contribution that broadens the applicability of risk-averse policy gradient methods. The results constitute a natural extension of standard risk-neutral policy gradient techniques, with the authors providing clear derivations.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The mathematical exposition is rigorous, and the technical arguments appear correct upon review. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the connection to existing literature (particularly [26]) is well-established.

A critical clarification is needed in Section 3 regarding tractability. The current text states that "MDPs with Markov risk measures are intractable," which is misleading. In fact, MDPs employing Markov risk measures (e.g., via dynamic programming) are generally tractable, as demonstrated in foundational works like [26]. The intractability addressed in this paper arises solely from the *policy parametrization* (i.e., optimizing over a parametric policy class), not from the risk measure itself. This distinction is essential for readers to avoid confusion. The authors should explicitly state: "While MDPs with Markov risk measures are tractable under standard dynamic programming, the optimization challenge here stems from the parametric policy representation, not the risk measure."

Regarding minor issues:
- **Theorem 2.1**: The notation $\xi P_\theta$ is non-standard and potentially confusing. The authors should adopt the notation $\xi P_\theta$ used in Theorem 6.6 of [26] for consistency and clarity.
- **Line 141**: "risk enevlop" is a typo and should read "risk envelope."
- **Section 3**: The suggested clarification on tractability (as noted above) is crucial for reader comprehension.

The paper’s technical contribution is modest but valuable. It fills a small gap by formalizing the gradient for general risk measures, which is necessary for extending risk-averse reinforcement learning to broader contexts beyond CVaR. The approach is methodical and aligns with recent trends in risk-sensitive RL. However, the novelty is limited to the technical extension rather than introducing new algorithms, risk measures, or empirical insights. The work is a solid theoretical step but does not constitute a significant conceptual advance.

The writing is generally strong, but the discussion of tractability in Section 3 requires immediate revision to avoid misrepresenting the problem’s source of difficulty. Once this is addressed, the paper will be significantly clearer. The minor notational and typographical issues are easily corrected.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper that provides a minor but useful generalization of existing risk-averse policy gradient methods. The core contribution is a straightforward extension of prior CVaR-specific results, and the theoretical development is correct. The clarity of the exposition would be greatly enhanced by the suggested clarification regarding tractability. The paper meets the standards of NIPS for a minor but solid contribution in the risk-averse RL space.

**Recommendation**: Weak Accept. The work is technically correct and contributes to an important subfield, but the novelty is limited. With minor revisions (especially the tractability clarification), it would be a strong fit for NIPS.
