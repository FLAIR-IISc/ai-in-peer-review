

This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific measures like CVaR or variance as in previous work. The authors present a unified framework that generalizes and extends existing results, providing both static and dynamic risk formulations with theoretically grounded gradient estimators. Their approach builds upon the coherent risk framework established by Artzner et al. [2] and extends policy gradient methods from risk-neutral settings [15, 28] to risk-sensitive contexts.

The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that coherent risk measures can be handled through a single unified framework, rather than developing separate algorithms for each risk measure. This generalization is valuable because it enables practitioners to choose risk preferences that best suit their specific application, as demonstrated in the numerical example in Section 6 where different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to substantially different policy choices. The authors correctly note that the choice of risk measure is problem-dependent, depending on factors such as cost distribution, sensitivity to rare events, estimation difficulty, and computational tractability.

Quality: The paper provides technically sound results with well-supported theoretical analysis. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures using convex optimization and the envelope theorem, establishing consistency results for their sampling-based estimators. The proofs are sound, and the paper correctly references foundational work on coherent risk [2, 25, 26] and policy gradient methods [15, 28]. The numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical utility of their framework, showing how different risk preferences lead to different policy choices in a financial trading example.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough introduction to coherent risk measures and their relevance to RL. The main contributions are clearly stated, and the technical development follows a logical progression. The authors make good use of examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) to illustrate their general approach. However, the notation $\xi P_\theta$ (used in Theorem 2.1) is confusing and could be clarified by referencing Theorem 6.6 in [26] as suggested in the draft review. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how their approach relates to the robust MDP framework [19] and the dynamic programming approach [24] for dynamic coherent risk, particularly regarding the computational advantages.

Originality: The paper's main contribution is the unified treatment of coherent risk measures in RL, which generalizes previous work that focused on specific risk measures. While the generalization is not trivial, the authors correctly position their work as extending rather than reinventing previous approaches. The paper shows how several previous results (e.g., for CVaR [30], mean-semideviation [26]) are special cases of their framework, demonstrating the broader applicability of their approach. This unification represents a meaningful contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive RL.

Significance: The framework has clear practical significance for risk-sensitive applications, particularly in finance, robotics, and other domains where managing cost variability is critical. By providing a general method for coherent risk measures, the paper enables practitioners to select risk preferences tailored to their specific problem characteristics. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates this flexibility, showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a trading scenario. The authors also correctly note that this flexibility is important for selecting appropriate risk measures that align with both user preferences and problem-specific properties.

Strengths: The paper provides a unified theoretical framework for coherent risk measures in RL, which is a meaningful extension of previous work. The technical development is sound, with clear gradient formulas and consistency results. The numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical utility of their approach. The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it accessible to readers familiar with standard RL concepts.

Weaknesses: The paper understates the technical contribution by describing their approach as "a simple extension" of risk-neutral policy gradients. While the framework generalizes previous results, the derivation of the gradient formulas for coherent risk measures represents a non-trivial technical contribution. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with the robust MDP approach [19] and the dynamic programming method [24] for dynamic coherent risk, particularly regarding computational advantages.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for coherent risk measures represents a meaningful extension of previous work, with clear theoretical foundations and practical utility. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and would be of interest to researchers working on risk-sensitive decision making. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify notation and strengthen the comparison with related work.
