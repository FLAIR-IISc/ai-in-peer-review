This paper addresses the problem of policy gradient optimization for coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The authors propose a unified approach that generalizes previous work focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR, extending it to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and time-consistent dynamic. The core contribution lies in formulating a risk-sensitive policy gradient and providing algorithms leveraging sampling and convex programming (for static risk) and actor-critic methods (for dynamic risk).

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the need for risk-sensitive RL and the benefits of utilizing the broader framework of coherent risk measures. The connection to financial applications and operations research is well-established. The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers in risk-sensitive RL and coherent risk theory. The proposed gradient formulas, particularly Theorem 4.2, are significant as they provide a general framework for deriving estimators. The discussion of the dynamic risk case, and the connection to robust MDPs, is also valuable. The numerical illustration, though limited, provides some intuition for the behavior of policies optimized with different risk criteria.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. The presentation is dense and, at times, difficult to follow. Many of the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which makes it harder to assess the validity of the technical claims without significant effort. While the authors acknowledge the curse of dimensionality in dynamic programming, the scalability of the proposed actor-critic algorithm, particularly the sampling procedure in the dynamic case, is not adequately addressed. The discussion of function approximation in the dynamic case is brief and lacks detail on how the approximation error impacts the convergence guarantees. The numerical example is very limited and doesn't provide a strong empirical validation of the proposed methods. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, perhaps on benchmark RL tasks, would significantly strengthen the paper.

Specifically, while Proposition 4.4 provides conditions for consistency of the SAA estimator, the practicality of verifying these conditions in real-world applications is questionable. It would be beneficial to discuss more practical considerations for choosing the sample size *N* to achieve a desired level of accuracy. Also, the reliance on Assumption 2.2 regarding the form of the risk envelope limits the applicability of the approach to coherent risk measures that fit that structure. A discussion of potential extensions to more general risk envelopes would be useful. Finally, the connection to existing work on robust MDPs could be more thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel and general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient.
*   Extension to the entire class of coherent risk measures.
*   Clear theoretical foundation with well-defined gradient formulas.
*   Connection to robust MDPs and dynamic programming.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Dense and difficult-to-follow presentation.
*   Limited discussion of scalability and approximation error.
*   Weak empirical validation with a small-scale numerical example.
*   Practicality of consistency conditions for SAA estimator is unclear.
*   Reliance on Assumption 2.2 limits applicability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in reinforcement learning.
*   The work generalizes existing results and provides a unifying framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient optimization.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the gradient formulas, are significant and could potentially lead to further research in this area.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The presentation is dense and may hinder understanding.
*   The lack of a strong empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
*   Scalability issues and the impact of approximation error are not adequately addressed.
*   The conditions for consistency of the SAA estimator may be difficult to verify in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is borderline acceptable. It presents a valuable theoretical contribution, but the lack of a more thorough experimental evaluation and the concerns regarding scalability and practical considerations are significant drawbacks. If the authors can address these concerns – either by providing a more compelling empirical validation, a more detailed discussion of scalability, or a clarification of the practical implications of their assumptions – I would be more inclined to recommend acceptance. As it stands, I lean towards rejection, but encourage the authors to address the reviewers' comments and resubmit a revised version.
