- Paper develops a unified policy-gradient framework for all coherent risk measures in static and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) settings.  
- For static coherent risk, derives a general gradient formula using dual representation, envelope theorem, and likelihood-ratio identities.  
- Provides closed-form policy-gradient expressions for CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases.  
- Proposes an SAA estimator that computes gradients via sampling plus a small convex program; proves consistency under mild conditions.  
- For dynamic risk, establishes a policy-gradient theorem expressing gradients as expectations under ξ-weighted transitions with a stage cost involving value and dual variables.  
- Proposes an actor-critic scheme: critic uses robust-MDP equivalence and function approximation to estimate Vθ; actor uses the new gradient theorem.  
- Demonstrates approach on a toy trading example to illustrate flexibility in choosing risk criteria.  
- Situates work relative to prior risk-sensitive RL: generalizes and unifies prior methods (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR, some dynamic coherent-risk work).  
- Connects dynamic gradient derivation to robust MDP value estimation literature.  
- Strength: generality — a single clean framework covering all coherent risks, static and dynamic.  
- Strength: clear technical contributions including static gradient via duality and envelope theorem, and new derivations simplifying CVaR/mean-semideviation results.  
- Strength: dynamic policy-gradient theorem extends classic results to Markov coherent risk.  
- Strength: practical pathway via tractable convex programs for many popular coherent risks.  
- Strength: clear problem formulation, assumptions, and well-situated related work.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is very limited; no dynamic MDP benchmarks or comparisons to baselines for control tasks.  
- Weakness: computational considerations underdeveloped — per-iteration convex program costs and ξ-weighted sampling overhead not analyzed or timed.  
- Weakness: assumptions requiring explicit convex envelope descriptions and differentiability may be restrictive for black-box risk functionals.  
- Weakness: dynamic method assumes a generative model and multiple next-state samples; on-policy single-trajectory case is not addressed.  
- Clarity issue: actor-critic algorithm is sketched without full pseudocode, estimator definitions, stepsizes, or function approximation details, hurting reproducibility.  
- Clarity issue: notation in dynamic theorem is dense; worked examples for common risks would aid readability.  
- Originality: unification and dynamic policy-gradient theorem are novel and extend beyond prior risk-specific work; SAA gradient consistency is a useful methodological addition.  
- Significance: potentially impactful toolkit for risk-sensitive RL and robust control, but limited empirical validation and computational discussion weaken immediate practical impact.  
- Essential suggestions: add at least one dynamic MDP experiment with baselines, report performance/variance/runtime.  
- Essential suggestions: provide complexity analysis for static estimator and dynamic algorithm overhead.  
- Essential suggestions: include explicit envelopes and derivative expressions for CVaR, mean-semideviation, and spectral risks.  
- Nice-to-have: discuss or prototype importance sampling for tail risks and extend discussion to infinite/continuous spaces and regularity conditions.  
- Nice-to-have: clarify generative model requirement and outline off-policy or single-trajectory variants.  
- Pros for acceptance: unified, technically sound framework; new dynamic theorem; SAA estimator with consistency; principled critic via robust MDP links.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited empirical validation, unclear scalability, reliance on generative model, and missing algorithmic details hindering reproducibility.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with high novelty; needs stronger experiments and computational guidance to improve practical impact.
