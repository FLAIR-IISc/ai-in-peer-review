**Summary**

This paper introduces a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning settings. This work generalizes previous approaches that have developed algorithms for specific risk measures, such as variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), by extending the methodology to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The core technical contribution is based on the dual representation of coherent risk, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability distributions known as a risk envelope.

The paper presents two main results. First, for static coherent risk measures, a novel formula for the policy gradient is derived using the envelope theorem. This leads to a general estimation algorithm that combines sampling with solving a convex optimization problem to find the worst-case distribution from the risk envelope. The utility of this general formula is demonstrated by re-deriving the gradient for CVaR with a simpler proof and deriving a new gradient formula for the mean-semideviation measure. Second, for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk, the work develops a new policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one for expected returns. This theorem forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm for solving dynamic risk problems in large-scale Markov Decision Processes (MDPs).

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-supported piece of work. The theoretical development is rigorous, leveraging established results from convex optimization and stochastic programming (e.g., the dual representation of coherent risk, the envelope theorem) and applying them effectively to the policy gradient setting. The derivation of the main gradient formulas in Theorem 4.2 (static risk) and Theorem 5.2 (dynamic risk) appears correct and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms.

The consistency of the sample-based gradient estimator for the static case is established in Proposition 4.4 under a set of reasonable assumptions. The connection drawn between dynamic coherent risk, robust MDPs, and a risk-sensitive Bellman equation is well-founded and builds upon existing literature. The authors are careful to state their assumptions clearly (e.g., Assumptions 2.2, 4.1, 5.1). The primary limitation is that the full algorithmic details and convergence analysis for the more complex actor-critic method are deferred to supplementary material, making a complete assessment of that component difficult from the main text alone.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for using a general class of coherent risk measures is clearly articulated in the introduction. The logical flow from preliminaries to the static risk case and then to the more complex dynamic risk case is easy to follow. The inclusion of examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation is particularly effective, as it grounds the abstract theoretical result of Theorem 4.2 in concrete, understandable cases and demonstrates the unifying power of the proposed framework. The notation is consistent and the mathematical formulations are precise.

To further improve clarity, a more detailed algorithm block for the proposed actor-critic method could be included in the main paper, even if the analysis is left to the appendix. This would give the reader a more concrete understanding of the implementation steps involved.

**Originality**

The work is highly original and represents a significant conceptual advance. While risk-sensitive RL is an active area of research, previous work has largely focused on developing bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures. This paper is the first to provide a unified policy gradient framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The approach of combining the likelihood ratio trick with the dual representation of coherent risk is novel in this context. The resulting policy gradient theorems generalize several previous results and provide a principled method for deriving new ones. The work adequately references related literature and clearly situates its contributions with respect to prior art in risk-sensitive RL, robust MDPs, and stochastic programming.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are of high significance to the reinforcement learning and decision-making communities. By providing a general and principled framework, the work equips researchers and practitioners with a much richer toolkit for managing risk. It moves the field beyond a small set of ad-hoc risk measures towards a systematic approach grounded in axiomatic principles (coherence, time consistency). The proposed algorithms, particularly the actor-critic method, offer a path to applying these sophisticated risk measures to large-scale problems where traditional dynamic programming is intractable.

The numerical experiment, while simple, effectively highlights a key benefit of this work: the flexibility to choose a risk measure appropriate for the problem. It demonstrates how a common choice like standard deviation can lead to counter-intuitive behavior (penalizing desirable upside volatility), which can be corrected by using a more suitable coherent measure like semideviation. This flexibility is likely to be crucial in real-world applications such as finance, robotics, and operations management. The ideas presented here are likely to be widely used and to inspire further research into risk-sensitive learning algorithms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and powerful unification of policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures, a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The theoretical development is elegant, technically sound, and well-supported.
*   The framework successfully generalizes and simplifies previous results while also enabling the derivation of new ones.
*   The work is well-written, clearly motivated, and addresses an important problem in decision-making under uncertainty.
*   The results are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact on future research in risk-sensitive RL.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is limited to a simple illustrative example, and does not test the performance or scalability of the proposed algorithms on a standard, more complex RL benchmark.
*   Full details of the actor-critic algorithm and its analysis are deferred to supplementary materials, making a complete evaluation of the dynamic risk contribution challenging.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its theoretical novelty and significance, far outweigh its weaknesses. The limited empirical evaluation is understandable for a paper introducing a broad new framework. This is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.
