Summary
The paper develops a unified policy-gradient framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both in static settings and for time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) risk. For static coherent risk, the work derives a general gradient formula by combining the dual representation of coherent risk (as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope) with the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio identities. This yields (i) closed-form policy-gradient expressions for important special cases such as CVaR and mean-semideviation, and (ii) a general sample-average approximation (SAA) approach that computes the gradient via sampling and a small convex program whose KKT multipliers enter the gradient expression; a consistency result is provided. For dynamic risk, the work establishes a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, expressing the gradient as an expectation along trajectories under ξ-weighted transitions and a stage cost hθ defined via the value function and dual variables. An actor-critic scheme is proposed: the critic estimates Vθ using the robust-MDP equivalence and function approximation, and the actor uses the new gradient theorem to update the policy. A toy trading example illustrates the flexibility of choosing among risk criteria.

Relation to prior work
There is substantial prior work on risk-sensitive RL for specific risk objectives: exponential utility [6], mean-variance [18, 29, 21], CVaR in static RL [8, 30], and dynamic coherent risk for special dynamics [20, 9]. Robust MDPs and dynamic coherent risk are closely related [19], with scalable planning via function approximation studied in [31]. The paper generalizes and unifies these strands by deriving policy gradients for general coherent risks (static and Markov coherent), subsuming CVaR gradients [30] and mean-semi-deviation as special cases, and connecting the dynamic gradient to robust MDP value estimation [19, 31].

Strengths
- Generality: A single framework covering all coherent risks, with both static and time-consistent dynamic formulations, is novel and conceptually clean.
- Technical contributions: Clear gradient formula for static coherent risk via duality and envelope theorem; extension recovers and simplifies known results for CVaR; new derivation for mean-semideviation; SAA-based estimator with consistency under mild conditions.
- Dynamic policy gradient theorem: Extends classic policy-gradient results to Markov coherent risk using ξ-weighted transitions and a stage cost built from dual variables; leverages robust MDP equivalence for the critic.
- Practical pathway: For many popular coherent risks (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation), the risk envelope admits tractable convex programs, enabling the proposed sampling+convex optimization estimator.
- Clarity of problem formulation and assumptions; related work is well-situated.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Empirical evaluation is very limited. The dynamic risk actor-critic is not validated on MDP benchmarks. The single static trading example does not demonstrate scalability, sample efficiency, or comparative performance versus established baselines (e.g., CVaR-specific policy gradients, mean-variance methods) in control tasks.
- Computational considerations are underdeveloped. Each static-gradient estimate requires solving a convex program whose size grows with sample count; the dynamic algorithm requires computing ξ∗ and dual variables per state, then sampling under ξ-weighted kernels. There is no complexity/runtime analysis or empirical timing to assess feasibility at scale.
- Assumptions on the risk envelope (explicit convex description, differentiability of constraints with respect to Pθ) are reasonable for standard measures but may be restrictive in applications where only black-box risk functionals are available. The dependence of the gradient on ∇θ of envelope constraints deserves more discussion and examples.
- The dynamic method assumes access to a generative model (multiple next-state samples per state and sampling from ξ-weighted transitions). The on-policy setting with a single trajectory is deferred to future work; this limits near-term applicability.

Clarity
- The actor-critic algorithm is only sketched; essential details (pseudocode, estimator definitions, stepsizes, function approximation architecture, projection schemes) are deferred, making it hard to assess reproducibility.
- Notation in the dynamic gradient theorem is dense (multiple dual variables per state, derivatives of envelope constraints). Providing worked-out envelopes and derivative expressions for common risks (e.g., CVaR, spectral) would improve readability.

Originality
- The unification for coherent risks and the dynamic policy gradient theorem appear novel and go beyond prior risk-specific treatments. The CVaR gradient derivation under weaker assumptions is a nice simplification of [30]. The SAA-based gradient with consistency adds methodological breadth.

Significance
- A general policy-gradient toolkit for coherent risks is potentially impactful for risk-sensitive RL and robust control. However, the lack of empirical validation on dynamic problems and limited discussion of computational trade-offs weaken the immediate practical significance.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Include at least one dynamic MDP experiment demonstrating the actor-critic method on benchmarks (e.g., gridworld, inventory control, LQ with disturbances), comparing to risk-neutral PG, mean-variance methods [29, 21], and CVaR-specific baselines [8, 30]. Report performance, variance, and runtime.
- Provide a complexity discussion: per-iteration costs for the static gradient estimator (convex program size vs. sample count), and dynamic algorithm overhead (estimating Vθ, computing ξ∗, sampling under modified kernels).
- Add a table or appendix giving explicit envelopes and required derivatives for CVaR, mean-semideviation, and spectral risks, making the method easier to instantiate.

Nice-to-have:
- Discuss and/or prototype importance sampling to reduce variance for tail-sensitive risks (as noted in the conclusion).
- Elaborate on extensions beyond finite spaces: required regularity conditions and what changes in the proofs.
- Clarify the requirement of a generative model; outline an off-policy or single-trajectory variant (even if preliminary).

Pros for acceptance
- Unified, technically sound framework covering a broad and important class of risk criteria.
- New dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- General SAA-based gradient estimator with a consistency guarantee.
- Connections to robust MDPs provide a principled critic design.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation, especially for the dynamic algorithm.
- Scalability and computational burden not characterized.
- Reliance on a generative model and ξ-weighted sampling limits applicability; no on-policy workaround presented.
- Some algorithmic details deferred, hindering reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely theoretical contribution that unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients under coherent risk, with a promising dynamic extension. With additional empirical validation and clearer computational guidance, this could have substantial impact. As it stands, the technical novelty is high, but practical validation is insufficient; strengthening the experiments and clarifying computational aspects would significantly improve the paper.
