## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper tackles the important problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending existing policy gradient methods to the broader class of coherent risk measures. The authors successfully generalize previous work focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR, providing a unified framework for incorporating risk aversion into RL. The core contribution lies in deriving novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, leveraging convex programming and actor-critic methods respectively.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of standard expected cost minimization and the benefits of coherent risk measures. The mathematical presentation is rigorous, building upon established concepts in risk theory and RL. The derivation of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) is a significant technical achievement, and the authors demonstrate how existing results for CVaR and mean-semideviation can be recovered as special cases. The proposed algorithms, combining sampling with convex optimization and actor-critic techniques, appear sound and offer a practical approach to tackling this complex problem.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of extensive empirical validation. While a numerical illustration is provided (Section 6), it is relatively limited in scope and focuses on a specific trading scenario. A more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on a range of benchmark environments, would significantly strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the discussion of computational complexity and scalability is somewhat limited. While the authors acknowledge the challenges of applying their approach to large-scale MDPs, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the convex programming steps would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods.
*   Clear and rigorous mathematical presentation.
*   Potential for broad applicability across various domains requiring risk-aware decision-making.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation.
*   Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   The numerical example, while illustrative, is not particularly compelling.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL. While the lack of extensive empirical evaluation is a concern, the novelty and potential impact of the work are significant. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically requesting a more thorough experimental evaluation and a more detailed discussion of computational considerations. The authors should also consider expanding the numerical illustration to include more diverse environments and comparisons with state-of-the-art algorithms.
