### Summary

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While prior work has focused on specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), this paper extends the policy gradient approach to the entire class of coherent risk measures, a widely accepted axiomatic framework for risk from finance and operations research. The authors tackle two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk measure of the total discounted cost and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measure.

The main contributions are twofold. First, for the static risk case, the paper derives a new, general formula for the policy gradient. This result leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures as a worst-case expectation over a "risk envelope" of probability distributions. The resulting gradient formula involves the solution (and Lagrange multipliers) of a convex optimization problem. This leads to a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient. Second, for the dynamic risk case, the paper presents a new policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one, which relates the gradient of the risk-sensitive value function to a risk-weighted advantage-like term. This theorem forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm for optimizing dynamic coherent risk measures. The paper demonstrates that its general results can recover and simplify previous findings for specific risk measures like CVaR.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. By moving from specific risk measures to the entire class of coherent risk measures, the authors provide a unified and principled framework. This is a novel and important contribution that advances the theoretical foundations of risk-sensitive RL. It connects RL with a mature body of literature on risk measures and opens the door for practitioners to design and use a much wider variety of risk objectives tailored to their specific problems. The unification and simplification of prior results (e.g., for CVaR) is a strong testament to the power of the proposed framework.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical development is elegant. The key idea of using the envelope theorem on the dual representation of coherent risk to derive the gradient is insightful and powerful. The main results, Theorem 4.2 for the static case and Theorem 5.2 for the dynamic case, appear correct and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. The connection drawn between the risk-sensitive Bellman equation, robust MDPs, and the policy gradient formulation is clear and well-executed.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and clearly situates the work within the existing literature. The problem formulation and preliminaries are concise and precise. The logical flow from the static risk problem to the more complex dynamic risk problem is easy to follow. The use of CVaR and mean-semideviation as running examples effectively illustrates the generality and utility of the main theoretical results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the empirical evaluation. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is a stateless multi-armed bandit problem. While it effectively demonstrates why flexibility in choosing a risk measure is important (contrasting mean-semideviation with mean-standard-deviation on a heavy-tailed distribution), it does not test the core algorithmic contributions for sequential decision-making problems. In particular, the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk setting is not evaluated at all. The performance, stability, and scalability of the proposed algorithms on standard RL benchmarks remain an open question.

2.  **Practicality of the Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms introduce additional complexity. The general algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex program within each gradient update step, which could be computationally expensive, especially with a large number of samples N. For the dynamic actor-critic algorithm, the paper notes that the actor requires sampling from an MDP with modified transition probabilities (the ξ-weighted transitions) and estimating the `h(x, a)` term, which involves another layer of sampling. This two-phase sampling procedure may be sample-inefficient and relies on a simulator, potentially limiting its application in real-world scenarios where only a single stream of experience is available. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical viability is not fully established.

3.  **Missing Details in the Main Paper:** Due to space constraints, crucial details of the actor-critic algorithm, including the critic's update mechanism (which relies on work from [31]) and the full convergence analysis, are deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable, this makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess the completeness and complexity of the proposed solution for the dynamic risk problem from the main paper alone.

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   The paper would be much stronger with an empirical evaluation on a simple but standard MDP (e.g., a gridworld with stochastic costs, or a classic control task). This would allow the authors to demonstrate the behavior of the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm and show how different choices of coherent risk measures lead to different policies in a sequential setting.
*   A discussion on the computational complexity of the general gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) would be beneficial. How does the cost of solving the convex program scale with the number of samples, and how does this compare to methods for specific risk measures?
*   It would be helpful to provide more intuition in the main text about the practical implementation of the actor-critic algorithm. For instance, a brief explanation of how the ξ-weighted trajectory sampling is performed would aid the reader's understanding.
*   The connection to robustness against model uncertainty is a very powerful motivation mentioned in the conclusion. This could be highlighted more prominently in the introduction to further emphasize the significance of the framework.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for coherent risk measures is elegant and powerful. The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation for the proposed algorithms in a sequential decision-making context. Despite this, the theoretical novelty and importance of the work make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
