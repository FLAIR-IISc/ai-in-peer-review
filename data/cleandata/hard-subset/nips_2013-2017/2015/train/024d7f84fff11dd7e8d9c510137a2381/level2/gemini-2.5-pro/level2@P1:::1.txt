This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending previous work on specific risk measures to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors tackle two settings: 1) optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted cost, and 2) optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The core technical contribution is a novel formula for the policy gradient of a general coherent risk measure. This formula is derived by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures (as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities) and applying the Envelope Theorem.

For the static case, this gradient formula leads to two algorithmic approaches. For risk measures like CVaR or mean-semideviation where the worst-case density has a known analytical form, the framework recovers and simplifies existing gradient formulas. For the general case, the authors propose a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming: a sample average approximation (SAA) of the dual problem is solved to estimate the worst-case density and corresponding Lagrange multipliers, which are then used to estimate the policy gradient. A consistency result for this estimator is provided.

For the dynamic case, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, analogous to the standard policy gradient theorem for expected returns. This theorem relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and forms the basis for a proposed actor-critic algorithm. The work positions itself as a unification and generalization of prior research in risk-sensitive RL that has focused on individual risk measures like variance or CVaR.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant theoretical contribution. By developing a policy gradient method for the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a powerful and unified framework for a topic that has previously been addressed in a piecemeal fashion. This is a substantial step forward for the field of risk-sensitive RL.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Technical Approach:** The derivation of the main gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) using the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope theorem is elegant. It provides a principled way to compute gradients that was not previously available for general coherent risks. The extension to a policy gradient theorem for the dynamic case (Theorem 5.2) is also a strong and novel result.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides clear motivation for using coherent and time-consistent risk measures, carefully introduces the necessary mathematical preliminaries, and builds its argument logically from the static to the dynamic case.

4.  **Potential for Impact:** The framework allows researchers and practitioners to select or even design problem-specific risk measures from a broad, axiomatically-justified class, rather than being limited to a few standard choices. The numerical example, while simple, effectively illustrates why this flexibility is important (e.g., distinguishing between downside and upside variability).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Cost of the General Algorithm:** The proposed general gradient estimation algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem at each gradient update step. The computational cost of this step, which scales with the number of samples `N`, could be a significant practical bottleneck, especially in a stochastic gradient descent setting where many updates are required. A discussion of this complexity would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Details on the Actor-Critic Algorithm:** Many of the implementation details and the analysis of the proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk are deferred to the supplementary material. This makes it difficult to fully assess the practicality and challenges of this part of the contribution from the main paper alone. For instance, the two-phase sampling procedure required for the actor update seems complex and its sample efficiency is unclear.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration is a stateless multi-armed bandit problem. While it effectively demonstrates a key conceptual point about the choice of risk measure, it does not validate the proposed algorithms on a sequential decision-making problem (an MDP with non-trivial state dynamics). An evaluation on a standard RL benchmark would have been much more convincing in demonstrating the performance and scalability of the proposed methods, particularly the actor-critic algorithm.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you comment on the practical feasibility of the SAA-based gradient estimator? How does its computational cost compare to methods for specific risk measures like CVaR, where the estimator can be computed more directly?
*   The actor-critic algorithm requires sampling from an MDP with transition probabilities weighted by ξ*. Does this necessitate a generative model or simulator of the environment? Could this approach be adapted for model-free learning from a single stream of experience, perhaps using off-policy correction techniques?
*   The numerical example powerfully argues against using standard deviation. It would be interesting to also see the performance of CVaR in this setting, which is a coherent risk measure that is also insensitive to upside gains.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong and elegant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The generalization to the full class of coherent risk measures is a significant achievement. The main weaknesses lie in the limited experimental validation and the potential computational burden of the general algorithm. However, the theoretical novelty and significance of the work are substantial, and I believe it will be of great interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
