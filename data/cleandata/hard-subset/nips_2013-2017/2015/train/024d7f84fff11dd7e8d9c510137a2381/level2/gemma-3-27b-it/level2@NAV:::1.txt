## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper tackles the important problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending existing policy gradient methods to encompass the broad class of coherent risk measures. The authors convincingly argue for the generality and applicability of coherent risk, moving beyond specific measures like VaR and CVaR. The core contribution is a unified framework, providing gradient formulas for both static and time-consistent dynamic coherent risk measures, and demonstrating how previous results are special cases of their approach.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing risk-sensitive RL methods and the benefits of using coherent risk. The mathematical development is rigorous, building upon established concepts from stochastic programming and optimization. The theorem relating the gradient to the value function for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) is particularly noteworthy. The inclusion of numerical results, though brief, effectively demonstrates the practical impact of choosing different coherent risk measures.

A strength is the generalization provided by applying the coherent risk framework, offering a more principled approach to risk management in RL. The derivation of gradient formulas, and their connection to sampling-based algorithms, makes the approach tractable for large-scale problems. The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature, referencing key work in RL, stochastic programming, and risk theory.

However, the paper could be improved by providing more detailed discussion regarding the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly for the dynamic case. While the authors mention using function approximation, a more thorough analysis of its impact on convergence and performance would be beneficial. Further, while the numerical example illustrates the different behavior of policies under different risk measures, a more comprehensive comparison with baseline risk-sensitive RL algorithms would strengthen the validation. The discussion on potential implementation challenges and scalability concerns could also be expanded.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound extension of policy gradient methods.
*   Generalization to the broad class of coherent risk measures.
*   Potential for significant impact in risk-sensitive RL applications.
*   Clear and rigorous mathematical development.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*   Brief numerical evaluation; lacks comparison to strong baselines.
*   Scalability concerns for the dynamic case not fully addressed.

**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors present a novel and well-motivated framework with significant theoretical and practical potential. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding computational complexity and numerical evaluation.
