

This paper proposes a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures, enabling a general approach that subsumes previous work on specific risk measures like CVaR and mean-variance. For static risk, they present a sampling-based estimator using convex programming, while for dynamic risk, they develop a new policy gradient theorem that enables an actor-critic algorithm. The paper demonstrates that several previous results are special cases of their general framework.

The paper's main strength is its theoretical contribution: the unified treatment of coherent risk measures provides a principled way to incorporate risk preferences into RL. The authors successfully derive gradient formulas using the risk envelope representation (Theorem 2.1), which is elegant and generalizes previous results. The CVaR example (Section 4.1) demonstrates how their approach simplifies and generalizes a previously complex derivation. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively illustrates how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial trading context, highlighting the practical value of their framework.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with appropriate mathematical rigor. The connection to prior work is well-established, though the authors could better address the most recent NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL, such as Tamar et al. (2014) on policy gradients with variance-related risk criteria and Chow & Ghavamzadeh (2014) on CVaR optimization. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of their approach to these recent works.

While the theoretical results are sound, the paper has several limitations. The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms is not thoroughly analyzed. The sampling-based estimator requires solving convex programs at each iteration, which may be computationally expensive for large-scale problems. The numerical results are limited to a single example with three assets, which doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the scalability of the approach to more complex environments. Additionally, the assumption that the risk envelope U(Pθ) is given in explicit form (Assumption 2.2) may not hold for all coherent risk measures, limiting the practical applicability of the framework.

The significance of this work is moderate. While the unified framework is theoretically interesting, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their approach outperforms existing methods on standard RL benchmarks. The numerical example shows different risk measures lead to different policies, but doesn't quantify performance improvements. The connection to model uncertainty mentioned in the conclusion is intriguing but remains undeveloped.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1) A more thorough comparison with the most recent NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL
2) A detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability
3) Additional numerical experiments on standard RL benchmarks
4) Discussion of how to choose appropriate risk measures for specific applications

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. While the practical implications need further demonstration, the theoretical results are sound and the approach is general enough to encompass previous work. The paper would benefit from addressing the computational aspects more thoroughly and providing more substantial empirical validation.

The quality of the theoretical results is high, with appropriate proofs and consistency results. The clarity is good, with well-structured exposition. The originality is moderate - the paper generalizes previous work rather than introducing entirely novel concepts. The significance is promising but not yet fully demonstrated through empirical results. The paper would be stronger with more comprehensive experiments and a clearer comparison to recent work.
