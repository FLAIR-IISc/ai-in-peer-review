

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic. The main contributions are: (1) a new gradient formula for static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, (2) a convex programming-based algorithm for gradient estimation, and (3) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures with an associated actor-critic algorithm.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding, correctly identifying the coherent risk framework as a natural extension to previous risk-sensitive RL work on specific measures like CVaR and mean-variance. The authors properly reference seminal works (Artzner et al. [2], Ruszczynski & Shapiro [26]) and position their work within the broader context of risk-sensitive optimization. The theoretical derivations (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are well-supported with proofs in the supplementary material, and the examples (CVaR and mean-semideviation) effectively demonstrate how their framework generalizes prior results.

The numerical illustration in Section 6 is particularly compelling, showing how different risk criteria (risk-neutral, mean-semideviation, mean-standard-deviation) lead to substantially different policy choices in a financial trading context. This demonstrates the practical utility of the framework for decision-makers who need flexibility in risk preferences.

The paper's quality is high, with technically sound arguments and a clear connection between the theoretical results and their practical implementation. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition that builds logically from preliminary concepts to the main results. The originality is significant, as the work generalizes rather than merely replicates previous approaches to specific risk measures. The significance is evident in the potential to provide decision-makers with a flexible framework for managing cost variability across diverse applications.

The main limitation is that the actor-critic algorithm requires a simulator for sampling multiple state-transitions from each state, which may limit applicability in settings with limited simulation capabilities. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest extending to single-trajectory roll-outs as future work.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified theoretical foundation while demonstrating practical utility. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and would be valuable to the community. I recommend acceptance.
