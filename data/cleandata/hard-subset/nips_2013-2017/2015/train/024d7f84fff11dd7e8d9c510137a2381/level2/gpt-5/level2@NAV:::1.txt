Summary and relation to prior work: The paper develops risk-sensitive policy gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures, covering both static and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) settings. The main claims are: (1) a new gradient formula for static coherent risks, enabling sampling-based estimation via a likelihood-ratio term combined with convex programming; (2) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator with a consistency result; and (3) a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that yields an actor-critic algorithm. This unifies and generalizes prior work focused on specific risks (mean-variance, CVaR) and connects to robust MDPs (e.g., Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian; Chow et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh).

Quality: The theoretical development is sound and carefully grounded in the dual representation of coherent risks and the envelope theorem. The static gradient formula specializes cleanly to known results (e.g., CVaR) and the SAA consistency is plausible under mild regularity. The dynamic result extends the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk, a notable technical contribution. However, empirical support is minimal (a small trading toy), and algorithmic details and convergence proofs are deferred to the supplement. Computational overhead (solving a convex program to obtain ξ* and multipliers, potentially per state) and sample complexity are not quantified.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and situates itself in the literature. Assumptions (e.g., finite Ω; smoothness of envelope constraints; availability of gradients dfi/dp) are stated, but guidance on verifying them for common envelopes and implications for continuous spaces could be clearer. The actor-critic procedure is only sketched.

Originality: High. Providing a unified gradient framework for coherent risks and a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk goes beyond prior work restricted to variance- or CVaR-based criteria.

Significance: Potentially high. A general recipe for risk-sensitive RL can influence both research and applications. Practical impact depends on computational feasibility and broader empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually unifying framework with new gradient theorems.
- Generality across coherent risks (static and dynamic).
- Solid theoretical support; recovers known special cases.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited experiments and missing ablations on scalability.
- Heavy inner-loop optimization (convex programs, ξ-weighted transitions).
- Several practical assumptions and details left to the supplement.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add experiments on standard RL benchmarks; compare to CVaR-PG, mean-variance, robust MDP baselines.
- Provide complexity and wall-clock analyses; discuss variance-reduction/importance sampling.
- Clarify how to compute multipliers and constraint derivatives in model-free settings; extend to continuous states/actions.
- Explore single-trajectory variants and off-policy estimators.

Overall: Strong theoretical paper with promising scope; acceptance recommended if empirical section can be strengthened.
