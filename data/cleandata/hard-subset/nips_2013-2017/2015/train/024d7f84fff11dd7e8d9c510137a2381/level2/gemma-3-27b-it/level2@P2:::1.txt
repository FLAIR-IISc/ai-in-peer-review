This paper presents a policy gradient framework for coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning, generalizing previous work focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or variance. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, utilizing sampling and convex programming techniques. The contribution lies in providing a unified approach that leverages the theoretical properties of coherent risk to extend existing policy gradient methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The introduction effectively establishes the context of risk-sensitive RL and the importance of coherent risk measures. The authors diligently review related work, identifying limitations of existing approaches and positioning their contribution as a generalization. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the presentation of the core results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) is clear. The connection to robust MDPs and the discussion of model uncertainty in the conclusion are insightful. The numerical example, while simple, helps illustrate the practical implications of different risk preferences.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The framework extends to the entire class of coherent risk measures, offering a significant generalization over existing methods. This is a substantial theoretical contribution.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon established results in coherent risk theory (Artzner et al., Ruszczynski & Shapiro) and utilizes the envelope theorem effectively.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed approach translates the theoretical results into practical algorithms (sampling with convex programming and an actor-critic method) suitable for large-scale MDPs.
* **Clear Presentation:** The mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably clear and organized manner. The authors highlight key assumptions and provide intuition behind the results.
* **Interesting Discussion:** The concluding remarks about the connection to model uncertainty are thought-provoking and suggest future research directions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the need for approximation techniques (function approximation for the value function), the computational cost of repeatedly solving convex programs within the gradient estimation loop (especially for static risk) is a significant concern. This is not fully addressed, and the scalability of the approach to truly high-dimensional problems remains unclear.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example is illustrative but limited in scope. A more comprehensive evaluation on benchmark RL tasks with varying risk preferences would be valuable.  The focus on a single problem makes it hard to judge the practical utility.
* **Assumption Dependence:** The assumptions (2.2 and 4.1, 5.1) are reasonably standard but could be further discussed in terms of their practical implications and potential limitations. For example, Assumption 2.2, regarding the form of the risk envelope, might not hold for all coherent risk measures.
* **Dynamic Risk Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is brief. More detail regarding the function approximation scheme, sampling strategy, and convergence analysis would strengthen the paper. A complete algorithm presented would be helpful.
* **Comparison to Robust MDP:** While the connection to robust MDPs is mentioned, a more detailed comparison in terms of computational cost and performance would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in RL: incorporating risk sensitivity in a principled manner.
* The theoretical contribution is substantial, providing a unifying framework for coherent risk measures.
* The proposed algorithms are well-motivated and offer a viable path for practical implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the proposed approach remains a concern, and its scalability is not adequately demonstrated.
* The empirical evaluation is limited, making it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the framework.
* The technical details of the dynamic risk algorithm are insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding computational complexity and empirical evaluation, the theoretical novelty and generality of the proposed framework are significant. The paper is well-written and clearly presented. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Discuss Computational Complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed algorithms, particularly the convex programming step in the static risk case.
* **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Include more comprehensive experiments on benchmark RL tasks with different risk preferences.
* **Elaborate on Dynamic Risk Algorithm:** Provide a more detailed description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case, including the function approximation scheme and sampling strategy.
* **Compare to Robust MDPs:**  Offer a more detailed comparison between the proposed approach and existing methods for robust MDPs.
* **Discuss Assumption Limitations:** Elaborate on the practical implications and limitations of the key assumptions.
