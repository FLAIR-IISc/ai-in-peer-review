- Introduces a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in RL, extending beyond specific measures like variance or CVaR.  
- Core technical contribution uses the dual representation of coherent risk (risk envelope) to derive gradient formulas.  
- Presents two main results: a static coherent risk gradient formula using the envelope theorem and a dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk policy gradient theorem.  
- Static-case method yields a sample-based estimator that combines sampling with solving a convex optimization to find the worst-case distribution.  
- Re-derives CVaR gradient simply and derives a new gradient formula for mean-semideviation as concrete examples.  
- Dynamic result leads to an actor-critic algorithm for large-scale MDPs based on a risk-sensitive Bellman equation.  
- Theoretical development is rigorous and technically sound, leveraging convex optimization and stochastic programming tools.  
- Main theorems (Theorem 4.2 and 5.2) appear correct and provide a solid foundation for the algorithms.  
- Consistency of the static sample-based gradient estimator is established under reasonable assumptions (Proposition 4.4).  
- Connections between dynamic coherent risk, robust MDPs, and risk-sensitive Bellman equations are well-founded.  
- Authors clearly state assumptions, but full actor-critic algorithmic details and convergence analysis are deferred to supplementary material.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, with consistent notation and effective examples that ground abstract results.  
- Suggestion: include a detailed algorithm block for the actor-critic method in the main paper for clarity.  
- Highly original: first unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures and a novel use of likelihood-ratio + dual representation.  
- Significant contribution: provides a principled, axiomatic approach to risk-sensitive RL and tools likely useful for real-world applications.  
- Numerical experiment effectively illustrates flexibility of choosing appropriate risk measures (e.g., semideviation vs. standard deviation).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel unification, elegant and supported theory, generalizes prior work, well-written, and high potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical evaluation on complex benchmarks and deferred actor-critic details hinder full assessment.
