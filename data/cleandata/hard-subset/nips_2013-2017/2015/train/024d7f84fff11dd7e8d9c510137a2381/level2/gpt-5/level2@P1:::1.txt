Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures, both in static and (time-consistent) dynamic settings. The key idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk measures as worst-case expectations over a convex “risk envelope.” For static risk, the authors derive a general gradient expression (Theorem 4.2) via the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio, and propose two estimators: (i) closed-form when the Lagrangian saddle point is analytically available (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), and (ii) a general sample-average approximation (SAA) that couples sampling with a convex program to recover the saddle point and KKT multipliers. They prove consistency of the SAA-based estimator under mild conditions. For dynamic risk, they consider Markov coherent risk measures (time-consistent nested coherent risks), derive a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) involving a risk-adjusted stage cost hθ and a reweighted transition kernel, and propose an actor-critic algorithm that estimates the risk-sensitive value function via function approximation (leveraging the robust MDP equivalence) and then updates the policy using the new gradient formula. A small numerical illustration highlights how different risk criteria lead to different asset selection behavior.

This work unifies and generalizes prior RL efforts that focused on specific objectives: mean-variance (Tamar et al., Prashanth and Ghavamzadeh), CVaR optimization (Chow and Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al., AAAI), exponential utility (Borkar), and dynamic coherent risk with linear dynamics. It leverages coherent risk duality (Ruszczyński and Shapiro), robust MDP connections (Osogami; Ruszczyński), and function approximation for robust MDPs (Petrik and Subramanian; Tamar, Mannor, Xu).

Strengths
- Generality and unification: The gradient formula for static coherent risk and the dynamic policy gradient theorem cover a broad family of objectives, subsuming CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases.
- Sound variational foundation: The use of the dual representation and envelope theorem is elegant and yields implementable estimators that are consistent.
- Bridge to robust MDPs: The value-function view and the reweighted transition dynamics provide a clear conceptual link between risk and distributional robustness.
- Clarity of assumptions: The finite-space and smooth risk-envelope assumptions are explicit; technical conditions for consistency are discussed.
- Practical recipe: For static risk, the two-step sampling-plus-convex-program approach is concrete; for dynamic risk, the actor-critic decomposition is natural.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: Only a small toy portfolio experiment is presented, and no dynamic-risk experiments or comparisons on standard RL benchmarks are provided. This makes it hard to assess scalability, sample efficiency, and practical trade-offs versus specialized methods (e.g., CVaR RL).
- Computational burden: The general SAA estimator requires solving a convex program with O(N) variables and constraints and extracting KKT multipliers for each gradient estimate. The dynamic case further requires sampling under ξ-weighted dynamics and multiple next-state samples per state. Discussion of runtime, memory, and solver choices is minimal.
- Model access requirements: The dynamic actor requires a simulator capable of resetting to states and sampling multiple transitions, and the gradient formula uses derivatives of risk-envelope constraints with respect to P. The method seems not directly applicable in strictly model-free, single-trajectory settings.
- Missing algorithmic details in main text: The actor-critic method is not fully specified (pseudocode, step sizes, two-timescale conditions), and convergence/approximation error bounds are deferred to the supplement.
- Assumptions may be restrictive: The finite-space exposition and the requirement that the risk envelope be available in a differentiable canonical form may limit applicability. Extensions to continuous state-action spaces and approximate envelopes are not empirically demonstrated.

Quality
The theoretical development is careful, leverages standard tools appropriately, and the consistency result is reassuring. However, the lack of substantial experiments and runtime/variance analyses weakens the empirical case. Some key proofs and all algorithmic convergence details are relegated to the supplement.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with helpful preliminaries and clear statements of assumptions. The main algorithms would benefit from concise pseudocode, complexity discussion, and a more self-contained actor-critic description. Moving at least one nontrivial dynamic-risk experiment into the main paper would improve readability and impact.

Originality
High. Providing a unified policy-gradient framework and gradient theorems for the entire class of coherent risks—both static and dynamic Markov—appears novel relative to prior RL work focused on specific risk measures.

Significance
Potentially high. A general recipe for risk-sensitive RL could influence both research and practice. That said, significance is currently undercut by limited experimental evidence demonstrating practical usefulness and scalability.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please include at least one dynamic-risk experiment (e.g., gridworld, inventory control, queueing) with comparisons to CVaR-specific and mean-variance baselines. Report sample complexity, runtime, and solver overhead for the SAA step.
- Provide pseudocode for the actor-critic algorithm with precise requirements (two-timescale conditions, function approximator class) and a brief convergence statement in the main text.
- Discuss model-free applicability: can one approximate ξ-weighted transitions via importance sampling without an explicit simulator reset? What variance penalties arise?
- Elaborate on computing dfi/dp and dge/dp for common risk envelopes (e.g., spectral risk). How sensitive is performance to misspecification of the envelope?
- Comment on continuous-state extensions (e.g., using function approximation for both actor and critic and sample-based solutions of the convex program).

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical framework unifying risk-sensitive policy gradients for coherent risk measures.
- Novel dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Sound use of convex duality with implementable estimators and consistency guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is too limited to establish practicality and scalability.
- Computational and model-access requirements are significant and under-characterized.
- Many algorithmic details and proofs are deferred to the supplement.

Overall
This is a technically solid and original contribution with promising impact, but the current experimental support is thin. I lean toward a borderline decision; a stronger empirical section demonstrating dynamic-risk learning and practical runtime would significantly strengthen the case.
