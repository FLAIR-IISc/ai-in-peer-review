

The paper develops a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using coherent risk measures, generalizing previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or variance. The authors present two main contributions: a new gradient formula for static coherent risk that combines sampling with convex programming, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that enables an actor-critic style algorithm.

The theoretical foundation is strong, built upon the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), which provides a clear path for deriving the gradient formulas. The proofs are rigorous, with the consistency result (Proposition 4.4) well-justified and properly contextualized within the literature. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of selecting appropriate risk measures for different problem settings, showing how different risk criteria can lead to distinct policy choices.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with careful development of the theoretical foundations before presenting the main results. The connection to related work is explicit, with clear statements about how previous results are special cases of the proposed framework. The examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) provide concrete illustrations of the theoretical results, making them more accessible to readers.

The originality is strong. While previous studies have investigated specific risk measures in RL, this paper successfully generalizes to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The representation theorem is leveraged in a novel way for policy gradient methods, and the paper properly acknowledges that previous approaches were limited to specific risk measures or particular system dynamics.

The significance is clear. By providing a flexible framework for risk-sensitive RL, the work addresses an important practical need for decision-makers with varying risk preferences. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (Section 7) suggests broader implications for handling uncertainty in dynamic decision making. The numerical example effectively demonstrates how different risk measures can lead to different policy choices, highlighting the importance of selecting the right risk measure for the problem.

Minor improvements could be made to enhance the paper. A more comprehensive comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods on standard benchmark problems would strengthen the practical relevance. While the numerical example is illustrative, including quantitative comparisons to previous methods would provide stronger evidence of the approach's advantages. The algorithmic details for the actor-critic approach are deferred to supplementary material, which might make implementation more challenging for readers. A brief discussion of computational complexity would also be helpful.

The paper makes a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework that generalizes and simplifies previous approaches. It offers greater flexibility for decision-makers to select appropriate risk measures for their specific problems, advancing the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL. The theoretical foundations are solid, the results are well-supported, and the practical implications are clearly articulated.

The work aligns well with NIPS's focus on machine learning and decision making under uncertainty. It advances the field by providing a principled approach to risk-sensitive RL that can accommodate diverse risk preferences, with potential applications in finance, operations research, and other domains where managing cost variability is critical. The connection to model uncertainty in Section 7 suggests promising directions for future work on handling uncertainty in dynamic decision making.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It addresses an important gap in the literature by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL, generalizing previous work while offering greater flexibility for practical applications. The work should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
