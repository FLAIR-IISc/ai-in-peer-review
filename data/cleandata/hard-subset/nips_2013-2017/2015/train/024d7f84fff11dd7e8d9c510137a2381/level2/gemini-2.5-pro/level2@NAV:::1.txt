This paper presents a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This work provides a significant unification and generalization of previous approaches that have focused on specific, individual risk measures such as variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors derive novel gradient formulas for both static coherent risk and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The proposed algorithm for the static case cleverly combines sampling with convex programming, while the dynamic case is addressed with a new actor-critic algorithm built upon a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem.

The primary strength of this work is its significant and elegant theoretical contribution. By leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures, the authors establish a principled and unified foundation for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. This connects the field of RL with well-established theory from finance and operations research. The derivation of the general gradient formulas is a key result, and the authors convincingly show that these can recover known results for specific risk measures like CVaR, which validates their approach. The paper provides the community with a versatile and powerful toolbox for incorporating a wide spectrum of rational risk preferences into learning agents.

The main weakness of the paper is its limited empirical validation. The numerical illustration, a three-asset portfolio problem, serves well to highlight the conceptual importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure. However, it fails to evaluate the performance, scalability, or sample efficiency of the proposed algorithms on a challenging RL benchmark. A stronger case for the practical utility of this framework would require experiments on more complex, high-dimensional control tasks, ideally with a comparison to existing specialized algorithms. Furthermore, the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is noted to require a simulator for sampling multiple next-state transitions, which is a practical limitation.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a strong and original theoretical contribution that significantly advances the state of risk-sensitive RL. The unification under the coherent risk framework is an important step for the field. While the experimental section could be strengthened to better demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed algorithms, the theoretical foundations are solid and impactful.
