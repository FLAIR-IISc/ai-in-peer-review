- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures, using dual representation (risk envelope) for static and time-consistent dynamic settings.
- For static risk, derives a general gradient (Theorem 4.2) and proposes (i) closed-form estimator when saddle point is analytic and (ii) SAA-based estimator that solves a convex program and recovers KKT multipliers.
- Proves consistency of the SAA-based static estimator under mild conditions.
- For dynamic (Markov coherent) risk, derives a policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) with a risk-adjusted stage cost and reweighted transition kernel, and proposes an actor-critic algorithm using function approximation.
- Unifies and generalizes prior RL work on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility) and leverages convex duality and robust MDP connections.
- Strength: broad generality—gradient formulas subsume CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases.
- Strength: solid variational foundation using duality and the envelope theorem, yielding implementable, consistent estimators.
- Strength: clear conceptual link between risk sensitivity and distributional robustness via reweighted dynamics.
- Strength: explicit assumptions and technical conditions are clearly stated.
- Strength: practical recipes provided (sampling-plus-convex-program for static risk; actor-critic decomposition for dynamic risk).
- Weakness: very limited empirical validation—only a small toy portfolio experiment and no dynamic-risk or standard RL benchmark comparisons.
- Weakness: computational burden—SAA requires solving convex programs with O(N) size and extracting KKT multipliers for each estimate; dynamic case needs ξ-weighted sampling and multiple next-state samples.
- Weakness: model-access requirements hinder purely model-free, single-trajectory applicability (needs simulator resets and derivative info of risk-envelope constraints w.r.t. P).
- Weakness: key algorithmic details (pseudocode, step sizes, two-timescale conditions) and convergence/approximation bounds are relegated to the supplement.
- Weakness: finite-space and differentiable-envelope assumptions may limit applicability; continuous-state extensions are not empirically demonstrated.
- Quality: theoretically careful and consistent, but empirical and runtime/variance analyses are weak.
- Clarity: generally well written, but main algorithms need concise pseudocode and complexity discussion; moving a dynamic experiment to main text would help.
- Originality: high—first unified policy-gradient framework for the full class of coherent risks, including dynamic Markov case.
- Significance: potentially high but currently undercut by sparse experimental evidence demonstrating practicality and scalability.
- Suggestions: add at least one dynamic-risk experiment with baselines and runtime/SAA overhead; provide actor-critic pseudocode and convergence sketch; discuss model-free options (importance sampling), envelope derivative computation, sensitivity, and continuous-state extensions.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong, general theoretical framework, novel dynamic policy-gradient theorem, and sound use of convex duality with consistency guarantees.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical support, significant computational/model-access requirements undercharacterized, and many algorithmic details/proofs deferred to supplement.
- Overall: technically solid and original with promising impact, but borderline due to thin experimental support—needs stronger empirical/detailed algorithmic evidence to be compelling.
