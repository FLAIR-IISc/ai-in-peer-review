Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures, both in static and time-consistent dynamic settings. For static risk, the main result (Theorem 4.2) gives a general gradient formula for ρ(Z) using the risk-envelope dual representation and an envelope theorem argument, leading to sampling-based estimators. Two concrete instances are shown: CVaR (recovering and simplifying prior results) and mean-semideviation (a new gradient formula). For general coherent risks, the authors propose a sample-average approximation (SAA) that combines Monte Carlo sampling with a convex program to recover the envelope optimizer and KKT multipliers, proving consistency of the resulting gradient estimator under mild conditions.

For dynamic risk, the paper considers Markov coherent risk measures (time-consistent nesting of coherent risks) and derives a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) that relates ∇θVθ(x) to an expectation under a ξ-weighted Markov chain, where ξ is the optimizer of the one-step risk envelope given the current value function. This yields an actor-critic style algorithm: a critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function using robust MDP tools with function approximation; an actor uses the critic to form ξ and to sample from the reweighted dynamics and update θ. A small trading example illustrates the flexibility of risk criteria and shows behavior differences between risk-neutral, mean-semideviation, and mean-standard-deviation objectives.

Relation to prior work
The work generalizes risk-sensitive PG methods beyond specific choices such as variance-related criteria (Tamar et al. ’12; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh ’13), CVaR (Chow & Ghavamzadeh ’14; Tamar et al. ’15), and exponential utility (Borkar ’01). For dynamic, time-consistent risk, it builds on Ruszczyński’s coherent dynamic risk framework and its robust MDP equivalence (e.g., Osogami ’12; Petrik & Subramanian ’12/’14), but provides a policy-gradient formulation and actor-critic procedure that allows policy-space approximation. The CVaR gradient is recovered as a special case with a simpler derivation, and the dynamic PG theorem appears novel.

Strengths
- Generality and unification: A single framework covers the entire class of coherent risks, yielding both specific known results (CVaR) and new ones (mean-semideviation, general SAA gradient).
- Sound variational foundation: The use of the dual envelope representation and the envelope theorem is principled; assumptions are stated and reasonably mild for common risks.
- Dynamic PG theorem: Extends classic policy-gradient results to time-consistent Markov coherent risk, bridging to robust MDPs and enabling actor-critic methods with function approximation.
- Practical estimators: Likelihood-ratio-based estimators and SAA with convex programming are implementable; consistency is established.
- Useful perspective: The paper highlights how risk design (e.g., semideviation vs. standard deviation) materially affects behavior.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical evaluation is minimal. The only illustration is a synthetic static example. There are no dynamic MDP experiments, no comparisons to CVaR-specific algorithms or robust MDP baselines, and no investigation of sample efficiency, variance, or scalability.
- Computational burden and practicality: The SAA approach solves a convex program and extracts KKT multipliers per batch; the dynamic method requires, for each state, solving an inner envelope problem and sampling from ξ-reweighted transitions. The paper acknowledges the need for a simulator allowing multiple next-state samples. The overall complexity and stability under function approximation are not quantified.
- Clarity gaps in estimator details: The gradient terms involving ∇θge(ξ*,Pθ) and ∇θfi(ξ*,Pθ) rely on derivatives of the constraints w.r.t. the probability vector p and then w.r.t. θ. How these are estimated from samples in practice (especially in the general SAA estimator) is not fully spelled out in the main text. Pseudocode and explicit dependence on ∇θ log P(ω) would help.
- Assumptions and scope: The finite probability space assumption simplifies analysis; extension to general Lp spaces is claimed but deferred. For the dynamic setting, sampling from the ξ-weighted chain requires either model access or specialized simulators; on-policy single-trajectory learning is not handled.
- Limited discussion of variance reduction and finite-sample behavior. Risk objectives sensitive to tails (e.g., CVaR) can have high estimator variance; no empirical or theoretical variance analysis is provided.

Quality
The theoretical contributions appear technically sound given the stated assumptions, and the consistency result for the SAA-based gradient is appropriate. However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive empirical validation and a clearer presentation of algorithmic details and computational costs. The actor-critic component is only sketched in the main text.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. The main theorems are clearly stated and related to prior work. However, several implementation details are deferred to the supplement. Notation around derivatives of constraint functions w.r.t. probability vectors could be clarified, and explicit pseudocode for static SAA and the dynamic actor-critic would improve readability.

Originality
High. The general gradient formula for coherent risks and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk provide a unifying perspective that extends multiple prior lines of work.

Significance
Potentially high. A unified and principled risk-sensitive PG framework can influence future RL research and applications in finance/operations where coherent risks are standard. The lack of empirical evidence on dynamic problems and scalability tempers the immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add dynamic MDP experiments (e.g., gridworlds, inventory control, LQG) comparing to CVaR-specific methods and robust MDP baselines; report performance, variance, and sample complexity.
- Provide algorithm pseudocode, computational complexity, and implementation details (how to compute/estimate the derivative terms, how KKT multipliers are obtained, batching, and solver choices).
- Discuss and, if possible, employ variance reduction (e.g., baselines for LR estimators, importance sampling for tail risks).
- Explore learning with single trajectory rollouts via off-policy corrections or importance sampling when ξ-weighted sampling is infeasible.
- Clarify extension beyond finite Ω and state assumptions needed.
- Expand discussion on when the risk envelope is available in canonical form and how to handle spectral risks or problem-specific envelopes.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, general theoretical contributions that unify static and dynamic coherent risk PG.
- Clear connections to robust MDPs and prior risk-sensitive RL; recovers and simplifies known special cases.
- Well-motivated and timely problem.

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation, especially for dynamic risk and actor-critic.
- Practicality and computational cost not thoroughly addressed.
- Some implementation details and estimator derivations are deferred and under-specified in the main text.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The theory is strong and original, but the paper would benefit from more substantial empirical evidence and clearer algorithmic exposition. If space permits to include dynamic experiments and practical details, it would strengthen the case for acceptance.
