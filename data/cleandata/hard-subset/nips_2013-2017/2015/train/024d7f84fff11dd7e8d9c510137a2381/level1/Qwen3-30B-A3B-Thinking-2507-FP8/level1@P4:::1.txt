

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using coherent risk measures. The authors extend previous work on risk-sensitive policy gradient methods (which focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR) to the entire class of coherent risk measures, which satisfy the four axioms of convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity. The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a new gradient formula for static coherent risk that combines sampling with convex programming, and (2) a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that leads to an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk measures.

The paper builds upon the seminal work of Artzner et al. (1999) on coherent risk measures and recent advances in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 18, 21, 29, 30]). It correctly identifies that previous approaches were limited to specific risk measures, whereas the proposed framework generalizes these results. The authors also properly contextualize their work against related literature on robust MDPs [19, 31] and optimization of coherent risk measures [25, 26].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-motivated assumptions and rigorous proofs for the main theorems (4.2 and 5.2), which are provided in the supplementary material. The authors carefully consider both static and dynamic risk measures, addressing different technical challenges for each case. The consistency result in Proposition 4.4 provides theoretical guarantees for the sampling-based estimator. The numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the framework by comparing different risk-sensitive policies on a financial trading example. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for a simulator for sampling multiple state-transitions and the computational cost of convex programming.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation and consistent terminology. The introduction provides an excellent motivation for the work, and the paper effectively relates the proposed framework to previous research. The examples (CVaR and mean-semideviation) help concretize the general approach. The supplementary material provides additional details on the algorithm and proofs that would be valuable for reproducibility. The writing is accessible to readers familiar with RL and risk theory, though the technical depth may require a strong background in optimization.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is its unified treatment of coherent risk measures in RL, which generalizes and extends previous work. While specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) have been studied in RL, this is the first work to provide a framework that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors correctly position their work as filling a gap in the literature, as previous approaches were limited to specific risk measures. The connection between coherent risk measures and their representation as worst-case expectations (Theorem 2.1) is central to the novel approach. The paper also makes a significant conceptual contribution by highlighting how the framework provides flexibility in designing risk criteria, as demonstrated in the numerical example.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in RL—risk-sensitive decision making—with a framework that has broad applicability across domains like finance and operations research. The unified approach simplifies the implementation of risk-sensitive RL by eliminating the need to develop separate algorithms for each risk measure. The numerical example demonstrates how different risk measures lead to different policies, which is valuable for practitioners. The connection to model uncertainty in Section 7 suggests broader implications for handling uncertainty in dynamic decision making. The work advances the state of the art by providing a principled framework that can be applied to many risk-sensitive RL problems.

**Strengths:**
- Provides a unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL
- Theoretical results are rigorously established with proofs
- Includes a practical numerical example demonstrating the framework's utility
- Properly contextualizes the work against previous literature
- Carefully addresses both static and dynamic risk measures

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational complexity of the convex programming step may be prohibitive for large-scale problems
- The need for a simulator to sample multiple state-transitions limits applicability to certain environments
- The paper does not provide a thorough comparison of computational efficiency with existing methods
- The theoretical results assume a finite probability space, which may not generalize directly to continuous settings

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The technical approach is sound, the theoretical results are well-justified, and the practical demonstration shows clear value. The paper advances the state of the art by generalizing previous specific-risk approaches to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The work is highly relevant to NIPS as it bridges theoretical advances in risk theory with practical RL algorithms.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it is technically sound, clearly written, novel in its unified framework, and significant for the field. The authors have made a substantial contribution to risk-sensitive RL that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners.

**Recommendation:** Accept
