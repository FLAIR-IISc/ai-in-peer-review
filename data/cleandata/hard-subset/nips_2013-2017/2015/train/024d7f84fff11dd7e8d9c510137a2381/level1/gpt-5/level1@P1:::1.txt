Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures in RL, covering both static risk of trajectory returns and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. For static risk, the key idea is to use the dual representation of coherent risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro), and apply the envelope theorem together with likelihood ratios to derive a general gradient formula. This yields (i) closed-form gradients in special cases (e.g., CVaR and mean-semi-deviation), and (ii) a practical two-step estimator for general coherent risks via sampling plus solving a small convex program (SAA), with consistency guarantees. For dynamic risk, the authors derive a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński 2010), expressing ∇θVθ in terms of the gradient of the log-policy and a state-action stage cost that depends on the saddle point of the dual problem at each state. They connect the value function to robust MDPs (Delage & Mannor; Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian), propose an actor-critic algorithm that alternates estimating Vθ with function approximation and computing the gradient via sampling from ξ-weighted transitions, and outline convergence arguments in the supplement. A small experiment illustrates how different coherent risks lead to different portfolio choices.

This work unifies and generalizes previous risk-sensitive PG methods: exponential utility/entropic risks (Borkar; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), mean-variance (Tamar et al.), and CVaR (Tamar et al., Chow & Ghavamzadeh). The dynamic result extends “policy gradient theorem” ideas (Sutton et al., Konda & Tsitsiklis) to the coherent risk setting.

Strengths
- General and unifying: A single framework covers the entire class of coherent risk measures, yielding previous results as special cases and offering new formulas (e.g., mean-semi-deviation) with clear derivations.
- Technically solid: The use of dual representations and the envelope theorem is elegant; the SAA-based gradient estimator is natural and comes with consistency conditions. The dynamic PG theorem is novel and well-motivated by time consistency.
- Practicality via convex programming: For general risk measures, the combination of sampling and small convex programs (with KKT multipliers) is implementable and exploits standard solvers.
- Clear connection to robust MDPs for the critic, leveraging recent scalable methods (Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al. 2015).
- The paper clarifies when and how different risk choices change learned behavior, with an illustrative example contrasting semi-deviation vs standard deviation.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and scope: The exposition is restricted to finite probability spaces; extensions to Lp spaces are claimed but omitted. The static and dynamic results require smoothness of risk-envelope constraints w.r.t. probabilities and availability of the envelope in canonical form, which may not hold or be easy to obtain for all coherent risks in practice.
- Computational burden: The approach solves a convex program (or obtains KKT multipliers) for each gradient estimate and, in the dynamic case, per state. Complexity and scalability in large/continuous state spaces are discussed only qualitatively.
- Generative model assumption: The actor requires a simulator to sample multiple next states and to sample from ξ-weighted transitions; the methods are not directly applicable to single-trajectory, online RL without additional importance sampling machinery.
- Limited empirical validation: Only a small static illustration is provided; no dynamic MDP experiments or comparisons to strong baselines. Variance properties and sensitivity to envelope misspecification are not explored.
- Some technical gaps in main text: Key proofs, algorithmic details (pseudocode), and convergence claims are deferred to the supplement; conditions for uniqueness/stability of saddle-point selections and subgradient handling at non-smooth points (e.g., CVaR quantiles) could be discussed more explicitly.

Quality
The technical core is sound and well-grounded in convex analysis and likelihood-ratio methods. The static gradient formula is convincing; the SAA consistency result uses standard assumptions that are reasonable for popular coherent risks (CVaR, spectral, semi-deviation). The dynamic theorem appears correct and insightful, though its practical deployment hinges on access to KKT multipliers per state and a robust value function approximator; more detail on function approximation error propagation is desirable. Empirically, evidence is thin.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful examples (CVaR, semi-deviation). Notation is heavy but consistent. However, the main text omits some algorithmic details; including concise pseudocode, a complexity discussion, and clearer guidance for spectral risks would improve readability. The finite-space restriction and needed assumptions should be more prominently stated in the main text.

Originality
High. Extending policy gradients to the entire class of coherent risks and deriving a dynamic PG theorem for Markov coherent risk are novel contributions that neatly unify disparate prior work.

Significance
Potentially significant. Coherent risk measures are widely used in OR/finance, and a unified PG framework can catalyze broader risk-sensitive RL research. The robust MDP linkage is timely. The impact would be strengthened by demonstrating scalability on larger dynamic tasks and by addressing the generative-model limitation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide pseudocode for the static SAA gradient and the dynamic actor-critic, with a complexity analysis.
- Include at least one dynamic MDP experiment with function approximation, comparing to CVaR- and mean-variance-specific baselines.
- Discuss handling of non-unique saddle points and subgradient choices; clarify stability of λ* selection (e.g., CVaR quantiles).
- Elaborate on extending beyond finite spaces (e.g., continuous states with sampling-based envelopes), and on spectral risk implementation details.
- Discuss variance and propose variance-reduction (e.g., baselines, control variates, importance sampling) beyond brief mention in conclusions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical contribution that unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive RL.
- Novel dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with an actionable actor-critic outline.
- Sound use of convex duality and SAA with clear conditions and illustrative special cases.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation; no dynamic experiments to substantiate practicality or performance.
- Requires a generative model and solving many convex programs; scalability/online applicability unclear.
- Key technical details relegated to supplement; finite-space assumption and smoothness conditions may limit applicability.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical novelty and unification merit publication, but the paper would benefit from stronger experimental validation and a clearer path to scalable implementation.
