Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a unified policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning, covering both static risk of trajectory costs and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key technical ingredients are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risks obtained via the dual representation (risk envelope) and the envelope theorem, leading to a likelihood-ratio estimator that combines sampling with solving a convex program to obtain the saddle-point (ξ*, λ*); (ii) an SAA-based estimator with consistency guarantees when the saddle point is not available in closed form; (iii) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk, expressing ∇θVθ(x) as an expectation under ξ*-weighted dynamics with a modified per-stage cost hθ, and an actor-critic procedure that estimates Vθ via robust MDP techniques and then updates the actor using the new gradient formula. The framework recovers known results for CVaR (e.g., Tamar et al., AAAI’15) and mean-variance-like criteria (Tamar et al., ICML’12; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, NIPS’13) as special cases, and connects to the theory of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and dynamic time-consistent risk in MDPs (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone). The robust MDP connection (Delage & Mannor; Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al., ICML’14) is used to motivate the critic.

Quality:
- Theory: The static-gradient result is elegant and general. Leveraging the dual representation ρ(Z)=maxξ∈U Eξ[Z] and the envelope theorem to remove dξ*/dθ is sound under the stated assumptions. The SAA-based estimator with convex programming per batch is justified with a consistency result (Proposition 4.4). The dynamic PG theorem (Theorem 5.2) appears novel and technically interesting; it reduces the risk-gradient to a REINFORCE-like expectation under altered dynamics Pθ(·|x)ξ* and a modified stage cost hθ involving Lagrange multipliers and derivatives of envelope constraints with respect to transition probabilities.
- Assumptions: Some conditions are nontrivial in RL practice: finite probability space (claimed to extend to Lp), existence of strictly feasible points (Slater), differentiability of envelope constraints w.r.t. p, access to ∇θ log P(ω) (or ∇θ log μ) with boundedness, and the ability to compute KKT multipliers reliably. For dynamic risk, sampling trajectories under ξ*-reweighted dynamics requires a generative model and the ability to modify transition sampling—this is stronger than the standard single-trajectory interaction setting.
- Algorithms and computability: The two-step estimator (sampling + convex program) is implementable for many coherent risk measures (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation), but per-iteration costs can be significant if N is large or if the risk envelope is complex (e.g., spectral risk with many weights), as it involves solving an LP/SOCP and extracting multipliers. The actor-critic uses function approximation for Vθ via robust MDP methods, but the main paper omits algorithmic and convergence details.
- Empirics: The single toy trading example demonstrates the flexibility of choosing risk criteria, but does not validate the dynamic component or scaling. There is no study of bias/variance of the estimators, sample complexity, or wall-clock trade-offs relative to CVaR-specific algorithms or robust-MDP baselines.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written, with clear motivation for coherent and time-consistent risk. The static section is self-contained and includes helpful special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation). However, many crucial details (proofs, actor-critic algorithm, convergence analysis, robust-MDP critic) are deferred to the supplement. The dynamic PG theorem introduces nonstandard notation (e.g., derivatives of constraints w.r.t. p) that would benefit from more intuition and a compact pseudocode outline of the two-phase sampling. It would also help to explicitly state the need for a generative model and to discuss feasibility when only on-policy data are available.

Originality:
The unification across the full class of coherent risks via a single gradient formula and an SAA-based estimator is novel and valuable. The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears new and extends the classical policy gradient theorem in a principled way. Recovering known CVaR gradient expressions without strong smoothness assumptions is a nice bonus.

Significance:
Conceptually significant: risk-sensitive RL often picks a single metric (variance, CVaR); this framework covers a broad, practically relevant class with time-consistent dynamics. If adopted, it can catalyze work on risk-aware policy search beyond CVaR and mean-variance. Practically, the per-iteration convex optimization and the requirement to sample under ξ*-reweighted dynamics limit immediate applicability in black-box RL. The lack of substantive experiments and dynamic-risk validation reduces impact for NeurIPS’15 standards. Nevertheless, the theory advances the state of the art and clarifies connections among coherent risk, DRO, and policy gradients.

Pros:
- Unified, principled treatment of coherent risks with clear dual-based gradient formula.
- New policy gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk.
- Consistency of SAA-based gradient estimator; recovers CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases.
- Sound connection to robust MDPs for the critic.

Cons:
- Strong practical assumptions: access to a generative model and the ability to sample under ξ*-reweighted transitions; KKT multipliers required each batch.
- Limited empirical validation; no dynamic-risk experiments; no scalability study.
- Computational overhead of solving convex programs within each gradient step not quantified.
- Important algorithmic/convergence details deferred to the supplement.
- Non-uniqueness of saddle points and potential nondifferentiability are not fully discussed (subgradient vs gradient).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide dynamic-risk experiments (e.g., gridworlds, inventory/queueing) comparing against CVaR-specific and robust-MDP baselines; report sample and runtime efficiency and estimator variance.
- Quantify per-iteration complexity and wall-clock for the convex programming step across different coherent risks; discuss warm-starting and online updates.
- Discuss handling non-unique ξ* and λ* (subgradients), and conditions for differentiability.
- Explore off-policy estimators or importance sampling to avoid requiring reweighted transition sampling; leverage known IS schemes for tail risks (e.g., Bardou et al., 2009).
- Include a concise pseudocode for the actor-critic and ablation on function approximation choices and their induced gradient bias.

Overall recommendation:
Technically strong and original theoretical contribution with a compelling unifying perspective, but empirically underdeveloped and with practical constraints. I lean toward acceptance if space permits a strengthened experimental section; otherwise, this is a borderline/weak accept based on the theoretical advance.
