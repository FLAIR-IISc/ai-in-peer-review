This paper proposes a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This work unifies and generalizes previous research that has largely focused on specific risk criteria like mean-variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors' core contribution is twofold. First, for static coherent risk measures, they derive a novel gradient formula by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope theorem. This leads to a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. Second, they extend this approach to time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk, presenting a new policy gradient theorem and outlining a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant conceptual contribution. By moving from specific risk measures to the general class of coherent measures, the authors provide a powerful and principled framework that is new to the policy gradient literature. This unification is a substantial advance for the field of risk-sensitive RL, allowing practitioners and researchers to apply a much broader set of well-founded risk criteria from finance and operations research. The elegant re-derivation of the CVaR gradient as a special case demonstrates the power of the proposed framework.

*   **Quality:** The theoretical development appears technically sound. The application of duality theory and the Envelope theorem to derive the gradient estimators is rigorous and well-founded. The consistency result for the static risk estimator (Proposition 4.4) further strengthens the paper's technical claims.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, clearly motivating the problem and building from the static to the dynamic case. However, the description of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is quite high-level, with crucial details regarding the algorithm, function approximation, and convergence analysis deferred to the supplementary material. This makes the second major contribution of the paper less self-contained and harder to fully assess from the main text alone.

*   **Weakness in Evaluation:** The empirical validation is the paper's main weakness. The single numerical illustration is a simple portfolio selection problem, not a sequential decision-making task. While it effectively motivates the need for flexible risk measures, it does not validate the performance of the proposed policy gradient algorithms, particularly the actor-critic method, in a meaningful RL environment.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a significant and novel theoretical framework that unifies a large body of prior work in risk-sensitive RL.
*   The technical approach is elegant and sound, providing a general recipe for deriving policy gradients for any coherent risk measure.
*   The work has high potential impact, enabling the use of a much wider class of risk measures in RL.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is minimal and does not sufficiently demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed algorithms in a standard RL setting.
*   The section on the dynamic risk algorithm is underdeveloped in the main paper, lacking crucial algorithmic details.
