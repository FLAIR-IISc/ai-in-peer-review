This paper presents a significant generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, extending previous work that focused on specific measures like variance or CVaR. The authors develop a unified approach, covering both static and time-consistent dynamic risk measures, using sampling and convex programming for static cases and an actor-critic style algorithm for dynamic cases. The contribution lies in formulating a risk-sensitive policy gradient within this coherent risk framework, providing new gradient formulas and algorithms.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions itself within the growing field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, acknowledging prior work on exponential utility, mean-variance, and CVaR. It distinguishes itself by moving beyond specific risk measures to the more general coherent framework, drawing on foundational work in finance and operations research (Artzner et al., 1997). The connection to robust MDPs (Osogami, 2012) and scalable planning techniques (Petrik & Subramanian, 2011) is also appropriately noted.

**Quality (8/10):** The paper appears technically sound. The derivation of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) is the core of the contribution, and while the detailed proofs are in the supplementary material, the presentation in the main paper is clear enough to understand the key steps. The connection to convex programming and the use of the envelope theorem are well-motivated.  The assumption of a well-defined and bounded likelihood ratio (Assumption 4.1) and the smoothness assumptions for the risk envelope (Assumption 2.2) are standard and reasonable. However, the paper relies heavily on these assumptions. A discussion of the limitations when these assumptions are violated would strengthen the work.  The generalization of previous results (e.g., CVaR gradient formula in Section 4.1) demonstrates the power of the proposed framework. The numerical illustration provides some empirical validation, but is somewhat limited. While the theoretical work appears complete, the presented algorithm is complex, and more extensive experimentation showcasing its scalability and performance compared to existing methods is desired.

**Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-written and organized, especially considering the mathematical complexity. The notation is mostly consistent, and the introduction provides a good overview of the motivation and contributions. However, the section on dynamic risk measures (Section 2.2) and the actor-critic algorithm (Section 5) are somewhat dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  The jump between the theoretical development and the numerical illustration is a bit abrupt.  A more detailed description of the experimental setup and a clearer explanation of the observed results would improve clarity.  More illustrative examples throughout the paper (e.g., for Theorem 4.2) would also be helpful.

**Originality (9/10):** The contribution is highly original.  Extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant generalization.  The unified approach and the derived gradient formulas represent a novel and valuable contribution to the field.  While building on existing work in risk-sensitive RL and coherent risk theory, the paper synthesizes these ideas in a new and impactful way.  The connection between coherent risk and robust MDPs, combined with the development of scalable algorithms, further enhances the originality.

**Significance (8/10):** The results are important and have the potential to be widely used by researchers and practitioners. The coherent risk framework provides a principled way to incorporate risk preferences into reinforcement learning problems. The ability to handle both static and dynamic risk measures makes the approach versatile and applicable to a wide range of real-world scenarios, particularly in finance and operations research. The potential for application in areas with model uncertainty (as alluded to in the conclusion) is particularly promising. The authors convincingly argue for the importance of flexibility in risk measure selection. The development of scalable algorithms, although needing further validation, is crucial for practical application.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods.
*   Novel and mathematically sound derivation of gradient formulas.
*   Unified framework covering both static and dynamic risk measures.
*   Potential for broad impact in finance, operations research, and other fields.
*   Well-motivated and clearly positioned within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Algorithm complexity and lack of extensive empirical validation.
*   Heavy reliance on assumptions (likelihood ratio boundedness, smooth risk envelope).
*   Clarity could be improved, particularly in the dynamic risk section.
*   Numerical illustration is limited in scope.



**Overall Assessment:**  I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The technical soundness, originality, and potential impact of the work are compelling. While some improvements in clarity and empirical validation would be beneficial, the paper is of high quality and worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions addressing the clarity concerns and suggesting further empirical investigation.
