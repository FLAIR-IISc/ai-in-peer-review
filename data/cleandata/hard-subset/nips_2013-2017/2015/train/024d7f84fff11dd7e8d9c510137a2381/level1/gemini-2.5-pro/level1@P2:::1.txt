**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning (RL) under risk-sensitive objectives. The key contribution is extending the policy gradient approach to the entire class of coherent risk measures, a standard and axiomatically-justified class of risk measures from finance and operations research. This work unifies and generalizes previous risk-sensitive RL studies that have focused on specific risk measures like mean-variance (Tamar et al., 2013; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, 2013) or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) (Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014; Tamar et al., 2012).

The authors' approach hinges on the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the "risk envelope"). By leveraging this structure and applying the envelope theorem, they derive novel policy gradient formulas. The paper addresses two distinct settings:
1.  **Static Risk:** The risk is applied to the total discounted cost of a trajectory. The authors derive a gradient formula that involves the solution to a convex optimization problem defined by the risk envelope. They propose a general algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate this gradient and show that it can re-derive known gradient formulas for CVaR in a simpler, more general way, as well as derive new ones for measures like mean-semideviation.
2.  **Dynamic Risk:** For time-consistent decision-making, the paper considers dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. It presents a new policy gradient theorem for this class, which is analogous to the standard policy gradient theorem (Sutton et al., 2000) but for a risk-sensitive value function. Based on this theorem, an actor-critic algorithm is proposed. This part of the work connects to and builds upon the insight that dynamic coherent risk in MDPs is equivalent to robust MDPs (Osogami, 2012; Tamar et al., 2014).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the main gradient formulas via the dual representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem is elegant and technically sound. The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 4.2 for static risk and Theorem 5.2 for dynamic risk, are solid contributions. The work appears complete, addressing both the static and dynamic settings. However, the evaluation has some weaknesses. The numerical illustration is a simple bandit problem that showcases the conceptual benefit of choosing an appropriate risk measure but does not stress-test the proposed algorithms on a challenging, sequential decision-making task. Furthermore, the details and convergence analysis of the proposed actor-critic algorithm are deferred to supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the practicality and robustness of the approach for dynamic risk.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides a clear introduction to coherent risk measures and their properties, successfully motivating their use in RL. The distinction between static and dynamic risk is well-explained. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, and the key assumptions are stated explicitly. The structure of the paper, moving from the general static case to specific examples and then to the dynamic case, is effective for building the reader's understanding.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While risk-sensitive RL is an active area, previous work has been largely piecemeal, developing custom algorithms for individual risk measures. This paper is the first to provide a unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risks. This is a significant conceptual leap. The technique of combining sampling with convex programming to estimate the policy gradient for a general risk envelope is novel and powerful. The extension of the policy gradient theorem to dynamic Markov coherent risks is also a novel and important contribution.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is substantial. It provides researchers and practitioners with a principled and versatile toolset for incorporating risk aversion into RL agents. Instead of being limited to a few specific measures, one can now, in principle, apply policy gradient methods to any risk measure that can be described by a convex risk envelope. This opens the door to "risk shaping"—designing problem-specific risk measures that capture desired behaviors, as alluded to by the authors. The connection drawn between coherent risk, robust MDPs, and policy gradients provides a valuable synthesis of different threads in the literature. These ideas are very likely to be built upon by future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Unification and Generalization:** The paper provides a powerful, unified framework for policy gradient methods with coherent risk measures, subsuming and simplifying several previous results. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL.
2.  **Novel Technical Approach:** The use of the dual representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem is an elegant and novel method for deriving policy gradient formulas, which is of independent interest.
3.  **New Policy Gradient Theorems:** The paper introduces new, general policy gradient theorems for both static and dynamic coherent risk, which are fundamental results.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** The framework enables the use of a much broader class of risk objectives in RL, potentially leading to more robust and reliable autonomous agents in high-stakes domains like finance and robotics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiment is on a simple bandit problem, which fails to demonstrate the performance and scalability of the proposed algorithms, particularly the actor-critic method for dynamic risk, on a standard RL benchmark.
2.  **Practicality Concerns:** The general algorithm requires solving a convex program at each gradient estimation step. The computational overhead of this step, especially with a large number of samples, is not analyzed or discussed, raising questions about its practicality compared to specialized algorithms.
3.  **Incomplete Algorithm Details:** Key details and the convergence analysis for the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm are not in the main paper, which prevents a full assessment of that part of the contribution. The actor's sampling requirement (multiple next-state samples from any state) may also limit its applicability.

**Conclusion:**
Despite the limited empirical validation, the theoretical and conceptual contributions of this paper are outstanding. It provides a highly original, elegant, and significant generalization for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the implementation details and empirical demonstration, which do not detract from the core theoretical advance. This work lays a strong foundation for future research and provides a much-needed unification for the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
