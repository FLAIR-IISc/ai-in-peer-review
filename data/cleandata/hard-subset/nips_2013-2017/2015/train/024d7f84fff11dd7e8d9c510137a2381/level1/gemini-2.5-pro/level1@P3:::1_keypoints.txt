- Presents a general policy-gradient framework for reinforcement learning objectives based on coherent risk measures using the dual (risk envelope) representation.
- Derives a static policy gradient formula for discounted total cost via the Envelope Theorem, expressing the gradient in terms of optimal dual variables.
- Recovers known CVaR gradients and derives new formulas (e.g., mean-semideviation); proposes a sampling + convex-programming estimator with consistency guarantees when dual solutions are not analytic.
- Extends the framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures and proves a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard case.
- Proposes an actor-critic style algorithm where the critic solves a robust Bellman equation (risk-sensitive value) and the actor uses worst-case distributions for gradient updates.
- Conceptually novel and unifying: moves from specific risk measures to the full class of coherent risks and links policy-based methods with robust MDP/value-based literature.
- Strong theoretical quality: sound derivations (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) that are non-trivial and interpretable.
- Clear and well-structured exposition with effective illustrative examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation.
- Empirical validation is weak: only a stateless bandit experiment testing static risk; no sequential MDP experiments to support the dynamic algorithm.
- Practicality concerns for the dynamic algorithm: actor requires sampling from MDPs with re-weighted transitions (P(·|x)ξ*), an impractical assumption without a reconfigurable simulator.
- Critic computation is challenging: solving the robust Bellman equation (especially with function approximation) is nontrivial and not integrated into the actor-critic discussion.
- Algorithmic complexity and stability concerns: nested dependency of actor on critic and ξ* raises unanswered questions about stability and sample complexity.
- Computational cost concern for static algorithm: convex program must be solved per gradient estimate and scales with sample size N, raising scalability questions.
- Recommendation: accept for strong, novel theoretical contribution, but the authors should add at least a simple MDP experiment and expand discussion of practical challenges in the final version.
