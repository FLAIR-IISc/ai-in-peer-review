This paper presents a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors address a crucial gap in the literature, which often focuses on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR. By providing a unified framework, they generalize and simplify existing approaches. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly written, demonstrating a strong understanding of both reinforcement learning and coherent risk theory.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [6, 18, 29, 21, 8, 30]). However, unlike these studies that target individual risk measures, this work aims for generality using the well-established concept of coherent risk measures from finance and operations research [2].  The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures [26] to develop new gradient formulas for both static and dynamic cases. For the static case, they present a sampling-based approach combined with convex programming. For dynamic risk, they propose an actor-critic algorithm leveraging value function approximation.  The connection to robust MDPs [19] is also acknowledged.  The work explicitly positions itself as a generalization of existing methods, allowing for their re-derivation within the more general coherent risk framework. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately reflects the relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The most significant strength is the generalization to the whole class of coherent risk measures. This is a substantial advancement over existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is rigorously grounded in mathematical theory, with clear definitions and derivations. The use of the envelope theorem and convex optimization principles is appropriate and well-executed.
*   **Novelty:** The derived gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk are novel and provide valuable tools for risk-sensitive RL.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making complex concepts accessible. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are clear.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed algorithms, while theoretically grounded, also have practical implications, as demonstrated by the numerical example.
* **Connection to Robust MDPs:** Recognizing the link to robust MDPs provides another perspective and potential pathway for leveraging existing algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the algorithms are theoretically appealing, the computational cost of solving the convex optimization problem in the static case and approximating the value function in the dynamic case could be significant for large-scale problems. This is mentioned but could benefit from more detailed discussion.
*   **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 regarding the risk envelope, Assumption 4.1 and 5.1 regarding gradient well-definedness and boundedness). While standard, their impact on the applicability of the method should be further discussed.
*   **Numerical Example:** The numerical example is somewhat limited and focuses on a relatively simple scenario. A more extensive empirical evaluation, perhaps with comparisons to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on benchmark tasks, would strengthen the claims.
* **Limited Discussion of Implementation Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm in Section 5 is high-level. Providing more details about the function approximation method and the specific implementation choices would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in RL – incorporating risk sensitivity – in a novel and general way.
*   The theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported.
*   The paper is clearly written and provides a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The generalization of existing results and the unification of different approaches are significant achievements.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms may limit their applicability to large-scale problems.
*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited.
* The paper assumes a lot of mathematical structure (i.e. smoothness of constraints), making it less broadly applicable.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work is a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The generality of the approach, combined with its theoretical soundness and clarity of presentation, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While the computational complexity and limited empirical evaluation are valid concerns, they do not outweigh the substantial benefits of this work. The authors have made a compelling case for their approach, and I believe it will stimulate further research in this important area.



**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms and potential strategies for mitigating it.
*   Expand the numerical evaluation with more comprehensive experiments on benchmark tasks.
*   Elaborate on the implementation details of the actor-critic algorithm, including the function approximation method.
* Discuss the limitations imposed by the assumptions made and potential directions for relaxing them.
