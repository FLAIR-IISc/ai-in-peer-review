

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures as previous works have done. The authors establish a unified framework that generalizes and extends previous results on risk-sensitive RL, including those for CVaR, variance, and mean-semideviation.

The paper begins by establishing the theoretical foundation of coherent risk measures, referencing Artzner et al.'s seminal work [2] and the representation theorem (Theorem 2.1) that enables their approach. The authors then develop two main contributions: a gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) that enables sampling-based estimation, and a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) that leads to an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk. The paper demonstrates that several previous results are special cases of their framework, such as the CVaR gradient formula in [30] and mean-semideviation in [26].

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors provide clear proofs for their main theorems (in supplementary material) and establish consistency results for their gradient estimators (Proposition 4.4). The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical utility of their framework by showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial trading scenario. The paper carefully acknowledges limitations, such as the need for a simulator for multiple state transitions, and identifies promising future directions.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical exposition is precise, with consistent notation throughout. The authors effectively use examples (Sections 4.1-4.2) to illustrate how their general framework encompasses specific risk measures previously studied in the literature. The connection between coherent risk measures and their representation theorem (Theorem 2.1) is particularly well-explained.

*Originality:* This work represents a substantial theoretical advance. While previous papers have studied risk-sensitive RL for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR in [8, 30], mean-variance in [18, 21]), this paper provides a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors clearly articulate how their approach generalizes and simplifies previous results. This is not merely a combination of existing techniques but a fundamental theoretical unification that advances the field.

*Significance:* The significance of this work is substantial. Risk-sensitive RL is increasingly important for applications where managing variability is crucial (e.g., finance, robotics). By providing a single framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures, the paper enables practitioners to choose the most appropriate risk measure for their specific problem without having to develop new algorithms for each measure. The connection to model uncertainty in the conclusion (Section 7) suggests even broader potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with existing methods beyond citing them. While it correctly identifies previous work as special cases, it would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed empirical comparison showing how their method performs relative to prior approaches for specific risk measures. The numerical example (Section 6) is informative but limited to a simple trading scenario.

*The computational complexity of the proposed methods is not thoroughly analyzed. The paper mentions using convex programming for the static case and function approximation for the dynamic case, but does not discuss how these methods scale with problem size or complexity compared to previous approaches.

*The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) is intriguing but remains largely conceptual. The authors could strengthen the significance of this contribution by providing more concrete examples or initial theoretical results showing how their framework can be used to handle model uncertainty.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro: This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that unifies and generalizes previous work on risk-sensitive RL. The framework is sound, the theoretical results are well-supported, and the practical significance is clear. The paper represents a substantial advance in the field.

*Con: The empirical validation is limited to a single numerical example, and the computational complexity is not thoroughly analyzed. While these are not major flaws, they represent opportunities for strengthening the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical framework is sound, the presentation is clear, and the unification of previous approaches represents a significant advance. The paper meets all NIPS criteria at a high level:

*Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development and empirical validation.
*Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*Originality: The work provides a fundamental unification of risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework.
*Significance: The framework enables practitioners to apply policy gradient methods to a broad class of risk measures, with clear applications in finance, operations research, and safety-critical systems.

The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. It represents a significant theoretical advance that will likely influence future research in risk-sensitive RL. While the empirical validation could be expanded, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper's unifying perspective on risk-sensitive RL is particularly valuable as the field moves toward more flexible and principled approaches to managing risk in sequential decision making.
