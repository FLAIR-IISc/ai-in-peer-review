Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that covers the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) risks. The key technical idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) as a worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope, and derive policy-gradient formulas via a Lagrangian saddle-point and an envelope theorem argument. For static risk, the authors provide (i) a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) that reduces to a likelihood-ratio expectation under the adversarial density ξ*, plus terms involving Lagrange multipliers for envelope constraints; (ii) closed-form instantiations for CVaR and mean-semideviation that recover and generalize previous results (e.g., Tamar et al. for CVaR); and (iii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) scheme that combines sampling with a convex program to obtain ξ*, multipliers, and a consistent gradient estimator. For dynamic risk, they extend the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), relating ∇θVθ(x) to an expectation under ξ*-reweighted dynamics with a per-step surrogate cost hθ(x,a), and propose an actor-critic algorithm: the critic estimates the robust value function using function approximation (building on robust MDP literature, e.g., Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al.), while the actor uses the theorem to estimate gradients.

The work unifies disparate strands: variance-related objectives (Tamar et al., ICML’13), CVaR optimization (Rockafellar & Uryasev; Tamar et al., AAAI; Chow & Ghavamzadeh), exponential risk (Borkar), and dynamic coherent risk / robust MDPs (Ruszczyński; Iancu et al.; Osogami; Chow & Pavone). The numerical illustration shows how different risk measures induce different asset selection policies, underscoring the value of flexibility in risk criteria.

Quality:
- Theory: The static gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) is soundly derived from the dual representation and an envelope theorem; its specialization to CVaR produces the known tail-conditional gradient in a simpler and more general way (works for discrete settings). The SAA gradient estimator with convex programming (Eq. 7–8) is natural; the consistency result (Proposition 4.4) rests on mild assumptions (Slater condition, smoothness of constraints in p, compactness of saddle points). The dynamic policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) is a substantial contribution, correctly handling time-consistent risk via reweighted transitions and yielding an actor-critic recipe. These align with known equivalences between coherent dynamic risk and robust MDPs.
- Assumptions and practicality: The finite probability space assumption simplifies exposition; the authors claim extensions to Lp but omit details. The LR assumptions on ∇θ log P and ∇θ log μ are standard. The approach requires access to a simulator to draw multiple next states per visited state to estimate hθ and the derivatives dfi/dp, dge/dp; this is feasible in simulated environments but limiting for purely model-free, single-trajectory settings. Solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers at every gradient step is computationally heavier than standard PG, though tractable for many envelopes (CVaR, spectral). The dynamic algorithm’s convergence and function-approximation error analysis are deferred to the supplement; in the main text, algorithmic details are sparse.
- Empirics: The experimental evaluation is minimal (a simple asset selection toy), without dynamic MDP experiments or comparisons to strong baselines on standard RL tasks (e.g., CVaR-optimized policies, variance-based methods). This limits evidence of scalability and sample efficiency.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, with clear motivation for coherence and time consistency, and precise notation for envelopes and the Lagrangian setup. The connection to prior work is accurate and fair. However, key algorithmic components (actor-critic details, sampling under ξ*-weighted transitions, computation of dfi/dp and dge/dp from samples) are only sketched; pseudo-code and a more explicit discussion of computational costs and variance reduction would improve reproducibility. The reliance on the supplement for all proofs and convergence details detracts somewhat from self-containment.

Originality:
High. Extending policy gradients to the entire coherent risk class and deriving a general dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is novel and unifying. Recovering CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases and offering a generic SAA+convex programming estimator is an elegant contribution.

Significance:
Moderate to high. A unifying framework for coherent risk policy gradients is likely to influence both theoretical work (risk-sensitive RL, robust control) and practical algorithms in safety-critical RL. The dynamic theorem is particularly valuable. The impact would be strengthened by more substantial empirical validation on larger MDPs and by addressing practical constraints (single-trajectory data, computational overhead).

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually unifying framework covering a broad and important class of risk measures.
- Strong theoretical contributions: general static gradient formula, consistency of SAA estimator, and a new dynamic policy gradient theorem.
- Recovers and simplifies several prior results (e.g., CVaR gradient).
- Clear link to robust MDPs enabling function approximation.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation; no dynamic MDP experiments demonstrating the actor-critic method at scale.
- Practical requirements (convex program per update, simulator with multiple next-state samples, access to KKT multipliers) may limit applicability.
- Many proofs and algorithmic details relegated to the supplement; main text lacks pseudo-code and complexity analysis.
- Finite-space assumption and the need to compute derivatives of envelope constraints with respect to transition probabilities deserve more discussion for continuous or unknown-dynamics settings.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include dynamic MDP experiments with function approximation, comparing to CVaR-only and variance-based baselines, with ablations (SAA vs. analytic ξ*, effect of reweighting).
- Provide pseudo-code, computational complexity, and wall-clock comparisons for the convex programming step and the overall actor-critic loop.
- Discuss variance reduction (e.g., importance sampling for tail events), and strategies for single-trajectory learning.
- Elaborate on extensions beyond finite spaces, and on estimating dfi/dp, dge/dp from samples when dynamics are unknown.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong theoretical paper with clear novelty and potential impact. The main weakness is empirical validation and practical detail. I lean toward acceptance provided NeurIPS values theoretical advances with modest experiments; otherwise, a more extensive experimental section would make the case unequivocal.
