

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning, rather than focusing on specific risk measures like CVaR or variance as previous works have done. The authors develop a unified framework that handles both static and time-consistent dynamic coherent risk measures, generalizing and extending previous results in the field. The core technical contribution is Theorem 4.2, which provides a gradient formula for static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, and Theorem 5.2, which extends the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures. The paper demonstrates that several previous approaches (e.g., for CVaR and mean-semideviation) are special cases of their framework, providing a more elegant and general treatment.

The paper excels in quality, with technically sound theoretical development supported by clear consistency proofs and a well-structured mathematical exposition. The clarity is excellent, with precise definitions, well-organized sections, and a compelling numerical example that demonstrates the practical value of their framework in selecting appropriate risk measures for specific problem characteristics. The originality is strong, as the unified coherent risk framework represents a meaningful advancement beyond previous risk-sensitive RL work that focused on individual risk measures. The significance is high, as the approach provides a flexible and theoretically grounded method for risk-sensitive decision making across various applications, with potential implications for handling model uncertainty in dynamic decision problems.

Some minor weaknesses include the somewhat limited numerical evaluation (only a simple trading example) and the computational complexity of the convex programming steps, which might pose challenges for very large-scale problems. However, the authors appropriately acknowledge these limitations. The paper also appropriately cites relevant prior work, including recent NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., Chow & Ghavamzadeh, NIPS 27; Tamar et al., AAAI 2015).

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a theoretically sound and unified framework for coherent risk measures. It advances the state of the art by generalizing previous specific approaches and offering a more flexible methodology for practitioners. The work is well-suited for NIPS and would be valuable to researchers working on risk-sensitive decision making.

Pro: Unified framework for coherent risk measures, strong theoretical foundation, clear connection to previous work, significant practical implications.
Con: Limited empirical evaluation, computational considerations for large-scale problems.
