

# Peer Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper presents a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing a unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors extend previous work on risk-sensitive RL (which focused on specific risk measures like CVaR, variance, or mean-variance) to a general coherent risk framework, both for static and time-consistent dynamic risk measures. This work builds on the seminal characterization of coherent risk measures by Artzner et al. (1999) and the recent interest in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [18, 29, 21, 8, 30]), while providing a more general theoretical foundation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors establish a clear connection between coherent risk measures and their policy gradient formulation through Theorem 2.1 and subsequent results. The proofs (in supplementary material) are well-structured and follow standard optimization techniques. The paper provides two key algorithmic contributions: (1) a sampling-based gradient estimator for static coherent risk measures that combines convex programming with likelihood-ratio techniques, and (2) a dynamic programming-based policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The consistency result in Proposition 4.4 is particularly valuable. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of the flexibility provided by their framework, showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial trading scenario. The paper carefully addresses both strengths and limitations of their approach, acknowledging that their method requires the risk envelope to be known in explicit form (Assumption 2.2).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results. The connection between coherent risk measures and the proposed gradient formulas is well-explained, with concrete examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) illustrating how previous results emerge as special cases. The distinction between static and dynamic risk measures is particularly well-articulated. The supplementary material provides additional implementation details for the algorithms that would be helpful for replication.

**Originality:** This work is highly original in its approach to risk-sensitive RL. While several papers have explored specific risk measures in RL (e.g., CVaR [8, 30], mean-variance [18, 29]), this is the first to provide a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors demonstrate how previous methods are special cases of their framework, which represents a significant conceptual advance. The use of the risk envelope representation (Theorem 2.1) to derive gradient formulas is particularly novel for RL applications. This represents a meaningful extension beyond the NIPS proceedings from recent years (e.g., the 2014 paper by Chow and Ghavamzadeh on CVaR optimization [8] and the 2015 paper by Tamar et al. on policy gradients with variance-related risk [30]).

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. Risk-sensitive RL has important applications in finance, operations research, and safety-critical systems, where managing variability and tail risks is crucial. The authors' unified framework greatly simplifies the development of risk-sensitive RL algorithms by providing a single approach that can handle any coherent risk measure. This flexibility is demonstrated in the numerical example, where different risk measures lead to different, problem-appropriate policy choices. The paper also makes a compelling case for the importance of risk measure selection in practice, going beyond the usual "risk-neutral" RL paradigm. The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) suggests potential for broader impact in robust decision-making.

**Weaknesses:** The main limitation is the assumption that the risk envelope is known in explicit form (Assumption 2.2), which may not hold for all coherent risk measures or in complex settings where the envelope would need to be estimated from data. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of how this limitation might be addressed in future work would strengthen the paper. The numerical example, while illustrative, is relatively simple and doesn't fully demonstrate the scalability of the approach to large-scale or continuous MDPs. The actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is not fully detailed due to space constraints, which limits the practical implementation guidance.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**

*Pro:*
- Unifies previous risk-sensitive RL work under a single coherent risk framework
- Provides theoretically sound gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk
- Demonstrates how previous methods are special cases of their framework
- Offers practical algorithms with consistency guarantees
- Numerical example effectively shows the importance of risk measure selection

*Con:*
- Requires explicit knowledge of the risk envelope (Assumption 2.2)
- Limited empirical demonstration of scalability to large problems
- Actor-critic details are not fully provided due to space constraints
- Does not compare computational efficiency with existing methods

This paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The unified framework provides researchers and practitioners with a flexible tool for designing risk-sensitive policies across various applications. The technical quality is excellent, and the paper is clearly written with sufficient detail for replication. While the assumption about the risk envelope is a limitation, it is a reasonable one for the class of coherent risk measures they consider, and the authors appropriately acknowledge this constraint. The paper makes a clear case for the importance of risk measure selection in practice, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications where risk preferences vary.

I recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS. It addresses an important problem in RL with a novel theoretical contribution that has both theoretical significance and practical utility. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in risk-sensitive decision making.
