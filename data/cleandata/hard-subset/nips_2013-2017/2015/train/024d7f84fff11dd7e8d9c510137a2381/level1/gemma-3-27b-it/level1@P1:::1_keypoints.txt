- Extends policy gradient methods to the full class of coherent risk measures, filling a gap in risk-sensitive RL literature.  
- Leverages the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to derive new gradient formulas for static and dynamic cases.  
- Proposes a sampling-based convex-programming approach for static risk and an actor-critic algorithm with value-function approximation for dynamic risk.  
- Provides a unified framework that generalizes and re-derives existing risk-sensitive RL methods (e.g., variance, CVaR).  
- Technically rigorous: clear definitions, sound mathematical derivations using the envelope theorem and convex optimization.  
- Novelty: first to present gradient formulas covering the whole class of coherent risk measures.  
- Clarity and presentation: well-written, well-organized, consistent notation, accessible explanations.  
- Practical relevance: algorithms have practical implications and a numerical example demonstrating feasibility.  
- Connects the approach to robust MDPs, offering additional perspectives and potential algorithmic leverage.  
- Computational complexity concern: convex optimization in static case and value-function approximation in dynamic case may be costly for large-scale problems.  
- Reliance on multiple technical assumptions (e.g., risk envelope, gradient boundedness) may limit applicability; their impact needs further discussion.  
- Limited empirical evaluation: numerical example is simple and lacks comparisons to existing benchmarks or algorithms.  
- Implementation details lacking: actor-critic description is high-level with little on function approximation or implementation choices.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a significant RL problem, offers substantial theoretical contributions, and provides a clear, general unifying framework.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential scalability issues, limited experiments, and strong mathematical/smoothness assumptions reduce broad applicability.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept—significant, general, and well-supported contribution likely to stimulate further research.  
- Suggested improvements: detail computational complexity and mitigation strategies, expand empirical benchmarks and comparisons, and elaborate actor-critic implementation and assumption limitations.
