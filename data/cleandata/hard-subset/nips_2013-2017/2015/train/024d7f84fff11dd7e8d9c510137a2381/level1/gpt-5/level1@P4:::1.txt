Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops policy gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static risk over trajectory returns and time-consistent dynamic (Markov) coherent risk. The central idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures as worst-case expectations over a convex “risk envelope” of test densities, and to differentiate the resulting Lagrangian using an envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio identities.

For static risk ρ(Z), the paper derives a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) expressed in terms of a saddle point of a convex program defining the risk envelope. This yields sampling-based estimators for ∇θρ(Z) that combine likelihood-ratio terms with Lagrange multipliers and constraint derivatives. The paper demonstrates that known results emerge as special cases, including the CVaR gradient (recovering and generalizing prior derivations such as Chow et al., NIPS 2014; Tamar et al., AAAI 2015) and a new expression for mean-semideviation. When the saddle point is not analytically available, the paper proposes a sample-average approximation (SAA) procedure that solves an empirical convex program and uses the KKT multipliers to form a consistent gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4).

For dynamic risk, the paper considers Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński 2010; Shapiro et al.) and uses their Bellman fixed-point characterization to prove a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2). The gradient is an expectation under a ξ∗-reweighted transition kernel of a likelihood-ratio term multiplied by a stage-wise “risk-adjusted” cost that depends on the value function and the KKT multipliers of the per-state envelope problem. Building on the robust MDP equivalence (Delage & Mannor 2010; Osogami, NIPS), the paper proposes an actor-critic scheme: a critic approximates the risk-sensitive value function with function approximation (adapting Petrik & Subramanian/UAI; Tamar et al., ICML 2014), while the actor estimates the gradient using trajectories sampled under ξ∗-weighted dynamics.

A small empirical illustration in a portfolio selection toy problem highlights how different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. variance) can induce different policies, motivating the need for a flexible, unified framework.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The static-case derivation is grounded in a well-known dual representation of coherent risk and a standard envelope theorem. Assumptions (finite spaces; Slater condition; smooth dependence of constraints on Pθ; bounded likelihood ratios) are clearly stated and reasonable for many coherent risks (e.g., CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation). The SAA-based estimator’s consistency is plausible under the given conditions; rates are not provided but are consistent with standard SAA.
- The dynamic-case policy gradient theorem aligns with the Bellman characterization of Markov coherent risk and extends known risk-sensitive PG theorems (e.g., exponential utility, variance-related criteria) in a principled way. However, key details (proofs, convergence analysis with function approximation, conditions ensuring ergodicity and boundedness under ξ∗-weighted transitions) are deferred to the supplement. The practicality of sampling under ξ∗-weighted dynamics requires access to a simulator capable of multiple next-state samples and an operational way to implement the reweighted kernel; this is acknowledged but remains a significant implementation constraint.
- The per-batch convex optimization (static) and per-state envelope/KKT computations (dynamic) introduce nontrivial computational overhead that is not analyzed in depth.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized and concise about assumptions and the main theorems. The notation is heavy but standard for the area; the connection to robust MDPs is clearly articulated. Many proofs and the full actor-critic algorithm are in the supplement; the main text adequately conveys the approach but leaves some implementation steps (e.g., generating ξ∗-weighted trajectories) under-specified.
- The numerical evaluation is minimal and does not test the dynamic method.

Originality:
- The paper provides a unified gradient formula and algorithms covering the full class of coherent risks (static and Markov dynamic), generalizing prior work focused on specific risks (variance-related, CVaR, exponential utility) and special dynamics. The dynamic PG theorem for general Markov coherent risk, and the static Lagrangian-based gradient formula applicable via convex programming, appear novel and of independent interest.

Significance:
- The unified framework can influence risk-sensitive RL by enabling practitioners to choose among coherent risks (e.g., spectral) while retaining a common algorithmic structure. The connection to robust MDPs strengthens relevance.
- Practical adoption may be limited by assumptions (finite or well-approximated spaces; explicit risk envelopes; access to simulators permitting multiple samples per state) and computational cost. Broader empirical validation would help establish impact.

Strengths:
- General, theoretically grounded gradient formulas covering broad classes of coherent risks.
- Unification and simplification of several prior results (e.g., CVaR gradient) with fewer smoothness assumptions.
- Consistent SAA-based gradient estimator using convex programming and KKT multipliers.
- Extension to time-consistent dynamic risk with a policy gradient theorem and an actor-critic blueprint.

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical evaluation; no dynamic-risk experiments and no comparisons on standard RL benchmarks.
- Practicality of sampling under ξ∗-weighted transitions is not fully specified; requires strong simulator access.
- Computational overhead of solving envelope problems and extracting multipliers at scale is not analyzed.
- Assumptions (finite spaces; differentiable dependence of constraints on Pθ) may restrict applicability; extensions to continuous spaces are deferred.
- Convergence guarantees with function approximation in the dynamic setting are only sketched.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide experiments for dynamic risk on benchmark MDPs, with comparisons to CVaR-specific and robust MDP baselines.
- Clarify how to implement ξ∗-weighted sampling when only a generative model of P is available; discuss importance sampling or rejection sampling strategies and variance trade-offs.
- Analyze computational complexity and propose scalable solvers or approximate envelopes (e.g., parametric ξ).
- Include ablations on SAA batch size, solver noise, and sensitivity to multiplier estimation.
- Discuss continuous-state extensions and stability/ergodicity conditions under reweighted kernels.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, general theoretical contributions that unify and extend multiple lines of risk-sensitive RL work.
- Clear derivations and consistent estimators with well-motivated assumptions.
- Relevance to time-consistent risk and robust control communities.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient to establish practicality or performance, especially for the dynamic case.
- Implementation feasibility and computational costs are not convincingly addressed.
- Several critical details are deferred to the supplement.

Overall assessment:
A solid and novel theoretical contribution offering a unified framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients with coherent risks. The main limitation is the lack of substantive empirical validation and practical guidance for dynamic settings.
