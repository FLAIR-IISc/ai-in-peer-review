## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a significant extension to risk-sensitive policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning. The authors generalize existing approaches, which typically focus on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR, to the entire class of coherent risk measures – a widely accepted framework in finance and operations research. They tackle both static and time-consistent dynamic risk measures, offering a unified approach. For static measures, they leverage sampling and convex programming, while for dynamic measures, they employ an actor-critic style algorithm with value function approximation.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of previous research in risk-sensitive RL, citing key papers like those by Rockafellar & Uryasev (CVaR optimization), Tamar et al. (CVaR policy gradients), and Ruszczyński & Shapiro (stochastic programming). It correctly identifies the limitations of prior work – their focus on specific risk measures and often, scalability issues – and positions itself as a more general and flexible solution. The connection to robust MDPs (Osogami, 2017) is also appropriately noted.

**Strengths:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its generality. Extending policy gradients to the full class of coherent risk measures is a substantial theoretical contribution. The proposed algorithms, combining sampling, convex programming, and actor-critic methods, appear well-motivated and technically sound. The inclusion of both static and dynamic risk measures broadens the applicability of the work. The numerical illustration, while simple, effectively demonstrates the impact of different risk preferences on policy selection.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical framework is strong, the paper lacks extensive empirical validation. The numerical example is limited in scope and doesn't demonstrate scalability to more complex environments. The discussion of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is somewhat brief, and details regarding convergence analysis and practical implementation challenges are relegated to the supplementary material. The assumption of known risk envelopes (Assumption 2.2) might be restrictive in some real-world scenarios.  A more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with solving the convex programs within the static risk gradient estimation would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradients.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and well-motivated algorithms.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in RL.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation and scalability demonstration.
*   Brief discussion of the dynamic risk algorithm.
*   Potential restrictiveness of the assumption regarding known risk envelopes.

**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical novelty and potential impact of the work are significant. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and a more extensive empirical evaluation, potentially including comparisons to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on benchmark tasks.
