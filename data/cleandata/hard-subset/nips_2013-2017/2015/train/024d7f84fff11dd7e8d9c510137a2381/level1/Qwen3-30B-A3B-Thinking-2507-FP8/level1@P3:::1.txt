

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the class of coherent risk measures. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by generalizing previous work on specific risk measures (such as CVaR, variance, mean-semideviation) to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a sampling-based gradient estimator for static coherent risk using convex programming, and (2) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures that enables actor-critic algorithms.

The paper builds on key theoretical foundations from risk measure theory (Artzner et al. 1999) and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto 1998). It correctly identifies that previous risk-sensitive RL work (e.g., [8, 21, 29, 30]) focused on specific risk measures, while coherent risk measures provide a unified theoretical framework with desirable properties (convexity, monotonicity, etc.). The authors demonstrate how their framework subsumes and generalizes previous approaches, as shown in their examples (Section 4.1-4.2) where their gradient formulas recover known results for CVaR and mean-semideviation.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical foundations with well-constructed proofs for the main theorems (4.2 and 5.2). The use of the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) is particularly elegant and enables the derivation of the gradient formulas.
2. **Unification:** The paper successfully unifies previous work under a single theoretical framework, demonstrating how diverse risk measures can be treated as special cases. This is a significant conceptual advance.
3. **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) is well-motivated and includes a consistency result (Proposition 4.4) that provides theoretical grounding for its use.
4. **Clear Illustration:** The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of the framework by showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial context.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Computational Complexity:** The proposed approach requires solving convex optimization problems for each gradient estimate. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide sufficient discussion on how this scales to large-scale problems. For instance, how does the computational cost compare to alternative approaches like [30] for CVaR? The paper mentions "standard convex programming tools" but doesn't address practical scalability.
2. **Finite Space Assumption:** The theoretical development is restricted to finite probability spaces (Section 2), with only brief mention of extension to Lp-normed spaces. This limits applicability to continuous state-action spaces, which are common in RL. The authors should elaborate on how their approach would extend to continuous settings, as this is critical for real-world applications.
3. **Limited Empirical Comparison:** The numerical example (Section 6) is illustrative but lacks comparison with existing methods. It would strengthen the paper to include a comparison of sample efficiency or computational cost between their approach and prior methods for specific risk measures.
4. **Model Uncertainty Connection:** The conclusion mentions a promising connection to model uncertainty but doesn't develop this insight. A more detailed discussion of how their framework can be used to handle model misspecification would enhance the paper's significance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper successfully unifies previous work under a coherent risk framework, providing a theoretically sound and practically applicable approach. While the computational complexity and finite space assumptions are notable limitations, they are common challenges in risk-sensitive RL and the paper provides a clear path forward.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), presents a novel unification of risk measures (Originality), and has significant potential for future work (Significance). The authors have carefully positioned their work within the literature and correctly identified the gap they're filling.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by providing a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL that generalizes previous approaches. While the computational aspects could be more thoroughly addressed, the theoretical advances are significant and the paper is well-executed. The framework provides researchers with a flexible tool for designing risk-sensitive policies, which is an important step forward in the field.

**Pro Arguments:**
- Unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL
- Rigorous theoretical foundations with novel gradient formulas
- Demonstrated practical utility through numerical example
- Clear connection to existing risk measure theory

**Con Arguments:**
- Limited discussion of computational scalability
- Finite space assumption restricts immediate applicability
- Lacks comparative empirical evaluation with prior methods
- Model uncertainty connection remains underdeveloped
