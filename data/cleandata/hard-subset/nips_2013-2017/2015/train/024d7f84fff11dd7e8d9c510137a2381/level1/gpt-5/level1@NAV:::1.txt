This paper develops policy-gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures, covering both static and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) settings. For static risk, the authors derive a new gradient formula via the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio, yielding sampling-based estimators that combine Monte Carlo with a small convex program over the risk envelope; they show consistency via an SAA argument. The framework recovers known results for CVaR and mean-semi-deviation as special cases. For dynamic risk, they establish a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk by coupling the static gradient with a risk-sensitive Bellman equation, and propose an actor-critic scheme that uses robust-MDP-style value approximation and sampling under ξ-weighted transition kernels. A small trading example illustrates how different risk criteria induce distinct policies. The work connects to Artzner et al. (coherence), Ruszczyński–Shapiro (risk envelopes, SAA), Rockafellar–Uryasev (CVaR), and prior NIPS work on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., mean-variance, CVaR, and actor-critic methods).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound under stated assumptions (finite spaces, Slater condition, bounded likelihood ratios, differentiable envelope constraints). The static estimator’s consistency is well-motivated. The dynamic theorem is plausible and aligns with robust-MDP equivalence. However, key proofs and the full actor-critic algorithm are deferred to the supplement, and there are no finite-sample or high-probability error bounds. Practical issues—computing KKT multipliers, non-unique saddle points, and sampling from ξ-weighted kernels—deserve deeper analysis.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; notation is heavy but consistent. The dynamic section would benefit from pseudocode, a clearer discussion of computational cost per update (convex program + sampling), variance-reduction baselines, and guidance for environments where ξ-weighted sampling is infeasible.

Originality: High. The unified coherent-risk gradient for static risk and the policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appear novel and subsume several prior risk-specific methods.

Significance: Potentially substantial, offering a general toolkit for risk-sensitive RL and tying risk preferences to robust control. That said, empirical validation is limited to a toy example; broader benchmarks (e.g., large MDPs, heavy-tail and rare-event regimes) are needed to demonstrate scalability and impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Unifying, general theoretical contribution (static and dynamic coherent risk).
- Clear connections to and generalization of prior NIPS results.
- Consistency guarantees for the static estimator.

Cons for acceptance:
- Sparse experiments; no large-scale validation.
- Practicality concerns (convex program and ξ-weighted sampling per update).
- Missing finite-sample analysis and some proofs in main text.
