This paper presents a policy gradient approach for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, generalizing prior work that focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR. The authors consider both static and time-consistent dynamic risk measures, offering a unified framework. For static measures, they leverage sampling and convex programming, while for dynamic measures, they utilize an actor-critic style algorithm with value function approximation. The core contribution lies in formulating a risk-sensitive policy gradient within this coherent-risk framework.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing risk-sensitive RL approaches and the benefits of a more general framework based on coherent risk measures. The introduction effectively positions the work within the broader context of risk management in finance and operations research. The problem formulation is precise, and the assumptions are clearly stated. The derivation of the gradient formula for static risk (Theorem 4.2) is a significant contribution, providing a general result that encompasses previous results as special cases. The extension to dynamic risk measures and the actor-critic algorithm (Theorem 5.2) are logical extensions and offer a promising approach for tackling complex problems. The numerical illustration, while limited, demonstrates the potential impact of using different risk measures and reinforces the benefits of the proposed framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The framework applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, offering a significant generalization over existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations of the gradient formulas appear mathematically sound and are supported by relevant theorems and references.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of combining sampling with convex programming for static risk and using an actor-critic framework for dynamic risk within the coherent-risk setting is novel.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the concepts and derivations.
*   **Connection to Robust MDPs:** The connection to robust MDPs is insightful and provides an alternative perspective on the dynamic risk problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The proposed algorithms involve solving convex programs within the gradient estimation loop, which may be computationally expensive for large-scale problems. This concern is mentioned by the authors, but the practical implications are not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Implementation Details:** While the algorithms are described conceptually, the paper lacks detailed implementation guidelines. Issues such as the choice of function approximators for the value function in the dynamic case are not addressed.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is relatively simple and does not demonstrate the scalability or performance of the proposed algorithms on more challenging benchmark problems. The comparative analysis with other risk-sensitive RL methods would be beneficial.
*   **Assumption Dependence:** The theoretical results rely on several assumptions, such as the smoothness of the constraints in the risk envelope. The sensitivity of the algorithms to violations of these assumptions is not explored.
*   **Potential for Sample Inefficiency**: The two-phase sampling procedure in the dynamic risk case could be sample inefficient. More investigation of methods to reduce variance and improve sampling efficiency is needed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.
*   The proposed framework offers a general and principled approach to incorporating coherent risk measures into RL algorithms.
*   The derivations are mathematically sound and well-supported by references.
*   The work opens up new avenues for research in risk-aware decision making.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the algorithms may limit their practical applicability.
*   The limited empirical evaluation does not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The paper lacks detailed implementation guidelines and practical considerations.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong theoretical contribution with the potential to advance the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, the lack of thorough empirical validation and the potential computational challenges raise concerns about its practical impact. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on providing more detailed implementation guidance, conducting more comprehensive experiments on benchmark problems, and discussing the computational complexity of the algorithms in greater detail. A discussion of potential variance reduction techniques to address the sample efficiency concerns in the dynamic case would also be valuable.
