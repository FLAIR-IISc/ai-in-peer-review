This paper presents a compelling generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic. The work builds on a strong foundation of related research in risk-sensitive RL, particularly focusing on variance, CVaR, and mean-variance approaches, and successfully unifies them under the more general coherent risk framework. The core contribution lies in deriving novel gradient formulas suitable for sampling-based approximation, a crucial aspect for scaling to large MDPs.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations, while complex, appear logically consistent and well-motivated. The use of convex duality and the envelope theorem is effectively leveraged. The theoretical grounding, particularly the connection to robust MDPs and the discussion of time consistency, is strong. The assumption regarding the smoothness of constraints (Assumption 2.2) is reasonable and justified by referencing existing literature. The proposition regarding the convergence of the SAA estimator (Proposition 4.4) provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the conditions under which the SAA estimator converges at a reasonable rate. The empirical validation, while present, is limited (Section 6) and doesn’t fully demonstrate the performance benefits of the proposed framework across diverse environments.  It primarily serves to illustrate the potential for different risk preferences to drive different policy choices.  A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, perhaps comparing against existing risk-sensitive RL methods on standard benchmark tasks, would significantly strengthen the paper. It is not entirely clear whether the proposed approach offers substantial computational advantages over existing methods in practice, given the need for solving convex programs at each iteration.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though dense. The notation is standard in the field but can be overwhelming at times. The presentation of the theoretical results is precise but could be made more accessible with more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples. The separation of static and dynamic risk treatments is clear. The authors do a reasonable job of explaining the core concepts of coherent risk measures. However, the connection between the mathematical formalism and practical implications could be further emphasized.  For instance, a clearer explanation of how the choice of the risk envelope impacts the resulting policy would be helpful. The discussion of the actor-critic algorithm in Section 5 is somewhat brief; a more detailed description of the implementation aspects (e.g., function approximation choices, sampling strategies) would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The originality of the work lies in its generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradients to the entire class of coherent risk measures. While previous work has focused on specific risk measures, this paper provides a unified framework, offering greater flexibility and theoretical insights. The derivation of novel gradient formulas is a significant contribution. The connection between the coherent risk framework and robust MDPs is a valuable observation. The proposed actor-critic algorithm extends existing methods to handle dynamic coherent risk.  However, the originality is somewhat tempered by the reliance on existing techniques like convex programming and function approximation.  The novelty primarily resides in the *combination* and *generalization* of these techniques.

**Significance:** The work has the potential to be highly significant. A general framework for risk-sensitive RL is valuable for a wide range of applications, including finance, robotics, and healthcare, where managing uncertainty and avoiding costly failures are critical. The ability to choose different coherent risk measures allows for a more nuanced approach to risk management. The theoretical results provide a strong foundation for future research in this area. If the practical computational challenges can be addressed, this work could lead to the development of more robust and reliable RL agents. The identified connection between risk-shaping and model uncertainty is a particularly promising direction for future exploration.  The ability to incorporate model uncertainty directly into the risk objective is a potentially transformative idea.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a strong theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive RL using coherent risk measures.
*   Offers a generalization of existing methods, increasing flexibility and applicability.
*   Derives novel gradient formulas suitable for sampling-based approximation.
*   Identifies a potentially important connection between risk-shaping and model uncertainty.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation; lacks a comprehensive comparison against existing methods.
*   Computational complexity of the proposed algorithms may be a concern.
*   The paper is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.




**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL.  Addressing the concerns regarding the empirical evaluation and clarifying the computational complexity would significantly strengthen the paper. The potential impact of this work is considerable, and the contribution warrants publication in NIPS.  Specifically, I recommend adding more experimental results, and providing a clearer discussion of the practical limitations of the approach and potential avenues for addressing them.
