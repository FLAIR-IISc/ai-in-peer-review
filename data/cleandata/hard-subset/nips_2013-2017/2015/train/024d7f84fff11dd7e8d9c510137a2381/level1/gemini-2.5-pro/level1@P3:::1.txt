This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning problems with objectives based on coherent risk measures. This work significantly generalizes previous risk-sensitive policy gradient methods, which have typically focused on specific measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors' key insight is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability distributions (the "risk envelope").

The paper makes two main technical contributions. First, for static coherent risk measures applied to a total discounted cost, the authors derive a general policy gradient formula using the Envelope Theorem. This formula elegantly expresses the gradient in terms of the optimal dual variables (the worst-case distribution and Lagrange multipliers) from the dual representation. The paper demonstrates that this general result can recover known gradient formulas for CVaR with a simpler proof and derive new ones for measures like the mean-semideviation. For general coherent risks where the dual solution is not analytical, they propose a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient and prove its consistency.

Second, the paper extends this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. By exploiting the recursive structure of these measures, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one for expected returns. This leads to an actor-critic style algorithm where the critic estimates a risk-sensitive value function (the solution to a robust Bellman equation), and the actor updates the policy using a gradient estimate that depends on this value function and the corresponding worst-case distributions.

This work builds upon and unifies a growing body of literature on risk-sensitive RL. While prior work at NIPS and elsewhere has developed policy gradient methods for mean-variance criteria (e.g., Tamar et al., 2013; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, 2013) and CVaR (Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014; Tamar et al., 2012), this paper is the first to provide a principled approach for the entire class of coherent risk measures. It also connects policy-based methods to the value-based literature on dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs (e.g., Ruszczynski, 2010; Osogami, 2012).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual contribution. It provides a highly elegant and unifying theoretical framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients. By moving from specific risk measures to the general class of coherent risks, the authors provide a powerful toolkit that can be adapted to a wide variety of risk preferences. The use of the dual representation and the Envelope Theorem is novel in this context and provides significant insight.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical derivations appear sound and are a significant advance. Theorem 4.2 provides a general and interpretable formula for the static risk gradient. The extension to dynamic risk in Theorem 5.2, resulting in a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem, is a non-trivial and important result that connects policy-based optimization with the structure of robust MDPs.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation for coherent and time-consistent risk is clear, and the progression from the static to the dynamic case is natural. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation are very effective at illustrating the power and generality of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration, while making an interesting conceptual point about the choice of risk measure, is insufficient to validate the proposed algorithms. It is a stateless bandit problem that only tests the static risk formulation. There are no experiments on a sequential decision-making problem (an MDP with multiple states), which would be necessary to demonstrate the effectiveness and challenges of the proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk. The claims about the practicality of the dynamic risk algorithm are therefore unsupported by empirical evidence.

2.  **Practicality of the Dynamic Algorithm:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is very high-level and defers crucial details to the supplementary material. Several significant practical challenges are not adequately addressed in the main paper:
    *   The actor requires sampling from an MDP with modified transition probabilities (`P(·|x)ξ*`). This is a strong assumption, essentially requiring a simulator that can be re-weighted at will for every state. The paper acknowledges this is a limitation and that extension to a single trajectory is future work, but this significantly narrows the immediate applicability of the method.
    *   The critic's task of computing `Vθ` involves solving a robust Bellman equation, which is itself a challenging problem, especially with function approximation. The paper cites [31] but does not discuss the complexities of integrating this estimation process into the actor-critic loop.
    *   The overall algorithm appears to be a complex nested procedure where the actor depends on the critic's estimate of `Vθ`, which in turn determines `ξ*`, which then alters the dynamics for the actor's gradient estimation. The stability and sample complexity of such a scheme are not discussed.

3.  **Computational Cost of the Static Algorithm:** The general algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex program (Eq. 7) for each gradient estimate. While convex, the size of this problem scales with the number of samples `N`. A brief discussion of the computational scalability of this "inner loop" would strengthen the paper's claims of practicality.

**Summary of arguments:**

*   **For acceptance:** The paper presents a significant, elegant, and unifying theoretical contribution to reinforcement learning. It generalizes a large body of prior work on risk-sensitive policy gradients and opens the door to using a much richer class of objective functions in RL. The theoretical ideas are novel, sound, and likely to be influential.

*   **Against acceptance:** The empirical validation is very weak and does not support the claims made about the more complex dynamic risk algorithm. The practical implementation of the proposed actor-critic algorithm faces significant challenges and assumptions that are not fully discussed or addressed in the main paper.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contribution is strong, novel, and significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS. The unification of risk-sensitive policy gradients under the coherent risk framework is a major conceptual advance that will likely spur future research. While the experimental validation is a clear weakness, it does not invalidate the core theoretical results. The paper provides a solid foundation that others can build upon, both theoretically and empirically. I would strongly urge the authors to include at least a simple MDP experiment and a more detailed discussion of the actor-critic algorithm's practical challenges in the final version.
