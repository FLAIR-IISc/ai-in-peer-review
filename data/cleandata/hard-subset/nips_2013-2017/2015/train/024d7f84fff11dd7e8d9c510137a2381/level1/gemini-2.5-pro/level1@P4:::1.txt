### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems where the objective is a coherent risk measure, rather than the standard expectation of returns. The authors' approach moves beyond prior work that focused on specific risk measures (e.g., mean-variance, CVaR) to provide a unified method applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, which satisfy a set of desirable axiomatic properties.

The core of the contribution rests on the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which states that any such measure can be expressed as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the "risk envelope"). The paper addresses two settings:

1.  **Static Risk:** For a static coherent risk applied to a random variable (e.g., the total discounted cost of a trajectory), the authors derive a novel formula for the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2). This formula is derived using the Envelope Theorem on the Lagrangian of the dual representation problem. The resulting gradient expression involves the optimal solution and Lagrange multipliers of a convex program. Based on this, they propose a general gradient estimation algorithm that combines sampling (to form an empirical distribution) with convex programming (to solve the corresponding Sample Average Approximation problem). They demonstrate that this framework elegantly recovers and generalizes recent results for CVaR and provides a new gradient formula for the mean-semideviation risk.

2.  **Dynamic Risk:** The paper extends this framework to time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. The authors derive a corresponding policy gradient theorem for this dynamic setting (Theorem 5.2), which relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. This theorem forms the basis for a proposed actor-critic algorithm, where the critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function and the actor updates the policy using a sampling-based estimate of the new gradient formula.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness)**

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical development of the paper is strong and appears technically sound. The application of the Envelope Theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk is an elegant and powerful technique that provides the central results. For the static case, the derivation is complete, and the consistency of the proposed SAA-based gradient estimator is established (Proposition 4.4). The recovery of the CVaR gradient formula from [30] under weaker assumptions serves as strong validation for the correctness and generality of the framework.

*   **Weaknesses:** The treatment of the dynamic risk case in Section 5 is less complete than the static case. The full actor-critic algorithm, including the two-phase sampling procedure and the method for value function approximation, is not detailed in the main paper, with the reader referred to supplementary material. This makes it difficult to fully assess the practical implementation and soundness of the dynamic algorithm based on the main text alone.

**Clarity**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly positioning the work relative to prior art and explaining the concepts of coherent and dynamic risk. The theoretical development follows a logical progression from preliminaries to the static case and then to the dynamic case. The use of CVaR and mean-semideviation as running examples effectively illustrates the power and generality of the main gradient formula (Theorem 4.2). The numerical experiment, while simple, clearly communicates the practical value of having the flexibility to choose among different risk measures.

*   **Weaknesses:** The conciseness of Section 5, while likely necessitated by space constraints, leaves key details of the proposed actor-critic algorithm ambiguous. A more thorough description of the sampling scheme for the actor update would have improved the paper's self-containedness.

**Originality**

*   **Strengths:** The work is highly original. The central contribution—a unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures—is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance over previous literature that treated risk measures on a case-by-case basis. While the paper leverages established theory on coherent risk measures from operations research (e.g., [26]), its application to derive policy gradient formulas in an RL context is a novel and insightful contribution. This approach not only generates new algorithms but also provides a more fundamental understanding of existing ones.

**Significance**

*   **Strengths:** The paper's contribution is significant. It provides researchers and practitioners with a principled and extensible toolkit for risk-sensitive RL, freeing them from the limitations of a few pre-defined risk criteria. The ability to tailor the risk measure to a specific problem, as argued in the numerical illustration, is a powerful feature that could broaden the application of risk-sensitive methods to real-world problems. The unification of several disparate results under a single theoretical framework is a valuable contribution that clarifies the structure of risk-sensitive policy gradients.

*   **Weaknesses:** The practical significance may be tempered by the computational cost of the proposed method. The general algorithm requires solving a convex optimization problem as a subroutine for each gradient estimation step. The scalability of this approach, particularly with a large number of samples `N`, is not discussed or evaluated and could be a bottleneck in practice.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel, elegant, and unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients.
*   The technical contribution is sound and general, subsuming and simplifying several previous results.
*   The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and accessible.
*   The work has high potential significance, enabling the use of a much broader class of risk objectives in RL.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The proposed algorithm for the dynamic risk case is not fully specified in the main paper, making a complete evaluation difficult.
*   Potential computational concerns regarding the inner-loop convex optimization are not addressed.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are significantly stronger. The theoretical contribution is substantial and represents a clear advance for the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.
