- Proposes a unified policy-gradient framework covering the full class of coherent risk measures (static and time-consistent dynamic/Markov coherent risks).
- Key technical idea: use the dual representation of coherent risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope, derive gradients via a Lagrangian saddle-point and envelope theorem.
- Static contribution: general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) that reduces to a likelihood-ratio expectation under adversarial density ξ* plus Lagrange-multiplier terms.
- Static instantiations: recovers and generalizes known results for CVaR and mean-semideviation, including discrete settings.
- Provides an SAA scheme combining sampling with a convex program to compute ξ*, multipliers, and a consistent gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4).
- Dynamic contribution: extends policy-gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), yielding gradients as expectations under ξ*-reweighted dynamics with a per-step surrogate cost hθ.
- Algorithmic recipe: an actor-critic approach where the critic estimates a robust value function via function approximation and the actor uses the derived theorem to estimate gradients.
- Theory quality: derivations are sound; dynamic theorem is a substantial contribution aligning with robust-MDP equivalences.
- Assumptions/practicality: finite probability-space assumption simplifies exposition; LR conditions standard; claims about Lp extensions lack details.
- Practical limitations: requires a simulator to draw multiple next states per visited state and to compute derivatives dfi/dp, dge/dp — limiting for single-trajectory model-free settings.
- Computational cost: solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers at every gradient step is heavier than standard policy gradient, though tractable for common envelopes (e.g., CVaR).
- Empirics: experimental evaluation is minimal (simple asset selection toy), no dynamic MDP experiments or strong baseline comparisons, limiting evidence of scalability/sample efficiency.
- Clarity: well written with precise notation and fair prior-work connections, but key algorithmic components are only sketched and many proofs/details are relegated to the supplement.
- Originality: high — novel and unifying extension of policy gradients to the entire coherent risk class and a new dynamic policy gradient theorem.
- Significance: moderate-to-high; likely to influence theory and practice in risk-sensitive RL, but impact would be strengthened by more empirical validation and practicality solutions.
- Pros for acceptance: conceptually unifying framework, strong theoretical results (static and dynamic gradients, SAA consistency), and recovery/simplification of prior results.
- Cons against acceptance: limited empirical validation, practical requirements that may hinder applicability, lack of pseudo-code/complexity analysis in main text, and finite-space assumptions needing more discussion.
- Suggested improvements: add dynamic MDP experiments with function approximation and baselines, provide pseudo-code and complexity/wall-clock comparisons, discuss variance reduction and single-trajectory strategies, and elaborate on extensions beyond finite spaces.
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper with clear novelty and potential impact; leans toward acceptance if NeurIPS values theory with modest experiments, otherwise needs more extensive empirical work.
