- Proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures, covering static trajectory risk and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.  
- Key technical contributions: a general static gradient formula via the dual representation and envelope theorem, a likelihood-ratio estimator requiring solving a convex program for the saddle point, an SAA-based consistent estimator, and a dynamic PG theorem plus actor-critic using a robust-MDP critic.  
- Recovers known results (CVaR, mean-semideviation) and connects to coherent risk, DRO, and robust MDP literature.  
- Static-gradient theory is elegant and sound under stated assumptions; SAA estimator has a proved consistency result.  
- The dynamic policy-gradient theorem is novel and reduces the gradient to an expectation under ξ*-weighted dynamics with a modified stage cost.  
- Assumptions are strong/nontrivial in practice: finite probability space (extension claimed), Slater condition, differentiability of envelope constraints, access to ∇θ log P(ω) with boundedness, and reliable KKT multiplier computation.  
- Dynamic-risk approach requires sampling under ξ*-reweighted transitions (a generative model), stronger than standard single-trajectory RL.  
- Two-step estimator (sampling + convex program) is implementable for many risks but per-iteration computational cost can be high for large N or complex risk envelopes.  
- Actor-critic uses function approximation and robust-MDP techniques, but algorithmic details and convergence analysis are largely omitted.  
- Empirics are limited to a single toy trading example; no dynamic-risk validation, scalability study, or analysis of estimator bias/variance and sample/runtime trade-offs.  
- Presentation is generally clear; static section is self-contained, but many proofs and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement and dynamic notation needs more intuition and pseudocode.  
- Originality: unifying coherent risks under one gradient formula and a new dynamic PG theorem are novel and valuable contributions.  
- Significance: conceptually important for risk-sensitive RL, but practical applicability is limited by computational and sampling assumptions and the lack of empirical validation.  
- Pros: principled unification, novel dynamic PG theorem, SAA consistency, and a sound connection to robust MDPs for the critic.  
- Cons: strong practical assumptions (generative model, reweighted sampling, KKT multipliers), limited experiments, unquantified convex-program overhead, deferred algorithmic/convergence details, and insufficient handling of saddle-point non-uniqueness/nondifferentiability.  
- Suggestions: add dynamic-risk experiments vs CVaR/robust baselines; quantify per-iteration complexity and wall-clock; discuss warm-starts/online updates for convex solves; address non-unique ξ*/λ* (subgradients); explore off-policy/importance-sampling alternatives; include concise actor-critic pseudocode and ablations.  
- Overall recommendation: technically strong and original with important theoretical advances; borderline/weak accept as-is due to empirical and practical limitations, but acceptable if experiments and practical details are strengthened.
