This paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, encompassing both static and time-consistent dynamic formulations. The authors successfully generalize previous work focused on specific risk measures like CVaR, offering a unified framework and providing new gradient formulas suitable for sampling-based approximation. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and the technical arguments appear sound.

The core idea is to leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) and the envelope theorem to derive tractable gradient expressions. This allows for the application of policy gradient methods even when analytical solutions are unavailable, which is often the case in complex RL settings. The extension to dynamic risk measures, using an actor-critic approach, further enhances the applicability of the framework. 

The paper effectively situates its contributions within the existing literature, referencing relevant work in risk management, stochastic programming, and reinforcement learning. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and identify potential areas for future research, such as improving convergence rates with importance sampling. The numerical illustration, while relatively simple, effectively demonstrates the impact of different risk preferences on policy selection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The extension to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant advance, providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivations appear rigorous and build upon established results in convex optimization and reinforcement learning.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the subject matter.
*   **Practicality:** The sampling-based gradient estimation algorithms are well-suited for large-scale problems where analytical solutions are intractable.
*   **Connection to Robust MDPs:** The link between dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs, mentioned in the conclusion, highlights a promising avenue for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Clarity:** The notation “ξPθ” in Theorem 2.1 could be clarified. Aligning it with the notation used in Theorem 6.6 of Ruszczynski and Shapiro [26] might enhance readability.
*   **Minor Typo:** A minor typo exists on line 141 ("risk enevlop[e]").
*   **Tractability Discussion:** The discussion regarding tractability could be refined. While dynamic programming for Markov coherent risk can be computationally expensive, the statement implies inherent intractability of MDPs *with* Markov risk measures. The primary tractability challenges stem from the policy parametrization and the dimensionality of the state space, rather than the risk measure itself.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is limited in scope and could benefit from more extensive evaluation on benchmark problems. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods.
*   The technical contributions are significant and address an important gap in the literature.
*   The clarity of writing and organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.
*   The potential impact of the work on risk-averse RL is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The extension, while valuable, is incremental. The core idea builds upon existing concepts and doesn’t represent a radical departure from previous work.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited, making it difficult to fully assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This paper represents a solid and important contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors have successfully generalized existing results and provided a unified framework for applying policy gradient methods to a broad class of coherent risk measures. The minor weaknesses identified above can be easily addressed with revisions, and the potential impact of the work justifies its inclusion in NIPS. I encourage the authors to address the notational clarity and tractability discussion points and consider expanding the empirical evaluation in a future version of the paper.
