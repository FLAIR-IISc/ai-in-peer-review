## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper tackles the important problem of extending policy gradient methods to a broad class of coherent risk measures, moving beyond the commonly studied variance or CVaR. The core contribution lies in a unified derivation of policy gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risks, offering a significant generalization of existing work. The authors present a novel formula for static risk gradients suitable for sampling-based approximation, and provide a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures. This is a valuable contribution to risk-sensitive decision making, particularly given the widespread acceptance of coherent risk measures in fields like finance.

The strength of the paper resides in its theoretical rigor and the generality of its approach. The derivation, leveraging the duality representation of coherent risk measures, is well-motivated and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. The connection to robust MDPs for dynamic risk is also insightful. The paper builds upon existing literature in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [6, 18, 29, 30]) and appropriately positions itself within that context. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical framework is compelling, the absence of experimental results – even simple comparisons to existing methods like CVaR using the proposed sampling-based gradient estimation – significantly limits the assessment of its practical utility. The section on dynamic risk policy gradients feels particularly underdeveloped, lacking algorithmic details and experimental evaluation, making its practicality unclear. The authors acknowledge this limitation, and the sheer breadth of the work (attempting to cover both static and dynamic cases, and various theoretical aspects) within the page limit does detract from clarity. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel and unified theoretical framework; generalization of existing risk-sensitive RL methods; potential for broad applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of empirical validation; limited detail and evaluation of dynamic risk component; potentially too ambitious in scope for the given page limit.

**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful contribution. However, the lack of comprehensive experiments prevents a full assessment of its practical value. I recommend acceptance with the strong encouragement to either significantly expand the empirical evaluation or to focus the work on static risk measures, allowing for more detailed analysis and validation. Splitting the work into two papers might also be a viable path forward.
