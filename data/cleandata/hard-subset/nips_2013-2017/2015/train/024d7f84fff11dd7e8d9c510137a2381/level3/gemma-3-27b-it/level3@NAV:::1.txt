## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper proposes a unified framework for policy gradient algorithms applied to reinforcement learning problems with objectives based on coherent risk measures. The authors extend existing risk-sensitive RL methods (focused on specific measures like CVaR) to the broader class of coherent risks, addressing both static and dynamic scenarios. A key contribution is a novel formula for static coherent risk gradients, enabling analytical derivation for some measures and a sampling-based algorithm for others, with proven consistency. They also provide policy gradient expressions for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

This work builds upon the growing area of risk-sensitive RL, drawing connections to prior work on variance-based approaches [18, 29], CVaR optimization [8, 30], and robust MDPs [19]. The authors correctly position their contribution as a generalization, offering a more principled approach via coherent risk theory [2]. The presentation of the theoretical results – particularly Theorem 4.2 and 5.2 – appears sound, and the connection to stochastic programming and robust optimization is well articulated.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical completeness and the unified derivation of policy gradients for a broad class of risk measures. This offers a valuable contribution to the field, providing a strong foundation for future research. The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations seem rigorous.

However, the paper suffers from a significant weakness: a complete lack of empirical validation. While the authors demonstrate the application with a numerical example in Section 6, it’s limited to illustrating the risk-averse policy selection based on different risk measures and does not quantitatively compare the performance of their algorithms (analytical vs. sample-based gradients) or with existing risk-sensitive RL methods. The absence of experimental results makes it difficult to assess the practical impact and scalability of their approach. Additionally, the section on dynamic risk policy gradients (Section 5) feels relatively high-level and lacks algorithmic details and experimental validation, leaving its practicality unclear.

The authors acknowledge the limitation of eight pages leading to a lack of clarity, and this is apparent. Splitting the work – focusing either on static risk measures with comprehensive empirical evaluation or developing a fully functional and tested dynamic risk algorithm – would significantly strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound unified framework for coherent risk measures.
*   Useful theoretical results (Theorems 4.2 & 5.2) extending policy gradient methods.
*   Well-written and rigorous mathematical derivations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation.
*   Limited practical demonstration of dynamic risk algorithms.
*   Potentially overly ambitious scope for the page limit.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting and potentially broadly applicable contribution. However, the lack of comprehensive experiments prevents a full assessment of its practical value. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit with thorough empirical validation and a more focused scope.
