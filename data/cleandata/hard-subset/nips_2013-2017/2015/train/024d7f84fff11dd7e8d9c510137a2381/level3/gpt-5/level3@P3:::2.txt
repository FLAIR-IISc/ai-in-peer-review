Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient estimators for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning objectives defined by the general class of coherent risk measures, covering both static and dynamic (time-consistent) settings. For static risk, the authors exploit the risk-envelope representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński and Shapiro) and the envelope theorem to derive a convenient gradient formula that combines likelihood-ratio sampling with a small convex program whose KKT multipliers appear in the gradient. They give closed-form specializations for CVaR and mean-semideviation, and a sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator with a consistency result for general envelopes.

For dynamic risk, they adopt Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński), derive a policy-gradient theorem that expresses the gradient as an expectation under ξ-weighted transitions with a stage-wise term hθ that depends on the value function and Lagrange multipliers, and propose an actor-critic algorithm. The critic leverages the equivalence to robust MDPs (Delage and Mannor; Petrik and Subramanian; Tamar, Mannor, Xu) to approximate the risk-sensitive value function. The actor uses two-phase sampling to generate trajectories under ξ-weighted dynamics. A toy finance experiment demonstrates the flexibility to choose different risk criteria and the resulting risk-averse behavior.

The work unifies and generalizes several strands: exponential utility and mean-variance PG (Borkar; Tamar et al.), CVaR optimization in RL (Rockafellar and Uryasev; Chow and Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al.), and time-consistent dynamic risk in MDPs (Ruszczyński). The contribution is to lift risk-sensitive PG beyond specific objectives to the entire coherent class with a single derivation and corresponding algorithms.

Strengths
- Clear, principled unification: Extends risk-sensitive PG from specific risk measures (variance, CVaR) to all coherent risks via the risk-envelope representation; the framework is clean and connects naturally to robust MDPs.
- Technically sound derivations: The static gradient formula via envelope theorem and likelihood ratios is elegant; the dynamic policy-gradient theorem is a natural extension of the classic PG theorem under Markov coherent risk.
- Concrete algorithms: Provides practical estimators for static risks (closed-form for CVaR and mean-semideviation; SAA+convex program for general cases) and an actor-critic method for dynamic risk.
- Clarity and completeness: Writing is above average; equations and assumptions are explicit; related work is well covered and connections to prior NIPS work are clear.
- Nontrivial originality: Generalizing to coherent risk (including dynamic, time-consistent measures) is a meaningful step beyond prior risk-specific PG methods.
- Significance: Addresses an important problem—risk-sensitive RL—offering a toolkit adaptable to different risk preferences and distributions.
- Empirical illustration, though simple, effectively shows how risk preference changes decisions (e.g., mean-semideviation versus standard deviation with heavy tails).

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is minimal: a single, static toy finance example; no dynamic MDP experiments, no comparisons to robust MDP planning or specialized CVaR/mean-variance RL baselines on standard benchmarks.
- Computational overhead: Each gradient estimate for general coherent risks requires solving a convex program (and retrieving KKT multipliers); scalability and wall-clock costs are not benchmarked.
- Sampling assumptions: The actor-critic requires the ability to simulate multiple next-states per state and to sample under ξ-weighted transitions; many RL settings only permit single on-policy trajectories.
- Finite-sample behavior: While consistency is proved for the SAA estimator, there are no variance analyses, finite-sample error bounds, or discussions on sensitivity to rare events beyond noting importance sampling as future work.
- Assumptions and practicalities: The finite outcome space assumption (with claimed extensions), smoothness and Slater-type conditions on envelopes, and uniqueness/stability of saddle points are standard but not stress-tested empirically.
- Limited guidance on choosing envelopes: The flexibility to encode risk via envelopes is powerful, but the paper does not provide practical design guidance for real tasks.

Quality
- Theoretical development is solid and connects established tools (risk envelopes, envelope theorem, PG theorem, robust MDP equivalence). Claims are well supported by analysis. Algorithmic sketches are reasonable, though some implementation details (e.g., critic approximation class, stability, and exploration) are deferred.

Clarity
- Well organized and readable. Assumptions are stated; the linkage between static and dynamic results is clear. Including a summarized pseudo-code for the actor-critic in the main text would further help reproducibility.

Originality
- Sufficient and meaningful. The step from specific risks to general coherent risks, and the dynamic PG theorem for Markov coherent risk, are nontrivial extensions that unify prior work.

Significance
- High potential impact. Provides a general-purpose approach to risk-sensitive RL with clear pathways to handle different risk attitudes and distributional characteristics; likely to influence subsequent work in risk-sensitive and robust RL.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add dynamic MDP experiments with function approximation; compare against robust MDP baselines and CVaR/mean-variance RL on standard tasks.
- Provide computational benchmarks (per-iteration cost, scaling with sample size and constraint count).
- Explore variance reduction (importance sampling) empirically for tail-sensitive risks.
- Clarify how to implement ξ-weighted transition sampling in environments without a generative model; discuss off-policy corrections.
- Include pseudo-code for the actor-critic and practical details (feature choices, stepsizes, convergence diagnostics).

Arguments for acceptance
- Unified, technically sound framework for coherent risk PG (static and dynamic).
- Clear connections to and simplification of multiple prior results; nontrivial generalization.
- Practical algorithms with consistency guarantees; good clarity and motivation.
- Important problem with broad relevance in RL, finance, and operations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Very limited empirical validation; lack of dynamic tasks and comparative baselines.
- Unclear scalability and practicality due to reliance on inner convex programs and special sampling.
- No finite-sample or variance guarantees; limited guidance for practitioners on envelope design.

Recommendation
- Leaning accept. Despite modest experiments, the conceptual unification and technical contributions are strong and of clear relevance to NIPS. Strengthening the empirical section would make this a clear accept.
