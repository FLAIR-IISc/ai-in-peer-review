## Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper tackles the important problem of extending policy gradient methods to a broad class of coherent risk measures, moving beyond the typical focus on specific measures like variance or CVaR. The authors achieve this by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures and deriving analytic solutions and gradient estimation algorithms for both static and dynamic cases. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and organized, making it accessible even to those not deeply familiar with risk-sensitive optimization. 

The contribution is significant. By providing a unified analysis covering a wide range of coherent risk measures, the authors offer a powerful framework for risk-aware reinforcement learning. The derivation of the gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) is a key strength, as it allows for sampling-based estimation, making the approach scalable to large problems. Extending this to the dynamic case with an actor-critic algorithm further expands the applicability of the work. The paper effectively builds upon existing literature in robust MDPs and stochastic programming, providing a valuable synthesis of ideas. The numerical illustration, though brief, demonstrates the potential for different risk preferences to lead to divergent policies.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are compelling, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. A more in-depth comparison of the proposed gradient estimation algorithm with existing methods (e.g., those tailored to CVaR) on a wider range of benchmark problems would strengthen the paper considerably.  Specifically, the variance and computational cost of the proposed estimation should be compared to existing ones.

I have a few specific requests and questions. First, the preliminaries (Section 2) are a bit dense, and the roles of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ are not immediately clear. A more intuitive explanation of these concepts would be beneficial. Second, Equation 2 appears to be a standard convex envelope definition. It's not clear why the authors claim it is "more restrictive" than Theorem 2.1; clarification here is needed. Third, it would be valuable to briefly comment on how the analysis generalizes to continuous distributions, even if a full treatment is beyond the scope of the paper. Finally, the role of smoothness of constraint functions in proving Theorem 4.2 is not explicitly stated. Mentioning this connection would improve the completeness of the theoretical discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with novel and important results.
*   Unified framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients.
*   Clear and accessible writing style.
*   Significant contribution to the field of risk-aware RL.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation.
*   Some minor clarifications needed regarding the preliminaries and technical details. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with compelling results. While the proofs should be carefully verified, the work represents a significant advance in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. Addressing the empirical evaluation and clarifying the noted points would further strengthen the paper and make it an even more valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions.
