- Proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for the full class of coherent risk measures, covering static and dynamic (Markov, time-consistent) risks.
- Main contributions: (1) general static policy-gradient formula via dual representation, (2) sampling + convex-program estimator with consistency proof for cases without analytic dual solutions, (3) policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk and an actor-critic algorithm outline.
- The static gradient derivation (Theorem 4.2) generalizes and simplifies known formulas (e.g., CVaR) and is theoretically elegant.
- Provides a sample-average-approximation algorithm for static risks lacking closed-form dual solutions and proves its consistency (Proposition 4.4).
- The dynamic risk result links the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function, forming the basis for a proposed actor-critic method.
- Strong originality and significance: novel unification of policy gradients with axiomatic coherent risk theory, offering broad flexibility for risk-sensitive RL.
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound and well-supported for the static case.
- Motivation and the simple numerical example effectively illustrate why choice of risk measure matters.
- Major weakness: almost no empirical validation—single stateless example does not test algorithms in sequential RL settings.
- Paper attempts too broad a scope in limited space, harming clarity and depth, especially in later sections.
- Dynamic coherent risk section is underdeveloped and not self-contained; algorithmic details and analysis are deferred to the supplement.
- Analytical gradient approach has limited applicability since closed-form Lagrangian saddle points exist for few risk measures; sample-based estimator performance is not demonstrated.
- Missing important empirical comparisons (e.g., analytic vs. sample-based CVaR estimators) in the main paper weakens confidence in practical utility.
- Recommendation: theoretically strong and potentially influential but insufficiently validated experimentally; would be stronger if focused on a narrower contribution with thorough experiments.
