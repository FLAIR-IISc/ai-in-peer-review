This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-averse reinforcement learning that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures, covering both static objectives over returns and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) risk measures. For static risk, the key idea is to use the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk to express the objective as a convex program and then differentiate its Lagrangian via the envelope theorem combined with the likelihood-ratio trick. This yields a generic sampling-based gradient estimator and a sample-average approximation (SAA) variant with a consistency result. The framework recovers and simplifies known special cases (e.g., CVaR and mean-semi-deviation) and provides a route to handle spectral risks and others whose envelopes satisfy mild conditions. For dynamic risk, the paper presents a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk by coupling the static gradient with a Bellman recursion, yielding an actor-critic scheme that leverages the equivalence between dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs for value-function estimation. The contribution unifies prior risk-sensitive PG results that focused on particular risk measures (e.g., variance, CVaR) and connects to robust MDP planning.

Relation to prior work: The static part generalizes prior CVaR-specific gradient results (e.g., Tamar et al.; Chow & Ghavamzadeh) and variance-related policy gradients (Tamar et al., ICML’12). The dynamic result extends risk-sensitive actor-critic ideas (Borkar; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, NIPS’13) and builds on Ruszczyński’s dynamic coherent risk and the robust-MDP equivalence (Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian). The main novelty is the unified envelope-based derivation and its sampling/convex-programming instantiation.

Quality: The theoretical development is sound and careful. The gradient formula for static coherent risks (via KKT multipliers at a saddle point) is clear and the SAA-based estimator has a well-stated consistency theorem under reasonable assumptions (compactness/Slater, continuity of constraints). The dynamic PG theorem is plausible and consistent with the robust-MDP value function characterization. Proof sketches and references are appropriate. The empirical section is minimal but illustrative.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and generally easy to follow. The derivations reuse familiar policy-gradient and convex duality tools, aiding readability. Minor notation issues remain (see detailed comments).

Originality: Conceptually, the extension from specific risk measures to the coherent class is incremental—it largely reuses the dual characterization plus envelope theorem—but it is nontrivial and useful. The dynamic PG theorem for Markov coherent risk in an actor-critic form is a valuable unification and may simplify deriving future risk-sensitive PG algorithms.

Significance: Moderate. The work provides a general recipe practitioners can instantiate for different coherent risks without re-deriving problem-specific gradients. The approach could influence risk-aware RL by making coherent risks more accessible. However, practical impact is currently limited by computational overhead (solving a convex program and extracting multipliers per update) and by the need for a simulator for multiple next-state samples in the dynamic setting. Experimental validation is light.

Pros:
- Unified, conceptually clean derivation of policy gradients for the entire coherent risk class (static and dynamic).
- Recovers and simplifies known results (e.g., CVaR gradient) and provides new estimators (e.g., mean-semideviation) with a consistency guarantee for the general SAA approach.
- Extends the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk and proposes an actor-critic framework that leverages robust MDP techniques.
- Well written, with assumptions stated and connections to prior work made explicit.

Cons:
- Practical tractability is not fully addressed: per-iteration convex programs with KKT multipliers may be costly for high-dimensional or continuous outcome spaces; discussion of solver complexity and warm-starting would help.
- The dynamic algorithm requires sampling multiple next states per visited state; the single-trajectory setting is not handled.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to a small toy example; no scaling evidence or comparison of variance/efficiency against tailored CVaR/variance baselines.
- Some assumptions (finite probability spaces; differentiability of envelope constraints) may restrict immediate applicability, and the extension to Lp spaces is deferred.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Theorem 2.1 notation ξPθ is confusing: it reads like a product of a density and a measure. Consider adopting the notation used in Shapiro et al. (Thm. 6.6 in [26]) and writing the envelope as a set of densities ξ ∈ U(Pθ) with expectations Eξ[Z] defined via Radon–Nikodym weights, or explicitly denote the reweighted kernel Pθ^ξ.
- Please correct the typo on line 141: “risk enevlop[e]”.
- Clarify the discussion of tractability in the dynamic setting: Markov coherent risk MDPs admit dynamic programming via robust Bellman operators and are tractable in principle; the difficulty arises from function approximation/large state spaces and from the need to solve the inner maximization (envelope) repeatedly. Making this distinction explicit will help readers.
- Make explicit early on that the convex program and its KKT multipliers must be computed at each θ (and at each state for the dynamic case). Discuss practical strategies (e.g., warm starts, parametric convex optimization sensitivity, structure for spectral/CVaR envelopes) and the cost relative to standard PG.
- For the actor-critic, define precisely the ξ-weighted transition dynamics and the sampling procedure, and discuss bias introduced when Vθ is approximated. A brief variance analysis or guidance on mini-batch sizes for the two-phase sampling would be valuable.
- Consider adding at least one larger experiment (e.g., continuous control with spectral risk or CVaR) demonstrating scaling and showing the impact of risk choice on policies, plus wall-clock and sample efficiency comparisons.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a minor but solid and useful generalization that unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients under coherent risk, with clean theory and reasonable assumptions. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying notation/tractability would further improve the paper.
