- Proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures (static and dynamic).

- Derives a new static-gradient formula via the Lagrangian saddle-point and likelihood-ratio/envelope theorem, yielding analytic gradients for CVaR and mean-semideviation.

- Presents a sampling-plus-convex-programming (SAA) estimator for cases without closed-form saddle points, with a consistency proof under standard conditions.

- Introduces a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk and an actor-critic style algorithm leveraging the robust MDP connection.

- Theoretical development is elegant, technically sound, and generalizes prior risk-sensitive PG methods.

- Closed-form Lagrangian saddle points exist only for some risks; otherwise the method needs per-batch convex programs and access to KKT multipliers.

- Exposition assumes a finite probability space; claimed extension to Lp spaces is not detailed.

- Dynamic algorithm requires a simulator that can produce multiple next-state samples and support sampling from ξ-weighted transitions.

- Empirical validation is weak: only a small static illustration in the main paper and no experiments for the dynamic setting.

- No main-text comparison between analytical and SAA-based gradients (e.g., for CVaR); supplement reliance limits reproducibility.

- Paper is generally well written, but the ambitious scope for eight pages leaves the dynamic section high-level and defers key algorithmic details to the supplement.

- Originality is high: unified derivation across coherent risks and a novel dynamic policy-gradient theorem.

- Potential significance is high due to principled, general framework and robust MDP connections, but practical impact is unclear without stronger experiments.

- Strengths: unified theory, new analytic gradients, consistent sampling-based estimator, dynamic extension, clear links to prior work and robust MDPs.

- Weaknesses: reliance on convex-program solving and KKT extraction in practice, limited empirical characterization of variance/bias/computation, lack of dynamic experiments, and reduced clarity from covering many topics.

- Suggested improvements: add thorough empirical comparisons (analytical vs SAA) for static risks, at least one dynamic experiment, pseudocode, complexity/KKT implementation guidance, and details on infinite-space extensions.

- Arguments for acceptance: strong, general theoretical contribution and novel dynamic theorem likely to influence future work.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation, dynamic section lacks algorithmic detail and experiments, over-reliance on supplementary material.

- Overall: solid and potentially impactful theoretical contribution but requires substantially more empirical evidence or a narrowed focus to be practical and reproducible.
