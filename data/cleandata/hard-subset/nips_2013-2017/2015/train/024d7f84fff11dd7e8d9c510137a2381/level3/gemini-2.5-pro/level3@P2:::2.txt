This paper presents a principled and general framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors extend previous work, which has largely focused on specific risk measures such as variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The core of the contribution is a new formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure, derived by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risks and the Envelope Theorem. This result enables a general policy gradient algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. The paper further extends this result to the dynamic setting by proposing a new policy gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, leading to a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The work unifies and generalizes several recent results in risk-sensitive RL, providing a single, powerful framework for a broad set of problems.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical derivations, particularly the main gradient formula in Theorem 4.2, are well-founded and elegant. The paper carefully builds upon established results in stochastic programming (the dual representation of coherent risk) and reinforcement learning (the likelihood-ratio trick) to arrive at its main conclusions. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis, and the proposed algorithms for both static and dynamic risk problems are a logical consequence of these findings. The authors are clear about the assumptions required for their methods to work (e.g., Assumption 2.2 on the risk envelope, access to a simulator for the actor-critic method).

The main weakness in this area is the experimental validation. While the numerical illustration effectively demonstrates the flexibility and importance of the proposed framework, it is limited to a simple, static multi-asset trading problem. The experiment does not test the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk case, which is a significant part of the paper's contribution. Demonstrating the dynamic algorithm on a sequential decision-making benchmark would have made the experimental support much stronger.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for moving beyond specific risk measures to the general class of coherent risks is compelling and clearly stated. The authors provide the necessary background on coherent and dynamic risk measures before systematically developing their gradient formulas, first for the static case and then for the more complex dynamic case. The mathematical exposition is precise and easy to follow. The examples provided for CVaR and Mean-Semideviation effectively illustrate how the general framework recovers and simplifies known results, which greatly aids the reader's understanding.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the field. While risk-sensitive RL is an active area of research, previous work has been somewhat fragmented, with different papers proposing specialized algorithms for specific risk measures. This paper's primary novelty lies in providing a unified approach for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The idea of combining the dual representation of coherent risk with the Envelope Theorem to derive a policy gradient formula is a novel and non-trivial technical step. This approach not only generalizes prior work but, as demonstrated with the CVaR example, can also lead to simpler and more general proofs of existing results.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are of high significance to the reinforcement learning community. The framework provides researchers and practitioners with a powerful and flexible toolkit for tackling risk-sensitive problems. Instead of being constrained to a handful of predefined risk measures, users can now select or even design coherent risk measures tailored to their specific application needs, knowing that a principled method for policy optimization exists. The work addresses an important problem—managing risk in decision-making under uncertainty—in a more general and theoretically grounded way than previous research. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon in future work on robust and risk-aware learning agents.

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and elegant unification of policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures, subsuming several previous results.
*   The theoretical contributions are sound, and the derived gradient formulas represent a significant advance for risk-sensitive RL.
*   The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow.
*   The work is highly significant, as it equips the community with a general and flexible tool for a crucial class of problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is limited to a simple, static problem and does not validate the proposed algorithm for dynamic risk measures in a sequential decision-making environment.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical contribution is strong, original, and significant. The limited experimental section is a minor drawback in an otherwise excellent paper. This work provides a meaningful and substantial advance to the state of the art in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.
