This paper presents a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, encompassing both static and time-consistent dynamic formulations. The work builds upon recent advances in risk-sensitive RL, which often focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR, and provides a unifying framework with broader applicability, particularly in fields like finance and operations research where coherent risk measures are widely accepted. The authors correctly identify the importance of coherent risk measures and articulate a clear motivation for extending existing policy gradient techniques to this more general setting.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it accessible to researchers familiar with reinforcement learning and optimization. The introduction provides a comprehensive overview of the field, effectively relating the work to existing literature, including references to Artzner et al.’s seminal work on coherent risk measures and previous RL studies focused on specific risk objectives.  The mathematical derivations appear technically sound, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the theoretical foundations of coherent risk and policy gradient methods.  A particularly valuable aspect is the derivation of a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, coupled with an algorithm leveraging convex programming for efficient approximation, and the extension to dynamic Markov coherent risk with an actor-critic approach.

A key strength of this work lies in its generality. The authors don't restrict themselves to a particular risk measure, instead providing a framework applicable to the entire class of coherent measures. This flexibility is highlighted in the numerical illustration, where different risk preferences lead to varying investment strategies, demonstrating the impact of choosing the appropriate risk criterion.  The generalization of existing results is also noteworthy. The approach allows for the re-derivation of previously established results in a more general and simpler manner.  

However, there are a few areas where the presentation could be improved. The notation “ξPθ” in Theorem 2.1 is indeed somewhat unconventional and potentially confusing. Aligning it with the notation used in Theorem 6.6 of [26], as suggested, would enhance clarity and facilitate comparison with related work. A minor typographical error on line 141 ("risk enevlop[e]") should be corrected. Furthermore, while the paper effectively explains the benefits of coherent risk measures, it could benefit from a clearer distinction between the inherent tractability of Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) and potential computational challenges.  The tractability issues primarily stem from policy parametrization, particularly when dealing with large state spaces and the need for function approximation, rather than being an intrinsic limitation of employing Markov coherent risk measures within the MDP framework.

Regarding originality, the work doesn’t present a radically new idea but rather a powerful generalization and unification of existing techniques.  This is, however, a valuable contribution, as it provides a more comprehensive and flexible approach to risk-sensitive RL. The significance of the work lies in its potential to influence the design of robust and reliable decision-making systems in a wide range of applications where risk management is critical.  The demonstrated ability to adapt the policy gradient framework to handle coherent risk measures opens up possibilities for applying RL to complex real-world problems with sophisticated risk preferences.  The numerical illustration reinforces this potential by demonstrating how different risk measures can lead to distinctly different policies. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Generality:** The work extends policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations are mathematically rigorous and build upon a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of real-world problems.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Extends Existing Work:** The results generalize previously established results for specific risk measures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Nature:** The contribution is largely a generalization of existing techniques, rather than a fundamentally new approach.
*   **Minor Notational Issue:** The notation "ξPθ" could be improved for clarity.
*   **Potential for Clarification:** The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the sources of tractability challenges.



**Overall, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.** The paper presents a solid and important extension of existing results in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors have addressed a significant gap in the literature by providing a unifying framework for handling coherent risk measures. Addressing the minor points regarding notation and clarification would further enhance the quality and impact of this work.
