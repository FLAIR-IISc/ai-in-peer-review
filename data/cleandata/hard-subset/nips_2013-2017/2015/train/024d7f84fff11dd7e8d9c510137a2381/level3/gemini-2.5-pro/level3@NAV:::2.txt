This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods by extending them to the entire class of coherent risk measures. Building on prior work that developed algorithms for specific measures like mean-variance (Tamar et al., 2012) or Conditional Value-at-Risk (Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014), the authors provide a general approach for both static and time-consistent dynamic coherent risks. The core contribution is the derivation of novel policy gradient theorems that leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures. These theoretical results naturally lead to the development of a sampling-based policy gradient algorithm for static risks and an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risks.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Quality and Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The problem is clearly motivated, and the derivations, which connect the envelope theorem to the policy gradient formulation, are elegant and follow logically. The clarity is well above average for a paper of this technical depth.
**Originality and Significance:** The primary strength lies in its originality and significance. While the proposed algorithms are natural extensions of prior work, the generalization to the broad class of coherent risk measures is a non-trivial and important contribution. It provides researchers and practitioners with a principled and flexible framework for incorporating a wide spectrum of risk preferences into reinforcement learning, moving beyond ad-hoc choices of risk measures. This work meaningfully advances the state-of-the-art in risk-sensitive RL.

The main weakness of the paper is the limited empirical evaluation. The experiments are confined to a simple, static financial trading problem. While this example effectively illustrates the algorithm's ability to produce different risk-averse behaviors depending on the chosen risk measure, it does not demonstrate the scalability or performance of the proposed actor-critic algorithm in a complex, dynamic environment. The paper would be strengthened by testing the approach on standard RL benchmarks. Furthermore, the actor-critic algorithm's requirement for a simulator to sample multiple next-state transitions could limit its applicability.

In summary, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and address an important problem in reinforcement learning. The unification of policy gradient methods under the coherent risk framework is a significant achievement. Despite the limited experimental results, the paper's quality, clarity, and novelty are high. I strongly recommend acceptance.
