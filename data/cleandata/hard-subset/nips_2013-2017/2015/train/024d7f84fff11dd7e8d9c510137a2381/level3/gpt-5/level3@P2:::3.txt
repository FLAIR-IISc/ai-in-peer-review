Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies risk-averse reinforcement learning with both static and time-consistent dynamic risk objectives. Using the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro), the authors derive: (i) a general policy-gradient formula for static coherent risk measures, together with sampling-based estimators that combine likelihood-ratio methods with a small convex program to recover the risk-envelope saddle point; and (ii) a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone), yielding an actor-critic style algorithm that estimates value functions under robust/ξ-reweighted dynamics and uses them to estimate policy gradients. The results unify and generalize prior work focused on specific risks (mean-variance, CVaR, exponential utility) and connect to robust MDPs (Delage & Mannor; Tamar et al.; Petrik & Subramanian). The static CVaR gradient recovers and simplifies Tamar et al. (AAAI’15), and the dynamic result extends the standard policy gradient theorem to the Markov coherent setting.

Quality:
- The main derivations appear correct and rest on standard, sound tools (dual representation, envelope theorem, likelihood ratio). Assumptions (finite spaces, Slater condition for the envelope, bounded score functions) are clearly stated and reasonable.
- The static estimator includes a consistency result via SAA and KKT multipliers; the dynamic theorem is well-motivated via the risk-sensitive Bellman equation and the robust-MDP equivalence.
- The empirical section is illustrative but limited (static toy trading example); no dynamic experiments are shown in the main text. More empirical validation would strengthen the paper.

Clarity:
- The paper is well-written and well-organized; proofs are deferred appropriately. The intuition behind coherent risk and time consistency is clearly conveyed.
- Notational issue: in Theorem 2.1, the use of “ξ Pθ” is confusing; please align notation with Theorem 6.6 in [26] (e.g., denote the set of densities ξ ∈ U(Pθ) and explicitly write expectations Eξ[Z] with respect to ξ dPθ).
- Minor typo: “risk enevlop[e]” on line 141.
- Clarify that the main tractability challenges arise from policy parametrization and sampling in large MDPs; the dynamic programming formulation with Markov coherent risk is, in principle, tractable for small MDPs (the intractability is due to scale, not the measure itself).

Originality:
- Conceptually, the extension from specific risks (CVaR/variance) to the full class of coherent risks via the dual representation and envelope theorem is incremental but useful. The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is a neat and natural generalization.
- The unified framework and the combination of sampling with a convex inner problem (to recover ξ and multipliers) are well-motivated and general.

Significance:
- Provides a general recipe to obtain gradients for a broad family of risk criteria commonly used in finance/OR. This is valuable for practitioners who wish to tailor risk measures to applications.
- Potential impact is moderated by practical considerations: inner convex problems and per-state KKT multipliers in the dynamic setting, requirement of a simulator for multiple next-state samples, and limited empirical validation. Still, as a methodological contribution, it can catalyze follow-up work and adoption.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, correct, and unified treatment of risk-sensitive policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures (static and dynamic).
- Generalizes several prior results (e.g., CVaR gradient, mean-variance) in a simpler and more principled way.
- Provides consistency guarantees for the static estimator and a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty: primarily a careful generalization of known ideas (duality + envelope + LR gradient) rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.
- Experimental validation is minimal and does not demonstrate dynamic risk results at scale.
- Practicality in large-scale settings hinges on solving inner convex problems and estimating per-state multipliers/ξ*, which may be costly.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Theorem 2.1: revise notation to avoid “ξ Pθ”; explicitly denote the risk envelope as a set of densities U(Pθ) and write ρ(Z) = supξ∈U(Pθ) Eξ[Z].
- Line 141: fix typo “risk envelope.”
- Section 3: clarify that the intractability claim refers to the dependence on θ and large state spaces; small MDPs with Markov coherent risks are solvable by DP (as in [24]).
- Static SAA estimator: provide complexity estimates for the inner convex program (variables/constraints in terms of N, |E|, |I|) and practical guidance for common envelopes (CVaR, spectral risk).
- Dynamic actor-critic: clarify how ξ* and KKT multipliers are computed per state in practice with function approximation; discuss stability when Vθ is approximate and the sensitivity of hθ to envelope gradients dfi/dp.
- Discuss variance reduction (e.g., baselines/advantage functions) for the dynamic gradient; can the theorem admit control variates without bias?
- Provide at least one dynamic benchmark (even in the supplement) demonstrating the actor-critic with Markov coherent risk on a moderately sized MDP.

Questions for the authors:
- How does the method scale when the envelope has many constraints (e.g., spectral risk with fine discretization)? Any heuristics to amortize the inner solve across minibatches?
- Can the dynamic method be made model-free (single trajectory, no simulator) via importance sampling for ξ-weighted transitions?
- Are there conditions under which the inner saddle point admits a closed form beyond CVaR (e.g., certain spectral measures)?

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. This is a minor but solid and useful extension that unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients under coherent risk measures, with correct theory and clear exposition. The empirical section is thin, and the novelty is incremental, but the contribution is likely to be valuable to the community.

Reviewer confidence: High.
