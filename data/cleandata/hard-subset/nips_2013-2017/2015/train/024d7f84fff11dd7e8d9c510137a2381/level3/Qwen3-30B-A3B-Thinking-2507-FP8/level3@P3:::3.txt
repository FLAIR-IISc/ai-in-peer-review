

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a unified approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures like CVaR or variance as in prior work. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static coherent risk measures and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures, providing a general framework that encompasses previous results as special cases. The main contributions include a new gradient formula for static risk that enables sampling-based approximation, an algorithm for gradient estimation using sampling with convex programming, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that supports actor-critic style algorithms.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the technical content accessible to the RL community. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, with the key representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) providing the foundation for the gradient derivations. The authors effectively demonstrate how their approach generalizes and simplifies prior results for specific risk measures like CVaR and mean-semideviation through two detailed examples. The numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of choosing appropriate risk measures for different problem contexts.

The paper excels in technical quality: the main results appear correct, with well-supported theoretical derivations and a clear connection to prior work in finance and operations research. The authors acknowledge limitations appropriately, noting that their approach requires a simulator for sampling multiple state-transitions, which is a reasonable constraint for practical applications.

Regarding clarity, the paper is well-structured and easy to follow. However, there are a few specific issues that should be addressed: (1) The notation "\xi P_\theta" in Theorem 2.1 is confusing; the authors should adopt the notation used in Theorem 6.6 of [26] (which uses P_ξ) for consistency; (2) There is a typo on line 141 ("risk enevlop[e]"); (3) The paper should clarify that tractability issues arise from policy parametrization, not from Markov risk measures themselves (MDPs with Markov risk measures are tractable).

The originality of the work is moderate: the extension from specific risk measures to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a natural generalization, but it's a meaningful contribution that unifies previous approaches. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by showing how specific risk measure results are special cases of their general framework.

The significance of this work is substantial. By providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL, the paper offers practitioners flexibility in designing risk preferences that suit both their risk preferences and problem-specific properties. The approach is practical, as it provides sampling-based algorithms that can be applied to large-scale MDPs. The numerical example demonstrates how different risk measures can lead to different, and sometimes counter-intuitive, policy choices, highlighting the importance of risk measure selection.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that makes a meaningful extension to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The extension from specific risk measures to the class of coherent risk measures is minor but important, as it provides a more general and flexible framework. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the literature.

Pro for acceptance: The paper provides a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL that generalizes and simplifies prior work, with clear practical implications for practitioners. The writing is clear and well-organized, and the technical content is sound.

Con for acceptance: The extension from prior specific-risk results is technically modest, though the practical value of having a unified framework is significant.

This paper represents a minor but important extension to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors have successfully demonstrated how their framework encompasses previous results while providing a more general approach to risk-sensitive decision making. I recommend acceptance.
