This paper presents a novel and unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The core contribution is to generalize previous work, which has largely focused on specific risk measures like mean-variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), to a principled and axiomatic class of measures. The authors tackle two settings: static risk, where the objective is a coherent risk measure of the total discounted cost, and dynamic risk, which uses time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. For the static case, they derive a new policy gradient formula and propose a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. For the dynamic case, they develop a corresponding policy gradient theorem and an actor-critic style algorithm. The work is well-situated within the literature, building upon the foundational theory of coherent risk measures and connecting it to modern policy gradient and actor-critic methods in RL.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's primary strength lies in its successful unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. By leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), the authors derive elegant and general gradient formulas (Theorem 4.2 for static risk and Theorem 5.2 for dynamic risk) that subsume several previously published results as special cases. This provides a clear and powerful theoretical foundation for a wide range of risk-sensitive problems.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations appear sound, and the problem formulation is clear and logical. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 on the risk envelope, Assumption 4.1 on the policy). The proposed algorithms are natural consequences of the theoretical results. The consistency result for the general gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) further strengthens the technical contribution. The work is a complete piece, presenting both the theoretical framework and the resulting algorithms for both static and dynamic settings.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for using coherent risk measures is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the necessary preliminaries are presented concisely. The progression from the static risk case to the more complex dynamic risk case is logical and easy to follow. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrate how the general framework can be applied to recover or derive new, useful gradient formulas.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While policy gradient methods and coherent risk measures are established concepts, their combination into a general, practical RL framework is a novel and significant contribution. The key technical novelty lies in using the envelope theorem and the dual representation of coherent risk to derive a sampling-friendly gradient estimator. This approach is non-trivial and provides a new perspective that unifies disparate results in the field. The paper correctly positions itself relative to prior work, showing how it generalizes existing methods.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. It addresses the important problem of managing risk in sequential decision-making in a more principled way than has been done previously. By providing a general toolkit for any coherent risk measure, this work empowers practitioners to select or design risk measures that are appropriate for their specific problem, rather than being confined to a few standard options. The numerical illustration, though simple, effectively demonstrates the importance of this flexibility, showing how a mean-standard-deviation objective can lead to counter-intuitive behavior for heavy-tailed distributions, while a more suitable coherent risk measure (mean-semideviation) performs as expected. This framework is likely to be built upon by future researchers in risk-sensitive RL and robust decision-making.

The main weakness of the paper is the limited experimental evaluation. The numerical illustration is a static, single-step portfolio selection problem. While it effectively makes its conceptual point about the flexibility of risk measures, it does not validate the proposed algorithm for the dynamic, multi-step setting (the DRP). The actor-critic algorithm developed in Section 5 is a key contribution, but its empirical performance on a standard RL benchmark is not demonstrated. Demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on a more complex sequential decision-making task would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel, elegant, and unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients.
*   The contributions are technically sound, original, and of high significance to the RL community.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   Generalizes and simplifies several previous results, advancing the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical validation is limited to a simple, static problem and does not test the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk setting.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a clear and important contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical framework is a significant step forward, and despite the limited experiments, the quality, originality, and significance of the work warrant its acceptance. The weakness in the experimental section can be addressed in future work and does not detract from the paper's core theoretical value.
