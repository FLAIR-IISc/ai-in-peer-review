This paper introduces a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The authors' central idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, where any such measure can be expressed as the maximum expected value over a convex set of probability densities, known as the risk envelope. This formulation allows for the development of gradient-based optimization methods applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, unifying and extending previous work that focused on specific instances like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) or mean-variance criteria.

The paper presents two main contributions. First, for the static risk setting, it derives a novel, general formula for the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2) by applying the Envelope Theorem to the Lagrangian of the dual optimization problem. This result leads to a general gradient estimation algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming, which is necessary when the saddle point of the Lagrangian cannot be found analytically. Second, for the dynamic case, the paper extends this analysis to time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. It presents a new policy gradient theorem for this setting (Theorem 5.2) that relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function, forming the basis for an actor-critic algorithm. The work successfully places prior results, such as the policy gradient for CVaR [30], within its more general framework.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. By focusing on the entire class of coherent risk measures, the authors provide a principled and powerful framework that subsumes many prior ad-hoc approaches. This is a significant theoretical contribution that could standardize the approach to risk-sensitive RL and encourage the exploration of a wider variety of risk measures tailored to specific applications.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is elegant and appears sound. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk measures is the key insight, and its combination with the Envelope Theorem to derive the gradient is a novel and powerful technique in this context. The resulting gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are the core technical achievements and represent a substantial advance.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and accessible. It builds logically from the preliminaries on coherent risk to the static case and then to the more complex dynamic case. The inclusion of examples like CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively demonstrates how the general theory applies to concrete, well-known risk measures, making the abstract concepts easier to grasp.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The main weakness of the paper is the lack of a thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed algorithms. The numerical illustration in Section 6 serves as a good motivational example for using different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation), but it does not evaluate the performance of the gradient estimation algorithm itself. A more compelling experimental section would:
    *   Compare the sample efficiency, bias, and variance of the general SAA-based gradient estimator (Section 4.3) with specialized analytical estimators (e.g., for CVaR) on a benchmark problem.
    *   Demonstrate the proposed actor-critic algorithm on a non-trivial sequential decision-making problem.
    *   Provide insight into the computational overhead introduced by solving the convex program at each gradient estimation step.
    Without this, the practical utility of the proposed method remains undemonstrated.

2.  **Clarity in Preliminaries:** While generally clear, the preliminaries could be improved. The distinction and interplay between the underlying probability measure $P_{\theta}$ parameterized by the policy and the re-weighting density $\xi$ from the risk envelope could be made more explicit for readers not deeply familiar with this area. A brief sentence clarifying that $\xi$ is a density function that modifies the original measure $P_{\theta}$ to define a "worst-case" distribution within the risk envelope $U$ would be helpful.

3.  **Assumptions and Scope:**
    *   In Assumption 2.2, the paper defines the risk envelope $U(P_{\theta})$ using a specific convex programming formulation. It is stated that this is justified because coherent risk envelopes are convex and compact. However, is this formulation more restrictive than the general definition in Theorem 2.1? Or is it simply a way to represent any such convex set computationally? A clarification would be valuable.
    *   The paper focuses on finite probability spaces and mentions that results can be extended to $L_p$ spaces. A brief discussion on the key challenges or modifications required for continuous state/action spaces and continuous distributions would significantly strengthen the paper and broaden its perceived applicability. For instance, would the convex program in the SAA method become infinite-dimensional, and how would that be handled?

4.  **Connection Between Assumptions and Results:** The proof of Theorem 4.2 relies on the smoothness of the constraint functions $g_e$ and $f_i$ with respect to the probability measure $P_{\theta}$, as stated in Assumption 2.2. It would be beneficial to explicitly mention in the main text how this smoothness property enables the application of the Envelope Theorem, thereby better connecting the assumptions to the main theoretical result.

### Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a compelling and significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures is a novel and important result that generalizes a large body of previous work. The theoretical derivations are elegant and technically sound. The primary drawback is the lack of empirical validation for the proposed algorithms, which makes it difficult to assess their practical performance. Despite this, the theoretical significance of the work is high enough to warrant acceptance. The paper is likely to be influential and inspire future research into both the theory and application of a broader class of risk measures in RL.
