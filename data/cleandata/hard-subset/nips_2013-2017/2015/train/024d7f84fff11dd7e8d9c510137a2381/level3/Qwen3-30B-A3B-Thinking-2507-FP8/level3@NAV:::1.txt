

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, extending previous work on specific risk measures like CVaR and mean-variance to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive a new formula for static coherent risk gradients that enables analytic derivation for many common risk measures, and develop a general sampling-based algorithm with consistency guarantees. They also provide expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures, generalizing prior results.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical novelty and unification of risk-sensitive RL. The key contribution of Theorem 4.2 provides a unified approach to computing policy gradients for coherent risk measures, which generalizes several previous results. The consistency proof for the sampling-based estimator (Proposition 4.4) is technically sound and addresses an important gap in the literature. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (Section 7) represents a valuable conceptual contribution.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses that prevent a strong recommendation. The most critical issue is the lack of comprehensive empirical validation. While the authors mention an "empirical comparison" for CVaR in supplementary materials, the main paper lacks any experimental results comparing analytical versus sampling-based gradients, or evaluating performance across different risk measures. The dynamic risk section is particularly underdeveloped, offering only a high-level description without algorithmic details or experiments, making the practical applicability unclear. The paper attempts to cover both static and dynamic risk measures within eight pages, resulting in insufficient depth for either topic.

The paper is well-written but attempts too many contributions for its length. The absence of experimental comparison between analytical and sampling-based approaches for risk measures like CVaR (which the authors claim can be handled analytically) is particularly problematic. The authors' suggestion to "split the work or focus on static risk measures" is well-founded.

While the theoretical contributions are significant and potentially broadly applicable, the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation prevents a full assessment of the practical value. The paper would benefit from focusing on static risk measures to allow for more detailed analysis and thorough experimental validation, rather than attempting to cover both static and dynamic cases with limited space.
