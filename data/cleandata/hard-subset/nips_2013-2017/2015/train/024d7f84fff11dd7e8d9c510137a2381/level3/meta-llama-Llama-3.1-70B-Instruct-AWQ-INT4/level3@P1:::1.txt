This paper proposes a novel approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending the policy gradient method to the whole class of coherent risk measures. The authors provide a unified framework for both static and dynamic risk measures, generalizing and extending previous results.

The main contribution of the paper is the formulation of the risk-sensitive policy-gradient under the coherent-risk framework. The authors derive a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that is convenient for approximation using sampling. They also provide an algorithm for the gradient of general static coherent risk that involves sampling with convex programming and a corresponding consistency result. Furthermore, they present a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, relating the gradient to a suitable value function and a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the technical details. However, the paper attempts to cover too many issues in a limited number of pages, which can make it difficult to follow at times. The section on dynamic risk policy gradients is particularly high-level and lacks algorithmic details and experiments, making it unclear how practical the approach is.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its unified derivation, which is a valuable contribution to risk-sensitive decision making. The authors also provide expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures, which is a novel and interesting result.

However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the sample-based approximation, which is an important aspect of the proposed approach. Additionally, there is no experimental comparison between sample-based and analytical gradients, which would be helpful in understanding the trade-offs between the two approaches.

Overall, the paper is interesting and potentially broadly applicable, but the lack of comprehensive experiments prevents a full assessment of its contributions. It may be beneficial to split the work or focus on static risk measures to allow for more details and comprehensive empirical evaluation.
