Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The key idea is to exploit the dual representation of a coherent risk ρ(Z) as a worst-case expectation over a convex “risk envelope” of reweightings ξ of the underlying distribution Pθ. For static risk (single random variable Z that depends on θ), the authors derive a gradient formula via an envelope-theorem argument applied to the saddle-point formulation of the associated convex program. This yields a likelihood-ratio style estimator that combines sampling with solving a small convex program. They illustrate special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation), and propose a general sample-average approximation (SAA) estimator with consistency guarantees.

For dynamic risk in MDPs, the paper considers time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński and collaborators), whose value function solves a “risk-sensitive” Bellman equation with an inner maximization over the risk envelope at each state. The authors establish a policy gradient theorem that expresses ∇θVθ(x) as an expectation over trajectories under a ξ-weighted transition kernel and a stage cost hθ that depends on Lagrange multipliers from the risk-envelope optimization. They suggest an actor-critic scheme: a robust-MDP-style critic to approximate Vθ, and an actor that uses the gradient theorem with ξ-weighted rollouts. The work unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive policy gradient methods for exponential utility, mean-variance, and CVaR (e.g., Tamar et al., Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, Chow et al., Rockafellar & Uryasev, Ruszczyński & Shapiro), and connects to approximation techniques for robust MDPs (e.g., Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar, Mannor & Xu).

Strengths
- Unified framework: A single derivation covers the full class of coherent risk measures (both static and dynamic, time-consistent), subsuming CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases. This is novel and conceptually valuable.
- Technical contribution: Clear and reusable gradient formulas. The static result via the envelope theorem is elegant; the dynamic policy-gradient theorem extends the classic theorem to Markov coherent risk.
- Practical estimators: SAA-based estimator with convex programming per batch, and an actor-critic scheme for large MDPs. Consistency is established under reasonable conditions.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is well written and accessible; examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) illustrate how the general formula recovers known results.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is light: beyond a small illustrative example, there is no thorough assessment of bias/variance, sample complexity, wall-clock cost, or performance on standard RL benchmarks. It is unclear how practical the convex-program-in-the-loop estimator is at scale.
- Assumptions and smoothness: The static/dynamic gradient theorems rely on differentiability/smoothness of the constraints ge, fi and boundedness of likelihood ratios. The paper could better highlight where these are essential, and what happens for popular envelopes that involve non-smooth points (e.g., CVaR quantile).
- Notational clarity: The roles of Pθ and ξ can be confusing, especially in Eξ[Z] = Σ Pθ(ω) ξ(ω) Z(ω). Explicitly stating ξ as a density ratio relative to Pθ and reminding that ξPθ ∈ U(Pθ) would help. Similarly, (2) appears to restrict the envelope to affine/convex constraints and twice differentiable dependence on p; clarifying how this compares to the general representation in Theorem 2.1 would be useful.
- Practicality of the actor-critic: The dynamic algorithm requires sampling from ξ-weighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per visited state. This may be expensive or infeasible without a generative model; a discussion of on-policy single-trajectory variants and the bias they introduce would strengthen the paper.

Quality
- The derivations are plausible and align with known dual representations and envelope theorems. The static CVaR and mean-semideviation examples are correct and informative. The dynamic policy-gradient theorem is a substantial result; however, given reliance on a fixed-point (risk-sensitive Bellman) and saddle-point differentiability, the supplementary proofs merit careful checking. A brief discussion of conditions ensuring uniqueness/differentiability of Vθ and interchange of gradient and fixed point would increase confidence.
- The SAA consistency result is appropriate, but computational complexity and variance analysis are missing. For risk measures sensitive to the tail, discussion of importance sampling (as noted in the conclusion) is important.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. Consider:
  - Explicitly clarifying the meaning of Eξ[·], whether ξ is a Radon–Nikodym derivative, and how the constraint Σ ξ(ω)Pθ(ω)=1 enforces a valid reweighting.
  - Explaining why Assumption 2.2 (affine/convex constraints with smooth dependence on Pθ) is “more restrictive” than the general representation; is any coherent envelope representable in this form on finite Ω?
  - Summarizing in the main text how the smoothness of fi, ge underpins application of Milgrom–Segal’s envelope theorem in Theorem 4.2.
  - Stating explicitly the contraction/regularity conditions (from [24]) that ensure a unique Vθ and its differentiability in θ.

Originality
- High. While specific risk-sensitive PG methods exist (mean-variance, CVaR), a general policy-gradient theorem and consistent estimators for arbitrary coherent risks, including a dynamic Markov coherent setting, are new to my knowledge.

Significance
- High potential impact. Coherent risk measures are widely used; providing general-purpose tools to optimize them in RL could influence both theory and applications (finance, operations, safety-critical RL). Practical impact depends on scalability and availability of efficient solvers/simulators.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Preliminaries: Please clarify the roles of Pθ and ξ throughout. Is ξ always interpreted as a density ratio w.r.t. Pθ? Make the notation consistent across static and dynamic sections.
- Eq. (2) vs. Theorem 2.1: Is (2) simply a convenient parametrization of U(Pθ) on finite spaces? In what sense is it more restrictive than the most general convex, compact U(Pθ)? Are there coherent risks whose envelopes cannot be written in this canonical form?
- Smoothness: Highlight in the main text where twice differentiability in p of fi, ge is used (Theorem 4.2, Prop. 4.4). How sensitive are the results to non-smooth envelopes (e.g., piecewise-linear constraints)?
- Continuous spaces: You note extensions beyond finite Ω. Please sketch how the results adapt for continuous distributions (e.g., conditions for interchange of differentiation/integration, existence of saddle points, and how to implement SAA).
- Dynamic algorithm: Discuss computational cost, need for a generative model, and possible single-trajectory approximations. Provide guidance on sampling from ξ-weighted kernels in practice.
- Experiments: Include larger-scale experiments on MDP benchmarks; comparisons to CVaR-specific baselines (e.g., Tamar & Glassner; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh; Chow et al.); variance and runtime analyses; sensitivity to α in CVaR and to envelope misspecification; and ablations of critic approximation quality.

Pros for acceptance
- General, elegant theory unifying risk-sensitive PG under coherent risks.
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risks.
- Consistent estimators and clear connections to robust MDPs.
- Well written and potentially influential.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and scalability assessment.
- Heavy reliance on smoothness and availability of a simulator.
- Some notational/assumptional clarity gaps that could hinder adoption.

Overall recommendation
- I lean toward acceptance for the strong and general theoretical contribution and unification. With improved clarity on assumptions and a more substantial empirical section, this could be a notable NIPS paper.

Reviewer confidence
- Medium: proofs appear sound but require careful verification in the supplement.
