This paper presents a valuable extension of policy gradient methods to the broader class of coherent risk measures, encompassing static and dynamically consistent settings. The work builds upon recent advancements in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, moving beyond specific risk measures like CVaR to a more general framework grounded in finance and operations research. The authors effectively demonstrate how to derive gradient forms for both static and dynamic coherent risk, providing a unified approach.

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The notation is generally accessible, and the logical flow from motivation and background (Section 1 & 2) to the core technical contributions (Sections 3, 4, and 5) is smooth and easy to follow. The inclusion of a numerical illustration (Section 6) enhances understanding and demonstrates the practical relevance of the proposed methods. The quality of the technical presentation is high; the claims appear well-supported by theoretical analysis, and the paper feels complete rather than merely a position statement. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and directions for future work.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The extension from CVaR-specific results to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations of the gradient formulas appear correct and are based on established concepts like the Envelope Theorem and convex optimization duality.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex ideas accessible.
* **Practical Relevance:** The potential application of the framework to real-world risk management problems is apparent.
* **Unified Approach:** Combining static and dynamic risk within a single framework provides a cohesive and valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notation (Theorem 2.1):** The notation “ξ Pθ” in Theorem 2.1 is indeed somewhat confusing. Adopting the notation from Theorem 6.6 in [26], as suggested, would improve readability and consistency.
* **Typo:** The typo on line 141 (“risk enevlop[e]”) should be corrected.
* **Tractability Clarification:** The paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between tractability issues stemming from policy parametrization versus inherent limitations of Markov risk measures. Emphasizing that MDPs *with* Markov risk measures are tractable would avoid potential misinterpretations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in reinforcement learning: incorporating robust risk management principles.
* It provides a solid, theoretically grounded extension of existing policy gradient methods.
* The clarity of the presentation makes the work accessible to a broader audience.
* The results are likely to be influential in motivating further research in risk-sensitive RL.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The incremental nature of the extension – while useful – might be considered insufficient for a top-tier conference like NIPS by some reviewers. However, the generality achieved and the clarity of presentation significantly bolster the contribution.
* The computational complexity of solving the convex programs involved in the gradient estimation (especially in the static case) is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a minor but solid and important extension of existing results. The paper successfully generalizes risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to a broader class of coherent risk measures, provides clear and theoretically sound derivations, and is exceptionally well-written. While the extension is not revolutionary, its generality, clarity, and potential impact warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper. The suggested minor revisions (notation, typo, tractability clarification) would further enhance its quality.
