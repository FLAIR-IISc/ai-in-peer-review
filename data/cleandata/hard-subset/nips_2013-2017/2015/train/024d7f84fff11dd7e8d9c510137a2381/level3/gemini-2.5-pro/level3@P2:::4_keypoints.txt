- Proposes a general policy gradient framework for optimizing policies under coherent risk measures using the dual representation and envelope theorem.  
- Main contribution (static): Theorem 4.2 gives a general gradient formula for applying risk to total trajectory return and an algorithm combining sampling with convex programming (Section 4.3).  
- Main contribution (dynamic): Theorem 5.2 extends the framework to time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures and presents a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.  
- Re-derives and generalizes existing results for CVaR and derives a new gradient formula for mean-semideviation.  
- Provides a consistency result for the general gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4).  
- Theoretical work is strong, technically sound, and conceptually elegant.  
- Primary weakness is limited empirical evaluation: only a simple one-step demo and no experiments validating the proposed algorithms.  
- Lacks empirical assessment of practical issues such as sample complexity, computational cost of inner convex optimization, and convergence speed.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and explains coherent risk concepts accessibly.  
- Recommend clarifying the relationship between the base probability P_θ and the adversarial re-weighting density ξ, ideally with an illustrative example.  
- Recommend clarifying whether Assumption 2.2 is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1 or simply a practical instantiation for finite spaces.  
- Highly original: first policy gradient method applicable to the whole class of coherent risk measures.  
- Novel technical approach: combines dual representation, envelope theorem, and likelihood-ratio trick.  
- Potentially high impact if algorithms are shown practical, enabling tailored risk preferences across applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretical significance, generality, elegant results, and clarity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation of proposed algorithms.  
- Suggest briefly discussing extensions and technical challenges for continuous distributions and state/action spaces.  
- Suggest explicitly highlighting the dependence on smoothness of constraint functions g_e and f_i when applying the envelope theorem.  
- Recommend empirical learning-curve plots in Section 6 to demonstrate convergence of the proposed algorithm.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with significant interest to the community but hampered by lack of empirical evaluation.
