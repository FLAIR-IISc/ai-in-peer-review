- Presents a unified policy-gradient framework for the full class of coherent risk measures using the dual risk-envelope and envelope-theorem differentiation.
- Static-risk contributions: a general likelihood-ratio + convex-program gradient formula, closed-form gradients for CVaR and mean-semideviation, and an SAA estimator with consistency guarantees.
- Dynamic-risk contributions: policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risks linking gradients to value functions under adversarially reweighted transitions and an actor-critic proposal via robust MDP connections.
- The envelope-based derivation unifies and generalizes prior risk-specific PG results (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility).
- Theoretical development is careful, grounded in convex analysis, and makes assumptions (finite spaces, Slater condition, bounded likelihood ratios) explicit.
- Strength: a single framework covers a wide family of coherent risks, subsuming many prior derivations.
- Strength: the Lagrangian/envelope-theorem approach yields implementable estimators and clarifies gradient structure.
- Strength: provides new closed-form gradient results and a general SAA estimator with mild-condition consistency.
- Strength: dynamic policy-gradient theorem parallels the standard PG theorem and enables actor-critic designs via robust-MDP links.
- Weakness: very limited empirical validation — only a small static example; SAA estimator and variance/sample-complexity not evaluated.
- Weakness: dynamic algorithmic details and empirical results are missing; actor-critic method is only sketched without implementation costs.
- Weakness: breadth over depth — compressed exposition in eight pages with key implementation details relegated to the supplement.
- Weakness: assumptions (finite probability spaces, multi-sample simulators) may limit applicability; Lp-extension claims lack experimental support.
- Clarity: generally well written but dense; dynamic section needs pseudo-code, explicit computations for ξ*, λ*, hθ, and complexity discussion.
- Originality: high — unifying coherent-risk PG framework and dynamic PG theorem are novel contributions.
- Significance: potentially high if practical issues and experiments are addressed, but current impact limited by sparse empirical guidance.
- Suggested experiments: compare SAA vs analytical CVaR gradients across distributions for bias/variance, sample complexity, and runtime.
- Suggested experiments: dynamic evaluations on standard RL benchmarks comparing actor-critic to risk-neutral and risk-specific baselines.
- Requested clarifications: per-step computational complexity, KKT multiplier computation, ξ-weighted sampling scalability in large/continuous MDPs and single-trajectory settings.
- Requested robustness analysis: behavior under non-unique saddle points and sensitivity to envelope constraint smoothness.
- Requested additions: main-text pseudo-code for static SAA estimator and dynamic actor-critic, and justification or caveats for Lp-space extensions.
- Pros for acceptance: strong general theoretical contribution, clean derivations, novel dynamic PG theorem tied to robust MDPs.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient empirical evidence, unclear practicality and computational costs, and compressed exposition with deferred details.
- Recommendation: lean toward rejection in current form, encouraging revisions focused on either extensive static empirical evaluation or a fully developed dynamic algorithm with comprehensive experiments. Confidence: medium.
