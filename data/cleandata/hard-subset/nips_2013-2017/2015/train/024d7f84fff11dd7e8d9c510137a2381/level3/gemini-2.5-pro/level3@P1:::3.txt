This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending previous work to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors tackle two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted cost and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key insight is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a set of alternative probability densities (the risk envelope).

For the static case, the paper derives a novel formula for the policy gradient using the Envelope Theorem. This leads to a practical algorithm that combines sampling to estimate expectations with convex programming to solve for the worst-case density within the risk envelope. This approach generalizes prior results for specific measures like CVaR, which are shown to be special cases of this more general framework. For the dynamic, time-consistent case, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one, relating the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. This naturally leads to an actor-critic algorithm where the critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function (which is the solution to a robust Bellman equation) and the actor updates the policy using the derived gradient formula. The paper provides a numerical example illustrating how the flexibility of choosing different coherent risk measures can lead to more rational policies compared to standard mean-variance approaches, especially in the presence of heavy-tailed distributions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Generalization and Unification:** The primary strength of this work is its generalization from specific risk measures (e.g., mean-variance, CVaR) to the broad and axiomatically justified class of coherent risk measures. This provides a unified and principled framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods, subsuming and simplifying several previous results. This is a solid and important contribution to the field.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Elegance:** The theoretical results appear to be correct. The derivation of the gradient formulas by combining the dual representation of coherent risk with the Envelope Theorem is elegant and insightful. The proposed algorithms, particularly the combination of sampling and convex programming for the static case, provide a clear and actionable path for applying these ideas.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the problem is formally defined, and the theoretical development is presented logically. The authors do an excellent job of connecting their general framework back to specific examples like CVaR, which helps build intuition.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Framing of Originality:** While the generalization is powerful, the core technical approach can be seen as a clever but direct extension of existing policy gradient and risk-measure optimization techniques. The likelihood-ratio trick is standard, and the dual representation of coherent risk is well-established in optimization literature. Acknowledging that the primary novelty lies in the synthesis and application of these tools to create a general RL framework would provide a more nuanced positioning of the work.

2.  **Clarification on Tractability:** The paper could be clearer about the source of intractability it addresses. The text implies that dynamic risk problems are generally intractable for large state spaces. However, for a known tabular MDP, dynamic programming for Markov coherent risk measures is tractable, as it is equivalent to solving a robust MDP [24]. The intractability that necessitates policy gradient methods arises from large or continuous state spaces and the use of function approximation for the policy, not from the risk measures themselves. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the paper's motivation.

3.  **Minor Notation Issue:** In Theorem 2.1, the notation `ξPθ` is a bit unusual and could be confusing. It appears to represent the re-weighted probability measure. To improve clarity, I would recommend adopting the more standard notation used in the cited reference [26] (e.g., defining a new measure `Q` such that `dQ/dPθ = ξ`).

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It provides a theoretically sound and practically relevant generalization of policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The work is novel, clearly presented, and provides a valuable unifying perspective on previous research. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with small revisions to the text. This paper advances the state of the art and is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners interested in developing robust and risk-aware learning agents. I strongly recommend acceptance.
