

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The theoretical foundation rests on the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), which enables the development of sampling-based gradient estimators for previously intractable cases.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a new gradient formula for static coherent risk measures that facilitates analytical derivation for common risk measures; (2) a general sampling-based algorithm for cases lacking analytical solutions with consistency proofs; and (3) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures with a corresponding actor-critic approach. These contributions significantly extend the literature on risk-sensitive RL, as previous work has typically focused on specific risk measures rather than the entire coherent class.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their gradient formulas and consistency results, building on well-established concepts from risk theory and reinforcement learning. The connection to coherent risk theory (Artzner et al., 1999) is well-motivated and appropriately contextualized. The paper also correctly acknowledges related work, including the connection to robust MDPs (Osogami, 2012) and previous risk-sensitive policy gradient approaches (Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, 2013).

However, the paper's empirical evaluation is limited. The numerical illustration in Section 6 compares three different risk-averse policies but fails to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. The authors train policies using different risk objectives but do not provide a direct comparison of their gradient estimation methods. Crucially, the paper lacks a comparison between the analytical gradient approach (for CVaR and mean-semideviation) and the general sampling-based method, which would be valuable for understanding the practical trade-offs. The absence of comprehensive experiments prevents a thorough assessment of the proposed methods' empirical performance.

The paper is well-written but attempts to cover too much within the page limit. The dynamic risk section (Section 5) is particularly high-level, lacking sufficient algorithmic details and empirical validation. The authors mention a "two-phase sampling procedure" but do not provide the full algorithm or convergence analysis. This makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the dynamic risk approach. The paper would benefit from either splitting the work into two separate papers or focusing on one aspect (static or dynamic risk) to allow for more detailed theoretical and empirical treatment.

The originality of this work is strong. The unified framework for coherent risk measures represents a significant theoretical advance, generalizing previous results and providing a more comprehensive foundation for risk-sensitive RL. The connection between coherent risk theory and reinforcement learning is novel and valuable, offering a principled approach to risk management in sequential decision problems.

The significance of the work is high. Risk-sensitive decision making is crucial in many real-world applications, particularly in finance and operations research. By providing a unified framework that encompasses a wide class of risk measures, the paper opens the door to more flexible risk management in reinforcement learning. The potential for handling model uncertainty through risk shaping (as discussed in the conclusion) is particularly promising.

In summary, this paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with significant potential impact. The main weaknesses are the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation and the insufficient development of the dynamic risk approach within the page constraints. To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide a detailed comparison between the analytical and sampling-based gradient approaches, include more empirical results demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed methods, and either expand the dynamic risk section or focus the paper on the more developed static risk results.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution that unifies risk-sensitive RL under a coherent risk framework, significant advancement over previous work on specific risk measures, well-motivated and technically sound.

Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient empirical validation, particularly for the dynamic risk approach; the paper attempts to cover too many topics within a tight page limit, compromising depth and clarity; lack of comparative evaluation between the analytical and sampling-based gradient methods.

The paper represents an important step toward more flexible risk management in reinforcement learning, but would benefit from more comprehensive empirical validation and clearer presentation of the dynamic risk approach.
