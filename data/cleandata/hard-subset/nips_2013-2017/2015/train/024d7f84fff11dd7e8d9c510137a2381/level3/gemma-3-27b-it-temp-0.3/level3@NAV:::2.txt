## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a significant extension to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by formulating policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors successfully generalize existing approaches focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR, providing a unified framework grounded in established finance and operations research theory. The core contribution lies in deriving novel policy gradient algorithms – both standard policy gradient and actor-critic – naturally from the problem formulation and prior work. 

The paper builds logically upon existing literature in risk-sensitive RL, clearly motivating the need for a more general approach than focusing on individual risk measures. The connection to coherent risk measures, and their desirable properties, is well-explained. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the presentation is generally clear, with equations well-defined. The inclusion of relevant references demonstrates a strong understanding of the field. 

A key strength is the derivation of a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, suitable for sampling-based approximation, and the subsequent actor-critic algorithm for dynamic Markov coherent risk. The authors acknowledge the computational challenges associated with dynamic programming and propose a function approximation approach, referencing relevant work in robust MDPs. The numerical example, while simple, effectively demonstrates the algorithm’s risk-averse behavior and its ability to adapt to different risk types, highlighting the flexibility of the proposed framework.

However, the experiments are indeed limited in scope. The toy finance example, while illustrative, lacks the complexity of real-world applications. Expanding the experimental evaluation to more challenging environments, potentially including continuous state/action spaces, would significantly strengthen the paper. Furthermore, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed algorithms, particularly the actor-critic method, would be beneficial. While the authors mention function approximation, a more thorough discussion of its impact on convergence and performance is needed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and open problem in risk-sensitive RL.
*   Presents a theoretically sound and well-motivated framework.
*   Offers a significant generalization of existing methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Demonstrates promising results in a simplified setting.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed scalability analysis.
*   Further discussion on the impact of function approximation is needed.

**Overall:**

Despite the limitations in experimental validation, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical framework and the proposed algorithms are well-developed and offer a promising direction for future research. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more comprehensive experimental evaluation and a more detailed discussion of scalability and the impact of function approximation.
