- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for coherent risk measures using the dual representation and derives gradient formulas for static and dynamic settings.
- Static result: envelope-theorem-based gradient and likelihood-ratio style estimator; SAA estimator with consistency; examples recover CVaR and mean-semideviation.
- Dynamic result: policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risks; gradient is an expectation under a ξ-weighted transition kernel with stage cost hθ using Lagrange multipliers.
- Proposes an actor-critic scheme: robust-MDP-style critic for Vθ and an actor using ξ-weighted rollouts.
- Unified framework that subsumes prior risk-sensitive PG methods (CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility) — novel and conceptually valuable.
- Strong technical contribution: clear, reusable static and dynamic gradient formulas; elegant use of the envelope theorem.
- Practical estimators: convex-program-in-the-loop SAA approach per batch with consistency guarantees.
- Paper is well written and organized with helpful illustrative examples.
- Empirical evaluation is light — lacks bias/variance, sample complexity, runtime, and benchmark comparisons to assess scalability.
- Reliance on differentiability/smoothness of constraints and bounded likelihood ratios; unclear behavior for non-smooth envelopes (e.g., CVaR quantile).
- Notational clarity issues: roles of Pθ vs. ξ are confusing; need to state ξ as density ratio and that ξPθ ∈ U(Pθ).
- Eq. (2) vs Theorem 2.1: (2) may be more restrictive—clarify whether any coherent envelope on finite Ω can be written in that parametrization.
- Dynamic algorithm practicality concerns: sampling from ξ-weighted transitions and multiple next-state samples likely requires a generative model and may be expensive.
- Proofs appear plausible but depend on fixed-point differentiability and saddle-point conditions; supplementary proofs need careful checking and clearer conditions for uniqueness/differentiability of Vθ.
- SAA consistency is appropriate but lacks computational complexity and variance analysis; importance sampling for tail risks should be discussed.
- Request clearer exposition: define Eξ[·]/Radon–Nikodym interpretation, clarify Assumption 2.2 scope, and state how Milgrom–Segal envelope theorem is applied.
- Ask for a sketch of extensions to continuous spaces: conditions for differentiation/integration interchange, existence of saddle points, and SAA implementation.
- Experimental requests: larger-scale MDP benchmarks, comparisons to CVaR-specific baselines, runtime/variance analyses, sensitivity to α and envelope misspecification, and critic-ablation studies.
- Originality: high — general policy-gradient theorem and estimators for arbitrary coherent risks (including dynamic Markov coherent) appear new.
- Significance: high potential impact for theory and applications (finance, operations, safety), contingent on scalability.
- Pros for acceptance: elegant general theory, new dynamic theorem, consistent estimators, and strong writing.
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical validation, heavy smoothness/simulator assumptions, and notational/assumptional clarity gaps.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance for strong theoretical contribution, but needs clearer assumptions and stronger empirical work.
- Reviewer confidence: medium — proofs seem sound but require careful verification in the supplement.
