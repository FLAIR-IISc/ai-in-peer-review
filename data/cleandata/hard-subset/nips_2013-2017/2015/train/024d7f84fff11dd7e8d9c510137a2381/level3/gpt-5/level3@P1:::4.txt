Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning, covering both static risk of trajectory returns and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) as a worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope U(Pθ). For static risk, the authors derive an envelope-theorem-based gradient formula that expresses ∇θρ(Z) in terms of (i) a likelihood-ratio term under the adversarial reweighting ξ*, and (ii) derivatives of the envelope constraints via KKT multipliers. This yields analytic gradients for CVaR and mean-semideviation and a general sample-average approximation (SAA) estimator that combines sampling with a convex program to recover ξ* and multipliers; consistency is established under mild conditions. For dynamic risk, the paper adopts time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone) whose Bellman equation resembles a robust MDP (Delage & Mannor; Petrik & Subramanian). The authors prove a policy gradient theorem for this setting: the gradient is an expectation over trajectories generated by ξ*-reweighted transitions, with a per-step cost hθ that depends on Vθ and the KKT multipliers. An actor-critic scheme is proposed: the critic approximates Vθ using robust MDP techniques; the actor estimates gradients via two-phase sampling under ξ*-weighted dynamics. A small trading example illustrates how different coherent risks lead to qualitatively different policies. The work unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL for exponential utility (Borkar), mean-variance (Tamar et al.), and CVaR (Rockafellar-Uryasev; Chow & Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al.).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The static gradient formula via the Envelope Theorem and likelihood ratios is elegant and appears correct; the special cases recover known CVaR and semideviation gradients with simpler derivations. The SAA-based estimator with consistency is well-motivated; the assumptions (convex-compact envelope; Slater condition; continuity/smoothness of constraints) are reasonable for popular coherent risks. The dynamic policy gradient theorem is a substantive contribution; connecting the gradient to ξ*-weighted dynamics and a modified stage cost hθ is insightful. However, full proofs are deferred to the supplement; some differentiability/uniqueness conditions at the saddle point could be more explicitly stated (e.g., conditions ensuring the envelope theorem applies without ambiguity when ξ* is non-unique).
- Empirics: The empirical section is illustrative but limited. There is no large-scale MDP evaluation, comparison against CVaR-specific policy gradients, mean-variance baselines, or robust MDP planning. Variance, sample complexity, and computational overhead of solving the per-batch convex program are not quantified.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-written and organized, with accessible exposition of coherent risk and clear positioning relative to prior RL work. That said, a few clarifications would help:
  - Roles of Pθ and ξ: Pθ is the policy-induced distribution (over outcomes in the static case; over next states Pθ(·|x) in the dynamic case). ξ reweights Pθ within the risk envelope; in the dynamic setting, ξθ*,x defines a density over next states. Being explicit early (Sec. 2.1/2.2) that ξ is a Radon–Nikodym derivative over the base transition probabilities would reduce confusion.
  - Equation (2): Please clarify whether this “canonical” form is simply a convenient parameterization of the convex compact envelope in Theorem 2.1 or whether it imposes additional restrictions (e.g., smooth dependence on Pθ and availability of KKT multipliers). If more restrictive, specify which coherent risks are excluded.
  - Smoothness: The assumptions on bounded derivatives of ge, fi w.r.t. p are critical for Theorem 4.2 and Proposition 4.4 (differentiation through the max and stability of SAA). It would help to restate precisely where these enter the proofs and whether weaker assumptions (e.g., directional derivatives, subdifferentials) suffice.
  - Continuous distributions: Although the finite-Ω setting is adopted for exposition, an explicit discussion of the extension to continuous spaces (e.g., Lp settings, Gâteaux differentiability, measurable selection for ξ*, and practical SAA discretization) would broaden applicability.

Originality:
- The work provides a unified policy-gradient treatment for the entire class of coherent risks (static and dynamic), subsuming and simplifying several previous results. The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears novel and important. The combination of sampling with convex programming via the dual risk representation is also a compelling design pattern.

Significance:
- The results could impact risk-sensitive RL broadly by allowing practitioners to plug in different coherent risks (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation) while retaining a common gradient machinery. The dynamic result advances theory for time-consistent risk in MDPs and ties risk to robustness. Practicality will hinge on computational cost (solving envelope programs and sampling under ξ*-weighted dynamics) and on empirical performance at scale.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Can you elaborate on conditions ensuring differentiability of ρ(Z) in θ when the saddle point is not unique (e.g., CVaR at mass points)? Do subgradient variants of Theorem 4.2 apply?
- For Eq. (2), please justify the “canonical” envelope form and explicitly compare it to the general dual characterization in Theorem 2.1. Under what coherent risks does Assumption 2.2 fail?
- Dynamic sampling under ξ*: Do you require a generative model to sample Pθ(·|x)ξ*, or can importance sampling be used from the nominal dynamics? What is the variance trade-off?
- Critic details: State the contraction and approximation properties of the robust Bellman operator used, the function class, and the error propagation to the actor (bounds and dependence on approximation error).
- Complexity and scalability: Provide runtime comparisons (per gradient step) versus standard policy gradient and CVaR-specific methods. How large can the envelope program (7) be in practice, and can stochastic/online solvers be used instead of full KKT solves?
- Empirics: Include at least one benchmark MDP (e.g., inventory control, queuing) with comparisons to CVaR-PG, mean-variance PG, and robust MDP planning; report variance, bias (due to function approximation), and ablation on envelope assumptions.
- Continuous-state/action extensions: Outline the required conditions (e.g., dominated densities, Lipschitz envelopes) and the SAA scheme.

Pros:
- Unified and general framework spanning static and dynamic coherent risks.
- Clear gradient formulas; elegant use of duality and the envelope theorem.
- Dynamic policy gradient theorem with a principled actor-critic outline.
- Connections to robustness; covers important special cases (CVaR, spectral, semideviation).

Cons:
- Limited empirical validation; unclear practical performance and scalability.
- Dependence on explicit risk envelopes and KKT multipliers may be heavy in practice.
- Some assumptions and measurability/differentiability conditions are not fully discussed.
- Requirement to sample under reweighted dynamics limits model-free applicability.

Overall assessment:
A strong theoretical contribution that unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients for coherent risk measures and offers a novel dynamic policy gradient theorem. With strengthened empirical evaluation and added clarity around assumptions, sampling under ξ*, and continuous-space extensions, this work would be a valuable addition to the literature. I lean toward acceptance for its originality and potential impact, contingent on improved experiments and clarifications.
