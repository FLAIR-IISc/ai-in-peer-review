This paper proposes a general policy gradient framework for optimizing policies under a coherent risk measure objective. Coherent risk measures are a broad class of functions satisfying desirable axioms (e.g., convexity, monotonicity) and have a dual representation as the maximum expected value over a convex set of probability distributions, known as the "risk envelope." The authors skillfully leverage this dual representation, in conjunction with the envelope theorem, to derive a novel policy gradient formula.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, for the static risk setting (where risk is applied to the total return of a trajectory), it provides a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2). This formula is used to re-derive and generalize existing results for specific measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) and to derive a new formula for the mean-semideviation measure. A general algorithm is also proposed that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient when an analytical solution for the worst-case distribution is not available. Second, for the dynamic setting, the paper extends the framework to time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. It presents a new policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) tailored for this setting and outlines a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

This work provides a unified treatment that generalizes several previous studies in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, which have largely focused on specific risk measures such as mean-variance (e.g., Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, NIPS 2013) or CVaR (e.g., Chow & Ghavamzadeh, NIPS 2014).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is theoretically strong and technically sound. The core idea of using the dual representation of coherent risk measures to derive a policy gradient is elegant and powerful. The derivations leading to the main gradient formulas in Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2 are insightful and represent a significant technical contribution. The consistency result for the general gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) further strengthens the theoretical foundation of the proposed method.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its empirical evaluation. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is a simple one-step decision problem that serves to highlight the difference between various risk measures, but it does not evaluate the performance of the proposed gradient estimation algorithms. There is no empirical validation for the general static risk algorithm (Section 4.3) or the actor-critic algorithm (Section 5). This makes it difficult to assess the practical challenges of these methods, such as sample complexity, computational cost of the inner convex optimization loop, and convergence speed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and does a commendable job of introducing the concepts of coherent risk to a general machine learning audience. The motivation is clear, and the progression from static to dynamic risk measures is logical. A few points could be clarified to improve readability:
*   In the preliminaries, the relationship between the base probability measure P_θ (parameterized by the policy) and the re-weighting density ξ (chosen adversarially from the risk envelope U) could be explained more explicitly. An illustrative example would help build intuition.
*   The paper states that Assumption 2.2, which represents the risk envelope using convex programming constraints, is justified because for finite probability spaces, U(P_θ) must be convex and compact. It would be helpful to clarify if Assumption 2.2 is any more restrictive than the general representation in Theorem 2.1, or if it is simply a practical way to instantiate any such set.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While risk-sensitive RL is an active area, this is the first work to develop a policy gradient method applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. It provides a unified perspective that subsumes much of the prior work, which has been piecemeal in its focus on individual risk measures. The technical approach of combining the dual representation, the envelope theorem, and the likelihood-ratio trick is novel and insightful.

**Significance:** This paper has the potential for high impact. It provides a principled and general framework for incorporating a wide range of risk preferences into RL agents. This generality is a significant step forward for risk-sensitive RL, empowering researchers and practitioners to design risk measures tailored to specific applications rather than being confined to a few standard ones. The proposed algorithms, if shown to be practical, could be widely adopted in fields where risk management is critical, such as finance, robotics, and autonomous systems.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Theoretical Significance:** The paper presents a novel and unifying framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients, a fundamental contribution to the field.
*   **Generality:** The approach is applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, generalizing several previous results and opening the door to new applications.
*   **Elegant Results:** The derived gradient formulas are insightful and provide a clean connection between the dual representation of risk and policy gradient methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and makes a complex topic accessible to a broad audience.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experiments evaluating the proposed gradient estimation algorithms is a major drawback. The practicality, sample efficiency, and scalability of the methods in Sections 4.3 and 5 are not demonstrated.

### Detailed Questions and Suggestions:
*   The paper assumes a finite probability space and mentions that the results can be extended. It would strengthen the paper to briefly comment on the technical challenges or changes required for extending the analysis to continuous distributions and state/action spaces.
*   The smoothness of the constraint functions g_e and f_i with respect to P_θ (Assumption 2.2) seems crucial for applying the envelope theorem in the proof of Theorem 4.2. It would be beneficial to explicitly highlight this dependency in the main text when discussing the theorem.
*   The numerical example in Section 6 is compelling for motivating the use of different risk measures. However, it would be much more convincing if it also showed the learning curves (e.g., risk objective vs. iteration) for the proposed algorithm, demonstrating its ability to converge to the expected solutions.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with compelling and original results. The main reservation is the lack of empirical evaluation for the proposed algorithms. Despite this, the theoretical contribution is significant enough to be of great interest to the NIPS community.
