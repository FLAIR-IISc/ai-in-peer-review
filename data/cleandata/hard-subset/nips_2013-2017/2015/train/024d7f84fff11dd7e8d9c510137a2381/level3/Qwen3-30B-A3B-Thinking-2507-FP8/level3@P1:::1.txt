

This paper proposes a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific measures like CVaR or variance as previous works have done. The authors build on the foundational work of Artzner et al. on coherent risk measures and extend it to MDPs, generalizing and unifying previous approaches that focused on individual risk measures. The paper makes two main theoretical contributions: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that enables analytic derivation for many common risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation), and (2) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk, along with an actor-critic algorithm.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical contribution is sound and well-motivated. The new gradient formula for static risk (Theorem 4.2) elegantly combines the risk envelope representation (Theorem 2.1) with the envelope theorem to derive a sampling-friendly gradient expression. This enables the derivation of closed-form gradients for multiple risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) in a unified manner, as shown in Sections 4.1-4.2. The authors also develop a general sampling-based algorithm (Section 4.3) with a consistency proof (Proposition 4.4), which extends the applicability of their framework to cases where analytical solutions are unavailable. For dynamic risk, the policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) successfully extends the classic policy gradient framework to Markov coherent risk measures, providing a theoretically sound basis for the actor-critic approach.

The significance of this work is high. Risk-sensitive RL has gained traction in applications where managing cost variability is crucial, but previous work has been fragmented across different risk measures. This paper provides a unified framework that allows practitioners to select appropriate risk measures for specific problems without needing to develop new algorithms for each measure. The numerical example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates how different risk measures lead to different policy choices, highlighting the value of the flexible framework.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's impact. The most significant issue is the lack of comprehensive experimental validation. The paper claims to generalize previous approaches but provides no empirical comparison between their sample-based gradient estimator and analytical approaches (e.g., for CVaR), despite noting in the supplementary material that such a comparison was conducted. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is limited to a single trading example with no comparative analysis of the different risk measures' performance. This omission makes it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed algorithms.

The paper's scope is also problematic. Attempting to cover both static and dynamic risk in eight pages results in insufficient detail, particularly for the dynamic risk section (Section 5). The actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is described at a high level without sufficient algorithmic details or convergence analysis. The lack of experimental results for the dynamic risk approach makes its practical utility unclear. The paper would benefit from focusing on one aspect (e.g., static risk) to allow for more detailed treatment and comprehensive empirical evaluation.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but the ambitious scope and technical density make some sections challenging to follow. The theoretical development is rigorous, but the practical implementation details for the dynamic risk case are notably sparse. The writing is clear and well-organized within each section, but the paper's attempt to cover too much in limited space hinders overall clarity.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel unified framework for risk-sensitive RL, which is a significant contribution to the field. While some previous work has addressed specific risk measures (e.g., [8, 29, 30] for CVaR; [18, 21] for mean-variance), the authors successfully generalize these approaches under the coherent risk framework. The connection to robust MDPs in the dynamic risk section is particularly insightful.

For acceptance considerations: The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework. The theoretical results are sound, and the significance of the work is high. However, the lack of comprehensive empirical validation, particularly for the dynamic risk approach, prevents a full assessment of the practical utility of the proposed algorithms. The paper would be significantly stronger with more experimental results comparing the sample-based and analytical approaches, and with a more detailed treatment of the dynamic risk case.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, novel unified framework, well-motivated problem, significant potential impact on risk-sensitive RL.

Con: Limited empirical validation, lack of algorithmic details for dynamic risk, scope too broad for eight pages, practical significance unclear without more experiments.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially broadly applicable paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution. However, the lack of comprehensive experiments prevents a definitive assessment of its practical utility, which is a significant limitation for NIPS. The paper would be substantially stronger if it focused on one aspect (e.g., static risk) to allow for more detailed treatment and empirical validation.
