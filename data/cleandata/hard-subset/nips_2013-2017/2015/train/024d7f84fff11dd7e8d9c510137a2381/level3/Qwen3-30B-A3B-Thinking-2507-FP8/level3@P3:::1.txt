

# Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing a unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors extend previous work on specific risk measures (such as CVaR and mean-variance) to a general coherent risk framework, which has been widely adopted in finance and operations research. The paper's main contributions include a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk enabling analytic derivation of policy gradients for many common risk measures, a general sampling-based algorithm with consistency proof, and expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

The work builds upon the coherent risk framework established by Artzner et al. [2], and extends the risk-sensitive RL literature that has focused on specific risk measures [8, 18, 21, 29, 30]. It also connects to the dynamic risk work of Ruszczyński [24] and the robust MDP connection of [19]. The paper's unified approach represents a significant theoretical advance over previous studies that addressed risk-sensitive RL for individual risk measures.

Strengths:
1. The unified theoretical framework is novel and valuable for risk-sensitive decision making. The authors successfully derive a general gradient formula that encompasses previous results as special cases.
2. The analytical derivation for static risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) is elegant and simplifies previously complex derivations.
3. The sampling-based algorithm with consistency proof provides a practical approach for cases where analytical solutions are unavailable.
4. The paper demonstrates the theoretical foundation through clear examples and connects to well-established concepts in risk theory.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's scope is too ambitious for its length (8 pages). The dynamic risk section is particularly high-level, lacking algorithmic details and empirical validation. The authors attempt to cover both static and dynamic risk measures, time-consistent and non-time-consistent settings, and analytical and sampling-based approaches, which dilutes the focus and reduces clarity.
2. While the analytical Lagrangian saddle-point solutions exist for some risk measures (like CVaR), they don't for others, limiting closed-form applicability. The paper doesn't adequately address this limitation.
3. The sample-based approximation could significantly extend applicability, but the paper lacks empirical validation of this approach. No experimental comparison between sample-based and analytical gradients (e.g., for CVaR) is provided, despite the authors noting they "reported an empirical comparison" in supplementary materials.
4. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is insufficient to demonstrate the practical value of the proposed methods. It compares different risk-averse policies but doesn't validate the gradient estimation algorithms themselves.

Quality: The theoretical contributions are sound and well-supported by the representation theorem for coherent risk measures. The consistency proof for the sampling-based estimator is rigorous. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation limits the assessment of practical quality.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, but attempts to cover too many distinct concepts within eight pages. The dynamic risk section is particularly vague, lacking algorithmic details that would help readers understand how to implement the proposed method. The paper would benefit from splitting into two separate works: one focused on static risk measures with detailed experiments, and another on dynamic risk measures.

Originality: The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures is genuinely novel. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work by showing how specific results (e.g., for CVaR) are special cases of their general framework.

Significance: The approach has significant potential to advance risk-sensitive decision making across multiple domains. However, the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation prevents a full assessment of its practical significance. The paper demonstrates the theoretical flexibility of the framework but doesn't convincingly show how it outperforms existing methods in practice.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially broadly applicable paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework represents an important advance over previous work that focused on specific risk measures. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly for the sampling-based approach, and the overly ambitious scope for the paper's length prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The authors should consider focusing on static risk measures in a separate paper to allow for more detailed theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical evaluation. Alternatively, they could significantly expand the dynamic risk section with algorithmic details and experiments to make the practical contribution clearer.
