This paper tackles policy optimization under a broad class of coherent risk measures by leveraging their dual representation: any coherent risk can be written as the worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope (a convex hull of probability reweightings). The authors derive policy-gradient formulas for both static (one-step) and dynamic (time-consistent Markov coherent) settings, using an envelope-theorem argument and likelihood-ratio identities. For static risks, they obtain analytic gradients in important special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and propose a general gradient estimation scheme that combines sampling with a convex program over the risk envelope. For dynamic risk, they establish a policy-gradient theorem that expresses the gradient via an expectation under ξ-weighted transitions and propose an actor-critic method. The work unifies and generalizes prior NIPS contributions on CVaR optimization in MDPs, risk-sensitive actor-critic, and variance-related risk criteria, and connects to robust MDP interpretations of dynamic coherent risk.

Quality: The theoretical development is compelling and appears technically sound, contingent on assumptions such as Slater feasibility and smoothness of the envelope constraints. The consistency of the sample-average estimator is reassuring. However, proofs (especially Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) merit careful verification, and the practicality of repeatedly solving convex programs and sampling under ξ-weighted dynamics should be examined empirically. The empirical section is illustrative but too light to assess the proposed gradient algorithm’s variance, bias, and scalability.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and accessible, even to non-experts. That said, the preliminaries could better distinguish the roles of Pθ (data-generating distribution) and ξ (adversarial reweighting), and the notation Eξ[·] would benefit from an explicit statement that expectations are taken under the reweighted measure ξPθ.

Originality: High. The unified analysis across coherent risks and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem are novel and impactful extensions of prior risk-specific work.

Significance: High. Coherent risk is widely used in finance and OR; a general policy-gradient toolkit can influence both theory and applications.

Questions and suggestions:
- Assumption 2.2: Is Eq. (2) simply a convenient convex (conic) representation of the risk envelope? In what sense is it more restrictive than Theorem 2.1, and which coherent measures are excluded if smoothness is required?
- Please clarify and emphasize the role of smoothness of the envelope constraint functions (and the M-Lipschitz bound) in applying the envelope theorem in Theorem 4.2 and for Prop. 4.4 (Assumption IV).
- Dynamic case: sampling under ξ-weighted transitions assumes a modified simulator; discuss feasibility and any off-policy or importance-sampling alternatives.
- Extension: Provide a brief discussion on generalization to continuous distributions (Lp spaces), required regularity, and how SAA and KKT-based gradients carry over.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, unified theoretical contribution across a broad class of risks.
- Clear connection to robust MDPs and prior NIPS literature.
- Practical gradient estimators with consistency guarantees.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation of the general gradient/actor-critic algorithms.
- Some assumptions and notational choices need clearer justification and exposition.
- Computational and sampling requirements not fully assessed.
