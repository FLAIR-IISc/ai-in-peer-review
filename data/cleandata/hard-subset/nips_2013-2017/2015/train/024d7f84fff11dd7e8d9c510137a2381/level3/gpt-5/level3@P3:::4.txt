Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies risk-sensitive policy optimization where the objective is a coherent risk measure of return rather than the standard expectation. The key idea is to exploit the dual (robust) representation of coherent risk: ρ(Z) = maxξ∈U(Pθ) Eξ[Z], i.e., risk equals the worst-case expected value over a convex risk envelope U that depends on the policy-induced distribution Pθ. Building on this, the authors derive policy-gradient formulas for (i) static risk of a one-step or trajectory return and (ii) time-consistent dynamic (Markov) coherent risk in MDPs, the latter via a Bellman fixed point and an actor-critic scheme. A central assumption is that the risk envelope admits an explicit convex program with affine/convex constraints whose dependence on Pθ is smooth and M-Lipschitz in the probability components. Results include analytic derivations for specific risks (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), and a general sample-average plus convex programming estimator applicable to a broad class of coherent risks. For the dynamic case, they provide a policy-gradient theorem under Markov coherent risk with a weighted transition kernel and a stage cost hθ that depends on saddle-point multipliers.

The work unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive RL studies: mean-variance methods, CVaR-based policy gradients, and robust MDP connections. It relates to coherent risk duality (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and dynamic time-consistent risk (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone). The dynamic gradient leverages the equivalence between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs, connecting to approximate dynamic programming for robust MDPs (Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al.).

Strengths
- Unified framework: A single analysis yielding gradient estimators for a very broad class of coherent risks (static and dynamic), subsuming CVaR, mean-semideviation, and spectral risks.
- Sound variational foundation: Clean use of dual representation and envelope theorem to derive gradients; clear accounting for how Pθ and U(Pθ) interact through ξ.
- General gradient estimation algorithm: Practical SAA-based estimator combining sampling and convex programming; consistent under mild conditions.
- Dynamic extension: A policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and an actor-critic design, tying risk-sensitive RL to robust MDP value functions.
- Clarity and exposition: Well-written and organized; non-experts in risk-sensitive optimization can follow the preliminaries and main ideas.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical depth: Experiments are illustrative but limited. The dynamic algorithm is not thoroughly evaluated at scale; no comparisons on benchmarks or variance/efficiency analyses of the gradient estimators.
- Sampling requirements: The actor requires sampling multiple next states per state (simulator access), limiting applicability in online/trajectory-only settings.
- Variance of estimators: Likelihood-ratio gradients for tail-sensitive risks (e.g., small-α CVaR) can be high-variance. Importance sampling is briefly noted as future work but not explored.
- Assumption restrictiveness: Assumption 2.2 (explicit convex envelope with twice differentiable dependence on Pθ and M-Lipschitz bounds) is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1’s existence of a convex compact envelope. Please clarify the breadth of coherent risks and state-dependent envelopes that satisfy these smoothness bounds in practice.
- Preliminaries clarity: The roles of Pθ and ξ could be clarified earlier. Pθ is the policy-induced base distribution over outcomes (or transitions), and ξ is a nonnegative density ratio that reweights Pθ to define adversarial measures within U(Pθ). Eξ[Z] = Eω∼Pθ[ξ(ω)Z(ω)].
- Equation (2) vs Theorem 2.1: Eq. (2) is an explicit convex program formulation of the risk envelope (convex/affine constraints, Slater condition, smooth dependence on Pθ). This is effectively a constructive subset of all convex compact U guaranteed by Theorem 2.1. If the paper claims Eq. (2) is “more restrictive,” please state this explicitly and justify why the added smoothness is needed (e.g., to apply the envelope theorem and differentiate the saddle-point value in Theorem 4.2 and to ensure SAA consistency).
- Theorem 4.2 smoothness: Please emphasize that the smoothness (and Lipschitz) of constraint functions with respect to p = Pθ is used to justify differentiation under the saddle-point, bounded multipliers, and interchanging limits (envelope theorem application). A short sketch outlining where these properties enter would help.
- Continuous distributions: The preliminaries restrict to finite Ω. It would be useful to add comments on generalization to continuous spaces (Lp): sums become integrals, ξ lies in a suitable L∞-bounded set, risk envelopes defined via Radon–Nikodym derivatives, and technical conditions ensuring compactness and differentiability (e.g., Gâteaux/Fréchet differentiability of constraints in the density). For SAA, discuss discretization or kernel density approaches and any measurability subtleties.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong; derivations are plausible and consistent with convex analysis and RL policy-gradient techniques. However, some proofs are deferred, and given the nontrivial use of envelope/KKT differentiation and saddle-point stability, careful verification is warranted.
- The empirical support is modest relative to the theoretical ambition.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-structured. Minor clarifications as noted (Pθ vs ξ roles; restrictiveness of Eq. (2); smoothness in Theorem 4.2) would further improve readability.

Originality
- High. A general policy-gradient treatment for coherent risks (static and dynamic) is novel and unifying, extending multiple prior special cases.

Significance
- High. Provides broadly applicable tools for risk-sensitive RL and connects to robust MDPs. Likely to influence subsequent work on risk-aware policy optimization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand empirical evaluation: include larger MDPs, dynamic actor-critic performance, comparisons to CVaR-specific and robust MDP baselines, gradient variance and sample efficiency analyses, and ablations on the SAA vs closed-form saddle-points.
- Explore variance reduction: importance sampling for tail events, control variates leveraging λ* and ξ* structure, baselines adapted to hθ.
- Discuss practical envelope modeling: guidance on specifying U(Pθ) for common risks beyond CVaR/MSD (e.g., spectral risks) and how to verify Assumption 2.2.
- Add a brief section on continuous distributions and any additional technical assumptions needed.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, unified theoretical contribution covering a broad class of risks.
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem and actor-critic framework for Markov coherent risk.
- Clear derivations that subsume prior special cases (e.g., CVaR gradient).
- Relevance to robust RL and safety-critical applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation, especially for the dynamic algorithm.
- Some technical assumptions are stronger than the general theory of coherent risk and warrant more discussion and examples.
- Proof details are deferred; correctness depends on nontrivial regularity conditions.

Overall assessment
A strong theoretical paper with compelling, general results that unify risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk umbrella. While the proofs merit careful checking and the experiments are not yet comprehensive, the contribution is significant and timely. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions, adding guidance on continuous cases, and strengthening empirical evidence.
