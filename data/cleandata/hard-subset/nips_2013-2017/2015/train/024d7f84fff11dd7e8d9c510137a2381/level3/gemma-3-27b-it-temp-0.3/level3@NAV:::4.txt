## Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper presents a compelling theoretical framework for extending policy gradient methods to a broad class of coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The authors successfully address a significant gap in the literature, moving beyond specific risk measures like variance or CVaR to a unified approach grounded in the well-established theory of coherent risk. The core idea – leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions – is elegant and allows for analytic derivation of optimal policy parameters under a given risk measure. The development of both static and dynamic gradient estimation algorithms is a substantial contribution, offering a versatile toolkit for risk-sensitive RL. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making a complex topic accessible even to those not deeply familiar with risk-sensitive optimization. The presentation is clear, and the authors effectively connect their work to existing literature in RL, stochastic programming, and finance. The originality of the work lies in the generality of the approach, providing a unifying analysis that encompasses many previously studied risk measures as special cases. The significance is high, as this framework has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners seeking to incorporate robust risk management into RL applications.

However, the paper’s strength in theory is somewhat offset by a relative lack of extensive empirical evaluation. While the authors provide a motivating numerical example in Section 6, a more thorough investigation of the proposed gradient estimation algorithm’s performance across a range of benchmark environments would significantly strengthen the paper. Specifically, comparing the performance and sample efficiency of the proposed algorithm to existing risk-sensitive RL methods (e.g., those based on exponential utility) would be valuable. 

I have a few specific requests for clarification and technical points. First, the role of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ in the preliminaries (Section 2) could be more clearly explained, particularly for readers less familiar with measure-theoretic concepts. Second, Equation 2 appears to be a standard definition of the convex envelope. It would be helpful to clarify why the authors claim it is “more restrictive” than Theorem 2.1. Finally, a brief comment on how the analysis generalizes to continuous distributions would be beneficial, as many real-world applications involve continuous state and action spaces. Regarding Theorem 4.2, mentioning the role of smoothness of the constraint functions in proving it would add to the theoretical completeness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and novel contribution to risk-sensitive RL.
*   Elegant and unifying framework for a broad class of coherent risk measures.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Potential for significant impact on both research and practice.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation.
*   Minor clarifications needed regarding preliminaries and technical details.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong theoretical paper with compelling results. While the proofs require careful verification, the potential impact of this work is substantial. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the minor points raised above and potentially expanding the empirical evaluation.
