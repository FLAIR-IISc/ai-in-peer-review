

This paper presents a unified approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures like CVaR or variance as previous works have done. The authors formulate both static and dynamic Markov coherent risk measures within a policy gradient framework, providing novel gradient formulas that combine sampling with convex programming. Their approach generalizes and unifies previous results for specific risk measures, offering a more comprehensive theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive RL.

The paper makes a significant contribution by showing that coherent risk measures can be treated within a unified framework, leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to derive gradient formulas. For static risk, they develop a sampling-based estimator that combines convex programming with likelihood-ratio estimation, while for dynamic risk, they extend the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures, enabling an actor-critic algorithm. This represents an important theoretical advancement over prior work that focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR in [8, 30], mean-variance in [18, 29, 21]).

Quality: The technical content is sound, with well-justified theoretical results and consistent proofs. The authors carefully establish the conditions under which their gradient estimators are consistent (Proposition 4.4). The numerical experiments in Section 6 effectively demonstrate the flexibility of the framework in selecting appropriate risk measures for different problem characteristics. The paper properly acknowledges related work and distinguishes its contributions from previous studies, particularly noting how their approach generalizes results in [8, 30] and [29, 21]. The only technical concern is the notation "\xi P_\theta" (used in Theorem 2.1), which is confusing and inconsistent with the standard notation in [26] (where the risk envelope is written as U(Pθ)). This notation issue should be addressed to improve clarity.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written, making complex theoretical concepts accessible. The structure flows logically from problem formulation to theoretical results to numerical validation. The key contributions are clearly articulated, and the relationship between their work and prior research is well-explained. The main weakness is the typo "risk enevlop" on line 141, which should be corrected. Additionally, the paper could clarify that tractability issues arise from policy parametrization (not from Markov risk measures themselves), as MDPs with Markov risk measures are tractable when using the dynamic programming approach of [24].

Originality: The paper's main contribution is the unified treatment of coherent risk measures within policy gradient methods. While extending specific risk measures to the general coherent risk class might seem like a minor extension, it provides a theoretically principled framework that enables risk-sensitive RL to be applied to a much broader range of problems. The authors demonstrate this by showing how previous results for CVaR and mean-semideviation are special cases of their framework. This represents a meaningful conceptual advance over the existing literature, which has treated different risk measures as separate problems.

Significance: The framework provides practitioners with flexibility to design risk preferences that match specific problem characteristics and risk attitudes. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates this by showing how different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to different policy choices in a financial trading application. This flexibility is particularly valuable in real-world applications where decision-makers need to balance expected returns with risk management. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (discussed in the conclusion) also suggests broader potential applications beyond standard risk-sensitive RL.

The paper's main limitation is that the extension from specific risk measures to the general class is relatively straightforward (as acknowledged in the paper), but this does not diminish its value as a unifying theoretical framework. The proposed algorithms are practical, with the static case involving standard convex programming and the dynamic case building on existing actor-critic approaches. The authors have made an important contribution to the field by providing a theoretically sound foundation for risk-sensitive RL that covers the entire class of coherent risk measures.

Overall, this is a solid and important contribution that extends the theoretical understanding of risk-sensitive RL. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. While the extension from specific risk measures to the general class is not revolutionary, it is a necessary and valuable step toward a more comprehensive theory of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.

Strengths: Unified theoretical framework, clear writing, sound technical results, effective numerical illustration, proper contextualization within related work.

Weaknesses: Notation inconsistency in Theorem 2.1, minor typo, need to clarify tractability concerns.
