## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a significant extension to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by proposing methods to estimate policy gradients for problems framed using the broad class of coherent risk measures. The authors successfully generalize existing approaches (focused on specific measures like variance or CVaR) into a unified framework, deriving concrete policy gradient and actor-critic algorithms naturally from the problem formulation. 

The work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in risk-sensitive RL, clearly situating itself within the existing literature and acknowledging relevant contributions from areas like stochastic programming and robust MDPs. The motivation is well-articulated: coherent risk measures offer a theoretically sound and widely accepted approach to risk management, making their application to RL highly relevant. The paper’s contribution lies in making this application tractable via policy gradient methods. 

A key strength is the technical soundness of the paper. Equations are clearly presented and justified, and the derivation of gradient formulas, especially Theorem 4.2, appears rigorous. The extension to dynamic risk measures via an actor-critic approach is a valuable contribution, addressing the challenge of time-consistency. The numerical example, while simple (a static three-asset portfolio allocation), effectively demonstrates the algorithm’s risk-averse behavior and highlights the potential for different risk measures to lead to distinct investment strategies.

However, the experiments are indeed limited in scope. A more complex environment, perhaps a dynamic trading scenario with time-varying market conditions, would significantly strengthen the evaluation. Furthermore, a more detailed ablation study analyzing the impact of different hyperparameter choices and convex programming solvers would be beneficial. While the theoretical framework is comprehensive, the practical implications of choosing different coherent risk measures beyond the demonstrated example remain somewhat unexplored. The reliance on Assumption 2.2 regarding the form of the risk envelope might also limit applicability in certain scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem with high significance – risk-sensitive RL is crucial for real-world applications.
*   Presents a clear, meaningful, and novel contribution by extending policy gradient methods to coherent risk measures.
*   Technically sound, well-written, and clearly organized.
*   The unification of existing methods under a single framework is a significant achievement.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Experiments are limited and static, lacking complexity and thoroughness.
*   Practical considerations regarding the choice of coherent risk measures and the applicability of Assumption 2.2 are not fully explored.



**Overall:**

Despite the limitations in experimental validation, the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The clarity of the presentation, the technical rigor, and the potential impact of the work outweigh the shortcomings. I recommend **acceptance** with the encouragement for the authors to expand the experimental evaluation in future work.
