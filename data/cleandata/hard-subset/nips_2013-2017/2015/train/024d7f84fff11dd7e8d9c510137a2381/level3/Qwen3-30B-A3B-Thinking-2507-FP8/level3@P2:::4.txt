

# Review for "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper addresses the important problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific measures like variance or CVaR as previous work has done. The authors present a unified framework that encompasses both static and dynamic coherent risk measures, providing a significant theoretical contribution to the field.

The paper builds on the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), which states that any coherent risk measure can be expressed as the maximum expected value over a convex set of probability distributions (the risk envelope). This insight enables the authors to derive two key results: (1) a general formula for the gradient of static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2), and (2) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2). These results generalize previous work on specific risk measures (such as CVaR in [8, 30] and mean-semideviation in [29, 21]) and provide a unified approach to risk-sensitive RL.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Quality:** The paper makes a strong technical contribution with rigorous proofs of the main theorems. The use of the dual representation and envelope theorem leads to a novel and elegant approach. The consistency result (Proposition 4.4) is well-justified and demonstrates the practical applicability of the gradient estimators.
2. **Originality:** This is a highly original contribution that significantly extends the literature. Previous NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 21, 29, 30]) have focused on specific risk measures, while this paper provides a general framework that encompasses them all. The authors clearly articulate how their work generalizes previous results.
3. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making a complex topic accessible even to non-experts in risk-sensitive optimization. The structure is logical, with clear progression from problem formulation to theoretical results to empirical validation.
4. **Significance:** The framework provides decision-makers with flexibility to choose risk measures appropriate for their specific problem, as demonstrated in the numerical example (Section 6). The connection between coherent risk and model uncertainty (Section 7) opens promising new research directions for handling model misspecification.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Empirical Evaluation:** While the numerical example in Section 6 is illustrative, it lacks depth. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation comparing the proposed algorithm with existing methods across multiple environments would strengthen the paper's claims about practical utility.
2. **Preliminaries Clarity:** The roles of Pθ and ξ in the preliminaries (Section 2) need clearer explanation. The relationship between the risk envelope U(Pθ) and the probability space (Ω,F,Pθ) could be better motivated for readers unfamiliar with risk theory.
3. **Technical Clarification:** Equation 2 appears to be more restrictive than Theorem 2.1's representation. The authors should clarify why they assume this specific convex programming formulation rather than working with the more general representation from Theorem 2.1.
4. **Continuous Distributions:** The paper focuses on finite probability spaces, but the authors should explicitly discuss how their framework generalizes to continuous distributions, which is crucial for most RL applications.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**
- Provides a unified theoretical framework that generalizes multiple previous approaches to risk-sensitive RL
- Offers practical algorithms for gradient estimation using sampling and convex programming
- Demonstrates the importance of flexible risk criterion selection through a compelling numerical example
- Makes a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive optimization in RL

**Con:**
- Empirical validation is limited to a single illustrative example without comprehensive comparisons
- Some technical aspects (risk envelope representation, continuous distribution extension) need further clarification
- The paper does not fully address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper is well-written, the theoretical results are sound, and the approach provides a valuable unification of previous work. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the theoretical contribution is substantial and likely to be of broad interest to the NIPS community. The connection between coherent risk and model uncertainty (Section 7) suggests important future research directions that could have significant impact.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in reinforcement learning with a novel theoretical approach. The authors have made a compelling case for the importance of their framework, and the results are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors could strengthen the paper by addressing the clarifications mentioned above, particularly regarding the risk envelope representation and the extension to continuous distributions. The theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant publication at NIPS despite the limited empirical evaluation.
