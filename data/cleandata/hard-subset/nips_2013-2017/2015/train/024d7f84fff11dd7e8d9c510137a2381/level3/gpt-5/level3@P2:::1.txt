Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The key technical tool is the dual representation of coherent risk via a risk envelope and an envelope-theorem-based differentiation of the associated Lagrangian. For static risks, the authors provide: (i) a general gradient formula that unifies likelihood-ratio estimation with convex programming over the risk envelope, (ii) closed-form expressions for important special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), and (iii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) approach with a consistency result for general coherent risks where analytical saddle points are unavailable. For dynamic risks, the paper treats time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, derives a policy-gradient theorem that connects the gradient to a value function under an adversarially reweighted transition model, and outlines an actor-critic approach leveraging robust MDP connections.

This work generalizes several risk-sensitive RL lines: exponential utility and risk-sensitive control; mean-variance and variance-related criteria; CVaR optimization for both static and dynamic settings; and robust MDP/value function approximation links. The unified, envelope-based derivation appears novel and clarifies how prior risk-specific gradient results (e.g., for CVaR) fall out as special cases.

Strengths:
- Clear unification: A single framework covers a wide family of coherent risks (static and dynamic), subsuming prior risk-specific PG derivations.
- General gradient formula: The Lagrangian/envelope-theorem approach yields implementable estimators and illuminates the structure of risk gradients.
- New results: Closed-form gradients for common measures (e.g., a succinct derivation for CVaR and mean-semideviation) and a general SAA-based estimator with a consistency guarantee under mild conditions.
- Dynamic extension: A policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risks that parallels the standard PG theorem and connects to robust MDPs, enabling actor-critic designs.
- Clarity of assumptions: The finite-space setting, Slater condition for the envelope, and bounded likelihood ratio are stated, helping assess applicability.

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical validation: The general SAA gradient estimator is not empirically evaluated or compared against analytical solutions (e.g., CVaR). The sole numerical illustration is a small static example, insufficient to gauge sample complexity, variance, or robustness.
- Dynamic section is high-level: The actor-critic method is only sketched; key algorithmic details, computational costs (e.g., repeated convex programs, ξ-weighted sampling), and empirical results on MDP benchmarks are missing, making practicality unclear.
- Breadth over depth: Attempting both static and dynamic cases plus multiple examples in eight pages leads to compressed exposition. Important implementation details (e.g., obtaining KKT multipliers in practice, complexity per update, stability when saddle points are non-unique) are deferred to the supplement.
- Scope of assumptions: Finite probability spaces and access to simulators for multiple next-state samples may limit immediate applicability. Claims that Lp extensions are straightforward are unsubstantiated experimentally.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears careful and grounded in convex analysis and likelihood-ratio methods, with reasonable assumptions and connections to prior literature. However, the lack of comprehensive experiments (particularly for the general SAA estimator and the dynamic actor-critic) prevents a full assessment of correctness in practice (variance, bias, convergence behavior).

Clarity:
The writing is generally good and the motivation is well articulated, but the paper is dense. The dynamic section would benefit from concrete pseudo-code, details on computing ξ*, λ*, and hθ, and a discussion of computational/memory complexity. A small running example tracing one gradient computation end-to-end would help.

Originality:
High. Providing a unified policy-gradient framework for coherent risk measures and a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risks is novel and goes beyond prior risk-specific treatments.

Significance:
Potentially high. A general-purpose recipe for risk-sensitive PG could influence both theory and applications. The impact is currently limited by the paucity of empirical validation and practical guidance for dynamic settings.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide an empirical comparison between the SAA gradient estimator and the analytical gradient for CVaR across distributions (light- and heavy-tailed), including bias/variance, sample complexity, and runtime.
- Include dynamic experiments (e.g., standard RL benchmarks) demonstrating the actor-critic method, comparing against risk-neutral baselines and risk-specific methods (e.g., CVaR-focused algorithms).
- Report computational complexity per PG step: cost of solving the convex program, obtaining KKT multipliers, and sampling ξ-weighted transitions. How does this scale with state/action space size?
- Clarify how to sample from ξ-weighted transitions in large/continuous MDPs; discuss feasibility when only a single trajectory is available.
- Discuss robustness to non-unique saddle points and the sensitivity of gradients to envelope constraint smoothness.
- Provide pseudo-code in the main text for the static SAA gradient estimator and the dynamic actor-critic.
- Substantiate the claim that extensions to Lp spaces are straightforward, or bound the scope more cautiously.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, general theoretical contribution unifying risk-sensitive PG across coherent risks.
- Clean derivations that simplify and generalize prior results (notably for CVaR).
- Novel dynamic PG theorem with a principled link to robust MDPs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient empirical evidence, especially for the general estimator and the dynamic algorithm.
- Practicality and computational costs are not substantiated.
- Exposition is compressed; crucial algorithmic details are deferred.

Overall recommendation:
Interesting and potentially impactful, but the experimental evaluation is too limited to assess practicality and robustness. I would lean toward rejection in its current form, with a strong encouragement to either (a) focus on static coherent risks with thorough empirical comparisons and ablations, or (b) expand the dynamic section with complete algorithms and comprehensive experiments. A revised version addressing these points could be a strong contribution.

Confidence: Medium.
