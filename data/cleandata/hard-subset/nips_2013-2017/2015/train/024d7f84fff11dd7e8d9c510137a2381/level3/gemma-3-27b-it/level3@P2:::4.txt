This paper presents a compelling and theoretically strong contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors extend policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, a significant generalization of existing work that typically focuses on specific measures like variance or CVaR. The approach leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures and provides a unified framework for both static and dynamic risk optimization.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with risk-sensitive optimization. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the broader literature. The presentation of the theoretical results is rigorous, and the inclusion of example applications (CVaR, mean-semideviation) helps illustrate the generality of the approach. The authors correctly situate their work within the landscape of related research, citing key papers in risk management, stochastic programming, and reinforcement learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The key strength is the generalization to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This provides a powerful framework for addressing a wide range of risk preferences and application scenarios.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper builds upon a solid theoretical foundation, utilizing the duality representation of coherent risk measures and leveraging the envelope theorem to derive policy gradient formulas.
* **Unified Approach:** The authors successfully present a unified approach for both static and dynamic risk optimization, simplifying the analysis and providing a clear connection between the two settings.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The development of sampling-based gradient estimation algorithms makes the proposed approach applicable to large-scale problems where analytical solutions are intractable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, facilitating understanding of the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the paper presents a numerical illustration using a trading example, the empirical evaluation is relatively limited. A more comprehensive set of experiments, perhaps on standard RL benchmark tasks with different risk preferences, would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Preliminaries Clarity:** The notation in the preliminary section, particularly concerning P<sub>θ</sub> and the role of ξ, could benefit from further clarification. A more intuitive explanation of how these components interact would improve readability.
* **Equation 2 and Theorem 2.1:** The authors claim that Eq. 2 is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1. This warrants further justification. Is it simply a restatement of the convex envelope definition, and if so, why is it presented as a restriction?
* **Smoothness Assumption:** While Assumption 2.2 regarding the smoothness of constraint functions is stated, the paper doesn’t explicitly highlight its importance in proving Theorem 4.2. Discussing the role of this assumption would enhance the technical clarity.
* **Continuous Distributions:**  The paper focuses on finite probability spaces. While acknowledging that the results can be extended, a brief comment on the challenges and potential modifications for continuous distributions would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in reinforcement learning with significant theoretical implications.
* The results are novel and represent a substantial generalization of existing work.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
* The proposed algorithms offer a practical pathway towards risk-sensitive control in large-scale systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical performance and scalability of the proposed algorithms.
* The minor ambiguities in the preliminary section and regarding Equation 2 detract slightly from the overall clarity of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with significant potential impact. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified and general framework for optimizing coherent risk measures. While the empirical evaluation is limited, the theoretical results are compelling and well-supported. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the minor clarity issues and perhaps including a more detailed discussion of the extension to continuous distributions. A stronger empirical validation in the camera-ready version would further bolster the paper’s impact.

**Confidence:** 8/10.  The proofs are intricate and require thorough verification, lowering my confidence slightly, but the overall approach and presentation are excellent.
