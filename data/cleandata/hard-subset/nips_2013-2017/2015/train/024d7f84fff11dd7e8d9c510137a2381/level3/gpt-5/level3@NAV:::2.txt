This paper develops policy-gradient estimators for risk-sensitive RL under the broad class of coherent risk measures, covering both static and time-consistent dynamic settings. The core idea leverages the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risks and the envelope theorem to derive a general gradient formula for static risk, with concrete instantiations (CVaR, mean-semideviation) and a sample-average approximation that couples Monte Carlo with a small convex program. For dynamic (Markov coherent) risk, the authors prove a policy-gradient theorem that relates the gradient to a value function defined by a risk-sensitive Bellman equation, and propose an actor-critic procedure that uses robust-MDP structure to estimate values, then samples under ξ-weighted transitions to estimate gradients. A toy finance example illustrates how different coherent risks induce distinct, interpretable behaviors. This work unifies and extends prior NeurIPS work on CVaR optimization in MDPs and risk-sensitive actor-critic, as well as robust MDP connections.

Quality: The derivations are technically sound and well-grounded in convex analysis; the static gradient formula and the consistency result for the SAA-based estimator are carefully stated. The recovery of known special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation) validates the framework. The main weakness is empirical: the dynamic algorithm lacks experimental validation, and the toy example is static and small-scale, leaving open questions about sample efficiency, stability, and computational overhead when the convex subproblems and saddle-point multipliers are computed per update.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. Minor suggestions: discuss uniqueness/non-uniqueness of saddle points and its impact on estimator variance; provide computational complexity (per-step) of the convex program and KKT retrieval; clarify assumptions needed to sample from ξ-weighted transitions and the practical mechanism to obtain them.

Originality: Extending risk-sensitive policy gradients from specific measures to the full coherent class is a substantial and nontrivial generalization that yields a unified view and new algorithms.

Significance: High. The framework should be of broad interest to researchers and practitioners who need flexible risk modeling beyond expectation or CVaR.

Pros for acceptance:
- Unified, principled extension to coherent risks with clean theory.
- Recovers and simplifies prior results; offers general estimators with consistency.
- Connects risk-sensitive RL to robust MDPs in an actionable way.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited experiments; no dynamic benchmarks.
- Practicality concerns: finite-space assumptions, need for simulators and ξ-weighted sampling, and per-iteration convex programs.
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines for risk-sensitive control on standard RL tasks.

Constructive suggestions: add dynamic experiments (e.g., control/gridworlds) across several coherent risks (e.g., spectral), report variance and wall-clock, compare to CVaR actor-critic and mean-variance methods, and study sensitivity to envelope specifications and function-approximation errors.
