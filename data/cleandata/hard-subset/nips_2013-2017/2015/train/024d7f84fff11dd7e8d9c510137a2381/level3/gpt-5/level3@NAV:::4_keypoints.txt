- Tackles policy optimization under coherent risk using dual representation and derives policy-gradient formulas for static and dynamic settings via envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio identities.  
- Static-risk results: analytic gradients for key cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation) and a general gradient estimator combining sampling with a convex program over the risk envelope.  
- Dynamic (time-consistent Markov coherent) results: policy-gradient theorem expressed via ξ-weighted transitions and a proposed actor-critic algorithm.  
- Unifies and generalizes prior CVaR-in-MDP, risk-sensitive actor-critic, and variance-related work; links to robust MDP interpretations.  
- Theoretical development is compelling and appears technically sound under assumptions (e.g., Slater feasibility, smoothness); sample-average estimator consistency is shown.  
- Key proofs (notably Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) require careful verification.  
- Practicality concern: repeatedly solving convex programs and sampling under ξ-weighted dynamics may be computationally and implementationally heavy.  
- Empirical evaluation is illustrative but too limited to assess estimator variance, bias, and scalability.  
- Clarity is good overall, but preliminaries should better distinguish Pθ (data-generating) vs ξ (adversarial reweighting) and make Eξ explicit as expectation under ξPθ.  
- Originality: high—unified analysis and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem are novel and impactful.  
- Significance: high—coherent risk is important in finance/OR and a general policy-gradient toolkit has broad impact.  
- Question: clarify Assumption 2.2’s convex/conic representation vs Theorem 2.1 and which coherent measures are excluded by smoothness requirements.  
- Suggestion: emphasize the role of envelope-constraint smoothness and the M-Lipschitz bound when applying the envelope theorem in Theorem 4.2 and Proposition 4.4.  
- Dynamic-case suggestion: discuss feasibility of sampling under ξ-weighted transitions and possible off-policy or importance-sampling alternatives.  
- Extension request: briefly discuss generalization to continuous distributions (Lp spaces), required regularity, and how SAA and KKT-based gradients extend.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong unified theoretical contribution, clear robust-MDP connections, and practical gradient estimators with consistency guarantees.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited empirical validation, some assumptions/notation need clearer justification, and computational/sampling burdens are not fully assessed.
