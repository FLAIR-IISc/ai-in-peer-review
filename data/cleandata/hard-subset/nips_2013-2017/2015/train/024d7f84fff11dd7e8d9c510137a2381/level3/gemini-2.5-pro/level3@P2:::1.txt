This paper proposes a unified framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending their application to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This work generalizes previous studies that have focused on specific risk measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors address both static coherent risk, applied to the total discounted return, and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

The main contributions are threefold. First, for the static case, the paper leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures to derive a new, general formula for the policy gradient. This formula is shown to recover and simplify existing gradient expressions for specific measures like CVaR. Second, for static risks where an analytical solution to the dual problem is unavailable, the authors propose a general algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming (a sample average approximation) and provide a proof of its consistency. Third, for the dynamic case, the paper presents a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, which relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The most significant contribution of this work is the development of a unified theoretical framework that connects policy gradient methods with the axiomatic theory of coherent risk measures. This is a novel and valuable contribution that generalizes a large body of prior work in risk-sensitive RL. By providing tools for any coherent risk measure, the paper offers practitioners and researchers significant flexibility in designing risk-sensitive objectives tailored to specific problems, moving beyond the standard mean-variance or CVaR criteria.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the policy gradient formula for static risk (Theorem 4.2) is elegant and provides new insights. The theoretical results for the static case, including the consistency of the general sampling-based estimator (Proposition 4.4), appear technically sound and well-supported.
*   **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the importance of coherent risk measures and time consistency. The numerical example in Section 6, while simple, effectively demonstrates why having a choice of risk measures is crucial, as different measures can lead to qualitatively different and potentially more rational policies.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper's primary weakness is the near-total absence of empirical validation for the proposed algorithms. The work is almost entirely theoretical. The single numerical example is a stateless multi-armed bandit problem designed only to illustrate the conceptual difference between risk measures. It does not test the performance, sample efficiency, or stability of the proposed gradient estimation algorithms in a sequential decision-making problem. This makes it impossible to fully assess the practical utility and potential limitations of the proposed methods.
*   **Clarity and Scope:** The paper attempts to cover a very broad scope—static risk with analytical gradients, static risk with sample-based gradients, and dynamic risk with an actor-critic algorithm—all within eight pages. This ambition comes at the cost of clarity and depth. The presentation feels rushed, particularly in the later sections.
*   **Underdeveloped Dynamic Risk Section:** The treatment of dynamic coherent risk is high-level and feels incomplete. While Theorem 5.2 provides a policy gradient expression, the corresponding actor-critic algorithm is only sketched out, with key details, analysis, and experiments deferred to the supplementary material. As a standalone paper, this section does not provide enough information for a reader to understand or implement the proposed method, nor to evaluate its feasibility.
*   **Limited Applicability of Analytical Approach:** The elegant closed-form gradient derivations rely on finding an analytical solution for the Lagrangian saddle point. As the authors acknowledge, this is only possible for a limited number of risk measures. The more general sample-based approximation is proposed to address this, but its practical performance is not demonstrated. For instance, a comparison between the analytical and sample-based estimators for CVaR is mentioned as being in the supplement but is absent from the main paper, which would have been a valuable inclusion to build confidence in the general method.

**Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and elegant unification of policy gradient methods and the theory of coherent risk measures, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the risk-sensitive RL community.
2.  The derived gradient formulas generalize several previous results and provide a principled foundation for developing new risk-sensitive algorithms.
3.  The work is likely to be influential and could spur further research into the use of diverse risk measures in reinforcement learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The claims are not supported by sufficient experimental evidence. The lack of experiments on standard RL benchmarks makes it difficult to assess the practical value and performance of the proposed algorithms.
2.  The section on dynamic risk is underdeveloped and not self-contained, weakening the overall contribution of the paper.
3.  The paper's scope is too broad for its length, resulting in a presentation that lacks depth, particularly concerning the practical implementation and evaluation of the proposed methods.

**Recommendation**

This paper introduces interesting and theoretically sound ideas that could have a significant impact on the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, the work in its current form feels more like a position paper or a theoretical report than a complete conference paper, primarily due to the severe lack of empirical validation. A stronger paper would have focused on a smaller part of the contribution (e.g., only the static risk measures) and provided a thorough experimental section to demonstrate the effectiveness and properties of the proposed algorithms. While the theoretical novelty is high, the absence of experiments makes a full assessment of the contribution difficult.
