

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The work is well-motivated, as the authors correctly note that the choice of risk measure is problem-dependent, and there is a need for a general approach that can accommodate different risk preferences across applications.

The paper's main technical contribution is a rigorous theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive policy gradients under the coherent risk framework. The authors derive a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, and they develop a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that supports an actor-critic style algorithm for dynamic risk measures. These results generalize and extend previous work that focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), with the authors demonstrating that prior results are special cases of their general framework.

Quality is strong throughout. The mathematical derivations are technically sound, with careful application of the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1). The authors provide a consistency result for their sampling-based estimators, showing convergence to the true gradient as sample size increases. The paper carefully addresses the challenges of applying coherent risk measures to RL problems, including the handling of time consistency in dynamic settings. The theoretical results are well-supported by the proofs and assumptions, and the authors demonstrate appropriate care in evaluating both strengths and limitations.

Clarity is excellent. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear definitions, logical flow from problem formulation to solution, and precise mathematical notation. The connection between the new results and previous work in risk-sensitive RL is explicitly articulated, making it easy for readers to understand the paper's contribution within the broader literature. The experimental section effectively demonstrates the flexibility of the framework across different risk preferences, showing how different risk measures lead to different optimal policies in a portfolio selection problem.

Originality is significant. While risk-sensitive RL has been studied for specific risk measures (e.g., [8, 21, 29, 30]), this paper's contribution is to provide a unified framework that encompasses all coherent risk measures. The extension to dynamic risk measures using Markov coherent risk is particularly novel, as it addresses the important issue of time consistency in risk-sensitive decision making. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach generalizes and simplifies previous results, which is a meaningful conceptual advance.

Significance is high. Risk-sensitive RL is an important problem with applications in finance, operations research, and other domains where decision-makers need to account for variability in outcomes. The paper's unified approach provides a practical framework that can be applied to a wide range of risk-sensitive problems. The experimental results, while simple, effectively illustrate the key insight that different risk measures lead to different optimal policies, which has important implications for practitioners. The connection to model uncertainty in the conclusion suggests broader applications beyond the immediate problem.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, building on recent work in risk-sensitive RL while making a clear conceptual contribution. The authors cite relevant prior work thoroughly, and the paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

Strengths include the unified framework that generalizes previous specific-risk approaches, rigorous theoretical foundation with consistency results, clear connection to prior work, well-executed experimental demonstration of the framework's flexibility, and strong technical writing.

Weaknesses include limited experimental scope (the financial example, while illustrative, could be supplemented with more complex benchmarks) and insufficient discussion of computational complexity trade-offs between the static and dynamic approaches. The connection to model uncertainty in the conclusion could also be more fully developed as a practical implication.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful conceptual advance by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The work generalizes previous approaches and provides a practical foundation for risk-sensitive RL applications. The results are clearly aligned with NIPS topics and would be of interest to researchers working on risk-sensitive decision making, reinforcement learning, and optimization under uncertainty. I recommend acceptance.
