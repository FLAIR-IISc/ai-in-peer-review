- Main contribution: Extends policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, providing a unified risk-sensitive RL framework.  
- Technical advances: Derives (1) a new sampling-friendly gradient formula for static coherent risk and (2) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk enabling actor-critic algorithms.  
- Key insight: Uses the representation of coherent risk as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope (Theorem 2.1) and the envelope theorem to obtain gradient formulas.  
- Generality: Framework recovers prior special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation, etc.) as instances, unifying earlier results.  
- Algorithms: Combines convex programming with policy gradient sampling to produce practical estimation procedures.  
- Experiments: Simple finance example shows different risk measures produce different risk-averse investment choices, demonstrating flexibility.  
- Writing and exposition: Clear, well-written paper with rigorous proofs and reproducible supplementary material.  
- Conceptual significance: Connects coherent risk measures to model uncertainty, offering broader interpretation beyond technical results.  
- Strength — theoretical soundness: Rigorous development with well-justified assumptions and clear proofs.  
- Strength — applicability: Provides practical algorithms and a concrete example illustrating behavior under different risks.  
- Weakness — experiments: Empirical evaluation is limited to a single toy example and lacks comparisons on complex benchmarks.  
- Weakness — simulator assumption: Assumes access to a simulator for sampling multiple transitions per state, which may limit real-world applicability.  
- Weakness — model-uncertainty connection: The link to model uncertainty is intriguing but underdeveloped in the current work.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 4/5, Clarity 5/5, Originality 4/5, Significance 4/5.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept for NIPS — technical depth, clarity, and significance outweigh limited experiments.  
- Pro-acceptance: Unified framework, rigorous theory, natural algorithms, demonstrated risk-averse behavior, excellent writing.  
- Con-acceptance: Limited experimental scope, simulator dependence, and incomplete development of model-uncertainty implications.
