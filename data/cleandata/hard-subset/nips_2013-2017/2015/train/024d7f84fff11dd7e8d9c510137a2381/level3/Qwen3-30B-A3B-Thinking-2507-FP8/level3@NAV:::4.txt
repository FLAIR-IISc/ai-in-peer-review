

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, addressing a significant gap in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions to derive gradient estimators for both static and dynamic risk settings. This approach generalizes previous work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance) and provides a theoretically grounded foundation for risk-sensitive policy optimization.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical contribution is compelling, with Theorem 4.2 providing a general gradient formula that unifies disparate risk measures under a single framework. The presentation is exceptionally clear and accessible, making sophisticated risk theory approachable for non-specialists in the field. The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous proofs of consistency (Proposition 4.4) and successfully connects to established risk theory through Theorem 2.1.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The empirical evaluation is insufficiently detailed, lacking comparative analysis with existing risk-sensitive methods and quantitative metrics on convergence rates. The preliminaries are unclear regarding the roles of P_θ and ξ, which would benefit from more precise definitions. A critical technical question remains: Equation 2 appears to be the standard convex envelope definition, yet the authors claim it's more restrictive than Theorem 2.1—this requires clarification. Additionally, the paper should discuss how the analysis extends to continuous distributions, and the role of smoothness in Theorem 4.2's proof needs explicit mention.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by unifying a broad class of risk measures, but the empirical validation is weak. While the theoretical results are strong, the limited empirical evaluation and unclear technical points prevent full assessment of practical utility. The work is highly original, significantly extending previous risk-sensitive RL approaches, and addresses an important problem with clear significance for risk-averse decision-making.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution, excellent presentation, significant unification of risk measures.
Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient empirical evaluation, unclear technical points requiring clarification.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical paper with compelling results that would benefit from deeper empirical validation and clarification of technical points.
