### Summary

This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) under coherent risk measures. It generalizes recent work that developed risk-sensitive policy gradient methods for specific measures, such as Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) or variance, to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive policy gradient theorems for two settings: (1) optimizing a static coherent risk measure of the total discounted cost, and (2) optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measure. For the static case, the proposed algorithm combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient. For the dynamic case, an actor-critic style algorithm is developed. The work successfully unifies and simplifies several previous results, presenting them as special cases of this more general framework.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is of high quality. The theoretical results appear to be technically sound, building upon the dual representation of coherent risk measures and applying the Envelope Theorem to derive the gradient formulas. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors first introduce the necessary background on coherent and dynamic risk measures, clearly state the problem formulation, and then systematically derive the gradient formulas for the static and dynamic cases. The exposition is easy to follow, and the connection between the static and dynamic risk settings is well-motivated. The inclusion of examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively demonstrates the utility and generality of the main result (Theorem 4.2).

### Originality and Significance

The main contribution of this paper is the generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the full class of coherent risk measures. While methods for specific risk measures like CVaR have been explored previously (e.g., [8, 30]), this paper provides a unified theoretical foundation and a general algorithmic approach. The extension itself is a relatively direct application of the dual representation of coherent risk measures and standard policy gradient techniques (the 'likelihood-ratio trick'). As such, the work can be seen as a minor but important and solid extension of existing results rather than a completely novel approach.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential impact on the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. By providing a single, principled framework, it allows researchers and practitioners to easily apply policy gradient methods to a wide variety of risk preferences without having to derive custom algorithms for each one. This flexibility is crucial, as the choice of an appropriate risk measure is often application-dependent. The paper advances the state of the art by making risk-sensitive RL more accessible and broadly applicable. The connection drawn between coherent risk and model uncertainty also points toward promising future research directions.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact.

**Pros:**
*   Provides a unified and general framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures, which is a valuable contribution.
*   Generalizes and simplifies the derivation of several previous results (e.g., for CVaR).
*   The paper is very well-written, structured, and easy to follow.
*   The theoretical results appear correct and are derived rigorously.

**Cons/Suggestions:**
1.  **Notation in Theorem 2.1:** The notation `ξPθ` used to define the set `U` and in the maximization problem is a bit confusing. It appears `ξ` is intended to be the density of a new measure with respect to `Pθ`. Using the notation from Theorem 6.6 in the cited reference [26] (Shapiro et al.), where the risk is represented as a maximum expectation over a set of probability measures, might be clearer for readers familiar with that literature.
2.  **Typo:** There is a minor typo on line 141: "risk enevlop[e]".
3.  **Clarification on Tractability:** In Section 3, the paper states that the Static Risk Problem (SRP) and Dynamic Risk Problem (DRP) are generally not tractable. It would be beneficial to clarify that this intractability primarily arises from the potentially complex and non-convex dependency of the state-visitation distribution on the policy parameters `θ`, especially in large or continuous spaces where function approximation is needed. This is a standard challenge in policy gradient methods. The clarification would help distinguish this from the tractability of the underlying risk-averse planning problem; MDPs with dynamic Markov risk measures are, in fact, tractable and can be solved with dynamic programming in tabular settings, as shown in [24].

### Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that presents a minor but solid and important extension of existing results in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It provides a clean, unified framework for applying policy gradient methods to any coherent risk measure, which is a significant step forward in generality and practical utility. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a relevant problem. It is a strong contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.
