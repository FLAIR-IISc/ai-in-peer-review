

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, extending previous work on risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors recognize that while numerous risk measures have been studied (e.g., CVaR, variance, mean-semideviation), there exists no general approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures (satisfying convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity). The paper's key contribution is formulating a gradient-based approach that applies to all such risk measures, both static and dynamic.

The theoretical foundation rests on the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), which expresses any coherent risk as the maximum expected value over a convex set of probability distributions (the risk envelope). The authors leverage this representation to derive novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk measures. For static risk, they develop a sampling-based estimator that combines convex programming with likelihood ratio techniques (Theorem 4.2), while for dynamic risk, they extend the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2), enabling an actor-critic algorithm. Crucially, the paper demonstrates that numerous specific risk-sensitive approaches (e.g., for CVaR, mean-semideviation) are special cases of their framework, providing a more general and simpler derivation.

The paper is well-organized and accessible, with clear mathematical exposition that would be understandable to NIPS readers beyond the risk-sensitive RL niche. The theoretical contributions are significant: they unify previous work, generalize results, and provide a principled approach for risk-sensitive policy optimization. The numerical example in Section 6 compellingly demonstrates the practical importance of choosing appropriate risk measures for specific problem characteristics, showing how different risk criteria (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to different policy behaviors even when optimizing the same objective.

The paper's quality is strong, with careful theoretical development and clear connections to prior work. The authors properly address related work, acknowledging that prior approaches were limited to specific risk measures or system dynamics. The assumptions (particularly Assumption 2.2) are well-justified, though the role of Pθ and ξ in the preliminaries could be clarified (e.g., explicitly stating that Pθ represents the probability distribution induced by the policy, and ξ is the risk envelope weight). The technical question regarding whether Equation 2 is more restrictive than Theorem 2.1 is important; while the paper assumes a specific convex programming formulation for the risk envelope, this is a standard and well-motivated representation for many practical risk measures.

The main weakness is the limited empirical evaluation. While the numerical example demonstrates conceptual differences between risk measures, the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons of the proposed gradient estimators against baselines (e.g., for CVaR, how does the SAA approach in Section 4.3 compare to the analytical approach in Section 4.1?). A more thorough empirical analysis would strengthen the paper's significance claim. Additionally, the paper could benefit from discussing how their approach extends to continuous distributions (as suggested in the key points), though the finite probability space assumption is common in theoretical RL papers.

The paper's significance is high: it provides a general framework for risk-sensitive RL that is applicable to a broad range of problems in finance, operations research, and other domains where risk management is critical. The connection to model uncertainty in the conclusion is particularly promising and suggests broader applicability beyond the specific risk measures considered.

Strengths:
- Unified framework for coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work
- Novel gradient formulas with clear theoretical derivation
- Well-written and accessible presentation
- Demonstrated flexibility in risk preference design through numerical example
- Strong theoretical foundation with appropriate assumptions

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical evaluation of the gradient estimation algorithms
- Some unclear exposition in preliminaries regarding Pθ and ξ
- No discussion of extension to continuous distributions
- Theoretical proofs require verification (as noted in key points)

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a general approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of risk measure selection in decision making, and its framework offers both theoretical and practical value. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded, the theoretical contributions are substantial and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the preliminaries and strengthen the empirical analysis.
