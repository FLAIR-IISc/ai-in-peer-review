This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) with coherent risk objectives. The authors derive policy gradient formulas for two settings: (1) optimizing a static coherent risk measure of the total discounted cost, and (2) optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measure. The proposed methods generalize several recent works that have developed policy gradient algorithms for specific risk measures, such as variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). For static risk, the approach combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient, leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures. For dynamic risk, the paper develops a corresponding risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem and an actor-critic algorithm.

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported. The derivation of the main gradient formula in Theorem 4.2, using the envelope theorem and the standard likelihood-ratio trick, appears correct and is a clever way to handle the maximization inherent in the definition of coherent risk. The authors provide a consistency result (Proposition 4.4) for their general gradient estimation algorithm, which strengthens the theoretical grounding of the proposed method. The extension to the dynamic, time-consistent case is also well-founded, resulting in a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) that naturally extends its risk-neutral counterpart. The work is presented as a complete contribution, with theoretical derivations, algorithms, and a numerical illustration.

### Clarity
The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The introduction and preliminaries do an excellent job of motivating the problem and introducing the concept of coherent risk measures to an RL audience. The progression from the static risk case to the more complex dynamic risk case is logical.

A few points could be clarified to further improve the manuscript:
*   The notation "ξPθ" used in Theorem 2.1 and throughout the paper is a bit confusing. It seems to denote the density of a new measure with respect to the original measure Pθ. Using a more standard notation, such as the Radon-Nikodym derivative dQ/dPθ or simply ξ(ω), as used in the cited reference [26] (Theorem 6.6), would improve readability.
*   In the introduction and problem formulation, the authors should be more precise about the source of computational intractability. They state that dynamic programming for dynamic risk does not scale up. While this is true for MDPs with large state spaces, MDPs with dynamic Markov coherent risk objectives are, in principle, tractable and can be solved with dynamic programming. The primary motivation for using policy gradient methods here is, as is standard in RL, the use of policy parameterization (function approximation) to handle large or continuous state/action spaces. Clarifying this would strengthen the problem's motivation.
*   There is a minor typo on line 141: "risk enevlop[e]".

### Originality
The paper's main contribution is the generalization of policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. While previous work (e.g., [8, 21, 30]) has addressed specific risk measures, this paper provides a unified framework that subsumes these earlier results. For example, the authors show how their general gradient formula for static risk recovers the recently derived formula for CVaR [30] as a special case, and with a simpler proof. The core techniques—the likelihood-ratio method, sample average approximation, and actor-critic algorithms—are themselves well-established. However, their combination with the dual representation of coherent risk measures to create a general-purpose, risk-sensitive policy gradient algorithm is novel and insightful.

### Significance
The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be useful for both researchers and practitioners in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. By providing a single, principled framework for a broad class of risk measures, the work obviates the need for deriving bespoke algorithms for every new risk criterion. This is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art. The flexibility this framework provides is important, as highlighted by the numerical experiment in Section 6. The example effectively illustrates that the choice of risk measure is problem-dependent and that having a general toolbox can lead to more rational and desirable policies compared to using a one-size-fits-all risk measure. This work provides a strong foundation for future research in risk-sensitive RL, for example, in developing more sample-efficient algorithms or exploring the connection between risk-shaping and model uncertainty.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper provides a significant and useful generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, unifying several disparate prior results.
2.  The work is technically solid, with correct derivations and supporting theoretical analysis.
3.  The paper is very well-written and presents its contributions in a clear and accessible manner.
4.  The proposed framework is of practical importance, giving users the flexibility to design risk objectives tailored to their specific problems without needing to develop new algorithms from scratch.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The contribution could be viewed as an incremental extension, as it applies well-known techniques (policy gradients, SAA, envelope theorem) to the established theory of coherent risk measures.

### Overall Assessment
This is a solid and important paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While the technical tools used are not new in isolation, their synthesis to create a general policy gradient framework for all coherent risk measures is novel, useful, and elegant. The paper is well-executed in terms of technical quality, clarity, and motivation. The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
