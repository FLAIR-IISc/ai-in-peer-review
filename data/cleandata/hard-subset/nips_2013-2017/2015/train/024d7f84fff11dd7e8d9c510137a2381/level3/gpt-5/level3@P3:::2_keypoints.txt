- Summary: Develops policy-gradient estimators for coherent risk measures (static and dynamic), deriving gradient formulas via risk envelopes and the envelope theorem and proposing an actor-critic algorithm for Markov coherent risk.
- Strength: Unifies risk-sensitive policy gradient across all coherent risks using a clean framework linked to robust MDPs.
- Strength: Static gradient derivation (envelope theorem + likelihood ratios) and dynamic policy-gradient theorem are technically sound and elegant.
- Strength: Provides concrete algorithms: closed-form estimators for CVaR and mean-semideviation, SAA for general static risk, and an actor-critic for dynamic risk.
- Strength: Writing, equations, and assumptions are clear; related work and connections to prior NIPS work are well covered.
- Strength: The generalization to coherent and time-consistent dynamic risks is nontrivial and original.
- Strength: Addresses an important problem with high potential impact across RL, finance, and operations.
- Strength: Empirical toy example effectively demonstrates different risk preferences yield different behaviors.
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is minimal—single static toy finance example with no dynamic MDP experiments or baseline comparisons.
- Weakness: Computational overhead concerns—each gradient estimate may require solving a convex program and KKT retrieval; scalability and wall-clock cost are untested.
- Weakness: Sampling assumptions are strong—the actor-critic needs multiple next-state simulations or ξ-weighted sampling not available in many settings.
- Weakness: No finite-sample, variance, or rare-event sensitivity analyses; only consistency for SAA is provided.
- Weakness: Some assumptions (finite outcome space extensions, Slater-type conditions, saddle-point uniqueness) are standard but not empirically stress-tested.
- Weakness: Limited practical guidance on designing risk envelopes for real tasks.
- Quality: Theoretical development is solid and well-supported; algorithmic sketches are reasonable though implementation details are deferred.
- Clarity: Paper is well organized and readable; adding pseudo-code for the actor-critic would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: Meaningful and sufficient—extends prior risk-specific work to the full class of coherent risks including dynamic measures.
- Significance: High potential impact as a general approach to risk-sensitive RL with adaptable risk attitudes.
- Suggestion: Add dynamic MDP experiments with function approximation and comparisons to robust MDP and CVaR/mean-variance baselines.
- Suggestion: Provide computational benchmarks (per-iteration cost, scaling with samples and constraints).
- Suggestion: Empirically explore variance reduction techniques (e.g., importance sampling) for tail risks.
- Suggestion: Clarify implementation of ξ-weighted transition sampling in environments without a generative model and discuss off-policy corrections.
- Suggestion: Include actor-critic pseudo-code and practical details (features, stepsizes, diagnostics).
- Argument for acceptance: Unified, technically sound framework that generalizes and simplifies multiple prior results with practical algorithms and guarantees.
- Argument for acceptance: Clear connections to prior work and nontrivial generalization to coherent, time-consistent risks.
- Argument against acceptance: Very limited empirical validation and lack of dynamic tasks or comparative baselines.
- Argument against acceptance: Scalability and practicality concerns due to inner convex programs and special sampling requirements.
- Argument against acceptance: No finite-sample/variance guarantees and limited practitioner guidance on envelope design.
- Recommendation: Leaning accept—strong conceptual and technical contributions, but empirical strengthening would make acceptance clearer.
