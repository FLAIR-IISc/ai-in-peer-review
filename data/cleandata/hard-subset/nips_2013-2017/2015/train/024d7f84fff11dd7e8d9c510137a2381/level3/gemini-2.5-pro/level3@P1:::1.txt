This paper introduces a unified framework for policy gradient reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors extend risk-sensitive RL beyond specific measures like variance or CVaR, which have been studied in prior work, to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The core contributions are threefold: (1) a novel formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure, derived using its dual representation and the envelope theorem; (2) a general, sampling-based algorithm that combines convex programming with sample average approximation (SAA) to estimate this gradient when an analytical solution is unavailable, along with a proof of consistency; and (3) an extension of these ideas to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, resulting in a new policy gradient theorem and a sketch of an actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its generality. By leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures, the authors provide a single, elegant framework that unifies and generalizes several previous results on risk-sensitive policy gradients (e.g., for CVaR). This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of risk-sensitive decision-making, providing a principled approach for a broad and axiomatically justified class of risk measures.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound. The derivation of the static gradient formula in Theorem 4.2 is novel and provides a clear path for deriving policy gradients for any coherent risk measure, provided its risk envelope is known. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively demonstrate the power of this result. The consistency proof for the general SAA-based gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) further strengthens the paper's technical foundation.
*   **Potential Impact:** The framework offers practitioners significant flexibility. The numerical example in Section 6, while simple, effectively illustrates why having access to different risk measures (like mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) is crucial, as the choice can lead to qualitatively different and more rational policies, especially in the presence of heavy-tailed distributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Scope:** The paper attempts to cover too much ground in eight pages. It addresses static risk with analytical gradients, static risk with sampling-based gradients, and dynamic risk with an actor-critic approach. This breadth comes at the cost of depth and clarity. The section on dynamic risk measures (Section 5) is particularly high-level. It presents a policy gradient theorem but provides very few details on the proposed actor-critic algorithm, its implementation, or its analysis, deferring most of it to supplementary material. This makes the second half of the paper feel rushed and incomplete, and its contribution difficult to fully assess.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the paper's claims. The only experiment is a simple three-armed bandit problem illustrating the static case. Crucially, there are no experiments for the more complex dynamic risk setting. Furthermore, the proposed general SAA-based gradient estimator—a key practical contribution for risk measures without a closed-form saddle point—is not evaluated at all. A valuable and necessary experiment would have been to compare the performance (e.g., variance, bias, sample complexity) of the SAA estimator against the analytical gradient for a case like CVaR where both are applicable. The paper mentions such a comparison in the supplementary material, but its absence from the main paper is a notable weakness.
*   **Practicality Concerns:** The practical applicability of the proposed methods remains unclear due to the lack of empirical evidence and algorithmic detail. The SAA algorithm requires solving a convex optimization problem at each step, which could be computationally expensive. The dynamic risk algorithm appears to require a simulator for multi-sample rollouts from each state, which may limit its use in real-world scenarios where only single trajectories are available. These practical aspects are not adequately discussed or evaluated.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper would be much stronger if it were split into two separate submissions or if it focused solely on the static risk case. A focused paper on static risk could provide a more detailed exposition of the SAA algorithm, including pseudocode, and a comprehensive empirical evaluation on more challenging problems. This would allow for a thorough comparison between analytical and sample-based estimators and an analysis of the computational trade-offs.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pro:**
*   Provides a novel, elegant, and unified theoretical framework for policy gradients with the entire class of coherent risk measures.
*   Generalizes and simplifies several previous results for specific risk measures.
*   The core theoretical results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are technically sound and significant contributions.

**Con:**
*   The scope is too broad, leading to a superficial treatment of the dynamic risk case.
*   The empirical evaluation is very limited and does not validate the main algorithmic contributions (the SAA estimator and the dynamic actor-critic algorithm).
*   The lack of detail and experiments makes the practical significance and performance of the proposed methods difficult to judge.

In conclusion, this paper presents interesting and theoretically sound ideas that have the potential for broad impact. However, the current manuscript feels like two papers in one, and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation significantly weakens its overall contribution.
