

This paper makes a solid contribution by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, unifying previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static and time-consistent dynamic coherent risk measures, generalizing prior results while maintaining computational tractability. Their approach for static risk combines sampling with convex programming, while the dynamic case employs an actor-critic framework with explicit value function approximation.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to the RL community. The technical content appears sound, with correct derivations and appropriate theoretical grounding. The extension from specific risk measures to the general coherent risk framework is valuable, though the authors correctly characterize it as a minor but useful generalization rather than a major breakthrough. The numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical benefits of their framework by showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a trading application.

The paper addresses several important points that enhance its quality and clarity. The authors clarify that tractability issues stem from policy parametrization rather than the Markov risk measures themselves, which is crucial for understanding the scope of their approach. However, I recommend addressing two specific issues: (1) The notation "ξP_θ" in Theorem 2.1 is confusing and should follow the notation used in Theorem 6.6 of [26] for consistency; (2) There's a typo on line 141 ("risk enevlop[e]").

The paper demonstrates significant technical quality through its rigorous treatment of coherent risk measures and their application to RL. It advances the field by providing a unified framework that simplifies the implementation of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods for practitioners. While the extension itself is not revolutionary, it represents an important step toward making risk-sensitive RL more accessible and broadly applicable across different problem domains.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature. The results are correct, the presentation is clear, and the framework provides valuable flexibility for risk-sensitive decision making. The minor issues noted are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's overall quality. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
