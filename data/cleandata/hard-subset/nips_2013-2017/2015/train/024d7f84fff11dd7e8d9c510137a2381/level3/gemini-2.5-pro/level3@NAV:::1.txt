This paper presents a unified framework for deriving policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures, a significant generalization of prior work in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning which has largely focused on specific measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The core contribution is a novel formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure, derived from its dual representation. This formula enables both the analytical derivation of policy gradients for specific measures and the development of a general, sampling-based algorithm for cases where analytical solutions are intractable. The authors also extend this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its **originality** and **significance**. The unified derivation is elegant and provides a novel, principled approach to a problem previously addressed in a piecemeal fashion. It successfully generalizes and simplifies several existing results, such as the policy gradient for CVaR. The theoretical **quality** is also strong; the paper proposes a general sampling-based gradient estimation algorithm and provides a proof of its consistency, which is a valuable contribution. The conceptual extension to dynamic risk measures is also a promising direction.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the strong theoretical foundation, the paper's main weakness is a significant lack of empirical validation, which affects its overall **quality** as a complete piece of work. The proposed sample-based algorithm, which is key to the framework's general applicability, is not experimentally tested. A crucial and seemingly straightforward experiment would be to compare the performance of the sample-based estimator against the analytical gradient for a measure like CVaR, which would provide insight into its practical performance and sample efficiency.

Furthermore, the paper's **clarity** suffers from its ambitious scope. Attempting to cover static risk, a general sampling algorithm, and dynamic risk within eight pages results in a dense presentation. The section on dynamic risk, in particular, is presented at a very high level, lacking sufficient algorithmic detail or experimental results to assess its practical utility. This makes the latter part of the paper feel more like a preliminary proposal than a fully-realized contribution.

**Conclusion:**
This paper introduces an interesting and potentially impactful theoretical framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients. However, the lack of comprehensive experiments prevents a full assessment of the proposed methods' practical value. I would encourage the authors to consider focusing the paper on the static risk case. This would allow for a more thorough presentation and, most importantly, provide space for a detailed empirical evaluation of the novel sampling-based algorithm. While the ideas are promising, the work in its current form feels incomplete.
