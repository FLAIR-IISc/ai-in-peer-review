This paper presents a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the general class of coherent risk measures. The motivation is clear – existing methods often focus on specific risk measures, while a unified framework based on coherence is desirable due to its theoretical grounding in finance and operations research. The paper logically builds upon prior work, offering both theoretical results and concrete algorithms (policy gradient and actor-critic) for both static and dynamic risk settings.

The core idea is to leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to formulate and optimize risk-sensitive policy gradients. The derived gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are the key contributions, generalizing previous results and allowing for sampling-based estimation. The proposed algorithms are well-defined and naturally extend standard policy gradient and actor-critic approaches. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, including references to mean-variance optimization, CVaR, robust MDPs, and stochastic programming.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is rigorously developed with well-defined mathematical notation and clear derivations. The use of convex optimization and the envelope theorem is appropriate and effectively applied. The assumptions are clearly stated and justified.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the work, and the subsequent sections build upon each other logically. Equations are presented clearly and explained concisely.
* **Originality:** Extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a non-trivial contribution. The generalization of existing techniques and the unified framework represent a significant advance.
* **Significance:** Risk-sensitive RL is an important area, with applications in finance, robotics, and other domains. Providing a general and theoretically sound approach based on coherent risk measures has the potential to impact research and practice in these areas. The paper tackles a difficult problem and offers meaningful progress.
* **Comprehensive Treatment:** The paper covers both static and dynamic risk measures, providing a complete picture of the problem and solution. The inclusion of an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk adds further value.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are relatively simple and focus on a toy finance example. While the results demonstrate the algorithm's risk-averse behavior and flexibility, they lack the scale and complexity of real-world problems. More extensive empirical validation on benchmark RL environments would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost:** The sampling-based algorithms, particularly for the dynamic case, involve solving convex optimization problems repeatedly. The computational cost of this process, especially for large state spaces, is not fully addressed. A discussion of scalability and potential optimization techniques would be beneficial.
* **Assumption Dependence:** While the assumptions are reasonable, their impact on the applicability of the proposed methods is not thoroughly explored. For instance, the assumption of bounded likelihood ratios (Assumption 4.1) might not hold in all practical scenarios.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and original contribution to risk-sensitive RL.
* The generalization to coherent risk measures provides a unified and principled framework.
* The derived gradient formulas and proposed algorithms are well-defined and potentially impactful.
* The clarity and rigor of the paper are exceptional.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited empirical evaluation hinders a full assessment of the algorithm's performance and scalability.
* The computational cost of the sampling-based algorithms could be a practical limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. While the empirical evaluation is limited, the theoretical contributions and the clarity of presentation are compelling. The paper addresses an important problem in a rigorous and innovative way. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational cost and to expand the empirical validation. Specifically, adding results on more challenging RL benchmarks or a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's scalability would further strengthen the paper.
