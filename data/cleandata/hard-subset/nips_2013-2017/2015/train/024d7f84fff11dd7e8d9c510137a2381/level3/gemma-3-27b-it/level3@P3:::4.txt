This paper presents a compelling theoretical framework for policy gradient methods applied to coherent risk measures, unifying previous work and extending it to a broader class of risk preferences. The core idea – leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk as a max-expectation over probability distributions – is elegant and allows for a general gradient derivation. The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible even to those not deeply familiar with risk-sensitive optimization.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:** The paper addresses the growing field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, moving beyond standard expected cost minimization. Existing approaches often focus on specific risk measures (variance, CVaR). This work distinguishes itself by tackling the more general class of coherent risk measures, drawing upon concepts from finance and operations research. It correctly positions itself within the literature, referencing relevant work in risk-sensitive RL ([6, 18, 29, 21, 8, 30]) and optimization of coherent risk ([2, 25, 26]). The connection to robust MDPs [19] is also appropriately highlighted. The authors effectively demonstrate how previous results become special cases of their unified approach.

**Strengths:**
* **Generality:** The unified framework covering all coherent risk measures is a significant strength.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The analytic derivation of the optimal policy parameters and the gradient estimation algorithms are well-motivated and appear theoretically solid.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured, facilitating understanding of the complex concepts.
* **Novelty:** Extending policy gradients to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a novel contribution.
* **Practicality:** The proposed gradient estimation algorithm offers a pathway to applying these theoretical results in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is limited. While a single example (Section 6) is provided, a more comprehensive assessment of the gradient algorithm's performance across different environments and risk measures is needed. Specifically, convergence rates and comparisons to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms would strengthen the paper.
* **Preliminaries:** The initial setup (Section 2) is somewhat dense and lacks sufficient motivation for the roles of  P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ. A clearer explanation of why P<sub>θ</sub> is parameterized and how ξ modifies it would be beneficial, particularly for readers less familiar with measure theory.
* **Technical Question (Equation 2 & Theorem 2.1):** Equation 2 appears to simply be the definition of a convex envelope. The claim that it’s “more restrictive” than Theorem 2.1 is unclear and requires further justification. Why is the assumed structure of U(P<sub>θ</sub>) a restriction?
* **Extension to Continuous Distributions:** The paper explicitly focuses on finite probability spaces. A comment on how the analysis might generalize to continuous distributions, and any challenges that arise, would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
* The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in risk-sensitive RL.
* The unification of existing approaches under a coherent risk framework is a valuable contribution.
* The clarity of presentation makes the work accessible to a broad audience.
* The gradient estimation algorithms provide a practical tool for implementing risk-sensitive policies.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
* The limited empirical evaluation weakens the claim of practical impact.
* Certain technical aspects (Equation 2, Preliminaries) require clarification.



**Specific Comments/Suggestions:**

* **Section 2:** Re-write the introduction to this section to better motivate the use of P<sub>θ</sub> and ξ. Explain their roles in a more intuitive manner.
* **Equation 2:** Clarify why the structure imposed on U(P<sub>θ</sub>) is necessary or introduces a restriction compared to Theorem 2.1.
* **Section 4.3:** Discuss the computational complexity of solving the convex program in the SAA estimator.
* **Section 5:** Briefly mention the challenges of estimating ξ<sup>*</sup><sub>θ,x</sub> in practice and potential approximation techniques.
* **Theoretical Note:** Explicitly mention that the smoothness assumptions on the constraint functions in Assumption 2.2 play a crucial role in proving Theorem 4.2 (and likely other results). This connects the technical assumptions to the mathematical rigor of the derivations.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong theoretical paper with compelling results. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL is substantial. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the minor clarifications requested and potentially including more extensive experimental validation in a revised version. The proofs, while appearing sound, should be carefully verified during the rebuttal process.
