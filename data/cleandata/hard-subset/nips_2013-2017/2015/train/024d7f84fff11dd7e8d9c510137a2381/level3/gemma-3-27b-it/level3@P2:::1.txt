This paper presents a novel and unified approach to policy gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors extend existing risk-sensitive RL techniques, which often focus on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR, to the broader class of coherent risk measures, encompassing both static and time-consistent dynamic formulations. The key contributions include a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, a sampling-based algorithm with consistency results, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk leading to an actor-critic algorithm.

The work builds upon a strong foundation in risk theory and reinforcement learning, clearly situating itself within the existing literature. The authors appropriately reference relevant work in both fields, including Artzner et al.’s seminal paper on coherent risk measures and recent advances in risk-sensitive RL. The connection drawn between coherent risk and robust MDPs is also insightful. The paper’s strength lies in its generality; by framing the problem in terms of coherent risk, the authors provide a unifying framework that generalizes several previous results. The derivation of the gradient formulas, particularly for the static case, is a significant technical contribution. The use of the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick is elegant and allows for a convenient expression of the gradient suitable for sampling-based estimation.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is sound, the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation is a significant drawback. The numerical illustration presented in Section 6, while demonstrating the potential for different risk preferences to lead to different policies, is limited in scope and doesn’t rigorously validate the proposed algorithms. Specifically, there’s no comparative analysis between the sample-based gradient estimator and analytical solutions (where available, such as for CVaR). Such a comparison would bolster confidence in the accuracy and efficiency of the sampling method.

Furthermore, the treatment of dynamic risk is somewhat high-level. While the authors present a policy gradient theorem and outline an actor-critic approach, the details of the algorithm are lacking, and there are no experimental results demonstrating its effectiveness. The complexity of implementing the actor-critic algorithm, particularly the need for a simulator to sample trajectories with ξ*-weighted transitions, is acknowledged but not fully addressed.  The paper attempts to cover a substantial amount of ground – static and dynamic risk, theoretical derivations, and algorithmic outlines – within the eight-page limit, leading to a degree of conciseness that sometimes hinders clarity. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** Provides a unifying framework for risk-sensitive RL using coherent risk measures.
*   **Novelty:** Derives new gradient formulas and algorithms for both static and dynamic risk.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:**  The derivations are well-motivated and build upon established results in risk theory and optimization.
*   **Potential Impact:** The framework could be broadly applicable to various decision-making problems in finance, operations research, and beyond.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** Lack of comprehensive experiments to validate the proposed algorithms.
*   **High-Level Dynamic Risk Treatment:**  Insufficient algorithmic details and experimental results for the dynamic risk case.
*   **Conciseness:** The paper attempts to cover too much material in a limited space, potentially sacrificing clarity.
*   **Missing Comparison:** Absence of a comparison between sample-based and analytical gradient estimates.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in reinforcement learning.
*   The unified framework and gradient formulas have the potential to significantly advance the field of risk-sensitive decision making.
*   The work is likely to stimulate further research in this area.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation makes it difficult to fully assess the practical value of the proposed algorithms.
*   The high-level treatment of dynamic risk and the absence of implementation details raise concerns about the algorithm’s practicality.
*   The paper's conciseness may hinder reproducibility and understanding.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. However, the lack of thorough experimental evaluation is a significant concern. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Include a more extensive experimental evaluation, including a comparison between the sample-based and analytical gradient estimators (where applicable).
2.  Provide more details about the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk, including pseudocode and preliminary experimental results.
3.  Consider expanding the paper or focusing on either static or dynamic risk to allow for a more in-depth treatment of the topic and more comprehensive evaluation.
