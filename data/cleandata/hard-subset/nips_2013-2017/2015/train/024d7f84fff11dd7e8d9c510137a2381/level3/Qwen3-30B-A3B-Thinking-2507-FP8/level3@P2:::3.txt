

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR. The authors present a unified framework that handles both static and time-consistent dynamic coherent risk measures. For static risk, they derive a gradient formula that combines sampling with convex programming, while for dynamic risk, they develop an actor-critic algorithm that explicitly approximates the value function. This work generalizes and unifies previous results that focused on specific risk measures.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the technical developments. The authors provide thorough theoretical analysis, including a consistency result for their gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4), and demonstrate their approach with concrete examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation). The numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of selecting appropriate risk measures for specific problems.

The main contribution of this paper is the theoretical unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods under the coherent risk framework. The authors show that several previous results (e.g., for CVaR and mean-semideviation) are special cases of their general approach. While the extension from specific risk measures to the general class might appear minor at first glance, it represents a significant conceptual contribution by providing a unified theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive RL.

The paper is technically sound with correct derivations and appropriate theoretical guarantees. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, such as noting the computational trade-offs of their convex programming-based method. The results appear correct, and the mathematical arguments are well-supported.

However, there are a few minor issues that should be addressed. The notation "\xi P_\theta" in Theorem 2.1 is confusing and inconsistent with the notation used in Theorem 6.6 of [26], which the authors cite. I recommend adopting the more standard notation used in the cited reference. There is also a typo on line 141 ("risk enevlop[e]"). The authors should clarify that tractability issues arise from the policy parametrization (not from the Markov risk measures themselves), as MDPs with Markov risk measures are tractable.

The paper addresses all four criteria for quality assessment:

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results. The authors provide a rigorous consistency analysis for their gradient estimator, and the mathematical derivations are correct. The paper is complete, not merely a position paper, and the authors are careful to acknowledge limitations.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The structure follows a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical development to numerical illustration. The mathematical notation is mostly consistent, though the suggested notation change would improve clarity.

**Originality**: While the extension from specific risk measures to the general coherent risk framework might seem incremental, it is conceptually significant as it unifies previous results and provides a more general theoretical foundation. The paper adequately references related work and clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior publications.

**Significance**: The work has practical significance for risk-sensitive decision making in RL. The framework provides flexibility in designing risk criteria that can be tailored to specific problem requirements, as demonstrated in the numerical example. The approach generalizes previous results and advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL
- Generalizes and simplifies previous results for specific risk measures
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Contains a practical numerical illustration demonstrating the importance of flexible risk criteria
- Theoretical guarantees for gradient estimation

Arguments against acceptance:
- The extension from specific risk measures to the general class might be considered minor by some reviewers
- Some minor notational inconsistencies and typos need correction

Overall, this is a solid contribution that makes a meaningful extension to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper is well-executed, the results are correct, and the framework provides valuable theoretical unification. While the extension may seem modest at first, it represents an important step toward a more general understanding of risk-sensitive RL. The minor notational issues and typos should be easily corrected in the final version.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods, and the quality of the work meets the high standards of NIPS.
