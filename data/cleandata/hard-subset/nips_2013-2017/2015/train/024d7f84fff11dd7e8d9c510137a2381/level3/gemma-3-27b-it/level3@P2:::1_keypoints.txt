- Proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for reinforcement learning under coherent risk measures, covering static and time-consistent dynamic cases.  
- Main contributions: new gradient formula for static coherent risk, a sampling-based estimator with consistency guarantees, and a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk leading to an actor-critic method.  
- Builds on established risk theory and RL literature, citing Artzner et al. and recent risk-sensitive RL work.  
- Draws an insightful connection between coherent risk measures and robust MDPs.  
- Strength: generality — the framework unifies and generalizes many prior risk-sensitive RL results.  
- Strength: technical novelty — derivation of gradient formulas (static case) using the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick.  
- Strength: theoretical soundness and well-motivated derivations grounded in optimization and risk theory.  
- Potential impact: broadly applicable to decision-making problems in finance, operations research, and beyond.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation; numerical illustration is narrow and doesn’t robustly validate algorithms.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison between the sample-based gradient estimator and available analytical solutions (e.g., CVaR).  
- Weakness: dynamic risk treatment is high-level with insufficient algorithmic details and no experimental demonstration.  
- Weakness: implementation complexity (need for simulator to sample ξ*-weighted transitions) acknowledged but not addressed.  
- Weakness: paper attempts to cover too much in eight pages, resulting in conciseness that can hinder clarity and reproducibility.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, theoretically solid work likely to advance risk-sensitive RL and stimulate further research.  
- Rejection concerns: insufficient experiments, missing implementation details for dynamic case, and conciseness limiting practical assessment.  
- Recommended revisions: add extensive experiments (including sample vs analytical comparisons), provide actor-critic pseudocode and preliminary results, or narrow scope to allow deeper treatment.
