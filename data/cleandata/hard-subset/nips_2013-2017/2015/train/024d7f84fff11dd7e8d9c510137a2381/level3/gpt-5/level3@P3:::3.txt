Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-averse reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static risk measures and time-consistent dynamic (Markov coherent) risk measures. For the static case, the authors use the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Shapiro, Dentcheva, Ruszczyński) and an envelope-theorem argument to derive a likelihood-ratio style gradient formula that depends on the saddle-point (density and KKT multipliers) of a convex program. When the saddle point is not available in closed form, they propose a practical sample-average approximation (SAA) scheme: estimate the envelope and multipliers by solving a finite convex program built from samples, and plug them into the gradient formula. For the dynamic setting, they leverage the Bellman equation for Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone; robust MDP equivalence) to derive a policy-gradient theorem that reweights transitions by the optimal density and modifies the stage cost; this yields an actor–critic algorithm with a robust-MDP-inspired critic.

This framework generalizes prior risk-sensitive PG results that focused on specific metrics, especially CVaR (e.g., Tamar et al. AAAI’15; Chow & Ghavamzadeh NIPS’14) and mean-variance variants, by replacing CVaR-specific derivations with a coherent-risk envelope that subsumes CVaR, mean-semi-deviation, spectral risk, etc. The extension is conceptually simple—essentially a careful extension of the standard risk-neutral policy gradient to worst-case reweightings defined by the envelope—but useful because it unifies multiple objectives under one estimator/algorithmic template.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The static gradient (Theorem 4.2) follows cleanly from the dual representation and the envelope theorem, and reproduces known CVaR gradients as a special case. The SAA-based estimator and its consistency (Proposition 4.4) are standard and the assumptions are reasonable for common coherent risks. The dynamic PG theorem (Theorem 5.2) is consistent with the robust-MDP interpretation of Markov coherent risk and mirrors the classical policy-gradient structure with reweighted kernels and a modified stage cost. Overall, the derivations appear correct.
- Assumptions and practicality: Finite probability spaces (for exposition) and differentiability of envelope constraints are explicit; the approach extends to Lp spaces. Practically, each gradient step requires solving a convex program (static) and obtaining KKT multipliers; for dynamic risk, one must approximate Vθ and sample from ξ-weighted dynamics. These are reasonable but may be computationally heavier than risk-neutral PG. Variance control and sample complexity are not analyzed beyond LLN-style guarantees (authors note importance sampling as future work).
- Experiments: The illustrative example is small-scale but sufficient to show how different coherent risks induce qualitatively different policies. A more extensive evaluation would strengthen the paper, though the main contribution is theoretical.

Clarity
The paper is well organized, well written, and easy to follow. The dual-representation viewpoint and the bridge to robust MDPs help readability. A few suggestions:
- Theorem 2.1 notation: The product “ξ Pθ” is confusing. I recommend adopting the notation from Theorem 6.6 in [26] and writing the envelope as a set of probability vectors/measures Q(Pθ) ⊂ Δ(Ω) with ρ(Z) = maxq∈Q(Pθ) ∑ω q(ω) Z(ω), where q is absolutely continuous w.r.t. Pθ and satisfies explicit convex constraints. This avoids overloading ξ and eliminates the visual “product.”
- Typo: line 141 “risk enevlop[e]” → “risk envelope.”
- Tractability statement (Sec. 3): Please clarify that the intractability the paper confronts arises primarily from policy parametrization/optimization and from large state spaces (necessitating sampling and function approximation). In contrast, MDPs with Markov coherent risk measures admit dynamic programming and are tractable in tabular, model-known settings (subject to the usual curse of dimensionality).
- Minor: It would help to include a short algorithm box for the SAA-based static gradient and a high-level pseudocode for the actor–critic, and to reiterate the assumptions under which the ξ-weighted sampling is implementable.

Originality
The core ideas generalize known CVaR-specific PG results to the full class of coherent risks and extend the policy-gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk. Conceptually, the extension is modest—mostly replacing CVaR-specific envelopes with general coherent envelopes and applying the envelope theorem—but it is nontrivial to execute cleanly and broadly useful. The unification and the dynamic PG theorem are the main novelty.

Significance
This is a solid and important extension: it provides a single template to obtain policy gradients for many coherent risks (CVaR, spectral, mean-semi-deviation, etc.) and for dynamic, time-consistent risk. Researchers and practitioners interested in risk-sensitive RL can directly reuse the framework to plug in their risk of choice. The dynamic result connects risk-sensitive RL to robust MDPs, potentially influencing future algorithm design. While empirical breadth is limited, the theoretical contribution is likely to be used and built upon.

Pros
- Unified, principled treatment of coherent risk measures (static and dynamic) for policy gradients.
- Generalizes CVaR and other special cases with a clean envelope-theorem based derivation.
- Dynamic PG theorem for Markov coherent risk; actor–critic recipe leveraging robust MDPs.
- Clear writing and organization; assumptions are explicit; results appear correct.

Cons
- Computational overhead: requires solving a convex program and accessing KKT multipliers; dynamic method needs ξ-weighted sampling and multiple transitions per state.
- Limited empirical validation and no variance/sample-complexity analysis.
- Notational choices (ξ Pθ) can be confusing; minor clarity issues.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Theorem 2.1: Replace “ξ Pθ ∈ U(Pθ)” with q ∈ Q(Pθ) ⊂ Δ(Ω); write Eξ[Z] as ∑ω q(ω) Z(ω). Align with Theorem 6.6 in [26].
- Line 141: “risk envelope” typo.
- Sec. 3: Clarify that dynamic-programming solutions exist for Markov coherent risk (e.g., [24]) in tabular/model-known settings; the practical difficulty in RL stems from policy parametrization, sampling, and large state spaces.
- Variance reduction: Since rare-event sensitivity (e.g., small-α CVaR, tail-heavy spectral risks) induces high variance, a short discussion on practical control variates/baselines or importance sampling designs (e.g., [3]) would be valuable.
- Implementation details: Briefly describe how KKT multipliers are retrieved from standard solvers, and any regularization used when the envelope constraints are near-degenerate.

Overall assessment
Minor but solid and important extension of existing results. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and likely to be useful as a unifying reference and template for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions addressing the notation, typo, and tractability clarification.
