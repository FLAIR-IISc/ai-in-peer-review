This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in risk-averse Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). Building on prior work that developed algorithms for specific risk measures like CVaR or variance, this research generalizes the policy gradient theorem to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static risk measures and time-consistent dynamic (Markov) risk measures. For the static case, the proposed algorithm combines sampling with convex programming, while the dynamic case is addressed with an actor-critic style approach. This work successfully unifies and extends several previous results in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, providing a more general theoretical foundation.

The primary strengths of this paper are its quality and clarity. The manuscript is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written, making the theoretical contributions easy to follow. The technical results appear to be sound and correct. The generalization from specific risk measures like CVaR to the broader class of coherent risk measures is a significant contribution. It provides researchers and practitioners with a principled and flexible framework for designing risk-sensitive agents, which is an important direction for the field. The unified approach simplifies and contextualizes previous, more specific results.

While the contribution is valuable, its originality is somewhat incremental. The core result is a well-executed extension of the standard risk-neutral policy gradient theorem, leveraging the known dual representation of coherent risk measures. The extension is useful and solid, but not a fundamental breakthrough. I have a few specific suggestions for improvement:

*   The notation "ξP_θ" in Theorem 2.1 is potentially confusing. I recommend adopting the clearer notation used in Theorem 6.6 of reference [26] for consistency and readability.
*   There is a minor typo on line 141: "risk enevlop[e]".
*   The paper should clarify that the potential intractability of the optimization problems arises from the complexity of the policy parametrization, not from the use of Markov risk measures themselves, as MDPs with these measures are known to be tractable.

Overall, this is a solid and important paper that provides a valuable generalization of existing risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. Despite the incremental nature of the technical extension, the resulting unified framework is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art. The work is well-presented, technically sound, and will likely be built upon by others in the risk-sensitive RL community. I recommend acceptance.
