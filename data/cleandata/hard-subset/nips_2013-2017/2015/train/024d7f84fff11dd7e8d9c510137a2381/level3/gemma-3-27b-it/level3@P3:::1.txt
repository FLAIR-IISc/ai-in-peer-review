This paper tackles the important problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, extending policy gradient methods to the broad class of coherent risk measures. The authors provide a unified framework, generalizing previous work focused on specific risk measures like CVaR, and present both analytical and sampling-based approaches for calculating policy gradients. The work is ambitious and addresses a crucial gap in the literature.

The core contribution lies in the derivation of a novel formula for static coherent risks, enabling analytical gradient computation in certain cases, and a general sampling algorithm with consistency proofs for more complex scenarios. The extension to dynamic Markov coherent risk measures, while high-level, demonstrates the potential for applying the framework to sequential decision-making problems with time-consistency concerns. The connection to robust MDPs (via [19]) and the potential for addressing model uncertainty, hinted at in the conclusion, are promising avenues for future work.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Primarily, the limited empirical validation significantly hinders a full assessment of the method's effectiveness. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the lack of experimental results demonstrating the benefits of the proposed approach—particularly a comparison between the sampling-based and analytical gradients (e.g., for CVaR) – is a major drawback. The numerical illustration in Section 6, while insightful regarding risk preference, doesn’t directly evaluate the policy gradient algorithms themselves.

Furthermore, the paper attempts to cover a considerable amount of ground – static and dynamic risk, analytical and sampling methods – within a relatively constrained page limit (eight pages). This leads to a lack of depth in certain areas. The section on dynamic risk policy gradients (Section 5) feels particularly concise and lacks sufficient algorithmic details and experimental backing to convincingly demonstrate its practicality. It’s difficult to gauge the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed actor-critic algorithm without more concrete implementation details.

The writing quality is generally good, but could be improved. While the mathematical derivations appear sound, the explanations sometimes lack clarity, making it challenging to fully grasp the intricacies of the proposed methods. More detailed explanations of the assumptions, especially Assumption 2.2, would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The unified framework for coherent risk measures is a significant contribution, generalizing existing work.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The analytical derivations and consistency proofs provide a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Potential Significance:** The method has the potential to be broadly applicable in risk-sensitive decision-making problems across various domains.
* **Connection to Robust Control:** The link to robust MDPs offers a valuable perspective on addressing model uncertainty.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of comprehensive experimental results prevents a thorough evaluation of the proposed algorithms.
* **Limited Depth:** The paper attempts to cover too much material within the page limit, sacrificing detail and clarity.
* **High-Level Dynamic Risk Section:** The section on dynamic risk is underdeveloped and lacks practical details and experiments.
* **Missing Comparison:** No comparison is made between the sample-based and analytical gradient approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and potentially impactful paper, but its lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but they need to be substantiated by empirical evidence. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after significantly expanding the experimental evaluation. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a detailed experimental comparison of the sampling-based and analytical gradient methods for a representative risk measure like CVaR, demonstrating their performance on benchmark MDPs.
2.  Elaborate on the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk, providing pseudocode, implementation details, and experimental results on relevant problems.
3.  Consider splitting the work into two papers – one focusing on static risk measures with a comprehensive empirical evaluation, and another dedicated to dynamic risk with a more in-depth exploration of the actor-critic algorithm. This would allow for more detailed explanations and thorough experimentation.
