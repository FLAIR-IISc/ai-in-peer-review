- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures in both static and time-consistent dynamic settings.  
- For static risk, uses dual representation and envelope theorem to derive a gradient formula leading to sampling-based estimators that use likelihood ratios plus solutions/KKT multipliers from a small convex program.  
- For dynamic, derives a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk connecting ∇θVθ to expectations under a ξ*-reweighted Markov chain and proposes an actor-critic algorithm using a robust-MDP-style critic.  
- Unifies and generalizes prior work (CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility, robust MDPs) and recovers known special cases.  
- Main novelty: coherent-risk-wide gradient formula and its dynamic analogue with practical estimator constructions.  
- Strength: Conceptual unification providing a single framework that simplifies and extends special-case derivations.  
- Strength: Solid theoretical foundations using convex duality and envelope theorems, with clear gradient formulas and SAA consistency for static case.  
- Strength: Algorithmic clarity—static methods use sampling plus small convex programs; dynamic uses actor-critic aligning with robust MDP interpretations.  
- Strength: High relevance to safety-critical and financial applications where tailored risk measures matter.  
- Strength: Well written, well organized, and well referenced.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is minimal, lacking dynamic-risk experiments and standard RL benchmark comparisons (scalability, sample complexity, runtime).  
- Weakness: Practical requirements—explicit risk envelopes, solving convex programs per update and obtaining KKT multipliers, and needing simulator access for multiple/ξ*-weighted transitions—may be restrictive.  
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on finite probability/state space assumptions; continuous spaces and function approximation are treated only at a high level.  
- Weakness: No variance or sample-complexity analysis for gradient estimators; sensitivity to rare events and envelope misspecification is unaddressed.  
- Weakness: Some actor-critic details and convergence guarantees are deferred to the supplement.  
- Clarity assessment: Above average; suggests summarizing assumptions on U(Pθ), KKT availability, and adding pseudocode for static and dynamic algorithms.  
- Originality assessment: Nontrivial and novel extension to general coherent risks and a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.  
- Significance assessment: High—provides a general toolset likely to spur further research, though impact would be stronger with richer experiments.  
- Suggestions: Add dynamic-risk experiments on standard domains, compare to CVaR/variance/robust baselines, and report runtime, convex-program counts, and sample usage.  
- Suggestions: Propose practical implementations of ξ*-weighted transitions (e.g., importance sampling/reweighting) and analyze their variance.  
- Suggestions: Clarify computational complexity and scalability of the static estimator, including dimension of ξ and constraint counts, and discuss warm-starts/structure exploitation.  
- Suggestions: Elaborate on continuous state/action extensions, critic function classes, projection schemes, and stability of approximation.  
- Suggestions: Consider variance-reduction techniques (baselines, control variates) for tail-sensitive risks.  
- Suggestions: Discuss robustness to risk-envelope modeling errors and guidelines for selecting envelopes in practice.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Elegant, general theoretical extension to all coherent risks with a new dynamic policy-gradient theorem and actor-critic construction that unifies prior results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited empirical validation, practical implementation hurdles (KKT multipliers, ξ*-weighted sampling), and restrictive assumptions narrowing immediate applicability.  
- Overall assessment: Strong, timely theoretical contribution likely to be accepted if empirical section and practical implementation details are strengthened.
