- Proposes a unified framework extending policy gradient methods to the full class of coherent risk measures, generalizing prior CVaR/variance-centric work.  
- Main theoretical contributions: a new gradient formula for static coherent risk and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk with an actor-critic algorithm.  
- Theorem 4.2 combines the risk envelope representation with the envelope theorem to produce a sampling-friendly static risk gradient.  
- Enables derivation of closed-form gradients for multiple risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) in a unified way.  
- Presents a general sampling-based algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) with a consistency proof (Proposition 4.4) for cases lacking analytic solutions.  
- Theorem 5.2 extends classic policy gradient results to Markov coherent risk measures, providing a theoretical basis for dynamic risk actor-critic methods.  
- Significance: unifies fragmented risk-sensitive RL literature, allowing practitioners to choose risk measures without designing new algorithms per measure.  
- Numerical example demonstrates that different risk measures yield different policy choices, highlighting framework flexibility.  
- Major weakness: insufficient empirical validation and no thorough comparison between the sample-based estimator and analytical approaches (e.g., CVaR).  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to a single trading example with no comparative performance analysis across risk measures.  
- Dynamic-risk coverage is thin: Section 5 and actor-critic algorithm lack sufficient algorithmic detail and convergence analysis.  
- Practical implementation details and experimental support for the dynamic risk approach are notably sparse.  
- Clarity is generally good, but the paper’s ambitious scope and technical density make some sections hard to follow.  
- Originality is strong: a novel unified framework with an insightful connection to robust MDPs.  
- Recommendation caveat: theoretically valuable but needs more comprehensive experiments (and/or narrower focus) to establish practical utility; pros include strong theory and high impact potential, cons include limited experiments and incomplete dynamic-risk details.
