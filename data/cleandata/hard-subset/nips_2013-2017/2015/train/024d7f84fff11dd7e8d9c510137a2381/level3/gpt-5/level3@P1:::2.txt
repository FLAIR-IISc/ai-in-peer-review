This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general class of coherent risk measures. The central technical idea is to exploit the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk measures and apply an envelope theorem to derive policy-gradient formulas amenable to sampling. For static risks, the authors give a gradient identity that involves the Lagrangian saddle point of the convex program defining the risk and propose a sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator with a consistency guarantee via SAA. They show how classic cases emerge as special instances: for CVaR, the gradient reduces to a conditional REINFORCE estimator on tail events; for mean-semideviation, they derive an explicit estimator. For dynamic, time-consistent risk, they extend the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk, expressible via a robust Bellman operator, and propose an actor-critic method: the critic estimates a robust value function with function approximation; the actor uses the new gradient theorem, which requires sampling under ξ-weighted transitions induced by the risk envelope. A simple trading example illustrates how choosing different coherent risks leads to different policies, highlighting flexibility in risk modeling.

Relation to prior work: Prior RL work studied specific risk functionals—exponential utility [Borkar], mean-variance [Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh], and CVaR [Rockafellar & Uryasev; Tamar et al.; Chow & Ghavamzadeh]. In stochastic programming, Ruszczyński & Shapiro developed optimization for coherent risks but with policies that do not affect system dynamics. For dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs, there is a known equivalence (e.g., Ruszczyński; Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian). This paper unifies these strands by deriving general policy-gradient estimators for coherent static and dynamic risks, recovering known special cases (e.g., CVaR gradient), and extending policy-gradient theory to Markov coherent risk.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The static gradient identity follows cleanly from the coherent risk dual, Lagrangian saddle-point characterization, likelihood-ratio identity, and envelope theorem. The SAA-based estimator and consistency (under standard Slater/compactness/smoothness assumptions) are appropriate. The dynamic result leverages the robust MDP interpretation to write a risk-sensitive Bellman equation and extends the policy gradient theorem to the ξ-weighted chain with a stage-wise term hθ. The assumptions (bounded likelihood ratios, finite Ω, smooth constraints, availability of a generative model) are clearly stated.
- Empirical support: Experiments are minimal and static; they validate flexibility rather than algorithmic performance or scalability. There is no dynamic MDP evaluation, no comparison of actor-critic to baselines on larger problems, and no variance/sample-complexity study.
- Practicality: The approach requires (i) knowledge of the risk envelope (constraints and their derivatives), (ii) solving a convex program (and extracting KKT multipliers) per batch/state-distribution, and (iii) sampling from ξ-weighted transitions. These are feasible in simulators with a generative model, but may be heavy for large-scale or online settings.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and organized; notation is clean. The connections to prior work are thorough and correct. The derivations are sketched clearly with references to supplementary material.
- Suggestions: Provide pseudo-code for the SAA gradient estimator and actor-critic; explicitly discuss computational cost per iteration (number/size of convex programs, calls to the simulator, and value-function updates); add a short section on how to construct U(Pθ) for common risks and how state dependence in dynamic risk is handled.

Originality:
- Extending policy gradients to the entire coherent risk class is a nontrivial and elegant generalization that unifies disparate risk-sensitive RL results. The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears new and useful. The application of the envelope theorem to obtain a sampling-friendly gradient is also a neat insight.

Significance:
- The contribution is conceptually significant: it provides a common foundation for risk-sensitive RL, enabling practitioners to plug in different coherent risks. The dynamic extension bridges risk-sensitive control and RL. The main caveat is the empirical evidence; demonstrating the approach on more realistic MDPs (e.g., inventory control, queueing, portfolio with transaction costs) and showing stability/variance properties would strengthen impact.

Pros:
- Unified framework covering static and time-consistent dynamic coherent risks.
- Clear gradient formulas; recovery and simplification of known special cases (e.g., CVaR).
- Consistency guarantee for the SAA estimator; principled actor-critic design for dynamic risk.
- Solid connections to robust MDPs and coherent risk theory; good clarity and references.

Cons:
- Experimental evaluation is very limited; no dynamic tasks or scalability analysis.
- Requires access to risk envelopes and KKT multipliers; per-iteration convex programs may be costly.
- Assumes a generative model and ability to sample under ξ-weighted transitions; online on-policy learning from a single trajectory is not addressed.
- Finite probability space assumptions and smoothness of envelope constraints may restrict immediate applicability; discussion of continuous/state-action spaces is brief.

Questions/Suggestions for authors:
- Can you include pseudo-code and a complexity analysis for the SAA estimator and the dynamic actor-critic?
- How do you sample under ξ-weighted transitions in practice without rejection sampling or importance sampling? Can you propose an importance sampling scheme and analyze variance?
- How sensitive are the gradient estimates to errors in the critic’s Vθ and in approximate saddle-point solutions?
- Can you report results on at least one dynamic benchmark and compare against CVaR/mean-variance baselines?
- Discuss extensions to continuous state-action spaces and neural function approximation.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a clear, technically sound, and broadly useful theoretical contribution by unifying risk-sensitive policy gradients under coherent risks and extending them to dynamic, time-consistent settings. Despite limited experiments, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance at NIPS.

Confidence: High.
