This paper presents a general framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors tackle two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of a cumulative cost and optimizing a dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The core contribution is a novel approach for estimating the policy gradient that leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which defines risk as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability distortions (the "risk envelope"). For the static case, this leads to an algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient. For the dynamic case, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and propose a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

This work provides a significant generalization and unification of previous research in risk-sensitive RL, which has largely focused on specific risk measures such as mean-variance (Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh, 2013) or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) (Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014). By addressing the general class of coherent risk measures, the paper offers a principled and flexible framework applicable to a wide range of risk preferences and problem domains.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivations of the gradient formulas for both static (Theorem 4.2) and dynamic (Theorem 5.2) risk measures are theoretically sound and elegant, cleanly combining the envelope theorem with the standard likelihood-ratio trick. The proposed algorithms follow naturally from these theoretical results. The work is presented as a complete contribution, establishing the theoretical foundation and providing concrete algorithmic proposals.

The main weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The paper demonstrates its approach on a simple, static financial portfolio problem. While this experiment effectively illustrates the core concept—showing how different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to different, interpretable behaviors—it does not validate the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk setting. The performance, scalability, and sample efficiency of the dynamic algorithm in a non-trivial MDP remain unevaluated.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for using coherent risk measures is clearly articulated, and the necessary mathematical preliminaries are introduced concisely. The logical flow from the static risk case to the more complex dynamic risk case is easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of placing their work in the context of prior literature, making their contributions clear. The clarity is well above average for a conference paper on a technical topic.

**Originality:** The work is sufficiently original. While it builds upon established concepts—namely, the dual representation of coherent risk from stochastic programming and policy gradient methods from RL—its contribution is the novel synthesis of these ideas to create a unified framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients. The extension from specific, well-studied risk measures to the general class of coherent measures is a non-trivial and valuable step forward for the field. The derived policy gradient theorems are new and insightful.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Risk-sensitivity is critical for deploying RL systems in real-world applications where worst-case performance and outcome variability are major concerns (e.g., finance, autonomous driving, and resource management). This paper provides a powerful and general toolkit for addressing these problems. By providing a unified framework, it not only generalizes past work but also opens the door for researchers and practitioners to design and optimize for novel, problem-specific coherent risk measures. The ideas presented here are very likely to be used and built upon by the community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Provides a novel, theoretically sound, and unified framework for policy gradient optimization with the entire class of coherent risk measures, a significant generalization of prior work.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very well-written, with clear motivation, logical organization, and sound technical derivations.
*   **Pro:** The work is of high significance to the field of risk-sensitive RL, offering a principled approach to a critical problem and paving the way for future research and applications.
*   **Pro:** The algorithms are derived naturally from the problem formulation and are presented clearly.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The empirical validation is limited to a simple proof-of-concept example for the static case and does not provide any results for the more complex dynamic risk algorithm.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a clear, significant, and original contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. Its primary strength is the elegant theoretical framework that unifies and generalizes a large body of previous work. The only notable weakness is the limited empirical evaluation. However, the strength and importance of the theoretical contribution far outweigh this limitation. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way and provides a solid foundation for future work in this important area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
