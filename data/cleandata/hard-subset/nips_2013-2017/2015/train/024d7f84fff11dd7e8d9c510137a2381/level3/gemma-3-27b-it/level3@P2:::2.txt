This paper presents a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors provide a unified framework generalizing previous work focused on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR, offering both theoretical grounding and practical algorithms for static and dynamic risk settings. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the technical development is sound and well-structured.

The paper begins by framing risk-sensitive optimization as crucial for scenarios where managing cost variability is paramount, particularly in finance and operations research. It correctly identifies coherent risk measures as a desirable class due to their theoretical properties and widespread acceptance. The authors clearly define the problem, outlining both static and dynamic risk formulations and highlighting the challenges of optimizing with these measures. 

The core technical contribution lies in deriving gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk. The formula for the static case, leveraging the envelope theorem, is particularly elegant and generalizes existing results for CVaR and mean-semideviation. The proposed sampling-based estimator, combined with convex programming, offers a practical approach for scenarios where analytical gradients are unavailable. The extension to dynamic risk, through a novel policy gradient theorem and actor-critic algorithm, is a substantial achievement. The connection to robust MDPs and the use of function approximation to address the curse of dimensionality are insightful.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The extension to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant step forward, providing a unifying framework for risk-sensitive RL.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations are mathematically rigorous and well-justified, building upon established results in optimization and reinforcement learning.
* **Algorithmic Contribution:** The proposed algorithms – both for static and dynamic risk – are concrete and offer practical guidance for implementation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of key concepts. The notation is consistent and easy to follow.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors carefully relate their work to previous research, highlighting the novel contributions and providing appropriate citations.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application example in finance, while simple, effectively demonstrates the algorithm's ability to capture different risk preferences.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the challenges of scaling to large state spaces, the computational cost of the convex programming step in the static case, and the nested sampling procedure in the dynamic case, could be prohibitive for complex problems. Further discussion of computational efficiency and potential approximations would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example is relatively basic and lacks a thorough comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on benchmark tasks. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation would strengthen the claims of the paper.
* **Assumption Dependence:** The performance of the proposed algorithms relies on several assumptions, such as the smoothness of constraints in the risk envelope and the availability of a simulator for sampling transitions. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithms to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
* **Dynamic Risk Algorithm Details:** The details of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk are somewhat limited, deferring a full description to the supplementary material. Providing a more complete overview within the main paper would improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in reinforcement learning.
* The technical contributions are novel and substantial, extending existing methods to a more general setting.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported.
* The proposed algorithms are practical and offer clear guidance for implementation.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the algorithms could limit their applicability to large-scale problems.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and does not provide a strong demonstration of the algorithms' performance on challenging benchmarks.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The extension to coherent risk measures is a significant advance, and the proposed algorithms are well-motivated and theoretically sound. While the computational complexity and limited empirical evaluation are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of computational efficiency and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on standard benchmark tasks. Additionally, expanding the description of the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm within the main text would enhance clarity.
