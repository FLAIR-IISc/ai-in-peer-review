Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a unified policy gradient framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that covers the entire class of coherent risk measures. For static risk, the authors derive a new gradient formula via the saddle-point of the Lagrangian associated with the risk-envelope representation (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński and Shapiro). This yields analytic policy-gradient expressions for several common risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), and suggests a general sampling-plus-convex-programming algorithm with a consistency proof when closed-form saddle points are unavailable. For dynamic risk, the paper introduces a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures (Ruszczyński; Iancu et al.; Chow and Pavone), relating the gradient to a suitable risk-sensitive value function and enabling an actor-critic style algorithm by leveraging the robust MDP connection (Osogami; Petrik and Subramanian; Tamar et al.).

The work meaningfully generalizes prior risk-sensitive PG methods that focused on individual criteria like mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth and Ghavamzadeh) or CVaR (Rockafellar and Uryasev; Tamar et al.; Chow and Ghavamzadeh). It also goes beyond stochastic programming settings where policy parameters do not affect state distributions.

Quality
- Theory: The static-gradient formula built on the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick is elegant and appears technically sound. It recovers known results (e.g., the CVaR gradient) and extends them to discrete settings. The SAA-based estimator with convex programming and the consistency result are well motivated under standard conditions (Slater-type feasibility, smoothness of envelope constraints). The dynamic policy gradient theorem is a substantial extension of the classic policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk; the actor-critic sketch is plausible given the robust MDP equivalence.
- Assumptions and limitations: Analytical Lagrangian saddle-point solutions exist only for some risks (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation); otherwise, the approach requires solving a per-batch convex program and accessing KKT multipliers. The finite probability space assumption simplifies exposition; extendability to Lp spaces is claimed but details are omitted. The dynamic algorithm requires a simulator capable of multiple next-state samples per state, and sampling from ξ-weighted transitions.
- Empirics: The main paper provides only a small static illustration; there is no thorough empirical validation of the sample-based gradient, nor any experiments for the dynamic setting. A direct comparison between the analytical and sampling-based gradients (e.g., for CVaR) is missing in the main text; even if present in the supplement, a careful, reproducible experimental section would significantly strengthen the submission.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and contextualized, and the unified derivation is clearly articulated for static risk. However, the scope is very ambitious for eight pages. The section on dynamic risk policy gradients remains high-level, deferring algorithmic details and convergence analysis to the supplement, making practical applicability hard to assess. Including more guidance on computing KKT multipliers and on the computational cost of the convex programs would aid readers.

Originality
High. Presenting a single, coherent derivation that yields policy gradients for a broad family of coherent risk measures (static and dynamic) is novel. The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is also new to my knowledge.

Significance
Potentially high. A unified gradient framework can make risk-sensitive RL more principled and broadly applicable, enabling practitioners to select risk measures matching domain needs (e.g., tail sensitivity vs. dispersion). The connection to robust MDPs and function approximation suggests impact on scalable planning under risk. However, the lack of comprehensive experiments limits the ability to judge practical effectiveness and robustness.

Strengths
- Unified, principled derivation of policy gradients for coherent risk measures.
- New analytic gradient expressions for several common risks; recovers and broadens known results (e.g., CVaR).
- General sampling-based algorithm with a consistency guarantee when analytical solutions are not available.
- Extension to Markov coherent dynamic risk with a policy gradient theorem and an actor-critic approach.
- Clear relation to robust MDPs and prior risk-sensitive RL literature.

Weaknesses
- Closed-form Lagrangian saddle points exist only for some risks; practical applicability relies on solving convex programs and extracting KKT multipliers.
- The sample-based approximation lacks substantive empirical validation in the main paper; variance, bias, and computational trade-offs are not characterized.
- No main-text experimental comparison between analytical and sampling-based gradients (e.g., CVaR) to guide practitioners.
- Dynamic section is high-level with no experiments; practicality (sampling from ξ-weighted dynamics, function approximation errors) remains unclear.
- Attempts to cover too many topics in limited space, which hurts clarity and reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a thorough empirical section on static risks:
  - Compare analytical vs. SAA-based gradients for CVaR and mean-semideviation across tasks (synthetic and standard RL benchmarks).
  - Report accuracy, variance, bias, and wall-clock cost as a function of N and problem size; include sensitivity to envelope parameterization and constraint smoothness.
- Add at least one dynamic experiment (e.g., gridworld, portfolio MDP), demonstrating the actor-critic method, robustness to function approximation error, and the effect of sampling from ξ-weighted transitions.
- Include pseudocode for the SAA-based gradient and actor-critic, and discuss computational complexity and solver/KKT details.
- Clarify extension beyond finite probability spaces and provide implementation guidance for common risk envelopes (spectral risk, mean-semideviation).
- Consider splitting the work or focusing the current manuscript on static risks to allow rigorous empirical evaluation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical contribution that unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients across coherent risk measures.
- Novel dynamic policy gradient theorem likely to influence subsequent research.
- Clear connections to robust MDPs, offering a principled route to scalable algorithms.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation to assess practicality, estimator variance, and computational trade-offs.
- Dynamic section lacks algorithmic detail and experiments; current utility is unclear.
- Reliance on supplementary material for key components; main paper does not yet support reproducibility.

Overall
An interesting and potentially impactful contribution with solid theory, but empirical support is currently too limited for a definitive assessment of practical value. I recommend focusing/splitting to provide deeper experiments, or substantially expanding the empirical section if space allows.
