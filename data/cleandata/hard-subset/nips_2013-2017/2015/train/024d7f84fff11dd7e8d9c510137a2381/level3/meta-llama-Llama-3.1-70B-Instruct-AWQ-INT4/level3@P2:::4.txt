The paper "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures" presents a unified approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that generalizes and extends previous results. The authors develop a policy gradient method for a broad class of coherent risk measures, which are widely accepted in finance and operations research. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the problem, including a clear definition of the risk measures, a discussion of the assumptions, and a derivation of the optimal policy parameters.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a general gradient estimation algorithm for both static and dynamic risk measures. The algorithm is based on the dual representation of coherent risk measures as a convex hull of probability distributions. The authors show that the risk can be represented as the maximum expected value over the hull, and they derive an analytic expression for the optimal policy parameters.

The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to non-experts in risk-sensitive optimization. The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the main results, including the derivation of the gradient estimation algorithm. The paper also includes a numerical illustration that demonstrates the importance of flexibility in designing risk criteria.

One of the strengths of the paper is its unified analysis, which covers a broad class of risk measures, including most well-known ones. The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the main results, making it easy to follow and understand the paper.

However, the paper could benefit from a deeper empirical evaluation of the proposed gradient algorithm. While the numerical illustration is helpful, it is limited to a simple example, and more extensive experiments are needed to fully evaluate the performance of the algorithm.

Additionally, some clarifications are needed in the preliminaries section. The roles of Pθ and ξ need clearer explanation, as they are crucial to understanding the rest of the paper. Furthermore, it is unclear why Equation 2 is claimed to be more restrictive than Theorem 2.1, as it appears to be simply the convex envelope definition.

Finally, the paper could benefit from a discussion on how the analysis generalizes to continuous distributions. While the authors mention that their results can be extended to Lp-normed spaces, they do not provide any details on how to do so.

In conclusion, the paper presents a strong theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors provide a unified approach to risk-sensitive optimization, and their results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications. However, more extensive empirical evaluation and clarification of some technical points are needed to fully appreciate the contribution of the paper.
