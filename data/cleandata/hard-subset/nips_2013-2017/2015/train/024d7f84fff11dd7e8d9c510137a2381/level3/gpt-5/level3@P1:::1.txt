This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures, in both static and time-consistent dynamic settings. The key technical idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risks (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and apply the envelope theorem to derive a general gradient formula for static coherent risks. This yields: (i) closed-form gradient identities for several popular risks (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), recovering and generalizing prior results; and (ii) a sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator with consistency guarantees when analytic saddle points are unavailable. For dynamic (Markov coherent) risk measures (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone), the paper proves a policy-gradient theorem that expresses the gradient as an expectation under a ξ-weighted Markov chain, leading to an actor-critic style algorithm that estimates the risk-sensitive value function via its equivalence to a robust MDP (Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al.). Conceptually, this unifies a range of risk-sensitive PG methods (mean-variance, CVaR, exponential utility) under a single framework.

Relation to prior work: Prior RL work largely targets specific risks such as exponential utility (Borkar), mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), or CVaR (Rockafellar & Uryasev; Chow & Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al.). Dynamic, time-consistent risk in MDPs has been handled via DP and robust MDP connections (Ruszczyński; Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian). This paper’s contributions are broader in scope: a general gradient formula for static coherent risks and a risk-PG theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk measures that subsume many special cases.

Strengths
- Unifying framework: The envelope-theorem-based gradient identity for coherent risks is elegant and general, subsuming known CVaR and mean-variance results and enabling new cases (e.g., spectral measures).
- Practical estimation pathway: The sample-average approximation paired with convex programming provides a concrete route to gradients when closed-form saddle points are unavailable; the consistency result is technically sound under mild conditions.
- Dynamic extension: The policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risks extends the classical PG theorem to a time-consistent risk setting and clarifies the role of ξ-weighted dynamics; the actor-critic outline is promising.
- Potential impact: Offers a toolkit that could let practitioners choose risk measures suitable to their domain rather than being constrained to a few canonical choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: The paper offers only a small static illustration (asset selection) and gives high-level dynamic results without experiments. This makes it hard to assess practical viability, variance, and sample complexity.
- Algorithmic detail gaps: The dynamic actor-critic is described at a high level, with nontrivial steps (e.g., sampling under ξ-weighted transitions, coupling with function approximation, computation of constraint derivatives) deferred to the supplement. Without pseudocode and ablations, implementability is unclear.
- Dependence on solving inner convex programs: While justified theoretically, the scalability of repeatedly solving saddle-point problems (state-wise in the dynamic case) is not quantified. Practical guidance on problem sizes, solver choices, and warm-start strategies is missing.
- Assumptions and scope: Finite outcome spaces and smooth risk-envelope constraints are assumed; while the authors state extensions to Lp, the conditions and implications for continuous state/action problems are not demonstrated empirically.

Quality
- Theoretical development appears sound: the application of the dual representation and envelope theorem is appropriate, the CVaR and mean-semideviation derivations check out, and the SAA-consistency assumptions are reasonable for common coherent risks. The dynamic gradient theorem is plausible and aligns with robust MDP interpretations.
- However, the lack of experimental stress-testing (variance, bias under function approximation, impact of ξ-weighted sampling errors) leaves open questions about robustness and efficiency.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but it attempts to cover static and dynamic theory, estimation, and applications in limited space. The dynamic section, in particular, is too terse to be actionable. Splitting the work or focusing one paper on static methods (with thorough experiments), and a follow-up on dynamic AC details, would improve clarity.

Originality
- High. Moving from specific risk measures to the coherent class and deriving general PG formulas is novel. The dynamic PG theorem for Markov coherent risk adds to the theory beyond robust MDP approximations.

Significance
- Potentially high, contingent on practical viability. If the sampling-plus-convex-program approach proves efficient and stable, this could become a standard tool for risk-sensitive RL across domains (finance, operations).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide algorithm pseudocode for the static SAA gradient and the dynamic actor-critic, including how to compute and reuse saddle points and how to sample under ξ-weighted transitions in simulators.
- Include empirical comparisons between analytical and SAA gradients for CVaR and mean-semideviation (variance/accuracy vs. sample size), and timing breakdowns for the convex subproblems.
- Add dynamic experiments on benchmark MDPs demonstrating time-consistent risk optimization, and compare against risk-neutral PG, mean-variance, and CVaR-specific baselines.
- Discuss variance reduction (e.g., baselines, importance sampling as in Bardou et al.) and sensitivity to the choice of coherent risk (e.g., spectral risks).
- Clarify extensions beyond finite spaces and assumptions required for continuous problems.

Arguments for acceptance
- Unified, general theoretical framework that cleanly subsumes a broad set of risk measures.
- New dynamic PG theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk with a principled AC pathway.
- Conceptually important bridge between coherent risk theory and RL.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient experimental evidence and missing algorithmic details to assess practicality and scalability.
- Heavy reliance on supplemental material; key validations should be in the main paper.
- Potential implementation complexity (inner convex programs, weighted dynamics) not addressed empirically.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are strong and of broad interest, but the paper needs substantially more empirical validation and algorithmic detail to justify acceptance. I encourage resubmission with focused experiments and clear implementation guidance.
