Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that cover the full class of coherent risk measures, both in static and time-consistent dynamic settings. For static risk, the authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) to express the risk as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope and derive a new gradient formula via an envelope theorem applied to the Lagrangian of the convex risk maximization. This yields sampling-based estimators that combine likelihood-ratio terms with quantities obtained from a small convex program, including KKT multipliers. The framework recovers known results as special cases (e.g., CVaR gradient; mean-semideviation) and provides a general SAA-based estimator with a consistency result.

For dynamic, time-consistent risk, the paper considers Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński) and derives a policy gradient theorem that relates ∇θVθ to an expectation over trajectories of a ξ*-reweighted Markov chain, with a stage cost that depends on the value function and KKT multipliers. This leads to an actor-critic style algorithm: the critic approximates the robust-MDP value function (building on robust MDP connections and approximation schemes), and the actor estimates the gradient using simulated trajectories under the ξ*-weighted dynamics. A small finance example illustrates how different risk measures yield qualitatively different policies.

The work unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL methods that targeted specific risk criteria: mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), CVaR (Rockafellar & Uryasev; Chow & Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al.), exponential utility (Borkar), and robust MDP planning (Petrik & Subramanian; Osogami). The main novelty is a coherent-risk-wide gradient formula and its dynamic analogue, with practical estimators.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Provides a single framework for policy gradients across all coherent risks, recovering and simplifying special-case derivations (e.g., CVaR) and supporting others (mean-semideviation, spectral).
- Solid theory: Careful use of convex duality and envelope theorem; clear gradient formulas; SAA-based estimator with consistency; extension to dynamic, time-consistent risk via a new policy gradient theorem.
- Algorithmic clarity: Natural sampling-plus-convex-program routines for static risk; actor-critic construction for dynamic risk that aligns with robust MDP interpretations.
- Relevance: Risk-sensitive RL is important in safety-critical and finance applications; flexibility to tailor the risk envelope is valuable.
- Writing and positioning: Well organized, technically sound derivations, and well referenced.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Empirical evaluation is minimal and largely static; no dynamic-risk experiments or comparisons on standard RL benchmarks to demonstrate scalability, sample complexity, or wall-clock cost.
- Practicality assumptions: Requires explicit risk envelopes, ability to solve a convex program (and retrieve KKT multipliers) per update, and a simulator that supports multiple next-state samples and ξ*-weighted transitions; these may be restrictive in some domains.
- Finite probability/state space assumptions are central to the development; continuous spaces and function approximation receive high-level treatment only (details in supplement).
- Variance and sample-complexity analysis of the gradient estimators is not developed; sensitivity to rare events and to envelope misspecification could be substantial.
- Some actor-critic details and convergence guarantees are deferred to the supplement.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with correct use of convex analysis and likelihood-ratio methods; proofs and consistency statements (for SAA) support the claims. Algorithmic details for the dynamic setting are sketched but credible, though more empirical validation would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: Above average. The motivation, setup, and connections to prior work are clear. Suggestions: briefly summarize the assumptions on U(Pθ) and availability of KKT multipliers in the main text; add pseudocode for both static and dynamic algorithms.
- Originality: The extension from specific risks to general coherent risks, and the new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, are nontrivial and novel. The unification is valuable.
- Significance: High. Provides a general toolset that others can build on to optimize varied risk criteria; connects RL with coherent risk and robust MDPs in a way that invites further applications. Demonstrated impact would be stronger with richer experiments.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide empirical results on at least one standard RL domain (e.g., inventory control, queueing, gridworld, continuous control) for the dynamic risk case, comparing to CVaR- or variance-based baselines and robust MDP methods. Report runtime per update, number/size of convex programs, and sample usage.
- Discuss how to implement ξ*-weighted transitions in practice without modifying the simulator; propose importance sampling or reweighting schemes and analyze their variance.
- Clarify the computational complexity of the static estimator (dimension of ξ, number of constraints) and scalability with sample size N. Any warm-starting or structure that can be exploited?
- Elaborate on continuous state/action extensions and the stability of function approximation in the critic; what function classes and projection schemes are assumed?
- Consider variance-reduction techniques (baselines, control variates) for the gradient, especially for tail-sensitive risks.
- Comment on robustness to modeling errors in the risk envelope and any guidelines for selecting envelopes in applications.

Arguments for acceptance
- General, elegant extension of policy gradient methods to all coherent risks with clear theoretical backing.
- New policy gradient theorem for time-consistent dynamic risk and actor-critic construction.
- Unifies and simplifies prior results; likely to stimulate further research and applications in risk-aware RL.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation; dynamic-risk algorithm not demonstrated on substantive benchmarks.
- Practical constraints (KKT multipliers, ξ*-weighted sampling) may hinder adoption without additional engineering guidance.
- Some assumptions (finite spaces; availability of explicit envelopes) narrow immediate applicability.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely theoretical contribution that generalizes risk-sensitive policy gradients to coherent risks and offers a principled actor-critic approach for dynamic, time-consistent risk. While the experiments are modest, the ideas are well motivated, technically solid, and potentially impactful. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on the authors strengthening the empirical section and clarifying practical implementation aspects.
