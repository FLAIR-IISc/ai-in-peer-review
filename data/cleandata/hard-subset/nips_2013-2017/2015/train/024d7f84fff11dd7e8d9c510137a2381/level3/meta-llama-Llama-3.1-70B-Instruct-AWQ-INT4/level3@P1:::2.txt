This paper proposes a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (RL) using coherent risk measures. The authors extend existing methods for specific risk measures to the general class of coherent risk measures, providing a more comprehensive approach to risk-sensitive RL. The paper presents methods to estimate policy gradients for risk-sensitive problems and proposes corresponding RL algorithms, including policy gradient and actor-critic methods.

The authors demonstrate the approach on a toy finance example where each asset has its own return distribution. The experiments are simple and static but show the algorithm's risk-averse behavior and flexibility across different risk types. The motivation is clear, and the problem formulation and algorithms follow logically from prior work.

The paper is well-written, and the equations are technically sound. All relevant references are included, and the clarity is above average. The originality of the paper is sufficient, as the extension to coherent risk measures is nontrivial. The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses an important problem and presents clear, meaningful contributions to risk-sensitive RL.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL. By extending existing methods to the general class of coherent risk measures, the authors provide a more comprehensive approach to risk-sensitive RL. The paper also demonstrates the flexibility of the approach by applying it to different risk types.

Another strength of the paper is its technical soundness. The equations are well-derived, and the algorithms are clearly explained. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the related work and the contributions of the paper.

One potential weakness of the paper is the simplicity of the experiments. While the toy finance example is useful for illustrating the approach, more complex and dynamic experiments would be beneficial in demonstrating the effectiveness of the algorithm in real-world scenarios.

In terms of originality, the paper is sufficient, but not groundbreaking. The extension to coherent risk measures is nontrivial, but it builds upon existing work in risk-sensitive RL. The paper's significance, however, is high, as it addresses an important problem and presents clear, meaningful contributions to risk-sensitive RL.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a significant contribution to risk-sensitive RL. The authors demonstrate the flexibility and effectiveness of the approach, and the paper is a valuable addition to the field.
