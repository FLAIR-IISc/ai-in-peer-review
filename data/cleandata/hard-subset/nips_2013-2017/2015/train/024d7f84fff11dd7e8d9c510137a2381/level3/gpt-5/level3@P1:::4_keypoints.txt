- Develops policy-gradient methods for coherent risk measures (static trajectory risk and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk) using the dual worst-case expectation representation U(Pθ).

- Static case: derives an envelope-theorem-based gradient formula expressing ∇θρ(Z) via a likelihood-ratio term under adversarial reweighting ξ* and KKT multipliers.

- Provides analytic gradients for CVaR and mean-semideviation and a general SAA estimator that recovers ξ* and multipliers; establishes consistency under mild conditions.

- Dynamic case: adopts time-consistent Markov coherent risk with a Bellman-like equation and proves a policy gradient theorem as an expectation over ξ*-reweighted transitions with per-step cost hθ depending on Vθ and KKT multipliers.

- Proposes an actor-critic scheme: critic uses robust MDP techniques to approximate Vθ; actor estimates gradients via two-phase sampling under ξ*-weighted dynamics.

- Includes a small trading example illustrating how different coherent risks yield qualitatively different policies.

- Unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL results (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR) under a common gradient framework.

- Static gradient derivation is elegant and appears correct; SAA consistency assumptions (convex-compact envelope, Slater, smoothness) are reasonable for common coherent risks.

- Dynamic policy gradient theorem is a substantive, novel contribution that ties risk to robustness.

- Some full proofs are deferred to the supplement; differentiability/uniqueness conditions at the saddle point (e.g., non-unique ξ*) need clearer, explicit statements.

- Empirical evaluation is illustrative but limited—no large-scale MDP benchmarks or comparisons to CVaR/mean-variance/robust baselines.

- Computational aspects (variance, sample complexity, runtime, overhead of per-batch convex solves) are not quantified.

- Clarity could improve on roles of Pθ and ξ; explicitly state ξ as a Radon–Nikodym derivative over base transitions early on.

- Equation (2) needs clarification on whether it is a canonical parameterization or imposes extra restrictions on the envelope and which risks it may exclude.

- Smoothness assumptions (bounded derivatives of ge, fi wrt p) should be restated where they enter proofs and whether weaker assumptions (directional derivatives/subgradients) suffice.

- Must discuss extension to continuous distributions (Lp settings, Gâteaux differentiability, measurable selection for ξ*, and practical SAA discretization).

- Question: conditions ensuring differentiability of ρ(Z) when the saddle point is non-unique and whether subgradient variants of Theorem 4.2 apply.

- Question: justify the canonical envelope form in Eq. (2) and clarify when Assumption 2.2 fails for certain coherent risks.

- Question: dynamic sampling under ξ*—do you need a generative model or can importance sampling from nominal dynamics be used; what is the variance trade-off?

- Suggestion: state contraction and approximation properties of the robust Bellman operator, specify function classes used, and provide error-propagation bounds to the actor.

- Suggestion: provide runtime comparisons per gradient step versus standard PG and CVaR-specific methods; discuss practical size of envelope program and use of stochastic/online solvers.

- Suggestion: include benchmark MDP experiments (e.g., inventory, queuing) with comparisons to CVaR-PG, mean-variance PG, and robust planning, and report variance, bias, and ablations.

- Suggestion: outline conditions and practical SAA scheme for continuous-state/action extensions (dominated densities, Lipschitz envelopes).

- Pros: unified and general framework; clear gradient formulas; elegant use of duality and envelope theorem; novel dynamic policy gradient theorem; links to robustness and important special cases.

- Cons: limited empirical validation and scalability analysis; reliance on explicit envelopes and KKT multipliers could be heavy in practice; some measurability/differentiability assumptions under-discussed; sampling under reweighted dynamics limits model-free applicability.

- Overall: strong theoretical contribution that likely merits acceptance pending stronger experiments and clarifications on assumptions, sampling, and continuous extensions.
