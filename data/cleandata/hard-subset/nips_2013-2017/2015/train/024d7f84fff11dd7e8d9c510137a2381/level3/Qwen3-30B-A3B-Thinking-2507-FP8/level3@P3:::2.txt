

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a significant extension to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures like variance or CVaR as previous work had done. The authors formulate a unified framework that generalizes existing approaches while providing clear theoretical foundations and practical algorithms.

The paper begins by establishing that coherent risk measures (which satisfy convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity) are widely accepted in finance and operations research, and that time-consistent dynamic risk measures are particularly important for sequential decision problems. The authors then develop two key contributions: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, and (2) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that facilitates actor-critic style algorithms for dynamic risk.

The paper's key insight stems from Theorem 2.1, which characterizes coherent risk measures through a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope. This representation allows the authors to derive gradient formulas using the envelope theorem, leading to sampling-based algorithms that combine convex programming with policy gradient techniques. The authors demonstrate how previous results (for CVaR, mean-semideviation, etc.) are special cases of their framework, providing a more general and unified perspective.

The paper is well-written with clear technical exposition, and the supplementary material provides necessary details for reproducibility. The experiments, while simple, effectively demonstrate the approach's ability to produce risk-averse behavior across different risk criteria (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) in a finance example with assets having different return distributions. The results show how different risk preferences lead to different investment choices, highlighting the framework's flexibility.

**Strengths:**
- The extension to the general class of coherent risk measures is technically sound and non-trivial, providing a unified framework that encompasses previous specific approaches.
- The theoretical development is rigorous, with clear proofs and well-justified assumptions.
- The paper demonstrates practical applicability through a clear finance example that illustrates how different risk measures lead to different policy choices.
- The connection to model uncertainty (discussed in the conclusion) provides additional conceptual significance beyond the immediate technical contribution.
- The writing is clear and accessible to the RL community, with sufficient mathematical detail and well-chosen examples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental section is somewhat limited in scope, focusing on a single toy example without comparing against other risk-sensitive RL methods on more complex benchmarks.
- The paper assumes access to a simulator for sampling multiple state-transitions from each state, which may limit applicability to some real-world settings without such simulation capabilities.
- The connection to model uncertainty, while conceptually interesting, is not fully developed in the current work, leaving the full potential of the framework unexplored.

**Evaluation:**
Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development and well-supported claims. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The assumptions are clearly stated and justified. (Score: 4/5)

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with a logical flow from problem formulation to solution. Key concepts are well-explained with appropriate examples, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. (Score: 5/5)

Originality: The extension to the entire class of coherent risk measures represents a meaningful theoretical contribution that generalizes previous specific approaches. The unified framework is novel and valuable for the risk-sensitive RL community. (Score: 4/5)

Significance: Risk-sensitive RL is an important problem with applications in finance, robotics, and other domains where variability matters. The paper provides a principled approach to handling different risk preferences in a unified framework, which has clear potential for practical application. The conceptual connection to model uncertainty further enhances the significance. (Score: 4/5)

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a strong contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The technical content is sound, the writing is excellent, and the significance of the problem addressed is high. While the experimental section could be expanded, the theoretical contribution stands on its own merits. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL and provides a foundation for future work in this important area.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it combines technical depth with clear exposition and addresses an important problem with meaningful contributions. The work is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 10, 21, 29, 30]) by providing a unified framework rather than focusing on specific risk measures.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Unified framework for coherent risk measures that generalizes previous specific approaches
- Rigorous theoretical development with clear proofs
- Practical algorithms derived naturally from the theory
- Clear demonstration of risk-averse behavior across different risk types
- Well-written and logically structured paper

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited experimental evaluation (single toy example)
- Requires simulator for sampling multiple state-transitions
- Connection to model uncertainty is promising but not fully developed

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning and should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
