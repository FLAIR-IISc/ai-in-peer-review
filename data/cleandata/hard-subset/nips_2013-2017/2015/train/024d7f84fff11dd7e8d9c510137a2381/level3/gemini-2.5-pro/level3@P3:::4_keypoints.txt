- Paper proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for optimizing policies under the full class of coherent risk measures using the dual (risk-envelope) representation.  
- Key theoretical contributions: a general gradient formula for static coherent risk measures (Theorem 4.2) and a policy-gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2).  
- Algorithms: a sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator for the static case and an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case, generalizing prior specialized methods.  
- Significance/originality: unifies a fragmented literature, subsuming many existing risk-sensitive RL approaches and enabling tailored risk-measure design.  
- Technical quality: rigorous, elegant derivations using the dual representation and the Envelope Theorem; solid examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) demonstrate utility.  
- Clarity/presentation: well-written, accessible introduction of risk theory and clear progression from static to dynamic settings.  
- Weakness — empirical: limited numerical evaluation; needs thorough experiments comparing sample complexity, variance, and convergence to specialized analytic estimators.  
- Weakness — preliminaries: relationship between the base measure P_θ and re-weighting density ξ needs a clearer, more intuitive explanation.  
- Technical question: Assumption 2.2 may be more restrictive than the general convex-compact risk envelope—clarification needed on whether this limits the class of coherent risks covered.  
- Technical question: analysis is for finite probability spaces; the paper should briefly discuss extensions and key challenges for continuous distributions (densities, functional derivatives).  
- Technical question: the smoothness of constraint functions f_i and g_e in Assumption 2.2 is crucial for applying the Envelope Theorem and should be explicitly linked in the text.  
- Overall assessment: substantial and original theoretical advance with sound proofs; main shortcoming is limited empirical validation, but paper merits acceptance to a top-tier conference.
