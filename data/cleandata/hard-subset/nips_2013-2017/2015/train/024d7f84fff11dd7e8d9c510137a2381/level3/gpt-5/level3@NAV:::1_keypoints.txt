- Develops policy-gradient methods for the full class of coherent risk measures, covering static and dynamic settings.
- Static case: introduces a new gradient formula using the dual (risk-envelope) representation and envelope theorem, giving analytic gradients for risks like CVaR and mean-semideviation.
- Proposes a sampling-plus-convex-programming (SAA) estimator when closed-form saddle points are unavailable, and proves its consistency.
- Dynamic case: provides a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, linking gradients to a value function and proposing an actor-critic approach.
- Unifies and generalizes prior risk-specific results and connects to robust MDP formulations of dynamic coherent risk.
- Theoretical development is solid and technically careful, employing coherent risk duality, likelihood-ratio identities, and dynamic programming.
- The consistency proof for the sample-based estimator is a valuable theoretical contribution.
- Empirical support is minimal: no experiments validating the SAA estimator or comparing it to analytic gradients (e.g., CVaR).
- Dynamic section is high-level and lacks algorithmic detail (no pseudo-code, sampling/projection procedures, simulator requirements, or complexity analysis).
- Paper is over-ambitious for eight pages, which hurts clarity—static section clearer, dynamic section needs expansion or focus.
- Originality is high: the unified derivation and Markov extension appear novel beyond prior work.
- Significance is potentially broad for risk-sensitive decision making and robust RL if methods are demonstrated empirically and made practical.
- Pros: unified elegant derivation, consistency of sampling-based estimator, extension to dynamic Markov coherent risk.
- Cons: limited analytic applicability (closed-form only for some risks), no empirical validation/comparison, dynamic practicality unclear, scope too broad.
- Suggestions: provide experiments comparing SAA vs analytic gradients (variance, bias, runtime), include dynamic actor-critic experiments, detail algorithmic steps and costs, or narrow scope to static risks for comprehensive empirical evaluation.
