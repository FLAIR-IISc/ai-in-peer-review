This paper presents a novel approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by extending the policy gradient method to the whole class of coherent risk measures. The authors provide a unified framework for both static and dynamic risk measures, which is a significant contribution to the field. The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the theoretical results.

The key strength of the paper is its ability to generalize previous results on risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors provide a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk measures, which is convenient for approximation using sampling. They also present a general sampling-based algorithm for cases lacking analytical solutions and prove the consistency of the policy gradient estimates. Additionally, they provide expressions for policy gradients of Markov coherent dynamic risk measures.

The paper's technical contributions are substantial, and the authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the subject matter. The use of the Envelope theorem and the likelihood-ratio trick to derive the gradient formula is particularly noteworthy. The authors also provide several examples, including the CVaR and mean-semideviation risk measures, to illustrate the applicability of their approach.

However, the paper has some limitations. The analytical Lagrangian saddle-point solutions exist only for some risk measures, limiting the closed-form applicability of the approach. While the sample-based approximation could extend the applicability, it lacks empirical validation in the paper. Furthermore, there is no experimental comparison between the sample-based and analytical gradients, which would be helpful in assessing the performance of the proposed approach.

The paper's clarity is generally good, but the authors attempt to cover too many issues in eight pages, which hurts the clarity in some sections. The section on dynamic risk policy gradients is high-level and lacks algorithmic details and experiments, making the practicality of the approach unclear. The authors could consider splitting the work or focusing on static risk measures to allow for more details and comprehensive empirical evaluation.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to risk-sensitive decision-making. The unified derivation is a significant advancement in the field, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper is substantial, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results have the potential to impact various fields, including finance, operations research, and reinforcement learning. The paper provides a unique theoretical approach and a novel combination of familiar techniques, which advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Overall, the paper is interesting and potentially broadly applicable, but the lack of comprehensive experiments prevents a full assessment of its impact. The authors could consider providing more experimental results and comparisons to strengthen their claims.
