This paper introduces a unified framework for computing policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures, a standard in finance and operations research. This work generalizes previous risk-sensitive reinforcement learning methods that have focused on specific measures like variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The core contribution is a new gradient formula for static coherent risks, derived from their dual representation as a worst-case expectation. This formula enables both the analytical derivation of policy gradients for some common risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and a general sampling-based algorithm for cases where no analytical solution exists. The authors prove the consistency of this sampling-based estimator. The paper further extends these ideas to the dynamic setting, presenting a policy gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent dynamic risk measures, which lays the groundwork for an actor-critic style algorithm.

The paper's primary strength is its originality and significance. The proposed unified derivation is a novel and valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive decision-making. By leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures, the authors provide an elegant and general approach that encompasses and simplifies many previous, disparate results. This framework not only re-derives gradients for known measures like CVaR with a simpler proof but also provides a clear recipe for tackling any risk measure within this large and important class. The theoretical work appears sound, with a clear derivation of the main gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) and a consistency proof for the general sample-based estimator (Proposition 4.4). The potential impact is high, as it provides a principled and flexible tool for practitioners to incorporate a wide variety of risk preferences into learning agents, which is crucial in domains like automated trading and robotics.

Despite its strong theoretical contributions, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily concerning its empirical evaluation and clarity.

1.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The general sampling-based algorithm is a key practical contribution for risk measures lacking analytical solutions, yet it is not empirically validated in the paper. While the authors state in the supplementary material that they compared it to the analytical solution for CVaR, this analysis is crucial and should be in the main text. An experiment comparing the sample efficiency, variance, and computational overhead of the general approach (which requires solving a convex optimization problem at each step) against the direct analytical gradient would be highly informative. The absence of such experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical utility and trade-offs of the proposed core algorithm.

2.  **Overly Ambitious Scope and Lack of Depth:** The paper attempts to cover static risks, a general sampling algorithm, and an extension to dynamic risks within the strict page limit. This breadth comes at the expense of clarity and depth. The section on dynamic risk measures is particularly high-level, presenting a policy gradient theorem but omitting a concrete algorithm, pseudocode, or any experimental results. Key details are deferred to supplementary material, making the second half of the paper feel incomplete and its contributions hard to evaluate fully. The practicality of the proposed actor-critic approach remains unclear without a more thorough treatment. To improve the paper, the authors could consider focusing solely on static risk measures. This would allow for a more detailed explanation and, critically, a comprehensive empirical evaluation of the proposed general algorithm.

3.  **Limited Applicability of Analytical Solutions:** The paper highlights the derivation of analytical gradients for measures like CVaR. However, these elegant results are only possible when the Lagrangian saddle-point problem has a closed-form solution. This is not the case for many coherent risk measures, which somewhat limits the applicability of the "analytical" part of the framework and underscores the need for a stronger empirical validation of the more general sampling-based method.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound framework that could be broadly applicable to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified derivation of policy gradients for coherent risks is a significant and novel contribution. However, the paper's impact is currently limited by a lack of comprehensive experimental validation and an overly ambitious scope that compromises the clarity and completeness of the proposed methods, especially for the dynamic risk setting. While the theoretical ideas are promising, the absence of empirical results makes it difficult to fully assess the practical significance of the work.
