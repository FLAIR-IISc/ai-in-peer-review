### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning (RL). The work addresses a significant gap in risk-sensitive RL, where prior research has largely focused on developing algorithms for specific risk measures such as mean-variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors' primary contribution is a unified approach that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures.

The paper tackles two distinct problem settings:
1.  **Static Risk:** Optimizing a coherent risk measure applied to the total discounted cost of a trajectory.
2.  **Dynamic Risk:** Optimizing a time-consistent, dynamic Markov coherent risk measure.

The core technical innovation is the use of the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which states that any coherent risk can be expressed as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities called the "risk envelope." By applying the Envelope Theorem to this dual formulation, the authors derive a novel, general formula for the policy gradient of a static coherent risk measure. This formula elegantly expresses the gradient in terms of the optimal solution (the worst-case density and corresponding Lagrange multipliers) to the dual problem.

Building on this static gradient formula, the paper presents several key contributions. For static risk, it shows how its general formula can recover and simplify proofs for known policy gradients (e.g., for CVaR) and derive new ones (e.g., for mean-semideviation). For cases where the dual problem cannot be solved analytically, it proposes a general gradient estimation algorithm based on Sample Average Approximation (SAA), which involves solving a convex program at each step, and provides a consistency proof for this estimator.

For the dynamic risk setting, the paper derives a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures. This theorem extends the standard policy gradient theorem to this risk-sensitive context, relating the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. Based on this result, the authors outline an actor-critic algorithm where the critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function (leveraging its connection to robust MDPs) and the actor updates the policy using the derived gradient.

This work relates to and generalizes a significant body of prior literature. It unifies approaches from [8, 21, 29, 30] which targeted individual risk measures. It also extends the optimization framework for coherent risk from stochastic programming [25, 26] to the RL setting, where the policy parameters directly influence the underlying probability distributions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main strength lies in its generality. By providing a policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures, it unifies a fragmented area of research. This is a significant conceptual advance that provides a principled foundation for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods. The connection between the dual representation, the Envelope Theorem, and policy gradients is novel and insightful.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical derivations appear sound. The application of the Envelope Theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) is an elegant way to obtain a general gradient formula. The extension to a policy gradient theorem for the dynamic, time-consistent setting (Theorem 5.2) is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The framework is theoretically well-grounded.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It provides the necessary background on coherent and dynamic risk measures before systematically presenting the contributions for the static and dynamic cases. The inclusion of examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrates the utility and generality of the main theoretical results.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Cost of the General Algorithm:** The proposed SAA-based algorithm for general static coherent risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem at each gradient estimation step. The complexity of this step scales with the number of samples `N`. This may render the approach computationally intensive for large `N` or complex risk envelopes, potentially limiting its practical scalability compared to methods tailored to specific risk measures with analytical solutions. A discussion on the computational trade-offs would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Lack of Detail on the Dynamic Risk Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem is described at a high level, with most details deferred to the supplementary material due to space constraints. Key aspects, such as the two-phase sampling procedure for the actor and the handling of function approximation error from the critic, are central to the algorithm's viability but are not sufficiently detailed in the main text for a full assessment of their soundness and practicality.
3.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration is performed on a stateless multi-armed bandit problem. While effective at highlighting the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure (mean-semideviation vs. standard deviation), it does not validate the core algorithmic contributions for sequential decision-making problems (MDPs). An experiment demonstrating the performance of the proposed actor-critic algorithm on an MDP would have provided much stronger evidence for the method's effectiveness in the dynamic risk setting.
4.  **Sample Efficiency Concerns:** The paper rightly notes that risk measures sensitive to rare events can lead to high-variance gradient estimates when using likelihood ratio methods. While this is acknowledged as an area for future work, the practical impact of this issue on the proposed algorithms is not deeply explored. Given that managing rare, high-cost events is a primary motivation for using risk measures like CVaR, this is a significant practical challenge that warrants more discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel and unifying theoretical framework for policy gradient methods with coherent risk measures, a significant contribution to risk-sensitive RL.
*   The technical approach is sound, elegant, and provides new insights by connecting optimization theory (duality, Envelope Theorem) with RL.
*   The paper is clear, well-organized, and successfully generalizes and simplifies several existing results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical viability of the general algorithm is not fully established due to potential computational costs and a lack of detailed description for the dynamic case.
*   The experimental validation is limited to a simple bandit setting and does not test the main algorithm for dynamic risk in an MDP.

In summary, the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by providing a unified and principled framework for policy gradient optimization of coherent risk measures. While the practical aspects and experimental validation could be strengthened, the novelty, significance, and technical quality of the theoretical results make this a valuable contribution to the field.
