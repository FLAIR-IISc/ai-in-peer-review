

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors introduce a general approach that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific instances like CVaR or mean-variance as previous works have done. The key innovation is the derivation of a gradient formula for static coherent risk using the representation theorem of coherent risk measures, which enables sampling-based estimation. The paper further extends this to dynamic Markov coherent risk, providing a new policy gradient theorem and actor-critic algorithm.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, Theorem 4.2 provides a general formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that can be implemented through sampling and convex programming. This generalizes previous results for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) into a single framework. Second, the paper develops a novel policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), extending the classic policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings. Third, the authors establish a connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty, suggesting broader applications beyond risk-sensitive RL.

The theoretical foundations are solid, with clear proofs of key results. The paper properly situates its work within the literature, acknowledging prior research on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) and distinguishing its unified approach. The numerical example demonstrates the practical utility of the framework by showing how different risk measures lead to different optimal policies in a trading scenario, highlighting the importance of risk preference design.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While risk-sensitive RL has been studied extensively, this work provides a comprehensive theoretical framework that unifies previous approaches. Previous papers have focused on specific risk measures, requiring separate derivations for each. This paper shows that all coherent risk measures can be handled within a single framework, which is a significant conceptual advance. The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) further elevates the work beyond a mere technical extension.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear exposition of the mathematical concepts. The authors properly address the technical challenges, including the need for convex programming in the gradient estimation procedure. The theoretical results are presented with appropriate rigor, and the consistency results (Proposition 4.4) provide important guarantees.

The main limitation is the limited empirical evaluation. While the numerical example is illustrative, a more comprehensive set of experiments across different domains would strengthen the practical claims. However, as a theoretical contribution with strong conceptual novelty, this limitation is less critical than it would be for a more incremental paper.

This work is highly significant for the reinforcement learning community. It provides a principled foundation for risk-sensitive decision making that can be applied to a wide range of problems, from finance to operations research. The framework's flexibility in handling different risk preferences is particularly valuable for real-world applications where risk tolerance varies across contexts.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NeurIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in reinforcement learning with a novel mathematical approach. The connection to coherent risk theory from finance (Artzner et al.) is well-motivated and provides a strong theoretical foundation.

Strengths:
- Novel unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL
- Rigorous theoretical contributions with clear proofs
- Generalizes and simplifies previous approaches to risk-sensitive RL
- Connects risk measures to model uncertainty, suggesting broader applications
- Well-written and well-organized

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical evaluation (though this is less critical for a theoretical contribution)
- Assumes finite probability space (though authors note this can be extended)
- Computational complexity of convex programming step not fully explored

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors successfully generalize previous results to the entire class of coherent risk measures, which is a substantial conceptual advance. The paper's novelty and theoretical soundness outweigh the minor limitations in empirical evaluation. As a reviewer who prioritizes bold new concepts over minor methodological details, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS. The work opens up new research directions for risk-sensitive decision making and provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.
