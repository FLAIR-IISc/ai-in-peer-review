### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives. It moves beyond prior work, which typically focused on specific risk measures (like CVaR or mean-variance), by providing algorithms applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The core technical contribution is leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a "risk envelope" of probability densities.

The paper makes two main contributions:
1.  **Static Risk:** For a static coherent risk of a random return, the authors derive a new, general formula for its policy gradient using the envelope theorem. They demonstrate that this formula recovers and simplifies existing results for specific measures like CVaR and can be used to derive new ones, such as for the mean-semideviation risk. For the general case where an analytical solution is unavailable, they propose an algorithm that estimates the gradient by solving a sample average approximation (SAA) of the dual problem, combining sampling with convex programming. A consistency result for this estimator is provided.
2.  **Dynamic Risk:** The paper extends this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. It presents a new policy gradient theorem for this setting, analogous to the standard one, which relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. Based on this theorem, the authors outline an actor-critic algorithm to solve the dynamic risk problem, where the critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function and the actor updates the policy.

### **Strengths**

*   **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. By tackling the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a unified theoretical and algorithmic framework that subsumes and generalizes many previous ad-hoc approaches. This is a significant conceptual advance for the field of risk-sensitive RL.
*   **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty:** The technical approach is elegant and novel. The combination of the dual representation of coherent risk with the envelope theorem and the likelihood-ratio trick to derive a general policy gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) is a powerful and insightful contribution. The extension to a full policy gradient theorem for the dynamic case (Theorem 5.2) is also a substantial theoretical result.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It provides a clear motivation for using coherent risk measures, introduces the necessary mathematical preliminaries effectively, and builds its contributions from the static case to the more complex dynamic case. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrate the power of the general static gradient formula.

### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   **Lack of Algorithmic Detail for the Dynamic Case:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is a key contribution, but its description is extremely brief and defers all significant details to supplementary material. The main paper would be much stronger if it included a more detailed sketch of the algorithm, particularly regarding: (i) how the critic leverages the connection to robust MDPs to approximate the value function Vθ, and (ii) the specifics of the two-phase sampling procedure for the actor. As it stands, this part of the contribution is difficult to fully evaluate from the main text.
*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration in Section 6 is a simple one-step (bandit) problem. While it effectively demonstrates the conceptual benefit of having a flexible choice of risk measures, it does not validate the proposed *learning algorithms*. The paper would be significantly strengthened by applying the actor-critic algorithm to a standard sequential decision-making benchmark (e.g., a gridworld, CartPole) to demonstrate that it can successfully learn a policy that optimizes a dynamic coherent risk objective. This would provide evidence of the algorithm's practical viability.
*   **Unaddressed Computational Concerns:** The general gradient estimator for static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem at each gradient update step. The complexity of this subproblem, which has O(N) variables and constraints for N samples, is not discussed. For large N, which may be necessary for accurate estimation, this could be a computational bottleneck. A brief discussion of this complexity and how it compares to simpler, specialized estimators would be valuable.
*   **Clarity of Assumptions:** The consistency result in Proposition 4.4 relies on a set of assumptions that, while plausible, could benefit from more justification. For example, Assumption (III), stating that the set of saddle points for the empirical problem `SN` is non-empty and bounded w.p. 1 for large N, could be briefly elaborated upon for the reader's intuition.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   The derivation of the CVaR gradient as a special case is presented as simpler and more general than in [30]. This is a strong point and highlights the elegance of the proposed framework.
*   The connection between dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs is crucial but mentioned only briefly. Expanding on this, especially in the context of the critic's design, would improve the paper's completeness.
*   The final discussion on "risk shaping" to account for model uncertainty is thought-provoking and suggests exciting avenues for future work, strengthening the paper's long-term significance.

### **Assessment of Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The theoretical contributions are of high quality, technically sound, and well-supported. However, the paper feels incomplete as a whole due to the sparse description of the dynamic risk algorithm and the lack of empirical validation for it.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is mostly very clear, especially in its motivation and the development of the static risk case. The clarity declines in the dynamic risk section due to its high level of compression.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The unified approach to policy gradients for coherent risk is novel and provides a new, powerful perspective that advances the state of the art beyond previous work on specific risk measures.
*   **Significance:** The paper has high potential significance. It provides both theoretical tools and an algorithmic blueprint that could enable widespread application and research into a broad class of risk-sensitive RL problems. Researchers and practitioners are likely to build on these ideas.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a significant and elegant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. Its main achievement is a unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are novel and insightful. The primary weaknesses are the lack of algorithmic detail for the dynamic setting in the main paper and an experimental section that illustrates a concept rather than validating the proposed learning algorithm. Despite these weaknesses, the strength and novelty of the theoretical framework make this a strong paper. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to use the final version to incorporate more details about their actor-critic algorithm and, if possible, to add an experiment on a sequential task.
