Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. It treats both (i) static coherent risk of a random return Z and (ii) time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk for MDPs. The core technical contributions are:

- A general gradient formula for static coherent risk measures using the dual representation (risk envelope) and the envelope theorem, yielding a likelihood-ratio-style estimator. The formula recovers known special cases (e.g., CVaR) and yields new ones (e.g., mean-semideviation).
- A sample average approximation (SAA) scheme that computes saddle points of the dual problem via convex programming and uses the associated KKT multipliers to form a consistent gradient estimator.
- A “policy gradient theorem” for Markov coherent risk that expresses the gradient as an expectation over trajectories of a modified Markov chain with ξ*-reweighted transitions, involving a stage-wise signal hθ that depends on the value function and derivatives of the risk-envelope constraints. This enables an actor-critic algorithm: a critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function (via the robust MDP equivalence) and an actor uses the gradient formula to update the policy.

The work is positioned against risk-sensitive RL literature focused on specific risk criteria: exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR (static and dynamic), and robust MDPs. It leverages the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and the connection between dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs (e.g., Osogami; Ruszczyński). The SAA consistency builds on stochastic programming theory. The dynamic gradient result aims to be a unifying extension of the classic policy gradient theorem to coherent risk.

Quality
- Theory: The static gradient derivation via the Lagrangian and envelope theorem is elegant and appears sound under the stated assumptions (finite spaces, known risk envelope with smooth dependence on Pθ, bounded likelihood ratios). Recovering the CVaR gradient and deriving the mean-semideviation gradient support correctness and utility. The SAA-based estimator and its consistency proposition are natural and technically reasonable; assumptions (Slater condition, continuity, compactness) are standard. The dynamic gradient theorem is novel and technically interesting; it carefully accounts for the derivative of the risk-envelope constraints and correctly changes the sampling distribution to the ξ*-reweighted kernel.

- Practicality/algorithmics: The approach requires solving a convex program to obtain ξ* and KKT multipliers; in the dynamic case, this can be per state, potentially expensive. The dynamic actor requires sampling from a ξ*-weighted model; this generally needs a generative model or importance sampling, which the paper notes but does not resolve. The critic relies on function approximation for a robust MDP; details and convergence analysis are deferred to the supplement.

- Empirical evaluation: The experimental evidence is very limited (a toy “trading” bandit showing that different risk criteria bias action selection differently). There are no dynamic MDP experiments, no comparisons to risk-sensitive RL baselines (e.g., CVaR-PG, robust MDP planning) in control tasks, and no evaluation of estimator variance, computational cost, or scalability.

Clarity
The paper is clearly structured and well-written for a theory contribution. The notation is heavy but consistent. Key assumptions are stated. However:
- The need to compute derivatives dfi/dp and dge/dp in hθ deserves more concrete guidance for common envelopes (CVaR, spectral).
- The dynamic theorem would benefit from explicitly listing additional Markov chain assumptions (ergodicity/mixing under the ξ*-weighted kernel) and conditions for interchanging gradient and infinite sum.
- Non-differentiability of risk (e.g., CVaR at quantile ties) and non-uniqueness of saddle points are not discussed; stating that the formula yields a subgradient would improve rigor.
- Pseudocode for the SAA-based gradient and the actor-critic, and a complexity discussion, would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The unifying envelope-theorem-based gradient for general coherent risk measures is a novel synthesis that simplifies and generalizes prior ad hoc derivations (e.g., CVaR). The SAA/KKT-based estimator for general envelopes is also a useful contribution.
- The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears new and conceptually important, extending policy gradient theory beyond expected return.

Significance
- If made practical, the framework could influence risk-sensitive RL by enabling policy-gradient methods for a wide class of coherent risk criteria and time-consistent objectives.
- The current empirical evidence and algorithmic details are insufficient to establish practical significance. The requirements (convex program per state/batch; generative model for ξ*-weighted sampling) may limit applicability. Nonetheless, the theoretical results are potentially impactful.

Pros
- Broad, principled, and unifying framework covering the entire class of coherent risks (static and dynamic).
- Clean theoretical results: general static gradient, SAA consistency, and a dynamic policy gradient theorem.
- Recovers and simplifies known results (CVaR) and yields new ones (mean-semideviation).
- Appropriate connections to robust MDP literature; clear motivation for time consistency.

Cons
- Minimal experiments; no dynamic MDP evaluation; no scalability or variance analysis.
- Strong assumptions: finite spaces, known and smooth risk envelope; bounded score functions; generative model and ability to sample from reweighted dynamics.
- Practical burden of solving convex programs and computing constraint derivatives; no complexity/runtime study.
- Missing discussion of subgradients at non-smooth points; lack of explicit ergodicity conditions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add dynamic MDP experiments (e.g., gridworld, inventory control, portfolio MDP) comparing to CVaR-PG, robust MDP baselines, and risk-neutral PG. Report sample complexity, runtime, and variance with/without control variates/baselines.
- Provide pseudocode and implementation details for the SAA gradient and actor-critic, including how ξ* and KKT multipliers are computed efficiently and how dfi/dp, dge/dp are handled for common envelopes.
- Discuss subgradient handling at non-differentiable points and conditions ensuring differentiability almost everywhere.
- State ergodicity/mixing assumptions for the ξ*-weighted chain and justify exchanging limits/expectations.
- Explore importance sampling (off-policy) strategies for the ξ*-weighted kernel; quantify bias/variance trade-offs.
- Consider variance reduction (baselines/advantages, compatible function approximation) for the dynamic estimator.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical contributions are elegant and potentially impactful, but the lack of substantive empirical validation and the practicality gaps (sampling assumptions, computational cost) make it premature for NeurIPS in its current form. A strengthened empirical section and clearer algorithmic guidance would likely tip this to acceptance.

Confidence: 0.7
