### Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel and general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with risk-sensitive objectives. Instead of focusing on a single risk measure, the authors develop a unified approach for the entire class of coherent risk measures, a principled family of functions satisfying desirable axiomatic properties. The paper's main contributions are:
1.  A new gradient formula for static coherent risk measures (Theorem 4.2). This formula is derived using the dual representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem, expressing the gradient in terms of the solution to a convex optimization problem.
2.  A general gradient estimation algorithm for the static case that combines sampling (via Sample Average Approximation) with convex programming. A consistency result for this estimator is provided.
3.  An extension of the classic policy gradient theorem to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This new theorem relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and an expectation over a "distorted" trajectory distribution, defined by the dual variables of the risk measure.
4.  The outline of an actor-critic algorithm based on this new policy gradient theorem.

This work significantly generalizes previous research in risk-sensitive policy gradients, which has largely been piecemeal, developing bespoke algorithms for specific risk measures like CVaR (e.g., [8, 30]) or mean-variance/standard deviation (e.g., [21, 29]). By leveraging the fundamental structure of coherent risk, the authors provide a single, elegant framework that can instantiate these previous results and also be applied to any other coherent risk measure.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical contribution. It provides a principled and unified framework for a broad and important class of risk-sensitive RL problems. The idea of using the dual representation of coherent risk to derive a general policy gradient formula is highly novel and insightful. This work has the potential to become a foundational reference for risk-sensitive RL, enabling researchers and practitioners to design algorithms for new, problem-specific risk objectives without having to re-derive the theory from scratch. The connection made to robust MDPs and the future vision of "risk shaping" for model uncertainty are compelling.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is elegant and appears sound. The application of the envelope theorem to the dual form of the coherent risk measure is a clever technique that simplifies the gradient derivation considerably. Theorem 4.2 is a powerful result, and the subsequent policy gradient theorem for the dynamic case (Theorem 5.2) is a non-trivial and important extension of classic results [28]. The paper also shows how this general framework cleanly recovers and simplifies existing results for CVaR, demonstrating its power and correctness.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts by motivating the problem, introduces the necessary mathematical preliminaries on coherent risk, and then builds its contributions sequentially, moving from the static risk setting to the more complex dynamic one. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation are very effective at illustrating the application of the main theoretical result.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Incompleteness of the Proposed Algorithm:** While the theoretical foundation is strong, the description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is very high-level and feels incomplete within the main paper. Key details regarding the critic's function approximation scheme (referencing [31]), the actor's two-phase sampling procedure, and the overall convergence analysis are deferred to supplementary material. This makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess the practicality and complexity of the proposed solution. A conference paper should ideally present a more self-contained contribution.

*   **Potential Computational Complexity:** The proposed methods may be computationally demanding. The general algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) for each gradient estimate, which could be slow if the number of samples N is large. The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case requires a two-phase sampling procedure (first sampling a trajectory from a distorted MDP, then sampling next states at each step), which seems more complex and potentially less sample-efficient than standard single-rollout methods. A discussion of this computational overhead is missing.

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is a simple 3-armed bandit problem. While it effectively demonstrates the importance of choosing the right risk measure, it does not evaluate the performance of the proposed algorithm (e.g., convergence speed, variance of the gradient estimate) or compare it against specialized algorithms on a standard RL benchmark. This makes it hard to gauge the algorithm's practical effectiveness.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper introduces a highly novel, elegant, and unifying theoretical framework for a major subfield of RL. This is a significant advance over prior art.
2.  **Generality:** The results apply to the entire class of coherent risk measures, which is of great importance to communities in finance, operations research, and increasingly, machine learning.
3.  **Potential for High Impact:** This work provides the tools for other researchers to build upon, potentially spurring a new wave of research in principled risk-sensitive learning.

**Arguments for Rejection**
1.  **Practical Algorithm is Underdeveloped:** The main practical contribution, the actor-critic algorithm, is only sketched, with critical details and analysis relegated to an appendix. This weakens the paper as a self-contained work.
2.  **Lack of Substantive Experiments:** The empirical results are purely illustrative and do not validate the algorithm's performance on any challenging task, leaving questions about its practical viability unanswered.

**Recommendation**

Despite the weaknesses in the practical and empirical sections, the theoretical contribution of this paper is outstanding. It solves a long-standing problem of unifying risk-sensitive policy gradient methods in a principled and elegant manner. This is precisely the kind of foundational work that NIPS should encourage. The weaknesses, while notable, primarily concern the algorithmic implementation and evaluation, which can be addressed in future work that will undoubtedly build on this paper's foundation. Therefore, I recommend **acceptance**.

**Suggestions for Improvement**
*   I would strongly encourage the authors to bring more details of the actor-critic algorithm into the main paper, even if it requires shortening other sections. For instance, a more concrete algorithm box and a brief summary of the function approximation and sampling schemes would greatly improve the paper's completeness.
*   Adding a discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods and potential ways to mitigate it would be valuable.
*   While a full empirical evaluation might be out of scope, even applying the dynamic risk algorithm to a simple control problem (e.g., gridworld) and showing its learning curve would add significant value.
