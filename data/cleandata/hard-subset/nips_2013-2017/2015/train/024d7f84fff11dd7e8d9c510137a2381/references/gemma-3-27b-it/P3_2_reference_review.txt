## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and welcome contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It provides a unifying framework for applying policy gradients to a broad class of coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work and offering practical algorithms for both static and dynamic risk settings. The theoretical results are sound, and the paper is generally well-written. While the numerical example is limited, it effectively illustrates the potential of the approach. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of optimizing policies in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) when the objective is not simply maximizing expected reward, but minimizing a coherent risk measure of the cumulative cost.  Coherent risk measures, satisfying properties like convexity and monotonicity, are crucial in applications where managing downside risk is paramount. The authors formulate policy gradient algorithms capable of handling the entire class of coherent risk measures, bridging a gap between methods tailored to specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance) and those relying on more restrictive assumptions. 

The paper correctly positions its contribution within the existing literature, referencing key works on risk-sensitive RL, robust MDPs, and stochastic programming.  It clearly highlights how their approach generalizes previous work, offering a more unified and flexible framework. The connection to robust MDPs [19] and the discussion of limitations of existing optimization techniques for coherent risk (e.g., dynamic programming [24], stochastic programming [25,26]) are well articulated.  

**2. Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The central strength is the ability to handle the entire class of coherent risk measures. This broad applicability is a major advantage over existing methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas, particularly Theorem 4.2, appears rigorous and leverages established results like the Envelope Theorem. The consistency result for the sampling-based estimator (Proposition 4.4) is crucial for practical application.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The combination of sampling-based gradient estimation with convex programming is a pragmatic approach for tackling the computational challenges of coherent risk optimization.  The proposal of an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is a significant step towards scalable implementations.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are mostly clear and easy to follow (though some could benefit from more intermediate steps).
* **Connection to Robust MDPs:** Highlighting the connection to robust MDPs strengthens the theoretical foundation and opens avenues for leveraging existing techniques.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Numerical Evaluation:** The numerical example, while illustrative, is relatively simple and focuses on a single scenario. A more comprehensive evaluation with diverse MDP environments, potentially including continuous state/action spaces, would significantly strengthen the paper. Demonstrating scalability and comparing performance against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms would be highly valuable.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors acknowledge the challenges of implementing the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case, a more detailed discussion of practical considerations (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, function approximation choices) would be beneficial. 
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of a known risk envelope (Assumption 2.2) is strong. While often reasonable, it might not hold in all practical scenarios.  Discussing the implications of relaxing this assumption or exploring methods for learning the risk envelope would be valuable.
* **Minor Clarifications:**  There are a few points where the explanation could be clearer. For example, the connection between the convex programming solution in (7) and the derivation of the gradient estimator in (8) could be more explicitly explained.  



**4.  Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical contribution unifying policy gradient methods for coherent risk.
*  Addresses a gap in the literature with a broadly applicable framework.
*  Provides well-defined algorithms based on sound mathematical foundations.
*  Clear writing and organization.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Limited numerical validation.
*  Strong assumption about knowledge of the risk envelope.
*  Complexity of implementation isn’t fully addressed.


**5.  Specific Comments/Questions:**

* **Section 4.3:** It would be helpful to briefly discuss the trade-off between the sample size *N* and the accuracy of the gradient estimate.  What is the expected convergence rate as *N* increases?
* **Section 5:**  Can you provide more details on the function approximation methods used for estimating the value function in the actor-critic algorithm? How does the choice of function approximator affect the convergence properties?
* **Theorem 5.2:** The expression for `hθ(x, a)` appears quite complex.  Can you provide some intuition as to why this form is necessary and how it relates to the coherent risk objective?
* **Numerical Example:**  Consider including a comparison to a standard policy gradient algorithm trained with an L1 regularization term, to provide a baseline for risk aversion.




**Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by expanding the numerical evaluation, would further enhance its contribution and make it an even more compelling publication.**
