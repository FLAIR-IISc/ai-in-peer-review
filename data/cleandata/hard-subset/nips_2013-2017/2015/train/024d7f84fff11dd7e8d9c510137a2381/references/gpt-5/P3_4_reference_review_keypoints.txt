- Proposes policy-gradient methods for coherent risk measures using the dual (risk-envelope) representation to reduce risk evaluation to worst-case reweighted expectations.  
- Derives a general gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) yielding a likelihood-ratio estimator dependent on optimal dual variables.  
- Recovers known special cases (e.g., CVaR) and provides a new gradient for mean-semideviation.  
- Proposes an SAA-based sampling estimator that solves a convex program to obtain ξ* and KKT multipliers, with an almost-sure consistency proof under mild conditions.  
- Extends results to time-consistent Markov coherent risk, deriving a dynamic policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) linking gradients to value functions and statewise saddle-point quantities.  
- Outlines an actor-critic method using robust-MDP connections for the critic and ξ*-weighted sampling for the actor.  
- Provides a small numerical illustration showing different risk functionals produce different asset choices in a bandit-like setting.  
- Static gradient derivation is clean and elegant, using KKT multipliers to avoid ∂ξ*/∂θ dependence; assumptions are reasonable for common coherent risks.  
- Dynamic gradient theorem is noteworthy, generalizing policy gradient to Markov coherent risk via expectations under ξ*-reweighted kernels.  
- Important technical details are deferred: regularity for envelope theorem, uniqueness/measurability of saddle points, and treatment of non-differentiability (e.g., CVaR at atoms).  
- Actor-critic convergence and approximation error analyses are not provided.  
- Static estimator requires solving convex programs and extracting KKT multipliers per batch; dynamic method requires per-state saddle-point solves and ξ*-weighted sampling—computationally heavy at scale.  
- Paper is well written and grounded but notation is heavy; should explicitly state when gradients are subgradients.  
- Dynamic section needs pseudo-code, sampling guidance for Pθ(·|x)ξ*(·) with standard simulators, and per-update computational cost.  
- The unifying static gradient and dynamic policy-gradient theorem are original and generalize prior risk-specific work.  
- Linking the critic to robust MDPs is appropriate but follows known results.  
- Theoretical significance is strong; empirical significance is limited due to minimal experiments and no dynamic MDP validation.  
- Strengths: principled, general treatment; clear derivations; SAA consistency; new Markov coherent risk policy-gradient theorem.  
- Weaknesses: limited experiments, heavy computational requirements, unaddressed non-differentiability and saddle-point measurability, and restrictive differentiability assumptions for constraint maps.  
- Suggestions: clarify subgradient status in non-smooth cases, provide actor-critic pseudo-code, discuss sampling via importance sampling/resampling, and add at least one dynamic MDP experiment with baselines and variance analysis.  
- Ask for complexity analysis of per-state convex solves and whether closed forms/efficient updates exist for common risks (e.g., CVaR/spectral).  
- Request discussion on critic stability with function approximation under ξ*-weighted dynamics and actor bias from approximation errors.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with limited empirical validation and practical guidance; recommendation: weak accept.
