Summary and contributions
The paper develops policy gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, both in static (single random variable) and dynamic (time-consistent Markov) settings. The key technical idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) as worst-case expectations over a convex risk envelope. Using an envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick, the authors derive:

- A general gradient formula for static coherent risk ρ(Z) that depends on the saddle point (ξ*, λ*) of a convex optimization over the risk envelope. This unifies and extends prior gradient results for specific risks (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance) and leads to sampling-based estimators.
- A sample-average-approximation (SAA) approach: given i.i.d. samples, solve a finite convex program to obtain ξ* and KKT multipliers, plug them into the gradient formula, and prove consistency under mild assumptions.
- A policy gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński): the gradient is an expectation over trajectories of an adversarially reweighted transition kernel, with a stage-wise cost term hθ that incorporates the value function and constraint multipliers. This yields an actor-critic scheme: a critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function (via robust MDP approximation), and the actor uses the new gradient formula.

The paper relates to risk-sensitive RL for specific risk criteria (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR) and to dynamic coherent risk/robust MDPs. The contribution is to provide a single framework and gradient machinery that covers all coherent risks defined via a convex risk envelope.

Strengths
- Generality and unification: The static gradient formula and the dynamic policy gradient theorem apply to the entire class of coherent risks, subsuming many prior special cases. The CVaR gradient is rederived more simply and without restrictive smoothness assumptions.
- Sound variational foundation: Using the convex dual (risk envelope) and an envelope theorem is conceptually clean and technically appropriate. The SAA estimator leveraging KKT multipliers is natural and the consistency result is valuable.
- Dynamic setting: Extending the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk is novel and important for time-consistent risk-aware RL. The link to robust MDPs is well explained, and using a robust value function approximation for the critic is reasonable.
- Clarity of main ideas: The paper lays out the risk-envelope assumptions, gradient derivations, and the structure of the actor-critic method. The simple portfolio example is illustrative of the flexibility of coherent risks beyond variance-based criteria.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Practicality/assumptions for dynamic gradient: The actor requires sampling under a reweighted kernel Pθ(·|x) ξ*, and hθ includes derivatives of envelope constraints w.r.t. the transition distribution. This seems to require either access to a generative model with controllable sampling or importance sampling schemes that are not provided, and partial knowledge of P to compute dfi/dp, dge/dp. The required multiple next-state samples per state further limits applicability to online, single-trajectory RL.
- Computational burden: The method repeatedly solves convex programs (to obtain ξ* and KKT multipliers) in the static estimator and per-state in the dynamic actor. For large or continuous spaces, scalability is a concern; details on how often these are solved and how approximations affect bias/variance are deferred to the supplement.
- Experimental evidence: The only experiment is a toy portfolio selection. There is no dynamic MDP experiment, no comparison to risk-averse baselines (e.g., CVaR-RL in MDPs), and no analysis of sample complexity or runtime. This significantly weakens the empirical case for NeurIPS.
- Technical conditions: Finite spaces are assumed; the extension to Lp is stated but not developed. In the dynamic case, issues of non-uniqueness of saddle points ξ* across states, stability/measurability of the selection as θ changes, and the effect on stochastic gradients are not discussed.
- Variance and baselines: The gradient forms suggest high variance (especially for tail-focused risks). Variance-reduction techniques (control variates/baselines, importance sampling) are mentioned as future work but not integrated.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The theory is sound at a high level, with careful use of convex duality and envelope theorems. However, many proofs are deferred; and some key practicality issues (sampling under reweighted kernels, computing derivatives of constraints in model-free settings) are under-specified. The empirical section is minimal.
- Clarity: Generally well written, with clear notation and helpful examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation). Some notational density around dynamic constraints and derivatives could be streamlined, and the actor-critic algorithm is only sketched.
- Originality: High. A general policy gradient for coherent risks and a dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk are novel contributions beyond prior work on specific risks.
- Significance: Potentially high for researchers building risk-sensitive RL methods. Immediate practical impact is tempered by computational and sampling requirements and minimal experiments.

Questions and suggestions
- Dynamic sampling: How do you implement sampling from Pθ(·|x) ξ* in practice if you only have a simulator for Pθ? Can you provide an importance-sampling scheme with variance analysis?
- Constraint derivatives: For common envelopes (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation), please give explicit forms of dfi/dp, dge/dp in the dynamic setting and discuss whether they can be estimated from samples without knowing P.
- Scalability: Can ξ* and multipliers be obtained by online primal-dual updates (e.g., mirror descent) to avoid repeatedly solving convex programs? Any results on the complexity and convergence of such an approach?
- Experiments: Add dynamic MDP benchmarks (e.g., gridworlds with hazards, queueing) comparing to CVaR-RL and robust MDP baselines, with ablations on gradient estimators (analytic vs SAA), effect of α/tail weight, and runtime.
- Non-uniqueness and regularity: Discuss selection rules for ξ* when saddle points are non-unique (e.g., CVaR at quantiles), and continuity of ξ*(θ) needed for stable SGD.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether the normalization constraint ∑x′ ξ(x′) Pθ(x′|x) = 1 implies Pθ(·|x) ξ*(·) is a proper kernel (it does), and mention this explicitly.
- State the finite-state assumption for the dynamic section up front; be precise about extensions to continuous spaces (function approximation, sampling).
- Some references are incomplete (years/pages missing); please fix.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is novel and unifying, and the dynamic policy gradient theorem is of clear interest. However, the paper would benefit greatly from stronger empirical validation and more practical guidance on sampling and computation in the dynamic setting.

Reviewer confidence: High.
