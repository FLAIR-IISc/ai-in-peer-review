- Overall assessment: Strong, theoretically sound contribution providing a general policy-gradient framework for coherent risk measures; merits acceptance.

- Main idea: Develops a unified framework to derive policy-gradient algorithms for static and dynamic coherent risk using the worst-case expectation representation.

- Main idea: Presents novel gradient formulas for static coherent risk and a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, leading to an actor-critic algorithm.

- Main idea: Provides a sampling-based gradient estimation approach and connects the framework to robust MDPs and existing approximation techniques.

- Strength: Generality — handles the entire class of coherent risk measures, unifying and extending prior specific-risk results.

- Strength: Theoretical rigor — derivations and consistency results are mathematically grounded and appear sound.

- Strength: Novelty — static-case gradient formulas and use of the envelope theorem are original contributions.

- Strength: Practicality — algorithms are presented with attention to implementability and sampling-based scalability.

- Strength: Clear motivation — effectively motivates risk-sensitive RL and the advantages of coherent measures.

- Strength: Literature connections — situates and generalizes previous work carefully.

- Weakness: Clarity and organization — presentation is dense, notation heavy, and some assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2) need more intuitive explanation.

- Weakness: Limited empirical evaluation — numerical illustration is narrow; broader benchmarks and comparisons are needed.

- Weakness: Computational cost — convex programming in gradient estimation may be expensive for large-scale problems; mitigation strategies are under-discussed.

- Weakness: Assumptions — reliance on Assumption 2.2 might be restrictive; limitations and extensions should be acknowledged.

- NIPS evaluation scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 7/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 9/10.

- Arguments for acceptance: Highly general framework, novel gradient formulas, unifies existing work, addresses an important RL problem.

- Arguments against acceptance: Limited experiments, potential computational cost, and presentation clarity issues.

- Recommendation: Strong Accept, with suggested improvements to clarity and expanded empirical evaluation in future versions.
