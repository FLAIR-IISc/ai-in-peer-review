

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper proposes policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as the objective. The authors present a unified framework that generalizes previous approaches focused on specific risk measures (such as CVaR, mean-variance, etc.) by leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures. The main contributions include a gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm for gradient estimation using sampling and convex programming, and a new policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk.

The paper builds upon established results in risk theory (Artzner et al. [2]) and risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical approach is grounded in convex optimization and the envelope theorem, which are well-established techniques. The authors correctly cite related work on risk-sensitive RL, including recent NIPS papers on CVaR optimization [8, 30] and risk-averse MDPs [20, 9].

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretically sound framework that unifies multiple risk-sensitive RL approaches under the coherent risk umbrella.
- The mathematical treatment is rigorous, with well-structured proofs in the supplementary material.
- The connection to the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) is well-explained and properly contextualized.
- The approach has potential for extending risk-sensitive RL to a broader class of risk measures than previous work.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The paper's most significant weakness is the absence of extensive empirical evaluation. The numerical illustration (Section 6) is extremely limited, involving only a simple trading agent with three assets and three policies. There is no comparison with established risk-sensitive RL baselines or standard RL methods. The authors claim their approach "generalizes and unifies previous approaches," but provide no evidence of performance improvements or practical advantages over existing methods.

2. **Unjustified Claims of Novelty:** The paper claims to "generalize and unify previous approaches" but doesn't adequately demonstrate how their method differs from or improves upon existing risk-sensitive RL work. For instance, the paper cites [25, 26] for optimization of coherent risk measures, but doesn't explain why their approach is better suited for RL than the stochastic programming methods they reference. The connection to [19] on robust MDPs is acknowledged but not leveraged to demonstrate practical advantages.

3. **Computational Complexity Concerns:** The proposed algorithm involves a two-step procedure (sampling followed by convex programming) that may be computationally prohibitive for large-scale RL problems. The paper doesn't provide any analysis of computational complexity or scalability, which is critical for RL applications. The claim that their approach "scales-up to large or continuous MDPs" is unsubstantiated.

4. **Lack of Comparison with Alternative Approaches:** The paper doesn't compare with existing methods for risk-sensitive RL that use different techniques (e.g., robust optimization, distributional RL). The authors cite [31] but don't demonstrate how their approach outperforms the function approximation method they reference.

5. **Overstated Significance:** The paper claims that "our approach extends risk-sensitive RL to the whole class of coherent risk measures," but the practical significance of this extension is not convincingly demonstrated. The numerical example shows that different risk measures lead to different policy choices, but doesn't show that the proposed method is necessary or superior to existing techniques for this purpose.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the theoretical framework is sound and the paper makes a technically correct contribution to the literature on coherent risk measures, the paper fails to meet NIPS's standards for empirical validation and practical significance. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the lack of comprehensive experimental validation particularly concerning. The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension but does not convincingly demonstrate that it provides meaningful advantages over existing methods in practice.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Extensive empirical comparisons with established risk-sensitive RL methods
2. A detailed analysis of computational complexity
3. A clearer articulation of how the proposed method differs from and improves upon existing approaches
4. More realistic experimental scenarios that demonstrate scalability

Without these improvements, the paper represents a marginal extension of existing work rather than a significant advancement in the field. The theoretical contribution is interesting but lacks the empirical grounding that would be expected in a NIPS paper, particularly for a topic as applied as risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims of generalization and practical utility. While the theoretical framework is sound, the absence of comprehensive experimental results means the work does not make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field.
