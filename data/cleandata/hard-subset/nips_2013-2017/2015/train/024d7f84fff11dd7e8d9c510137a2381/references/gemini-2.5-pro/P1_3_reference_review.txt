**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a general framework for deriving policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The authors tackle two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of a cumulative random return, and optimizing a dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measure. The core technical contribution is leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the risk envelope).

For the static case, the authors apply the Envelope Theorem to this dual formulation to derive a novel, general formula for the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2). This formula elegantly expresses the gradient in terms of the optimal worst-case density and the Lagrange multipliers of the dual problem. They demonstrate its utility by recovering and simplifying the gradient formula for Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) and deriving a new one for the mean-semideviation risk. For general coherent risk measures where the optimal density is not analytically available, they propose a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming via Sample Average Approximation (SAA) and prove its consistency.

For the dynamic case, they derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This result extends the standard policy gradient theorem to the risk-sensitive setting, showing that the gradient can be expressed as an expectation under a distorted state-transition distribution defined by the worst-case density. Based on this theorem, they outline an actor-critic algorithm for finding a locally optimal policy. The paper is well-situated in the literature, unifying and generalizing previous works that developed algorithms for specific risk measures like mean-variance or CVaR.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. Instead of focusing on a single risk measure, it provides a principled framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant contribution, as it unifies many previous, disparate results and provides a recipe for deriving policy gradients for any new coherent risk measure that can be described via a risk envelope.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Elegance:** The technical approach is sound and elegant. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk combined with the Envelope Theorem is a powerful technique that leads to a clean and insightful gradient formula. The proofs provided for the examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) demonstrate the power of this framework by re-deriving existing results more simply and deriving new ones.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds its case logically, starting with preliminaries, moving to the simpler static risk case, and then using those results as a foundation for the more complex dynamic risk case. The examples in Section 4 are very helpful for building intuition.

4.  **Practical Relevance and Significance:** The work is highly significant for the field of risk-sensitive RL. It provides researchers and practitioners with a concrete set of tools to optimize a wide variety of risk preferences beyond simple expectation or variance. The numerical illustration in Section 6, while simple, is effective at demonstrating why flexibility in choosing a risk measure is important, particularly when dealing with heavy-tailed distributions common in finance and other domains.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Cost of the General Algorithm:** The proposed general gradient estimation algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) at each gradient step, with the number of variables and constraints scaling with the number of samples `N`. While convex, this could be computationally demanding for large `N`, potentially making it slower than methods with analytical solutions. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability issues.

2.  **Details of the Actor-Critic Algorithm:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm in Section 5 is quite high-level, with many crucial details (e.g., the function approximation scheme for the critic, the full two-phase sampling procedure for the actor) deferred to the supplementary material. While this is understandable given space constraints, it makes the second major contribution of the paper (the dynamic risk algorithm) harder to fully evaluate from the main text alone. Adding a bit more detail on the critic's update or the estimation of the `h_θ` function would strengthen this section.

3.  **Variance of Gradient Estimates:** The paper mentions consistency of the gradient estimator but does not delve into the variance properties. Estimating risk measures, especially those sensitive to tail events like CVaR, can suffer from high variance. While a full analysis is likely beyond the scope, a brief discussion on how the properties of the risk envelope `U` might influence the sample complexity and variance of the gradient estimates would be a valuable addition. The authors rightly point this out as an area for future work.

**Qualitative Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically solid, with well-supported theoretical claims. The connection between the dual representation and policy gradients is a complete and valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is a model of clarity. The motivation, methods, and results are presented in a clear, logical progression.
*   **Originality:** High. The paper provides a novel, unifying framework for policy gradients with coherent risk. While it builds on established concepts (coherent risk, policy gradients, envelope theorem), their combination is new and powerful, clearly distinguishing this work from prior art that focused on specific risk measures.
*   **Significance:** High. This work significantly advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL. It provides a foundational tool that other researchers are likely to build upon. The ability to easily incorporate a wide class of axiomatically-justified risk measures into policy optimization is a major step forward.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and elegant unifying framework for policy gradients under coherent risk.
*   Generalizes and simplifies several previous results in risk-sensitive RL.
*   The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   The results are significant and will likely be influential in the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation details and computational analysis of the proposed algorithms, particularly for the dynamic case, are somewhat sparse in the main paper.
*   The work does not provide an in-depth analysis of the sample complexity or variance of the proposed gradient estimators.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong theoretical and practical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The strengths—its generality, technical elegance, and clarity—far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to computational discussion and algorithmic detail. It successfully unifies a scattered landscape of results for specific risk measures into a single, coherent framework. I strongly recommend acceptance.
