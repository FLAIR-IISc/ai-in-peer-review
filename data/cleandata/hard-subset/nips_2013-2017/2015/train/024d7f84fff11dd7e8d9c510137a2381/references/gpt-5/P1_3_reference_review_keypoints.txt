- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures, covering static coherent risk and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk via the dual risk-envelope representation.

- Static gradient derived using the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick (Theorem 4.2), expressing ∇θρ(Z) via expectations under adversarial density ξ* and Lagrange multipliers.

- Closed-form gradients provided for CVaR and mean-semideviation; when analytic ξ* unavailable, a two-step SAA estimator (solve convex program + sampling) is proposed and shown consistent (Proposition 4.4).

- Dynamic policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) extends classic PG to Markov coherent risk using nested risk/Bellman form, yielding REINFORCE-like expectations with modified stage cost hθ and ξ*-reweighted transitions.

- Sketches an actor-critic algorithm exploiting the robust MDP equivalence for the critic and a two-phase sampling scheme for the actor; toy trading example shows different risk choices induce different policies.

- Unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL work, recovering CVaR and mean-variance results and connecting to robust MDP formulations.

- Strength: principled, unified framework for the whole class of coherent risks using the dual perspective.

- Strength: solid theoretical derivations for static gradients and consistency; dynamic policy-gradient theorem is novel.

- Strength: explicit connection between coherent risk, worst-case reweighting, and robust MDPs, subsuming prior special cases.

- Strength: practical SAA-based recipe for static risk optimization using standard convex optimization.

- Weakness: empirical evaluation is very limited; no experiments validating the dynamic actor-critic or comparisons to baselines.

- Weakness: practical implementation of ξ*-weighted sampling in model-free settings is unclear; importance-sampling variance for tail risks not analyzed.

- Weakness: computational overhead concerns—solving convex programs per state/update may not scale, and frequency of recomputing ξ* as θ changes is unspecified.

- Weakness: strong finite probability-space assumption; extensions to Lp spaces are claimed but omitted and stability/measurability of ξ* across states/θ not fully addressed.

- Weakness: actor-critic convergence guarantees and approximation bias are deferred to the supplement; main text lacks summary of assumptions and main theorem.

- Weakness: variance and sample-complexity issues for rare-event risks; no variance-reduction techniques (baselines, control variates) provided.

- Weakness: related work should cite Iyengar (2005), Nilim & El Ghaoui (2005) and entropic-risk PG literature to clarify connections.

- Quality: technically sound and well-grounded; static results careful; dynamic theorem compelling but lacking empirical demonstration.

- Clarity: generally clear but notation dense; needs algorithmic pseudocode for SAA and actor-critic and clearer statement of simulator and derivative access requirements.

- Originality: high — general coherent-risk PG formula and dynamic Markov coherent risk PG theorem appear novel.

- Significance: potentially high if strengthened by empirical validation and discussion of computational/variance tradeoffs.

- Suggestions: add dynamic MDP experiments and baselines; provide pseudocode and complexity analysis; explain/quantify importance-sampling implementation and variance; summarize convergence theorem in main text; expand related work.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel, general theoretical contribution; clean derivations and consistency guarantees; bridges coherent risk and robust MDPs.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation; unresolved practical feasibility and scalability questions; assumptions may limit applicability.

- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on stronger experiments and clarification of practical aspects; otherwise borderline.
