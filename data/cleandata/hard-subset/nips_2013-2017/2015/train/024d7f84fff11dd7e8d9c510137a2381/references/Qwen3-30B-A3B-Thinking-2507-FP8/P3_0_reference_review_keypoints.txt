- Paper develops policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, providing a unified framework beyond specific risk metrics.  
- Key theoretical contributions: a new sampling-friendly gradient formula for static coherent risk measures and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures.  
- Approach leverages coherent risk representation as worst-case expectations (Theorem 2.1) to derive practical algorithms.  
- Proposed practical algorithm: two-step sampling followed by convex programming for gradient estimation.  
- Numerical example (financial trading) shows different risk measures yield different policy choices.  

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation using coherent risk theory and Theorem 2.1.  
- Unified framework generalizes prior results (CVaR, mean-semideviation) as special cases.  
- Practical algorithm design is implementable and well-motivated.  
- Clear problem formulation distinguishing static vs dynamic risk and reasonable assumptions.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and suggest directions for future work (e.g., model uncertainty).  

Weaknesses / Concerns:
- Computational complexity: convex optimization per gradient estimate may be prohibitive for large-scale problems and lacks complexity analysis.  
- Heavy reliance on Assumption 2.2 requiring explicit risk envelope form, which may not hold or be obtainable in practice.  
- Limited empirical comparison: main paper lacks head-to-head evaluation against existing estimators (e.g., CVaR analytical estimators).  
- Dynamic risk section feels rushed with insufficient motivation and implementation/scalability details for the actor-critic algorithm.  
- No theoretical convergence guarantees for the overall policy gradient algorithm beyond consistency for the static estimator.  

Comparison to related work:
- Correctly situates and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL literature, but could more explicitly compare to works on dynamic coherent risk for linear systems and stochastic programming.  

Technical assessment:
- Theoretical results are sound; proofs use envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick applied novelly to risk-sensitive settings.  
- Assumptions (bounded likelihood ratios, constraint smoothness) are reasonable for typical RL applications.  

Significance:
- Addresses important applications (finance, robotics, operations research) by allowing a wide class of risk measures; connects to model uncertainty and robust decision-making.  

Recommendation:
- Accept — significant theoretical contribution, technically sound, novel, and practically relevant despite computational limitations.  

Specific suggestions:
- Provide a detailed discussion of convex program computational cost and possible large-scale approximations.  
- Elaborate implementation and scaling details for the actor-critic algorithm in Section 5.  
- Move empirical comparisons with existing estimators (e.g., CVaR) into the main paper.  
- Give more concrete examples of risk measures that satisfy Assumption 2.2 beyond CVaR and mean-semideviation.  
- Add guidance on choosing appropriate risk measures for specific problems to complement the numerical example.  

Final assessment:
- Strong, well-written theoretical contribution that meaningfully advances risk-sensitive RL and merits acceptance.
