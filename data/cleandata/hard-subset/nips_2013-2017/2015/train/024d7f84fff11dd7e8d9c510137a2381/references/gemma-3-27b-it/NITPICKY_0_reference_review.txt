This paper presents policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, generalizing previous approaches. The core contribution lies in formulating risk-sensitive policy gradients within a coherent risk framework, providing a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm with a consistency result, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk. While the ideas are interesting and potentially impactful, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet significant, issues in presentation, technical detail, and justification.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The framework aims for broad applicability across coherent risk measures, a commendable goal.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper attempts to provide a rigorous mathematical treatment, grounding the algorithms in established concepts from coherent risk theory.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on risk-sensitive RL and robust MDPs.
*   **Potential Impact:** A general, gradient-based approach to coherent risk optimization in RL *could* be valuable if implemented and demonstrated effectively.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The writing is dense and often lacks sufficient motivation for intermediate steps. Many equations are presented without adequate explanation, hindering understanding. The transition between static and dynamic risk is abrupt.
*   **Technical Rigor & Justification:** While aiming for rigor, several assumptions are stated without sufficient justification. Assumption 2.2, regarding the structure of the risk envelope, feels particularly ad hoc. The proof sketches in the supplementary material lack detail and leave many steps unjustified. The convergence analysis for the actor-critic algorithm is only mentioned and not fully elaborated on in the main body of the paper.
*   **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies are present throughout the paper. For example, the MDP definition uses both *C(x)* and *Cmax* without clear differentiation, and the notation *Eξ[Z]* is cumbersome and not standard. There's a typo in the definition of the semi-deviation (should be inside the square root).
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is weak. It lacks a detailed comparison to baseline methods or sensitivity analysis to different risk preferences. The conclusion about asset selection feels overstated based on the limited results.
*   **Overclaiming & Lack of Nuance:** The paper frequently uses strong statements like “generalizing and unifying previous approaches” without fully substantiating these claims. The discussion of limitations is superficial.
*   **Formatting:** Minor formatting inconsistencies exist, particularly in equation numbering and citation style.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Introduction:** The motivation for focusing on coherent risk measures is adequate, but the connection to time consistency is underdeveloped. The listed contributions are vague and lack specificity.
*   **Preliminaries:** Restricting attention to finite probability spaces is a significant limitation, and should be explicitly addressed in the discussion. The definition of *Eξ[Z]* is non-standard and obscures the underlying concepts.
*   **Coherent Risk Measures:** Theorem 2.1 is stated without discussion of its assumptions or limitations. The link between the risk envelope and its practical computation is not fully explained.
*   **Gradient Formula for Static Risk:** Theorem 4.2 is a key result, but its derivation is relegated to the supplementary material. The SAA algorithm is presented without a clear discussion of its convergence properties. Example 1 (CVaR) re-derives a known result without significant new insights.
*   **Gradient Formula for Dynamic Risk:** The derivation relies heavily on the static gradient formula, but the connection between the two is not fully elucidated. The actor-critic algorithm is described only at a high level, leaving many implementation details unclear.
*   **Numerical Illustration:** The experiment is too simplistic to draw meaningful conclusions. The choice of assets seems arbitrary, and the results are not rigorously analyzed.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in risk-sensitive RL.
*   The framework has the potential to generalize existing approaches and provide a unified treatment of coherent risk.
*   The theoretical results, if correct, could be valuable for developing new algorithms.

**Con:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous technical weaknesses and inconsistencies.
*   The presentation is unclear and hinders understanding.
*   The empirical evaluation is inadequate.
*   The authors overstate the contributions of their work.
*   The level of polish and attention to detail is below the standard for NIPS.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential significance of the research topic, the numerous flaws in this paper outweigh its strengths. While the authors demonstrate some understanding of the relevant literature and propose a potentially interesting framework, the lack of technical rigor, clarity, and empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the true value of their contributions. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject.**

I believe that substantial revisions, addressing the points raised above, are necessary before this paper would be suitable for publication at NIPS. Specifically, the authors need to provide more detailed proofs, clarify their assumptions, improve the presentation, and conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation. The authors should also tone down the overclaiming and acknowledge the limitations of their work more honestly.
