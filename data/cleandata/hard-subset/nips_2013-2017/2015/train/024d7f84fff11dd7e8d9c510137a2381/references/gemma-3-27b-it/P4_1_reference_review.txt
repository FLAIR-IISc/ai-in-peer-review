This paper addresses the problem of policy gradient optimization for coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning, offering a generalization of existing approaches that typically focus on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The core contribution lies in formulating risk-sensitive policy gradients under a coherent risk framework, providing a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm involving convex programming for its approximation, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk along with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The paper is well-motivated, clearly connecting to existing work in risk-sensitive RL and coherent risk theory.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The work provides a unifying framework for handling a broad class of coherent risk measures, which is a significant step forward compared to existing methods tailored to specific risk functions.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas appears rigorous, building upon established results from coherent risk theory and optimization (e.g., duality, envelope theorem). The theorems (4.2 and 5.2) are clearly stated and provide a foundation for the proposed algorithms.
*   **Practical Algorithms:** The paper doesn’t just present theory; it outlines algorithms for computing the gradients, including a sampling-based approach suitable for large-scale problems and an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk.
*   **Connection to Robust MDPs:** Recognizing the connection between dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs provides a valuable perspective and potentially opens avenues for leveraging existing techniques from robust control.
*   **Illustrative Example:** The numerical illustration, though simple, effectively demonstrates the impact of different risk measures on policy selection and highlights the trade-offs between risk aversion and potential reward.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The theoretical framework, particularly the use of convex programming and the reliance on the risk envelope representation (Theorem 2.1), may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with coherent risk theory to fully grasp. While the paper provides some background, a more detailed introduction to these concepts would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** The proposed algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) involves solving a convex optimization problem at each iteration, which can be computationally expensive for high-dimensional problems. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability issues is warranted.
*   **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2, 4.1, 5.1), some of which may be restrictive in practice.  The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated.  For example, Assumption 2.2 requires knowledge of the risk envelope, which might not always be readily available.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is quite basic. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks, comparing the performance of the proposed algorithms with existing risk-sensitive RL methods, would significantly strengthen the paper. The choice of the trading agent example, while illustrative, feels somewhat disconnected from standard RL benchmarks.
*   **Dynamic Risk Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk (Section 5) is somewhat high-level. More details on the function approximation scheme for the value function and the sampling procedure would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient optimization, generalizing existing methods.
*   The proposed algorithms are well-defined and offer potential benefits for problems where a flexible and principled approach to risk management is needed.
*   The connection to robust MDPs and the discussion of computational aspects demonstrate a careful consideration of the practical implications of the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The complexity of the framework and the computational cost of the algorithms may limit their applicability in practice.
*   The limited empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The reliance on strong assumptions and the lack of sensitivity analysis could undermine the robustness of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While the theoretical framework is complex and the computational cost may be a concern, the generality and theoretical soundness of the approach are compelling.  The limited empirical evaluation is a major weakness, and addressing this through more comprehensive experiments would substantially strengthen the paper. 

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should consider: 1) providing a more detailed introduction to coherent risk theory; 2) discussing the computational complexity of the algorithms more thoroughly; 3) expanding the empirical evaluation with experiments on standard RL benchmarks; and 4) addressing the sensitivity of the results to the key assumptions.
