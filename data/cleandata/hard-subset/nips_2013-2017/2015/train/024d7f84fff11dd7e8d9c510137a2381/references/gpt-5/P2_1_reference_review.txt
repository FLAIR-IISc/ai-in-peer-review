Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general framework of coherent risk measures. The key idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk (risk envelopes) and the envelope theorem to derive gradient formulas that are amenable to sampling.

Static case: The authors derive a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) for coherent risk of a random variable Z. It recovers known formulas for special cases (e.g., CVaR) and yields a new expression for mean-semideviation. When the Lagrangian saddle point is not analytically available, they propose a sample average approximation (SAA) that solves a convex program over the empirical distribution and uses the KKT multipliers to form a consistent gradient estimator.

Dynamic case: For Markov coherent (time-consistent) risk in MDPs, they prove a policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) expressing the gradient in terms of an expectation over trajectories under a ξ-weighted transition kernel, with a stage-wise cost hθ that depends on the value function and derivatives of the risk-envelope constraints. They outline an actor-critic algorithm: a critic computes Vθ (via robust-MDP equivalence and function approximation), and an actor estimates the gradient using the theorem.

Relation to prior work
The work unifies disparate strands: exponential utility, mean-variance, and CVaR policy-gradient methods become special cases. It builds on robust MDP connections for dynamic coherent risk (Ruszczyński; Osogami), CVaR optimization (Rockafellar-Uryasev; Tamar et al.), and robust MDP function approximation (Petrik-Subramanian; Tamar et al.). The static CVaR gradient matches and simplifies earlier derivations. The dynamic PG theorem for general Markov coherent risk appears novel.

Quality
– Theory: The derivations are technically sound under clearly stated assumptions. The static Theorem 4.2 follows appropriately from the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick; the SAA estimator with convex programming and KKT multipliers has a reasonable consistency argument. The dynamic theorem leverages the robust MDP Bellman equation and gives a clear PG-style expression.

– Assumptions: The finite probability space, bounded likelihood ratios, explicit risk envelope with smooth dependence on the base distribution, and existence of saddle points are standard in convex risk and PG analyses, though not trivial. The dynamic result requires access to ξ-weighted transitions and derivatives dfi/dp, dge/dp, which may be strong in practice but are transparent.

– Empirics: The experimental evaluation is very limited (a toy asset-selection example). There is no demonstration on canonical RL benchmarks or comparison to specialized methods (e.g., CVaR policy gradient) beyond informal remarks. Practical feasibility (solver calls per batch/state, sampling under ξ-weighted kernel, variance) is not empirically assessed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with good context on coherence, time consistency, and risk envelopes. The notation is heavy but standard. Several important algorithmic details (full actor-critic, step-sizes, sampling procedures for ξ-weighted transitions, function approximation architecture) and most proofs are deferred to the supplement. It would help to more explicitly explain how to compute ∇θ ge and ∇θ fi in common envelopes, and to discuss the computational overhead.

Originality
The main contributions—general static coherent risk gradient via risk-envelope duality and a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk—are original and nontrivial. Subsuming known results (CVaR, mean-semideviation) under a single framework is valuable.

Significance
Conceptually significant: provides a unifying PG framework for coherent risk, likely to influence future work on risk-sensitive RL and robust control. Practically promising: supports a range of risk preferences beyond CVaR/variance. However, the current empirical evidence is insufficient to demonstrate effectiveness, sample efficiency, and scalability; the requirement to sample under ξ-weighted transitions is a practical barrier in many RL settings.

Strengths
- Clear, general theoretical framework that unifies and extends existing risk-sensitive PG methods.
- Static gradient formula applicable to any coherent risk with an explicit envelope; consistent SAA estimator.
- Dynamic policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk is novel.
- Connections to robust MDPs are well used.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is minimal and does not validate the algorithms on realistic MDPs or against strong baselines.
- Practical requirements are strong: solving convex programs (static) and per-state saddle-point problems (dynamic), sampling under ξ-weighted transitions, and multiple next-state samples per state.
- Some assumptions may be restrictive in common settings (finite Ω; smooth dependence on P; uniqueness/selection of saddle points; differentiability of spectral envelopes with order statistics).
- Limited discussion of variance, sample complexity, and computational cost.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the dynamic gradient be estimated off-policy without access to ξ-weighted transitions, e.g., via importance sampling or density-ratio estimation?
- Provide experiments on standard RL tasks (gridworlds, queueing/inventory control, continuous control) comparing to CVaR PG and mean-variance baselines, including ablations on convex solver calls and variance.
- Discuss extensions beyond finite Ω to continuous state/action spaces and provide concrete envelopes (e.g., spectral risk) with their ∂/∂p terms.
- Analyze the bias/variance and complexity of the SAA + KKT estimator versus closed-form cases, and robustness when the envelope constraints are non-smooth.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are solid and broadly applicable, but the paper lacks empirical validation demonstrating practicality and scalability, and relies on strong sampling/solver assumptions. With more substantial experiments and practical guidance on implementing the dynamic algorithm without ξ-weighted simulators, this would be a strong accept.

Confidence
High on assessment of theory and related work; medium on practicality due to missing experiments.
