- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for coherent risk objectives, covering static coherent risk and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk in MDPs.
- Key technical contributions: gradient formula for static coherent risk via convex dual and envelope theorem, yielding a likelihood-ratio estimator using Lagrangian saddle points.
- Introduces an SAA approach that computes the saddle point via a convex program and uses KKT multipliers to produce a consistent gradient estimator.
- Provides a dynamic policy-gradient theorem expressing ∇θVθ with a modified worst-case reweighted trajectory distribution, enabling an actor–critic scheme.
- Framework unifies and recovers existing special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and subsumes prior gradient derivations under weaker conditions.
- Builds on coherent risk and dynamic Markov coherent risk literature (Artzner et al., Ruszczyński & Shapiro, Ruszczyński 2010) and connects to robust MDP results.
- Strength: broad generality and unification across coherent risk measures with mild risk-envelope assumptions.
- Strength: sound theoretical development using envelope theorem and convex analysis, with SAA consistency guarantees.
- Strength: provides a concrete, implementable algorithmic path (sampling + convex optimization + multipliers) and a motivated dynamic actor–critic.
- Strength: clear separation of static and dynamic cases and illustrative examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation, portfolio example).
- Weakness: SAA estimator requires solving convex programs and retrieving KKT multipliers per iteration/state, raising scalability and runtime concerns.
- Weakness: dynamic theorem requires sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions, effectively needing a model or a generative simulator—stronger than model-free, single-trajectory RL.
- Weakness: assumptions (finite Ω, smooth constraint dependence) and extensions to continuous spaces/function approximation lack detailed proofs; saddle-point non-uniqueness and stability not fully addressed.
- Weakness: limited empirical validation—only a small static portfolio example and no demonstration of the dynamic actor–critic or benchmarking on standard RL tasks.
- Weakness: some algorithmic details (critic with function approximation, two-phase sampling, importance sampling variants) are deferred to the supplement; pseudocode and ablations are missing.
- Quality assessment: theoretically rigorous and well-scoped, but empirical evidence insufficient to judge practical performance and stability.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized though notation is heavy; reviewer requests high-level pseudocode, complexity analysis, and clarification on sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions.
- Originality: high—novel general policy-gradient expressions for coherent risk and dynamic Markov coherent risk extension.
- Significance: moderate-to-high contingent on development of scalable implementations and broader empirical validation.
- Reviewer questions for authors: how to sample from ξ*-weighted transitions without a generative model; computational costs per iteration/state for convex programs and KKT multipliers; sensitivity of SAA to sample size and solver tolerances and variance-reduction strategies; request experiments for dynamic actor–critic vs. CVaR methods.
- Pros summary: general elegant theory, recovers known results, provides consistent SAA estimator, extends policy-gradient to time-consistent dynamic risk.
- Cons summary: strong model-access assumptions, heavy computational overhead, sparse empirical validation, deferred assumptions and algorithmic details.
- Recommendation: weak accept, pending clarification of computational aspects and ideally additional experiments for the dynamic algorithm.
