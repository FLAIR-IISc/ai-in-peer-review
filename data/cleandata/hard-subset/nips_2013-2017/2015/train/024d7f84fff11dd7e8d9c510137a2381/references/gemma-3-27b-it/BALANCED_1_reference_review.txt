This paper tackles the important problem of policy gradient optimization under coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work focused on specific risk functions. The authors provide a novel formulation, deriving gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, and proposing an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic settings. The core contribution lies in unifying existing approaches under a coherent risk framework, providing a more general and theoretically grounded methodology.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The work successfully extends risk-sensitive RL to the entire class of coherent risk measures, a significant step beyond existing literature. This allows for flexible risk preference modeling.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas, based on the envelope theorem and convex optimization duality, appears rigorous and well-motivated. The theoretical groundwork is strong.
* **Novelty:**  The policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is a valuable contribution, enabling the development of an actor-critic algorithm for this setting.
* **Connections to Robust MDPs:**  Highlighting the connection to robust MDPs provides further insight and potential for leveraging existing techniques.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear motivation and problem statement. The mathematical notation is standard and manageable.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical illustration effectively demonstrates the practical implications of different risk measures and the resulting policy choices.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The reliance on Assumption 2.2 (the general form of the risk envelope) is somewhat strong. While justified, the practical applicability might be limited if obtaining this structure is difficult for certain coherent risk measures. A discussion on the challenges of verifying this assumption would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** The proposed algorithms, involving both sampling and convex programming, could be computationally expensive, particularly for high-dimensional state spaces. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational complexity.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the numerical example is helpful, the evaluation is limited to a single problem.  More extensive empirical results, comparing the performance against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on benchmark tasks, would strengthen the claims.
* **Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm is somewhat high-level. Providing more details regarding the function approximation scheme, learning rates, and exploration strategy would enhance reproducibility.
* **Convergence Analysis:**  While Proposition 4.4 provides some convergence guarantees, the convergence analysis for the actor-critic algorithm is deferred to the supplementary material. A more concise summary of the convergence results within the main paper would be useful.
* **Missing Related Work:**  The paper could benefit from discussing recent advances in distributional reinforcement learning, which also address uncertainty and risk in a different but related manner.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution by unifying risk-sensitive RL under a coherent risk framework.
* The derived gradient formulas are novel and provide a foundation for developing efficient algorithms.
* The work addresses an important problem with potential applications in various domains.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms is a concern.
* The empirical evaluation is limited.
* The strong assumptions regarding the risk envelope might limit practical applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a strong contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical framework is solid, the results are novel, and the writing is clear. However, the computational concerns and limited empirical validation are significant weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Discuss the practical challenges of verifying Assumption 2.2** and explore potential alternatives if it cannot be easily satisfied.
2. **Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity** of the proposed algorithms.
3. **Conduct more extensive empirical evaluations** on benchmark tasks, comparing against existing risk-sensitive RL methods.
4. **Elaborate on the details of the actor-critic algorithm**, including function approximation and exploration strategies.
5. **Summarize the key convergence results** of the actor-critic algorithm in the main paper.
6. **Discuss related work in distributional reinforcement learning.**



Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
