Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general class of coherent risk measures. The key technical ingredients are: (i) a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) for static coherent risk ρ(Z) exploiting the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and the envelope theorem, which yields a sampling-friendly likelihood-ratio expression involving the worst-case density ξ* and KKT multipliers; (ii) a sample-average approximation (SAA) based estimator for general coherent risks when the Lagrangian saddle point is not available in closed form, with a consistency result (Prop. 4.4); and (iii) a policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone) relating ∇θVθ to an expectation under a ξ*-reweighted transition kernel, enabling an actor–critic style algorithm. The paper shows how known results for CVaR and mean-semi-deviation fall out as special cases and connects dynamic coherent risk to robust MDPs (Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian).

Strengths
- Generality and unification: The static gradient formula covers all coherent risks with risk envelopes of the assumed form, subsuming prior work that focused on individual risks (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility).
- Conceptual clarity: The dual representation of risk and envelope-theorem approach lead to elegant likelihood-ratio gradients. The relation to robust MDPs is well used for the dynamic case.
- Soundness of the main derivations: The static gradient formula and its SAA extension are technically plausible and the conditions are close to standard convex-analytic assumptions. The Markov coherent risk gradient theorem is a natural and useful extension of the policy gradient theorem.
- Practical route: The SAA-based estimator with convex programming is implementable for a broad class of envelopes; examples elucidate CVaR and mean-semi-deviation clearly.

Weaknesses
- Experimental validation is minimal. The single numerical illustration is essentially a one-step asset-selection example; there are no dynamic MDP experiments and no quantitative comparisons to existing risk-sensitive RL methods (e.g., CVaR optimization in MDPs, robust MDP approximation, risk-sensitive actor–critic).
- Computational burden and practicality are underexplored. The methods require solving an inner convex program (and extracting KKT multipliers) for each θ in static settings and, for dynamic settings, per state x to obtain ξ* and multipliers, plus sampling under a reweighted kernel. The paper does not study runtime, scalability, or stability of these nested procedures.
- Assumptions and regularity: The gradient formulas hinge on an envelope theorem argument with saddle points and differentiability of constraints in Pθ. Non-uniqueness of ξ* (common for CVaR quantiles) and potential nondifferentiability are not carefully treated (e.g., subgradient versions, Clarke calculus). The consistency proof relies on several technical assumptions that are reasonable but not verified for concrete envelopes beyond brief statements.
- Sampling requirement for dynamic risk: The actor needs a simulator able to sample from the ξ*-weighted kernel; this is strong and may preclude many RL settings. No off-policy or importance-sampling workaround is provided.
- Variance/sample complexity: There is no variance analysis or empirical study of estimator variance, especially crucial for tail-sensitive risks (CVaR) where rare-event issues are severe.

Quality
The main mathematical results are well motivated and mostly sound under the stated assumptions. However, several proof details are deferred and key technical nuances (uniqueness, measurable selections of ξ*, subgradients) should be addressed explicitly. The algorithmic pieces are described at a high level; convergence analysis for the actor–critic is deferred. The empirical section does not validate the dynamic method, nor compare gradient estimators or ablations (analytic vs SAA, variance reduction).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Notation is heavy but largely consistent. Some parts would benefit from clarifications: precise statement of constraint qualifications for the envelope theorem; treatment of non-unique saddle points; explicit examples of U for common risks with the required smoothness in Pθ. Minor editorial issues exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
To my knowledge, the general static gradient formula for coherent risks and the policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk are novel, beyond prior work that focused on particular risks or special dynamics. The unification is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
Potentially high: a general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients could influence both theory and practice. However, the current experimental evidence is too thin to support claims of practical impact, and computational trade-offs are not assessed. As such, the demonstrated significance is limited.

Questions and requests for clarification
- How do you handle non-unique ξ* and λ* (e.g., CVaR at mass points)? Is your result a gradient or a subgradient? Please formalize assumptions ensuring differentiability or state a subgradient theorem.
- For dynamic risk, can the expectation be computed with importance sampling under the original kernel rather than requiring a ξ*-modified simulator? Any variance bounds?
- What is the per-iteration complexity of solving the inner convex programs and extracting KKT multipliers? How does this scale with sample size N and with state dimension?
- Can you provide convergence guarantees for the actor–critic with function approximation beyond consistency (e.g., two-timescale SA conditions)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add substantial empirical evaluation on dynamic MDPs (e.g., gridworlds, queues, portfolio management with dynamics), including comparisons with CVaR-MDP algorithms [8,30], robust MDP approximation [31], risk-sensitive AC [22], and mean-variance methods [21,29]. Report performance, variance, and runtime.
- Provide an ablation comparing analytic vs SAA gradients (bias/variance, wall-clock), and study sensitivity to N and to envelope parameters.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement an importance-sampling alternative that avoids modifying the simulator. Explore variance-reduction techniques (e.g., baselines, control variates, adaptive IS for CVaR).
- Strengthen the theory by handling non-uniqueness via subgradients and by explicitly stating constraint qualifications (e.g., Slater, LICQ/MFCQ) needed for the envelope theorem and KKT-based differentiation.
- Clarify examples of envelopes satisfying the differentiability-in-Pθ assumption (e.g., spectral risk) and provide explicit formulas for ∂g/∂p and ∂f/∂p.

Pros for acceptance
- General, elegant gradient formulas unifying static and dynamic coherent risk.
- Clear connection to robust MDPs; extends policy gradient theorem to coherent risk.
- Potentially impactful framework applicable to many risk criteria.

Cons against acceptance
- Very limited experiments; no dynamic RL validation.
- Unclear practicality due to nested convex programs and simulator requirements.
- Technical assumptions and nondifferentiability issues insufficiently addressed.

Recommendation
Reject (encourage resubmission after strengthening empirical validation and addressing the theoretical and practical concerns above).
