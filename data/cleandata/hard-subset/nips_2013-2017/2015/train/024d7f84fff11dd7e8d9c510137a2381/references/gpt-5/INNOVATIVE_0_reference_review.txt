Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static coherent risk of returns and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key ideas are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risks via the risk-envelope representation and an envelope-theorem argument (Theorem 4.2), yielding sampling-friendly estimators; (ii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator that combines Monte Carlo with solving a small convex program to obtain the saddle-point multipliers, with a consistency result; (iii) a new policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) that expresses the gradient as an expectation along trajectories of a ξ-weighted Markov chain, leading to an actor-critic algorithm leveraging the equivalence to robust MDPs. The approach unifies and generalizes prior work on particular risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance/semideviation) and recovers known gradients as special cases. A small trading example highlights the flexibility to encode different notions of risk.

Strengths
- Novel, unifying framework: Moving from specific risks (CVaR/variance) to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a bold and valuable step. The use of the dual (risk-envelope) representation to derive policy gradients is elegant and general.
- Theoretical contributions: Theorem 4.2 (static) and Theorem 5.2 (dynamic) are clear, plausible, and recover known special cases (e.g., CVaR) with simpler derivations. The SAA-based gradient estimator with consistency is useful in practice when closed-form saddle points are unavailable.
- Connection to robust MDPs: Leveraging the equivalence for the critic and adapting function-approximation methods for large-scale problems makes the dynamic result practically relevant.
- Potential impact: Provides a principled way to tailor risk criteria to applications, which is increasingly important in RL and control.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is minimal: Only a toy portfolio example is shown. There is no quantitative study of estimator variance, sample complexity, scalability, or comparison to strong baselines in nontrivial MDPs.
- Practical burden: The general estimator requires solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers at every iteration and, for dynamic risk, at every state (or per batch). The paper lacks a concrete complexity/efficiency analysis and guidance on solver integration.
- Assumptions and applicability: The results are presented for finite probability spaces and envelopes with smooth constraints; many coherent risks fit, but the practicality in continuous/large spaces depends on function approximation details left to the supplement. The dynamic method requires a generative model and the ability to sample under ξ-weighted transitions, which hinders purely model-free rollouts.
- Missing implementation details: The main text omits pseudocode and convergence guarantees for the actor-critic, and does not discuss baseline/variance-reduction techniques or bias introduced by approximate critics.

Quality
Technically sound in scope, with proofs referenced to the supplement. The derivations are careful, and the consistency of the SAA estimator is reassuring. However, the experimental section does not stress-test the algorithms, and computational trade-offs are not quantified. Some edge cases (e.g., non-unique saddle points/quantiles yielding subgradients) could be discussed more explicitly.

Clarity
Well organized and mostly clear. The exposition of the risk-envelope assumptions and their role in the gradient is helpful. Suggestions:
- Include pseudocode for the static gradient estimator (sampling + convex program) and for the dynamic actor-critic, with a brief complexity discussion.
- Clarify how to compute ∂g/∂p and ∂f/∂p in practice for common envelopes, and how to handle non-unique saddle points (subgradients).
- Explain how one samples from ξ-weighted transitions without an explicit generative model (e.g., importance sampling alternatives).

Originality
High. To my knowledge, this is the first general policy-gradient treatment for the full coherent-risk class (static and dynamic), subsuming prior CVaR and variance-based methods and providing a dynamic PG theorem for Markov coherent risk.

Significance
High. The work can catalyze research on risk-sensitive RL beyond a few canonical objectives, and the dynamic theorem is a valuable building block for time-consistent risk-aware control.

Questions for the authors
- Can the dynamic estimator be realized from a single trajectory via per-decision importance sampling rather than a generative model with multiple next-state samples?
- How sensitive is performance to errors in the critic (robust value function) and to approximate KKT multipliers? Any stability guarantees?
- Do you have empirical results on runtime/variance vs. sample size for the SAA estimator, and comparisons to closed-form estimators when available (beyond CVaR)?
- Could primal-dual updates avoid solving a full convex program each iteration?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong novelty and unification across coherent risks
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem with actor-critic instantiation
- Solid theoretical foundations; recovers/extends multiple prior results
- High potential impact on risk-sensitive RL

Cons for acceptance
- Very limited empirical evaluation and no large-scale benchmarks
- Practicality concerns: reliance on convex solvers/KKT multipliers and generative models
- Missing details on variance reduction, complexity, and robustness to approximation

Overall recommendation
Accept. Despite sparse experiments, the conceptual advance and generality are substantial and likely to influence future work. The paper is bold and novel, and I am willing to overlook the limited empirical validation in favor of the theoretical contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium-high.
