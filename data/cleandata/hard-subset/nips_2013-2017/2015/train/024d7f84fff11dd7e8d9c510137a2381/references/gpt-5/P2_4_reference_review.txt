Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general umbrella of coherent risk measures. The key technical contributions are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risk measures using the dual (risk-envelope) representation and the envelope theorem, leading to sampling-based estimators; (ii) a sampling-plus-convex-programming (SAA) estimator for the gradient when the saddle point is not known in closed form, with a consistency result; (iii) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures (via the robust MDP equivalence), yielding an actor–critic approach. The paper recovers known results for specific risks (e.g., CVaR) as special cases and provides a mean-semideviation gradient formula. A small numerical illustration shows how different risk criteria lead to different asset choices.

Relation to prior work
Risk-sensitive RL has prior treatments for exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR (static), and some dynamic coherent risks (often with linear dynamics or planning methods). The paper’s novelty is to unify these with a general policy gradient framework tied to coherent risk via the dual representation (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński–Shapiro). The dynamic result builds on the robust MDP equivalence (Osogami; Ruszczyński) and value-function approximation for robust MDPs (Petrik & Subramanian).

Strengths
- Generality and unification: The envelope-theorem-based gradient (Theorem 4.2) applies to any coherent risk with a convex risk envelope satisfying mild assumptions. This both subsumes and simplifies previous risk-specific derivations (e.g., the CVaR gradient is recovered cleanly).
- The SAA-based estimator (Eq. 8) offers a practical way to handle risk measures whose saddle points are not available analytically, and Proposition 4.4 provides a consistency guarantee under reasonable conditions.
- The dynamic policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) is conceptually significant: it mirrors the classic policy gradient theorem but for Markov coherent risk, connecting gradients to a value function and reweighted dynamics.
- The work clarifies how robust MDP methods (for the critic) can be combined with sampling (for the actor) to optimize time-consistent risks.
- The paper is well-grounded in convex analysis and likelihood-ratio methods, and it contextualizes several prior risk-sensitive RL results as special cases.

Weaknesses
- Experimental validation is minimal. The single, static portfolio example does not demonstrate the dynamic algorithm, sample efficiency, variance, or scalability to large MDPs. Given the algorithmic claims (especially the actor–critic), at least one nontrivial dynamic benchmark is needed.
- Practicality of the dynamic actor: Theorem 5.2 and the proposed two-phase sampling require sampling from ξ*-reweighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per state. This presumes access to a simulator/generative model and may be infeasible in standard online RL. The paper acknowledges this but does not empirically study the implications.
- Missing variance/sample complexity analysis. Likelihood-ratio estimators for risk often suffer high variance, particularly with tail-sensitive risks. The paper cites general LLN rates but does not quantify variance or propose control variates/importance sampling beyond a brief outlook.
- Heavy dependence on explicit risk envelopes and derivatives of constraints with respect to the underlying distribution p. While common for popular coherent risks, extracting and differentiating envelope constraints may be nontrivial for some spectral measures in practice; concrete worked examples beyond CVaR and mean-semideviation would help.
- Some assumptions are strong or under-discussed: bounded likelihood ratios, finite probability spaces (extensions to Lp claimed but omitted), and access to KKT multipliers from a solver. The computational overhead of solving a convex program per batch/iteration is not quantified.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with plausible derivations: the use of the dual representation plus envelope theorem is appropriate and yields a clean gradient formula; the CVaR and mean-semideviation examples support correctness. The SAA consistency assumptions are standard in stochastic programming. The dynamic theorem follows from the robust MDP Bellman equation and a weighted-transition expectation, though full proofs and the actor–critic algorithm details are deferred to the supplement. The main gap is empirical: no dynamic experiments and no quantitative analysis of variance or computational cost.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Notation is heavy but standard. The main text sketches the actor–critic but leaves crucial details (algorithmic steps, function-approximation setup, convergence) to the supplement, limiting reproducibility from the main paper. Some references are incomplete in formatting. A concise pseudo-code in the main body and a concrete example detailing how to compute dfi/dp and dge/dp would improve clarity.

Originality
High in scope and unification. While individual components (CVaR PG, robust MDP link) exist, the general coherent-risk policy gradient (static and dynamic) with an implementable estimator and consistency result is a novel synthesis that streamlines prior ad hoc treatments.

Significance
Potentially significant to risk-aware RL and robust control: a general method to differentiate through coherent risk opens the door to tailoring risk criteria to applications. However, the lack of dynamic empirical validation and performance comparisons limits immediate impact. With stronger experiments and practical guidance (variance reduction, computational costs), this could become a go-to reference.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide at least one dynamic MDP experiment demonstrating the actor–critic, including comparisons to CVaR-specific PG and robust MDP baselines, and report variance and wall-clock analysis.
- Clarify how to implement sampling under ξ*-weighted transitions in practice and the implications when only a single-trajectory on-policy rollout is available.
- Give concrete examples showing how to compute the constraint derivatives dfi/dp, dge/dp for spectral risk measures.
- Discuss variance reduction (baselines/control variates, importance sampling for tails) more concretely, even as preliminary results.
- Include pseudo-code for both the static SAA estimator and the dynamic actor–critic in the main paper.

Pros
- General, elegant gradient formulas; unifies prior risk-sensitive PG results.
- Consistent SAA estimator with mild assumptions.
- Dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk; principled actor–critic design.

Cons
- Very limited experiments; no dynamic validation.
- Practical constraints (need for a simulator; solving convex programs; variance) underexplored.
- Some strong assumptions and implementation details deferred to the supplement.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty and potential impact, but the empirical evaluation is insufficient for NeurIPS standards that emphasize demonstrated practicality. With added dynamic experiments and practical analysis, I would support acceptance. As is, I lean to weak accept if the committee values theory-focused contributions; otherwise weak reject due to limited empirical support.
