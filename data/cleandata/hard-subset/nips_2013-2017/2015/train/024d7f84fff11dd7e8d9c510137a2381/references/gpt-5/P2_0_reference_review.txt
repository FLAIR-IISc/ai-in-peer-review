Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk objectives in reinforcement learning. The key idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk measures as a supremum over a “risk envelope” of test densities and apply an envelope-theorem–style argument to derive gradient formulas amenable to sampling.

Static risk: The authors provide (i) a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) for any coherent risk whose risk envelope is given in a convex form; (ii) concrete instances recovering known results (CVaR) and deriving a new formula for mean-semideviation; and (iii) a general sample-average approximation (SAA) estimator that combines Monte Carlo sampling with solving a convex program, with a consistency result (Proposition 4.4).

Dynamic risk: For time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures (nested composition of a static coherent ρ), they derive a policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) relating ∇θVθ to an expectation over trajectories under a ξ-weighted transition model and a stage-wise term hθ that depends on the value function and Lagrange multipliers. They sketch an actor-critic algorithm: the critic estimates the robust-MDP value function with function approximation, while the actor estimates the gradient using the new theorem. A small numerical example illustrates that different coherent risks can select substantially different policies.

Relation to prior work is appropriately drawn: prior RL work on specific risks (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR; static and dynamic) and robust MDPs; this paper aims to unify and generalize beyond a single risk measure.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The static gradient formula follows cleanly from the dual representation and envelope theorem and is technically solid under the stated assumptions (finite Ω, Slater-type conditions, differentiability of constraint maps). The SAA estimator plus consistency is standard but useful; assumptions (compactness of saddle-point set, continuity, stability of feasible sets) are reasonable for common risks (CVaR, mean-semideviation, spectral).
- The dynamic result is conceptually strong: a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk. However, it relies on having access, at each state, to a saddle point (ξ*, λ*) for the local risk envelope with respect to the transition distribution and the current value function, and on being able to sample from ξ*-reweighted transitions. The actor-critic algorithm is only sketched; convergence statements and practicalities (e.g., estimation error from function approximation, conditions on ergodicity under reweighted dynamics, bias from approximate ξ*) are deferred to the supplement.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal (a static, single-step asset-selection toy example). There are no dynamic MDP experiments validating the actor-critic, variance properties, or computational overhead.
- Missing finite-sample error analysis (variance bounds, sample complexity) and practical variance-reduction tools beyond suggesting importance sampling as future work.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the key ideas and the role of the risk envelope are clear.
- Some notation is heavy; giving explicit pseudocode for the general static estimator and for the actor-critic would substantially improve reproducibility.
- Assumption 2.2 is central; it would help to instantiate it explicitly for CVaR, mean-semideviation, and a spectral risk in the main text (not only in references), including the gradients dfi/dp, to clarify computational requirements.
- The need for a simulator capable of multiple next-state samples per state and the ability to simulate ξ*-weighted transitions should be highlighted earlier; discuss feasibility when only single on-policy rollouts are available.

Originality
- High. Extends policy-gradient methods to the entire class of coherent risks via a unifying duality-based approach; recovers known special cases (e.g., CVaR) with simpler arguments, provides a new gradient for mean-semideviation, and introduces a policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The linkage to robust MDPs for the critic is known, but integrating it into an RL actor-critic for coherent risk is novel.

Significance
- The framework is broadly applicable and conceptually important for risk-sensitive RL and robust control. If made practical, it can influence safe RL and decision-making under model uncertainty.
- Practical impact is tempered by computational and access requirements: solving a convex program (and obtaining Lagrange multipliers) per batch/state, simulating reweighted transitions, and reliance on function approximation quality for Vθ. The lack of dynamic experiments limits immediate adoption, but the theory is valuable and likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Pros
- General, unifying policy-gradient framework for coherent risks (static and dynamic).
- Clean theoretical derivations using duality/envelope theorem; consistency of SAA estimator.
- Recovers and simplifies known results (CVaR) and contributes new ones (mean-semideviation gradient, dynamic PG theorem).
- Appropriate connections to robust MDPs.

Cons
- Sparse empirical validation; no dynamic experiments.
- Practicality challenges: solving per-state convex programs, computing ξ*, sampling under ξ*-weighted dynamics, gradients of envelope constraints wrt transition probabilities.
- Many assumptions and proofs deferred to supplement; finite-sample/variance analysis lacking.
- Algorithmic details (pseudocode, step sizes, function approximation choices) are thin, hindering reproducibility.

Questions/Suggestions
- Clarify how to sample from ξ*-weighted transitions without a generative model; can importance sampling from the true dynamics be used, with variance control?
- Provide explicit examples of dfi/dp and dge/dp for CVaR, mean-semideviation, and a spectral risk in the main text.
- Discuss ergodicity/regularity assumptions of the ξ*-reweighted Markov chain needed for Theorem 5.2 and actor convergence.
- Include at least one dynamic MDP experiment (even small) comparing expected return vs CVaR vs spectral risk actor-critics, and report runtime/solver overhead.
- Consider baseline/advantage formulations to reduce variance, and address non-differentiability at quantile boundaries for CVaR in discrete settings.

Minor
- Some references have incomplete bibliographic details; fix for camera-ready.
- A few footnotes interrupt flow; consider moving technical qualifiers into an appendix.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty and potential impact; practicality and empirical validation are currently limited, but the generality and unification argue for inclusion.
