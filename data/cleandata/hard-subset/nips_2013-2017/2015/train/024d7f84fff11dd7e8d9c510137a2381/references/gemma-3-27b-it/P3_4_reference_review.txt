## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and welcome contribution to the growing field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The authors successfully generalize existing approaches by framing risk-sensitive policy gradients within the broader, well-established framework of coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are strong, the connections to existing work are clearly articulated, and the potential impact on applications in finance and robust control is considerable. While the practical implementation details (particularly for the dynamic case) require further elaboration, the paper provides a solid foundation for future research. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of optimizing policies in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) under a coherent risk measure, moving beyond traditional expected cost objectives. Coherent risk measures, like CVaR, possess desirable properties (convexity, monotonicity, etc.) identified by Artzner et al. Their advantage lies in offering a more nuanced approach to handling uncertainty than simply minimizing expected cost. The authors aim to provide a unified framework for policy gradient algorithms applicable to *any* coherent risk measure, both static (single-horizon) and dynamic (multi-horizon, time-consistent). 

The work builds upon existing literature on risk-sensitive RL, including approaches focusing on specific risk measures (e.g., mean-variance, CVaR) and robust MDPs. Importantly, it distinguishes itself by *not* restricting to a specific risk measure, offering a generalization and simplification of previous results. The paper correctly positions itself relative to work by Ruszczynski & Shapiro (stochastic programming limitations) and Petrik et al. (dynamic programming scalability issues), highlighting the advantage of their sampling-based policy gradient approach for large-scale problems.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The most significant strength is the generalization to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This provides a unifying framework and avoids the limitations of existing methods tied to specific risk preferences.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk is mathematically sound and well-motivated. The use of the Envelope Theorem is clever and leads to practical, sampling-based estimators.
* **Novelty:** The gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) and the policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) are novel contributions. The paper demonstrates how existing results (e.g., for CVaR) are special cases of their general framework.
* **Practicality:** The proposed algorithms are well-suited for large-scale problems where dynamic programming is infeasible. The combination of sampling and convex programming allows for tractable gradient estimation.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear definitions and explanations of key concepts.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example, while simple, effectively demonstrates the impact of different risk measures on policy selection, highlighting the value of flexibility in risk criteria.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Implementation Complexity:**  While the authors outline an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case, the practical details (e.g., function approximation choices, hyperparameter tuning) are not fully elaborated. The "two-phase sampling procedure" described in Section 5 feels a bit hand-wavy and would benefit from more detailed explanation and perhaps a pseudo-code algorithm.
* **Assumption Dependence:** The proposed approach relies heavily on Assumption 2.2 (the general form of the risk envelope).  While reasonable, the practical verification of this assumption for complex risk measures could be challenging.  A discussion of potential issues or alternative approaches if this assumption doesn't hold would be valuable.
* **Scalability of Convex Programming:** The repeated solving of convex programs (in the static case, and potentially within the critic step of the dynamic case) could become a bottleneck for very large state spaces or high-dimensional action spaces. Investigating techniques to reduce the computational cost of solving these programs would be beneficial.
* **Variance of Gradient Estimates:** While the paper mentions consistency, it lacks a detailed analysis of the variance of the gradient estimators, particularly in the dynamic case with the proposed two-phase sampling. Understanding the variance is crucial for selecting appropriate step sizes and ensuring convergence.

**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*  Provides a significant theoretical advancement in risk-sensitive RL.
*  Offers a unifying framework with broad applicability.
*  Presents novel gradient formulas that enable tractable algorithms.
*  Connects to and generalizes existing work in a meaningful way.
*  The illustrative example demonstrates the potential impact of the approach.

**Against:**

*  Implementation details for the dynamic case are relatively sparse.
*  The reliance on Assumption 2.2 could limit applicability in some scenarios.
*  Scalability concerns regarding convex programming within the algorithm.
*  Lack of variance analysis for the gradient estimators.



**5. Recommendations & Questions for Authors:**

* **Elaborate on Implementation Details:** Provide more specifics regarding the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case.  Pseudo-code would be particularly helpful.
* **Discuss Assumption 2.2:**  Address the potential limitations of Assumption 2.2 and suggest possible workarounds or alternative approaches if it doesn't hold.
* **Scalability Discussion:** Briefly discuss potential techniques to mitigate the computational cost of solving the convex programs, such as approximate solutions or decomposition methods.
* **Variance Analysis:**  Provide an initial analysis (even if preliminary) of the variance of the gradient estimators, particularly for the dynamic case.
* **Clarify the Two-Phase Sampling:** Provide a clearer explanation of the two-phase sampling procedure in Section 5. What are the benefits of this approach versus a single-phase sampling?

**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL. Addressing the relatively minor weaknesses outlined above will further strengthen the work and increase its impact.**
