This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient optimization in reinforcement learning where the objective is a coherent risk measure, rather than the standard expectation. The authors tackle two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted return, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key technical insight is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability distributions (the "risk envelope"). By applying the Envelope Theorem to this formulation, the authors derive novel policy gradient formulas.

For the static case, the main contribution is a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) that depends on the solution (the worst-case distribution and its dual variables) to the dual representation problem. The paper demonstrates that this general formula can recover and simplify proofs for known gradients of specific risk measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) and derive new ones for others like the mean-semideviation. For general coherent risk measures where the solution is not analytic, a sample-based algorithm is proposed which involves solving a convex program on an empirical distribution to estimate the gradient. A consistency result for this estimator is provided.

For the dynamic case, the authors derive a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This result extends the standard policy gradient theorem to the risk-sensitive setting. The gradient expression involves an expectation over trajectories generated by a distorted MDP, where transition probabilities are re-weighted by the worst-case distribution from the dual risk formulation. This theorem forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm.

This work builds upon and unifies a significant body of recent research in risk-sensitive RL that has largely focused on specific risk measures (e.g., mean-variance, CVaR). By addressing the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a more principled and general foundation for the field.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main strength is its generality. It provides a unified theoretical framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous works that developed bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures. The connection made between the dual representation of risk and the policy gradient is elegant and powerful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are sound. The use of the Envelope Theorem to derive the static gradient formula is novel in this context and technically solid. The subsequent derivation of the dynamic policy gradient theorem is a non-trivial extension that correctly identifies the structure of the risk-sensitive value function and its gradient.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It logically progresses from preliminaries on coherent risk to the static case and then to the more complex dynamic case. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation are excellent for building intuition and demonstrating the power of the general framework.

4.  **Effective Illustration:** The numerical experiment in Section 6, while simple, is highly effective. It clearly and convincingly demonstrates a scenario where a common risk measure (mean-standard deviation) fails due to its symmetric treatment of variance, while a coherent risk measure (mean-semideviation) that focuses on downside risk makes a more rational choice. This provides strong motivation for the flexibility offered by the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practicality of the Dynamic Algorithm:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem has significant practical challenges that are not fully addressed in the main paper. Theorem 5.2 requires sampling from an MDP with distorted transition probabilities (`Pθ(·|x)ξ*`). This necessitates a simulator that can provide multiple samples of the next state for any given state, which is a strong assumption that precludes its direct application in standard model-free RL settings where only a single trajectory is observed. The paper notes this and refers to the supplement, but this is a major limitation for the practical impact of the dynamic algorithm.

2.  **Computational Complexity:** The general gradient estimation algorithm for the static case (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem at each step of the SGD update. While convex, this adds a potentially significant computational overhead compared to standard policy gradient methods, especially if the number of samples `N` is large. The paper does not discuss this computational trade-off.

3.  **Details in Supplementary Material:** A substantial amount of detail regarding the actor-critic algorithm, including its full specification and convergence analysis, is deferred to the supplementary material. While space is limited, this makes it difficult to fully assess the completeness and correctness of one of the paper's key algorithmic contributions from the main text alone.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

**Pros:**
*   Provides a novel and elegant unifying framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures.
*   The theory is sound and generalizes/simplifies several previous results.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and the core ideas are communicated clearly.
*   The numerical example provides excellent motivation for the work.

**Cons:**
*   The practical applicability of the proposed dynamic actor-critic algorithm is limited by its requirement for a simulator.
*   The computational cost of the static gradient estimator is not discussed.
*   Key algorithmic and analytical details for the dynamic case are in the supplement, making the main paper feel slightly incomplete.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the practical limitations of the dynamic algorithm, the theoretical contributions of this paper are significant, elegant, and provide a strong foundation for future work in risk-sensitive RL. The unification of policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures is a major step forward for the field. The weaknesses, particularly regarding the dynamic algorithm, are acknowledged by the authors and represent clear directions for future research. Therefore, I strongly recommend acceptance.
