Summary
The paper proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives. For static risk, the authors exploit the dual risk-envelope representation to derive an envelope-theorem-based gradient formula, leading to likelihood-ratio estimators that either (i) use known analytic saddle points (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) or (ii) solve a sample-average convex program whose KKT multipliers yield a consistent gradient estimator. For dynamic risk, they consider time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures and establish a “policy gradient theorem” analog relating ∇θVθ(x) to expectations under ξ*-reweighted transition kernels, with a stage-wise signal hθ incorporating Lagrange multipliers and derivatives of the risk-envelope constraints. An actor-critic approach is outlined. A simple trading example illustrates that different coherent risks induce qualitatively different policies.

Relation to prior work
The static CVaR gradient reduces to known formulas; the dynamic setting relies on the risk-sensitive Bellman operator (Ruszczyński) and the robust MDP equivalence (Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al.). The paper aims to generalize methods beyond specific risks (mean-variance, CVaR) to the full coherent class, offering a unifying treatment.

Strengths
- Generality: A single framework that covers all coherent risk measures, retrieving known special cases with simpler derivations.
- Technical insight: Clean use of the dual representation and envelope theorem to obtain convenient likelihood-ratio gradient forms; consistent SAA estimator with mild assumptions.
- Dynamic extension: A policy-gradient-style theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk is novel and conceptually valuable; establishes a bridge between risk-averse RL and robust MDPs.
- Potential impact: If made practical, could standardize risk-sensitive RL beyond bespoke objectives.

Weaknesses
1) Empirical evaluation is inadequate. Only a toy portfolio example is shown; there are no MDP experiments, no dynamic-risk evaluation, no comparisons on standard RL benchmarks, no analysis of estimator variance or sample complexity, and no ablation of the SAA vs. analytic estimators beyond a brief supplement note.

2) Practical feasibility is unclear.
- The general estimator requires solving a convex program per batch and extracting KKT multipliers; scalability and numerical stability are not discussed.
- The dynamic algorithm requires simulating from ξ*-weighted transitions, which demands a generative model and repeated per-state reweighting. This assumption limits applicability and is at odds with single-trajectory RL settings.
- hθ includes terms like dfi/dp and dge/dp; computing these requires explicit differentiable envelopes and access to transition probabilities or multi-sample estimates, which is not always possible.

3) Regularity and differentiability concerns are underplayed.
- CVaR is nondifferentiable at parameters where mass lies at the quantile; the paper states “gradient” where only subgradients are guaranteed. Similar issues arise when saddle points are non-unique.
- The finite probability space assumption simplifies analysis but is not representative; the claimed extension to Lp is deferred without details. For dynamic risk, analogous technicalities (measurability, saddle-point selection across θ) are not addressed.

4) Theoretical scope is mostly asymptotic consistency. No finite-sample error bounds, no convergence rates for the SAA gradient, no variance reduction analysis, and no stability guarantees when solving the convex subproblems approximately.

5) Clarity and notation issues make reproduction difficult.
- The measure Eξ over trajectories is not fully formalized; normalization of ξ-weighted kernels is implicit and depends on constraints that vary with x, θ, and Vθ.
- It is unclear whether the dynamic result yields an unbiased estimator when Vθ is approximated and when ξ* is computed from approximate V; the bias/variance tradeoff is not quantified.

6) Formatting and referencing are sloppy, which undermines confidence.
- Inconsistent use of B (as densities vs. probability measures); ξ both denotes densities and elements of B; Eξ[Z] uses Pθ-weighted ξ but B was defined as distributions. Please reconcile.
- Equality/inequality constraint notation alternates between ge/fe and fi; mismatch in text.
- Typos and grammar: “John Willey & Sons,” “Unions,” multiple missing years and incomplete references; inconsistent capitalization; footnotes in Sec. 2 poorly integrated; hyphenation and spacing inconsistent.

7) Objective sign conventions are inconsistent (cost minimization vs. reward maximization in experiments); please unify or clearly state transformations.

Questions for the authors
- Under what conditions do your “gradients” exist versus subgradients, especially for CVaR and non-unique saddle points? Can you formalize a subgradient version of Thm 4.2/5.2?
- How does the actor-critic behave when ξ* is computed from an approximate Vθ? Any error propagation bounds?
- Can you operate without a generative model (single-trajectory setting)? If not, please state this limitation prominently.
- What is the computational complexity per policy update for the general estimator? Any empirical timing vs. CVaR-specific methods?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide substantial MDP experiments (including dynamic risk) with comparisons to CVaR-specific RL, mean-variance methods, and robust MDP baselines. Report variance, wall-clock, and scalability.
- Give pseudocode and implementation details in the main text for both static and dynamic algorithms; specify step-sizes, sampling budgets, and convex solver setup.
- Clarify differentiability vs subgradients; state and prove the relevant regularity conditions in main text with at least proof sketches.
- Clean up notation, define measures carefully for ξ-reweighted trajectories, and reconcile B/ξ/Pθ definitions. Fix references and formatting.

Overall evaluation
The paper’s central idea—a unified gradient framework for coherent risk in RL—is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the current submission is theory-heavy but incomplete empirically, and the practicality of the general estimator/actor-critic is not convincingly demonstrated. Numerous clarity and formatting issues further detract. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to revise: strengthen experiments (especially dynamic risk), clarify regularity and subgradient issues, and improve presentation.
