Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies reinforcement learning with risk-sensitive objectives drawn from the class of coherent risk measures. Using the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al., Ruszczyński & Shapiro), the authors derive policy gradient formulas for (i) static coherent risk of random costs and (ii) time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk in MDPs. For static risk, they obtain a general gradient expression via the Envelope Theorem applied to the dual (risk envelope) convex program, combined with a likelihood-ratio identity. This yields closed-form gradients for specific measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and a general sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator involving solving a convex program and using its KKT multipliers. For dynamic risk, they exploit the Bellman equation for Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone) to establish a policy gradient theorem that expresses ∇θVθ as an expectation over trajectories generated by an ξ*-reweighted transition kernel with a stage term hθ capturing the effect of risk-envelope constraints. This leads to an actor-critic method: a critic approximates the robust MDP value function, and an actor estimates the gradient using the derived expression.

The work unifies and generalizes prior RL studies focused on specific risk criteria: exponential utility (Borkar), mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), CVaR (Rockafellar & Uryasev; Tamar et al.; Chow et al.), and dynamic risk for structured systems (Chow & Pavone). It also builds on robust MDP connections to coherent dynamic risk (e.g., Iyengar/Nilim–El Ghaoui type results; here cited as Osogami and Petrik & Subramanian), and uses convex optimization machinery (Boyd & Vandenberghe) and SAA theory (Shapiro et al.).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The static-gradient derivation via the dual representation and envelope theorem is appropriate under the stated assumptions (compact convex envelope, Slater condition, differentiability of constraint maps in the base probability). The LR-style formulas for CVaR and mean-semideviation correctly recover known special cases and provide clear generalizations. The SAA-based estimator with consistency (Proposition 4.4) is standard and plausible given the smoothness and compactness assumptions.
- The dynamic-gradient theorem extends the classic policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk. It is conceptually sound: the ξ*-weighted kernel reflects the adversarial reweighting in the risk envelope, and the stage term hθ accounts for envelope constraint derivatives. The need to sample under the ξ*-weighted dynamics and to evaluate derivatives dfi/dp, dge/dp is properly acknowledged.
- Completeness: Several proofs and algorithmic specifics are deferred to the supplement; convergence properties of the actor-critic with function approximation are stated but not detailed in the main text. Variance, sample complexity, and computational complexity are not quantified. Nonetheless, the main claims are supported at a level typical for NIPS theory papers with supplementary material.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with a careful preliminaries section, clear assumptions, and helpful examples that recover CVaR and mean-semideviation. The roles of the Lagrangian saddle point and KKT multipliers are explained.
- Some notational density (e.g., dependence of risk envelopes on Pθ and state), and reliance on a finite probability space for exposition, may impede readability; extensions to Lp settings are stated but omitted. The dynamic section would benefit from explicit pseudo-code and a more concrete description of the two-phase sampling and the critic’s approximation method.

Originality
- The main novelty is a unified policy-gradient framework that applies to the full class of coherent risk measures, both static and time-consistent dynamic (Markov). Prior work largely targeted specific risks (CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility) or planning with value-function approximation for robust/dynamic risk MDPs. The envelope-theorem-based gradient formula with an SAA+convex-programming estimator is, to my knowledge, new in RL. The policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears original and conceptually useful.

Significance
- The results provide a principled and general toolset for risk-sensitive RL that could be adopted by researchers and practitioners needing different coherent risks. The connection to robust MDP value approximation and an actor-critic realization increases practical relevance.
- Current empirical validation is limited. The static portfolio example illustrates the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure but does not demonstrate scalability or performance relative to strong baselines on dynamic tasks. Broader experiments would better establish impact.

Strengths
- General, unifying framework covering all coherent risks (static and Markov dynamic).
- Clear derivations that subsume known special cases (e.g., CVaR gradient).
- Consistent SAA-based gradient estimator using convex programming and KKT multipliers.
- Dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk; connection to robust MDPs.
- Assumptions are explicit; discussion of time consistency and risk-envelope structure is thorough.

Weaknesses
- Practicality concerns: each gradient estimate requires solving a convex program and accessing KKT multipliers; dynamic gradient requires sampling under ξ*-weighted kernels and computing derivatives of envelope constraints w.r.t. transition probabilities.
- Finite probability space assumption for exposition; extensions to continuous spaces are claimed but not developed.
- Limited empirical evaluation; no dynamic MDP experiments; no runtime or variance analysis; no comparison to alternative risk-sensitive policy-gradient baselines on standard benchmarks.
- Many technical details are deferred to the supplement; main text lacks algorithmic pseudo-code and complexity discussion.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide experiments on standard RL benchmarks (including dynamic tasks) comparing against CVaR/mean-variance/exponential-utility methods, reporting performance, variance, and computational overhead.
- Include pseudo-code and a complexity analysis for both static and dynamic algorithms; discuss feasibility in continuous state/action spaces (e.g., envelope parameterization, stochastic optimization of ξ).
- Discuss variance reduction (e.g., baselines/control variates) and importance sampling for tail-sensitive risks.
- Clarify conditions ensuring interchange of max and gradient (regularity for envelope theorem) and when constraints depend on Pθ.
- Elaborate on how to implement ξ*-weighted sampling when only a generative model is available.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical contribution that unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients across coherent risk measures, including a novel dynamic theorem.
- Solid derivations that recover and simplify known results; potential to influence future work on safe and risk-aware RL.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation and practical guidance; unclear scalability to large/continuous problems.
- Some reliance on restrictive or hard-to-verify assumptions in practice (finite Ω, differentiability in P, availability of KKT multipliers and reweighted simulators).

Overall, the paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution with promising applicability, but would benefit from stronger empirical support and practical elaboration.
