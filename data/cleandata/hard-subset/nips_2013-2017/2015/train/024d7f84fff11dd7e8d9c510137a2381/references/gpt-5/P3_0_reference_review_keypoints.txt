- Develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering static coherent risk and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

- Main technical contributions: (i) general gradient formula for static coherent risk using dual risk-envelope and envelope theorem.

- (ii) A sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator that mixes sampling with solving a convex program and uses KKT multipliers, with a consistency result.

- (iii) A policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk expressing ∇θVθ as an expectation under ξ*-reweighted dynamics with a stage-wise hθ, enabling an actor-critic scheme.

- Unifies and generalizes prior work on CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility, and other specific risks to the full coherent risk class.

- Builds on the dual representation of coherent risk and robust MDP equivalence from existing literature.

- Strength: provides a conceptual unification of policy-gradient methods across coherent risks and static/dynamic settings.

- Strength: clean derivation leveraging envelope theorem and likelihood ratios; recovers CVaR gradients without extra smoothness assumptions.

- Strength: SAA estimator is broadly applicable; consistency assumptions are reasonable for common risks (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation).

- Strength: dynamic policy-gradient theorem clarifies reweighted dynamics and defines a risk-sensitive advantage for actor-critic algorithms.

- Strength: connects critic learning to robust MDPs, giving a principled path for value-function approximation.

- Weakness: many proofs and key details are deferred to the supplement; no finite-sample error or variance bounds for gradient estimators.

- Weakness: SAA requires solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers each batch; complexity and numerical stability for large supports are not discussed.

- Weakness: sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions is nontrivial; paper assumes simulator support and omits practical implementation details (importance sampling, modified kernels).

- Weakness: no convergence guarantees with function approximation for the critic; approximation bias effects on the actor are not analyzed.

- Clarity issues: some assumptions (finite probability space, differentiability of risk-envelope constraints, availability of ∇θ log P and ∇θ log µθ) need clearer upfront emphasis.

- Clarity issue: conditions and normalization for Pθ(·|x)ξ* to define a valid ergodic Markov kernel are not fully discussed.

- Reproducibility: lacks pseudo-code for static SAA gradient and actor-critic algorithms.

- Originality: extension via envelope/KKT to general coherent risks and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem is novel and valuable.

- Significance: potentially high impact as a unifying foundation for risk-sensitive RL, but empirical support is currently limited.

- Specific open questions: sensitivity to non-unique saddle points, explicit forms and costs for spectral risks, online primal-dual alternatives, importance sampling for single trajectories, ergodicity/variance conditions, and continuous state/action extensions.

- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, recovers known special cases, and links to robust control; likely a useful community reference.

- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical validation (no dynamic experiments), practical concerns about convex-program solves and ξ*-sampling, and missing finite-sample/variance analyses.

- Suggestions: add dynamic MDP experiments versus CVaR/mean-variance baselines, include pseudo-code and complexity/KKT stabilization details, provide finite-sample/variance discussion and variance-reduction strategies, and clarify ξ*-sampling and continuous-state/action extensions.

- Overall recommendation: promising and original theoretical work that needs stronger empirical evaluation and clearer practical implementation to maximize impact.
