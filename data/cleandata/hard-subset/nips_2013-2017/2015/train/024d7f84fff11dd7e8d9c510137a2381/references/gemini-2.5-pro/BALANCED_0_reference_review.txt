### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives. The authors' approach is notable for its generality, as it applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, thereby unifying and extending previous work that has focused on specific measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The paper addresses two distinct settings: (1) optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted return, and (2) optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

For the static risk problem, the authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the "risk envelope"). By applying the envelope theorem, they derive a novel and general formula for the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2). This formula is then used to propose a practical gradient estimation algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming (the Sample Average Approximation method), and a consistency result for this estimator is provided (Proposition 4.4). The paper demonstrates that this general formula can elegantly re-derive and simplify existing gradient formulas for specific risk measures like CVaR.

For the dynamic risk problem, the paper extends the classic policy gradient theorem to the setting of Markov coherent risk measures. It derives a new gradient formula (Theorem 5.2) that relates the gradient of the risk-sensitive value function to an expectation over trajectories generated by a modified, risk-adjusted transition model. This theoretical result forms the basis for a proposed actor-critic algorithm suitable for large-scale MDPs.

### Strengths

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this work lies in its principled and general approach. By building upon the foundational theory of coherent risk measures, the paper provides a unified framework that encompasses a wide range of risk preferences. This is a significant conceptual advance over prior works that developed bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures. The ability to re-derive results for CVaR and mean-semideviation highlights the power and elegance of the proposed framework.

2.  **Novel and Sound Theoretical Contributions:** The paper presents significant theoretical contributions. The derivation of the gradient for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) using the envelope theorem is both novel and insightful. The policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) is a non-trivial generalization of the standard policy gradient theorem and provides a solid theoretical foundation for developing new algorithms. The technical quality of the work appears high.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. It begins with a clear introduction to the necessary concepts of coherent and dynamic risk measures, making the work accessible. The progression from the static to the dynamic case is logical, with the results from the former serving as a building block for the latter. The relationship to prior work is clearly articulated throughout.

4.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** This work addresses a problem of growing importance in RL. As RL systems are deployed in real-world, high-stakes domains (e.g., finance, autonomous systems), managing risk beyond simple expectation becomes critical. By providing a flexible and theoretically grounded set of tools for the entire class of coherent risk measures, this paper is likely to have a substantial impact on future research and practice in risk-sensitive and robust reinforcement learning. The connection drawn to robust MDPs further enriches the contribution.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Feedback

While this is a strong paper, there are areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Computational Cost of the General Gradient Estimator:** The proposed algorithm for general static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) at each gradient estimation step. For a large number of samples `N`, this could be computationally expensive and may limit the practical applicability of the method in settings requiring many gradient updates.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could strengthen the paper by discussing the scalability of this SAA approach. For instance, are there specific structural properties of the risk envelope `U` (beyond convexity) that permit more efficient optimization? Could online or stochastic convex optimization techniques be leveraged to update the dual variables (the saddle point) incrementally, avoiding a full solve at each step?

2.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration in Section 6, while insightful for motivating the need for different risk measures, is quite simplistic. It validates the conceptual premise of the paper but does not test the performance or scalability of the core algorithmic contributions, namely the SAA-based gradient estimator for general risk or the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by including an empirical evaluation of the proposed algorithms on a more standard RL benchmark. For example, applying the SAA-based method to a problem where the saddle point is not analytically tractable and demonstrating its convergence would be very valuable. Similarly, showcasing the actor-critic algorithm, even on a moderately sized MDP, would provide crucial evidence of its viability.

3.  **Complexity of the Actor-Critic Algorithm:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is quite high-level, with most details deferred to the supplementary material. The proposed algorithm appears complex, involving a critic that solves a robust Bellman equation and an actor that requires sampling from a risk-distorted MDP.
    *   **Suggestion:** While acknowledging space constraints, providing more intuition in the main text about the actor's two-phase sampling procedure would be helpful. Furthermore, the reliance on a simulator capable of providing multiple next-state samples from any given state-action pair is a strong assumption. Highlighting this as a limitation and a key direction for future work (e.g., developing single-trajectory variants) more prominently would be beneficial.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes significant theoretical and conceptual contributions to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. Its main strengths are the unification of prior work under the rigorous framework of coherent risk measures and the novel derivation of general policy gradient theorems for both static and dynamic risk settings. The work is original, technically sound, and clearly presented. The primary weakness is the lack of empirical validation for the proposed general-purpose algorithms. However, the strength and importance of the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant publication at NeurIPS. The ideas presented here are likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work.
