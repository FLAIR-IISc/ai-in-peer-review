- Presents a novel framework applying policy gradient to the full class of coherent risk measures, covering static and dynamic cases.  
- Derives new gradient formulas for static and dynamic coherent risk, enabling sampling-based algorithms.  
- Re-derives existing special-case results and demonstrates framework flexibility via a portfolio optimization example.  
- Positions the work well relative to prior risk-sensitive RL, robust MDPs, and stochastic programming literature.  
- Grounds theory in established coherent risk and stochastic programming results (Artzner et al., Ruszczynski & Shapiro).  
- Core strength: generality—unified treatment avoids per-measure algorithm development.  
- Theoretical soundness: rigorous derivations using envelope theorem and convex duality; consistent sampling estimators.  
- Practicality: algorithms are sampling-based and link to actor-critic methods, suggesting scalability potential.  
- Clear presentation with consistent notation and well-defined concepts.  
- Illustrative numerical example shows benefits and flexibility of different coherent risk measures.  
- Weakness: framework is mathematically dense and may be hard for readers unfamiliar with coherent risk and convex optimization.  
- Weakness: computational cost—requires solving convex programs each iteration, potentially expensive in high dimensions.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation—only a simple example; needs benchmark tasks and baselines.  
- Weakness: reliance on Assumption 2.2 restricts applicability to risk measures meeting that structure.  
- Weakness: dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm is described at a high level with key details in supplementary material.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, general framework, novel gradient formulas, clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential prohibitive computational cost, insufficient empirical validation, and theoretical complexity hindering adoption.  
- Overall assessment: solid and original contribution with concerns about scalability and empirical validation.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: expand computational complexity discussion, strengthen empirical evaluation with benchmarks and baselines, move dynamic actor-critic details into main paper, and discuss limitations/extensions of Assumption 2.2.
