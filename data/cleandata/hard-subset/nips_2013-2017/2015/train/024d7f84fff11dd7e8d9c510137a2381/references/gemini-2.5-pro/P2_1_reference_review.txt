### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning settings where the objective is to optimize a coherent risk measure, rather than the standard expected return. The authors make several key contributions. First, for the static risk problem (e.g., optimizing the risk of the total discounted return), they derive a novel and general formula for the policy gradient. This formula leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures as a worst-case expectation over a "risk envelope" and employs the Envelope Theorem. Second, they propose a practical algorithm based on this formula that estimates the gradient by solving a Sample Average Approximation (SAA) of the dual problem, which is a convex program. They provide a consistency proof for this estimator. Third, they extend their analysis to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. They derive a new policy gradient theorem for this setting, analogous to the standard one, which relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. This leads to a proposed actor-critic algorithm for large-scale problems. The paper demonstrates that its framework unifies and simplifies previous results for specific risk measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) and derives new gradient formulas for others, such as the mean-semideviation measure.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, elegant, and well-motivated.
*   **Strengths:** The core theoretical result for the static case (Theorem 4.2) is powerful. Using the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope Theorem is a principled and clean way to derive the gradient, avoiding the measure-specific, often complex derivations seen in prior work. The consistency result for the SAA-based estimator (Proposition 4.4), while having several assumptions, provides important theoretical grounding for the proposed algorithm. The extension to the dynamic case via the risk-sensitive Bellman equation (Eq. 9) and the resulting policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) is a logical and significant step.
*   **Weaknesses:** The paper feels somewhat incomplete regarding the dynamic risk problem. The full details of the actor-critic algorithm, including the function approximation scheme for the critic and the convergence analysis, are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is common due to space constraints, the actor-critic part is a major contribution, and its absence from the main text makes it difficult to fully assess the completeness and practicality of this part of the work. The assumptions in Proposition 4.4 are stated but not deeply discussed in terms of which common risk measures might violate them, or how difficult they are to verify in practice.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   **Strengths:** The structure is logical, building from preliminaries to the static case and then to the more complex dynamic case. The inclusion of examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation is highly effective. They not only demonstrate the generality of the main result (Theorem 4.2) but also serve as a valuable sanity check, showing that the framework recovers and simplifies known results. The motivation for using coherent and time-consistent risk measures is well-articulated in the introduction.
*   **Weaknesses:** The notation can be dense, particularly in the derivation of the dynamic policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) and the definition of the cost function `hθ(x, a)`. A little more intuition behind the form of `hθ` could improve readability for a broader audience. As mentioned under Quality, the lack of detail on the actor-critic algorithm in the main body is a significant clarity issue for one of the paper's core claims.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original and provides a significant conceptual advance.
*   **Strengths:** While policy gradients and risk-sensitive RL are not new topics, this is the first work to provide a unified policy gradient framework for the *entire class* of coherent risk measures. Previous research has focused on deriving bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). This paper's main conceptual leap is to work directly with the fundamental dual representation of coherent risk, which allows for a single, elegant derivation that applies to all of them. This unification is a substantial contribution that reframes the problem in a more general and powerful way.
*   **Weaknesses:** The constituent components (policy gradients, coherent risk theory, SAA) are established. The novelty lies in their synthesis. However, this synthesis is non-trivial and leads to new theoretical results and algorithms, so this is a very minor weakness.

**Significance:**
The potential impact of this paper is very high.
*   **Strengths:** This work provides a "recipe" for researchers and practitioners to develop policy gradient algorithms for any coherent risk measure of their choosing, provided its risk envelope can be specified as a convex program. This dramatically lowers the barrier to exploring a much richer set of risk-sensitive objectives in RL, moving beyond simple mean-variance or CVaR formulations. The numerical example, though simple, effectively illustrates why this flexibility is crucial: different risk measures can lead to vastly different and potentially more desirable behaviors. The paper opens up numerous avenues for future work, including variance reduction techniques and the intriguing idea of "risk shaping" to account for model uncertainty.
*   **Weaknesses:** The practical significance is tempered by a potential computational bottleneck that is not addressed. The general algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) at *each step* of the gradient estimation process. For a large number of samples `N`, this could be prohibitively slow compared to standard policy gradient methods, potentially limiting its application in large-scale deep RL where gradient steps are frequent. The paper would be stronger if it included a discussion of this computational trade-off or experiments on a more demanding problem to assess scalability.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **High Originality and Significance:** Provides the first unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures, a significant conceptual advance over previous work on specific measures.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the general gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) is elegant, principled, and theoretically sound.
3.  **Unification and Simplification:** The framework elegantly recovers, generalizes, and simplifies existing results for specific risk measures like CVaR, demonstrating its power.
4.  **Practical Potential:** It provides a clear methodology for applying policy gradients to a wide array of risk objectives, enabling more nuanced risk-aware decision-making in RL.

### Arguments for Rejection/Improvement:
1.  **Incomplete Presentation:** Key details of the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem are relegated to the supplement, making a core contribution of the paper difficult to evaluate.
2.  **Practicality Concerns:** The computational cost of the proposed general gradient estimator (which requires solving a convex program per gradient step) is not discussed or evaluated, leaving open questions about its scalability to large-scale problems.

### Final Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical development is elegant and unifies a large body of prior work in a principled manner. The potential impact is high, as it opens the door to using a much wider variety of risk measures in RL. The main weaknesses are the incomplete presentation of the actor-critic algorithm and the unaddressed concerns about the computational scalability of the general method. However, the theoretical contributions for both the static and dynamic cases are substantial enough on their own. I recommend **acceptance**, with the suggestion that the authors use the final version to either incorporate more detail about the actor-critic algorithm or, at a minimum, include a thorough discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of their proposed methods.
