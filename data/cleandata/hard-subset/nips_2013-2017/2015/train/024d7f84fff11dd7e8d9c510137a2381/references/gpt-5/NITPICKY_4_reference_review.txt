Summary
The paper proposes policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The key idea is to leverage the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk to derive gradient formulas amenable to sampling. For static risk ρ(Z), Theorem 4.2 gives a gradient expression via the Lagrangian saddle point, combining a likelihood-ratio term with derivatives of the envelope constraints. This yields simple formulas for specific risks (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and a general sample average approximation (SAA) estimator that solves a convex program on empirical samples and uses the KKT multipliers to form a consistent gradient estimate. For dynamic Markov coherent risk (time-consistent), the authors present a “policy gradient theorem” (Theorem 5.2) for the risk-sensitive value function Vθ that expresses ∇θVθ(x) as an expectation over trajectories of a ξ-weighted transition kernel, with a stage cost involving the value function, a dual variable λ* and derivatives of the risk-envelope constraints. They sketch an actor-critic methodology and provide a small illustrative experiment on a static asset selection task showing that different coherent risk choices lead to different policies.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on the coherent risk representation (Artzner et al.; Shapiro & Ruszczyński) and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński 2010). It generalizes risk-sensitive RL beyond specific measures such as exponential utility (Borkar), mean-variance (Tamar et al.), or CVaR (Rockafellar-Uryasev; Tamar et al.; Chow & Ghavamzadeh). The dynamic part connects to the robust MDP equivalence (Osogami; Ruszczyński; Petrik & Subramanian) and value-function approximation in robust MDPs (Tamar, Mannor, Xu).

Strengths
- Conceptual generality and unification: One framework covers all coherent risks, recovers existing results (e.g., CVaR gradient) with simpler derivations, and offers a path to new ones.
- Technically sound high-level idea: Using envelope/KKT to differentiate through risk envelopes and combining with likelihood ratios is elegant and broadly applicable.
- The dynamic “policy gradient theorem” is of independent interest and plausibly novel for Markov coherent risk, tying risk gradients to a modified transition kernel.
- The SAA-based estimator is natural and consistent under stated conditions; using convex solvers to obtain multipliers is practical for moderate sample sizes.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality/technical correctness
- Differentiability vs. subdifferentials: Coherent risks (e.g., CVaR) are often non-differentiable for discrete distributions or non-unique quantiles. Theorem 4.2 claims a gradient at “any saddle point” but seems to require differentiability; at best, one obtains a subgradient. This needs to be stated precisely, with conditions or a subgradient version.
- Assumptions on the envelope mapping U(Pθ): Assumption 2.2 posits twice differentiability of ge, fi in p and bounded derivatives, but many common envelopes are polyhedral and only piecewise smooth; the derivative terms dfi/dp and dge/dp in Theorem 5.2 can be undefined or set-valued. The paper asserts these conditions hold for CVaR, spectral, and mean-semideviation without proof; please justify rigorously or weaken the theorem to subgradients.
- Finite probability space: The entire development is in finite Ω; the extension to Lp spaces is claimed but omitted. This is non-trivial (measurability, interchange of limits, differentiating through infinite-dimensional constraints). As is, the scope does not match many RL settings (continuous states/actions).
- Dynamic gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) lacks critical conditions: No assumptions about ergodicity/mixing of the ξ-weighted chain, existence/uniqueness of Vθ under function approximation, or boundedness/integrability of the likelihood-ratio sum. The proof is deferred; please state the exact regularity assumptions required (e.g., unichain, bounded features, contraction) to ensure validity and finite variance.
- Sampling under ξ-weighted dynamics: The actor requires simulating from the reweighted kernel Pθ(·|x) ξ*θ,x(·). This may not be feasible with a standard generative model; acceptance-rejection may be inefficient if ξ is unbounded or sharply peaked. The paper does not provide an implementable sampling scheme or discuss variance/normalization issues.
- Consistency of SAA gradient (Prop. 4.4) assumes compactness of saddle points, feasibility for all empirical distributions, and a closedness property (IV). These are strong and not easy to verify for general envelopes. Please provide concrete verification for the stated popular risks.
- Signs and objective consistency: The paper alternates between cost minimization and reward maximization (e.g., Section 6 maximizes returns while earlier sections are framed for costs). Ensure consistent sign conventions and correct translation (cash invariance A3) throughout.
- Gradient reduction to standard PG: For the expectation (risk-neutral) special case, it would help to show explicitly that Theorem 5.2 reduces to the classic policy-gradient theorem; the role and value of λ*P in that case are unclear.

Clarity/presentation
- Notation issues: The use of Pθ ∈ B where B is defined as densities is confusing; Eξ[Z] is defined as ∑ Pθ ξ Z, yet Theorem 2.1 writes ρ(Z)=max ξ: ξPθ∈U(Pθ) Eξ[Z]. Please cleanly distinguish densities ξ, base measures Pθ, and the induced reweighted measures.
- Footnotes and formatting are broken and intrude into the text (e.g., “1”, “2” mid-sentences; a dangling note about U depending on state). Some equations are hard to parse (e.g., (6) missing clear domain of multipliers).
- The stage cost hθ in Theorem 5.2 is dense and includes derivatives dfi/dp, dge/dp without definitions of p; make explicit that p denotes Pθ(·|x).
- Many references are incomplete (missing years, pages) and have inconsistent formatting. Several citations appear without sufficient context; e.g., claims about variance O(1/√N) are vague (bias vs. MSE).

Originality
- The static gradient derivation is a clean generalization but overlaps with known sensitivity analysis for convex risk optimization; the novelty is in treating θ-dependent distributions. Please more clearly differentiate from Ruszczyński–Shapiro’s sensitivity results and prior RL CVaR/variance PG work.
- The dynamic theorem appears new within the Markov coherent framework; however, the connection to robust MDP policy gradients is not explored and could contextualize novelty.

Significance
- Practical impact is limited by computational and sampling requirements: solving a convex program and obtaining KKT multipliers per batch/sample; requiring a simulator that can reweight transitions; multiple next-state samples per visited state.
- Empirical validation is insufficient: only a simple static asset-choice example is provided, no dynamic MDP experiments, no comparisons to baselines, no sensitivity to α or envelope choices, and no evaluation of variance or sample efficiency.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you recast Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 as subgradient results and state precise regularity conditions for differentiability?
2) How do you sample from Pθ(·|x) ξ*θ,x(·) in practice with only a generative model for Pθ? Any variance control?
3) What are the exact assumptions ensuring the ξ-weighted Markov chain is ergodic/mixing so that the infinite-horizon sum is well-defined?
4) Can you provide at least one dynamic MDP experiment and ablations showing computational cost, variance, and convergence?
5) For envelopes where ge, fi do not depend on P, do your formulas simplify (e.g., remove the dfi/dp terms)? Please include worked-out examples beyond CVaR.

Minor issues/typos
- Several grammatical errors and inconsistent notation (e.g., “the set B :={ ξ : ∫ ξ=1, ξ ≥0 }” used for both densities and Pθ).
- In Sec. 6, claims of stochastic dominance should be justified carefully; the Pareto support and Normal overlap matter.
- References missing years/pages and inconsistent capitalization.

Recommendation
Reject. The conceptual contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form has substantial technical gaps (differentiability, sampling feasibility, missing assumptions), limited empirical validation (no dynamic experiments), and clarity/notation issues. With clarified assumptions (including subgradient formulations), a practical sampling scheme, and stronger experiments, this work could be a valuable contribution.
