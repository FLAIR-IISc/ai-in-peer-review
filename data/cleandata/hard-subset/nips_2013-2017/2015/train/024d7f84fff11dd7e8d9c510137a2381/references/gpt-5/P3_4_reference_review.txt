Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general umbrella of coherent risk measures. The key ingredient is the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro), which reduces risk evaluation to a worst-case expectation over reweighted densities. The authors:

- Derive a general gradient formula for static coherent risk via an envelope-theorem argument (Theorem 4.2), yielding a likelihood-ratio estimator that depends on the optimal dual variables (saddle point of the risk-envelope optimization).
- Recover and unify known special cases (e.g., CVaR gradient) and provide a new gradient for mean-semideviation.
- Propose a general sampling-based estimator using sample average approximation (SAA) that solves a convex program to obtain ξ∗ and KKT multipliers, and prove almost sure consistency under mild conditions.
- Extend to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński, 2010; Chow & Pavone), derive a policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) that relates the gradient to a value function and saddle-point quantities at each state, and outline an actor-critic method using robust MDP connections for the critic and ξ∗-weighted sampling for the actor.
- Provide a small numerical illustration highlighting how different risk functionals lead to different asset choices in a bandit-like setting.

Quality
- Theory: The static gradient result follows cleanly from the dual representation and an envelope-theorem argument. Using the optimal KKT multipliers to remove dependence on ∂ξ∗/∂θ is elegant. The SAA estimator and the consistency proposition are standard but appropriate; the assumptions (Slater, boundedness, mild smoothness in p) match common coherent risks (CVaR, spectral, semideviation).
- The dynamic gradient theorem is noteworthy: it generalizes the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk by expressing the gradient as an expectation under a ξ∗-reweighted transition kernel with an augmented per-step term that includes dual contributions. The decomposition is sound given the risk-sensitive Bellman equation.
- However, several important details are deferred to the supplement: regularity needed for envelope theorem (uniqueness vs. measurable selection of saddle points), handling non-differentiability (e.g., CVaR at atoms/quantiles), and the actor-critic convergence and approximation error analysis. For discrete distributions, risk functionals like CVaR are generally non-smooth; the paper should be explicit that gradients are subgradients and that the SGD is effectively subgradient descent.
- Practicality: The static estimator requires solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers per batch; the dynamic method requires solving per-state saddle-point problems and sampling under ξ∗-weighted kernels. While polynomial-time, the computational overhead is nontrivial in large-scale RL. The authors note function approximation for the critic and a two-stage sampling for the actor, but empirical evidence is limited.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well grounded, with clear connection to coherent risk duality and robust MDPs. Notation is heavy; explicitly stating when gradients are subgradients would help. The dynamic section would benefit from pseudo-code of the actor-critic (even compressed), a brief discussion of how to sample from Pθ(·|x)ξ∗(·) when only a standard simulator is available (e.g., importance sampling vs. resampling), and the computational cost per update. Some assumptions (twice differentiability of constraint maps in p) could be justified for the main exemplars (CVaR, spectral) in the main text.

Originality
- The unifying gradient formula for static coherent risk and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appear novel and broadly generalize prior work limited to particular risks (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR). The view through risk envelopes and KKT multipliers in policy gradient is an original and useful synthesis.
- The link to robust MDPs for the critic follows known results but is applied here to support an actor-critic for general coherent risk.

Significance
- The work has strong theoretical significance: it provides a general recipe to derive policy gradients for a wide class of risk measures, potentially influencing future risk-aware RL research. The dynamic result is especially impactful in connecting time-consistent risk with RL algorithms.
- Empirical significance is limited in the current form. The single-step trading example illustrates qualitative differences between risk criteria but does not validate the proposed dynamic actor-critic or the scalability/variance properties of the estimators.

Strengths
- General, principled treatment of coherent risk in RL, both static and dynamic.
- Clear derivations leveraging duality and envelope theorem; unification of previous special cases.
- Consistency of the SAA-based gradient estimator under reasonable assumptions.
- A new policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental evaluation; no dynamic MDP experiments, no comparison to baselines in realistic settings, no variance/convergence study.
- Practical computation can be heavy (convex programs, KKT multipliers, ξ∗-weighted sampling). Guidance on scalability and engineering trade-offs is sparse.
- Non-differentiability subtleties (e.g., CVaR with atoms) and uniqueness/measurability of ξ∗ and Lagrange multipliers are not addressed explicitly in the main text.
- The requirement of differentiable constraint maps in p may not hold for all coherent risks; more explicit verification for key examples would help.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify whether Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 provide subgradients in non-smooth cases and how this affects convergence guarantees.
- Provide pseudo-code for the actor-critic and discuss sampling under ξ∗ when only a standard simulator is available (importance sampling corrections, variance implications).
- Report at least one dynamic MDP experiment (even small scale) comparing your method to CVaR-specific algorithms and risk-neutral baselines, with learning curves and variance bars.
- Comment on the complexity of solving the per-state convex programs and obtaining duals. Are there closed forms or efficient updates for popular risks (e.g., spectral/CVaR) that avoid heavy solvers?
- Discuss the stability of the critic with function approximation under ξ∗-weighted dynamics and the effect of approximation error on the actor gradient bias.

Overall assessment
- The paper offers a strong and broadly applicable theoretical contribution with clear connections to prior work and plausible algorithms. The main limitation is the lack of substantive empirical validation and limited practical guidance. With additional experiments and clarifications on non-smoothness and computational aspects, this would be a solid contribution.
- Recommendation: weak accept.
