This paper presents a significant and broadly applicable framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk criteria. The core idea – leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk to formulate tractable gradients – is both novel and powerful. It beautifully unifies existing risk-sensitive RL approaches as special cases and extends them to a much wider class of risk measures. While the theoretical development is dense and some validation is limited to a single illustrative example, the potential impact is high. I highly value the boldness and generality of the approach, and believe it addresses a crucial gap in the literature.

**Summary:** The authors tackle the problem of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. They derive a policy gradient formula applicable to both static and dynamic coherent risk, building on the duality representation of coherent risk. Key contributions include a novel gradient formula for static risk, a consistent sampling algorithm, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, enabling an actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty & Generality:** The work provides a truly general framework, encompassing numerous existing risk-sensitive RL methods (CVaR, mean-variance) as special cases. This unification is a major strength.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation leverages established results from coherent risk theory and provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms. The connection to robust MDPs is insightful.
*   **Practicality (Potential):** The sampling-based gradient estimation algorithms, while requiring convex optimization as a subroutine, appear scalable to larger problems, addressing a limitation of dynamic programming approaches.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly motivate the need for coherent risk measures in RL and articulate the challenges associated with optimizing them.
*   **Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper meticulously places the work within the context of existing research in risk-sensitive RL and stochastic programming.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration is limited to a single example and doesn't provide a comprehensive assessment of the algorithm's performance across different environments or risk measures. The comparison focuses on parameter tuning with respect to the objective function, rather than performance on a task.
*   **Implementation Complexity:** The algorithms involve nested optimization problems (sampling + convex programming). The practical overhead associated with solving the convex subproblems could be significant and is not fully addressed.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption regarding the smoothness of the constraints in Assumption 2.2, while common, might not hold for all coherent risk measures. Its implications are not thoroughly discussed.
*   **Theoretical Density:** The paper is mathematically demanding, potentially hindering accessibility for researchers less familiar with coherent risk theory.
*   **Actor-Critic Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm is high-level, and important details regarding function approximation and stability are relegated to supplementary material.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem in RL – risk management – with a novel and highly general approach.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and provide a strong foundation for future research.
*   The proposed algorithms offer a promising path towards scalable risk-sensitive RL.
*   The unification of existing methods is valuable and simplifies the landscape of risk-sensitive RL.
*   The work has the potential to impact both theoretical and applied research in RL and related fields.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited empirical validation raises concerns about the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
*   The implementation complexity and the need for convex optimization could be a barrier to adoption.
*   The theoretical density might limit the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in empirical validation and implementation details, I strongly recommend acceptance. The originality, theoretical depth, and potential impact of this work are exceptionally high. The authors have presented a significant contribution that advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The weaknesses are not fatal, and I believe the paper is sufficiently strong to warrant inclusion in NIPS, even with these limitations. I encourage the authors to address the empirical validation and implementation complexity in future work. I think a more detailed ablation study in the supplementary material would address many of the concerns raised by the reviewers.
