Summary
The paper studies policy gradients for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using the coherent risk framework. The main technical pieces are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risk via the convex-analytic dual (risk envelope) and an envelope/Danskin-type argument (Theorem 4.2), (ii) a sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator with a consistency result for general coherent risks (Proposition 4.4), and (iii) a “policy gradient theorem” for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) with an associated actor-critic scheme. The framework recovers existing special cases such as CVaR and mean-semideviation and suggests a unified route to optimizing a broad class of coherent risks. A small static numerical example illustrates behavior differences between mean-semideviation vs mean-variance.

Strengths
- Unifying viewpoint: Casting gradient computation through the coherent risk envelope is elegant and connects risk-sensitive RL to convex duality and robust MDPs. It cleanly subsumes CVaR and mean-semideviation.
- General gradient formula: Theorem 4.2 provides a compact LR-style expression involving the worst-case density and Lagrange multipliers, which is practically estimable if the saddle point is available or approximable.
- Consistency of the SAA-based estimator: Proposition 4.4 is useful for practitioners who will not have closed-form saddle points.
- Extension to dynamic Markov coherent risk: The attempt to derive a policy gradient theorem for this class is timely and, if practically implementable, could be impactful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy assumptions and practical limitations: The method assumes a known, explicitly parameterized risk envelope U(Pθ) with constraints differentiable w.r.t. the probability vector p and availability of saddle-point multipliers. In many RL settings, dynamics are unknown and even the envelope (as a function of Pθ) is not directly available. The dynamic algorithm further requires a generative model and sampling from ξ*-reweighted transitions, which is not feasible in standard model-free RL. The paper acknowledges the need for multiple next-state samples and a simulator, but this significantly limits applicability.
- Non-uniqueness and non-smoothness glossed over: Theorem 4.2 relies on envelope/Danskin-type arguments. For coherent risks (e.g., CVaR), the optimum is often non-unique (ties at the quantile), leading to set-valued saddle points and subgradients rather than gradients. The paper states “any saddle point,” but it does not clearly state conditions ensuring differentiability of ρ(Z) in θ or continuity/measurability of the multiplier selection mapping. In discrete CVaR, the “gradient” is generally a subgradient; this should be made explicit, including implications for convergence of SGD.
- Dynamic policy gradient theorem conditions: Theorem 5.2 defines expectations under ξ*-tilted transition kernels. There are no stated conditions ensuring ergodicity/mixing of the ξ*-weighted chain, existence/uniqueness of stationary distributions, or boundedness/variance properties needed for unbiased LR estimation. The hθ term contains derivatives of constraints dfi/dp and dge/dp evaluated at the next-state distribution; this presumes stronger smoothness than is typical for coherent risks and excludes important non-smooth cases without further discussion.
- Missing baselines and insufficient empirical evaluation: There is no empirical validation of the dynamic method or the actor-critic algorithm on MDP benchmarks (gridworlds, inventory control, queueing, control). The only experiment is a simple static portfolio-selection toy problem; it does not exercise the main claimed algorithmic contributions (sampling with convex programming, dynamic gradient, actor-critic). No comparisons to strong risk-sensitive baselines (e.g., CVaR policy gradient [30], risk-sensitive actor-critic [21], robust MDP solvers [31]) in realistic settings. This is a major weakness given the ambitious scope.
- Incremental novelty in special cases: The CVaR gradient is known (e.g., [30] and the classical Rockafellar–Uryasev formulation); the paper’s contribution here is mainly to derive it through the general envelope formula and to claim a simplified proof and discrete-case coverage. The dynamic result builds on known connections between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs [19,24,31], and the actor-critic direction overlaps with prior risk-sensitive PG/AC work [6,21]. The unification is appealing but the incremental advance is primarily theoretical framing rather than demonstrated algorithmic gains.

Quality
The theoretical direction is sound in spirit, but the paper relies on strong assumptions (finite sample spaces, differentiability of constraints in p, Slater, existence of saddle points, bounded LR, simulator access) and omits critical technical caveats (subgradients, multiplier selection, ergodicity under tilted kernels). Important details and proofs are deferred to the supplement, leaving the main text short on rigor for the dynamic case. The experimental support is inadequate to substantiate the practicality or benefits.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. However:
- Please clarify when results are gradients vs subgradients and specify convergence guarantees under non-smoothness.
- State precise conditions for Theorem 5.2 (ergodicity/mixing and boundedness), and discuss the feasibility of sampling under ξ*-weighted dynamics.
- Discuss computational complexity and variance of the SAA-based estimator, and the cost of solving a convex program per batch.

Originality
Moderate. The unifying envelope-based derivation is novel and elegant, but many components have close precedents (CVaR gradient, risk-sensitive AC, robust MDP equivalence). The new dynamic PG theorem is potentially original but underspecified.

Significance
Currently limited by practical constraints and lack of empirical validation. With stronger experiments on dynamic MDPs, variance-reduction strategies, and a model-free instantiation, the impact could be significant.

Questions for authors
- Are your results for CVaR and other non-smooth risks stated as subgradients? What selection rule do you use in practice, and how does it affect convergence?
- What conditions ensure ergodicity of the ξ*-weighted Markov chain? How sensitive is the actor’s gradient estimate to approximation error in ξ* and Vθ?
- Can the approach be made model-free (single trajectory) via importance sampling or density-ratio estimation, and how would that affect variance?
- Provide empirical results for the dynamic algorithm on at least one nontrivial MDP with comparisons to [21,30,31].

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually unifying framework applicable to a broad class of coherent risks.
- Useful static gradient formula and a consistent estimator employing convex programming.
- Potentially impactful dynamic PG theorem if practicable.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions and limited practicality in typical RL settings.
- Critical technical caveats insufficiently addressed (non-smoothness, saddle-point selection, ergodicity).
- Inadequate empirical validation; no dynamic experiments or comparisons to baselines.

Recommendation
Score: 4/10 (leaning reject)
Confidence: High

With substantial additional theory clarifications (subgradient treatment, conditions for Theorem 5.2) and strong empirical validation on dynamic MDPs, this work could become a solid contribution. As it stands, the claims outpace the evidence.
