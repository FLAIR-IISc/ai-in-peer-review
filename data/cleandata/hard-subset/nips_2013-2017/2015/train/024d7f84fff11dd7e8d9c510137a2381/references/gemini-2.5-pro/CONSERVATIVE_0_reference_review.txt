### Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Summary**

This paper presents a general framework for developing policy gradient algorithms for objectives defined by coherent risk measures. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities (the "risk envelope"). Using the envelope theorem, they derive a general formula for the policy gradient of a static coherent risk measure. This formula is then used in two ways: first, to re-derive and generalize existing gradient formulas for specific risk measures like CVaR and Mean-Semideviation; and second, to propose a general gradient estimation algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming for cases where an analytical solution is unavailable. The paper extends this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, proposing a corresponding policy gradient theorem and outlining an actor-critic algorithm. The primary contribution is the unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods under the single, powerful umbrella of coherent risk theory.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The theoretical development of the paper appears to be sound. The key technical insight—applying the envelope theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk to derive a policy gradient formula (Theorem 4.2)—is elegant and powerful. It provides a principled way to approach a broad class of risk-sensitive objectives. The subsequent derivation of a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) is a logical and important extension.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its empirical validation, which is far from sufficient to support the introduction of this new, complex algorithmic framework. The work presents a single numerical illustration on a three-armed bandit problem. While this example effectively demonstrates the difference between risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation), it does little to validate the proposed algorithm itself. It does not test the algorithm in a sequential decision-making context (i.e., a multi-state MDP), nor does it evaluate the scalability, sample efficiency, or computational cost of the proposed method. The claim in Section 4.3 that the general SAA-based estimator performed "very similarly" to the analytical one for CVaR is mentioned in passing and deferred to the supplementary material; this is a critical comparison that should be central to the paper's evaluation.

Furthermore, the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is presented only at a high level. The paper acknowledges that it requires a complex two-phase sampling procedure and relies on function approximation methods from other work, but provides no concrete implementation or evaluation. For a NIPS paper, introducing a new class of algorithms requires more rigorous evidence of its practicality and performance.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and the theoretical arguments are presented clearly. The preliminaries on coherent and dynamic risk measures are concise and informative. The structure, moving from the static case to the more complex dynamic case, is logical. However, the lack of detail on the actor-critic algorithm in the main body of the paper makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess this part of the contribution. Deferring the entire algorithm and its analysis to the supplement is a significant shortcoming.

**Originality:**
The work is original in its generalization. While policy gradient methods for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) have been explored, this paper is the first to propose a unified method for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The approach of combining sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient is novel in this context. The work successfully connects the theory of coherent risk from operations research and finance with modern reinforcement learning techniques, unifying several disparate results from prior work in a clean and principled manner.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high, as it could provide a general recipe for optimizing any risk-sensitive RL objective that can be framed as a coherent risk measure. However, the practical significance is currently unproven. A major concern is the computational overhead introduced by the general gradient estimator (Section 4.3), which requires solving a convex optimization problem for each gradient update. This is likely to be substantially more expensive than established, specialized policy gradient algorithms for common risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The paper does not provide any analysis of this trade-off between generality and computational efficiency.

Practitioners are unlikely to adopt this general but computationally demanding framework without clear evidence that it outperforms or enables solutions unattainable with simpler, established methods. The paper argues for the importance of "flexibility in designing risk criteria," but this argument is undermined by the lack of a compelling application or a performance comparison against specialized baselines that would justify the added complexity. The concluding remarks about "risk shaping" to handle model uncertainty are interesting but speculative.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper provides an elegant and theoretically sound unification of policy gradient methods for a wide class of risk measures.
2.  The main result (Theorem 4.2) is a novel and powerful tool that simplifies and generalizes previous derivations for specific risk measures.
3.  The work bridges the gap between the mature theory of coherent risk and the field of reinforcement learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The empirical validation is critically insufficient. A single bandit experiment does not demonstrate the viability of the proposed algorithm for solving complex sequential decision-making problems.
2.  The computational cost of the general approach, which requires solving a convex program at each step, is a major practical concern that the paper fails to address or evaluate.
3.  The paper makes claims about a novel actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk but omits the details and any form of evaluation from the main text, making the contribution difficult to assess.
4.  The work does not provide a compelling comparison against established, specialized algorithms for well-known risk measures (e.g., CVaR) to demonstrate that its generality does not come at an unacceptable performance cost.

**Conclusion:**

While the theoretical contribution of this paper is novel and elegant, it currently reads as a position paper rather than a complete piece of work. The proposed algorithms, while theoretically grounded, are unproven in practice. The lack of rigorous empirical validation against established baselines on meaningful RL problems is a fatal flaw. I am skeptical of endorsing a new and computationally intensive method without clear evidence of its practical utility. The paper lays an interesting theoretical foundation, but it does not meet the standard of a full contribution for NIPS. I would encourage the authors to conduct a thorough empirical study of their proposed methods on standard RL benchmarks and to provide a detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs involved.
