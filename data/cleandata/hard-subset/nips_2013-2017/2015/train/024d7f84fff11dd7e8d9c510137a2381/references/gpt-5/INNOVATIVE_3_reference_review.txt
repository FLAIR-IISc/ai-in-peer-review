Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static risk of returns and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key technical device is the dual representation of coherent risk via risk envelopes (Artzner et al., Ruszczyński & Shapiro), coupled with an envelope theorem argument to differentiate through the adversarial “worst-case” distribution. Contributions include: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) that yields sampling-friendly estimators; (ii) a practical estimator that combines sampling with solving a small convex program (SAA) and a consistency result; (iii) a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) relating the gradient to a value function under ξ-weighted transitions, leading to an actor–critic scheme that leverages the equivalence to robust MDPs; and (iv) specializations that recover and generalize known results for CVaR and mean-semi-deviation. A small numerical example illustrates that different coherent measures can materially change learned behavior.

Relation to prior work
The paper unifies disparate risk-sensitive PG results (e.g., CVaR optimization in MDPs, mean-variance, exponential utility, robust MDPs, time-consistent dynamic risk) under the coherent-risk umbrella. The static gradient for CVaR previously appeared in specialized form, and the dynamic result connects to robust MDP planning/approximation, but a general PG theorem and sampling-based estimators for the full coherent class appear to be new. The work builds on and appropriately cites prior NIPS/related work (Chow et al., Petrik & Subramanian, Borkar, Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh).

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A single gradient framework for the entire class of coherent measures (static and dynamic), generalizing multiple prior lines of work.
- Technically elegant: Clean use of the risk-envelope dual form and the envelope theorem to obtain likelihood-ratio-style gradients; the resulting formulas make the adversarial weights act like a principled baseline.
- Practical pathway: The SAA-based estimator requires only sampling and solving a convex program with O(N) variables, and the consistency result (Prop. 4.4) is reassuring.
- Dynamic PG theorem: Extends the policy-gradient theorem to time-consistent risk with a clear actor–critic blueprint and a thoughtful discussion on sampling under ξ-weighted transitions.
- Clarifies connections to robust MDPs and function approximation, suggesting scalability avenues.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited empirical validation: Only a small static example is shown; no experiments for the dynamic actor–critic, scalability, or comparisons across multiple coherent risks in realistic MDPs.
- Finite-space and smoothness assumptions: The main text assumes finite probability spaces and differentiability of envelope constraints; extensions to general Lp/continuous spaces are claimed but deferred.
- Computational burden: The need to solve convex programs (and obtain KKT multipliers) per gradient estimate and per state (dynamic case) could be significant; practical runtimes and stability are not discussed.
- Sampling requirements: The actor–critic needs a simulator capable of multiple next-state samples per state and sampling under ξ-weighted dynamics; on-policy single-trajectory learning is left to future work.
- Some algorithmic details and proofs are deferred to the supplement, limiting reproducibility from the main text.

Quality
Theoretical development is sound and builds on established convex duality and stochastic approximation principles. The consistency of the SAA gradient and the derivation of the dynamic PG theorem are plausible given the assumptions. However, the empirical section is too thin to judge practical performance, variance, and computational overhead. The paper is careful in assumptions and limitations, but would benefit from a brief complexity analysis and variance discussion in the main text.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with clear motivation for coherent and time-consistent risk. Notation is heavy but standard; a figure or algorithm box for the two-phase sampling and the actor–critic would help. Some important details are in the supplement; consider pulling the high-level algorithm pseudocode and complexity remarks into the main paper.

Originality
High. Providing a general policy-gradient formula for coherent risk (static and dynamic) is a notable step beyond prior risk-specific methods. The unifying view and the dynamic PG theorem constitute a new direction likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Significance
Potentially high. Risk-aware RL is increasingly relevant in safety-critical and finance domains. A general, sampling-based PG toolkit for coherent risk (with connections to robust MDPs) could influence both theory and practice. The impact would be strengthened by empirical demonstrations on larger MDPs and comparisons among coherent risks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments for the dynamic actor–critic on medium-scale MDPs, with ablations on sample complexity, computation per update, and comparisons to CVaR/robust baselines.
- Provide a small complexity and stability discussion for the convex subproblems and KKT extraction in both static and dynamic settings.
- Clarify conditions for uniqueness/existence of saddle points and implications when non-unique.
- Include a brief section on variance reduction (e.g., baselines, control variates, importance sampling for tail risk) and practical guidance on choosing U(Pθ).
- Outline the extension path to continuous spaces (e.g., function approximation for ξ*, constraints) more concretely.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel, and unifying theoretical contribution.
- Clear pathway to algorithms with consistency guarantees.
- Generality across coherent risks and dynamic, time-consistent settings.
- Timely and relevant to the NeurIPS community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Minimal empirical validation; no dynamic experiments.
- Practical computational and sampling burdens not empirically characterized.
- Reliance on finite-space and smoothness assumptions in the main text.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite limited experiments, the paper introduces a significant and general theoretical framework that unifies and extends risk-sensitive policy gradients. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the empirical limitations, which can reasonably be addressed in future work.

Confidence
High.
