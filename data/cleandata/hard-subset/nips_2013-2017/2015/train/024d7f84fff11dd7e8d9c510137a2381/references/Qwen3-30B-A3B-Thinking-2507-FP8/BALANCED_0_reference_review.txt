

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper presents a unified framework for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using coherent risk measures. The authors develop policy gradient algorithms that work for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The key contributions include a new gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), along with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The work generalizes previous research on specific risk measures [8, 18, 21, 29, 30] and builds on the theory of coherent risk measures [2, 25, 26].

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical foundations, with clear connections between coherent risk measures and their representation theorem (Theorem 2.1). The gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are well-motivated and properly justified.

2. **Unification of Previous Work:** The paper successfully demonstrates how various specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation) are special cases of their framework. This is a significant contribution that provides conceptual clarity to the field.

3. **Novel Insights:** The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (Section 7) is particularly insightful and opens up promising research directions for handling model misspecification in dynamic decision making.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and clearly explains the mathematical concepts. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the practical utility of the framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Assumptions and Practicality:** Assumption 2.2 (on the risk envelope) may be too restrictive for many practical applications. The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss whether common coherent risk measures (beyond CVaR and mean-semideviation) satisfy this assumption. The computational complexity of the convex programming step in the gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) is also underdiscussed, which could be prohibitive for large-scale problems.

2. **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical example (Section 6) is limited to a simple trading agent with only three assets. The paper lacks comparison with state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL methods on standard RL benchmarks, making it difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

3. **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper mentions scaling to large MDPs, it doesn't address how the proposed method compares to other scalable risk-sensitive RL approaches (e.g., [20, 21, 30]) in terms of sample efficiency or computational requirements.

4. **Missing Comparisons:** The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon [24] (which proposed a dynamic programming approach for dynamic coherent risk) in terms of computational tractability for large-scale problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a theoretically strong paper that makes a significant contribution by providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL using coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are novel, well-supported, and generalize previous work. The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

However, the paper's practical significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumptions about the risk envelope and the lack of thorough empirical validation on standard RL benchmarks. The computational complexity of the proposed method, especially the need for convex programming at each gradient step, is not adequately addressed, which is a crucial consideration for RL applications.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. Extend the numerical experiments to include standard RL benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari) to demonstrate scalability and performance advantages over existing risk-sensitive RL methods.

2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the convex programming step and explore potential approximations to make the method more scalable.

3. Clarify the practical applicability of Assumption 2.2 by showing how common risk measures (beyond CVaR and mean-semideviation) satisfy this assumption, or by discussing potential extensions to more general risk measures.

4. Include a comparison with [24] and other relevant works to clarify the computational advantages of the proposed sampling-based approach over dynamic programming methods for large-scale problems.

**Decision:**

The paper makes a theoretically important contribution to risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. However, the lack of thorough empirical validation on standard benchmarks and insufficient discussion of computational complexity for large-scale applications limits its practical significance. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that addressing the weaknesses mentioned above would substantially strengthen the paper.

The authors have made a valuable theoretical contribution that could significantly impact risk-sensitive RL research. With additional empirical validation and computational analysis, this work could become a standard reference in the field.
