Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning where the objective is a coherent risk measure. The main idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczynski & Shapiro) to express the risk as an adversarially reweighted expectation over outcomes. For static risk, the authors derive a gradient formula via the Lagrangian and the envelope theorem, leading to a likelihood-ratio estimator that is amenable to sampling. They show special cases recover known results: a simple derivation of the CVaR gradient and a new expression for the mean-semideviation gradient. For general coherent risk measures with known risk envelopes, they propose a sample average approximation (SAA): solve a finite convex program to get a saddle point and plug it into their gradient formula; they prove almost-sure consistency.

For dynamic, time-consistent evaluation, they consider Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczynski 2010; Chow & Pavone) and derive an analogue of the policy gradient theorem: the gradient of the risk-sensitive value can be written as an expectation over trajectories of a modified Markov chain with ξ*-reweighted transitions and a per-step term hθ that depends on the value function and the Lagrange multipliers. This yields an actor-critic scheme: a critic computes a robust-MDP value function (using Petrik & Subramanian-style function approximation), and an actor uses the new theorem to update policy parameters. A small numerical example illustrates how different risk criteria lead to different asset-selection behaviors, highlighting the flexibility of the coherent-risk framework.

Relative to prior work on specific risk objectives—exponential utility (Borkar), mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), CVaR (Rockafellar & Uryasev; Chow et al.; Tamar et al.)—this paper aims to generalize policy-gradient methods to the full class of coherent risks in both static and dynamic settings, while leveraging the robustness connection (Osogami; Ruszczynski; Petrik & Subramanian).

Quality
- The static gradient derivation is elegant and appears sound under the stated regularity (finite Ω, Slater condition, smoothness of envelope constraints, bounded likelihood ratios). The SAA-based estimator with KKT multipliers is plausible and the consistency proposition is appropriate.
- The dynamic policy gradient theorem is a solid and natural extension: combining the static dual with a risk-sensitive Bellman equation is insightful. However, many details, including the critic’s function approximation scheme, convergence guarantees, and bias/variance properties of the actor’s estimator, are deferred to the supplement.
- Practicality concerns arise: computing ξ* and multipliers requires solving a convex program repeatedly (per batch or per state), and the actor requires sampling from ξ*-weighted transition kernels—typically unavailable in standard RL without a generative model; the paper notes this and assumes a simulator that can draw multiple next-states per state.

Clarity
- The presentation is generally clear and well organized; key assumptions are stated. The use of the envelope theorem and likelihood ratio trick is transparent. The heavy notation (multiple Lagrange multipliers per state) could be streamlined, and pseudo-code for the actor-critic would help.
- Several technical conditions (uniqueness/measurable selection of saddle points, differentiability at non-smooth points like CVaR quantiles, continuity of U(Pθ)) should be discussed more explicitly in the main text to reassure readers.

Originality
- The unifying static gradient formula for general coherent risks and its use for sampling-based estimation is novel and insightful. The dynamic “policy gradient theorem” for Markov coherent risk also appears new and conceptually unifying, reducing to prior results for special cases.

Significance
- Conceptual significance is high: a general recipe for risk-sensitive policy gradients can catalyze broader use of coherent risk in RL and connect robust control and risk-sensitive learning.
- Empirical significance is currently limited: only a small static example is shown; there are no dynamic-risk experiments, ablations, or comparisons to strong baselines. Without such evidence, it is hard to assess practicality and sample efficiency.

Pros for acceptance
- General, elegant theoretical framework covering a broad class of coherent risks.
- Recovers and simplifies known results (e.g., CVaR gradient) and provides new ones (mean-semideviation, dynamic theorem).
- Clear connection to robust MDPs; actor-critic pathway is promising.
- Consistent SAA estimator that is conceptually straightforward to implement with convex solvers.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is minimal; no dynamic risk experiments or comparisons to recent CVaR/dynamic-coherent baselines.
- Practical requirements (convex program and ξ*-weighted sampling) may limit applicability in standard RL settings without a full simulator.
- Missing finite-sample error/variance analysis and computational complexity profiling.
- Some technical assumptions and regularity conditions (uniqueness/smoothness of saddle points, measurable selections) are under-discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add dynamic-risk experiments on standard RL tasks (e.g., gridworlds, inventory control, queueing, continuous control with risk) comparing against: risk-neutral PG, CVaR-specific methods (Chow et al.; Tamar et al.), mean-variance actor-critic, and robust MDP policy iteration. Report tail-risk metrics, expected cost, and training curves.
- Provide variance-reduction techniques (baselines/control variates) and empirical variance plots for the gradient estimators; quantify the cost of solving the inner convex program and total runtime vs. alternatives.
- Clarify the conditions for applying the envelope theorem: uniqueness vs. set-valued saddle points, handling non-differentiable points (e.g., CVaR quantile), and how measurable selections are ensured across states.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement importance sampling or reweighting so ξ*-weighted transitions can be approximated without modifying the simulator; analyze the induced bias/variance.
- Expand on the critic: specify the function class, projection scheme, and provide bounds on the induced gradient bias. Include pseudo-code for the full actor-critic.
- Comment on extensions to continuous state/action spaces and spectral risk measures, where U(Pθ) may be infinite-dimensional; suggest parametric approximations of ξ or dual variables.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning weak accept) for its general and potentially influential theoretical contributions. Strengthening the empirical section, clarifying technical conditions, and addressing practicality concerns would make this a clear accept.
