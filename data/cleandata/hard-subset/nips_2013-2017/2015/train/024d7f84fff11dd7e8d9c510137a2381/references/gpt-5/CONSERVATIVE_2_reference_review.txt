Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general umbrella of coherent risk measures. For static risk, the authors exploit the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński–Shapiro) and an envelope-theorem argument to derive a generic gradient formula expressed via a saddle point of a convex program over the risk envelope. They propose a sampling-based estimator using sample-average approximation (SAA) that requires solving a convex program and show consistency under regularity assumptions. They recover known special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and claim their derivations are simpler and more general than prior work focused on specific risks.

For dynamic risk, they consider time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński; Chow & Pavone) and present a “policy gradient theorem” that expresses the gradient of the risk-sensitive value function as an expectation along trajectories of a reweighted Markov chain, with a stage-wise term that depends on the KKT multipliers and constraint gradients of the risk envelope. They sketch an actor-critic algorithm that requires (i) a critic that estimates the robust MDP value function and (ii) an actor that samples from ξ-weighted transitions and uses the value estimate to compute the gradient. Empirical illustration is limited to a toy static portfolio selection with heavy tails to motivate flexibility in choosing risk measures.

Relation to prior work
The static gradient for CVaR connects to Rockafellar–Uryasev and recent sampling-based CVaR optimization in MDPs. Risk-sensitive PG/AC for exponential utility, mean-variance, and variance-related criteria (e.g., Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al.) are acknowledged. The dynamic setting builds on time-consistent risk measures and their equivalence to robust MDPs (Ruszczyński; Petrik & Subramanian). The contribution is a unifying derivation for coherent risks rather than a method tailored to one risk.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The use of the dual representation and envelope theorem yields a clean, general gradient expression that recovers several special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation).
- Static SAA estimator: The two-stage “sample + convex optimization” approach is principled; the consistency result is useful and reasonably mild under their assumptions.
- Dynamic PG theorem: Extends policy-gradient style results to time-consistent Markov coherent risk with a clear connection to robust MDPs; this is of theoretical interest.
- Clarity of the core derivation: The static case is well-motivated and relates cleanly to existing literature; the examples help position the work.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality and computational burden:
  - The approach requires explicit risk envelopes, KKT multipliers, and differentiability of constraints w.r.t. the underlying probability distribution. Many coherent risks (e.g., spectral families) can induce non-smooth dependence via ordering/quantiles; this is acknowledged only briefly and assumed away.
  - The static estimator solves a convex program of size O(N) per gradient step; the dynamic actor requires solving such problems per state (to get ξ*, λ*) and sampling from ξ*-reweighted transitions. The paper does not quantify the computational cost or propose scalable approximations.
  - The dynamic algorithm assumes a simulator that can generate multiple next-state samples per state and, effectively, sampling from Pθ(·|x) ξ*, which is non-trivial without model access; importance sampling is mentioned as future work, but practical procedures are absent.

- Assumptions and technical gaps:
  - Finite probability spaces throughout; the continuous-space extension is claimed but omitted. The smoothness assumptions on fi, ge in p and the existence of strictly feasible points are strong and not verified for many commonly used coherent risks beyond the listed examples.
  - Saddle-point non-uniqueness (e.g., CVaR quantile in discrete cases) and subgradient selection are not discussed; how does ambiguity in λP or ξ* affect the estimator?
  - The dynamic theorem relies on KKT objects computed with the true Vθ; the algorithm uses an approximate critic. Bias, stability, and error propagation due to approximation are not analyzed quantitatively; no sample complexity or variance bounds are provided.

- Empirical validation:
  - Experiments are extremely limited: a single one-step toy example for static decision-making. There is no demonstration on dynamic MDPs, no robust evaluation of the actor-critic, no comparisons to specialized baselines (e.g., CVaR PG, variance-related PG/AC, robust MDP planning), no ablations (analytic vs SAA gradient, effect of envelope complexity), and no scaling experiments.
  - The claim that SAA and analytic CVaR estimators perform similarly is relegated to the supplement and not substantiated in diverse settings.

- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Key algorithmic details (actor-critic pseudocode, how to sample from reweighted transitions, how constraint gradients are computed for specific envelopes) are deferred.
  - Notation around Eξ over trajectories and normalization could be clarified; while ξP sums to one by construction, it would help to state conditions ensuring a proper Markov kernel per state.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: Theoretical ingredients for the static case are sound and standard; the dynamic theorem is plausible given the robust MDP connection. However, the work stops short of establishing practical, statistically efficient algorithms, and lacks rigorous empirical corroboration. Important issues (bias/variance, sample complexity, computational complexity) are not addressed.
- Clarity: Generally clear in the static part; the dynamic part is sketched. Missing algorithmic details hinder reproducibility.
- Originality: The unification across coherent risks and the dynamic PG theorem are novel combinations of known tools (dual representation, envelope theorem, robust MDPs). The contribution is primarily theoretical unification.
- Significance: Potentially significant if made practical; as presented, without empirical validation on dynamic RL tasks, it is difficult to assess impact or usability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a full actor-critic algorithm with implementable steps, including how to obtain ξ*, λ* efficiently and how to sample under ξ*-weighted transitions (e.g., via importance sampling), with variance reduction.
- Add comprehensive experiments on standard MDP benchmarks (gridworlds, queueing, portfolio with temporal dynamics), with comparisons to CVaR-specific methods, variance-related PG/AC, and robust MDP approximations. Include scaling and runtime analysis.
- Quantify the effect of function approximation on bias and provide error bounds or stability guarantees; analyze variance and sample complexity of the SAA estimator.
- Clarify handling of non-unique saddle points, subgradients, and non-smooth envelopes; explicitly treat discrete CVaR quantile ambiguity.
- Discuss extensions to continuous state/action spaces and random costs with concrete assumptions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, general theoretical framework that unifies risk-sensitive PG across coherent risk measures.
- Extends policy gradient theory to time-consistent dynamic coherent risks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Minimal empirical validation; no dynamic experiments; unclear practicality.
- Strong assumptions and algorithmic steps that require oracle-like access (risk envelope derivatives, KKT multipliers, weighted-transition sampling).
- No complexity, variance, or sample-efficiency analysis; reproducibility is limited.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The theoretical unification is interesting, but the paper lacks the rigorous empirical validation and practical algorithmic development necessary for NeurIPS. With substantial experiments, clarified assumptions, and practical sampling/variance-reduction strategies, the contribution could become impactful.
