

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary

This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (RL) by developing policy gradient methods for coherent risk measures, which satisfy desirable properties including convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity. The authors present a unified framework for both static coherent risk measures and dynamic Markov coherent risk measures, generalizing previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR, mean-variance, and mean-semideviation.

The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 4.2, which provides a gradient formula for static coherent risk that is convenient for sampling-based approximation. This formula is then extended to dynamic risk measures through Theorem 5.2, which generalizes the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk. The authors demonstrate that their approach encompasses and generalizes previous results for specific risk measures, while providing a consistent framework that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work on risk-sensitive RL, particularly [8, 30] for CVaR, [18, 29, 21] for mean-variance, and [20, 9] for dynamic coherent risk. It also connects to foundational work on coherent risk measures [2, 26] and risk-sensitive optimization [25, 26].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures. Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 are novel and provide a solid theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive RL.

2. **Generalization of Prior Work**: The authors clearly demonstrate how their approach generalizes previous results for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation, etc.). For example, in Section 4.1, they show that their gradient formula for CVaR reduces to the previously known result but with a simpler proof that holds for discrete distributions without additional smoothness assumptions.

3. **Clear Technical Development**: The paper is well-structured and builds logically from the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to the gradient formulas and finally to the actor-critic algorithm. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-justified.

4. **Practical Illustration**: The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure for a specific problem context, showing how different risk-averse policies (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to different investment choices in a trading scenario.

5. **Appropriate Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper provides consistency results for their gradient estimators (Proposition 4.4), which is important for establishing the theoretical soundness of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The numerical example in Section 6 is valuable but relatively brief. The paper lacks comprehensive experiments comparing the performance of their method against existing approaches for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). More extensive experiments would strengthen the paper significantly.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The proposed two-step algorithm (sampling followed by convex programming) might be computationally expensive for large-scale problems. The paper doesn't provide detailed analysis of the computational complexity or scalability of their approach, which is important for practical RL applications.

3. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper doesn't compare their method with recent state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL algorithms beyond citing them as related work. A comparison would help establish the relative merits of their approach.

4. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation Challenges**: The paper mentions the need for a simulator to sample multiple state-transitions from each state (Section 5), but doesn't discuss how this might be addressed in practice or how the approach would perform with limited data.

5. **Underdeveloped Connection to Model Uncertainty**: The conclusion mentions the connection between coherent risk and model uncertainty, but this is only briefly touched upon without substantial development or examples.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations. The authors carefully develop their approach based on the representation theorem for coherent risk measures and provide clear theoretical guarantees. The main weakness is the limited experimental validation, which is somewhat surprising given the paper's focus on practical RL algorithms. The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, but would be stronger with more empirical results.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly explains the technical concepts. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, and the notation is consistent throughout. The main contributions are clearly stated in the introduction. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details that would be helpful for reproduction, which is good practice.

## Originality

The paper's main contribution is original in that it provides a unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures as previous work has done. While the connection between coherent risk and RL has been explored before, this paper provides a novel theoretical foundation that generalizes previous results. The specific gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are new and represent a significant step forward in the field.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in RL: risk-sensitive decision making. By providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures, it enables practitioners to more flexibly design risk criteria for their specific applications. The numerical example effectively demonstrates this flexibility. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and comparison with existing methods. The connection to model uncertainty mentioned in the conclusion is promising but not fully developed.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is strong and represents a significant advancement in the field of risk-sensitive RL. The unified framework for coherent risk measures is novel and well-justified, and the paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for future work in this area.

While the paper would be strengthened by more extensive experimental validation, the theoretical contribution alone is substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper clearly advances the state of the art by providing a more general approach to risk-sensitive RL than previous work.

## List of Arguments for Acceptance

- Provides a unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures
- Theoretical results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are novel and well-justified
- Clearly demonstrates how the approach generalizes previous work on specific risk measures
- Provides theoretical guarantees for the gradient estimators
- Includes a meaningful numerical example illustrating the importance of risk measure selection

## List of Arguments Against Acceptance

- Limited experimental validation compared to theoretical contribution
- Lacks comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL methods
- Does not thoroughly address computational complexity and scalability
- Connection to model uncertainty is underdeveloped

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include more extensive experimental results comparing the proposed approach with existing methods for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance).

2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the two-step sampling and convex programming approach, and how it scales with problem size.

3. Expand the numerical example to include more concrete performance metrics and comparisons.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the approach could be adapted to work with single trajectory rollouts rather than requiring multiple state transitions from each state.

5. Develop the connection to model uncertainty mentioned in the conclusion into a more substantial discussion or potential future work.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the field of risk-sensitive RL. With the suggested improvements, particularly in experimental validation, it would be an excellent NIPS paper.
