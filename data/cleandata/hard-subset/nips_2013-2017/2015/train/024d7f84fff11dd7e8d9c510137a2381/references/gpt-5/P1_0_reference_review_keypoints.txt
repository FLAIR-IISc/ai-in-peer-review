- Develops policy-gradient methods for coherent risk objectives, covering static coherent risk measures and dynamic Markov coherent risks with time consistency.  
- Provides a generic gradient formula for static coherent risks via dual (risk-envelope) representation and an envelope theorem, yielding likelihood-ratio estimators.  
- Proposes an SAA-based estimator combining Monte Carlo sampling with a small convex program to compute dual variables and proves consistency.  
- Derives a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that uses ξ-weighted Markov chains and a stage-wise hθ term, enabling an actor-critic approach.  
- Extends and unifies prior static risk work (CVaR, mean-variance) and recovers CVaR gradients in discrete settings.  
- Builds on dynamic coherent risk and robust MDP literature; the Markov coherent policy gradient theorem appears novel.  
- Uses standard stochastic programming tools (envelope theorems, SAA) in a new RL context.  
- Theory: static gradient formula and SAA consistency are sound; dynamic theorem is a strong analogue of classical policy gradient.  
- Assumptions: finite probability spaces, differentiable risk envelopes, and bounded score functions simplify analysis but limit generality.  
- Practical requirements: simulator must allow multiple next-state samples and ξ-weighted sampling (likely requiring importance sampling in practice).  
- Empirics: limited validation—only small static experiments in main text and no dynamic MDP experiments presented, weakening practical credibility.  
- Clarity: paper is well written with helpful examples, but some dependencies, saddle-point/quantile issues, and computational scalability need clarification.  
- Originality: strong—unifying static gradient formula and Markov coherent policy gradient are novel contributions.  
- Significance: potentially high for risk-sensitive RL, but immediate impact limited by weak empirical support.  
- Strengths: unified theoretical framework, subsumes prior special cases, consistent SAA estimator, new dynamic gradient theorem, and links to robust MDPs.  
- Weaknesses: minimal empirical validation, computational/practical concerns (per-state convex programs, ξ-sampling, variance/sample complexity), and restrictive assumptions.  
- Suggested improvements: add at least one dynamic MDP experiment with baselines, include pseudocode and complexity analysis, discuss importance sampling and variance, and clarify non-unique saddle-point handling.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, general theoretical contributions that simplify and extend prior results with potential broad impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation for dynamic setting, unclear scalability/practicality, and restrictive assumptions.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — strong theoretical contributions but needs stronger empirical support and practical discussion.
