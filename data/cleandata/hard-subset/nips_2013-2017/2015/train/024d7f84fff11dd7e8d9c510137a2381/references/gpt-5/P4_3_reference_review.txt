Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies reinforcement learning with risk-sensitive objectives using the class of coherent risk measures. It presents (i) a general formula for the gradient of static coherent risk ρ(Z), enabling sampling-based estimators via the likelihood-ratio trick and dual representations of risk; (ii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) based algorithm that combines Monte Carlo with a convex program to compute the saddle-point quantities required by the gradient formula and a consistency result; and (iii) a policy-gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, yielding an actor-critic method that leverages the robust MDP equivalence to approximate the risk-sensitive value function and to estimate gradients using trajectories from ξ-weighted transitions.

The work builds on the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and the dynamic programming framework for Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński 2010). It generalizes prior RL work focused on specific risks: exponential utility (Borkar), mean-variance (e.g., Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), and CVaR (Rockafellar & Uryasev for static optimization; Chow & Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al. for RL). The dynamic gradient result connects to the robust MDP equivalence for dynamic coherent risk (e.g., Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian) and to classic policy gradient/actor-critic theory (Sutton et al.; Konda & Tsitsiklis).

Quality (technical soundness)
- Static risk: Theorem 4.2 uses the dual representation to express ρ(Z) as a convex program over test densities and applies the envelope theorem plus a likelihood-ratio identity to obtain ∇θρ(Z) in terms of the saddle-point ξ*, λ*. The derivations are plausible under the stated assumptions (finite Ω, Slater condition, differentiability of constraints w.r.t. Pθ, bounded log-derivative). The recovery of the CVaR gradient in discrete settings and an expression for mean-semideviation support correctness. Proposition 4.4 provides consistency for the SAA-based estimator under mild compactness/continuity conditions.
- Dynamic risk: Theorem 5.2 derives a policy-gradient identity for Markov coherent risk by combining the risk-sensitive Bellman equation with the static gradient structure at each state. The resulting gradient uses expectations under the ξ*-reweighted kernel and a per-stage term hθ involving λ* and derivatives of envelope constraints w.r.t. the transition distribution p. The assumptions (bounded likelihood ratios, differentiability w.r.t. p, existence of saddle points per state) are clearly stated.

Potential technical concerns or gaps:
- The dynamic gradient requires sampling from ξ*-weighted dynamics. In many RL settings the environment kernel cannot be altered; unbiased estimation then needs importance sampling or acceptance-rejection, which is not detailed in the main text (noted as a simulator requirement). Clarifying how to obtain unbiased/low-variance estimates under a fixed simulator would strengthen the contribution.
- The function approximation-based critic relies on robust MDP methods; assumptions ensuring existence/uniqueness and error propagation to the actor are deferred to the supplement. A concise summary of those conditions in the main text would aid assessment.
- Extensions beyond finite Ω (to Lp spaces) are stated but omitted. Since the dynamic setting naturally has infinite spaces, a brief sketch of needed measurability/compactness conditions would improve completeness.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The use of duality and envelope theorem is explained, and the separation into static and dynamic parts is clear. Notation is heavy in places; suggestions:
- Define more explicitly the dependence of U(x, Pθ(·|x)) on state x in early sections.
- In Theorem 5.2, clarify that Pθ(·|x)ξ* is normalized by the constraint Eξ[1]=1, hence is a proper kernel.
- Specify precisely the derivatives dfi/dp and dge/dp and their dimensions.
- State the computational steps of the actor-critic in the main text at a higher level (even pseudocode), including how many next-state samples are used to estimate hθ.

Originality
The static gradient formula that applies to the entire coherent class and the SAA/KKT-based estimator appear novel and unify several known special cases (e.g., CVaR). The policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk also appears new and nontrivial. The work differentiates itself from prior RL literature that focused on particular risk measures or linear dynamics, and from stochastic programming results where policy parameters do not affect dynamics.

Significance
Conceptually, the paper broadens risk-sensitive RL by providing general-purpose gradient estimators for coherent risks, potentially enabling practitioners to choose risk measures matching domain needs. Technically, it offers reusable tools (gradient identities and an SAA approach) and a pathway to actor-critic methods for time-consistent risks. However, empirical evidence is limited: the single static bandit-like trading example illustrates flexibility but does not evaluate scalability, variance, sample efficiency, or performance on dynamic MDPs. Given the computational cost of solving a convex program per batch (static) and per state (dynamic), and the need to obtain KKT multipliers, more substantial experiments would be useful to establish practicality.

Pros for acceptance
- General, elegant gradient formulas covering the full coherent risk class.
- Unification and simplification of earlier derivations (e.g., CVaR gradient).
- Extension to dynamic, time-consistent risks with a policy-gradient theorem and an actor-critic framework.
- Sound use of convex duality and envelope theorem; consistency result for SAA estimator.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is minimal and does not demonstrate the dynamic method or scalability.
- Practical challenges (sampling from ξ*-weighted kernels; computational overhead per state) are acknowledged but not resolved in the main paper.
- Some technical assumptions (differentiability w.r.t. transition probabilities; existence of saddle points in dynamic case) may limit applicability; discussion of their prevalence in common coherent risks and MDPs could be expanded.

Questions for the authors
- How can one implement sampling under ξ*-weighted transitions when the simulator/environment kernel cannot be modified? Can importance sampling be incorporated with controlled variance?
- What is the computational cost per iteration for the actor (number/size of convex programs, KKT solves) and the critic on moderately sized MDPs?
- Can you report experiments on a dynamic MDP (e.g., gridworld, inventory control) comparing different coherent risks, including runtime and variance?
- For continuous state/action spaces, what function approximation architectures do you use and what assumptions ensure convergence?

Suggestions
- Include a short algorithmic summary of the dynamic actor-critic in the main text and a small dynamic experiment.
- Provide guidance on choosing and specifying risk envelopes U for popular coherent risks, with explicit derivatives dfi/dp.
- Discuss variance reduction (e.g., baselines, control variates, importance sampling) tailored to tail-sensitive risks like CVaR.

Overall assessment
A technically solid and conceptually broad contribution that unifies and extends policy gradients to coherent risk measures, including a novel dynamic result. The main weakness is the limited empirical evidence and practical guidance for implementation at scale. With stronger experiments and clarification on sampling under ξ*-weighted dynamics, the work would be highly impactful.
