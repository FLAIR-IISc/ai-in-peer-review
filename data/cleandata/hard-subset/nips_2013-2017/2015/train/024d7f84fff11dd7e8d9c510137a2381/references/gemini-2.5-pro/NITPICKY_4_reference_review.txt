**Summary**

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities (the risk envelope). Using this representation and the envelope theorem, they derive a general formula for the policy gradient of a static coherent risk measure. This formula is then instantiated for specific examples like CVaR and mean-semideviation, and a general algorithm is proposed that estimates the gradient by solving a sample-average approximation of the dual problem. The paper extends this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, deriving a corresponding policy gradient theorem and outlining an actor-critic algorithm. A simple numerical example on a bandit problem illustrates the flexibility of using different risk measures.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this work is its ambition to provide a unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant step beyond prior work that has largely focused on individual risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). The approach is theoretically elegant and provides a principled way to connect policy gradients with the dual representation of coherent risk.
2.  **Novel Gradient Formula:** Theorem 4.2 presents a novel and general formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure. The derivation, based on the envelope theorem, is clean and powerful. The ability of this formula to re-derive the CVaR policy gradient from [30] with a simpler proof and weaker assumptions highlights its utility.
3.  **Extension to Dynamic Risk:** The paper's extension to time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures is an important conceptual contribution. The derivation of a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) analogous to the standard one is a key theoretical result that connects the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are promising, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in its presentation, technical completeness, and experimental validation.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**

*   **Imprecise Definitions:** The preliminaries in Section 2 are not sufficiently clear. The definition of the risk envelope `U ⊂ B` and the notation `U(Pθ)` are confusing. If `U` is a set of probability distributions, how is it related to the densities `ξ` w.r.t `Pθ`? The notation `Eξ[Z]` is also defined in a slightly non-standard way. The crucial dependence of the risk envelope on the state `x` in the MDP setting is relegated to a brief footnote (Footnote 3), but it is a central element of the dynamic formulation in Section 5.
*   **Vague Claims:** The paper repeatedly uses phrases like "Our results may easily be extended" (Footnote 1) and "Our results can be extended" (Footnote 2) without providing any detail or justification. Such statements weaken the paper's claims and leave the reader to guess the technical steps involved.
*   **Missing Details for Core Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm proposed in Section 5 is described at a very high level. Key components, such as the "two-phase sampling procedure" for the actor and the function approximation scheme for the critic, are not specified. The paper states that "Space limitation restricts us from specifying the full details" and defers the algorithm and its analysis to supplementary material. For a paper whose contribution is an algorithm, this is a major omission in the main text.
*   **Formatting Issues:** There is no caption for Figure 1, making the plots difficult to interpret without carefully reading the main text.

**2. Technical Soundness and Completeness:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** Assumption 2.2, particularly the differentiability of constraints w.r.t. the probability measure `p`, is quite strong and its validity for a broad class of risk measures is asserted rather than demonstrated. The assumptions for the consistency of the SAA estimator (Proposition 4.4) are numerous and presented without sufficient intuition, making it difficult to assess how restrictive they are.
*   **Incomplete Treatment of the Dynamic Case:** The paper claims to handle large or continuous MDPs, but the proposed method relies on a critic that solves the risk-sensitive Bellman equation (9). This is intractable in large state spaces. While the paper alludes to using function approximation and modifying an algorithm from [31], no details are provided. This makes the contribution for the dynamic case feel incomplete and largely theoretical.
*   **Practical Limitations:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm requires a simulator to draw multiple next-state samples from each state visited along a trajectory. The authors acknowledge that extending this to a more practical single-trajectory setting is future work. This is a significant practical limitation compared to standard actor-critic methods.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**

*   **Oversimplified Experiment:** The numerical illustration in Section 6 is a simple multi-armed bandit problem, not a sequential decision-making problem (MDP). This experiment completely fails to validate the main theoretical contribution for the dynamic case (Section 5 and Theorem 5.2).
*   **Weak Comparison:** The experiment compares a risk-neutral agent, the proposed mean-semideviation agent, and a mean-variance agent. The chosen problem, with a heavy-tailed asset (infinite variance), is one where mean-variance is known to perform poorly. The result is therefore predictable and does not provide strong evidence for the superiority of the proposed framework over other robust risk-sensitive approaches in a more general setting.
*   **Missed Opportunity:** A much more convincing experiment would have been to validate the general SAA-based gradient estimator from Section 4.3. For instance, comparing the SAA estimator for CVaR against the analytical one on a challenging MDP would have directly tested the core algorithmic contribution of the paper. The authors mention this comparison is in the supplement, but it should be in the main paper to substantiate their claims.

**Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant unifying framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures.
*   The general gradient formula for static risk (Thm 4.2) is a strong theoretical contribution.
*   The extension to dynamic risk is conceptually interesting and lays theoretical groundwork.

**Con:**
*   The paper is poorly written in several parts, with imprecise notation and unsubstantiated claims.
*   The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is underspecified and its practical feasibility is questionable.
*   The experimental validation is extremely weak and does not support the main contributions regarding sequential decision-making.
*   Key algorithmic details and analysis are relegated to the supplement, making the main paper feel incomplete.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper is built on a strong and appealing theoretical idea, the execution is not up to the standards of NIPS. The lack of clarity, the incomplete description of the main algorithm for the dynamic case, and the profoundly weak experimental validation are major issues. The accumulation of these flaws, from minor formatting errors to major omissions in the algorithmic description, leads me to recommend **rejection**. The work has significant potential, but it requires a major revision to clarify the presentation, fully specify the proposed algorithms, and provide convincing empirical evidence of their effectiveness on actual sequential decision-making problems.
