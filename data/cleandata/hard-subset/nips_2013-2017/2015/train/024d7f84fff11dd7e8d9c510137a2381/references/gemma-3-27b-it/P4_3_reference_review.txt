This paper introduces policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, generalizing previous work that focused on specific risk measures. The authors provide a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm for its estimation using convex programming and sampling, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The work aims to provide a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL, offering flexibility in choosing risk preferences.

The paper is well-motivated, placing the work within the broader context of risk-sensitive optimization and its applications in finance and operations research. The authors clearly define coherent risk measures and their desirable properties, including time consistency. The contributions – a gradient formula, an estimation algorithm, and a policy gradient theorem – are well-defined and logically presented. The connection to robust MDPs and the discussion of model uncertainty in the conclusion are valuable. The numerical example highlighting the different choices driven by different risk measures is insightful. 

Strengths:
* **Generality:** The approach applies to the whole class of coherent risk measures, a significant generalization of existing methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations of the gradient formulas appear rigorous and build upon established results from stochastic programming and optimization. The use of the envelope theorem is appropriate.
* **Completeness:** The paper addresses both static and dynamic risk objectives and proposes algorithms for each.
* **Practicality:** The sampling-based estimation algorithms are well-suited for large-scale MDPs where exact solutions are intractable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of key concepts.

Weaknesses:
* **Computational Complexity:** The proposed algorithms rely on solving convex optimization problems at each iteration, which could be computationally expensive for high-dimensional state/action spaces, even with sampling. This is acknowledged, but a more detailed discussion of the computational cost, especially concerning the convex programming step, would be beneficial.
* **Assumption 2.2:** The assumption regarding the form of the risk envelope (Assumption 2.2), while common, might be restrictive for some coherent risk measures. The authors acknowledge this but further discussion on the limitations and possible extensions would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example is somewhat limited in scope. While illustrative, it lacks a more comprehensive comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on standard benchmark environments. Showing scalability to larger problems would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Actor-Critic Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm is high-level. More specifics on the function approximation methods used for the value function and the details of the sampling procedure would improve understanding and reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution by providing a general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods.
* The results generalize existing work and offer a flexible approach for incorporating various risk preferences into RL.
* The proposed algorithms are well-motivated and provide a path towards solving complex risk-sensitive MDPs.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms could limit their practical applicability.
* The limited empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the performance and scalability of the methods.
* The reliance on Assumption 2.2 might restrict the applicability of the framework to certain coherent risk measures.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While the computational complexity and limited empirical evaluation are concerns, the generality and theoretical soundness of the approach are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, specifically, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost, an assessment of the impact of Assumption 2.2, and an expanded empirical evaluation.
