This paper presents a policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, generalizing prior work by applying to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors derive novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, offering a unified approach to risk-sensitive RL. The technical contributions – a gradient formula for static coherent risk suitable for sampling, a sampling-based algorithm for the static case, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk – are significant and well-motivated.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively establishes the context, highlighting the importance of risk-sensitive RL and the benefits of utilizing coherent risk measures. The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the authors do a good job of building up the necessary background in Section 2. The related work section is comprehensive, accurately placing this work within the broader research landscape. The inclusion of a numerical example in Section 6 is helpful for illustrating the practical implications of the proposed framework.

A key strength is the generality of the approach. By working with the abstract notion of coherent risk, the authors provide a unifying framework that encompasses many specific risk measures as special cases. This is a valuable contribution, as it avoids the need to re-derive results for each individual risk measure. The derivation of the gradient formulas is mathematically sound, and the connection to robust MDPs is insightful. The use of sampling-based methods addresses the scalability issues associated with dynamic programming approaches, making the method applicable to larger and more complex problems.

However, there are also some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed experimental evaluation beyond the single example in Section 6. It would be beneficial to demonstrate the performance of the proposed algorithms on a wider range of benchmark tasks, comparing them against existing risk-sensitive RL methods. Specifically, comparisons to methods based on CVaR, which has been extensively studied, would be particularly informative. The numerical example, while illustrative, is limited in scope and doesn’t provide a rigorous assessment of the algorithm's performance.

The assumptions made throughout the paper, particularly Assumption 2.2 regarding the structure of the risk envelope, could be further justified. While the authors state that it holds for many popular coherent risk measures, a more detailed discussion of the conditions under which it is valid would be helpful. Furthermore, the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly the convex programming step in the static case, is not fully addressed. A discussion of the scalability of the algorithms to high-dimensional state and action spaces would be valuable.

The paper also mentions the potential for extending the approach to handle model uncertainty, but this is only briefly touched upon. Expanding on this idea and outlining a potential research agenda for addressing model uncertainty would strengthen the conclusion. The discussion on the actor-critic implementation is also somewhat brief, leaving the reader with a high-level understanding but lacking specific implementation details.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel and general framework for risk-sensitive RL based on coherent risk measures.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations are mathematically rigorous and well-motivated.
*   **Generality:** The approach encompasses a wide class of risk measures, providing a unifying perspective.
*   **Scalability:** The use of sampling-based methods addresses scalability issues.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The lack of a comprehensive experimental evaluation limits the ability to assess the practical performance of the algorithms.
*   **Assumption Justification:** The assumptions made regarding the risk envelope could be further justified.
*   **Computational Complexity:** A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms is needed.
*   **Limited Implementation Details:** The actor-critic implementation is briefly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical results are significant, and the proposed framework has the potential to be widely applicable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I would suggest:

1.  **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include results on a wider range of benchmark tasks and compare against existing risk-sensitive RL methods.
2.  **Provide a more detailed justification for Assumption 2.2:** Discuss the conditions under which it is valid and provide examples of risk measures that satisfy it.
3.  **Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithms:** Analyze the scalability of the algorithms to high-dimensional state and action spaces.
4.  **Elaborate on the actor-critic implementation:** Provide more specific implementation details.
