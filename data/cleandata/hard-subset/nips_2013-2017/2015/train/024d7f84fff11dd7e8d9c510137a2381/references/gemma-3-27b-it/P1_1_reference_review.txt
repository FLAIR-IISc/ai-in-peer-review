## Peer Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a general framework for policy gradient algorithms applicable to a broad class of coherent risk measures. The work is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and provides useful extensions and generalizations of existing methods. While the presentation could benefit from some clarity improvements, the significance of tackling a general coherent risk framework with policy gradients, and the accompanying theoretical results, warrant a strong positive recommendation for acceptance.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper addresses the problem of optimizing policies in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) under coherent risk measures, extending beyond traditional expected cost objectives. It argues that different risk measures are appropriate for different problems, and focuses on the desirable properties of *coherent* risk measures (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, positive homogeneity), alongside *time consistency* for dynamic settings. The core contribution is a unified framework for deriving policy gradient algorithms for both static and dynamic coherent risk, leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk (expressed as a worst-case expectation).  The paper presents novel gradient formulas for static coherent risk, an algorithm for estimating gradients involving convex programming, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, leading to a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.  Crucially, it shows that many prior results are special cases of their more general framework.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The framework’s ability to handle the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant strength, offering flexibility and unifying existing approaches. This is a departure from previous work focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is grounded in solid mathematical foundations. The derivations of the gradient formulas and the consistency results appear sound (though a more thorough review of the proofs would be ideal).
* **Novelty:** The gradient formulas presented, particularly for the static case, represent a novel contribution. The application of the envelope theorem to derive these gradients is clever.
* **Practicality:** The proposed algorithms, while theoretically focused, are presented in a way that suggests they can be implemented and used in practice.  The discussion of sampling-based estimation addresses the scalability concerns that arise with coherent risk calculations. The connection to robust MDPs and leveraging existing approximation techniques is smart.
* **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively motivates the need for risk-sensitive RL and the advantages of coherent risk measures.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper carefully positions itself within the existing literature, acknowledging and generalizing previous results.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Organization:** While the mathematical development is rigorous, the presentation is occasionally dense and difficult to follow. Some sections (e.g., the discussion of Assumption 2.2) could benefit from more intuitive explanations and motivating examples. The notation can be a bit overwhelming.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The numerical illustration is limited in scope. While it demonstrates the potential of the approach, a more comprehensive evaluation on benchmark MDPs, comparing performance against standard policy gradient methods and existing risk-sensitive algorithms, would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost:** The use of convex programming within the gradient estimation algorithm (for the static case) raises concerns about computational cost, especially for large-scale problems. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., approximation techniques) would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The reliance on Assumption 2.2 (regarding the structure of the risk envelope) might be restrictive in some applications. While the authors argue that it holds for many common risk measures, acknowledging its limitations and exploring potential extensions would be beneficial.



**4. Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported, though the proofs are not fully detailed within the manuscript. It represents a complete piece of work within its scope.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The presentation could be improved in terms of clarity and organization. More intuitive explanations and examples would be helpful.
* **Originality (8/10):** The framework and gradient formulas are novel and provide a significant generalization of existing work.
* **Significance (9/10):** The results are important and have the potential to significantly impact the field of risk-sensitive RL. The generality of the approach makes it broadly applicable, and the connection to robust MDPs opens up new avenues for research.

**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Provides a highly general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradient algorithms.
* Develops novel and theoretically sound gradient formulas.
* Unifies and generalizes previous work in the field.
* Addresses a significant and challenging problem in reinforcement learning.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical evaluation.
* Potential computational cost concerns.
* Presentation could be clearer and more accessible.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  Despite the minor weaknesses in clarity and the limited empirical evaluation, the originality, significance, and theoretical quality of this work are substantial.  The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and general approach. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS program. I recommend the authors address the clarity issues and consider expanding the empirical evaluation in a future version.
