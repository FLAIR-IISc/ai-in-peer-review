This paper presents policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, generalizing previous work to a broader class of risk measures. The authors formulate the risk-sensitive policy gradient under a coherent-risk framework, providing a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm with consistency results, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with an actor-critic algorithm. While the ideas are interesting and aim for a valuable generalization, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws and inconsistencies that, cumulatively, significantly detract from its overall quality.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:** The core idea—applying policy gradients to coherent risk measures—is a logical extension of existing risk-sensitive RL. Prior work, as acknowledged by the authors, has largely focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). This paper attempts to unify these approaches under the more general framework of coherent risk, leveraging the theoretical properties of such measures. The connection to robust MDPs (via Artzner et al.'s work and [19]) is also well-pointed out.

**Strengths:**
* **Generality:** The focus on the entire class of coherent risk measures is a notable strength. A unified approach offers potential benefits in terms of flexibility and applicability.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors ground their work in established results from coherent risk theory (Artzner et al., Ruszczynski & Shapiro), providing a solid theoretical basis.
* **Policy Gradient Formulation:** The derivation of policy gradient formulas, specifically Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2, are central contributions.
* **Connection to Robust MDPs:** Recognizing the link between coherent risk and robust MDPs provides an alternative perspective and potentially opens avenues for exploiting existing techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
* **Notational Inconsistency:** Throughout the paper, the notation is inconsistent. For example, Z is sometimes used as a generic random variable and other times seemingly representing a specific trajectory. The superscript 'θ' is frequently dropped without clear justification, making it difficult to track dependencies. This is particularly problematic in the proofs, which are relegated to the supplementary material.
* **Lack of Clarity in Assumptions:** Assumption 2.2, while aiming to be general, is vaguely stated. The condition regarding the smoothness of the constraints (twice differentiability) feels ad-hoc and lacks proper motivation. Furthermore, the requirement of a “strictly feasible point” is not adequately explained.
* **Missing Details in Algorithm Description:** The actor-critic algorithm in Section 5 lacks concrete details. The two-phase sampling procedure is mentioned but not fully elaborated. How are the hyperparameters tuned? How is the function approximation for Vθ implemented? Without this information, it’s difficult to assess the practicality of the approach.
* **Numerical Illustration Weakness:** The numerical example in Section 6, while showcasing the potential of the approach, is superficial. The experimental setup is minimal, and the analysis of the results is limited to qualitative observations. No quantitative comparisons are made with baseline algorithms. The conclusion that policy π3 choosing A1 is "counter-intuitive" is subjective and not rigorously supported.
* **Overly Ambitious Scope:** The paper attempts to cover a wide range of theoretical aspects and algorithmic details. This breadth comes at the cost of depth and rigor. Many claims are made without sufficient justification or supporting evidence.
* **Redundancy & Verbosity:** Several sections contain repetitive statements and lengthy expositions of established concepts. The introduction, in particular, could be significantly condensed.
* **Typos & Grammatical Errors:** Numerous typos and grammatical errors detract from the paper's professionalism. Examples include "ξPθ∈U" instead of "ξ ∈ U(Pθ)" and inconsistent use of capitalization.
* **Missing Proofs:** The reliance on supplementary material for crucial proofs is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. Proofs should be either included in the main text or presented with exceptional clarity and justification for their omission.
* **Incomplete References:** Some citations are missing crucial information (e.g., year of publication).

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**
* The paper tackles a theoretically interesting problem: extending risk-sensitive RL to a broader class of risk measures.
* The derivations of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 & 5.2) are potentially valuable contributions.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**
* The numerous flaws in notation, clarity, and completeness severely undermine the paper's quality.
* The numerical illustration is inadequate to support the claims made.
* The overly ambitious scope and lack of depth prevent the paper from making a significant contribution.
* The paper requires substantial revision and polishing before it could be considered for publication.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the ideas presented in this paper are intriguing, the numerous flaws and inconsistencies outweigh the potential benefits. The paper lacks the rigor, clarity, and completeness expected of a NIPS publication. The authors need to address the aforementioned weaknesses, provide a more thorough experimental evaluation, and significantly improve the presentation of their work before resubmission. It feels like a draft of a potentially strong paper rather than a finished product suitable for NIPS.
