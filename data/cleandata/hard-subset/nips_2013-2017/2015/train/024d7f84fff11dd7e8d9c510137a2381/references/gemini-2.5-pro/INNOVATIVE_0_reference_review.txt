This paper presents a unified policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning, a significant generalization over prior work that has largely focused on specific, individual risk measures like mean-variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The core idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the "risk envelope"). The authors use this representation, in conjunction with the Envelope theorem, to derive a general formula for the policy gradient of a static coherent risk measure. They then propose a practical algorithm that estimates this gradient by solving a convex program on a batch of samples to find the worst-case density. This framework is then extended to the dynamic, time-consistent case of Markov coherent risk measures, for which the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem and outline a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its remarkable novelty and ambition. By tackling the entire class of coherent risk measures, the authors move beyond the piecemeal approach of prior work and provide a single, elegant framework that subsumes many previous results. This unification is a significant conceptual advance for the field of risk-sensitive RL.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main contribution is introducing a new, general methodology rather than an incremental improvement. The approach of using the dual formulation and the Envelope theorem is a powerful and principled way to derive policy gradients for this broad class of objectives. This work has the potential to fundamentally change how risk is handled in policy gradient methods, empowering researchers and practitioners to design and use risk measures tailored to their specific problems, rather than being confined to the few that have been previously studied. The numerical example, though simple, effectively illustrates this key point: the choice of risk measure (semideviation vs. standard deviation) can lead to qualitatively different and more rational behaviors, especially in the presence of heavy-tailed distributions.

*   **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical derivations appear sound and are a clear highlight of the paper. Theorem 4.2 provides a clean and general gradient formula for static risk, from which results for specific measures like CVaR can be re-derived with greater simplicity. The extension to the dynamic setting in Theorem 5.2, yielding a new risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem, is a substantial theoretical achievement.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation for using coherent risk is clear, and the theoretical development, from the dual representation to the final gradient formulas, is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's weaknesses are primarily in its empirical validation and the completeness of the proposed algorithms, which is a common trade-off in papers that introduce a bold new theoretical framework.

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are confined to a single, simple multi-armed bandit problem for the static risk case. While illustrative, this falls short of a convincing demonstration of the algorithm's effectiveness on a standard reinforcement learning task. The more complex and arguably more interesting contribution—the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk—is not empirically evaluated at all.

*   **Practical Concerns:** The general gradient estimation algorithm requires solving a convex optimization problem at each step, with the number of variables scaling with the sample size N. The practical computational cost and scalability of this approach, especially for large N or high-frequency gradient updates, are not discussed or evaluated.

*   **Incomplete Algorithm Description:** The details of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case are largely deferred to the supplementary material. While space constraints are understandable, this makes the second half of the paper feel more like a sketch of an idea than a fully fleshed-out and validated contribution. The reliance on a simulator that can provide multiple next-state transitions from any given state also limits its direct applicability to standard single-trajectory online RL settings.

**Summary of evaluation:**

This is precisely the kind of paper that pushes the field forward. It trades comprehensive, but potentially incremental, empirical validation for a bold, novel, and unifying theoretical framework. The idea of a general-purpose policy gradient method for any coherent risk measure is powerful and opens up numerous avenues for future research, both in terms of theoretical extensions and practical applications. The weaknesses, particularly the lack of experiments, are significant but, in my view, are outweighed by the sheer novelty and importance of the core conceptual contribution. This work provides the foundation upon which many future empirical papers can be built.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty:** Proposes a completely new and general framework for policy gradients with coherent risk, unifying a fragmented area of research.
2.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** Derives new policy gradient theorems for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures using elegant mathematical tools.
3.  **Potential for High Impact:** Enables the use of a vast range of problem-specific risk measures in RL, which could be critical for real-world applications in finance, operations research, and robotics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experiments are minimal and do not test the main algorithmic contribution for the dynamic RL setting.
2.  **Practical Scalability is Unclear:** The proposed method relies on solving a convex program at each iteration, and its practicality for large-scale problems is not established.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

The conceptual leap presented in this paper is significant and exciting. While the empirical side is underdeveloped, the theoretical foundation is strong and points toward a promising new direction for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. This is a paper that introduces a major new idea, and its value lies in opening doors for future work.
