

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper proposes policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic. The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to derive gradient formulas that can be implemented via sampling and convex programming. The work aims to unify previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by establishing a general framework for policy gradients under coherent risk measures. The key technical contribution is Theorem 4.2, which provides a gradient formula for static coherent risk that is convenient for sampling-based estimation. The extension to dynamic risk in Theorem 5.2 generalizes the standard policy gradient theorem to coherent risk measures. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the flexibility of the coherent risk framework in selecting appropriate risk measures for different problem settings.

## Weaknesses

Despite the theoretical contribution, the paper contains several significant technical and presentation flaws that undermine its quality:

1. **Inconsistent notation and definitions**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, in Theorem 4.2, the gradient formula contains "Eξ∗θ [∇θ logP(ω)(Z − λ∗,Pθ)]" where "P(ω)" should be "Pθ(ω)" for consistency with the notation established earlier. The risk envelope notation is also inconsistent (U(Pθ), U, U(Pθ) with different arguments).

2. **Mathematical errors**: The Pareto distribution is incorrectly defined in Section 6: "f(z) = αzα+1 ∀z > 1" should be "f(z) = αz^(-α-1) for z > 1" (standard Pareto density). This is a significant error that undermines the numerical example's credibility.

3. **Missing citations**: Reference [19] is cited in the text ("Reference [19] showed that an MDP with a dynamic coherent risk objective is essentially a robust MDP") but is not included in the references list, which is a serious omission.

4. **Technical inaccuracies**: The paper states "the mean of the return from A3 is 3 and its variance is infinite" for a Pareto distribution with α=1.5, which is correct, but the description of the distribution is incorrect as noted above.

5. **Formatting issues**: The references contain numerous formatting errors, including trailing commas (e.g., "Spectral measures of risk: a coherent representation of subjective risk aversion, C. Acerbi, Journal of Banking & Finance, 26(7):1505–1518, , ") and inconsistent citation styles.

6. **Unjustified assumptions**: The paper claims "we assume that the risk envelope U(Pθ) is given in a canonical convex programming formulation" without clarifying what "canonical" means in this context, which is problematic for reproducibility.

7. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper refers to "risk-sensitive policy gradient" but then uses "risk-sensitive value-function" and "risk-sensitive Bellman equation" without explaining how these differ from standard RL terminology.

8. **Methodological gap**: The paper claims "our approach allows to rederive them in greater generality and simplicity" but provides no concrete examples of rederiving previous results, making this claim unsubstantiated.

9. **Theoretical gap**: The paper assumes "a solution to (7) may be computed efficiently" but doesn't address how the computational complexity scales with the number of samples N, which is critical for practical implementation.

10. **Terminology confusion**: The paper uses "cost" throughout but then refers to "reward" in the trading example (e.g., "maxθ E[Z]"), which is inconsistent with the cost minimization framework established in the paper.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning and contains some theoretically interesting contributions, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues significantly undermine its quality. The mathematical errors in the numerical example are particularly concerning as they directly impact the paper's main illustration. The missing citation of [19] is a serious oversight in a paper that heavily relies on this reference.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to fix the mathematical errors, standardize notation, clarify assumptions, and properly cite all referenced works. Without these corrections, the paper's claims cannot be properly evaluated or reproduced.

Given the number and severity of these issues, I recommend rejection. While the paper's main idea is potentially valuable, the current presentation contains too many fundamental errors and inconsistencies to be considered for publication at NIPS. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting.
