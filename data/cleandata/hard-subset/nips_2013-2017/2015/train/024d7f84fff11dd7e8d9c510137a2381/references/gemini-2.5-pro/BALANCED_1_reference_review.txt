**Summary**

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The work generalizes previous approaches that have been tailored to specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). The authors tackle two settings: 1) optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted cost, and 2) optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

For the static case, the key contribution is a new, general formula for the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2). This formula is derived using the dual representation of coherent risk measures, where the risk is the expected value under a worst-case probability distribution from a "risk envelope" set. By applying the Envelope Theorem, the authors express the gradient in terms of the optimal solution (the worst-case distribution and its corresponding Lagrange multipliers) to this dual problem. They show this framework elegantly recovers known results for CVaR and provides a new gradient formula for the mean-semideviation risk. For general coherent risks where the dual solution is not analytic, they propose a practical algorithm based on Sample Average Approximation (SAA): first, solve a convex program on a batch of samples to estimate the dual solution, then plug this estimate into the gradient formula. They provide a consistency proof for this estimator.

For the dynamic case, the authors derive a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This result connects the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function that satisfies a risk-sensitive Bellman equation. The resulting gradient formula resembles the standard policy gradient theorem but involves an expectation over trajectories generated by a modified MDP, where transition probabilities are re-weighted by the worst-case distribution from the risk envelope. Based on this theorem, they outline a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The paper concludes with a simple numerical example on a portfolio selection problem, illustrating how the choice of risk measure (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) can lead to different and more intuitive behaviors.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The main strength of this paper is its significant conceptual contribution. It provides a single, principled framework for deriving policy gradients for the entire class of coherent risk measures. This unifies and generalizes a large body of prior work that developed bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures. This level of abstraction is valuable and advances the theoretical foundations of risk-sensitive RL.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Technical Results:** The derivation of the general gradient formulas in Theorem 4.2 (static) and Theorem 5.2 (dynamic) is novel and insightful. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk in conjunction with the Envelope Theorem is a powerful technique that cleanly handles the non-differentiable `max` operator inherent in these risk measures. This approach is not only general but also simplifies the derivation for specific cases like CVaR.

3.  **Constructive and Practical Algorithm:** For the general static case, the paper proposes a concrete two-step algorithm (sampling followed by convex programming) that is applicable to any coherent risk measure whose risk envelope can be specified as a convex set. This provides a clear recipe for practitioners and researchers to apply policy gradients to new or custom-defined coherent risk measures.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation and background on coherent risk, builds the theory logically from the simpler static case to the more complex dynamic case, and clearly states its main results and assumptions.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration is effective at motivating the need for flexible risk measures but is too simple to serve as a proper evaluation of the proposed algorithms. It does not assess the learning performance (e.g., sample complexity, convergence speed, variance of the gradient estimator) or scalability of the proposed methods.
    *   **Suggestion:** To significantly strengthen the paper, I would recommend evaluating the SAA-based algorithm on a more standard RL benchmark (e.g., a continuous control task from MuJoCo or a classic problem like inventory management). This would allow for a comparison of its performance against existing risk-sensitive algorithms (e.g., [21, 29]) and risk-neutral baselines, providing valuable insights into its practical utility.

2.  **Computational Cost of the General Algorithm:** The SAA-based gradient estimator for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem at each gradient update step. The size of this problem scales with the number of samples `N`. For large `N`, which is often necessary for accurate gradient estimation, this could become a computational bottleneck. The paper does not discuss this limitation.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could briefly discuss the computational complexity. Furthermore, it would be interesting to explore whether online or stochastic convex optimization techniques could be applied to the dual problem to obtain estimates of the saddle point (`ξ*`, `λ*`) more efficiently, perhaps avoiding the need for a full batch solution at every step.

3.  **Lack of Detail on the Actor-Critic Algorithm:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is very high-level, with most details and the analysis deferred to the supplementary material. This makes it difficult for the reader to fully grasp and evaluate the proposed algorithm for the dynamic setting from the main paper alone. For instance, the two-phase sampling procedure required by the actor and the reliance on a simulator capable of providing multiple next-state samples are important practical considerations that are only mentioned in passing.
    *   **Suggestion:** While space is limited, including an algorithm box outlining the key steps of the actor-critic loop would greatly improve clarity. A brief discussion on the practical challenges of sampling from the `ξ*`-weighted MDP would also be valuable.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical derivations appear correct and are well-supported. The claims are carefully stated with clear assumptions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The mathematical notation is consistent and the progression of ideas is logical.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on the theory of coherent risk and policy gradients, the synthesis of these concepts to create a general policy gradient framework is a novel and significant contribution. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work by its generality.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for the risk-sensitive RL community. The paper provides both a deeper theoretical understanding and a practical "toolkit" for a broad class of risk measures, likely influencing future research and applications in this area. Researchers will be able to use these ideas to easily experiment with and develop algorithms for a wider variety of risk preferences.

**Decision**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant and original contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical framework is elegant and powerful. While the experimental validation is limited and some algorithmic details could be fleshed out more in the main text, the strength of the theoretical contribution is more than sufficient for acceptance at NeurIPS. I recommend accepting this paper.
