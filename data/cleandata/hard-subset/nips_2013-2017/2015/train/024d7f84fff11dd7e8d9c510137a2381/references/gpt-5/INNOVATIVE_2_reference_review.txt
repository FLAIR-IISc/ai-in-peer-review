Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the entire class of coherent risk measures, in both static and time-consistent dynamic settings. The key technical ideas are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risk using the dual “risk envelope” representation plus the envelope theorem, yielding likelihood-ratio style estimators; (ii) a sample-average approximation (SAA) estimator that combines sampling with a small convex program to obtain the reweighting (density) and Lagrange multipliers needed for the gradient; (iii) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk, expressing the gradient as an expectation under an adversarially reweighted transition kernel and involving a stage-wise cost function that includes derivatives of the envelope constraints; and (iv) an actor-critic scheme leveraging robust-MDP value-function approximation to estimate Vθ and then compute the policy gradient under the reweighted dynamics. The paper unifies and generalizes prior results for particular risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance/semideviation), and includes a small illustrative experiment.

Strengths
- Novelty and unification: Provides a coherent-risk policy gradient framework that subsumes many specific objectives (CVaR, mean-semideviation, spectral risk), both static and dynamic. The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is, to my knowledge, new and conceptually important.
- Clean theory via duality: The use of the risk envelope representation and envelope theorem leads to elegant gradient expressions. Recovering known CVaR gradients as a special case is reassuring and illuminating.
- Practical estimation route: The SAA-based estimator that pairs sampling with a convex program is simple, general, and consistent under mild conditions. It sidesteps analytical derivations for each risk measure.
- Connection to robust MDPs: Tying the critic to robust MDP value approximation is natural and leverages recent tools for scalability.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and carefully situates itself relative to prior work on specific risk criteria and risk-sensitive control.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is minimal: The paper presents only a toy trading example (and defers additional comparisons to the supplement). There is no evaluation on standard RL benchmarks or dynamic-risk tasks demonstrating the actor-critic performance, variance properties, or computational trade-offs.
- Practicality assumptions: The dynamic method requires (a) a simulator that allows multiple next-state samples per state and (b) the ability to sample under ξ-weighted transition kernels, which implies some model access or controlled reweighting; this may limit applicability in purely model-free or single-trajectory settings.
- Computational overhead: The SAA estimator solves a convex program per batch to obtain ξ and multipliers, and the dynamic method requires per-state saddle points; scalability and wall-clock cost vs. specialized methods (e.g., CVaR-specific gradients) are not quantified.
- Finite probability space and smoothness assumptions: While the authors claim extension to Lp spaces, details are omitted. The requirement for derivatives of constraints wrt probabilities (and bounded likelihood ratios) is strong; guidance on implementing these derivatives for common envelopes (beyond CVaR, semideviation) would help.
- Non-uniqueness and sensitivity: The gradient formula depends on a saddle point (ξ*, λ*). Existence is established, but the effect of non-uniqueness or numerically approximate KKT solutions on gradient bias/variance is not discussed.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are the gradient estimates to the choice among multiple saddle points? Does Theorem 4.2 imply invariance to the particular KKT selection, or is there practical variance due to solver tolerances?
2. Can you provide complexity estimates (time/memory) for the SAA convex program vs. closed-form cases (e.g., CVaR), and for the dynamic method per policy update?
3. How would you implement the ξ-weighted sampling in a setting without model access? Is there a tractable importance sampling scheme to approximate the reweighted dynamics?
4. For continuous state/action spaces, do you envision differentiating through the convex program (implicit differentiation) as an alternative to SAA? How does this compare in stability?
5. Could you report experiments on dynamic tasks (e.g., robust gridworlds, LQ problems) to demonstrate the actor-critic’s learning behavior and variance?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add empirical results on at least one dynamic-risk benchmark with comparisons to CVaR-specific algorithms and robust MDP baselines, reporting variance, sample complexity, and runtime.
- Provide practical recipes for computing dfi/dp and dge/dp for several common envelopes (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation) and discuss numerical stability.
- Discuss extensions to single-trajectory or off-policy settings using importance sampling and control variates, as these are common in RL practice.
- Clarify the role of the finite-space assumption and outline key steps for the Lp extension.
- Include an analysis or empirical study of the impact of approximate saddle points on the gradient accuracy.

Relation to prior work and significance
The paper goes beyond prior RL work that targets specific risk measures by delivering a general framework and deriving a dynamic policy gradient theorem for time-consistent coherent risk. This is a meaningful conceptual advance that is likely to inspire further work on risk-averse RL, robust control, and distributional methods. The unification via duality is elegant and timely, and the bridge to robust MDPs strengthens the practical relevance. The main shortcoming is the limited empirical validation, but the theoretical contribution is strong and sufficiently novel to merit acceptance at NeurIPS.
