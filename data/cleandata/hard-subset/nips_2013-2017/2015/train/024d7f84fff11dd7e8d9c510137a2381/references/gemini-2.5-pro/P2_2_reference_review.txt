### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning where the objective is a coherent risk measure, rather than the standard expectation. The authors make contributions for both static risk (applied to a trajectory's total discounted cost) and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The core technical contribution is a new, general formula for the policy gradient of any coherent risk measure. This formula leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures (as a maximum of expectations over a "risk envelope" of probability distortions) and the Envelope Theorem.

Based on this formula, the paper proposes a practical algorithm for the static case that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient. For the dynamic case, the authors derive a new risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem, analogous to the standard one by Sutton et al., and sketch an actor-critic algorithm to solve the problem in large-scale MDPs.

This work significantly generalizes and unifies a line of recent research in risk-sensitive RL. Previous papers, such as those on mean-variance/standard deviation objectives [29, 21] and Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) [8, 30], developed bespoke policy gradient algorithms for specific risk measures. This paper provides a single, principled framework from which these previous results can be re-derived (as demonstrated for CVaR) and new ones can be generated (as demonstrated for mean-semideviation). The approach builds upon the rich theory of coherent risk from stochastic programming [25, 26] and connects it directly to the likelihood-ratio policy gradient methods common in RL.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical development is sound and elegant. The application of the Envelope Theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) is a powerful and insightful technique that forms a solid foundation for the rest of the work. The derivations for specific cases like CVaR and mean-semideviation are clear and demonstrate the utility of the general theorem. The consistency result for the sample-based estimator (Proposition 4.4) is important for establishing the method's theoretical validity. The extension to the dynamic case via a risk-sensitive Bellman equation and the resulting policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) is a logical and well-executed extension of the static case.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  The proposed general algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem at each step to find the worst-case density `ξ*` and the KKT multipliers. While tractable for a moderate number of samples `N`, the computational cost is not negligible and may become a bottleneck for applications requiring a very large number of samples for accurate gradient estimation, especially for tail-sensitive risks. The paper does not analyze this computational complexity.
    2.  The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case appears to have a high sample complexity. The paper states that it requires a simulator for sampling *multiple* state transitions from each state in a trajectory to estimate the function `hθ(x, a)`. This is a much stronger assumption than the standard RL setting, which often assumes only a single trajectory can be sampled. The authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work, but it is a significant practical limitation.
    3.  The numerical illustration, while effective at demonstrating the value of flexibility in choosing a risk measure, is quite simple (a one-step bandit problem). It does not test the core contribution for the dynamic setting (the actor-critic algorithm) or provide insight into the convergence properties or computational trade-offs of the proposed gradient estimators in a sequential decision-making problem.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation is clearly articulated, and the necessary preliminaries on coherent risk measures are presented concisely. The logical flow from static risk to dynamic risk is easy to follow. Key assumptions are stated explicitly. The authors do a good job of placing their work in the context of related literature. The use of examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) greatly aids in understanding the application of the general theory.
*   **Weaknesses:** Due to space constraints, many crucial details about the actor-critic algorithm (critic architecture, two-phase sampling procedure, convergence analysis) are relegated to the supplementary material. While this is understandable, it makes the second half of the paper feel somewhat incomplete and difficult to fully evaluate without the supplement.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** The central idea of deriving a single policy gradient formula for the entire class of coherent risk measures is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work that tackled risk measures individually. The specific technical approach—combining the dual representation, the envelope theorem, and the likelihood-ratio trick—is a new and powerful combination of techniques for this problem domain. This framework not only generalizes but also simplifies the proofs of some existing results.

**Significance:** The paper has high potential for significant impact.

*   **Strengths:** Risk-sensitivity is a critical topic for deploying RL in real-world applications where catastrophic failures must be avoided (e.g., finance, robotics, autonomous systems). This paper provides a powerful and general toolkit for researchers and practitioners to tackle such problems. Instead of being limited to a few pre-defined risk measures, users can now, in principle, apply policy gradient methods to any coherent risk measure that can be described via a convex risk envelope. This opens the door to designing problem-specific risk measures. The unifying theoretical framework is a major contribution that will likely influence future research in this area.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and general framework that unifies and extends a large body of prior work on risk-sensitive policy gradients. This is a fundamental contribution to the field.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The core technical results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are elegant, well-supported, and provide deep insight into the structure of risk-sensitive optimization problems.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The framework is applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, providing practitioners with immense flexibility in specifying risk preferences.

### Arguments against Acceptance

1.  **Practical Limitations:** The proposed algorithms have practical drawbacks that are not fully addressed. The static algorithm may be computationally expensive, and the dynamic algorithm relies on a strong simulator assumption, limiting its applicability in standard RL settings.
2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are limited to a simple bandit setting and do not validate the proposed algorithm for sequential, dynamic risk problems, which is a major focus of the paper.

### Final Recommendation

Despite the practical limitations of the proposed algorithms and the simplistic nature of the experiment, the theoretical contribution of this paper is outstanding. It provides a beautiful, general, and unifying framework for a very important problem. The originality and significance of the theoretical results strongly outweigh the current practical weaknesses, which can be seen as clear and valuable directions for future work. This paper is a definite contribution to the NIPS community and should be accepted.
