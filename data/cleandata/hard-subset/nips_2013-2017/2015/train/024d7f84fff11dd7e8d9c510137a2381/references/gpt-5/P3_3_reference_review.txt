Summary
The paper develops policy gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. It treats two settings: (i) static risk of a random variable Z that depends on policy parameters through Pθ, and (ii) time-consistent dynamic risk in MDPs given by Markov coherent risk (Ruszczyński 2010). The key technical ingredients are:

- A general gradient formula for static coherent risk via the dual representation (risk envelope) and the envelope theorem: the gradient is an expectation under the adversarial density ξ* times a likelihood-ratio term, plus derivatives of the envelope constraints weighted by KKT multipliers (Theorem 4.2).

- Instantiations for CVaR and mean-semideviation that recover and simplify known results and yield new sampling-friendly expressions.

- A sample-average approximation (SAA) scheme: given samples from Pθ, solve a convex program to identify ξ* and multipliers, then plug into the gradient formula; a consistency result is provided under mild assumptions.

- A “policy gradient theorem” for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), showing that ∇θVθ(x) equals an expectation over trajectories of a ξ*-reweighted MDP of a score function times a stage cost hθ. This leads to an actor-critic algorithm: a robust-MDP-style critic estimates Vθ with function approximation; an actor uses ξ* and hθ to estimate the gradient.

A toy experiment compares risk-neutral, mean-semideviation, and mean-variance policies in a bandit with a heavy-tailed arm.

Relation to prior work
The paper unifies disparate risk-sensitive RL threads: exponential utility (Borkar), mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), CVaR (Rockafellar–Uryasev; Chow & Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al.), dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs (Ruszczyński; Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian; Chow & Pavone). The general coherent-risk treatment via envelopes builds on Shapiro–Dentcheva–Ruszczyński’s stochastic programming theory but extends it to the RL setting where Pθ depends on θ, which is essential for policy gradient.

Strengths
- Conceptual generality: Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 provide a unifying recipe for policy gradients across the entire class of coherent risks, reducing many special cases to a common template.

- Methodological novelty: Using the risk-envelope Lagrangian and envelope theorem to derive policy gradients is elegant and practically useful. The SAA-based estimator that pairs sampling with a convex program is broadly applicable and has a consistency guarantee.

- Dynamic setting: The policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk is a substantial extension of the classic policy gradient theorem, aligning risk-sensitive dynamic programming with actor-critic methods.

- Connections and reuse: The link to robust MDPs for the critic is appropriate and leverages recent scalable methods.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality and computation: Each gradient estimate requires solving a convex program to obtain ξ* and KKT multipliers. In the dynamic setting this must be done per visited state (and as Vθ changes). The paper does not provide complexity/runtime analysis, warm-starting strategies, or empirical cost measurements. This could be a bottleneck compared to risk-specific methods (e.g., CVaR) that avoid per-state convex solves.

- Sampling under ξ*-reweighted dynamics: Theorem 5.2 expects trajectories from a kernel proportional to ξ*(·)Pθ(·|x). This generally requires a generative model plus acceptance/importance sampling or explicit resampling of next states, and the method proposes multiple next-state samples per state. This assumption is strong in many RL settings where only on-policy rollouts are available. Variance and bias implications of approximate sampling are not analyzed.

- Regularity and differentiability: At points where the risk is non-smooth (e.g., CVaR when the quantile has positive mass), gradients may be set-valued. The paper claims broad applicability “regardless” of smoothness but should be precise about subgradients and the conditions under which the envelope theorem yields valid (sub)gradients.

- Assumptions on risk envelopes: The algorithm requires explicit, differentiable dependence of the envelope constraints on Pθ, and access to dfi/dp and dge/dp. While this covers common risks (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation), it limits applicability to risks with known, tractable envelopes and may be cumbersome to implement in practice.

- Empirical validation: Experiments are minimal and only cover a static bandit where policy dependence is simple; there is no dynamic MDP evaluation, no comparison of SAA versus analytic estimators beyond a brief note, and no study of variance, sample efficiency, or scalability. Given the strong theoretical claims, more substantial experiments (e.g., gridworlds, continuous control with dynamic coherent risk) are needed.

- Finite probability space: The exposition assumes finite Ω; while authors state extensions to Lp, details are omitted. In dynamic RL with continuous states, measurability and differentiability issues matter (e.g., function approximation errors), and the supplement’s guarantees should be summarized more concretely.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and well-grounded in convex analysis and stochastic programming. The consistency proof assumptions are reasonable, but several important details are deferred to the supplement. The dynamic theorem depends on strong sampling assumptions. The empirical section does not substantiate scalability or performance.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written given the technical density. The main results are well stated, and examples help. However, the actor-critic algorithm lacks a clear pseudocode; practical requirements (access to generative model, per-state convex solves, how to compute envelope derivatives) should be summarized explicitly in the main text. Clarify uniqueness of saddle points and treatment of non-smooth cases.

Originality
Generalizing policy gradients to the full class of coherent risks and to time-consistent dynamic risk is original and valuable. The approach subsumes prior special cases and connects naturally to robust MDPs.

Significance
If practical challenges are addressed, this could become a reference framework for risk-sensitive RL. As is, the lack of dynamic experiments and computational analysis tempers immediate impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Unifying, elegant theoretical framework with broad applicability.
- Clear generalization of several prior risk-specific results.
- New dynamic policy gradient theorem for coherent risk.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and no dynamic MDP experiments.
- Strong and potentially impractical sampling/computation requirements.
- Insufficient discussion of non-smoothness and multiplier uniqueness.

Suggestions
- Add dynamic MDP experiments with actor-critic, comparing to CVaR and mean-variance baselines; report runtime and variance.
- Provide algorithm boxes, implementation details for computing ξ* and envelope derivatives, and discuss warm-starting and amortization.
- Clarify subgradient handling at non-smooth points and uniqueness of KKT multipliers; discuss effect on estimators.
- Discuss how to handle single-trajectory settings (importance sampling) and analyze variance.

Recommendation: Weak accept (if space permits), contingent on strengthened empirical section and clarification of practical assumptions.

Confidence: High (familiar with risk-sensitive RL, robust MDPs, and coherent risk optimization).
