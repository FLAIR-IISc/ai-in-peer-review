## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of applying policy gradient methods to reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The authors generalize existing work by providing a unified framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures (both static and dynamic/time-consistent). They derive novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, leveraging convex optimization and a dynamic programming-style decomposition.  The key contributions are a convenient gradient formula for static risk, an algorithm involving sampling and convex programming for static risk gradient estimation, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk enabling an actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The work offers a significant generalization of existing risk-sensitive RL methods. Rather than focusing on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, variance), it provides a framework for *any* coherent risk measure, making it a highly versatile contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas appears mathematically rigorous, building upon established results in coherent risk theory (Artzner et al.) and leveraging the envelope theorem. The proofs, while deferred to supplementary material, are crucial for establishing credibility.
* **Practicality:**  The proposed algorithms (especially the sampling-based approach for static risk) offer a pathway to applying these techniques to large-scale or continuous MDPs where exact calculations are intractable. The connection to robust MDPs and use of function approximation in the dynamic case are sensible approaches to scalability.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions itself within the existing literature, highlighting relationships to previous work on risk-sensitive RL, robust MDPs, and stochastic programming. The authors demonstrate how their results encompass earlier, more specialized approaches.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example demonstrates the potential impact of choosing different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. variance) on policy decisions, highlighting the practical relevance of the framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical development is dense and requires a strong background in coherent risk theory and optimization. The jump between the general theory and the specific examples might be challenging for some readers.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the numerical example is illustrative, it’s relatively simple.  A more comprehensive evaluation on benchmark RL tasks (e.g., from the OpenAI Gym) would significantly strengthen the paper. It would be valuable to compare the performance of algorithms based on different coherent risk measures.
* **Practical Challenges of Convex Programming:** The proposed algorithm for static risk relies on solving a convex program at each iteration. While solvable, this can be computationally expensive, potentially negating some of the benefits of the sampling approach. The paper doesn't thoroughly address the scalability of this step.
* **Assumption Dependence:** The theoretical results rely on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 about the risk envelope) which, while common, might not hold in all practical scenarios.  Discussing the limitations of these assumptions and potential workarounds would be beneficial.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and appears well-researched. The derivations seem correct and the connections to established results are clearly explained. It’s a complete, rather than a position, paper, although the limited empirical validation leaves some room for concern.
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, but the mathematical notation is dense and can be challenging to follow. Improving the readability of the equations and providing more intuitive explanations of the concepts would be helpful.
* **Originality:** The work presents a novel generalization of risk-sensitive RL methods. While building upon existing theory, the unification under the coherent risk framework and the derived gradient formulas are original contributions.  The authors adequately reference related work.
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant. Enabling the application of policy gradients to a broader class of risk measures could lead to more robust and tailored RL solutions for applications in finance, robotics, and other domains. However, the practical impact hinges on the scalability of the algorithms and the benefits of using different coherent risk measures in real-world scenarios.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical contribution generalizing risk-sensitive RL.
* Novel gradient formulas and algorithms with potential for practical applications.
* Well-connected to existing literature and clearly positioned within the field.

**Con:**

* Limited empirical validation.
* Potential computational bottleneck from the convex programming step.
* High mathematical complexity that may limit accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** with minor revisions. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the work addresses an important problem in risk-sensitive RL. However, I strongly encourage the authors to:

1. **Expand the empirical evaluation:**  Include results on more challenging benchmark RL tasks.
2. **Discuss computational scalability:** Address the potential cost of the convex programming step and explore methods for mitigating it.
3. **Improve clarity:**  Simplify the notation and provide more intuitive explanations of the key concepts.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field.
