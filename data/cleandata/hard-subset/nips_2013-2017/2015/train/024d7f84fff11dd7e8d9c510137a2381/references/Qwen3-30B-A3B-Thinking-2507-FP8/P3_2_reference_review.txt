

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors develop gradient estimation algorithms for both static and dynamic Markov coherent risk measures, leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1). Their key contribution is a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) for static coherent risk that can be implemented using sampling and convex programming, and a new policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) for Markov coherent risk that extends the classical policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings. The paper demonstrates how their approach generalizes and unifies previous work on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation, etc.) and provides a numerical illustration showing how different risk preferences lead to different optimal policies.

This work builds on and extends prior research in risk-sensitive RL, particularly the work of Chow et al. (2014) on CVaR optimization in MDPs, Tamar et al. (2015) on CVaR optimization via sampling, and Ruszczyński & Shapiro (2006) on optimization of coherent risk measures. The paper correctly situates itself within the broader literature on risk-sensitive optimization in RL, while making a compelling case for the theoretical and practical benefits of a unified coherent risk framework.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical foundation is strong and well-motivated. The use of the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and central to the paper's contributions. The authors carefully establish the necessary conditions (Assumptions 2.2 and 4.1) for their approach to work.

2. The gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are novel and well-derived. The connection between the envelope theorem and likelihood ratio methods is particularly insightful, and the authors demonstrate how their framework recovers known results for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR in Section 4.1).

3. The sampling-based estimation approach (Section 4.3) is practical and addresses a key challenge in applying risk-sensitive RL to large-scale problems. The consistency result (Proposition 4.4) provides theoretical justification for their estimator.

4. The numerical example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates the practical importance of choosing appropriate risk criteria. The comparison between mean-semideviation and mean-standard-deviation policies in the trading example is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The computational complexity of the convex programming steps in the algorithms is not thoroughly analyzed. While the paper mentions using interior point methods, it doesn't discuss how the computational burden scales with problem size or the number of constraints in the risk envelope. For large-scale problems, this could be a significant limitation.

2. The convergence analysis of the actor-critic algorithm (Section 5) is mentioned but not provided in detail. The supplementary material is referenced, but the main paper should at least outline the key convergence guarantees.

3. The numerical results are limited to a single trading example. More extensive experiments across different domains (e.g., control tasks, robotic navigation) would strengthen the empirical validation of the approach.

4. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (mentioned in the conclusion) is intriguing but not developed in the main body of the paper. This represents an important potential direction for future work but feels underdeveloped in the current manuscript.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
1. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to applications.

2. The authors provide clear explanations of key concepts (e.g., coherent risk measures, Markov coherent risk measures) and effectively motivate their approach.

3. The examples (Sections 4.1-4.2) are well-chosen to illustrate how the general framework recovers specific known results.

**Weaknesses:**
1. Some technical details in the proofs (e.g., the proof of Theorem 4.2) are relegated to the supplementary material without sufficient indication of their importance to the main contributions.

2. The explanation of the two-phase sampling procedure for the actor-critic algorithm (Section 5) is somewhat vague. A more detailed description of how the critic and actor interact would improve clarity.

3. The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how their approach compares to existing risk-sensitive RL methods in terms of computational efficiency. For example, how does their convex programming-based approach compare to the iterative methods used by Tamar et al. (2015) for CVaR?

## Originality

**Strengths:**
1. The paper's main contribution—the unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL—is highly original. While specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) have been studied previously, the authors successfully generalize these to the entire class of coherent risk measures.

2. The new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) is a significant theoretical contribution that extends the classical policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings.

3. The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature: previous work has focused on specific risk measures, while this work provides a general framework applicable to all coherent risk measures.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The connection between dynamic coherent risk measures and robust MDPs (cited from [19]) is noted but not sufficiently developed. This connection could be leveraged to provide additional theoretical insights or practical advantages.

2. The paper doesn't clearly distinguish its approach from related work on risk-sensitive RL that uses the same representation theorem (e.g., the work by Ruszczyński & Shapiro [25] for stochastic programming). The paper correctly states that their approach is different because it handles MDPs where the policy affects the distribution, but this distinction could be made more explicit.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
1. The unified framework for coherent risk measures has significant practical implications. As demonstrated in the numerical example, the choice of risk measure can dramatically affect the optimal policy, and the authors provide a systematic way to incorporate different risk preferences.

2. The paper advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL by providing a general method applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than just specific cases.

3. The connection to model uncertainty (mentioned in the conclusion) suggests broader implications for handling model misspecification in dynamic decision making, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't fully explore the practical limitations of the approach. For example, how does the approach scale to high-dimensional state spaces? How sensitive is the performance to the choice of risk envelope constraints?

2. The significance of the theoretical results could be strengthened by discussing concrete applications where coherent risk measures offer clear advantages over expected cost objectives.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Expand the experimental section:** Include more diverse examples (e.g., standard RL benchmarks like Mountain Car or CartPole with risk-sensitive objectives) to better demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

2. **Clarify computational complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational requirements of the convex programming steps, especially for large-scale problems.

3. **Strengthen the connection to model uncertainty:** Develop the connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty more thoroughly, as this represents a potentially significant contribution.

4. **Improve the actor-critic algorithm description:** Provide a more detailed description of the two-phase sampling procedure and the interaction between the critic and actor components.

5. **Address limitations more explicitly:** The paper should more thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the computational requirements and the need for a simulator for multiple state-transitions.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper successfully develops a unified framework for coherent risk measures that generalizes and unifies previous work on specific risk measures. The theoretical results are sound, the approach is novel, and the numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical importance of the work.

The main weaknesses are the limited experimental validation, the lack of detailed computational complexity analysis, and the underdeveloped connection to model uncertainty. However, these are reasonable limitations for a theoretical contribution, and the paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL
- Solid theoretical foundation with well-derived gradient formulas
- Demonstrated generalization of previous results
- Practical numerical illustration showing the importance of risk criteria

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation
- Computational complexity not thoroughly analyzed
- Connection to model uncertainty underdeveloped

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive RL that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners. While the experimental validation could be expanded, the theoretical results are strong and the paper provides a clear path forward for future work. This is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.
