

# Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning (RL) with coherent risk objectives. The authors address a gap in the literature by developing algorithms that work for the entire class of coherent risk measures (which satisfy convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity) rather than focusing on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that enables sampling-based approximation; (2) an algorithm for gradient estimation of general static coherent risk using sampling with convex programming; and (3) a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that extends the standard policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings. The authors demonstrate how their approach generalizes previous results for specific risk measures while providing a more unified theoretical foundation.

The paper connects to previous work on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 18, 21, 29, 30]) by showing how these specific approaches are special cases of their general framework. It also relates to the broader literature on coherent risk measures in finance ([2, 26]) and dynamic risk measures ([24]).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations. The authors build on the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), which is a well-established result in the risk literature. They provide detailed proofs for their main theorems (Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2) in the supplementary material, which is important for a theoretical contribution.

The consistency result in Proposition 4.4 is particularly strong, as it establishes that their sampling-based estimator converges to the true gradient under mild assumptions. The numerical example in Section 6 provides a practical illustration of how different risk measures lead to different policy choices, demonstrating the value of their flexible framework.

However, there are a few minor concerns regarding completeness:
- The paper restricts to finite probability spaces for simplicity, which is a common technique but might limit applicability to continuous spaces. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide a detailed discussion of how to extend their approach to continuous cases.
- The dynamic risk algorithm requires a simulator for sampling multiple state-transitions from each state, which may not be available in all RL settings. While the authors note this as a future direction, a more thorough discussion of this limitation would be helpful.

Overall, the paper is well-supported by theoretical analysis and represents a complete piece of work, not just a position paper.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and explains why coherent risk measures are important in RL. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout, and the authors provide helpful examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) to illustrate their general framework.

The structure follows a logical progression: from problem formulation to theoretical results, to examples, and finally to a numerical illustration. The supplementary material provides additional details without overwhelming the main paper. The numerical example (Section 6) is particularly effective at demonstrating the practical significance of their framework.

One minor suggestion: while the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, adding more intuitive explanations of why their approach works (beyond the mathematical derivation) could further enhance clarity for readers less familiar with risk theory.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL across the entire class of coherent risk measures. Previous work has focused on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.), but this paper shows how these are all special cases of a more general approach. The authors explicitly state this in the introduction: "Our approach applies to the whole class of coherent risk measures, thereby generalizing and unifying previous approaches that have focused on individual risk measures."

The new policy gradient theorems (Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2) are novel contributions that extend the standard policy gradient framework to risk-sensitive settings. The authors also demonstrate how their approach can rederive previous results in greater generality and simplicity, which is a strong indication of the framework's unifying power.

This is a meaningful extension of the literature, not just a minor modification of existing work. The paper is clearly differentiated from previous NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 18, 21, 29, 30]) by providing a general framework rather than a specific risk measure.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Risk-sensitive decision making is crucial in many real-world applications (finance, operations research, autonomous systems), where managing cost variability is as important as minimizing expected cost. The paper's framework provides decision-makers with flexibility to select risk measures that suit their specific problem and risk preferences.

The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates this flexibility: different risk measures (risk-neutral, mean-semideviation, mean-standard-deviation) lead to different investment policies, with the risk-averse policies making counterintuitive choices that reflect their risk preferences. This shows the practical value of the authors' framework.

The paper also opens up new directions for future work, particularly regarding risk shaping to handle model uncertainty. As the authors note in the conclusion: "By carefully shaping the risk-criterion, the decision maker may be able to take uncertainty into account in a broad sense." This has significant implications for robust decision making in dynamic environments.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, the framework is novel and general, and the practical significance is clear.

The paper meets all four review criteria well:
- **Quality**: Technically sound with rigorous proofs and consistent with established risk theory
- **Clarity**: Well-written, well-organized, and effectively communicates complex concepts
- **Originality**: Provides a unified framework that generalizes previous work on specific risk measures
- **Significance**: Addresses an important problem with practical implications in real-world applications

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Provides a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL across the entire class of coherent risk measures
2. Presents novel gradient formulas (Theorem 4.2, Theorem 5.2) that extend the standard policy gradient theorem
3. Demonstrates how previous specific-risk approaches are special cases of the general framework
4. Includes a clear numerical example that illustrates the practical value of the framework
5. Rigorous theoretical analysis with consistency results

**Weaknesses:**
1. Restriction to finite probability spaces for simplicity (though acknowledged by authors)
2. Dynamic risk algorithm requires simulator for multiple state-transitions from each state
3. Limited discussion of computational complexity of the convex programming step
4. Numerical example is somewhat simplistic (could be extended to a more complex RL task)

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NeurIPS. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, with a clear unifying framework that generalizes previous work. The paper is well-written, rigorously supported, and addresses an important problem with practical significance. While there are some limitations in the scope (finite probability space, simulator requirement), these are acknowledged by the authors and don't undermine the paper's main contribution.

The paper's contribution is substantial enough to advance the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL and should be of interest to the NeurIPS community. The framework provides a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working on risk-sensitive decision making.

**Final Score: 8/10 (Strong Accept)**
