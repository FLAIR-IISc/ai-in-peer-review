

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors generalize previous work on specific risk measures (such as CVaR, mean-variance, and mean-semideviation) to the entire class of coherent risk measures, which satisfy the four axioms of coherence (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity). The paper makes three main contributions: a new gradient formula for static coherent risk that facilitates sampling-based approximation, a general algorithm for static risk using sampling with convex programming, and a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that extends the standard policy gradient theorem to dynamic risk settings.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature, building on prior work in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 10, 18, 21, 29, 30]) while distinguishing itself through its focus on the general coherent risk framework. It also relates to recent work on time-consistent risk measures [24] and dynamic programming approaches to risk-sensitive RL [20, 24, 29, 30].

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical foundations, including a representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) that enables the derivation of gradient formulas. The authors prove the consistency of their sampling-based estimators (Proposition 4.4) and demonstrate how their approach generalizes previous results (e.g., for CVaR in Section 4.1). The dynamic risk formulation (Theorem 5.2) extends the standard policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured with clear mathematical exposition. The examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) effectively illustrate the application of the general framework. The numerical example in Section 6 demonstrates the practical importance of flexible risk criteria by showing how different risk measures lead to different optimal policies for a trading agent.

3. **Originality:** The paper's core contribution is the unification of risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. The authors explicitly show how previous approaches to specific risk measures (e.g., [8, 29, 30]) are special cases of their general approach. This represents a significant conceptual advance beyond the literature, which has primarily focused on individual risk measures.

4. **Significance:** The framework provides a flexible tool for risk-sensitive RL that can be adapted to different risk preferences and problem characteristics. The numerical example demonstrates how this flexibility leads to meaningful differences in policy selection. The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) suggests potential for broader applications beyond risk-sensitive RL.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity:** The static risk approach requires solving convex programs for each gradient estimate, which may become computationally expensive for large-scale problems. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they do not explore potential computational trade-offs or approximations.

2. **Implementation Constraints:** The dynamic risk approach requires sampling multiple state-transitions from each state (a simulator), which may not be feasible in all practical settings. The authors note this limitation but do not propose extensions to work with single trajectory rollouts.

3. **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical example in Section 6 is valuable but limited to a simple trading problem. More extensive experimental results across diverse RL domains would strengthen the paper's claims about the practical utility of the approach.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Provides a theoretically sound, unified framework for risk-sensitive RL that generalizes prior work
- Demonstrates clear technical novelty through the derivation of new gradient formulas
- Offers practical insights into the importance of flexible risk criteria
- Well-written with clear theoretical contributions and proper contextualization within the literature

*Con:*
- Relies on computationally intensive convex programming for static risk
- Requires a simulator for multiple state-transitions in the dynamic case
- Limited experimental validation beyond a single numerical example
- Theoretical focus may limit immediate practical appeal for some RL practitioners

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a general framework that unifies previous approaches. The theoretical results are sound and well-supported, and the paper clearly articulates how it extends prior work. The numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical importance of the approach. While there are some computational limitations that should be acknowledged, these are inherent to the problem setting and the authors are transparent about them. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a unified framework that can be applied to any coherent risk measure.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a significant problem in RL with a technically sound approach that builds on but extends previous work. The contribution is novel, significant, and well-executed. The authors have provided a complete piece of work with clear theoretical foundations and practical illustrations.
