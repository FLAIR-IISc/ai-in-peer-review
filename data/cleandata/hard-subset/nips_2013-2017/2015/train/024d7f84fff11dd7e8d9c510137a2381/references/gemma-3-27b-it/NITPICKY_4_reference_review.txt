This paper presents policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, generalizing existing approaches and providing new gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk. While the topic is relevant and the motivation sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws in presentation, technical details, and a lack of compelling empirical validation, leading me to recommend rejection.

The introduction is overly verbose and repetitive, particularly regarding the benefits of coherent risk measures. The motivation for focusing on coherence and time consistency could be more concise. The claim of unifying previous approaches needs stronger justification; while the framework is general, it’s not immediately clear how much simpler it makes existing derivations. The listed contributions, while technically correct, feel incremental rather than revolutionary.

Section 2, "Preliminaries," is meticulously detailed, almost to a fault. The restriction to finite probability spaces, while stated, feels arbitrary and limits the immediate applicability of the results. The notation is consistently dense, making it difficult to follow the derivations. The justification for Assumption 2.2 feels hand-wavy; while referencing Ruszczynski & Shapiro is appropriate, a clearer explanation of its relevance to RL problems is needed. The discussion of dynamic risk measures feels rushed, relying heavily on citation of [24] without sufficient explanation for a broader NIPS audience.

The core technical contribution – the gradient formulas in Section 4 – is presented adequately, but the numerous examples (CVaR, Mean-Semideviation) feel disconnected from a cohesive narrative. While demonstrating generality is valuable, each example is presented almost as a separate paper, rather than illustrating the power of the overarching framework. The assertion that the SAA estimator is consistent (Proposition 4.4) relies on a complex set of assumptions that are not thoroughly justified within the context of RL. The comparison to analytical solutions in the supplementary material is mentioned but not fully detailed in the main text.

Section 5, dealing with dynamic risk, introduces Theorem 5.2, which is complex and relies heavily on the critic’s estimate of Vθ. The description of the actor-critic algorithm is frustratingly high-level. Key implementation details, such as the function approximation method used for Vθ, are omitted, making it difficult to assess the practical viability of the approach. The claim that the proposed algorithm avoids the “curse of dimensionality” is overly optimistic without substantial empirical support.

The numerical illustration in Section 6 is the weakest part of the paper. The setup is contrived, the results are not particularly striking, and the discussion of why policy π2 chooses A3 is superficial. The fact that A3 stochastically dominates A1 is not explicitly mentioned, leading to a potentially misleading interpretation. A more comprehensive set of experiments, with varying MDP complexities and risk preferences, is necessary to demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The framework does genuinely generalize existing risk-sensitive RL approaches.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations, while dense, appear to be correct.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of coherent risk measures with policy gradients is a worthwhile contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is poorly written, overly dense, and lacks a clear narrative flow.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration is insufficient to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The results, while technically sound, feel incremental rather than groundbreaking.
*   **Overly Detailed Preliminaries:** The level of detail in the preliminary sections is excessive and detracts from the core contributions.
*   **Missing Implementation Details:** Key implementation aspects of the actor-critic algorithm are omitted.
*   **Unjustified Assumptions:** The consistency result for the SAA estimator relies on a set of assumptions that are not adequately justified.
*   **Numerous Minor Flaws:** Throughout the paper, there are minor inconsistencies in notation, formatting, and terminology.



**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper’s lack of clarity and excessive detail obscure its core contributions.
*   The insufficient empirical validation fails to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms.
*   The incremental nature of the results does not justify publication at NIPS.
*   The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel generalization of existing risk-sensitive RL approaches.
*   The proposed algorithms have the potential to be useful for a range of applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its technical correctness and potential, this paper is ultimately marred by its lack of clarity, insufficient empirical validation, and numerous minor flaws. The paper feels unfinished and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication. I recommend rejection and encourage the authors to revise and resubmit with a more focused presentation, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, and a clearer justification of their assumptions.
