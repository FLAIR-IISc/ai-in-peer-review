- Presents a unified policy gradient framework for the full class of coherent risk measures in RL, generalizing beyond specific risks like CVaR.
- Key contributions: new gradient formula for static coherent risk enabling sampling, sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator for static risk, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Builds on the representation theorem for coherent risks and provides detailed proofs for main theorems (4.2, 5.2) in the supplement.
- Provides a strong consistency result (Proposition 4.4) showing sampling-based estimator converges to the true gradient under mild assumptions.
- Demonstrates that prior risk-sensitive RL methods are special cases of the proposed general framework.
- Includes a numerical example showing different risk measures produce different policies and illustrating practical significance.
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations and well-supported analysis.
- Exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear notation, helpful examples, and effective supplementary material.
- Original contribution: novel, general policy gradient theorems that unify and extend previous work on specific risk measures.
- Significant impact potential for real-world applications (finance, autonomous systems) and opens directions for risk shaping and model uncertainty.
- Strength: unifying theoretical framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risks.
- Strength: novel gradient formulas and generalization of previous results.
- Strength: rigorous proofs, consistency guarantees, and illustrative numerical example.
- Weakness: analysis restricted to finite probability spaces; extension to continuous spaces not fully discussed.
- Weakness: dynamic-risk algorithm needs a simulator capable of multiple state-transition sampling, which may not always be available.
- Weakness: limited discussion of computational complexity for the convex programming step.
- Weakness: numerical example is somewhat simplistic and could be extended to more complex RL tasks.
- Recommendation: Strong accept (8/10) — significant, well-supported theoretical contribution worthy of NeurIPS.
