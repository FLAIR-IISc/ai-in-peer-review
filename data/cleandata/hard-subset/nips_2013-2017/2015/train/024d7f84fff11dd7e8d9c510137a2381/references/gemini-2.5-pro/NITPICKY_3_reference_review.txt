**Summary**

This paper introduces a general policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk measure objectives. The work addresses both static risk, applied to the total discounted cost of an MDP trajectory, and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The primary contribution is a set of new policy gradient formulas. For the static case, the authors derive a gradient formula by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures and the Envelope Theorem. This leads to a practical algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient. For the dynamic case, the paper presents a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem, analogous to the standard one, which forms the basis for a proposed actor-critic algorithm. The presented framework unifies and generalizes several previous works that have focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Generality:** The core theoretical contribution is elegant and novel. By applying the Envelope Theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk, the authors derive a policy gradient formula applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This provides a principled and unified framework for a problem previously addressed with measure-specific methods.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations for the static gradient (Theorem 4.2) and the dynamic policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) appear sound and represent a significant theoretical advance for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The connection made between the gradient, the worst-case density `ξ*`, and the Lagrangian multipliers is insightful.
3.  **Significance:** The paper tackles an important problem. Moving beyond simple expectation optimization is crucial for deploying RL in real-world, safety-critical applications. Providing tools for a broad, axiomatically-justified class of risk measures like coherent risks could have a substantial impact on the field.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are strong, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor issues in its presentation and a major flaw in its experimental validation. My critique is as follows:

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish and precision expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   **Notation and Formatting:** There are numerous small but distracting inconsistencies. The arrow in `Z : Ω 7→ (−∞,∞)` is unconventional. The definition of the set of probability distributions `B` uses an integral, which is inconsistent with the paper's stated focus on finite probability spaces where a summation would be appropriate. Several critical clarifications are relegated to footnotes (e.g., Footnotes 1, 2, 3), which disrupts the flow and diminishes the importance of these points. The reference list contains formatting errors (e.g., inconsistent punctuation, capitalization, and truncated titles).
    *   **Ambiguity:** The recursive definition of the dynamic risk measure in Eq. (3) does not make the state-dependency of the inner risk measures explicit in the notation, which can be confusing. The notation for the constraints `ge(ξ, Pθ)` in Assumption 2.2 is also slightly ambiguous about the precise nature of the dependency.

2.  **Incomplete Exposition:** The paper relies heavily on the supplementary material, making it difficult to evaluate as a self-contained document. The proofs for all central results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2, Propositions 4.3 and 4.4) are deferred. More importantly, the entire actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case—a key contribution—is described only at a very high level, with crucial details on implementation, sampling scheme, and analysis missing from the main text. The brief mention that the algorithm requires a simulator capable of providing multiple next-state samples per state is a significant practical limitation that deserves more prominent discussion.

3.  **Flawed Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration in Section 6 is the most significant weakness of the paper.
    *   **Mismatch with Contributions:** The experiment is conducted on a simple one-step bandit problem, which only tests the static risk formulation (SRP). It completely fails to provide any empirical validation for the dynamic risk problem (DRP) and the corresponding actor-critic algorithm, which constitutes half of the paper's contributions.
    *   **Incorrect Use of Risk Measures:** The experiment compares a risk-neutral policy with two risk-averse policies. However, the objectives for the risk-averse policies (`maxθ E[Z] − SD[Z]` and `maxθ E[Z] − √Var[Z]`) do not correspond to minimizing a coherent risk measure. The mean-semideviation risk measure discussed in Section 4.2 is coherent, but the objective `maxθ E[Z] - SD[Z]` is not. This fundamentally undermines the experiment's purpose, which should have been to demonstrate the utility of the proposed algorithm for the class of *coherent* risks. As presented, the experiment fails to validate the central claims and methods of the paper.

4.  **Assumptions:** The consistency result for the SAA-based gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) rests on a lengthy set of assumptions. While the authors describe them as "rather mild," their practical verifiability for a newly defined coherent risk measure is not discussed, potentially limiting the plug-and-play nature of the proposed framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel, elegant, and unifying theoretical framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures.
*   The results generalize a significant body of prior work in risk-sensitive RL.
*   The topic is highly relevant to the NIPS community and addresses a critical need for principled risk-aware learning algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental section is critically flawed, as it uses non-coherent risk objectives to "validate" a method designed specifically for coherent risks, and it fails to test the dynamic risk setting entirely.
*   The paper is incomplete, with the details of the main algorithm for the dynamic case relegated to supplementary material, preventing a full assessment of its practicality and complexity.
*   The manuscript suffers from numerous minor issues in clarity, notation, and formatting, which collectively detract from the quality of the submission.

**Decision**

While the theoretical ideas presented in this paper are novel and significant, the current manuscript is not ready for publication. The combination of a critically flawed experimental validation and an incomplete description of the main algorithm for the dynamic case prevents a confident assessment of the work's practical value. The numerous minor presentational issues further suggest that the paper was hastily prepared. I therefore recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to thoroughly revise the manuscript, with particular attention to providing a compelling and correct experimental evaluation that directly supports the paper's theoretical contributions.
