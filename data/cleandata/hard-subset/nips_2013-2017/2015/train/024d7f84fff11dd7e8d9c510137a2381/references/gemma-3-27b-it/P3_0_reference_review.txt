## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It provides a general framework for applying policy gradient methods to a broad class of coherent risk measures, unifying and generalizing existing work. The technical contributions – a novel gradient formula for static coherent risk, a sampling-based algorithm for static risk optimization, and a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk – are all strong. While the paper is dense and requires a solid background in mathematical finance and RL, it is well-written and clearly motivated. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of optimizing policies in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) under coherent risk measures, a broader and more theoretically grounded approach than focusing on specific risk metrics like CVaR or mean-variance. Coherent risk measures, defined by desirable properties (convexity, monotonicity, etc.), offer a principled way to handle risk aversion in decision-making.  Previous work largely focused on individual risk measures, or relied on dynamic programming which doesn't scale well. This work provides a unified policy gradient approach applicable to *all* coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic (time-consistent), leveraging sampling to address computational challenges.

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of risk-sensitive RL, acknowledging prior efforts on specific risk functions (e.g., [6, 18, 29, 30]) and relating their approach to robust MDPs [19]. They clearly articulate the limitations of existing dynamic programming approaches [24] and the advantages of a policy-gradient based method for scalability. The connection to stochastic programming [25, 26] is also appropriately discussed. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The most significant strength is the unification of risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. This generality allows for easy application of the developed algorithms to a wide range of risk preferences.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is heavily grounded in mathematical theory. The derivation of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are rigorous and well-explained.
* **Practicality through Sampling:**  The proposed sampling-based approach effectively addresses the computational challenges inherent in optimizing coherent risk measures, making the framework applicable to larger and more complex problems.
* **Novel Contributions:** The new gradient formula for static coherent risk, the sampling algorithm, and the policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk all represent original contributions to the field.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the authors provide sufficient context for understanding the key ideas.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example (Section 6) demonstrating the different behaviour of policies optimized with different risk criteria (risk-neutral, mean-semideviation, mean-standard deviation) highlights the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper demands a strong mathematical background, particularly in stochastic optimization and mathematical finance. This may limit its accessibility to a broader RL audience. More intuitive explanations of the core concepts would be beneficial.
* **Implementation Details:**  While the paper outlines the actor-critic algorithm in principle, it lacks detailed implementation specifics.  A more thorough discussion of the practical considerations involved in applying the algorithm, such as function approximation techniques for the value function and hyperparameter tuning, would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example, while illustrative, is relatively simple. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks, comparing performance against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms, would strengthen the claims of the paper.
* **Assumption 2.2:** The assumption about the form of the risk envelope (Assumption 2.2) and its smoothness could be limiting.  A discussion of the practical implications of this assumption and potential approaches for dealing with risk envelopes that don’t fit this form would be valuable.
* **Lack of discussion of computational cost:** While sampling is presented as a solution to scalability, a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm (especially the convex programming step within the sampling) would be beneficial.



**4. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novel contributions.
* Significant advance in unifying risk-sensitive RL.
* Practical sampling-based approach for addressing computational challenges.
* Clear and well-motivated presentation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Steep learning curve for readers unfamiliar with the underlying mathematical concepts.
* Limited empirical validation.
*  Assumption 2.2 might be too restrictive in some applications.




**5. Recommendations and Questions for the Authors:**

* **Accessibility:** Consider adding a section with more intuitive explanations of coherent risk measures and their properties, aimed at an RL audience.
* **Implementation Details:** Provide more details about the actor-critic algorithm, including function approximation techniques, hyperparameter tuning strategies, and practical considerations for implementation.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks, comparing the proposed approach against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms.
* **Assumption 2.2:** Discuss the practical implications of Assumption 2.2 and potential approaches for dealing with risk envelopes that don’t conform to its requirements.
* **Computational Analysis:**  Provide an analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, particularly the convex programming step.
* **Future Work:**  Expand on the discussion of future research directions, such as exploring the application of importance sampling techniques to improve gradient estimation.



**Overall, I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution and enhance its impact.**
