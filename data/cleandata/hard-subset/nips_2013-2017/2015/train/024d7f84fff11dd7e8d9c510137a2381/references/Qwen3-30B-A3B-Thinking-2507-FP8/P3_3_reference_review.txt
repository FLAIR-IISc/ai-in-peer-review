

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary
This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient algorithms for coherent risk measures, which satisfy the four axioms of coherence (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity). The authors present a unified framework that generalizes previous work on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, mean-semideviation) by deriving gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures. Key contributions include a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, a sampling-based algorithm using convex programming, and a dynamic programming-based policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk. The approach is theoretically grounded in the representation theorem for coherent risk measures, and the authors demonstrate how their framework encompasses previous results as special cases.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Soundness and Originality (Quality):** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by unifying risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. The authors' use of the representation theorem (Theorem 2.1) to derive gradient formulas is elegant and well-justified. The two main theorems (4.2 and 5.2) extend the classical policy gradient theorem to coherent risk measures in a principled way. The paper's theoretical foundation is rigorous, with careful attention to the assumptions required for their results to hold.

**Generalization and Unification (Originality):** The paper's key contribution is its generality - it provides a single framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures rather than focusing on specific risk metrics. The authors convincingly demonstrate how previous results (e.g., for CVaR in [8, 30] or mean-semideviation in [26]) are special cases of their framework. This unification is novel and valuable, as previous work had treated different risk measures as separate problems.

**Practical Relevance (Significance):** The numerical example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates the practical importance of flexible risk criteria in decision making. The trading agent example shows how different risk measures (risk-neutral, mean-semideviation, mean-standard-deviation) lead to different investment strategies, highlighting the framework's utility for matching risk preferences to problem characteristics. The connection to model uncertainty in Section 7 suggests broader implications for handling model misspecification.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Complexity and Scalability:** While the paper acknowledges that their approach is suitable for large MDPs, the reliance on convex programming for each gradient estimation step raises significant computational concerns. For dynamic risk (Section 5), the two-phase sampling procedure and need to solve convex programs for each state could make the approach prohibitively expensive for large-scale problems. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of computational complexity or empirical comparisons with existing methods on standard RL benchmarks.

**Assumption Limitations:** The approach relies on Assumption 2.2, which requires the risk envelope to be expressible in a specific convex programming form. While the authors claim this holds for popular risk measures, they don't provide a comprehensive analysis of the applicability to less common coherent risk measures. The smoothness conditions (e.g., bounded derivatives of constraints) might not hold in practice for some risk measures.

**Empirical Validation:** The numerical example is limited to a simple trading problem with only three assets. A more comprehensive experimental section comparing the proposed method against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on standard benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari) would strengthen the paper's claims about practical utility.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by unifying the treatment of coherent risk measures. The mathematical development is sound, the theoretical results are novel, and the framework's generality is well-justified. The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of computational considerations and empirical validation, which are critical for a NeurIPS submission.

The paper aligns well with NeurIPS topics on reinforcement learning and optimization. While the technical contribution is strong, the lack of extensive empirical validation on standard benchmarks is a notable limitation. However, given the paper's theoretical novelty and the importance of risk-sensitive RL in applications, this work would be a valuable contribution to the field.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper is technically strong, original, and significant for the field. The reviewers recommend the authors address the following points in the revision:

1. Add a computational complexity analysis of the proposed algorithms, especially for the dynamic risk case
2. Include a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmarks
3. Discuss the limitations of Assumption 2.2 more thoroughly, particularly regarding its applicability to less common coherent risk measures
4. Clarify the connection between the proposed approach and existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms in the related work section

These revisions would strengthen the paper's practical relevance and make it an even more compelling contribution to NeurIPS.
