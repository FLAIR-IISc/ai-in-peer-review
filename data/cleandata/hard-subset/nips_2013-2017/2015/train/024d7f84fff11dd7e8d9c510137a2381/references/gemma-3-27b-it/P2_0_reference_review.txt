## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a comprehensive framework for applying policy gradient methods to reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. It generalizes existing approaches by providing gradient formulas applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic (time-consistent). The key contributions are a novel gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm combining sampling and convex programming for its estimation, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk leading to an actor-critic algorithm. The work leverages duality theory of coherent risk to derive tractable gradient expressions.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The paper offers a unified approach to risk-sensitive RL, encompassing a wide range of coherent risk measures. This is a significant advance over previous work often focused on specific risk functions (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance).
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations, based on coherent risk theory and convex duality, appear rigorous and well-motivated. The proofs, while referred to the supplementary material, seem crucial to establishing the validity of the approach.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The paper proposes concrete algorithms – a sampling-based gradient estimator for static risk and an actor-critic scheme for dynamic risk – making the theoretical results usable in practice.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors clearly position their work relative to prior research, demonstrating that their approach generalizes existing results and provides a simpler, more unified perspective.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example with asset allocation highlights the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure based on the specific problem and risk preferences, adding valuable intuition.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While the framework is powerful, its implementation may be complex. The need for convex programming in the static risk gradient estimation, and the two-phase sampling in the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm, could pose challenges for scalability.
* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions (e.g., boundedness of the likelihood ratio, smoothness of constraints in Assumption 2.2) which might not hold in all practical scenarios. The authors acknowledge these, but a discussion of their practical implications would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example is relatively simple and doesn't demonstrate the algorithm's performance on more complex RL environments. A more extensive empirical evaluation would strengthen the claims about scalability and effectiveness.
* **Convergence Analysis:** The convergence analysis, partially relegated to the supplementary material, could be more detailed. While Proposition 4.4 provides some guarantees, a more thorough analysis of the actor-critic algorithm’s convergence properties is desirable.
* **Clarity in Dynamic Risk:** The description of the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm is somewhat brief. More details on the implementation of the critic, and the specific challenges in estimating the gradient with weighted transitions, would improve clarity.



**Evaluation against NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are supported by theoretical analysis. The work appears complete, although the detailed proofs are in the supplementary material. The authors are generally careful about discussing the limitations and assumptions. (Score: Strong)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but some sections (particularly on the dynamic risk algorithm) could benefit from more detailed explanations.  (Score: Good)
* **Originality:** The unified treatment of coherent risk measures within a policy gradient framework is novel and represents a significant contribution. (Score: Strong)
* **Significance:** The results are potentially important, as they provide a flexible and principled approach to risk-sensitive RL.  The framework could be applicable to various domains (finance, robotics, healthcare) where risk management is crucial. (Score: Good)

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL.
* The framework is highly general and has the potential to impact a wide range of applications.
* The algorithms are well-defined and offer a practical way to implement the theoretical results.

**Against:**

* The complexity of the algorithms may limit their applicability to large-scale problems.
* The empirical evaluation is limited, and a more comprehensive assessment of performance is needed.
* The convergence analysis could be more detailed.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL. While the complexity of the algorithms and the limited empirical evaluation are concerns, the generality, soundness, and potential impact of the work outweigh these weaknesses.  I suggest the authors:

1.  Expand the empirical evaluation to include more complex RL environments.
2.  Provide more details on the implementation and convergence analysis of the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm.
3.  Discuss the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper.
