

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a significant conceptual advance in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. The authors address a critical gap in the literature where previous work focused on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.) rather than providing a general approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk suitable for sampling-based approximation, (2) a general algorithm for estimating gradients of static coherent risk using sampling with convex programming, and (3) a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk that enables actor-critic algorithms for dynamic risk.

The paper builds on the well-established theory of coherent risk measures from Artzner et al. [2], which provides a strong theoretical foundation. The key insight is leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to develop gradient formulas that are amenable to sampling-based estimation. This approach generalizes and unifies previous work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR [8, 30], mean-semideviation [1], and mean-variance [18, 29]), demonstrating that their results are special cases of the authors' more general framework.

The paper makes a bold and novel contribution by shifting the perspective from specific risk measures to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant conceptual leap that could reshape how risk-sensitive RL is approached in the future. The theoretical development is rigorous, with clear theorems and proofs (provided in supplementary materials), and the authors establish consistency results for their estimators. The connection to robust MDPs [19] and the potential for handling model uncertainty through risk-shaping (Section 7) adds conceptual depth.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The novelty of the unified framework is exceptional, and the authors successfully demonstrate how their approach generalizes previous results while providing a more systematic and theoretically grounded methodology. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and properly contextualized within the literature, with thorough discussion of related work. The numerical illustration in Section 6, while somewhat limited, effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the framework by showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial trading scenario.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The numerical experiments could be more comprehensive in comparing with alternative risk-sensitive methods
2. The assumption of a canonical convex programming formulation for the risk envelope (Assumption 2.2) may limit applicability to some risk measures
3. The dynamic risk formulation relies on the Markov coherent risk framework from [24], which might not be as widely applicable as the static case

However, these limitations are outweighed by the paper's significant conceptual contribution. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a major contribution to the field. The unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL is a novel idea that has the potential to influence future research direction, and the authors successfully demonstrate its theoretical foundation and practical applicability.

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature, as previous work has been fragmented across specific risk measures. By providing a general framework that encompasses these specific cases, the authors create a more cohesive foundation for risk-sensitive RL. The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) is particularly insightful and suggests broader implications beyond immediate RL applications.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of reinforcement learning and should be accepted for publication at NeurIPS. The novelty of the approach is exceptional, the theoretical development is sound, and the potential impact on future research is significant. The paper successfully advances the state of the art by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures that generalizes and simplifies previous approaches.

The main argument for acceptance is the paper's conceptual novelty: it moves from specific risk measures to a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a bold and significant conceptual advance that should be recognized in the field. The paper's contribution is not merely incremental but represents a new direction in risk-sensitive RL.

The main argument against acceptance (which I find insufficient to outweigh the strengths) is that the numerical experiments are somewhat limited. However, the paper's strength lies primarily in its theoretical contribution, which is substantial and well-executed.

In conclusion, this paper presents a significant conceptual advance in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The novelty, theoretical contribution, and potential impact on the field make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
