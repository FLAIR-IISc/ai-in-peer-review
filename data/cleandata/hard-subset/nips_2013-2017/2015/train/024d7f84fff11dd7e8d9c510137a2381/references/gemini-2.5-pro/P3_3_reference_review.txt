This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). It addresses both static risk of the total discounted cost and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk. The core contributions are: (1) a novel gradient formula for static coherent risk measures derived from their dual representation, utilizing the envelope theorem; (2) a practical algorithm for the static case that combines sampling with convex programming to estimate the gradient when an analytical solution is unavailable, along with a consistency proof; (3) a new policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk, which generalizes the standard theorem to this risk-sensitive setting; and (4) an outline of a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. This work elegantly unifies and generalizes several previous studies that focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance), providing a principled approach for a broad and important class of risk objectives.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. By leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), the authors provide a single, elegant framework that applies to the entire class of coherent risks. This moves the field beyond a piecemeal approach of developing specific algorithms for specific risk measures (like CVaR or mean-semideviation) and provides a powerful, unifying perspective. The demonstration that their framework recovers and simplifies existing results (e.g., for CVaR) and derives new ones (for mean-semideviation) is very compelling.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty:** The theoretical contributions are significant. The application of the envelope theorem to the dual formulation of coherent risk to derive the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2) is a novel and insightful approach in the RL context. The policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) is a non-trivial and important extension of the classical result by Sutton et al. The consistency proof for the sample-based gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) adds to the paper's technical rigor.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, defines the necessary mathematical preliminaries, and builds its arguments logically from the static case to the more complex dynamic case. The mathematical exposition is precise and easy to follow.

4.  **Strong Motivation and Implications:** The paper makes a strong case for the importance of flexibility in choosing a risk measure. The numerical example, while simple, powerfully illustrates how a naive choice like mean-standard-deviation can lead to counter-intuitive behavior (avoiding a stochastically dominant asset) that is corrected by a more suitable coherent risk measure (mean-semideviation). The discussion on "risk shaping" and the connection to model uncertainty and robust MDPs points to significant future implications of this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The most significant weakness is the experimental validation. The numerical illustration is a stateless multi-armed bandit problem. While it effectively demonstrates the paper's motivation for using different risk measures, it only evaluates the static risk gradient formulation. There is no empirical evaluation of the main algorithmic contribution for sequential decision-making: the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical performance, sample efficiency, and stability of the proposed dynamic algorithm in a non-trivial MDP.

2.  **Practicality of the Dynamic Algorithm:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm in Section 5 is very high-level. Key implementation details are omitted and deferred to a full version in the supplement. For instance, the actor update requires sampling from a modified MDP with `ξ*`-weighted transition probabilities, which could be challenging or expensive in practice, especially without a perfect simulator. The complexity of the critic, which involves solving a robust MDP value-function problem, is also not fully explored. The stated requirement for a simulator and multiple next-state samples from each state limits its applicability compared to standard single-trajectory actor-critic methods.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The proposed gradient estimation method for the general static case involves solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) for each gradient update. The computational cost of this step, which scales with the number of samples `N`, is not discussed. For large `N` required for accurate estimation, this could be significantly slower than standard policy gradient methods, presenting a potential trade-off between generality and computational efficiency.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper provides a significant and elegant theoretical contribution by unifying policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are novel, sound, and well-presented. The work has the potential to be highly influential, providing a principled foundation for future research in risk-sensitive RL.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The experimental validation is insufficient. The lack of any experiment on the proposed dynamic risk algorithm for a sequential decision-making problem is a major omission that leaves the practical viability of a core part of the contribution unevaluated.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the weak experimental section, the theoretical contributions of this paper are strong, novel, and significant enough to warrant acceptance. The unified framework for coherent risk is a major step forward for risk-sensitive RL. The paper is well-written, rigorously developed, and provides a solid foundation that others are likely to build upon. The lack of experiments on the dynamic case is a clear weakness, but the theoretical framework for it is valuable in its own right. I would strongly recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors acknowledge the experimental limitations and expand on the practical challenges of the dynamic algorithm in the final version.
