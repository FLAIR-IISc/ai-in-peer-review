This paper tackles a significant and largely unexplored area: policy gradient methods for coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The authors successfully generalize existing risk-sensitive RL approaches, providing a unified framework applicable to a broad class of risk measures. The core contribution – a new gradient formula for coherent risk – is well-motivated and technically sound. I particularly appreciate the connection drawn to robust MDPs and the acknowledgement of limitations regarding model misspecification. While the validation is somewhat limited, the theoretical groundwork laid is substantial and opens avenues for impactful future work.

**Summary:** The paper introduces policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning problems where the objective function involves a coherent risk measure. It derives a novel gradient formula applicable to both static and dynamic coherent risks, generalizing previous work. The approach leverages convex programming for gradient estimation and provides an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk optimization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the primary strength. Applying policy gradients to the *entire class* of coherent risk measures is a genuinely novel contribution. The unification of disparate risk-sensitive RL methods under a single framework is a significant achievement.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formula appears mathematically rigorous, building upon established results in coherent risk theory and optimization. The use of the envelope theorem is particularly elegant.
*   **Generality:** The framework is not limited to specific risk measures or system dynamics, making it broadly applicable. The authors clearly demonstrate how existing approaches are special cases of their more general formulation.
*   **Potential Impact:** This work has the potential to significantly advance risk-sensitive RL, providing a principled way to incorporate risk preferences into learning algorithms. The connection to robust MDPs hints at broader implications for handling uncertainty.
*   **Clear Writing (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written, although some sections (particularly around the dynamic risk and actor-critic algorithm) could benefit from further clarification (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration, while insightful, is relatively limited in scope. It focuses on a single example and doesn't provide a comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm’s performance compared to existing risk-sensitive RL methods on a variety of benchmarks. Specifically, scaling up with larger state spaces and more complex problems is not demonstrated.
*   **Complexity of Implementation:** The reliance on convex programming within the gradient estimation process could pose a computational bottleneck for large-scale problems. While the authors mention using function approximation for the dynamic case, the details of this approximation and its impact on performance are not fully explored.
*   **Dynamic Risk Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is somewhat high-level. A more detailed description of the implementation, including the function approximation scheme and sampling procedure, would be beneficial. The convergence analysis mentioned feels buried in the supplementary material.
*   **Assumptions:** The strong assumptions (especially Assumption 2.2 regarding the structure of the risk envelope) might limit the applicability of the approach in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of how to handle cases where these assumptions are violated would be valuable.
*   **Clarity in dynamic risk setup:** The explanation surrounding the evaluation of the cost function `hθ(x, a)` in Theorem 5.2 is not very intuitive and lacks sufficient motivation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL.
*   The generalized framework and gradient formula have the potential to significantly impact future research in this area.
*   The authors clearly identify the limitations of their work and suggest directions for future research.
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem with a principled and mathematically rigorous approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comprehensive empirical validation raises concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed algorithms.
*   The computational complexity of the approach could limit its scalability to large-scale problems.
*   The dynamic risk algorithm details are insufficient for a complete understanding of the implementation.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limited empirical validation and some implementation complexities, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty and theoretical soundness of the proposed framework outweigh the weaknesses. The authors have successfully generalized existing risk-sensitive RL methods and provided a principled way to incorporate coherent risk preferences into learning algorithms. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should address the reviewer concerns regarding empirical validation and algorithm details in a future version. The potential for future impact is substantial.
