Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general umbrella of coherent risk measures. The key technical idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński and Shapiro) as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope U(Pθ), and to combine it with the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio tricks to derive gradients.

Contributions:
- Static coherent risk: A general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) expressed via the saddle point of the Lagrangian of the dual problem; specializations recover known results for CVaR and yield a new closed-form for mean-semideviation. A sample-average-approximation (SAA) gradient estimator combining sampling with a convex program is proposed, with a consistency result under mild assumptions.
- Dynamic risk: A policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) for Markov coherent risk measures (time-consistent dynamic risk via nested coherent measures), tying ∇θVθ(x) to a value function and a worst-case transition reweighting ξ*. This yields an actor-critic style algorithm: a critic estimates Vθ (leveraging its equivalence to a robust MDP and function approximation), and an actor estimates gradients using trajectories sampled from ξ*-reweighted dynamics.
- A small illustrative experiment on asset selection shows how different coherent risk criteria lead to distinct policies.

Relation to prior work
The paper generalizes risk-sensitive PG approaches that target specific risks (mean-variance, CVaR, exponential utility) by giving a unified coherent-risk gradient formula. It connects to robust MDPs (dynamic coherent risk ≈ robust planning) and uses function approximation ideas from robust MDP literature. The CVaR static PG formula is recovered with weaker assumptions and a simpler proof than previous derivations. Compared to prior dynamic coherent risk work that focuses on dynamic programming and is not scalable, the proposed PG/actor-critic view aims to scale via sampling and approximation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The static gradient derivation via envelope theorem and dual representation is elegant and appears correct under the stated assumptions (finite probability space, smooth envelopes). The consistency of the SAA estimator is plausible given continuity/compactness conditions and law-of-large-numbers arguments. The dynamic gradient theorem is a substantial and nontrivial extension of the policy-gradient theorem; the form using ξ*-reweighted dynamics and a modified per-stage cost hθ is reasonable and aligns with robust MDP duality.
- Assumptions: The requirement that the risk envelope be given in an explicit, differentiable, convex form (Assumption 2.2), and that one can compute or approximate saddle points and their multipliers, is strong but standard in coherent-risk optimization. The finite Ω restriction is stated upfront; the Lp extension is claimed but deferred. For dynamic risk, sampling under ξ*-reweighted transitions implicitly requires either a simulator capable of drawing multiple next states per state-action or model access; this limits applicability in purely model-free, single-trajectory settings (acknowledged by the authors).
- Completeness: Many proofs, algorithmic details (especially the actor-critic), and convergence analyses are relegated to the supplement. The main text gives key formulas but insufficient detail for full reproduction. Empirical evaluation is minimal and does not test the dynamic method.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with precise assumptions and helpful intuition around coherence and time consistency. Theorems are well-stated, and the connection to robust MDPs is made explicit. However:
- The dynamic actor-critic procedure is only sketched; a brief pseudocode and computational complexity discussion would improve clarity.
- The definition and computation of the derivatives dfi/dp, dge/dp appearing in hθ merit more concrete examples beyond CVaR to reassure readers these are practical.
- A discussion on how to implement sampling from ξ*-weighted dynamics without an exact model (e.g., importance sampling) would help.

Originality
The unifying static gradient formula for coherent risk, the SAA-based estimator for general envelopes, and especially the policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, are novel and significant. While built on known duality and envelope theorems, the synthesis into policy-gradient RL and the generalization beyond specific risk measures constitute a substantial contribution.

Significance
The work advances risk-sensitive RL by providing a general toolkit that is not tied to a specific measure and that aligns with time-consistent dynamic risk. This has potential impact for applications where the choice of risk is context-dependent (finance, operations). The connection to robust MDPs and function approximation broadens applicability. That said, practical adoption hinges on algorithmic feasibility (convex programs per gradient estimate; access to simulators; variance of estimators) and stronger empirical validation.

Strengths
- Unifying, general framework covering a broad class of coherent risks (static and dynamic).
- Clean derivation using duality and envelope theorem; recovers and simplifies known CVaR results.
- New policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent dynamic risk with a natural actor-critic interpretation.
- Solid theoretical grounding with consistency guarantees for the SAA estimator.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on explicit, differentiable risk envelopes and access to KKT multipliers; computational cost of solving a convex program per gradient estimate is not assessed.
- Dynamic method requires sampling under ξ*-reweighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per state; this is nontrivial in model-free, online RL.
- Experimental evaluation is very limited, with no tests of the dynamic algorithm or scalability; no comparisons to strong baselines in RL tasks.
- Many details deferred to the supplement; the main paper lacks enough specifics to reproduce the actor-critic.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a more detailed algorithmic description (pseudocode) and complexity analysis for both static SAA gradient estimation and the dynamic actor-critic, including how ξ* and multipliers are computed in practice.
- Discuss and, if possible, demonstrate techniques for sampling under ξ*-reweighted dynamics without full model access (e.g., importance sampling, doubly robust estimators), and analyze estimator bias/variance.
- Add empirical results on dynamic tasks (e.g., gridworlds, control benchmarks) comparing to risk-neutral PG, CVaR-specific methods, and robust MDP baselines, with ablations on variance and sample efficiency.
- Clarify the Lp extension beyond finite Ω and provide at least one nontrivial example of dfi/dp and dge/dp beyond CVaR (e.g., spectral risk).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a theoretically strong and novel framework with clear potential impact. However, the lack of substantive experiments and limited algorithmic details reduce its immediate practical significance. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying implementation details would make this a clear accept.

Reviewer confidence
High on theory; moderate on practical feasibility pending more experiments.
