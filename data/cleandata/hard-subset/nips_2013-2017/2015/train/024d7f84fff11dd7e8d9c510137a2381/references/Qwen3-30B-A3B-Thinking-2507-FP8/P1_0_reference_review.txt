

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors focus on risk-sensitive optimization where the objective involves a risk measure of the random cost rather than just the expected cost. They specifically consider coherent risk measures, which satisfy desirable properties (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, positive homogeneity) identified by Artzner et al. [2], and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measures.

The main contributions are:
1. A new gradient formula for static coherent risk that is convenient for sampling-based approximation
2. An algorithm for general static coherent risk involving sampling with convex programming
3. A new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, extending the classic policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings

The paper generalizes previous work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR [8, 30], mean-variance [18, 21], mean-semideviation [26]) by providing a unified approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors relate their work to recent developments in risk-sensitive RL, including the work of Chow et al. [8, 21] on CVaR, and the dynamic programming approach for coherent risk by Ruszczyński [24].

The paper builds on the theoretical foundation of coherent risk measures (Ruszczynski and Shapiro [25, 26]) but extends it to the RL setting, where the policy parameters affect the distribution of the stochastic process (unlike standard stochastic programming). The authors connect their approach to robust MDPs [19] and provide a clear theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive RL.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL using coherent risk measures. The gradient formulas (Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2) are novel and general, encompassing previous results as special cases.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The paper is technically sound with well-justified assumptions (Assumptions 2.2 and 4.1) and rigorous proofs (provided in the supplementary material). The representation theorem (Theorem 2.1) is applied effectively to derive the gradient formulas.

3. **Generality**: The approach applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, not just specific ones like CVaR or mean-variance. This is a significant advancement over previous work that focused on individual risk measures.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The numerical example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates how different risk measures lead to different policy choices, highlighting the importance of selecting an appropriate risk measure for the problem at hand.

5. **Clear Structure**: The paper is well-organized with clear definitions, theorems, and examples. The connection between the static and dynamic risk formulations is well-motivated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: While the paper includes a numerical example, it's limited to a single trading problem with three assets. The authors don't provide extensive experiments on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., Atari, MuJoCo) to demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach compared to existing risk-sensitive RL methods.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper proposes a two-step procedure (sampling + convex programming) but doesn't thoroughly analyze the computational complexity or scalability of their approach, especially for large-scale MDPs. This is a significant omission given the practical importance of computational efficiency in RL.

3. **Assumption Restrictiveness**: The assumption that the risk envelope has a canonical convex programming formulation (Assumption 2.2) might be restrictive for some practical applications. The paper doesn't discuss how to handle risk measures that don't fit this form.

4. **Comparison with Existing Methods**: The paper cites related work but doesn't provide a clear, quantitative comparison of their approach with existing risk-sensitive RL methods in terms of performance, convergence rate, or computational requirements.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly in cases where the risk envelope doesn't satisfy their assumptions or for risk measures that aren't coherent.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The assumptions are reasonable and well-justified, and the proofs are rigorous (though they're relegated to the supplementary material). The authors are careful to discuss the strengths of their approach, but they could have been more explicit about the limitations. The paper is a complete piece of work, not just a position paper.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The definitions are precise, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The examples (Sections 4.1-4.2) help clarify the theoretical results. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the computational aspects and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.

## Originality

The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL that unifies previous approaches. The gradient formulas (Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2) are original contributions that generalize previous results. The connection between coherent risk measures and robust MDPs (Section 5) is also a novel perspective. The paper significantly differs from previous contributions by focusing on the entire class of coherent risk measures rather than specific risk measures.

## Significance

The results are important for risk-sensitive RL, as they provide a general framework applicable to a wide range of risk measures. The work advances the state of the art by unifying previous approaches and providing a theoretically sound foundation. The numerical example demonstrates how the choice of risk measure can impact policy selection, which is practically significant for applications where risk management is critical. However, the significance would be greater with more extensive experimental validation on standard RL benchmarks.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Significant theoretical contribution that unifies previous work on risk-sensitive RL
- Novel gradient formulas for coherent risk measures that are general and well-supported
- Clear connection between coherent risk measures and robust MDPs
- Demonstrates the importance of choosing appropriate risk measures through a numerical example

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation on standard RL benchmarks
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Restrictive assumptions (Assumption 2.2) that might limit practical applicability
- Lack of quantitative comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. The theoretical contribution is strong and represents a significant advancement in risk-sensitive RL. The paper makes a clear and well-justified contribution to the literature.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the experimental section**: Include experiments on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari) to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach compared to existing methods.

2. **Add computational complexity analysis**: Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, especially for large-scale problems. Consider comparing the computational requirements with existing risk-sensitive RL methods.

3. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: Address the limitations of the assumptions (particularly Assumption 2.2) and discuss how the approach might be extended to handle risk measures that don't fit the canonical convex programming form.

4. **Improve comparison with related work**: Provide a clearer comparison of the proposed method with existing risk-sensitive RL methods in terms of performance, convergence rate, and computational requirements.

5. **Clarify the connection to robust MDPs**: The paper mentions the connection to robust MDPs [19] but doesn't fully exploit this relationship. Consider expanding this discussion as it could provide additional insights into the practical implications of the work.

## Final Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically sound and general approach to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning using coherent risk measures. It makes a significant contribution to the field by unifying previous work on specific risk measures into a single framework. While the experimental validation could be strengthened, the theoretical contribution is substantial and would be valuable to the RL community. I believe this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
