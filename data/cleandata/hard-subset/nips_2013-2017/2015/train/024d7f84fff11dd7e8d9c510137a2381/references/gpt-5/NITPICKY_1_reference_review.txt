Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for a broad class of coherent risk measures in RL. For static risk, it leverages the dual representation of coherent risk (as a worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope) and the envelope/Danskin-type reasoning to derive a gradient formula requiring a saddle point of the Lagrangian. It yields simple expressions in special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and proposes a general sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator involving sampling plus solving a convex program, with a consistency result. For dynamic Markov coherent risk (time-consistent), it presents a policy-gradient theorem that relates the gradient to an expectation under a reweighted transition kernel defined by the risk-envelope multipliers, leading to an actor-critic scheme that uses a robust-MDP-style critic. A small static toy experiment (asset selection) illustrates differences between risk criteria.

Relation to prior work
The paper aims to unify several strands: static CVaR/mean-variance gradients in RL, robust MDP connections to coherent risk, and dynamic, time-consistent coherent measures. It cites foundational work (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro; Rockafellar & Uryasev), RL policy-gradient methods (Sutton et al., Konda & Tsitsiklis), and recent risk-sensitive RL papers (CVaR in MDPs, robust MDPs with approximation). The generalization to arbitrary coherent risk envelopes is appealing and helps re-derive known CVaR gradients more cleanly.

Strengths
- General framework: one gradient formula covers any coherent risk with a convex envelope; extends and simplifies prior special-case treatments (e.g., CVaR).
- Static→dynamic bridge: a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and an actor-critic outline grounded in robust MDP equivalence.
- Practical estimator: SAA-based estimator combining sampling and convex optimization; claims of consistency under reasonable conditions.
- Clarity of high-level ideas and assumptions; explicit risk-envelope modeling encourages flexible risk shaping.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Differentiability/uniqueness: The gradient equalities rely on a saddle-point selection. If the maximizer of the dual (or KKT multipliers) is non-unique, Danskin’s theorem generally yields subgradients; the paper asserts a gradient for any saddle point without establishing differentiability or selection rules. This is particularly delicate for CVaR in discrete distributions where quantiles are non-unique and mass at the threshold is nonzero.
- Mean-semideviation formula divides by SD(Z); behavior when SD(Z)=0 is not addressed; requires regularization.
- SAA estimator differentiates constraints w.r.t. p in Assumption 2.2, but then applies them to empirical distributions Pθ;N. Clarify that the derivatives exist componentwise for finite Ω and that the estimator uses exact derivatives, not subgradients, of the empirical problem.
- Consistency assumptions in Proposition 4.4 are heavy and somewhat implicit (compactness of saddle-point sets, Slater across empirical envelopes, continuity in both ξ and p, and property (IV)). Provide concrete verification for common envelopes beyond the asserted examples.
- Dynamic PG theorem requires sampling under the ξ*-reweighted transition kernel. It is assumed to define a valid ergodic Markov chain and to be implementable in a simulator; conditions ensuring irreducibility/stability are not stated. The dependence of ξ* on Vθ (and thus on function approximation error) can bias the actor’s gradient; no error propagation bounds are provided in the main text.
- The actor-critic uses a modified robust-MDP value approximation; convergence and bias-variance properties are deferred. No rates or variance analysis for the gradient estimators are provided, though rare-event sensitivity is central to coherent risks.

Algorithmic and practical issues
- Computational burden: The static estimator requires solving a convex program per batch; the dynamic actor requires solving a statewise convex problem to obtain ξ* and multipliers, potentially at every update. Complexity vs. baselines is not discussed.
- Learning setting: The dynamic method assumes a generative model (multiple next-state samples per state). This is a strong assumption for RL; handling single-trajectory data or off-policy reuse is left open.
- No pseudocode, step-size rules, or implementation details (e.g., inner-solver accuracy vs. outer SGD bias, two-timescale conditions). Multiplier estimation noise can destabilize the actor.

Empirical evaluation
- Extremely limited. Only a toy static bandit-like example is shown; no dynamic experiments, no comparisons to robust MDP or prior CVaR-RL algorithms on standard benchmarks, no ablation or sensitivity (α, envelope choices), no runtime or sample complexity reporting.

Clarity and presentation
- Several results central to the contribution are deferred to the supplement; the main paper lacks proofs, algorithmic details, or sufficient guidance to reproduce results.
- Notational overload and imprecision: B is introduced as a set of probability distributions but then used for density reweightings ξ; the integral notation for finite Ω is off; sometimes ξ and ξPθ are conflated. The derivative operators dge/dp(ω) and dfi/dp(ω) need clearer vector notation.
- CVaR example uses strict inequalities Z>qα in discrete cases; equality mass is ignored.
- Footnotes and cross-references are awkward; some formatting inconsistencies.

Missing or under-emphasized related work
- Limited discussion of variance reduction and importance sampling for tail risks in RL beyond a brief citation.
- More recent distributional RL or risk-in-RL developments are not covered (may be timing-dependent but helps position contributions).

Questions for the authors
- Under what conditions on U(Pθ) is ρ(Z) differentiable in θ, and how do you handle non-uniqueness of saddle points (provide subgradient statements or selection rules)?
- How do you treat atoms at the CVaR quantile in the discrete case? Can you give a correct general expression for ξ on equality sets?
- What happens when SD(Z)=0 in Prop. 4.3? Do you regularize?
- Provide complexity estimates per gradient step (static and dynamic), including inner solver tolerance vs. outer SGD bias, and two-timescale conditions.
- Can you provide at least one dynamic benchmark experiment and a comparison to CVaR/robust-MDP baselines?

Minor issues and typos
- In B’s definition use sum for finite Ω; current integral is inconsistent.
- “John Willey & Sons” should be “John Wiley & Sons.” Several references lack years or have inconsistent formatting/capitalization.
- Clarify that U depends on x in the dynamic case; this is footnoted but should be in main text and notation.
- Ensure consistent use of cost vs. reward conventions.

Assessment
- Quality: Medium. Interesting theory but key details deferred; assumptions and differentiability issues not fully addressed; experiments inadequate.
- Clarity: Medium. High-level ideas are clear; notation, details, and reproducibility need work.
- Originality: Medium-High. Generalization to arbitrary coherent envelopes and the dynamic PG theorem are valuable.
- Significance: Medium. Potentially impactful, but current empirical and practical evidence is weak.

Arguments for acceptance
- General, elegant framework unifying static and dynamic coherent risk gradients.
- Recovers and simplifies known results; extends to broader class.
- Timely topic bridging robust control and RL.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient experiments; no dynamic validations.
- Key technical nuances (differentiability, multiplicity of saddle points) and algorithmic details are missing from the main paper.
- Strong assumptions (generative model, per-state convex solves) and unclear practicality.
- Notational and formatting issues; several minor but pervasive weaknesses.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper has promising and potentially significant ideas, but the current submission lacks the technical precision, empirical validation, and clarity required for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to address the differentiability and uniqueness issues rigorously, add dynamic experiments with comparisons, provide algorithmic details and complexity analysis, and improve presentation. Confidence: high.
