### Paper Summary

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors tackle two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted return, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

The core technical contribution is a novel formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure. This formula is derived by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures, where the risk is expressed as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities (the risk envelope). By applying the Envelope Theorem to this dual formulation, the authors express the policy gradient in a form amenable to sampling-based estimation. This general formula is shown to elegantly re-derive and simplify existing results for specific risk measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) and derive new ones, such as for the mean-semideviation risk. For cases where an analytical solution is unavailable, a general algorithm is proposed that estimates the gradient by solving a sample average approximation (SAA) of the dual problem, which is a convex program.

For the dynamic risk setting, the paper introduces a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures. This theorem connects the gradient of the risk-sensitive value function to a risk-adjusted state-action value function, analogous to the standard policy gradient theorem. This result forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm for optimizing dynamic risk in large-scale MDPs. The work positions itself as a significant generalization and unification of previous research that has largely focused on individual risk measures (e.g., [8, 21, 30]) in an ad-hoc manner.

### Strengths

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The paper's primary strength is its highly general and principled approach. By building the framework upon the axiomatic foundation of coherent risk measures, it provides a unified method for a vast and important class of risk objectives. This is a significant conceptual leap beyond prior work that developed bespoke algorithms for specific risk measures like mean-variance or CVaR. This unified view simplifies the landscape and provides a clear recipe for applying policy gradients to any coherent risk measure, provided its risk envelope is known.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Theoretical Results:** The derivation of the gradient for static risk in Theorem 4.2 is novel and elegant. The use of the dual representation and the Envelope Theorem is a powerful technique that cleanly separates the policy-dependent probability measure from the risk-defining density. This not only simplifies proofs for existing risk measures (as shown for CVaR) but also makes the derivation of gradients for new measures straightforward. The policy gradient theorem for dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2) is also a significant theoretical contribution, extending the foundational principles of policy gradient methods to the domain of time-consistent risk.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear introduction to coherent risk measures and their dual representation, making the work accessible. The logical progression from the static risk case to the more complex dynamic risk case is effective and easy to follow.

4.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** This work has the potential for high impact. It equips researchers and practitioners with a powerful and flexible toolkit for risk-sensitive RL. The ability to select or even design a coherent risk measure tailored to a specific problem, and then have a general-purpose algorithm to optimize it, is a major advance. The discussion in the conclusion about using risk-shaping to handle model uncertainty is particularly insightful and points towards important future research directions. The simple numerical example, while not a complex benchmark, effectively illustrates why this flexibility is crucial, showing how a naive choice of risk measure (mean-standard-deviation) can lead to suboptimal behavior in the presence of heavy-tailed distributions.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Cost of the General Algorithm:** The general gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) for each gradient estimate. While convex programs can be solved efficiently, this adds a potentially significant computational overhead within the optimization loop, especially if the sample size N is large. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the practical computational complexity of this inner loop and its scalability. For instance, how does the cost scale with the number of samples N and the complexity of the risk envelope's constraints?

2.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration is effective for motivation but is too simple to demonstrate the efficacy and scalability of the proposed algorithms. The paper makes claims about applicability to large or continuous MDPs, but this is not substantiated experimentally. A stronger empirical evaluation, for example applying the proposed actor-critic algorithm to a standard continuous control benchmark from a suite like OpenAI Gym or DeepMind Control Suite, would make the paper's contributions more convincing. This would allow for a comparison of performance, sample efficiency, and computational time against risk-neutral baselines or methods tailored to specific risk measures.

3.  **Details of the Dynamic Risk Algorithm:** The details of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk are largely deferred to the supplementary material. While space limitations are understood, the main text is somewhat high-level. The description of the actor update mentions a "two-phase sampling procedure" but provides no detail on how it works. Furthermore, the paper notes that the algorithm requires a simulator to sample multiple next-state transitions from each state in a trajectory. This is a significant practical limitation compared to many modern RL algorithms that operate on a single stream of experience. This limitation and potential ways to overcome it (e.g., using off-policy corrections) should be discussed more prominently in the main paper.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures is novel, elegant, and impactful. The theoretical results are sound and clearly presented. The main weaknesses are the limited experimental validation and the lack of detail on the practical implementation and computational cost of the proposed algorithms. However, the strength and importance of the theoretical and conceptual contributions are substantial and outweigh these weaknesses. The paper provides a strong foundation for future research and practical applications in risk-sensitive decision-making. I recommend acceptance.
