

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors generalize previous work on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.) by developing gradient formulas for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The main contributions include a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, a sampling-based algorithm for general static coherent risk, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper establishes a strong theoretical foundation by leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1). The gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are rigorously derived using convex optimization theory and the envelope theorem, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Unification of Risk Measures:** The paper successfully unifies various risk-sensitive RL approaches under a single coherent framework. The examples (CVaR and mean-semideviation) demonstrate how previous results can be recovered as special cases, showing the generality of the approach.

3. **Practical Relevance:** The numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of selecting appropriate risk measures for specific problem characteristics, particularly highlighting how different risk measures lead to different investment strategies in a financial context.

4. **Connection to Robust MDPs:** The paper makes an insightful connection between coherent risk measures and robust MDPs (citing [19]), which provides a useful conceptual bridge to existing work in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The paper lacks comprehensive empirical validation. While Section 6 provides a simple numerical example, it doesn't compare the proposed method against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari). This is a significant omission for a NeurIPS submission, as it's difficult to assess the practical utility of the method without such comparisons.

2. **Algorithmic Details:** The paper mentions "space limitations" for the actor-critic algorithm details, but the supplementary material should contain these. Without seeing the full algorithm, it's difficult to assess the practical implementation challenges, particularly regarding the two-phase sampling procedure for dynamic risk.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper doesn't provide a clear analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing approaches. For instance, how does the convex programming step in the static risk case scale with the number of samples or the size of the risk envelope?

4. **Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper mentions that previous results are special cases, it could better clarify how their approach differs from or improves upon existing risk-sensitive RL methods, such as [29] for mean-variance or [8, 30] for CVaR.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Include experiments on standard RL benchmarks comparing the proposed method with existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms. At minimum, show how the method performs on a simple grid-world problem where risk-sensitive behavior is clearly beneficial.

2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, especially the convex programming step in the static risk case. How does the runtime scale with the number of samples and constraints?

3. Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and the robust MDP approach from [19] in the main text. The connection is mentioned but could be more thoroughly explained.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method handles continuous state and action spaces, which is a common scenario in modern RL applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unification of coherent risk measures under a single framework is a significant theoretical advance. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation on standard RL benchmarks and limited discussion of computational complexity are substantial weaknesses for a NeurIPS submission.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by including empirical results that demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed approach, particularly in comparison to existing methods. The theoretical contribution is strong, but NeurIPS places high value on both theoretical and empirical contributions, and the paper currently falls short on the latter.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend a borderline decision. The theoretical contribution is strong and novel, but the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation is a significant drawback for a NeurIPS submission. With additional empirical results on standard RL benchmarks, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, it would benefit from the additional experiments before being accepted.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Strong theoretical foundation based on coherent risk measure theory
- Unified framework for risk-sensitive RL
- Novel gradient formulas for coherent risk measures
- Insightful connection to robust MDPs

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited empirical validation on standard RL benchmarks
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity
- Algorithmic details for dynamic risk case are not fully specified
- Lacks comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods on practical problems
