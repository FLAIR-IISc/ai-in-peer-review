Summary
The paper studies reinforcement learning with risk-sensitive objectives, focusing on the broad class of coherent risk measures. It provides two main technical contributions:

1) Static setting: Using the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) and an envelope-theorem argument, the authors derive a general policy-gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) for ∇θρ(Z) that depends on a Lagrangian saddle point of the convex program induced by the risk envelope. This yields likelihood-ratio style estimators. They show that known results for specific risks follow as special cases, e.g., CVaR (recovering the tail-conditional gradient without continuity assumptions) and mean-semideviation. For general coherent risks with known risk envelopes, they propose a sample-average approximation (SAA) procedure that solves a finite convex program to obtain the saddle point and KKT multipliers and then forms a gradient estimator; they prove a consistency result (Proposition 4.4) under mild regularity assumptions.

2) Dynamic setting: For Markov coherent (time-consistent) risk measures (as in Ruszczyński [24]), they derive a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) for ∇θVθ(x) that expresses the gradient as an expectation under a ξ-weighted transition kernel arising from the static risk envelope at each state, with a stage-wise cost hθ that incorporates the value function and derivatives of the envelope constraints. This connects to the equivalence between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs (e.g., [19]) and motivates an actor-critic algorithm: a critic that estimates Vθ (adapting robust MDP function approximation methods, e.g., [31]) and an actor that estimates the gradient using trajectories generated under the ξ-weighted kernel. A small numerical illustration highlights the usefulness of choosing appropriate risk criteria; main experimental validation is otherwise limited.

Relation to prior work: The paper unifies several lines of risk-sensitive RL. Prior RL works targeted specific criteria: exponential utility [6, 22], mean-variance and related [18, 21, 29], and CVaR [8, 30]. Robust/Markov coherent risk in MDPs has been studied for planning via dynamic programming and approximations [19, 24, 31]. The present work generalizes policy-gradient methods to the entire coherent class, recovers known special-case gradients (e.g., CVaR) in a simpler way, and extends policy gradient theory to Markov coherent risk.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The static gradient derivation via envelope theorem and Lagrangian saddle points is well-motivated and correctly leverages the coherent risk dual form. The SAA estimator and its consistency are plausible under the stated assumptions (compactness, continuity, Slater-type conditions). The dynamic policy gradient theorem builds on the risk-sensitive Bellman equation and is consistent with the robust MDP interpretation. However, several important technical conditions are deferred: ergodicity/mixing of the ξ-weighted kernel, conditions ensuring interchange of differentiation and maximization, and uniqueness/selection of saddle points (especially for non-unique quantiles as in CVaR). The actor-critic convergence and approximation error analysis are in the supplement; the main text lacks details to fully assess them.
- Empirical support: The experiments are minimal and focus on a static, bandit-like example. There is no empirical validation of the dynamic actor-critic or scalability on larger MDPs. As a result, practical performance and robustness of the proposed algorithms remain unsubstantiated.
- Algorithmic practicality: The approach requires computing KKT multipliers by solving convex programs, potentially per batch (static) or per state (dynamic) to obtain ξ*, which may be computationally heavy in large-scale settings. The need to sample under ξ-weighted transitions is non-trivial in standard simulators; the paper proposes a two-phase procedure, but details and costs are largely in the supplement.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with clear problem statements, assumptions, and illustrative examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation. Notation is mostly consistent, though dense; clearer separation between dependence on θ via Pθ versus via the envelope constraints would help. Important algorithmic details (critic approximation scheme, actor sampling under ξ, pseudocode, complexity) are omitted due to space, making it hard to reproduce. Some regularity conditions needed for Theorem 5.2 are only sketched.

Originality
The main novelty is a unified policy-gradient framework for coherent risks: a general static gradient formula that recovers prior special cases, an SAA-based estimator with consistency guarantees, and a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk. While individual pieces (duality of coherent risks, robust MDP connections) are known, the combination into a general PG framework and the dynamic gradient theorem appear original and valuable.

Significance
Conceptually, the paper advances risk-sensitive RL by moving from ad hoc criteria to a unified treatment that can encompass CVaR, spectral measures, mean-semideviation, etc. This could influence how risk is incorporated into RL. Practically, the need for explicit risk envelopes, inner convex optimization, and ξ-weighted sampling may limit immediate applicability. The lack of dynamic experiments weakens evidence of impact on realistic RL tasks.

Pros
- General, principled gradient formula for static coherent risks; recovers and simplifies known special cases (e.g., CVaR).
- Consistent SAA-based estimator for general envelopes, broadening applicability beyond closed-form cases.
- New policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk; ties to robust MDPs and suggests actor-critic methods.
- Clear articulation of assumptions and relation to prior literature.

Cons
- Minimal experimental validation; no dynamic RL experiments or comparisons.
- Practicality concerns: inner convex programs, per-state envelopes, and sampling under ξ-weighted kernels.
- Some key technical conditions and algorithm details are deferred to the supplement; main text lacks convergence and complexity evidence.
- Assumptions restrict to finite spaces and explicit, smooth envelopes; extensions to continuous spaces are claimed but not developed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include dynamic RL experiments (e.g., gridworlds, queueing, portfolio, or continuous control) comparing to CVaR- and variance-based baselines and robust MDP planners; report sample efficiency and computational overhead.
- Provide pseudocode and a complexity analysis for the SAA gradient and actor-critic, including how KKT multipliers are obtained efficiently and how ξ-weighted sampling is implemented with standard simulators.
- State precise conditions for Theorem 5.2 (ergodicity of modified kernels, measurability, saddle-point selection) and summarize convergence guarantees in the main text.
- Discuss variance reduction/importance sampling for tail-sensitive measures and give practical guidance on sample sizes for small α in CVaR.
- Clarify extensions to continuous state/action spaces and function approximation for both critic and the envelope constraints.

Overall assessment
The paper offers a substantial and novel theoretical framework with potential to unify risk-sensitive RL methods. However, the lack of dynamic empirical validation and limited practical details make it difficult to assess applicability and impact. If strengthened with experiments and clearer algorithmic exposition, it would be a strong contribution. As it stands, it is a borderline case with strong theory but insufficient empirical support.
