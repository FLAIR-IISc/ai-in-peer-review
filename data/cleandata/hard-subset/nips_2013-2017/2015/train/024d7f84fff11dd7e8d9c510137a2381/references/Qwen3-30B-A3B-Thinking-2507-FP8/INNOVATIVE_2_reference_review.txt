

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a significant conceptual contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. The authors address a fundamental gap in the literature: most prior work on risk-sensitive RL focuses on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance), while this paper provides a general approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures.

The main contribution is a novel policy gradient theorem for coherent risk measures that generalizes and unifies previous approaches. The key insight comes from leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), which shows that any coherent risk measure can be expressed as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities. This leads to the crucial gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) that enables sampling-based policy gradient estimation for any coherent risk measure, not just specific instances like CVaR.

The paper makes a bold conceptual leap by connecting the well-established coherent risk framework from finance to reinforcement learning. This is significant because it provides a principled way to design risk preferences within a unified mathematical framework. The authors demonstrate how their approach subsumes and generalizes previous results for CVaR, mean-semideviation, and other specific risk measures, showing that their framework provides a more elegant and general solution.

The technical contribution is strong: Theorem 4.2 provides a new gradient formula that is convenient for sampling-based estimation, and Theorem 5.2 extends this to dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. The authors provide both analytical solutions for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation) and a general sampling-based algorithm that combines convex programming with likelihood-ratio estimation. The numerical example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates the practical importance of flexible risk criteria in financial applications.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with clear connections to previous work on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 30] for CVaR; [18, 29] for mean-variance) and coherent risk measures from finance ([2, 26]). The authors correctly identify that previous approaches are limited to specific risk measures, while their work provides a unifying framework.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper highly compelling. The conceptual contribution is significant: rather than developing another algorithm for a specific risk measure, the authors provide a general framework that opens up new research directions. This is exactly the kind of bold, unifying approach that advances the field. The authors successfully demonstrate how their framework can be applied to multiple risk measures, showing both theoretical elegance and practical utility.

The main limitation is that the paper focuses on finite probability spaces, which might limit direct applicability to continuous state spaces. However, the authors note this is a simplifying assumption for exposition, and the core ideas could be extended to continuous spaces. This minor limitation does not detract from the fundamental novelty of the approach.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient technical detail for reproduction. The connection to coherent risk from finance is particularly insightful and demonstrates the authors' deep understanding of the problem space.

This work is significant because it shifts the paradigm from developing separate algorithms for each risk measure to designing risk preferences within a unified framework. This has the potential to accelerate research in risk-sensitive RL and make it more accessible to practitioners. The authors' claim that "our approach allows to rederive them in greater generality and simplicity" is well-supported by their results.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, unifying framework for risk-sensitive RL that generalizes and simplifies previous approaches. The conceptual contribution is bold and significant, with clear potential to influence future research in the field. While the finite probability space assumption is a minor limitation, it does not undermine the paper's core contribution. As a reviewer who values novelty and conceptual advances, I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of reinforcement learning and should be accepted at NIPS.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Presents a unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL, a significant conceptual advance
- Generalizes and simplifies previous work on specific risk measures
- Provides a principled connection between finance and RL risk frameworks
- Demonstrates practical utility through numerical examples
- Clear theoretical contribution with well-supported theorems

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Finite probability space assumption limits direct applicability to continuous domains
- Numerical example is somewhat limited to a single financial application

However, the novelty and conceptual significance of this work outweigh these minor limitations. This paper represents a clear step forward in risk-sensitive RL by providing a general theoretical framework rather than incremental improvements to specific algorithms.
