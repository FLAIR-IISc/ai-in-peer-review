

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary

This paper develops a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning (RL) with coherent risk objectives. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by providing a general approach that works for the entire class of coherent risk measures (rather than being limited to specific measures like CVaR or mean-variance), while also handling both static and time-consistent dynamic risk measures. The core technical contribution is the derivation of gradient formulas for coherent risk measures using the representation theorem (Theorem 2.1), which characterizes coherent risk as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope. The paper presents two main algorithms: one for static coherent risk using sampling and convex programming, and another for dynamic Markov coherent risk using a policy gradient theorem extended to this risk framework. The work builds upon prior research on coherent risk measures [2, 26], dynamic risk measures [24], and policy gradient methods [15, 28], while generalizing previous approaches to specific risk measures [8, 18, 21, 30].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Generality**: The paper's main strength is its theoretical contribution - it provides a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant advancement over previous work that focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR [8, 30] or mean-variance [18, 21]). The representation theorem (Theorem 2.1) is central to their approach, and they successfully leverage it to derive gradient formulas that apply broadly.

2. **Rigorous Theoretical Development**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) with detailed proofs in the supplementary material. The assumptions are well-motivated, and the consistency results (Proposition 4.4) are properly established. The connection between the risk envelope and the gradient estimation procedure is elegantly developed.

3. **Practical Algorithm Design**: The proposed two-step algorithm (sampling followed by convex programming) for gradient estimation is well-motivated and practical. The authors clearly explain the computational procedure and provide a numerical example demonstrating its utility.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-written, logically organized, and effectively balances theoretical development with practical motivation. The examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) effectively demonstrate how their framework generalizes previous results.

5. **Significant Contribution to Risk-Sensitive RL**: The paper addresses an important gap in the RL literature by providing a general framework for risk-sensitive optimization that works for the entire class of coherent risk measures, which is a substantial improvement over previous work limited to specific risk metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: While the paper includes a numerical example (Section 6) showing different risk measures lead to different policies in a portfolio optimization problem, this is quite limited. More comprehensive experiments on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., Atari, MuJoCo) would strengthen the paper by demonstrating the approach's practical utility in more complex settings.

2. **Computational Complexity Discussion**: The paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, particularly the convex programming step required for each gradient estimation. For large-scale MDPs, solving a convex optimization problem for each gradient estimate could be prohibitively expensive, and this limitation should be more thoroughly discussed.

3. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper mentions related work but lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods. A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the advantages of the proposed approach over alternatives.

4. **Assumption Limitations**: The paper makes several assumptions (e.g., on the risk envelope structure, smoothness of constraints) that may not hold for all practical applications. The authors should more thoroughly discuss these limitations and potential extensions.

5. **Dynamic Risk Implementation Details**: The paper briefly mentions that their actor-critic algorithm requires a simulator for sampling multiple state-transitions from each state, but doesn't elaborate on how this could be implemented in practice, especially for environments where such a simulator isn't available.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical results are rigorously developed, and the assumptions are carefully justified. The paper is a complete contribution, not merely a position paper. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding computational complexity and the need for a simulator.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The paper provides sufficient information for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the theoretical results. The supplementary material provides the necessary details for the proofs and additional examples. However, the experimental section could be strengthened with more comprehensive results.

## Originality Assessment

This paper is highly original in its approach to risk-sensitive RL. While there is prior work on risk-sensitive RL for specific risk measures, this paper provides a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The connection to the representation theorem for coherent risk measures and the use of convex optimization to estimate gradients is novel. A thorough search of recent NIPS proceedings confirms this is a significant contribution beyond previous work.

## Significance Assessment

The results are highly significant as they provide a general method for risk-sensitive RL that works for the entire class of coherent risk measures. This addresses a fundamental limitation in the literature where previous work was limited to specific risk measures. The framework could be widely adopted by the RL community for risk-sensitive applications. The numerical example demonstrates practical utility, though more extensive experiments would better establish significance.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are rigorous, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper is clearly written. While the experimental validation is limited, the theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and has the potential to influence future research in risk-sensitive RL.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the experimental section with more comprehensive results on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., Atari, MuJoCo) to better demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, including potential approximations or heuristics for large-scale problems.

3. Include a more comprehensive comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods to better highlight the advantages of the proposed framework.

4. Consider adding a discussion of how the approach could be extended to handle model uncertainty, which is mentioned in the conclusion but not developed in the paper.

5. Clarify the implementation details of the dynamic risk algorithm, particularly regarding the need for a simulator and how this could be addressed in environments where a simulator isn't available.

## Final Thoughts

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for coherent risk measures is novel and well-executed. While the experimental results could be expanded, the theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper has the potential to influence future research in risk-sensitive RL by providing a general framework that can be applied to many risk measures.
