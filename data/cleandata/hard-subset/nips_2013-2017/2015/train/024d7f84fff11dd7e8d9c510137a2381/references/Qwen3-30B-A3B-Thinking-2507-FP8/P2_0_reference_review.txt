

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors develop gradient estimation algorithms for both static and dynamic Markov coherent risk measures, generalizing previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The key insight is leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (which states they can be expressed as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope) to derive gradient formulas that enable sampling-based estimation. For static risk, they provide a new gradient formula and a sampling-based algorithm involving convex programming. For dynamic risk, they derive a policy gradient theorem that extends the classic policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures. The paper demonstrates how their framework generalizes several previous results (e.g., for CVaR and mean-semideviation) and provides a numerical example showing the importance of flexible risk criteria.

The work relates to previous NeurIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 10, 21, 30] on CVaR; [18, 29] on mean-variance; [20, 9] on dynamic coherent risk) by unifying these approaches under a general coherent risk framework. It also connects to broader literature on coherent risk measures [2, 26] and policy gradient methods [15, 28].

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-grounded in established theory. The authors make appropriate use of the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) and apply the envelope theorem to derive their gradient formulas (Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2). The theoretical development is rigorous and well-justified, with clear assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 on the risk envelope). The proof sketches in the supplementary material appear to be complete. The numerical example in Section 6 provides a meaningful empirical validation of the framework's utility in selecting appropriate risk measures for specific problems.

### Originality
This is a significant theoretical contribution that successfully unifies previous work on risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. The authors demonstrate how their approach generalizes previous results for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), which is a substantial advance over prior specialized approaches. The paper clearly articulates how their work differs from previous contributions, particularly by showing that many previous results are special cases of their general framework. The connection between coherent risk measures and robust MDPs (citing [19]) is also a valuable theoretical insight.

### Significance
The paper addresses an important problem in RL where managing risk (beyond just expected cost) is crucial for real-world applications, particularly in finance and operations research. The framework provides a principled way to incorporate a wide range of risk preferences into RL algorithms. The authors convincingly argue that the choice of risk measure should be problem-dependent, and their unified approach enables practitioners to select the most appropriate risk measure for their specific application. The numerical example effectively demonstrates this point, showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a trading scenario.

### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and clearly states the contributions. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The connection between the coherent risk framework and the policy gradient approach is explained with sufficient detail. The examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) help ground the theoretical development in concrete cases that readers will recognize from previous work.

## Weaknesses

### Quality
While the theoretical development is strong, the empirical validation is limited to a single numerical example. NeurIPS typically values more comprehensive experimental evaluation, especially for a method with potential practical applications. The paper doesn't compare the sample efficiency or computational complexity of their approach with existing methods for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR). It would be valuable to see how their convex programming-based gradient estimation compares to other approaches in terms of computational cost and sample requirements.

### Significance
The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the practical limitations of their approach. For instance, the requirement for a simulator that can generate multiple state transitions from each state (mentioned in Section 5) is a significant constraint for many real-world applications where simulation is expensive or unavailable. The paper could better address whether their approach is feasible for large-scale RL problems, which is a common concern in the RL community.

### Clarity
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms. How does the cost of solving the convex optimization problem scale with the size of the problem (number of states, samples, etc.)? This information would help readers assess the practical feasibility of the approach.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. The technical quality is high, the approach is novel in its generality, and the significance of the problem is well-established.

The main weakness is the limited empirical evaluation, which is somewhat surprising given the practical nature of the problem. While the single numerical example is useful, more comprehensive experiments would strengthen the paper's case for practical relevance. Additionally, the computational considerations of their approach could be better addressed.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that significantly advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL. The paper clearly demonstrates how their framework generalizes and unifies previous work, providing a principled way to incorporate a wide range of risk preferences into RL algorithms. While more empirical validation would be welcome, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.

The paper addresses an important problem in RL with clear connections to real-world applications (finance, operations research), and the unified framework it provides has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers in the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include more comprehensive empirical results comparing the proposed method with existing approaches for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance).

2. Add a discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the proposed algorithms, particularly regarding the convex programming step.

3. Address the practical limitations of the approach (e.g., the requirement for a simulator that can generate multiple state transitions) and suggest potential extensions for cases where this is not feasible.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the proposed framework might integrate with other recent advances in RL (e.g., deep RL, model-based methods).

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Provides a theoretically sound, unified framework for risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures
- Generalizes and unifies previous work on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.)
- Includes a clear numerical example demonstrating the importance of flexible risk criteria
- Well-written and well-structured paper with solid theoretical foundations

**Con:**
- Limited empirical validation beyond a single numerical example
- Does not thoroughly address computational complexity and practical feasibility
- Does not compare the proposed method with existing approaches for specific risk measures

The strength of the theoretical contribution outweighs the limitations in empirical validation, making this a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
