This paper presents policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, generalizing existing approaches and providing novel formulas for static and dynamic risk gradients. While the core idea of extending risk-sensitive RL to the full class of coherent risk measures is valuable, the paper suffers from a plethora of minor flaws – in presentation, technical detail, and completeness – that collectively detract from its overall impact and raise concerns about the rigor of the work.

**Summary:** The authors aim to provide a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk criteria. They derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, leveraging the duality representation of coherent risk measures. The work builds upon previous research in risk-sensitive RL, specifically addressing the limitations of existing methods tied to particular risk measures.

**Strengths:**
* **Generality:** The approach’s potential to encompass the entire class of coherent risk measures is a significant strength. It provides a unifying framework and avoids the need to derive separate algorithms for each specific risk measure.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The use of coherent risk duality (Theorem 2.1) provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed algorithms.
* **Gradient Formulas:** The derived gradient formulas are potentially useful for implementing risk-sensitive policy gradient methods.
* **Connection to Robust MDPs:** Recognizing the connection between dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs (Reference [19]) is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The notation is often inconsistent and confusing. For example, the use of both *Z* for a random variable and as a set/space is problematic. The suppression of θ-dependence, while mentioned, is often overlooked in equations making them difficult to follow.  The definition of 'ξ' varies throughout the paper without clear indication.
* **Lack of Rigor in Assumptions:** Assumption 2.2, while standard, is stated without sufficient justification for its applicability in various RL settings. The smoothness requirement on constraints is particularly strong and its practical relevance needs to be discussed.  The justification for restricting attention to finite probability spaces in Section 2 is weak ("details omitted for brevity") and significantly limits the scope of the work.
* **Missing Proofs & Details:**  The proof of Theorem 4.2 is “in the supplementary material,” which is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. Critical details are glossed over, hindering reproducibility.  For example, the SAA estimator in Section 4.3 relies on Proposition 4.4, whose assumptions, while stated, lack a clear explanation of why they hold for common risk measures.
* **Incremental Contribution:** While the generalization is valuable, many of the specific results (e.g., the CVaR gradient) are already known. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a substantial advance beyond re-deriving existing results in a more general framework.
* **Algorithm Specificity:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm in Section 5 is too high-level.  Key implementation details, such as how the critic estimates *V<sub>θ</sub>* and how the sampling is performed with ξ<sup>*</sup><sub>θ</sub>-weighted transitions, are missing.  The claim of leveraging [31] is vague and requires more elaboration.
* **Numerical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration in Section 6 is underwhelming. It only demonstrates the algorithm’s ability to select different assets based on risk preference, but doesn't quantify performance gains or compare against baseline algorithms. The choice of assets is also somewhat contrived and doesn't represent a realistic application.
* **Numerous Minor Errors:** Throughout the paper, there are minor typographical errors, grammatical inconsistencies, and unclear phrasing. For example, in the introduction, the phrase “thereby generalizing and unifying previous approaches” is vague and doesn’t clearly articulate the contribution. Several references are missing year information (e.g., "[5]", "[15]", "[27]")

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Generalization of risk-sensitive RL to coherent risk measures.
* Development of new gradient formulas.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
* Lack of rigor in assumptions and proofs.
* Insufficient detail for reproducibility.
* Incremental contribution and underwhelming numerical evaluation.
* Numerous minor flaws in presentation and technical detail.
* Weak justification for core assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper addresses a relevant and important problem, the numerous flaws outlined above significantly detract from its quality. The lack of rigor, incomplete details, and incremental contribution outweigh the potential benefits of the proposed framework.  A major revision, addressing the points raised above – including providing full proofs, clarifying notation, and performing a more thorough empirical evaluation – would be required for reconsideration. The authors should prioritize clarity, completeness, and a more compelling demonstration of the practical impact of their work. The current version, unfortunately, falls short of NIPS standards.
