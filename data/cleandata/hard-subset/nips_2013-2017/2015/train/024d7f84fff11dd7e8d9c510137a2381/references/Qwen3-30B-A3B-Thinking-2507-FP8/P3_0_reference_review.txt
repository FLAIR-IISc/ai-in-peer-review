

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper develops policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as the objective function. The authors address a gap in the literature by providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL that encompasses multiple specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation, etc.) rather than focusing on individual risk metrics. The paper makes two key theoretical contributions: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk measures that enables sampling-based estimation, and (2) a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures that extends the classic policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings. The work builds on the theory of coherent risk measures (Artzner et al., 1999) and leverages their representation as worst-case expectations (Theorem 2.1) to derive practical algorithms. The paper demonstrates the approach with a numerical example showing how different risk measures can lead to different policy choices in a financial trading context.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper builds on a well-established theoretical framework (coherent risk measures) and makes excellent use of Theorem 2.1 to derive new results. The connection between coherent risk measures and worst-case expectations is fundamental to the approach and well-explained.

2. **Unified Framework**: The paper successfully generalizes previous work on risk-sensitive RL. The authors explicitly show how existing results for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation) are special cases of their more general framework, which is a significant contribution.

3. **Practical Algorithm Design**: The proposed algorithms are grounded in practical considerations. The two-step procedure (sampling followed by convex programming) for gradient estimation is well-motivated and implementable with standard tools. The numerical example demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

4. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly distinguishes between static and dynamic risk problems, and the connection between them is well-articulated. The assumptions made (particularly Assumption 2.2) are reasonable and well-justified.

5. **Thoughtful Discussion of Limitations**: The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the computational cost of the convex programming step and the assumption that the risk envelope is known in explicit form. They also provide a promising direction for future work regarding model uncertainty.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Computational Complexity**: The proposed algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem for each gradient estimate. For large-scale problems, this could be prohibitively expensive. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity or discuss potential approximations to reduce this cost. For example, in the CVaR case, [30] provides a more efficient estimator that avoids convex programming.

2. **Assumption on Risk Envelope**: The approach relies heavily on Assumption 2.2, which requires the risk envelope to be known in explicit convex programming form. This might not hold for many risk measures in practice, or the explicit form might be difficult to obtain. The paper doesn't address how to handle cases where the risk envelope is not available in this form.

3. **Limited Empirical Comparison**: While the numerical example in Section 6 is useful, it only compares three specific policies trained with different risk measures. There's no comparison of the proposed gradient estimator against existing methods for specific risk measures (e.g., comparing the SAA estimator with the analytical estimator for CVaR). The paper claims the two approaches perform similarly (based on supplementary material), but this isn't verified in the main paper.

4. **Dynamic Risk Section**: The dynamic risk section (Section 5) feels somewhat rushed compared to the static case. The connection between the static gradient formula and the dynamic risk is not as clearly motivated. The actor-critic algorithm is described at a high level without sufficient detail on how it would be implemented or how it scales.

5. **Lack of Theoretical Convergence Guarantees**: The paper provides a consistency result for the static risk gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4), but doesn't provide convergence guarantees for the overall policy gradient algorithm. For a NeurIPS paper, some theoretical analysis of the convergence of the full algorithm would be expected.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly situates itself within the literature on risk-sensitive RL. It acknowledges previous work on specific risk measures (e.g., [8, 30] for CVaR; [18, 21, 29] for mean-variance) and correctly identifies that their approach generalizes these results. The connection to [24] (dynamic Markov coherent risk measures) and [19] (robust MDPs) is well-established.

However, the paper could have more explicitly compared its approach to [20, 9] (which studied dynamic coherent risk for linear systems) and [25, 26] (which studied optimization of coherent risk measures in stochastic programming). The authors claim their approach is different because it's for MDPs, but the connection to these works could be strengthened.

## Technical Assessment

The theoretical results are sound and well-supported. The proof techniques (using the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick) are standard in policy gradient literature but applied in a novel way to the risk-sensitive setting. The key insight (Theorem 4.2) is elegant and useful.

The paper makes reasonable assumptions (e.g., bounded likelihood ratio, smoothness of constraints), and these are well-justified for typical RL applications. The extension to dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2) is a significant contribution that properly generalizes the classic policy gradient theorem.

## Significance

Risk-sensitive RL is an important area with applications in finance, robotics, and operations research. The ability to handle a wide class of risk measures rather than just specific ones is valuable for practitioners who need to tailor risk preferences to specific problems. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates this point, showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a trading context.

The connection to model uncertainty mentioned in the conclusion (Section 7) is particularly promising and could lead to future work on robust decision-making. This is a significant contribution to the field.

## Recommendations

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework that generalizes previous work. The results are well-motivated, technically sound, and have clear practical implications. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding computational complexity), these are acknowledged by the authors and are reasonable for a theoretical contribution.

The paper is within the scope of NeurIPS as it makes a technical contribution to reinforcement learning with clear theoretical foundations. The work is novel, well-written, and addresses an important problem in the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 4.3, provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the convex programming step and potential approximations for large-scale problems.

2. In Section 5, elaborate on the implementation details of the actor-critic algorithm and provide more insight into how it would scale to large state spaces.

3. Include a comparison of the proposed gradient estimator with existing methods for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR) in the main paper, rather than relegating it to the supplementary material.

4. Provide more concrete examples of risk measures that satisfy Assumption 2.2 beyond CVaR and mean-semideviation.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how to choose an appropriate risk measure for a given problem, which would complement the numerical example in Section 6.

## Final Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The results are well-supported, the approach is novel, and the paper effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the framework. While there are some limitations regarding computational complexity, these are acknowledged and reasonable for a theoretical contribution. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a meaningful advance in the field. I recommend acceptance to NeurIPS.

The work has the potential to significantly impact risk-sensitive RL applications, particularly in finance and operations research, where the choice of risk measure is critical to decision-making. The connection to model uncertainty mentioned in the conclusion opens up promising avenues for future research.
