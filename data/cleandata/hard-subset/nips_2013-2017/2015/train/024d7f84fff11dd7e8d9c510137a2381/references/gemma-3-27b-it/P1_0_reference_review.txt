## Peer Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It provides a general framework for applying policy gradient methods to problems with coherent risk objectives, unifying and extending previous work. The theoretical results are solid, and the paper demonstrates a clear understanding of both coherent risk measures and policy gradient techniques. While the presentation could be slightly more accessible, the paper is well-structured and provides a strong foundation for future research. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of optimizing policies in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) under coherent risk measures, a class of risk measures satisfying desirable properties like convexity, monotonicity, and translation invariance.  Existing approaches often focus on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). This work generalizes these by providing policy gradient algorithms applicable to *any* coherent risk measure, both static and dynamic (time-consistent).  The core contributions are novel gradient formulas for static coherent risk and a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk, along with an actor-critic algorithm. 

The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging related work on risk-sensitive RL with specific risk functions, robust MDPs, and stochastic programming.  They clearly articulate how their approach differs by providing a more general, unified framework and focusing on sampling-based methods suitable for large-scale problems, contrasting with dynamic programming approaches that suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The connection to robust MDPs is also appropriately noted.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The primary strength is the broad applicability of the proposed framework.  By working with coherent risk measures, the authors provide a unifying theory for various risk-sensitive RL problems.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the gradient formulas and the policy gradient theorem appears mathematically sound, building on established results in convex optimization and stochastic gradient descent. The inclusion of proofs in the supplementary material is commendable.
* **Sampling-Based Approach:** The reliance on sampling makes the proposed algorithms scalable to large MDPs, a crucial advantage over dynamic programming approaches. The SAA-based gradient estimation for general coherent risk is particularly appealing.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The development of an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk provides a concrete method for implementing the theoretical results.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example highlights the practical importance of selecting appropriate risk measures and demonstrates the potential of the framework in a financial application.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper is quite dense and assumes a strong background in both stochastic optimization and reinforcement learning.  The notation is heavy and could be simplified in some places.  Providing more intuition and informal explanations alongside the mathematical derivations would improve clarity.
* **Assumptions:** The reliance on assumptions like Assumption 2.2 (the general form of the risk envelope) and Assumption 4.1 (bounded likelihood ratio) needs to be discussed further. While standard, it’s important to explicitly acknowledge potential limitations in cases where these assumptions don’t hold.
* **Computational Complexity:** While sampling addresses scalability, the convex programming step in the gradient estimation for static risk (Section 4.3) could be computationally expensive for very high-dimensional problems. A discussion of the computational cost and potential approximations would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is helpful but relatively simple.  More comprehensive experiments on benchmark RL tasks with different coherent risk measures would strengthen the empirical validation of the approach.
* **Actor-Critic Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm is high-level. The supplementary material mentions details, but providing a more fleshed-out algorithmic description within the main paper would be beneficial.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed algorithms are well-motivated.  The authors are careful to acknowledge the assumptions underlying their results. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:**  The paper is reasonably clear, but could be improved with more intuitive explanations and simplified notation.  The organization is logical, but the density of mathematical detail can be overwhelming. **(Good)**
* **Originality:** The work is original in its generalization of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods to the full class of coherent risk measures.  The novel gradient formulas and the dynamic policy gradient theorem are significant contributions. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:**  The results are likely to be important to researchers working on risk-sensitive RL and could have practical applications in domains such as finance, robotics, and healthcare where managing risk is crucial. The framework provides a valuable tool for designing and optimizing policies with specific risk preferences. **(Strong)**

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Provides a general and theoretically sound framework for risk-sensitive RL.
* Offers a novel contribution to the field with the gradient formulas and policy gradient theorem.
* Addresses a practically important problem with significant potential applications.
* The sampling-based approach offers scalability advantages.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and could be more accessible to a broader audience.
* The empirical evaluation is limited.
* The computational cost of the convex programming step could be a concern.

**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

* **Improve Clarity:**  Add more intuitive explanations and simplify notation where possible.
* **Discuss Assumptions:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations imposed by the key assumptions.
* **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Include more comprehensive experiments on standard RL benchmarks.
* **Elaborate on Algorithm:**  Provide a more detailed description of the actor-critic algorithm within the main paper.



This paper represents a significant step forward in the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, and I believe it has the potential to stimulate further research and development in this important area.
