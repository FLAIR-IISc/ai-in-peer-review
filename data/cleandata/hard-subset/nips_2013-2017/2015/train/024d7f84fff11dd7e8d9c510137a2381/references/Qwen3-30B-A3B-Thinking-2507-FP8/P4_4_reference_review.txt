

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient algorithms for coherent risk measures, which satisfy the four axioms of coherence (convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity). The authors present a unified framework that generalizes previous work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance, mean-semideviation) by leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1). The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a new gradient formula for static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, and (2) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk measures that extends the standard policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings.

The work builds upon prior NIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL, such as those by Chow et al. (2014, 2015) on CVaR and Prashanth and Ghavamzadeh (2013) on risk-averse MDPs. It differs from these by providing a general framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures rather than specific instances. The paper also relates to the broader literature on coherent risk measures in finance (Artzner et al., 1999) and their application to dynamic decision making (Ruszczynski and Shapiro, 2006).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-justified. The authors provide rigorous theoretical derivations (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) and establish consistency results (Proposition 4.4) for their sampling-based gradient estimators. The proof techniques appropriately combine convex optimization theory, the envelope theorem, and likelihood-ratio methods from policy gradient literature. The numerical example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial context, providing empirical validation of their theoretical framework. The paper carefully addresses both strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the assumptions about the risk envelope.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors provide sufficient technical details for the expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce their results, though some of the more complex derivations are relegated to the supplementary material. The numerical example is particularly effective in illustrating the practical implications of the theoretical framework.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. This generalizes previous work that focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and provides a new theoretical foundation that encompasses these special cases. The dynamic risk formulation (Theorem 5.2) extends the standard policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures, which is a novel contribution to the risk-sensitive RL literature. The paper explicitly demonstrates how previous results can be recovered as special cases of their framework, establishing clear novelty.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important problem in RL where managing risk is crucial (e.g., finance, operations research). By providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures, the paper enables researchers to more easily explore different risk preferences in RL applications. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (discussed in the conclusion) suggests broader implications for handling model misspecification in dynamic decision making, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications. The numerical example demonstrates how different risk measures can lead to different policy choices, highlighting the practical importance of this work.

**Strengths:**
- Provides a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures
- Derives new gradient formulas that generalize previous results
- Establishes consistency results for sampling-based estimators
- Demonstrates practical relevance with a numerical example
- Connects coherent risk measures to model uncertainty, suggesting broader implications

**Weaknesses:**
- Relies on Assumption 2.2 that the risk envelope U(Pθ) is known in explicit form, which may not hold for all applications
- The dynamic risk formulation (Section 5) requires solving a risk-sensitive Bellman equation that may be challenging for large state spaces
- The paper notes that extending the approach to single-trajectory roll-outs is a future direction, suggesting a limitation in current applicability
- The numerical example, while illustrative, is relatively simple and may not demonstrate the full potential in complex RL settings

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1. The paper provides a theoretically sound and unified framework for risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures, which generalizes previous work.
2. The gradient formulas and consistency results are well-justified and provide a solid foundation for future research.
3. The connection to model uncertainty suggests broader implications for handling real-world uncertainties in dynamic decision making.
4. The numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1. The assumption of an explicitly known risk envelope (Assumption 2.2) may limit applicability to some real-world problems where risk measures are not easily characterized.
2. The paper does not fully address how to handle cases where the risk envelope is not known in explicit form.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework that generalizes previous work on specific risk measures. The theoretical results are sound and well-justified, and the practical relevance is demonstrated through a clear numerical example. The work advances the state of the art by providing a more general approach to risk-sensitive RL that can be applied to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
