Summary
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk objectives in reinforcement learning. The central idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures as worst-case expectations over a risk envelope to derive general gradient formulas amenable to sampling. The contributions are threefold: (1) a new envelope-theorem-based gradient formula for static coherent risk, with practical estimators (including an SAA estimator that combines sampling with a convex program and a consistency result); (2) derivations that recover and simplify known special cases (e.g., CVaR and mean-semideviation) and provide new ones; and (3) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk, yielding an actor-critic algorithm where the critic solves a robust-MDP-like Bellman equation and the actor uses a likelihood-ratio estimator under ξ-reweighted transitions.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: the paper unifies prior risk-sensitive RL results that focused on specific measures (mean-variance, CVaR, exponential utility, etc.) and connects dynamic coherent risk to robust MDPs (Osogami; Ruszczyński; Petrik & Subramanian; Chow et al.). The approach generalizes CVaR policy gradients (and recovers Tamar et al.’s gradient) and links naturally to function-approximation methods in robust MDPs.

Strengths
- Novelty and unification: A general, measure-agnostic policy gradient framework for coherent risks is conceptually strong and fills a gap between bespoke algorithms (CVaR, mean-variance) and robust MDP planning. The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risks is especially notable.
- Technical clarity of the core idea: Theorem 4.2 (static gradient via Lagrangian saddle points) is elegant and practical; it sheds light on the structure of known gradients (e.g., CVaR) and extends beyond. The SAA-based estimator plus consistency (Prop. 4.4) gives a principled recipe when closed-form envelopes/saddle points are unavailable.
- Dynamic extension: The risk-sensitive Bellman equation and Theorem 5.2 provide a clean analog of the policy gradient theorem, expressing the gradient via ξ-reweighted occupancy and a stage-cost hθ. This is a meaningful theoretical step for time-consistent coherent risk in RL.
- Connections: The robust-MDP equivalence is leveraged in the critic, tying risk-aversion and model uncertainty; this is an appealing direction with practical relevance.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: The paper includes only a small trading toy example. There is no evaluation on MDP benchmarks, no comparison to strong baselines (e.g., CVaR-specific methods, variance-regularized RL) in dynamic settings, and no analysis of computational overhead (solving a convex program per gradient estimate).
- Heavy assumptions and practicality: Finite probability spaces, bounded log-derivatives, and differentiability of envelope constraints in the underlying probability vector may be restrictive in modern RL settings (continuous states/actions). The dynamic algorithm requires a simulator that can provide multiple next-state samples per state and solve for Lagrange multipliers/ξ repeatedly.
- Algorithmic details deferred: Key elements (full actor-critic algorithm, convergence under function approximation, stability with reweighted transitions) are relegated to the supplement. It would help to present at least one main convergence theorem and pseudo-code in the body.
- Potential bias/variance issues: The gradient relies on exact Vθ and ξ∗; approximating Vθ with function approximation introduces bias that is not quantified here. The variance of the estimator under rare-event-sensitive envelopes (e.g., CVaR) and the impact of SAA-induced randomness are not empirically explored.
- Clarity points: Some notation is dense (e.g., derivatives dfi/dp in hθ, and how ∇θ of envelope constraints is computed via chain rule). Assumptions ensuring absolute continuity/full support for Pθ needed for the likelihood-ratio trick and ξ-reweighting could be spelled out.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The static gradient derivation is clear and correct under stated assumptions; the SAA consistency result is reasonable. The dynamic policy gradient theorem is plausible and valuable; however, the main text lacks proofs and convergence guarantees with function approximation. Empirical evidence is insufficient for practical claims.

Clarity
Generally well-written but dense. Including a concise pseudo-code for both the static SAA estimator and the dynamic actor-critic would substantially improve readability. A short subsection listing common envelopes (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation) and how to compute the necessary derivatives/Lagrange multipliers would help practitioners.

Originality
High. The general coherent-risk policy gradient and the dynamic Markov coherent risk policy gradient theorem are novel and unify disparate strands in risk-sensitive RL and robust MDPs. The work moves beyond incremental extensions to specific risk measures.

Significance
High potential impact. A general-purpose toolkit for risk-aware RL that preserves time consistency is important for safety-critical and finance applications. With stronger empirical validation and some engineering to reduce overhead, this could influence future work on risk-aware policy optimization.

Questions/clarifications for authors
- Please clarify assumptions ensuring absolute continuity/full support so that ξ-reweighted transitions define a valid Markov chain and LR estimators are applicable.
- How sensitive is performance to solving the inner convex program approximately? Any tolerance/dual-gap guidance?
- Can you report runtime and sample complexity comparisons vs. CVaR-specific methods?
- How does function approximation error in Vθ propagate to gradient bias in Theorem 5.2? Any bounds?
- Any guidance for continuous state/action spaces (e.g., parametric envelopes, sampling-based ξ estimation)?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually bold, unifying framework for coherent-risk policy gradients (static and dynamic).
- New policy gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk.
- Practical SAA estimator with consistency.
- Recovers and simplifies known special cases; likely to stimulate further research.

Cons for acceptance
- Very limited experiments; no dynamic MDP benchmarks.
- Computational overhead and practicality not convincingly addressed.
- Core algorithmic details and convergence placed in supplement.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper’s conceptual and theoretical novelty outweighs the limited empirical evaluation. Strengthening the main text with at least one convergence statement, pseudo-code, and a small dynamic benchmark would make this an easy accept.
