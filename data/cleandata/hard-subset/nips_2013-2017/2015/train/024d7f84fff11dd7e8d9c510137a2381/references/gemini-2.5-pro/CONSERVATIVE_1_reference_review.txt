### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems where the objective is to optimize a coherent risk measure. The authors address two settings: 1) the "static risk" problem, where a risk measure is applied to the total discounted cost of a trajectory, and 2) the "dynamic risk" problem, which uses a time-consistent Markov coherent risk measure. The primary contributions are theoretical. For the static case, the authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures (as a worst-case expectation over a "risk envelope") and the Envelope Theorem to derive a general formula for the policy gradient. This leads to a proposed algorithm that estimates the gradient by sampling and solving a convex optimization problem based on the Sample Average Approximation (SAA) of the risk measure. For the dynamic case, they present a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, analogous to the standard policy gradient theorem, and outline an actor-critic algorithm based on this result. The paper claims that this framework unifies and generalizes several previous works that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The main strength of the paper lies in its theoretical formulation. The derivation of the static risk gradient in Theorem 4.2 by combining the dual representation of coherent risk with the envelope theorem is elegant and methodologically sound. It provides a principled way to think about gradients for an entire class of risk objectives. The extension to a policy gradient theorem for dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2) is also a valuable theoretical step, connecting the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and a modified transition dynamic.

However, the paper's quality is severely undermined by a lack of empirical validation. A work that proposes new algorithms for reinforcement learning must provide evidence that these algorithms are effective in practice.
- The only numerical result is a one-step, three-armed bandit problem. This experiment serves only to illustrate a well-known qualitative difference between mean-semideviation and mean-variance risk measures. It does not test the performance, sample efficiency, or stability of the proposed gradient estimation algorithm itself. Crucially, it does not evaluate the general SAA-based algorithm from Section 4.3.
- The more complex and novel contribution—the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk—is not accompanied by any experiments. Without evaluation on standard RL benchmarks, it is impossible to judge whether the algorithm is practical, stable, or offers any advantage over existing methods. This is a major omission.
- The theoretical analysis for the dynamic case is incomplete. The paper defers the full algorithm description and convergence analysis to a supplementary "full paper." The main submission should be self-contained enough to evaluate its core claims. Critical aspects, such as the handling of function approximation error in the critic, are non-trivial in this robust/risk-sensitive context and require careful exposition and analysis, which is missing here.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation for using coherent risk measures is clear, and the mathematical derivations for the static case are presented logically. The connection to prior work is also well-articulated.

**Originality:**
The paper's originality lies in providing a unified policy gradient framework for the broad class of coherent risk measures, whereas prior work has typically focused on specific instances. The re-derivation of the CVaR gradient (Section 4.1) in a simpler, more general way is a nice contribution. However, the core technical machinery employed—the dual representation of convex risk measures, the Envelope Theorem, the likelihood-ratio trick, and the Sample Average Approximation—are all well-established tools from optimization and reinforcement learning. The contribution is thus more of a novel and valuable combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamental breakthrough.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high. A robust and scalable method for optimizing general coherent risk measures would be a major contribution to risk-sensitive RL, giving practitioners a flexible toolset for managing risk. However, the significance is currently unrealized due to the lack of empirical support and practical considerations. The proposed general algorithm requires solving a convex program for each gradient estimate, which raises serious scalability concerns that are not addressed. For N samples, the SAA problem (Eq. 7) can have O(N) variables, which may be computationally prohibitive compared to specialized, closed-form updates available for established measures like mean-variance. Without a demonstration of practicality, the proposed framework remains a theoretical curiosity rather than a useful tool.

### Conclusion and Justification

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents an elegant and theoretically sound unification of policy gradient methods for the important class of coherent risk measures.
2.  The formulation provides novel theoretical results, including a general static risk gradient formula and a new policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk.
3.  The work generalizes and simplifies several previous results on specific risk-sensitive RL algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Critically Lacking Empirical Validation:** The paper proposes new algorithms but provides virtually no evidence that they work. The single toy experiment is insufficient, and the complete absence of experiments for the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm is a fatal flaw.
2.  **Unaddressed Practicality Concerns:** The reliance on solving a convex optimization problem at each step of the general algorithm raises significant questions about computational scalability, which are not discussed or evaluated.
3.  **Incomplete Analysis:** The core analysis for the dynamic risk setting is deferred to supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the contribution.

While the theoretical ideas in this paper are interesting and potentially valuable, the work as presented is incomplete. It introduces new algorithms but fails to provide the necessary evidence to demonstrate their viability or effectiveness. In its current state, it reads more like a theoretical proposal than a complete research paper suitable for NeurIPS. I would recommend rejection but strongly encourage the authors to conduct a thorough empirical evaluation of their proposed methods on established benchmarks and to include a complete analysis of their actor-critic framework. Such additions would make for a much stronger and more compelling submission.
