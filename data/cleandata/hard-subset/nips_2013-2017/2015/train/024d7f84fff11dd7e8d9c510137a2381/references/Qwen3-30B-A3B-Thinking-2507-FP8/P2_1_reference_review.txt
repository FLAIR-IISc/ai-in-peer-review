

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as the objective. Coherent risk measures (those satisfying convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity) have gained attention in risk-sensitive RL, but previous work has focused on specific measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The authors propose a general approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both in static and dynamic settings. They derive novel gradient formulas using the representation theorem for coherent risk measures, develop sampling-based algorithms for gradient estimation, and provide a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk. The paper demonstrates the approach with examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation, and a numerical illustration in a trading scenario.

The work builds on prior research in risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 18, 21, 29, 30] for specific risk measures, [24] for dynamic coherent risk) and generalizes these approaches. It also relates to the broader literature on risk measures in finance ([2, 26]) and policy gradient methods ([15, 28]).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors establish a solid theoretical foundation by leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) and applying the envelope theorem to derive their key results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2). The assumptions made (particularly Assumptions 2.2 and 4.1) are reasonable and well-motivated for the context of RL with coherent risk measures.

The paper provides a complete theoretical treatment of the problem, including:
- A new gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2)
- A sampling-based algorithm for gradient estimation (Proposition 4.4)
- A policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2)

The numerical example in Section 6 is appropriate for demonstrating the practical implications of different risk measures but could be strengthened by including more rigorous comparisons with existing methods. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for a simulator to sample multiple state-transitions), showing careful evaluation of their work's strengths and weaknesses.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to algorithmic implementation is easy to follow. The paper effectively balances theoretical rigor with practical implementation considerations.

The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results, including clear descriptions of the algorithms and their components. The use of examples (Section 4.1 and 4.2) to illustrate the application of the general framework to specific risk measures is particularly helpful for readers. The related work section is thorough and properly contextualizes the paper within the broader literature.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature by presenting a unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures. While previous work has focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR [8, 30], mean-variance [18, 21], mean-semideviation [26]), this paper generalizes to all coherent risk measures. The key insight is the application of the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to derive a general gradient formula that can be used with sampling and convex programming.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by stating that "our approach allows to rederive them in greater generality and simplicity" (Section 1). The authors also properly acknowledge related work, including [24] on dynamic Markov coherent risk and [25, 26] on optimization of coherent risk measures. The novel contribution lies in the unified framework that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than a specific instance.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with significant potential impact. Many real-world applications (finance, operations research) require managing not just the expected cost, but also the variability and tail risks. The proposed framework provides a principled way to incorporate a wide range of risk preferences into RL algorithms, which could be valuable for practitioners.

The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, general approach that can be applied to any coherent risk measure. This represents a substantial improvement over previous work that was limited to specific risk measures. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates how different risk preferences can lead to different policies, highlighting the practical importance of the framework.

The paper also points to a broader conceptual significance: the representation duality of coherent risk measures naturally relates risk to model uncertainty, suggesting potential applications beyond standard risk-sensitive RL.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
1. Unified theoretical framework for all coherent risk measures
2. Solid theoretical foundation based on representation theorem for coherent risk measures
3. Practical algorithms with consistency guarantees (Proposition 4.4)
4. Clear exposition with helpful examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation)
5. Thorough related work section that properly contextualizes the contribution
6. Demonstrates practical relevance through a numerical example

### Weaknesses
1. The numerical example is somewhat simplistic and doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with existing methods
2. The computational complexity analysis is limited, particularly for large-scale problems
3. The requirement for a simulator to sample multiple state-transitions from each state (Section 5) might limit applicability in some real-world settings
4. The paper doesn't fully address how the risk envelope can be efficiently learned from data when it's not explicitly known (beyond the assumed canonical form)

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NeurIPS. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with a well-justified, unified framework that generalizes previous work on specific risk measures. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem with potential practical impact.

The main limitation is the somewhat limited numerical evaluation, but this is a minor issue given the paper's theoretical focus and the fact that the primary contribution is a general framework rather than a specific algorithmic improvement. The authors' acknowledgment of the simulator requirement as a limitation shows appropriate awareness of practical constraints.

This paper would be a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community, as it advances the field of risk-sensitive RL by providing a principled approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific instances. The work has the potential to influence future research in risk-sensitive decision making across multiple domains.
