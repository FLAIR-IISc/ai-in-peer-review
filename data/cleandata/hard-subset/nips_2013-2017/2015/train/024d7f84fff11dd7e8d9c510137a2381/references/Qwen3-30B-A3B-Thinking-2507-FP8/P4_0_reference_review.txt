

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors develop gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures, which generalize previous work that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The main contributions include a general gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2), a sampling-based algorithm for gradient estimation (Section 4.3), and a dynamic programming-based policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2).

The paper builds on the theoretical foundation of coherent risk measures established by Artzner et al. [2] and the recent work on dynamic Markov coherent risk measures by Ruszczyński and Shapiro [26] and others. It connects to previous NeurIPS work on risk-sensitive RL, such as [8, 18, 21, 29, 30] that focused on specific risk measures, and generalizes these approaches. The authors also relate their work to robust MDPs [19, 31], highlighting the connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors establish rigorous theoretical foundations by leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1). The gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are derived using standard techniques (envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio trick) and are supported by consistency results (Proposition 4.4). The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the framework, showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial trading scenario. The paper carefully distinguishes between static and dynamic risk settings and provides clear mathematical formulations for both.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors provide necessary background on coherent risk measures in Section 2 before presenting their main contributions. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the paper is well-organized with clear section headings. The examples in Sections 4.1-4.2 help illustrate the theoretical results with specific risk measures, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with risk-sensitive RL. The authors are careful to explain their assumptions (Assumptions 2.2, 4.1, 5.1) and to clarify the connection between their work and previous literature.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution is the general framework for policy gradient methods applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures as previous works did. The authors demonstrate that several previous results (e.g., for CVaR [30] and mean-semideviation) are special cases of their framework. This is a significant theoretical advance that unifies disparate approaches in risk-sensitive RL. The paper's novelty is clear from its comparison with recent NeurIPS papers on risk-sensitive RL [8, 18, 21, 29, 30] and dynamic coherent risk [20, 9].

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in RL: risk-sensitive decision making. The ability to handle a broad class of risk measures is significant because different applications may require different risk preferences. The authors demonstrate this with their numerical example, where different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial trading scenario. The framework has practical implications for applications where risk management is crucial (e.g., finance, operations research). The paper also connects risk-sensitive RL to model uncertainty, suggesting broader applications beyond the scope of the current work.

**Strengths:**
- Provides a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures.
- Derives general gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk.
- Proposes practical algorithms that combine sampling with convex programming.
- Demonstrates the practical utility of the framework with a compelling numerical example.
- Connects risk-sensitive RL to model uncertainty, suggesting broader applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper focuses on finite probability spaces, which may limit applicability to continuous problems (though the authors note this can be extended).
- The computational complexity of the convex programming steps may be high for large-scale problems.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing risk-sensitive RL methods in terms of computational efficiency or sample complexity.
- The numerical example is somewhat limited, focusing on a simple trading scenario with three assets.

The paper is well within the scope of NeurIPS, addressing a significant problem in reinforcement learning with a theoretically grounded approach. The framework provides a principled way to handle a wide class of risk measures in a single framework, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Provides a unified theoretical framework that generalizes previous work on risk-sensitive RL.
- Derives rigorous gradient formulas with consistency results.
- Demonstrates practical utility with a clear numerical example.
- Connects risk-sensitive RL to model uncertainty, suggesting broader applications.

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Limited empirical comparison with existing methods.
- Focus on finite probability spaces may limit applicability.
- Computational complexity of convex programming steps not thoroughly analyzed.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the literature by unifying risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. I recommend acceptance.
