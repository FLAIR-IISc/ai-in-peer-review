

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper proposes policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, aiming to generalize previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures (like CVaR, mean-variance, etc.). The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to derive new gradient formulas for both static and dynamic Markov coherent risk. The key contributions include a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk, an algorithm for general static coherent risk using sampling with convex programming, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.

The paper builds on well-established foundations in risk theory (Artzner et al., 1999) and risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 18, 21, 29, 30]). The theoretical development is generally sound, with rigorous derivations of the gradient formulas using the representation theorem for coherent risk measures and the envelope theorem. The connection to robust MDPs (from [19]) is well-motivated and provides a useful perspective on the relationship between risk and model uncertainty.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by unifying previous work on risk-sensitive RL under a single coherent risk framework. The derivation of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) is mathematically sound and generalizes several previously known results (as shown in Sections 4.1 and 4.2). The approach of combining sampling with convex programming for gradient estimation (Section 4.3) is conceptually interesting and could be useful for practitioners who want to optimize for different risk preferences without having to derive specialized algorithms for each risk measure. The numerical example in Section 6 demonstrates the flexibility of the approach in selecting appropriate risk measures for specific problem characteristics.

**Weaknesses:** The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims. While Section 6 provides a simple trading agent example, it doesn't compare the proposed algorithms against established risk-sensitive RL baselines across multiple domains or problem types. The comparison is limited to visualizing policy selection over training iterations rather than demonstrating performance improvements in terms of risk metrics. The paper doesn't address how the proposed algorithms scale to larger MDPs or how they compare computationally to existing methods. The convergence analysis of the proposed actor-critic algorithm is only briefly mentioned as being in the supplementary material, with no concrete results provided.

The paper's claim of "generalizing and unifying previous approaches" is somewhat overstated. While the framework does encompass specific risk measures as special cases, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this approach differs from or improves upon the dynamic programming approach for coherent risk measures proposed in [24], which the authors cite. The paper also doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed methods compared to existing approaches.

**Quality:** The theoretical content is technically sound and well-motivated, but the paper lacks sufficient experimental evidence to validate the proposed methods in practical RL settings. The absence of comprehensive empirical comparisons against established baselines for multiple risk measures is a significant weakness.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear exposition of the theoretical concepts. The connection between coherent risk measures and the proposed algorithms is well-explained.

**Originality:** The core idea of leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to derive gradient formulas isn't entirely novel, as it builds on established work in risk theory. The paper's main contribution is the generalization to the whole class of coherent risk measures, but this is presented as a straightforward application of existing theory rather than a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:** The framework could be useful for practitioners who want to optimize for different risk preferences, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this approach provides a significant practical advantage over existing methods. The connection between coherent risk and model uncertainty (Section 7) is promising but remains largely theoretical without concrete demonstrations.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, its practical significance is limited by the lack of comprehensive empirical validation. The proposed algorithms are presented as generalizations of existing methods, but without substantial experimental evidence showing clear advantages over established baselines, it's difficult to assess their practical value. As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods with thorough validation, I find the paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed approach to be under-supported.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. The paper's main contribution is a theoretical generalization that doesn't provide sufficient empirical evidence to justify its adoption over existing methods. The field would benefit more from a paper that thoroughly evaluates risk-sensitive RL methods across multiple benchmarks rather than a theoretically interesting but empirically unverified generalization. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical evaluation to support the claims of the paper.
