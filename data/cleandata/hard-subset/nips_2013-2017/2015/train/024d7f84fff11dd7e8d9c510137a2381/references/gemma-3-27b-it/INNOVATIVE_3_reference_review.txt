This paper tackles the challenging problem of policy gradient optimization under coherent risk measures, a significant extension of traditional risk-neutral RL. The core idea – leveraging the duality representation of coherent risk to derive tractable gradient formulas – is both novel and elegant. The work successfully generalizes previous approaches focused on specific risk measures and provides a unifying framework for risk-sensitive RL. The gradient formulas presented, especially Theorem 4.2, are a valuable contribution and offer a practical route to implement risk-aware policies. The connection drawn to robust MDPs and the discussion of model uncertainty in the conclusion are insightful.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The paper presents a genuinely novel framework for applying policy gradients to a broad class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant generalization beyond existing work.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas appears rigorous, building upon established results in coherent risk theory and optimization.
* **Generality:** The approach is not limited to specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) but applies to the entire class of coherent measures.
* **Practicality:** The SAA algorithm (Section 4.3) provides a concrete method for estimating gradients when analytical solutions are unavailable, making the approach applicable to complex scenarios.
* **Potential Impact:**  The work has the potential to impact areas beyond traditional RL, such as finance and operations research, where coherent risk measures are widely used.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration (Section 6) is rather limited. While it demonstrates the framework's potential, a more extensive evaluation with diverse environments and risk measures would strengthen the paper. Specifically, the choice of a simple trading environment feels insufficient to highlight the benefits of coherent risk over simpler alternatives.
* **Computational Complexity:** The SAA algorithm involves solving convex programs within each gradient estimation step. The scalability of this approach to high-dimensional state/action spaces is not fully addressed. Although acknowledged, a more detailed discussion on computational bottlenecks and potential mitigations (e.g., approximation techniques for the convex programs) would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 regarding the risk envelope) which, while reasonable, are not thoroughly justified. A discussion on the robustness of the approach to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
* **Dynamic Risk Algorithm Detail:** While the framework for dynamic risk (Section 5) is presented, the specifics of the actor-critic algorithm, especially regarding handling the sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions, are left to the supplementary material. A more detailed description in the main paper would improve clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core technical contribution – the gradient formulas for coherent risk – is substantial and represents a significant advance in the field of risk-sensitive RL.
* The generality of the approach is a major strength, allowing for the application of policy gradients to a wide range of risk preferences.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic in a digestible manner.
* The potential impact on both theoretical understanding and practical applications is high.
* The minor weaknesses (limited empirical evaluation, computational complexity concerns) do not overshadow the novelty and significance of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited empirical validation leaves some doubt regarding the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The computational complexity could be a significant barrier to adoption in large-scale problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a highly original and theoretically sound contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The proposed framework for policy gradient optimization under coherent risk measures is a significant step forward and has the potential to inspire further research in this important area. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh its limitations, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should be encouraged to expand the empirical validation with more comprehensive experiments and to provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and potential mitigation strategies. Adding further detail about the dynamic risk algorithm within the main body of the paper would also improve clarity.
