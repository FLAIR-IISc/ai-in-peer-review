This paper introduces a general policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The work addresses both static risk, applied to the total discounted cost of a trajectory, and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The authors' approach provides a unified method for the entire class of coherent risk measures, thereby generalizing previous works that have developed algorithms for specific risk measures such as Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) or mean-variance criteria.

The main idea for the static risk setting is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the risk envelope). By applying the envelope theorem to this dual formulation, the authors derive a new, general formula for the policy gradient. This gradient formula depends on the optimal worst-case density and the Lagrange multipliers of the dual problem. The paper demonstrates that this general formula recovers known gradient expressions for CVaR and can be used to derive new ones for other measures like mean-semideviation. For general coherent risks where the optimal density is not analytically available, the paper proposes a sample-based algorithm that estimates the gradient by first solving a Sample Average Approximation (SAA) of the dual convex program to find the required density and multipliers.

For the dynamic risk setting, the paper extends the classic policy gradient theorem to dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. This is achieved by using the risk-sensitive Bellman equation and the static gradient result. The resulting dynamic policy gradient theorem leads to an actor-critic algorithm. The critic's role is to estimate the risk-sensitive value function, while the actor updates the policy using a gradient estimate derived from the new theorem.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its generality. By providing a policy gradient framework for the entire class of axiomatically-defined coherent risk measures, it unifies and extends a significant body of prior work in risk-sensitive RL. This moves the field from developing bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures to a principled, unified approach. This is a strong conceptual contribution that could enable researchers and practitioners to more easily explore and apply a wider variety of risk criteria tailored to specific problems.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development is elegant and appears sound. The application of the dual representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem to derive the static policy gradient (Theorem 4.2) is a novel and effective technique. The extension to the dynamic case, resulting in a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The assumptions required for the results are clearly stated.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It provides a concise introduction to coherent and dynamic risk measures before delving into the technical contributions for the static and dynamic cases. The motivation for a general framework is well-argued. The derivation is presented logically, starting from the static case and building upon it for the more complex dynamic case.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is limited to a stateless multi-armed bandit problem. While it effectively demonstrates the flexibility of using different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation), it does not test the core algorithmic contributions for sequential decision-making. The proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk, which represents a major part of the contribution, is not evaluated at all. Claims of scalability to large or continuous MDPs are therefore unsupported by empirical evidence. A more comprehensive evaluation on standard RL benchmark environments would be necessary to demonstrate the practical viability and performance of the proposed methods.

2.  **Practical and Computational Concerns:** The general gradient estimation algorithm requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) to obtain an estimate of the gradient. The computational cost of this step, which depends on the number of samples `N`, is not discussed. This could be a significant overhead in practice, potentially limiting the applicability of the method in scenarios requiring a large number of samples or frequent gradient updates.

3.  **Algorithmic Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem is high-level, with many crucial details deferred to the supplementary material. This includes the function approximation scheme for the critic, the two-phase sampling procedure for the actor, and the convergence analysis. While space limitations are a valid concern, the lack of these details in the main paper makes it difficult to fully assess the completeness and practicality of the proposed algorithm for the dynamic setting. Furthermore, the algorithm's reliance on a simulator that can provide multiple next-state samples for any given state is a practical limitation that restricts its use to certain settings.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

*   Provides a novel and significant theoretical contribution by unifying policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures.
*   The theoretical approach is elegant, sound, and builds a strong connection between the dual formulation of risk, convex optimization, and policy gradients.
*   The work generalizes several previous results and has the potential to be highly influential for future research in risk-sensitive RL.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**

*   The empirical validation is minimal and does not support the claims of the paper regarding its applicability to sequential decision-making problems (MDPs).
*   The practical implementation details and computational complexity of the proposed algorithms are not sufficiently addressed.
*   The actor-critic algorithm, a key contribution, is described too briefly in the main paper, making a full evaluation of its merits difficult.
