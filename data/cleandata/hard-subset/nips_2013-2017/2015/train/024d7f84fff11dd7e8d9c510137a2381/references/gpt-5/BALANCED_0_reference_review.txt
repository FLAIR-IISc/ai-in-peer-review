Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The key idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk via risk envelopes to derive likelihood-ratio gradient formulas. For static coherent risk ρ(Z), the authors give a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) based on the Lagrangian saddle point of the envelope problem, which yields (i) simple derivations for specific risks (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and (ii) a general sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator with a consistency result (Proposition 4.4). For dynamic, time-consistent risk in MDPs, they consider Markov coherent risk measures and derive a policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) relating the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. This motivates an actor-critic algorithm that leverages robust-MDP structure for the critic and a two-phase sampling scheme for the actor.

The work unifies and extends several lines of research: CVaR policy gradients (e.g., Tamar et al.; Chow et al.), mean/variance-related criteria (e.g., Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), exponential utility (Borkar), and dynamic coherent risk/robust MDP connections (Ruszczyński, Shapiro; Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian; Nilim & El Ghaoui-style robust MDPs). The dynamic result builds on [24, 31] by providing a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and proposing an actor-critic scheme with function approximation.

Strengths
- Generality: A single framework covers the entire class of coherent risks (static and dynamic), subsuming CVaR and semi-deviation as special cases. The risk-envelope view is leveraged cleanly.
- Technical contribution: Theorem 4.2 and its SAA-based estimator with consistency are useful and broadly applicable; the CVaR and semideviation examples are derived simply and recover/extend known formulas.
- Dynamic risk: Theorem 5.2 is a clear analogue of the standard policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and clarifies the role of reweighted transitions via ξ∗.
- Conceptual clarity: The connection between risk, adversarial reweighting, and robust MDPs is well-articulated; the paper motivates flexible risk shaping.
- Relevance: Risk-sensitive RL is a timely topic; coherent risk and time consistency are important in finance, operations, and safety-critical RL.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is very limited. The single “trading” example is essentially a bandit with three options and does not evaluate the proposed actor-critic or the dynamic-risk gradient in any MDP. There is no comparison on standard RL benchmarks or larger-scale settings to demonstrate practicality, efficiency, or variance properties.
- Practicality of the dynamic method: The actor requires sampling from ξ∗-reweighted dynamics and multiple next-state samples per state. This assumes access to a generative model and the ability to solve per-state convex programs to obtain ξ∗ and multipliers, which can be computationally heavy. The paper does not quantify complexity or discuss realistic online learning scenarios where only a single trajectory is available.
- Assumptions and regularity: The static theory assumes finite probability spaces, known risk envelopes in a canonical convex form, Slater-type conditions, and smooth dependence on Pθ. Many MDPs are continuous/large-scale, and it is unclear how the required differentiability and uniqueness (e.g., non-unique quantiles or saddle points) issues are handled in practice. The robustness of the estimator to solver inaccuracies is not discussed.
- Missing details in the main text: Important convergence/error analyses, algorithmic details, and proofs are deferred to the supplement. This makes it hard to assess guarantees for function approximation in the critic and the bias/variance of the actor’s gradient estimator.
- Notational/consistency issues: Z is “cost” throughout, but the experiment maximizes returns; the change of sign could be clarified. Some references lack years or complete details.

Quality
- Soundness: The approach is mathematically sound in spirit. The reliance on envelope theorems and KKT multipliers is standard, but several technical details are deferred. The dynamic theorem appears correct under the stated assumptions.
- Empirical support: Insufficient to substantiate claims of scalability and practicality.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, with good motivation and related work. However, the main text omits many algorithmic details required for reproduction and assessment. Assumptions and scope (finite Ω vs. Lp extension) could be more explicit and consolidated.

Originality
- The unifying gradient formula for coherent risks and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appear novel and extend prior specialized results.

Significance
- Potentially significant if the methods can be made practical. The generality could influence risk-sensitive RL broadly, but the current lack of empirical evidence and complexity analysis limits immediate impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide experiments on dynamic MDPs (e.g., gridworlds, control benchmarks) comparing against CVaR-specific methods and variance-based baselines, demonstrating the actor-critic with function approximation and reporting sample/compute costs and gradient variance.
- Clarify how ξ∗ and multipliers are computed in large or continuous state spaces; provide pseudocode and complexity per iteration; discuss warm-starts and solver tolerances.
- Discuss handling of non-unique saddle points and quantiles (CVaR) and how this affects differentiability and estimator bias.
- Explore single-trajectory learning and off-policy/importance sampling variants; variance reduction is especially important for tail-sensitive risks.
- Detail the error propagation from approximate critics to actor gradients and any stability guarantees under function approximation.
- Expand on the extension from finite Ω to general spaces: what additional regularity is required? Provide at least one nontrivial continuous example.

Arguments for acceptance
- Broad, elegant unification of risk-sensitive policy gradients via coherent risk envelopes.
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Clear connections to robust MDPs and prior specialized results.

Arguments against acceptance
- Very limited empirical validation; no demonstration on dynamic MDPs.
- Practical constraints (need for generative model, per-state convex programs) and computational complexity are not addressed.
- Many crucial details deferred to the supplement; reproducibility and robustness unclear.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical framework is promising and appears technically solid, but the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation and practical algorithmic detail to justify acceptance at NeurIPS. Strengthening the experiments, clarifying assumptions and computational aspects, and demonstrating the actor-critic on nontrivial MDPs would substantially improve the paper.
