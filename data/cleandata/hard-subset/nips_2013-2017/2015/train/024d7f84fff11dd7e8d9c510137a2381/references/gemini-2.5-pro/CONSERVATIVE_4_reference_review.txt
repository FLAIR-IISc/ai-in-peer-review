This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The core contribution is a set of new policy gradient formulas for both static coherent risk measures and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. The approach leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability distortions (the risk envelope). By applying the Envelope Theorem to the Lagrangian of this dual problem, the authors derive a general gradient formula that connects to the standard likelihood-ratio policy gradient estimator.

For the static risk setting, the paper demonstrates that its general formula can recover previously known results for Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) and can be used to derive new formulas for other measures, such as mean-semideviation risk. For cases where the solution to the dual problem is not analytically available, the authors propose a general algorithm based on Sample Average Approximation (SAA), which involves solving a convex optimization problem to estimate the worst-case distribution from samples. For the dynamic risk setting, the paper presents a novel policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and sketches an actor-critic algorithm to implement it. The work aims to unify and generalize a growing body of literature that has thus far focused on optimizing specific risk measures individually.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Generality and Elegance:** The main strength of the paper is its theoretical contribution. It provides a unified and principled framework for policy gradient methods applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The derivation of the static risk gradient in Theorem 4.2, by combining the dual representation of risk with the Envelope Theorem, is both sound and elegant. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods.

2.  **Unification of Prior Work:** The framework successfully places prior work on specific risk measures, such as CVaR, into a broader context. Showing that the CVaR policy gradient formula from [30] is a special case of their general result reinforces the validity and power of their approach.

3.  **Novelty in Dynamic Risk:** The extension to dynamic Markov coherent risk measures is non-trivial and results in a new policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2). This represents a significant theoretical step towards developing practical algorithms for time-consistent risk-sensitive RL problems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the lack of rigorous empirical validation. The work is supported by only a single numerical illustration on a three-armed bandit problem. While this example effectively illustrates the conceptual difference between various risk measures, it does not constitute a serious evaluation of the proposed algorithms. To be convincing, the paper must demonstrate its methods on standard, non-trivial RL benchmarks and show how they perform in practice.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's claim is to provide a general method. However, it fails to compare this general method against established, specialized algorithms for well-known risk measures. For instance, how does the proposed SAA-based algorithm for CVaR (Section 4.3) compare in terms of sample efficiency, variance, and computational time to the specialized CVaR algorithms presented in [8] or [30]? Without such a comparison, it is impossible to assess the practical trade-offs of this generalized approach. It is plausible that the generality comes at a significant cost in performance, but this is left unexplored.

3.  **Practicality and Computational Cost:** The general gradient estimation algorithm requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) to find the worst-case density `ξ*` and the Lagrange multipliers from a batch of `N` samples. This step must be performed repeatedly within a stochastic gradient descent loop. The computational complexity of this sub-problem is not discussed, but it could be a significant bottleneck, especially for large `N`, making the algorithm impractical for many RL applications. This is a critical aspect that is overlooked.

4.  **Incomplete Algorithm for Dynamic Risk:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is only sketched out, with key details about the critic's implementation (using function approximation for a robust MDP value function) and the actor's two-phase sampling procedure relegated to supplementary material or future work. Furthermore, the reliance on a simulator that can provide multiple next-state samples from any given state is a strong assumption that limits its applicability compared to more standard actor-critic methods that learn from a single trajectory. As presented, the contribution for the dynamic case feels more like a theoretical proposal than a fully-fledged and tested algorithm.

**Summary and Recommendation**

The paper presents a theoretically elegant and novel framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients. The mathematical derivations are sound and provide a valuable unification of the field. However, the work in its current form is heavily weighted towards theory, with empirical support that is far too weak for a conference like NeurIPS. The claims of providing practical algorithms are not substantiated with evidence or comparison against established, state-of-the-art methods. The significant practical concerns regarding computational cost and the incomplete nature of the dynamic risk algorithm further weaken the contribution.

While the theoretical ideas are promising, this is an incomplete piece of work. An endorsement of this method would be premature without a thorough empirical investigation of its performance, costs, and stability in comparison to existing, proven techniques. Therefore, I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form. The authors should be encouraged to conduct a comprehensive empirical study on standard RL benchmarks to validate their approach and resubmit to a future conference.
