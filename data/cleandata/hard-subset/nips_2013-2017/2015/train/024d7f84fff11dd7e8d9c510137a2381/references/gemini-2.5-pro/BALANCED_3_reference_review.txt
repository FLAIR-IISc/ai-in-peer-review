This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. It tackles two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk measure of the total discounted cost, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measure. The core of the work lies in leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities (the "risk envelope").

The main contributions are:
1.  A new, general formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure (Theorem 4.2). This formula connects the policy gradient to the solution of the dual problem (the worst-case density and Lagrange multipliers) and is derived using the envelope theorem.
2.  A sampling-based algorithm for estimating this gradient for any coherent risk measure whose risk envelope is specified as a convex program. This involves solving a sample average approximation (SAA) of the dual problem. A consistency result for this estimator is provided.
3.  A novel policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), which extends the standard policy gradient theorem to this risk-sensitive setting. This leads to the proposal of a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

The work successfully unifies and generalizes several previous results in risk-sensitive RL that focused on specific measures like CVaR [8, 30] or mean-variance [29, 21]. By deriving the gradient formula for CVaR as a special case, the authors demonstrate the power and simplicity of their general framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality and Unification:** The most significant strength of this paper is its generality. It moves beyond ad-hoc approaches for specific risk measures and provides a principled framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a valuable conceptual contribution that unifies a fragmented area of research. The use of the dual representation from stochastic programming [26] is elegant and provides deep insight into the structure of the policy gradient.
*   **Novel and Sound Theoretical Results:** The paper presents two key theoretical results (Theorem 4.2 for static risk and Theorem 5.2 for dynamic risk) that are novel and appear technically sound. The derivation of the static gradient using the envelope theorem is elegant. The extension to a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk is a non-trivial and important step, correctly identifying how the Bellman equation and the policy gradient structure are modified by the risk measure.
*   **Constructive Approach:** The paper goes beyond theory by proposing concrete, implementable algorithms. The SAA-based estimator for the static risk gradient is practical, combining sampling with standard convex optimization tools. The outline of an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case provides a clear roadmap for implementation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It provides the necessary background on coherent risk measures and effectively situates its contributions within the existing literature. The numerical example, while simple, is very effective at motivating the need for flexibility in choosing risk measures, highlighting the different behaviors induced by mean-semideviation versus mean-standard-deviation on a heavy-tailed distribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality and Computational Cost:** The proposed general gradient estimator requires solving a convex optimization problem within each gradient estimation step. While convex programming is efficient in principle, this inner loop could become a computational bottleneck for large sample sizes `N` or complex risk envelopes, potentially limiting the scalability of the approach. A discussion of this complexity and how it compares to specialized algorithms would be valuable.
*   **Simulator Dependence for the Dynamic Case:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk has a significant practical limitation: it requires sampling trajectories from an MDP with transition probabilities weighted by the worst-case density `ξ*` (as per Theorem 5.2). This implies that a generative model or a highly flexible simulator is required, from which one can sample arbitrary next-state transitions. This prevents the direct application of the algorithm in many real-world RL settings where one can only sample a single trajectory from the true, unknown environment. The authors acknowledge this limitation but its implications are substantial.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical validation is limited to a simple multi-armed bandit problem. While this example effectively illustrates a conceptual point about risk preferences, it does not evaluate the performance, scalability, or sample efficiency of the proposed algorithms (especially the actor-critic) on a standard, non-trivial sequential decision-making benchmark. An experiment on a more complex MDP would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the practical utility of its methods.
*   **Assumptions for Consistency:** The consistency result in Proposition 4.4 relies on a set of assumptions that, while seemingly standard in the SAA literature, may not be easy to verify for a given risk measure. A more intuitive discussion of these conditions would improve the paper's accessibility.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Addressing the Simulator Requirement:** This is the most critical point. The authors should elaborate on the simulator dependency in the main text and discuss potential avenues for creating a simulator-free algorithm. For example, could off-policy methods and importance sampling be used to correct for the distribution mismatch between the true environment dynamics and the `ξ*`-weighted dynamics? Even a brief discussion of this possibility would greatly enhance the paper's impact.
2.  **Expanding the Experiments:** Including an experiment on a canonical RL control task (e.g., from OpenAI Gym) would be highly beneficial. This would allow for a comparison against existing risk-sensitive RL baselines (e.g., [21, 8]) in terms of final performance, learning speed, and computational time per update, providing a much clearer picture of the algorithm's practical trade-offs.
3.  **Discussing Computational Complexity:** It would be helpful to include a brief analysis of the computational complexity of the gradient estimation step, particularly the dependence on the sample size `N` and the structure of the constraints defining the risk envelope.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures is a substantial advance over prior work that considered risk measures in isolation. The theoretical results are novel, elegant, and provide a strong foundation for future research.

While the practical applicability of the proposed dynamic risk algorithm is limited by its reliance on a simulator, and the empirical evaluation is minimal, the strength and importance of the theoretical contribution are sufficient for acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper opens up numerous avenues for future work, and the weaknesses identified are primarily limitations that can be addressed in follow-up research based on this foundational work. I recommend acceptance.
