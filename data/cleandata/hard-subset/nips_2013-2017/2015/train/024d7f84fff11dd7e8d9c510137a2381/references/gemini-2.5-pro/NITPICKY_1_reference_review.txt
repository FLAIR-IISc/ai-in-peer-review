### Summary

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as objectives. It tackles both static risk (applied to the total discounted return) and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The main contributions are: (1) a novel, general formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure, derived using the envelope theorem, which represents the risk as a worst-case expectation over a "risk envelope"; (2) a practical algorithm for estimating this gradient by combining sample average approximation (SAA) with convex programming; and (3) a new policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk, which connects the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and enables an actor-critic algorithm. The work unifies and generalizes several previous results that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance.

### Strengths

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its ambitious scope. By framing the problem in the language of coherent risk measures, it provides a single, elegant framework that encompasses a wide variety of risk preferences. This is a significant conceptual advance over prior work, which has largely treated different risk measures in a piecemeal fashion. The ability to derive existing results for CVaR (Section 4.1) and new results for mean-semideviation (Section 4.2) as special cases effectively demonstrates the power of this unified view.

2.  **Novel Theoretical Results:** The paper's core theoretical results, Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2, are novel and technically sound. The use of the envelope theorem to derive the static policy gradient (Theorem 4.2) is particularly elegant. The policy gradient theorem for dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2) is a non-trivial extension of the standard policy gradient theorem to the robust/risk-sensitive setting and provides a principled foundation for actor-critic algorithms.

3.  **Significance:** The work addresses the important and practical problem of incorporating risk aversion into reinforcement learning. By providing tools for the entire class of coherent risk measures, it gives practitioners significant flexibility to "shape" the risk criterion to match problem-specific needs, as compellingly argued in the numerical illustration. This could have a substantial impact on applications where managing downside risk is critical, such as finance and autonomous systems.

### Weaknesses

Despite the significant contributions, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws, oversights, and a lack of clarity in key sections, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   **Excessive Reliance on Supplementary Material:** The paper defers a vast amount of critical information—including proofs for all major theorems and propositions, the algorithm for the mean-semideviation gradient, and the entire description and analysis of the actor-critic algorithm—to the supplement. This makes the main paper a high-level summary rather than a self-contained scientific contribution, impeding a thorough evaluation of its claims.
    *   **Vague Algorithm Descriptions:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm in Section 5 is cursory to the point of being uninformative. It states it is "composed of two interleaved procedures" and mentions modifying an algorithm from [31] and using a "two-phase sampling procedure." These descriptions are too vague to be reproducible. Key practical questions, such as how one samples from the ξ*-distorted MDP, are left unanswered.
    *   **Important Details in Footnotes:** Crucial details are relegated to footnotes. Footnote 2 mentions an extension to history-dependent policies by augmenting the state space, a non-trivial point that warrants main-text discussion. Footnote 3 notes that the risk envelope `U` depends on the state `x` in the MDP setting, a critical piece of information for understanding the Bellman equation (9) that is introduced far too subtly.

2.  **Methodological and Technical Soundness:**
    *   **Practicality of the SAA Algorithm:** The general gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem with `O(N)` variables and constraints for each gradient estimate. While theoretically sound, the paper glosses over the computational burden this implies. For the large `N` required for accurate estimation, this "inner loop" could be prohibitively expensive, making the algorithm impractical. This critical trade-off is not discussed.
    *   **Assumptions:** The assumptions for the consistency of the SAA estimator (Proposition 4.4) are strong and not sufficiently justified. In particular, condition (III), which assumes the saddle points of the empirical problem `SN` remain within a fixed compact set `C`, is not self-evident and its plausibility is not argued. The statement that these assumptions are "rather mild" seems overly optimistic.
    *   **Finite Space Restriction:** The analysis is restricted to finite state spaces (`Ω has a finite number of elements`). The claim that results can be extended to `Lp`-normed spaces is made without proof or discussion of the technical challenges involved. This is a major limitation, as many interesting RL problems have continuous domains.
    *   **Imprecise Notation:** In several places, the notation is ambiguous. For instance, in the definition of `hθ(x, a)` in Theorem 5.2, the derivatives `dfi/dp(x')` and `dge/dp(x')` are used. It is unclear what `p(x')` refers to; it is presumably the transition probability `P(x'|x, a)`, but this should be made explicit.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The numerical illustration in Section 6 is far too simple to validate the paper's main contributions. It is a stateless, single-step bandit problem, not a sequential decision-making problem. Consequently, it demonstrates neither the dynamic risk framework nor the proposed actor-critic algorithm. While it makes a valid point about the flexibility of risk measures, it fails to provide any empirical support for the core algorithmic contributions of the paper (Sections 4.3 and 5).

4.  **Formatting and Presentation:**
    *   The paper contains several instances of awkward phrasing (e.g., "allows to re-derive them").
    *   The bibliography is inconsistently formatted: journal and conference name styles vary, and page number inclusion is erratic. For a top-tier conference submission, this lack of attention to detail is concerning.

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

This paper presents strong, novel, and significant theoretical ideas that could unify an important subfield of reinforcement learning. The core concept of a general policy gradient for the entire class of coherent risk measures is excellent.

However, the execution is severely flawed. The paper reads more like a proposal or an extended abstract than a complete, rigorous conference paper. Key algorithmic details are omitted, the experimental validation is wholly inadequate for the claims made, the assumptions are not well-motivated, and the overall presentation suffers from a lack of clarity and precision. The sheer number of minor issues, combined with the major weakness of the experimental section and the opacity of the proposed actor-critic algorithm, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The ideas are certainly of NIPS quality, but this manuscript is not yet ready. I would encourage the authors to perform a comprehensive revision, including a full description of their dynamic risk algorithm and a compelling experimental evaluation on a true sequential decision-making task, for a future submission.
