Summary
The paper develops policy gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static coherent risk measures of trajectory returns and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. The key technical tools are: (i) a generic gradient formula for static coherent risk measures (Theorem 4.2) derived via the dual (risk-envelope) representation and an envelope theorem, which yields sampling-friendly likelihood-ratio estimators; (ii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) based estimator that combines Monte Carlo sampling with a small convex program to compute the dual variables and prove consistency (Proposition 4.4); and (iii) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2) that expresses the gradient as an expectation over trajectories of a ξ-weighted Markov chain with a stage-wise term hθ, enabling an actor-critic algorithm. The results generalize prior risk-sensitive RL work that focused on specific metrics (mean-variance, CVaR, exponential utility) and connect to robust MDPs for the critic step.

Relation to prior work
- Static risk: Extends CVaR gradient results (e.g., Tamar et al., 2015; Rockafellar & Uryasev) and mean-variance work (e.g., Tamar et al., 2012; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh) by providing a unified treatment of coherent risks via risk envelopes. The CVaR gradient is recovered simply and in discrete settings.
- Dynamic risk: Builds on Ruszczyński’s dynamic programming for coherent risk and the equivalence between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs (Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al., 2013). The presented policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears novel.
- Methodologically, the use of envelope theorems and SAA is standard in stochastic programming (Shapiro et al.), but their deployment for policy gradients with coherent risk is new and well motivated.

Quality
- Theory: The static gradient formula (Thm. 4.2) under Assumptions 2.2 and 4.1 is sound and elegantly derives likelihood-ratio estimators using dual variables from a convex saddle point. The SAA estimator and consistency proof (Prop. 4.4) are appropriate. The dynamic result (Thm. 5.2) is a strong analog of the classical policy gradient theorem, carefully accounting for the ξ-weighted dynamics and constraint sensitivities.
- Assumptions: Finite probability spaces and explicit, differentiable risk envelopes simplify analysis but restrict generality. The need for bounded score functions and access to ∇θ log terms is standard. The dynamic algorithm requires a simulator that allows multiple next-state samples per state and the ability to sample under ξ-weighted transitions; in practice this may necessitate importance sampling rather than changing the environment, which the paper acknowledges as future work.
- Empirics: Empirical validation is limited. The main text includes a small static illustration (asset selection) and mentions supplementary comparisons (SAA vs. analytic CVaR gradients) and convergence analysis for the actor-critic. There are no dynamic MDP experiments in the main text, and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement. This weakens the practical credibility of the dynamic contribution.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized. The paper clearly lays out the risk-envelope formalism, assumptions, and gradient formulas. Examples (CVaR and mean-semideviation) help ground the theory.
- Some points could be clarified: dependence of U on state and θ, handling non-unique saddle points/quantiles, computational complexity and scalability (per-iteration convex programs; per-state dual variables), and how to implement ξ-weighted trajectory sampling in standard simulators (importance sampling).
- Several details, including the actor-critic algorithm and proofs, are relegated to the supplementary material. Including pseudocode and a brief complexity discussion in the main paper would help.

Originality
- Strong. The unifying gradient formula for general coherent static risks and the policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risks are new (to my knowledge) and generalize several prior special-case results. The SAA-based gradient estimator with consistency in this RL setting is a solid contribution.

Significance
- Potentially high. Coherent risks are widely used; providing general policy gradients and an actor-critic template could influence risk-sensitive RL practice and research. The connection to robust MDP methods for the critic is timely.
- The lack of substantive dynamic experiments and ablation/benchmarking limits immediate practical impact.

Strengths
- Unified, elegant theoretical framework covering a broad class of coherent risks.
- Clear derivations that subsume prior special cases (e.g., CVaR) and reduce assumptions.
- Consistent SAA estimator that is broadly applicable when analytic dual solutions are unavailable.
- New policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with a plausible actor-critic approach.
- Good linkage to robust MDP literature and function approximation.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is minimal; no dynamic MDP experiments in the main text.
- Practicality concerns: solving convex programs to obtain dual variables (possibly per state/distribution), sampling under ξ-weighted dynamics, and variance/sample complexity not analyzed.
- Assumptions: finite spaces and explicit differentiable envelopes; extension to continuous or implicit envelopes not demonstrated.
- Some implementation details and convergence guarantees are only in the supplementary.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include at least one dynamic MDP experiment comparing to CVaR/mean-variance baselines and robust MDP methods, with function approximation.
- Provide pseudocode and a short complexity/runtime analysis for the SAA estimator and the actor-critic loop (how often convex programs are solved; impact of envelope size).
- Discuss importance sampling strategies to avoid requiring modified simulators for ξ-weighted transitions; analyze variance implications.
- Clarify handling of non-unique quantiles/saddle points and the effect on gradient estimators.
- Expand on examples of envelopes satisfying Assumption 2.2 (e.g., spectral risk, mean-semideviation) and give concrete derivatives of constraints.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, general theoretical contributions that unify risk-sensitive policy gradients.
- Clean derivations that simplify and extend prior results.
- Potentially impactful direction for risk-sensitive RL and robust control.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation, especially for the dynamic setting.
- Practical feasibility and scalability not fully demonstrated.
- Reliance on assumptions that may limit immediate applicability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are substantial and of broad interest to the NIPS community, but the paper would benefit from stronger empirical support and more discussion of practical implementation and scalability.
