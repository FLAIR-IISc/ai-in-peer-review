This paper presents policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, generalizing previous work to a broader class of risk measures. The core contribution lies in formulating risk-sensitive policy gradients under this coherent-risk framework, providing novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk, and an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case. While the problem addressed – risk-sensitive RL – is a valuable one, and the generalization to coherent risk measures is conceptually appealing, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling empirical validation and relies heavily on theoretical development without demonstrating substantial practical impact.

**Summary:** The paper begins with a clear introduction to risk-sensitive optimization and coherent risk measures, motivating their use in RL. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches focused on specific risk measures and proposes a general framework. The technical contributions – the gradient formulas and actor-critic algorithm – are presented with a reasonable degree of mathematical rigor. The connection to robust MDPs and related work is well-established.

**Strengths:**
*   **Generality:** The framework applies to the whole class of coherent risk measures, offering a unifying perspective.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations of the gradient formulas appear mathematically correct, building upon existing results in stochastic optimization and coherent risk theory.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature, clearly identifying related work and highlighting the novel contributions.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem is well-defined, and the motivations for studying coherent risk in RL are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration (Section 6) is insufficient to demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. It focuses on illustrating a *choice* between risk measures rather than a comprehensive comparison of performance against standard RL algorithms or existing risk-sensitive methods, particularly in environments of realistic complexity. The experimental setup is not clearly defined, making it difficult to assess the significance of the results.
*   **Complexity & Scalability Concerns:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm, while theoretically sound, appears computationally intensive, particularly in the dynamic case, due to the need for convex programming within each iteration and the sampling requirements. The paper does not adequately address scalability concerns or provide any analysis of the algorithm’s computational complexity.
*   **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** The experiments lack comparison to strong baselines. Showing improvements over standard policy gradients or existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms (e.g., CVaR-based approaches) is crucial for establishing the value of this more general framework.
*   **Assumption Dependence:** While the authors address assumptions required for the method, they do not fully analyze the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions. A discussion of the impact of violating the assumptions (e.g. smoothness of constraints) would strengthen the work.
*   **Overly Theoretical Focus:** The paper is heavily focused on theoretical derivations, with relatively little attention paid to practical implementation details or potential challenges. This makes it difficult to assess the real-world applicability of the proposed methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The generalization to coherent risk measures is a potentially valuable contribution, offering a more flexible framework for risk-sensitive RL.
*   The theoretical results are sound and well-derived.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in RL with relevance to applications in finance and other domains.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The lack of comprehensive empirical validation significantly weakens the paper’s claims.
*   The computational complexity and scalability concerns raise doubts about the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
*   The paper does not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms.
*   The numerical illustration is insufficient to establish the value of the coherent risk framework.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the theoretical contributions are interesting and the problem is important, the lack of robust empirical evaluation and the potential scalability issues are significant concerns. The paper feels incomplete, presenting a promising theoretical framework without demonstrating its practical effectiveness. A substantially revised version with a more thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to strong baselines, would be necessary to warrant acceptance. The paper needs to move beyond theoretical derivations and provide convincing evidence that the proposed methods can deliver tangible benefits in real-world RL applications. I recommend the authors focus on a well-defined, challenging problem with clear performance metrics and demonstrate that their approach outperforms existing alternatives.
