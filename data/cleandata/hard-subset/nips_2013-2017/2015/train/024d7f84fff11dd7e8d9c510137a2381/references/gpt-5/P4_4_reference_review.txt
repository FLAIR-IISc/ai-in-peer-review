Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general class of coherent risk measures. It makes two main contributions:

1) Static risk: Using the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński and Shapiro), the authors derive a policy-gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) via the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio identity. This yields sampling-based estimators for ∇θρ(Z) for arbitrary coherent risks specified by convex risk envelopes. They instantiate the formula for CVaR, recovering and extending known gradients (e.g., Tamar et al. for CVaR) without additional smoothness assumptions, and for mean-semideviation. For general envelopes where analytic saddle points are unavailable, they propose a sample average approximation (SAA) approach that solves a convex program to obtain the (ξ, λ) saddle point and constructs a consistent gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4).

2) Dynamic risk: For Markov coherent, time-consistent dynamic risk measures (Ruszczyński; Chow and Pavone), the paper derives a policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) expressing ∇θVθ(x) as an expectation over trajectories generated by a ξ*-reweighted kernel, with a stage-wise term hθ that depends on the value function and the KKT multipliers of the local risk-envelope optimization. This yields an actor-critic method: a critic estimates Vθ (leveraging the equivalence to robust MDPs and function approximation per Petrik and Subramanian), and an actor uses Theorem 5.2 with a two-phase sampling scheme to update θ.

A small numerical illustration on a one-step portfolio selection problem shows how different coherent risk choices lead to different policies, underscoring the flexibility of the framework. The work unifies previous RL treatments focused on specific risks such as mean-variance, exponential utility, and CVaR, and connects to robust MDPs for the critic.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The static gradient derivation via the envelope theorem and dual representation is principled. The SAA-based estimator and its consistency are standard under the stated regularity conditions on the risk envelope and constraints. The CVaR and mean-semideviation examples are consistent with known results, strengthening confidence in the framework. The dynamic policy-gradient theorem is plausible and aligns with prior robust MDP interpretations of Markov coherent risk. However:
  - Differentiability and uniqueness: Coherent risks like CVaR can be non-differentiable (e.g., quantile ties). The paper states a “gradient” expression for any saddle point; in general, one obtains a subgradient unless additional regularity holds. Clarifying whether Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 yield gradients or subgradients under non-smooth cases would improve rigor.
  - Assumptions: The static theory assumes a finite probability space and bounded likelihood ratios. For RL settings where Z is a trajectory return, Ω is typically infinite; the paper claims extensions to Lp but defers details. Making these extensions explicit (conditions for dominated convergence/interchange of differentiation and maximization) would strengthen the contribution.
  - Practical sampling: The dynamic actor requires sampling from ξ*-reweighted transition kernels, which presumes access to a generative model to resample or an implementable change-of-measure scheme. This may limit applicability beyond simulator settings. The variance properties of the estimators (especially under rare-event-sensitive risks) and any variance-reduction strategies are not analyzed.
  - Algorithmic details: The actor-critic method’s full specification and convergence guarantees are relegated to the supplement. Complexity and stability considerations (computing per-state KKT multipliers and reweighting) deserve more discussion.

- Empirical evaluation: Evidence is limited to a toy single-step example. There are no dynamic MDP experiments, comparisons to specialized CVaR/mean-variance methods in control tasks, or ablations on sample complexity and computational overhead. This restricts assessment of practical impact.

Clarity
The exposition of the dual envelope and envelope-theorem-based derivations is generally clear, and the connection to prior work is well drawn. Notation is heavy; some definitions (e.g., whether results apply as gradients vs subgradients under non-smoothness; how Ω relates to trajectories in RL) should be clarified. Presenting at least high-level pseudocode for the actor-critic loop, sampling steps under ξ*-weighted kernels, and the SAA gradient routine in the main text would improve readability. Some references appear incomplete in formatting.

Originality
The general policy-gradient formula for coherent static risk and the dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appear novel. The unification across coherent risks via risk envelopes, and the SAA-with-convex-programming gradient estimator, are original in the RL literature and generalize several prior results for specific risks.

Significance
Conceptually significant: it provides a principled, unifying approach for risk-sensitive RL with coherent risks and connects to robust MDPs. If made practical, this could influence policy-gradient methods where risk preferences vary by application. The current empirical support is insufficient to demonstrate practical advantages or scalability; thus near-term impact on practice is uncertain.

Pros for acceptance
- General, principled framework that unifies and extends prior risk-sensitive PG results.
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Clean derivations rooted in convex duality and envelope theorems.
- Consistency result for a practical SAA-based gradient estimator.
- Clarifies links to robust MDPs and suggests an actor-critic architecture.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation; no dynamic RL experiments or scalability studies.
- Important algorithmic details (actor-critic implementation, convergence) are deferred.
- Practical requirements (access to generative model; per-state convex programs and KKT multipliers; ξ*-weighted sampling) may limit applicability.
- Non-smoothness issues (gradient vs subgradient) and extensions to infinite Ω are asserted but not fully developed in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify when Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 yield gradients versus subgradients; state explicit regularity conditions ensuring differentiability, and discuss handling ties (e.g., CVaR quantiles).
- Provide main-text pseudocode for the SAA gradient estimator and the actor-critic algorithm, including how to sample under ξ*-weighted kernels (e.g., acceptance-rejection, importance sampling) and how many auxiliary samples are needed to estimate hθ.
- Analyze computational complexity per update (convex program size, state-wise KKT solving), and discuss scalability with function approximation.
- Include experiments on dynamic MDP benchmarks, comparing to CVaR-specific and mean-variance baselines, with wall-clock and sample complexity metrics.
- Discuss variance properties of the estimators and incorporate variance-reduction (baselines/control variates; importance sampling for tail events).
- Make the extension beyond finite Ω explicit, with precise assumptions for RL trajectory spaces and boundedness of likelihood ratios.

Overall, this paper presents a strong theoretical framework with promising generality but lacks empirical depth and some practical clarity.
