- Goal: Develop policy gradient algorithms for the full class of coherent risk measures, unifying risk-sensitive RL.  
- Main contributions: new gradient formula for static coherent risk, sampling-based algorithm using convex programming, and a dynamic-programming policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.  
- Theoretical foundation: derivations leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk and provide rigorous proofs under stated assumptions (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2).  
- Generalization: framework subsumes prior results (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance, mean-semideviation) as special cases.  
- Practical example: trading-agent experiment shows different risk measures yield different investment strategies and illustrates connection to model uncertainty.  
- Strength: originality and rigor — elegant, principled extension of the classical policy gradient theorem to coherent risk measures.  
- Weakness: computational complexity and scalability — solving convex programs per gradient step and two-phase sampling for dynamic risk may be prohibitively expensive for large MDPs.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of Assumption 2.2 — requirement that the risk envelope fit a specific convex program may not cover all coherent risks and smoothness conditions may fail.  
- Weakness: empirical validation is narrow — only a simple three-asset trading example; lacks benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari) and comparisons to existing methods.  
- Overall assessment: significant and novel theoretical contribution with strong justification, but practical impact is weakened by limited computational discussion and empirical evidence.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: add computational complexity analysis (especially for dynamic risk), broaden empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmarks, more thoroughly discuss limitations of Assumption 2.2, and clarify relations to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms.
