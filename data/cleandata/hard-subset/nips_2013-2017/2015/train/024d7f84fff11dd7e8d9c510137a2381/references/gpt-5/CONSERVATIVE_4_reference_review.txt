Summary
The paper studies risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general umbrella of coherent risk measures. Leveraging the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk [Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro], the authors:

- Derive a general policy-gradient formula for static coherent risks (Theorem 4.2) using an envelope theorem and likelihood ratios. They show how known results for CVaR and mean-semideviation arise as special cases and propose a sample-average approximation (SAA) estimator that requires solving a convex program to obtain the saddle-point multipliers, with a consistency result.
- Develop a policy gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), leading to an actor-critic scheme that relates the gradient to a suitable value function under ξ-weighted transitions, and connects to robust MDPs. 
- Provide a small illustrative portfolio experiment.

Relation to prior work: The paper unifies previous risk-sensitive gradient methods (mean-variance, CVaR) and connects to robust MDP formulations for dynamic coherent risk (e.g., Ruszczyński; Chow et al.; Petrik & Subramanian). The idea of using dual representations is standard in risk optimization, but its systematic use in policy gradient RL across the coherent class is novel. The dynamic gradient theorem extends known policy-gradient results and relates to risk-sensitive actor-critic (e.g., Borkar; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh) in a more general coherent framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The static-gradient formula cleanly recovers CVaR and extends to other coherent risks (e.g., mean-semideviation), clarifying how to handle many risk envelopes in one framework.
- Theoretical development: The use of the envelope theorem to obtain gradients through the Lagrangian saddle point is elegant; the SAA-based gradient estimator is consistent under reasonable conditions; the dynamic policy-gradient theorem is a substantive theoretical extension with a clear link to robust MDPs.
- Potential impact: If practical, a general-purpose risk-sensitive PG/AC toolkit that covers coherent risks could be valuable for problems where CVaR or mean-variance are not appropriate.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and assumptions: 
  - The approach assumes a finite probability space (then claims extension to Lp without details), explicit knowledge of the risk envelope in a convex programming form, and differentiability of envelope constraints with respect to the probability vector Pθ. For many risk measures and RL settings, obtaining and differentiating these constraints w.r.t. Pθ is nontrivial.
  - The dynamic method requires sampling from a ξ∗-weighted transition kernel and multiple next-state samples per state; this presumes a simulator with reset and branching and limits applicability to model-based or controlled-simulator settings. Working with a single trajectory is deferred as future work.
  - Each gradient estimate involves solving a convex program to obtain ξ∗ and KKT multipliers (static), and per-state envelope solves (dynamic). The computational overhead and scalability are not evaluated.

- Experimental validation:
  - Only a toy static portfolio illustration is presented. There are no dynamic MDP experiments, no comparisons to established baselines (e.g., CVaR policy gradient/actor-critic, mean-variance PG, robust MDP approximation), no sensitivity analyses (e.g., α in CVaR), and no runtime/sample efficiency evaluations. For NeurIPS, the empirical support is far below expectations for an algorithmic contribution claiming generality.
  - No demonstration that the SAA-based gradient is competitive with closed-form estimators when available, beyond a brief statement relegated to the supplement.

- Theoretical clarity and scope:
  - The envelope theorem application relies on constraint qualifications and differentiability assumptions; multiple saddle points and non-unique quantiles (e.g., CVaR) complicate differentiability. While some conditions are stated, the main text does not fully articulate when the formula is valid and invariant to saddle-point selection.
  - The dynamic theorem presumes ergodicity and well-defined ξ-weighted kernels; assumptions ensuring stability/contraction and the effect of function approximation error are deferred to the supplement. No rates or variance analyses are provided; variance can be severe with likelihood ratios in long horizons, especially for tail risks.
  - Many details (algorithms, proofs, convergence) are in the supplement, leaving the main paper short on actionable guidance (e.g., no pseudo-code in the main text).

Quality
Technically interesting and mostly sound under the stated assumptions, but missing rigorous discussion of key technical conditions in the main text and lacking empirical validation of practicality and robustness.

Clarity
Generally well-written but notation-heavy. The paper would benefit from: clear pseudo-code for the static SAA estimator and the dynamic actor-critic; a consolidated assumptions section detailing constraint qualifications; and concrete guidance on computing dfi/dp and dge/dp for representative risks.

Originality
Moderately strong. The unifying static gradient via the dual representation is a neat consolidation; the dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears novel beyond the robust MDP equivalence.

Significance
Potentially significant if made practical, but current evidence is limited. Without experiments on dynamic MDPs and thorough comparisons, it is difficult to assess real-world utility.

Pros for acceptance
- Unifies risk-sensitive policy gradients across coherent risks.
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem with a principled actor-critic outline.
- Clean derivations that recover known special cases (CVaR) and extend to others.

Cons for acceptance
- Very limited empirical validation; no dynamic experiments or baseline comparisons.
- Strong assumptions and notable practicality constraints (need for simulator branching, per-step convex programs, envelope derivatives).
- Key algorithmic and convergence details are deferred; computational overhead unquantified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add dynamic MDP experiments with function approximation on standard benchmarks; compare to CVaR-AC, mean-variance PG, and robust MDP approximations; report sample efficiency and runtime.
- Include main algorithm pseudo-code and a worked example of computing the envelope derivatives for at least two coherent risks beyond CVaR.
- Clarify and tighten the regularity conditions for Theorem 4.2 (envelope theorem applicability, saddle-point uniqueness/selection) and Theorem 5.2 (ergodicity, contraction, effect of approximation).
- Discuss variance reduction (e.g., baselines, control variates, importance sampling) and provide initial empirical evidence.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical unification is appealing, but the paper currently lacks the empirical rigor and practical substantiation expected for NeurIPS. Strengthening the experiments and clarifying key assumptions would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence
High.
