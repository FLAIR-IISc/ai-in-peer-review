Summary
The paper proposes policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk objectives in RL. The core idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk (as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope) and apply an envelope theorem to derive likelihood-ratio style gradient formulas. The contributions are: (i) a general gradient expression for static coherent risks, yielding sampling-based estimators; (ii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) approach that couples sampling with convex programming to obtain saddle-point/KKT multipliers and a consistency result; (iii) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent (time-consistent) risk with an actor-critic algorithm that uses robust MDP structure. Prior work mostly handled specific risks (mean-variance, exponential utility, CVaR) or dynamic risk with DP in small MDPs; this paper aims to unify and generalize to the entire class of coherent risks.

Strengths
- Unifying perspective: The static gradient formula via the coherent risk dual and envelope theorem is a clean, general statement that recovers known special cases (e.g., CVaR) and gives a template for others (e.g., mean-semideviation).
- Methodological clarity (for static risk): The SAA+convex-programming estimator is conceptually straightforward and the consistency argument (under reasonable convex-analytic assumptions) is appreciated.
- Dynamic extension: The “policy gradient theorem” for Markov coherent risk is interesting and connects naturally to robust MDPs. Using a critic to estimate the robust value and an actor to apply the gradient identity is a plausible architecture.
- Relation to literature: The paper situates itself among work on CVaR optimization, risk-sensitive actor-critic, and robust MDPs.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Strong assumptions and practicality: The key Assumption 2.2 requires a known, tractable risk envelope expressed with smooth dependence on Pθ and access to derivatives dge/dp, dfi/dp. Many coherent risks have envelopes independent of P and some are not easily available in such parametric form. The paper assumes these are given and differentiable in p; this limits applicability and is not validated empirically beyond simple cases.
- Finite probability space: Most derivations assume finite Ω, with “extension to Lp” deferred; this is nontrivial in RL with continuous state/action spaces and continuous cost distributions. Without details, it is unclear whether the core results remain valid and how to implement them in realistic settings.
- Non-uniqueness and nonsmoothness: The gradient identities rely on saddle points that may be non-unique (common for CVaR at mass points). The paper states the formula holds “for any” saddle point; but without addressing selection issues, this is (at best) a subgradient statement. Clarify differentiability vs. subgradients and provide precise conditions, especially for CVaR and other nonsmooth risks.
- SAA estimator: The estimator requires solving a convex program to obtain KKT multipliers per batch. While theoretically clean, this is computationally heavy; there is no complexity analysis or discussion of scaling to high-dimensional problems. Moreover, the variance properties are not studied beyond general LLN remarks.
- Dynamic case sampling requirement: The actor requires sampling from an MDP with transitions reweighted by ξ*, i.e., Pθ(·|x)ξ*(·). In most RL environments one cannot alter dynamics. The proposed two-phase sampling (estimate Vθ, compute ξ*, then sample ξ*-weighted transitions) presumes an oracle or a simulator that can sample from modified kernels; this is a strong and often unrealistic requirement. Importance sampling alternatives are not developed.
- Convergence and error propagation: The critic relies on robust MDP value-function approximation (citing [31]) and then the actor uses those estimates within the gradient. The paper defers convergence analysis and approximation error impact to the supplement; no finite-sample or asymptotic guarantees are discussed in the main text, and no empirical evidence is provided for stability.

Originality
- Static gradient via envelope theorem is an elegant generalization, but it is, in essence, a combination of standard tools (dual representation + envelope theorem + likelihood ratio). For key risks like CVaR and mean-semideviation, specialized policy-gradient forms exist; the generalization is conceptually useful but may be seen as incremental.
- The dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risks is novel to my knowledge, though closely related to robust MDP formulations and prior dynamic coherent risk works. Its practical instantiation is, however, left largely undeveloped.

Significance and validation
- Experimental evaluation is far too limited. The single toy “trading” example is a 3-armed bandit illustrating differences between mean-variance, mean-semideviation, and risk-neutral objectives; it does not exercise any of the dynamic risk machinery, the SAA estimator, or the actor-critic scheme. No comparison to prior algorithms (e.g., CVaR PG, risk-sensitive AC) on standard MDP benchmarks. This makes it difficult to assess the practical usefulness, scalability, or robustness of the proposed methods.
- No runtime/complexity or sample-efficiency analysis relative to established baselines.

Clarity
- Overall writing is clear and well-organized. However:
  - The dependence of U on Pθ is unusual; many coherent risk representations have envelopes independent of the baseline distribution. Please clarify when and why U(Pθ) is needed versus standard law-invariant settings, and how this affects the dynamic case.
  - Be explicit about subgradients at nondifferentiable points (e.g., CVaR, semideviation at zero variance).
  - The dynamic actor-critic description is too high-level in the main text; key algorithmic steps and assumptions should be summarized (even if proofs remain in the supplement).

Pros
- Unifying, theoretically grounded gradient expressions for coherent risks.
- Clear derivation for static risk and connection to known special cases.
- Interesting dynamic policy gradient identity linking to robust MDPs.

Cons
- Heavy assumptions and computational requirements, especially the need for ξ*-weighted transition sampling.
- Very limited empirical evidence; no dynamic risk experiments; no comparisons.
- Practical scalability and variance not analyzed.
- Some technical subtleties (nonsmoothness, selection of saddle points) are glossed over.

Questions for authors
1. Can the dynamic PG be implemented with off-policy importance sampling under the original dynamics, avoiding ξ*-resampling? Any variance bounds?
2. How does the SAA+KKT approach scale when the outcome space (or state space) is large? Any empirical timing or convergence diagnostics?
3. For CVaR with discrete distributions (mass at quantile), do you claim subgradients? Please formalize.
4. In which common coherent risks does U actually depend on P? Can you handle law-invariant envelopes independent of P without the smoothness in p?
5. Can you provide at least one dynamic MDP experiment (e.g., gridworld/queueing) comparing against CVaR-PG and robust MDP baselines?

Recommendation
While the theoretical framework is neat and potentially unifying, the paper lacks convincing empirical validation and relies on assumptions that limit practical applicability, especially in the dynamic case. Given NeurIPS standards and the emphasis on rigorously tested innovations, I recommend rejection in its current form. Strengthening with concrete dynamic experiments, practical sampling strategies under original dynamics, clearer treatment of nonsmoothness, and scalability analysis would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
