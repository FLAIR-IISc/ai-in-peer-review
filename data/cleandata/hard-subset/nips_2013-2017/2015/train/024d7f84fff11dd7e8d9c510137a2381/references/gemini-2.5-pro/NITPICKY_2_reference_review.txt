**Summary**

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as objectives. The core contribution is a novel formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure, derived using the dual representation of such measures and the envelope theorem. This formula generalizes and unifies previous results for specific risk measures like CVaR. Based on this formula, the authors propose a general gradient estimation algorithm that combines sampling with solving a convex optimization problem. The paper then extends this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures, deriving a policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one for expected returns, and sketching an actor-critic algorithm to solve the corresponding optimization problem. A simple numerical example illustrates the benefit of having a flexible choice of risk measures.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The paper's primary strength is its generality. It moves beyond the common ad-hoc treatment of individual risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) and provides a principled approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could unify a fragmented area of risk-sensitive RL.
2.  **Novel Theoretical Approach:** The derivation of the static gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) by combining the dual representation of coherent risk with the envelope theorem is elegant and powerful. It provides a clean and insightful way to understand the structure of the policy gradient in this setting and simplifies the re-derivation of existing results (e.g., for CVaR).
3.  **Key Theoretical Results:** The paper delivers two main theoretical results: the gradient formula for static risk (Theorem 4.2) and the policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2). These are non-trivial extensions of fundamental RL concepts to the risk-sensitive setting.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are promising, the paper is marred by a substantial number of issues ranging from a lack of clarity and missing details to insufficient experimental validation.

1.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   Crucial details are relegated to footnotes, which disrupts the flow and diminishes their perceived importance. For instance, Footnote 1 lists non-trivial extensions, Footnote 2 discusses policy class assumptions vital for applying static risk to MDPs, and Footnote 3 clarifies the state-dependency of the risk envelope, a key concept for the dynamic case. These should be integrated into the main text.
    *   The presentation of the notation `ξPθ` is confusing. While it represents a new probability measure, the constraints in Assumption 2.2 and the optimization problem (1) are written in terms of the density `ξ`, which makes the notation somewhat inconsistent and cumbersome.
    *   The phrase "with a slight abuse of notation" in Section 5 when defining `Vθ(x)` is an indicator of imprecise writing that should be avoided in a formal paper.

2.  **Missing Technical Details and Reproducibility:**
    *   The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is a central contribution, yet its description is extremely sparse. The paper states it will "highlight only the key ideas and results" due to "space limitation" and refers to a full version in the supplementary material. This is unacceptable for a conference submission, which should be self-contained. The critic's modification of an algorithm from [31] and the actor's "two-phase sampling procedure" are described in a single paragraph without any concrete formulas or algorithmic pseudocode, rendering the method entirely non-reproducible from the main text.
    *   The general gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex program (Eq. 7) at each step. The paper claims this can be done "efficiently" but provides no discussion of the computational complexity, which is a critical practical concern. How does the runtime scale with the number of samples N? This could be a significant bottleneck.
    *   The assumptions for the consistency of the SAA estimator (Proposition 4.4) are extensive and presented without much justification for why they are "rather mild." Condition (III), in particular, seems strong and its validity is not obvious.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:**
    *   The numerical illustration in Section 6 is far too simplistic to support the paper's claims. A single-step, three-armed bandit problem does not test the sequential decision-making aspect of the framework. Crucially, the dynamic risk algorithm (the actor-critic method), arguably the more complex and novel part of the algorithmic contribution, is not evaluated at all.
    *   The experiment aims to show the importance of flexibility in choosing a risk measure, which is a valid point. However, it fails to validate the proposed gradient estimation method itself. There is no analysis of the estimator's variance, bias, or convergence speed. The paper mentions a comparison between the SAA estimator and an analytical one for CVaR in the supplementary material, but this should have been a key result in the main paper to build confidence in the proposed general algorithm.

4.  **Minor Formatting and Stylistic Issues:**
    *   The use of citations is inconsistent; some are parenthetical `[27]`, while others are integrated into the text as `Artzner et al. [2]`. A uniform style should be adopted.
    *   The use of `. =` for definitions (e.g., `ρCVaR(Z;α) . =`) is unconventional.
    *   Footnotes 1 and 2 are attached to the same word ("state") in the introduction, which is an odd formatting choice.
    *   In Assumption 2.2, the definition `B :={ ξ : ∫ ω∈Ω ξ(ω) = 1, ξ ≥ 0 }` appears to define a set of probability densities, but `Pθ ∈ B` is stated, where `Pθ` is a probability measure. This is imprecise.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a highly general and theoretically elegant framework for policy gradients with coherent risk, unifying a body of prior work.
*   The core mathematical results (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are novel and significant contributions to the theory of risk-sensitive RL.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper is incomplete, omitting critical details of the main proposed algorithm for the dynamic case, which severely hinders reproducibility and evaluation.
*   The experimental validation is superficial and does not support the algorithmic claims of the paper, particularly for the sequential decision-making setting.
*   The paper suffers from numerous clarity issues, imprecise statements, and formatting inconsistencies that detract from its quality.

**Decision**

While the theoretical ideas presented in this paper have significant merit and potential, the execution is unfortunately flawed. The lack of crucial algorithmic details for the dynamic case, combined with a wholly inadequate experimental section, makes it impossible to fully assess the practical significance and correctness of the contributions. The work feels preliminary and is not yet at the standard expected for a NIPS publication. I therefore recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to thoroughly revise the paper by providing a complete description of their algorithms, adding a comprehensive experimental evaluation on challenging sequential decision-making tasks, and polishing the overall presentation.
