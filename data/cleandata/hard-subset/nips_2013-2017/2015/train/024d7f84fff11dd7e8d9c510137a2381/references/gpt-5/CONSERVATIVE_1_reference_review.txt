Summary
This paper proposes policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The key idea is to use the dual (risk-envelope) representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and the envelope theorem to derive a general gradient formula for static coherent risks. The authors provide: (i) a new sampling-friendly gradient expression for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2), (ii) an SAA-based estimator that combines sampling with a convex program and proves consistency under assumptions, and (iii) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), leading to an actor-critic style algorithm that relies on robust MDP connections for the critic. Several known special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) are recovered. Experiments are limited to a toy static portfolio-selection illustration.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to risk-sensitive RL with specific criteria (mean-variance, CVaR, exponential utility), robust MDPs, and dynamic coherent risk. The novelty is the generalization to the full class of coherent risks in both static and dynamic settings, with gradient formulas derivable from the dual representation.

Strengths
- Conceptual generality: A unifying gradient framework that, in principle, applies to any coherent risk with a convex dual envelope. It subsumes CVaR and mean-semi-deviation gradients and clarifies their structure.
- Theoretical development: The static gradient formula via envelope theorem is clean; the SAA estimator and its consistency (Prop. 4.4) are well-motivated. The dynamic policy-gradient theorem (Thm. 5.2) is a nontrivial extension tying risk gradients to robust MDP value functions.
- Connections to established theory: Appropriate alignment with coherent risk duality and robust MDP equivalence; recovers known results as special cases with simpler derivations.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Empirical validation is extremely limited. The only numerical example is a simple static, single-step portfolio choice. There are no dynamic MDP experiments, no comparisons to existing risk-sensitive RL baselines (e.g., CVaR actor-critic; mean-variance policy gradients), and no scaling studies. This is below the bar for NeurIPS.
- Practicality concerns: The proposed estimators require solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers at each gradient step (static), and, in the dynamic case, obtaining ξ*-weighted transition samples and multiple next-state samples per state. Requiring a simulator that can resample many next states and alter transition kernels is often unrealistic in RL. The alternative (importance sampling from the nominal kernel) is not worked out and is likely to suffer high variance.
- Assumptions are strong and not convincingly justified: Finite probability spaces; explicit, smoothly parameterized risk envelopes with differentiability w.r.t. Pθ; existence and boundedness of saddle points and KKT multipliers; availability of ∇θ log P(ω) per outcome. Extension beyond finite Ω is claimed but omitted. For widely used coherent risks (e.g., spectral risks), the required smooth dependence on Pθ and derivative terms dfi/dp may be nontrivial.
- Variance and sample complexity: There is no quantitative analysis of estimator variance or convergence rates in the dynamic setting. The ξ-reweighting suggests potential heavy-tail/rare-event variance issues (especially for CVaR). Suggestions about importance sampling are deferred to future work without concrete methods or analysis.

Clarity
- The exposition of Theorem 5.2 and the operational algorithm is high-level; crucial details (how to compute ξ* efficiently per state with function approximation; how to sample from ξ*-weighted dynamics; actor-critic convergence) are deferred to the supplement. As written, reproducibility is limited.
- The role and computation of the derivatives ∂fi/∂p, ∂ge/∂p in Theorem 5.2 are not sufficiently illustrated for common envelopes beyond CVaR. Concrete worked examples (e.g., spectral risk) would help.
- Several claims (“extensions to Lp”, “analysis in supplement”) are not verifiable from the main text, which weakens the perceived rigor.

Originality
- The static gradient via risk-envelope duality and envelope theorem is a neat consolidation; recovering CVaR and mean-semideviation gradients with fewer assumptions is useful. The dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears novel. However, much of the structure leverages known duality and robust MDP equivalence; the contribution is more a careful synthesis than a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.

Significance
- If made practical, a general coherent-risk policy gradient would be impactful. However, the current paper does not demonstrate applicability beyond toy settings, and the requirements (convex programs + modified simulators) limit immediate utility. Without empirical evidence on nontrivial MDPs (e.g., control, inventory, portfolio with temporal dynamics), the significance is unclear.

Questions/clarifications for the authors
1. How do you compute ξ* and KKT multipliers efficiently in large state spaces with function approximation? Is there a scalable primal-dual approach that avoids solving a full convex program per state/iteration?
2. In the dynamic algorithm, how do you implement sampling from Pθ(·|x)ξ*θ,x(·) without altering the simulator? Can you provide an importance sampling scheme and analyze its variance?
3. For spectral risk measures, can you explicitly write the envelope constraints, show differentiability in p, and illustrate the computation of the derivative terms in hθ?
4. Do your actor-critic convergence guarantees hold with function approximation and approximate ξ*? What are the conditions (e.g., contraction, step-size schedules)?
5. Can you provide experimental comparisons against CVaR actor-critic and mean-variance baselines on standard RL benchmarks, including runtime and sample complexity?

Pros
- General, theoretically grounded framework; unifies multiple risk criteria.
- Clear derivations in the static case; consistency of SAA estimator.
- Novel dynamic policy-gradient theorem aligned with robust MDPs.

Cons
- Very limited experiments; no dynamic results or baseline comparisons.
- Strong assumptions and practicality gaps (modified simulator, convex programs per iteration).
- Missing variance/sample complexity analysis; many details relegated to supplement.

Recommendation
- Overall: Reject.
- Rationale: The theoretical unification is interesting, but the paper lacks convincing empirical validation and practical algorithmic development for dynamic settings. The reliance on strong assumptions and simulator access further limits applicability. With substantial experimental evidence, scalable implementations, and clarified assumptions, this work could become impactful.

Confidence: High.
