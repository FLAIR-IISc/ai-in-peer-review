

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary

This paper develops policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, providing a unified framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors recognize that while specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.) have been studied in RL, there is a need for a general approach that encompasses all coherent risk measures, which satisfy desirable properties like convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a new gradient formula for static coherent risk that facilitates sampling-based estimation, (2) a general algorithm for estimating gradients of static coherent risk using sampling with convex programming, and (3) a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures that extends the standard policy gradient theorem to dynamic risk-sensitive settings. The work builds on the theoretical foundation of coherent risk measures from Artzner et al. [2] and connects it to RL through the representation theorem from Ruszczynski and Shapiro [26], generalizing previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures [8, 10, 18, 21, 29, 30].

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The authors provide clear proofs of their main theorems (with details in supplementary material) and establish consistency results for their gradient estimators. The connection between coherent risk measures and dynamic programming is particularly well-executed, with the Markov coherent risk measure properly defined and analyzed. The authors are careful to state assumptions clearly (Assumptions 2.2 and 4.1), and the paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors systematically introduce necessary background before presenting their main results, and the examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) effectively illustrate the general approach. The presentation of theorems is precise, and the paper is accessible to RL researchers with some background in risk measures. The notation is consistent throughout, and the logical flow from static to dynamic risk is clear.

**Originality:** This is a significant contribution as it provides the first unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures. While specific risk measures have been studied before, this work generalizes and unifies previous approaches, showing that existing results are special cases of their framework. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly from [24] (which proposed a dynamic programming approach for dynamic risk that doesn't scale to large MDPs) and [25, 26] (which focused on stochastic programming, not MDPs).

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. Risk-sensitive RL is crucial for applications where decision-makers care about variability and tail risks (finance, operations research), not just expected outcomes. By providing a unified framework, the authors enable researchers to more easily experiment with different risk measures. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of selecting appropriate risk measures for specific problems, showing how different risk preferences lead to different investment strategies.

## Weaknesses

**Algorithmic Implementation Details:** The paper provides limited details on practical implementation of the proposed algorithms. While the theoretical framework is strong, the "actor-critic" algorithm for dynamic risk (Section 5) is only briefly described, and the paper lacks guidance on handling computational challenges of the convex programming steps in large-scale settings.

**Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example in Section 6 is limited to a simple three-asset trading problem. While it effectively demonstrates the theoretical point about risk measure selection, it lacks a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmarks. A more extensive set of experiments would strengthen the paper's practical relevance and demonstrate the real-world utility of the proposed framework.

**Comparison with Existing Methods:** The paper mentions that their approach generalizes previous work but doesn't provide a thorough comparison of computational efficiency or performance with specialized methods for specific risk measures. For example, it would be valuable to see how their CVaR gradient estimator compares to the specialized CVaR gradient estimators in [8, 30] in terms of sample complexity or computational cost.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are novel, well-justified, and general enough to encompass previous work on specific risk measures. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in RL.

While the empirical evaluation is somewhat limited, the theoretical contribution is substantial, and the paper is clearly written. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach and provide a clear path for future work.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Provides a unified framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures
- Theoretical results are rigorous, well-justified, and generalize previous work
- Clear connection between coherent risk measures and dynamic programming
- Well-written paper with good organization and illustrative examples
- Demonstrates practical importance of risk measure selection through a numerical example

**Con:**
- Limited empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmarks
- Some details on practical implementation of the algorithms are missing
- Comparison with specialized methods for specific risk measures could be more thorough

## Final Thoughts

This paper makes a significant contribution to the risk-sensitive RL literature by providing a unified theoretical framework. The authors successfully bridge the gap between the theory of coherent risk measures and practical RL algorithms. The framework they present is likely to be influential in future work on risk-sensitive RL, particularly as the field continues to explore more sophisticated risk management approaches. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel theoretical contribution with clear connections to existing RL work, and the results advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
