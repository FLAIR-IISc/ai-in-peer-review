### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as objectives. The core idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the "risk envelope"). The authors make two primary contributions. First, for the static risk setting (e.g., risk of the total discounted return), they derive a novel policy gradient formula using the Envelope Theorem on the Lagrangian of this dual formulation. This leads to a general estimation algorithm that combines sampling with solving a convex program. They show that this framework elegantly recovers and simplifies known results for specific risk measures like CVaR and derives new formulas for others like the mean-semideviation. Second, for the dynamic, time-consistent case, they introduce a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, analogous to the standard policy gradient theorem. This theorem relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function and forms the basis for a proposed actor-critic algorithm. The work aims to unify and generalize previous ad-hoc approaches for specific risk measures, providing a single, principled method for the entire class of coherent risk.

### Review

This paper presents a significant and elegant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. By unifying policy gradient methods under the umbrella of coherent risk theory, it provides a principled and generalizable approach that moves beyond the study of individual risk measures. The paper is well-written and the theoretical development is sound. However, the algorithmic details for the dynamic case are sparse, and the empirical validation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical viability of the proposed algorithms.

#### Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical novelty and significance. Prior work in risk-sensitive RL has often focused on deriving policy gradients for specific risk measures like mean-variance or CVaR, each requiring a separate, bespoke derivation. This paper provides a single, unified framework based on the fundamental dual representation of coherent risk. This is a powerful conceptual leap that not only simplifies and generalizes existing results (as demonstrated with CVaR) but also provides a clear recipe for deriving gradients for any coherent risk measure that can be described by a convex risk envelope. This significantly broadens the scope of objectives that can be optimized in RL and is likely to be built upon by future research.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical derivations appear sound and are technically impressive. The application of the Envelope Theorem to the Lagrangian of the dual risk formulation (Theorem 4.2) is a clever and elegant technique that forms the cornerstone of the paper. This approach cleanly separates the policy-dependent part (the likelihood ratio) from the risk-measure-dependent part (the saddle point of the Lagrangian), leading to a general and interpretable gradient formula. The extension to the dynamic case via the risk-sensitive Bellman equation (Theorem 5.2) is a logical and well-motivated next step, providing a risk-sensitive analogue to the classic policy gradient theorem.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It logically progresses from the necessary preliminaries on coherent risk to the static gradient formulation, and finally to the dynamic case. The inclusion of concrete examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation (Sections 4.1 and 4.2) is extremely helpful for grounding the abstract theory and demonstrating the framework's utility. The numerical illustration, while simple, effectively communicates the core motivation: the flexibility to choose a risk measure appropriate for the problem's characteristics (e.g., handling heavy-tailed distributions).

#### Weaknesses (Reasons for Rejection / Major Revisions)

1.  **Insufficient Algorithmic Detail for the Dynamic Case:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem (DRP) is described at a very high level, with key details deferred to the supplementary material. The paper states, "Space limitation restricts us from specifying the full details of our actor-critic algorithm and its analysis." While understandable, this makes the contribution in Section 5 feel incomplete and difficult to fully evaluate. Crucial questions remain unanswered in the main text: How is the critic (the value function Vθ) estimated and updated? How is the two-phase sampling for the actor update implemented? Without at least an algorithm box or a more detailed description, the actor-critic method is more of a high-level proposal than a fully-specified algorithm.

2.  **Weak Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration in Section 6 is a stateless multi-armed bandit problem. While it serves as an excellent motivation for *why* one might want to use different risk measures, it does not validate the performance of the proposed *algorithms*. Specifically, it does not test the Sample Average Approximation (SAA) gradient estimator from Section 4.3 on a sequential decision-making problem, nor does it provide any evaluation of the actor-critic algorithm from Section 5. The paper would be substantially stronger if it demonstrated that the proposed algorithms can successfully solve a canonical MDP benchmark problem (e.g., gridworlds, cart-pole, or a simple inventory management problem) where managing risk is meaningful.

3.  **Potential Computational Concerns:** The general gradient estimation algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) to find the saddle point (ξ*, λ*) for each gradient update. The complexity of this step depends on the number of samples N. In modern deep RL, gradient estimates often rely on large batches of samples. The paper does not discuss the computational feasibility of this inner-loop optimization, which could be a significant bottleneck for practical application, especially in large state/action spaces.

#### Conclusion and Recommendation

This is a very strong paper from a theoretical standpoint. It introduces a novel, elegant, and unifying framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures that represents a clear advance in the field. The work is well-presented and technically sound.

However, the weaknesses in the algorithmic specification and experimental validation are significant. The paper proposes a full actor-critic algorithm but provides scant details in the main text, and the experiments only motivate the problem rather than validating the proposed solution.

Despite these weaknesses, the core theoretical contribution is novel and important enough to warrant serious consideration at NIPS. Therefore, I lean towards **acceptance**, but I strongly recommend that the authors use the camera-ready version to (1) include a more detailed algorithmic description of the actor-critic method in the main paper, perhaps in an algorithm box, and (2) strengthen the experimental section by applying their method to at least one standard MDP benchmark to demonstrate its learning capabilities in practice. Addressing these points would turn a good theoretical paper into an excellent, well-rounded contribution.
