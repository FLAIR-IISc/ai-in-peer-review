This paper proposes policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, aiming to unify and generalize previous work. The approach focuses on formulating risk-sensitive policy gradients within a coherent risk framework, providing new formulas for static coherent risk gradients, an algorithm with consistency results, and a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk. While the paper tackles a relevant and interesting problem, and the theoretical contributions appear solid, the presentation suffers from a multitude of minor flaws that, cumulatively, detract from the overall impact and readability.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The framework aims for broad applicability across all coherent risk measures, which is a desirable goal.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivations appear mathematically sound, and the connections to existing results (robust MDPs, stochastic programming) are clearly established.
* **Potential Impact:** A general framework for risk-sensitive RL could be valuable to researchers and practitioners in fields like finance and robotics.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper properly situates itself within the existing literature, acknowledging related approaches and highlighting its generalizations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Formatting & Notation:** The paper is riddled with minor formatting inconsistencies. For instance, variable naming isn’t consistent (e.g., sometimes using boldface for vectors, sometimes not). The use of subscripts and superscripts is occasionally unclear. The equation numbering is also inconsistent (some sections have numbered equations, others do not).
* **Clarity & Readability:** While the mathematical derivations seem correct, the presentation is dense and difficult to follow. The authors assume a very high level of prior knowledge and do not adequately explain the intuition behind some of the steps. Definitions are often presented without sufficient context.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Assumption 2.2 regarding the risk envelope's form, while common, feels rather strong and may limit the practical applicability of the results. The justification for extending results to Lp spaces is weak (“details omitted for brevity”) – if it’s not crucial, it should be removed. The discussion around the smoothness requirements in Assumption 2.2 is also cursory.
* **Lack of Detailed Algorithm Specification:** The actor-critic algorithm is only described at a high level. Crucial implementation details, such as how function approximation is handled and how the ξ*-weighted samples are generated, are missing.
* **Numerical Experiment Detail:** The numerical experiment is too brief and lacks sufficient detail. It is not clear how the policies were trained (hyperparameters, optimization algorithm) or what metrics were used to evaluate their performance beyond a qualitative description.
* **Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the text impede readability (e.g., "w.r.t." should consistently be "w.r.t.", “Eq.” should be capitalized consistently).

**Specific Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.2:**  The connection between time consistency and Markov policies is stated, but not clearly explained. Why does the optimality of a policy segment imply risk optimality?
* **Theorem 4.2:** The proof is relegated to the supplementary material, which is acceptable, but a brief sketch of the key ideas would enhance understanding.
* **Section 4.3:** The phrase “SAA version” is slightly ambiguous. It’s unclear what is being approximated by the SAA.
* **Section 5:** The discussion regarding the “curse of dimensionality” feels somewhat hand-wavy. How does the proposed actor-critic algorithm specifically address this issue?
* **Figure 1:** The caption is missing. The axes aren’t labelled and the figure has no immediate value due to its lack of context.
* **References:** The citation style is not uniform. Some references are fully formatted, while others are incomplete.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning: risk-sensitive optimization.
* The proposed framework offers a degree of generality not found in previous approaches.
* The theoretical contributions appear novel and potentially impactful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is marred by a large number of minor flaws (formatting, clarity, typos) that detract from its overall quality.
* The lack of detailed algorithm specification and a comprehensive numerical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed methods.
* The strong assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2) limit the broader applicability of the framework.
* The density and complexity of the presentation make it challenging for readers to understand and appreciate the contributions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper, as currently written, falls short of the standards for acceptance at NIPS. While the ideas are promising and the technical contributions potentially valuable, the numerous minor flaws, lack of detail, and stylistic issues significantly diminish its impact. I would recommend **rejection** with the suggestion that the authors revise the paper to address these concerns. A thorough proofreading, clarification of the presentation, and a more detailed numerical evaluation are essential before resubmission. The authors should prioritize clarity and accessibility to ensure that their work can be readily understood and appreciated by the broader NIPS community.
