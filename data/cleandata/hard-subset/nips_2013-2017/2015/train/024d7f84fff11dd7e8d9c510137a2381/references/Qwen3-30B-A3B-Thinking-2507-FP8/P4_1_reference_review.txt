

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, addressing both static and dynamic risk formulations. The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to derive novel gradient formulas that generalize previous work focused on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, mean-semideviation). The key contributions include a new gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2), a general gradient estimation algorithm using sample average approximation (Proposition 4.4), and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

The paper builds on foundational work in coherent risk measures (Artzner et al., 1999; Ruszczynski and Shapiro, 2006) and extends the policy gradient framework from expected return (Sutton et al., 2000) to the broader class of coherent risk measures. The authors demonstrate how their approach recovers previous results as special cases, providing a more general and unified perspective on risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of flexible risk criteria by showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a trading example.

The paper exhibits strong technical soundness. The mathematical derivations are rigorous, with proofs provided in the supplementary material. The authors carefully establish the conditions under which their gradient estimators are consistent (Proposition 4.4), and they provide clear examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) that validate their theoretical results. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (Section 7) offers an interesting conceptual extension with potential broader implications for handling model misspecification.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The organization is logical, with clear sections that build upon each other. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the examples effectively illustrate the theoretical results. The paper adequately relates to prior work, explicitly stating how it generalizes previous approaches rather than merely replicating them.

The novelty of the work is significant. While risk-sensitive RL has been studied for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR in [8, 30], mean-variance in [18, 21]), this paper provides a unified framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The gradient formulas (Theorem 4.2 and 5.2) represent genuine theoretical advances that extend the policy gradient theorem to coherent risk measures. The paper clearly demonstrates how previous results are special cases of their framework.

The significance of the work is substantial. Risk-sensitive decision making is crucial in applications like finance and operations research, where managing variability alongside expected outcomes is essential. By providing a general framework that enables practitioners to choose the most appropriate risk measure for their specific problem, the paper addresses an important practical need. The numerical example effectively demonstrates how different risk measures can lead to different policy choices, highlighting the value of flexibility in risk criteria.

Strengths:
- Unified framework for coherent risk measures, generalizing previous specific-risk approaches
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with well-justified mathematical derivations
- Clear presentation of both static and dynamic risk formulations
- Effective numerical illustration demonstrating practical utility
- Strong connection to foundational work in coherent risk theory

Weaknesses:
- The finite probability space assumption (Section 2) limits applicability to continuous problems without additional justification
- Computational complexity analysis of the proposed methods is limited, particularly regarding the cost of convex programming for large-scale problems
- The numerical example is somewhat simplistic (only three assets), not fully demonstrating scalability to larger problems
- The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) is conceptually interesting but not deeply explored

Arguments for acceptance:
1. The paper provides a technically sound and novel theoretical framework that unifies previous work on risk-sensitive RL
2. The mathematical derivations are rigorous and well-justified
3. The work has clear practical significance for risk-sensitive applications
4. The paper clearly relates to and extends previous NIPS work on risk-sensitive RL

Arguments against acceptance:
1. The finite probability space assumption may limit applicability to continuous problems
2. The computational complexity of the proposed methods is not thoroughly analyzed
3. The numerical example is limited in scope and does not demonstrate scalability

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The paper makes a significant advance by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures, with clear theoretical justification and practical demonstration. While the finite probability space assumption and limited computational analysis are notable weaknesses, they do not undermine the paper's core theoretical contributions. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in risk-sensitive decision making and reinforcement learning. The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission, with significant novelty, technical soundness, clarity, and significance.
