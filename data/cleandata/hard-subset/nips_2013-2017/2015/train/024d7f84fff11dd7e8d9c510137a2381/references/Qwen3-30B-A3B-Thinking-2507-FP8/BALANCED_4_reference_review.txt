

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, addressing both static and dynamic risk objectives. The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to develop gradient estimation algorithms that generalize previous approaches focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The work connects to prior NIPS contributions on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 29, 30]) while extending them to the entire class of coherent risk measures.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main contribution—formulating a general policy gradient framework for coherent risk measures—is highly original. By establishing the connection between coherent risk measures and convex programming (Theorem 2.1), the authors develop a unified approach that subsumes previous specialized methods. The new gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) extend the classic policy gradient theorem to coherent risk, representing a significant theoretical advance.

2. **Technical Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations with rigorous proofs (in supplementary material). The assumptions are well-motivated and appropriate for the problem. The consistency result (Proposition 4.4) provides theoretical guarantees for the gradient estimator, which is crucial for RL applications.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, the notation is consistent, and the relationship between the general framework and specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation) is effectively demonstrated through concrete examples.

4. **Significance:** The framework provides decision-makers with flexibility to choose risk measures appropriate for specific problems (as demonstrated in the numerical example). This addresses an important practical need in risk-sensitive applications where the choice of risk measure significantly impacts policy selection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity:** The proposed algorithm involves a two-step procedure (sampling followed by convex programming) which may be computationally expensive. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability. For large-scale MDPs, the repeated convex programming solves could become prohibitive, especially compared to simpler gradient methods like those in [29, 30]. The authors should address this limitation and discuss how the approach scales with problem size.

2. **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example (Section 6) is somewhat simplistic (3 assets, 3 policies) and doesn't demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more complex RL settings. The paper should include more substantial experiments on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari) to evaluate sample efficiency, convergence rates, and performance against state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL methods.

3. **Assumption Limitations:** While the authors justify Assumption 2.2 as reasonable for many coherent risk measures, it restricts applicability to cases where the risk envelope has the specified convex structure. The paper should discuss how their framework would extend to risk measures without this structure, or acknowledge this as a limitation.

4. **Connection to Model Uncertainty:** The conclusion suggests a promising connection between coherent risk and model uncertainty, but this remains largely theoretical without empirical support. The authors should either provide preliminary evidence for this connection or acknowledge it as a direction for future work.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Add a section discussing computational complexity and scalability of the proposed algorithm, including comparisons with simpler risk-sensitive RL methods.

2. Expand the experimental section to include standard RL benchmarks, demonstrating how the method performs on more complex tasks compared to existing approaches.

3. Consider discussing how the framework might handle risk measures that don't satisfy Assumption 2.2, or explicitly state this as a limitation of the current approach.

4. Provide a more detailed comparison with related work [29, 30] in terms of computational requirements and performance, not just theoretical generality.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The technical quality is excellent, and the theoretical results are well-supported. While the paper's main contribution is highly original and valuable, the limited empirical evaluation and lack of computational complexity analysis are notable weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

The paper presents an important theoretical advance that could benefit the RL community, but the lack of substantial empirical validation on meaningful RL benchmarks and insufficient discussion of computational considerations prevent it from meeting NIPS' standards for significance and completeness. With stronger experimental results and more thorough discussion of computational aspects, this could be a strong paper.
