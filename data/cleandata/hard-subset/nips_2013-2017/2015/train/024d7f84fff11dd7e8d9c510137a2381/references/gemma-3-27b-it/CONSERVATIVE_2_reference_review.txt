This paper attempts to provide a general framework for policy gradient methods applied to reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The core idea is to leverage the duality representation of coherent risk measures to formulate gradients suitable for sampling-based optimization. While the motivation – a unified approach to risk-sensitive RL – is appealing, I find the presentation and, more importantly, the validation of the proposed methods lacking. The paper feels heavily theoretical and lacks a compelling empirical demonstration of its advantages over existing, more specialized techniques.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its attempt to generalize existing results for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) under a coherent risk framework. The derivation of the gradient formulas, particularly Theorem 4.2, is a reasonable starting point. However, the authors repeatedly state the generality of their approach without adequately demonstrating its practical benefits. For example, the re-derivation of the CVaR gradient (Example 1) doesn’t offer significant simplification or insight compared to existing literature, and the claim of broader applicability isn't substantiated. 

A significant weakness is the lack of thorough experimental evaluation. The single example in Section 6, while illustrative, is insufficient to justify the complexity of the proposed framework. The authors claim that the example highlights the “importance of flexibility in designing risk criteria," but the comparison only involves different risk measures applied to the *same* problem. A proper evaluation would involve comparing the performance of their general framework against specialized algorithms tailored to each risk measure on a range of benchmark tasks. Specifically, it is unclear if the computational overhead associated with solving the convex program within the gradient estimation step (as described in Section 4.3) is offset by any gains in performance or convergence speed. 

The theoretical analysis also feels incomplete. While Proposition 4.4 provides convergence guarantees for the sampling-based gradient estimator, the conditions are strong and their practical implications are not thoroughly discussed. Furthermore, the extension to dynamic risk (Section 5) relies heavily on the results from [24] and [31] without a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the actor-critic algorithm. The mention of needing a simulator for state transitions hints at potential limitations.

The paper’s writing is generally clear, but the numerous assumptions and technical details can be overwhelming. A more streamlined presentation, focusing on the core contributions and their practical implications, would improve readability. Moreover, there are numerous citations to the authors’ prior work, which feels somewhat self-referential. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Attempts to provide a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL.
*   Derives gradient formulas for coherent risk measures.
*   Provides theoretical justification for sampling-based gradient estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of comprehensive experimental validation.
*   Limited practical demonstration of the framework’s advantages.
*   Theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions.
*   The presentation is overly complex and lacks focus.
*   Relatively incremental contribution given the complexity.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper falls short of NIPS standards. While the theoretical ideas are sound, the lack of thorough experimental validation and the limited demonstration of practical benefits raise serious concerns. The paper feels more like a preliminary exploration of a potentially interesting direction than a fully developed and compelling contribution. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should significantly expand the experimental section, providing a more rigorous comparison against existing methods on a diverse set of benchmark tasks. They also need to address the computational complexities associated with their approach and provide a more detailed analysis of the assumptions underlying their theoretical results.
