Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the general umbrella of coherent risk measures. The key ideas are: (i) use the dual (risk envelope) representation of coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and the envelope theorem to derive a sampling-friendly gradient formula for static coherent risks (Theorem 4.2); (ii) instantiate this for important special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation), and (iii) extend to time-consistent dynamic settings via Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński) by deriving a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) that relates the gradient to an appropriate value function and worst-case transition reweightings. The authors propose a practical two-stage estimator for static risk based on sample average approximation (SAA) that combines sampling with solving a convex program and using the associated KKT multipliers, and an actor-critic scheme for dynamic risk that leverages the equivalence between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs (Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian) for the critic.

This work unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL approaches that target specific measures: mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh), CVaR (Rockafellar & Uryasev; Tamar et al.; Chow et al.), and exponential utility (Borkar), and ties to robust MDP planning (Petrik & Subramanian). The CVaR gradient formula is recovered (and simplified) as a special case.

Strengths
- Generality: A principled gradient formula for the entire class of coherent risks, covering many popular choices (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation).
- Technical novelty: Clean derivation via envelope theorem and risk envelopes; new dynamic risk policy gradient theorem; consistent SAA-based gradient estimator that uses KKT multipliers.
- Conceptual unification: Connects risk-sensitive RL and robust MDPs; recovers known results (e.g., CVaR PG) as special cases.
- Practicality of implementation for static risks: Likelihood-ratio estimators with convex programs that are standard and scalable for moderate sample sizes.
- Clarity of assumptions: Slater-type conditions, boundedness, and differentiability of envelope constraints are explicit.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is minimal. The dynamic algorithm is not demonstrated; the only experiment is a simple static portfolio choice. There is no evaluation on MDP benchmarks or comparison to state-of-the-art CVaR/variance PG baselines in dynamic settings.
- Sampling assumptions are strong. The actor-critic requires a simulator/generative model with the ability to sample multiple next states per visited state, and to sample under ξ-weighted transitions; no single-trajectory or off-policy method is provided.
- Computational overhead: The approach requires solving a convex program (and extracting KKT multipliers) each time the gradient is estimated (static) and, in the dynamic case, computing ξ* per state. Practical scalability, wall-clock time, and memory footprints are not analyzed.
- Finite probability space assumption: Extensions to continuous spaces are claimed but deferred; handling continuous state/action spaces, function approximation for ξ, and computing dfi/dp, dge/dp in practice need more detail.
- Some technical details are deferred to the supplementary material (e.g., proofs, convergence of actor-critic, approximation error bounds), making it difficult to fully assess robustness of the dynamic method.

Quality
The static gradient derivation looks sound and builds on established convex duality and likelihood-ratio techniques; the consistency of the SAA estimator under clear assumptions is reassuring. The dynamic policy gradient theorem appears novel and plausible, building on the robust MDP Bellman operator. However, the lack of empirical tests for the dynamic actor-critic and limited discussion of numerical stability/variance leave open questions about practical performance. The dependence on derivatives of envelope constraints with respect to transition probabilities may be nontrivial to compute for some risk measures.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with clear motivation and good use of the risk envelope representation. Notation is dense but consistent. Some key algorithmic steps (e.g., computing ξ* and KKT multipliers in the dynamic case, sampling under ξ-reweighted transitions) could be described more concretely, and pseudo-code for the actor-critic would help. Minor reference formatting issues exist.

Originality
High on the generalization front. The static formula unifies prior special-case gradients; the dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk appears new. The combination of convex-analytic risk envelopes with PG is elegant.

Significance
Potentially high: coherent risk is a standard in finance/OR, and providing general-purpose PG estimators could make risk-sensitive RL more broadly applicable. That said, adoption hinges on practical viability; stronger experimental evidence on dynamic benchmarks would significantly increase the impact.

Pros for acceptance
- General theoretical contribution with unifying framework and new dynamic PG theorem.
- Sound use of convex duality; proofs and consistency results included.
- Recovers/improves known results (e.g., CVaR PG) with simpler derivations.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is too limited; no dynamic MDP experiments.
- Practical sampling/computation requirements are heavy and not thoroughly analyzed.
- Extensions to continuous spaces and function approximation are asserted but not demonstrated.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide at least one dynamic MDP experiment (e.g., gridworld, inventory control) comparing to CVaR-PG and mean-variance PG baselines?
- How sensitive is the gradient variance to rare-event emphasis (small α in CVaR)? Any variance-reduction techniques used?
- How do you compute dfi/dp and dge/dp in practice for popular envelopes (e.g., spectral risk)? Examples would help.
- Can your actor-critic be adapted to a single-trajectory setting (e.g., via importance sampling or off-policy corrections)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add dynamic experiments with ablations: accuracy of critic, effect of function approximation, wall-clock/time per gradient step, and comparisons.
- Provide pseudo-code and implementation details for the actor-critic, including how ξ* and KKT multipliers are computed online.
- Explore stochastic primal-dual updates to avoid solving a full convex program per batch, and include baselines and control variates/baselines for variance reduction.
- Discuss continuous-state/action extensions explicitly, including parameterizations for ξ and practical computation of constraint derivatives.
- Analyze or empirically report the computational overhead versus standard PG, and memory/solver choice trade-offs.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a compelling and general theoretical framework with clear novelty and potential impact. The current experimental evidence is insufficient, especially for the dynamic method, but the contribution seems significant enough to merit acceptance provided the authors strengthen the empirical section and clarify practical aspects. Confidence: medium.
