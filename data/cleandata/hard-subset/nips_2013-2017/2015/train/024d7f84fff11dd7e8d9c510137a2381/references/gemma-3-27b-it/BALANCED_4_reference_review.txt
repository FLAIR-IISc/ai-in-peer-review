This paper addresses the important problem of policy gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives. The authors provide a unifying framework for handling a broad class of coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work focused on specific instances like CVaR or mean-variance. The main contributions include a novel gradient formula for static coherent risk, a consistency result for its sampling-based approximation, and a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk, accompanied by an actor-critic algorithm.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing risk-sensitive RL approaches and the benefits of using coherent risk measures, particularly regarding time consistency. The problem setting is clearly defined, and the authors convincingly argue for the broad applicability of their framework. The theoretical contributions – the gradient formulas and consistency results – are valuable and rigorously presented (though details are deferred to the supplementary material, which is a standard practice). The connection to robust MDPs and the discussion of model uncertainty in the conclusion are insightful and point towards promising future research directions. The numerical illustration, while limited, effectively demonstrates the impact of different risk preferences on policy selection.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, while the generalization to the entire class of coherent risk measures is a strength, the practical implications of this generality aren't fully explored. The numerical example focuses on relatively simple risk measures (mean-semideviation and CVaR), leaving the reader wondering how the approach performs with more complex, less-studied coherent measures. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation with a wider range of risk measures would significantly strengthen the paper. Second, the actor-critic algorithm proposed for the dynamic case relies on function approximation, and the convergence analysis presented in the supplementary material appears complex and may not fully address the challenges of non-stationarity inherent in actor-critic methods.  A more detailed discussion of the practical considerations of implementing and tuning this algorithm would be beneficial. Third, the paper lacks a clear comparison to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms beyond simply stating they are special cases. A more direct empirical comparison, perhaps on benchmark MDPs commonly used in the RL literature, would help establish the superiority (or limitations) of the proposed approach. Fourth, the assumptions, particularly Assumption 2.2 regarding the risk envelope, feel somewhat strong and may limit the applicability of the method. A discussion of how sensitive the results are to violations of these assumptions would be valuable. Finally, while the authors acknowledge the computational cost of convex programming within the sampling-based estimation, they do not provide a detailed computational analysis of the overall algorithm's scalability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents rigorous theoretical results, including novel gradient formulas and consistency proofs.
*   **Generality:** The framework generalizes existing risk-sensitive RL methods by encompassing the entire class of coherent risk measures.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of policy gradients with coherent risk measures is a novel and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Potential Significance:** The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in risk-sensitive RL and provide a more principled approach to decision-making under uncertainty.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example is limited in scope and doesn’t fully demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach across a wide range of risk measures and environments.
*   **Algorithm Complexity:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm is complex and its convergence analysis is deferred to the supplementary material, raising concerns about practical implementability.
*   **Lack of Comparison:** A more direct comparison to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms is needed to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
*   **Strong Assumptions:** The assumptions regarding the risk envelope may limit the applicability of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with potential for significant impact. However, the limited empirical evaluation and the complexity of the proposed algorithm raise concerns about its practical utility.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should significantly expand the empirical evaluation, providing a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods and exploring the performance with a wider range of coherent risk measures. They should also provide a more detailed discussion of the practical considerations of implementing the actor-critic algorithm and address the limitations imposed by the assumptions made. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of acceptance in a future submission.
