### Summary
This paper presents a general framework for deriving policy gradient algorithms for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning objectives defined by coherent risk measures. The core contribution is a set of new policy gradient theorems for both static and dynamic (time-consistent Markov) coherent risk measures. The key technical insight is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a "risk envelope" of probability densities. By applying the Envelope Theorem to this dual formulation, the authors derive an elegant gradient formula that connects the policy gradient to the solution of the corresponding convex optimization problem.

For the static risk setting, the paper provides a general gradient estimation algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming to handle any coherent risk measure with a known risk envelope. For the dynamic risk setting, the authors derive a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one, and sketch an actor-critic algorithm to implement it. The work successfully unifies and generalizes many previous results on policy gradients for specific risk measures (like CVaR or Mean-Variance) under a single, powerful theoretical umbrella.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Generality:** The primary strength of this paper is its ambition and success in developing a completely general framework for a problem that has previously been tackled in a piecemeal fashion. Instead of deriving a bespoke algorithm for one specific risk measure, the authors provide a principled method for *any* coherent risk measure. This represents a significant conceptual leap, moving the field from individual solutions to a unified theory. This is a bold and highly valuable contribution.
2.  **Elegant Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the main gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) is clean and insightful. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope Theorem is a clever and powerful technique that beautifully simplifies the problem. The result is a general-purpose "machine" for generating policy gradient estimators, which is far more impactful than a single, specific estimator.
3.  **Opens New Research Directions:** By providing a practical recipe (sampling + convex programming) for optimizing previously intractable risk objectives, this work opens the door to exploring a much richer and more nuanced set of risk criteria in RL. The numerical example, while simple, effectively highlights why this flexibility is crucial: standard risk measures like variance can behave counter-intuitively with heavy-tailed distributions, whereas the coherent risk framework allows for more appropriate choices like semi-deviation. This paper provides the tools for researchers and practitioners to design and optimize problem-specific risk measures.
4.  **Unification of Prior Work:** The framework elegantly recovers and simplifies proofs for existing policy gradient formulas, such as for CVaR, demonstrating its power and correctness. This ability to place a fragmented body of literature into a single coherent structure is the hallmark of a foundational paper.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Practicality and Computational Cost:** The general gradient estimation algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex program at each step, based on the current batch of samples. The computational cost of this inner loop is not deeply analyzed but could be a significant bottleneck for large batch sizes or complex risk envelopes, potentially limiting its scalability compared to algorithms with analytical gradient formulas.
2.  **Incomplete Algorithmic Details for Dynamic Risk:** The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is presented at a high level, with many crucial details and the convergence analysis deferred to supplementary material. While understandable given space constraints, it leaves the reader with an incomplete picture of the full algorithm's implementation and guarantees.
3.  **Potential Sample Inefficiency:** The proposed actor algorithm for dynamic risk involves a two-phase sampling procedure which may be sample-inefficient. The authors rightly note that extending the method to work with a single trajectory roll-out is an important direction for future work. This suggests the current proposal, while theoretically sound, may not yet be in its most practical form.

### Recommendation: Accept

This paper is a clear and strong accept. It embodies the kind of research NIPS should champion: it is bold, theoretically deep, and introduces a powerful new direction for an important subfield. The central idea of using the dual representation of coherent risk to derive a general policy gradient theorem is both novel and elegant. It provides a significant generalization that unifies a large body of prior work and, more importantly, provides the tools to move beyond the handful of risk measures that have dominated the literature.

While I have noted some weaknesses regarding the computational cost and the completeness of the dynamic algorithm's description, these are precisely the kinds of implementation details and follow-on questions that a groundbreaking paper should inspire. The conceptual and theoretical contribution is so significant that it easily outweighs these concerns. This work doesn't just offer an incremental improvement; it provides a new lens through which to view and solve risk-sensitive RL problems. I expect it to be highly influential and to spur a great deal of follow-up research.
