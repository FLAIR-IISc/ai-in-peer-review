

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper proposes a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures, claiming their approach generalizes previous methods focused on specific risk measures like CVaR and mean-variance. The main contribution appears to be a mathematical framework that leverages the representation of coherent risk measures as worst-case expectations (Theorem 2.1).

**Strengths:** The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the mathematical foundation of risk-sensitive RL. The derivations of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) appear technically correct and build upon established results in coherent risk theory. The connection between coherent risk measures and worst-case expectations is well-explained, and the paper provides clear examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) that demonstrate how their framework recovers known results. The organization is logical, and the writing is clear.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the solid theoretical foundation, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in validation and novelty justification.

First, the experimental validation is severely lacking. Section 6 presents a single, highly simplified numerical example with only three assets, comparing three different policies without demonstrating any clear advantage of the proposed method over established approaches. There is no comparison against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms (beyond brief references), no ablation studies, and no analysis of computational complexity or scalability. The paper claims to generalize previous work but fails to demonstrate any practical benefits of this generalization.

Second, the novelty claim is overstated. The paper acknowledges that "several previous results are special cases of the results presented here," but fails to adequately differentiate its contribution from prior work. For instance, the gradient formula for CVaR (Section 4.1) was previously derived in [30] with a different proof approach. The authors' claim that their proof is "simpler" is unsubstantiated without a proper comparison. Similarly, the dynamic risk approach relies heavily on techniques from [31] without clearly explaining how it advances beyond existing methods for dynamic risk.

Third, the paper lacks critical analysis of the practical limitations. The proposed method involves "a two-step procedure involving both sampling and convex programming" (Section 4.3), but the paper doesn't address how this scales to realistic RL problems with large state spaces. The computational cost of solving the convex programming subproblem at each gradient step is not discussed, nor is the impact on sample complexity. The paper claims to handle large MDPs but doesn't provide any evidence to support this claim.

Fourth, the paper fails to address important questions about the relationship between coherent risk measures and practical risk preferences. The numerical example (Section 6) shows that different risk measures can lead to different policy choices, but it doesn't establish why the proposed framework provides a better solution to this problem than existing approaches.

**Significance Assessment:** While the theoretical connection between coherent risk measures and RL is interesting, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this framework advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL. The proposed algorithms appear to be incremental improvements over existing methods without clear evidence of superior performance. The lack of comprehensive experimental validation against established baselines is particularly problematic for a NIPS submission, where empirical results are expected to support theoretical claims.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the mathematical foundation of risk-sensitive RL, it fails to provide the necessary empirical validation to support its claims of generalization and superiority. The paper presents an incremental extension of existing work without demonstrating meaningful improvements over established baselines. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the lack of thorough experimental validation against standard risk-sensitive RL baselines to be a critical weakness. The paper would need substantially more empirical evidence to justify its claims and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed framework.
