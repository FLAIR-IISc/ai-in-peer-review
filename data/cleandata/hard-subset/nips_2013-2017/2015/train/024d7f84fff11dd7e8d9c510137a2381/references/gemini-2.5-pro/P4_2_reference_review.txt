**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning (RL) under coherent risk measures. The work addresses both static coherent risk, applied to the total discounted cost of a trajectory, and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The central contribution is a unified approach that generalizes previous research, which has typically focused on specific risk measures like mean-variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR).

The key technical innovation is the use of the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the risk envelope). By applying the Envelope Theorem to this dual formulation, the authors derive a novel formula for the policy gradient of a static coherent risk measure (Theorem 4.2). This formula elegantly connects the gradient to the optimal dual variables of the underlying optimization problem. For cases where these dual variables lack an analytical solution, the paper proposes a general estimation algorithm that uses Sample Average Approximation (SAA) to solve a convex program based on empirical samples. This approach is shown to recover and simplify existing gradient formulas for CVaR and derive new ones for measures like mean-semideviation.

For the dynamic setting, the paper extends this methodology to derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This result generalizes the standard policy gradient theorem to the risk-sensitive case, relating the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. Based on this theorem, the authors outline an actor-critic algorithm for optimizing dynamic risk objectives in large-scale Markov Decision Processes (MDPs).

**Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The most significant strength of this work is its generality. By grounding the analysis in the axiomatic framework of coherent risk, the paper provides a single, principled method for deriving policy gradients for an entire class of risk measures. This unifies a body of prior work that has treated risk measures on a case-by-case basis (e.g., [21], [30]) and provides a clear recipe for extending policy gradient methods to other coherent risks not yet explored in RL.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Methodology:** The use of the dual representation of coherent risk in conjunction with the Envelope Theorem is a novel and powerful technique for this problem domain. This approach simplifies proofs for existing results (as noted for CVaR) and provides a clear path for deriving new ones, which is a valuable contribution to the RL theory toolbox.

3.  **Significant Theoretical Contributions:** The paper presents several new and important theoretical results. The static gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) and the policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) are fundamental contributions that extend the foundations of policy gradient methods to a risk-sensitive context. Furthermore, the consistency result for the SAA-based gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) provides important theoretical justification for the proposed general algorithm.

4.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and well-motivated. It provides a concise introduction to coherent risk and effectively situates the work within the existing literature. The numerical example in Section 6, while simple, effectively illustrates the practical importance of having the flexibility to choose different risk measures depending on the problem structure (e.g., heavy-tailed distributions).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Algorithmic Detail for the Dynamic Case:** A notable weakness is the high-level description of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk. The paper states that "Space limitation restricts us from specifying the full details" and defers the algorithm, its sampling procedure, and its convergence analysis to the supplementary material. While understandable, this makes the main paper feel incomplete and prevents a thorough evaluation of the practicality and soundness of the proposed algorithm for the dynamic setting. Key components, such as how the critic approximates the robust value function and how the actor's two-phase sampling is implemented, are crucial for assessing the work's full contribution.

2.  **Computational Considerations:** The general gradient estimation algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem for each gradient estimate (Section 4.3). Although convex programming is generally efficient, this step introduces computational overhead compared to standard policy gradient methods. The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability of this approach, which would be important for practitioners considering its use in large-scale problems with many samples (large N).

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important and growing area of risk-sensitive RL with a highly general and unifying framework.
*   The core theoretical contributions—the static gradient formula and the dynamic policy gradient theorem—are novel, sound, and significant. They provide a foundational extension of policy gradient theory.
*   The methodology is elegant and has the potential to be widely adopted for developing new risk-sensitive RL algorithms.
*   The paper is well-written and does an excellent job of connecting to and generalizing a wide range of previous work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is too brief in the main paper, making it difficult to fully assess the completeness and practicality of this part of the contribution.
*   The paper lacks a discussion on the computational trade-offs of the proposed SAA-based gradient estimator, which is an important practical consideration.

In conclusion, the paper's theoretical strengths and its contribution in unifying a significant area of risk-sensitive RL are substantial. The weaknesses, while notable, primarily relate to the completeness of the algorithmic description in the main text rather than fundamental flaws in the core ideas. The theoretical foundation laid by this work is a significant advance for the field.
