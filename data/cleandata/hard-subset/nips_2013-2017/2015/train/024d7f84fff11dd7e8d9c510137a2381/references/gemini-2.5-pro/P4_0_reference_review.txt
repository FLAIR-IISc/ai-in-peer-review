This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). The work addresses a significant gap in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, where prior research has largely focused on developing algorithms for specific risk measures such as mean-variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors propose a unified approach applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The paper considers two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted cost, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

The main contributions are theoretical and algorithmic. First, for the static risk problem, the authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities (the risk envelope). By applying the Envelope Theorem to this dual formulation, they derive a novel and general formula for the policy gradient (Theorem 4.2). For cases where an analytical solution is unavailable, they propose a general algorithm that estimates the gradient by solving a sample average approximation (SAA) of the dual problem, a convex program. Second, for the dynamic risk setting, the paper introduces a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This result generalizes the standard policy gradient theorem by relating the gradient of the risk-sensitive value function to a "risk-adjusted" advantage function, where expectations are taken with respect to worst-case transition dynamics derived from the risk measure's dual representation. Based on this theorem, an actor-critic algorithm is proposed.

This work builds upon the theoretical foundations of coherent risk from operations research [2, 26] and connects it to the policy gradient literature in RL [15, 28]. It successfully generalizes and simplifies results from recent papers that derived policy gradients for specific risk measures, such as CVaR [8, 30] and mean-variance [29, 21]. The approach for the dynamic case also draws upon connections between dynamic risk and robust MDPs established in prior work [19, 24].

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. By providing a principled method for computing policy gradients for any coherent risk measure (given a convex representation of its risk envelope), it unifies a fragmented area of research. This framework gives practitioners the flexibility to choose or design risk measures tailored to specific problems, a point well-argued in the introduction and effectively demonstrated in the numerical example. The theoretical results, particularly the gradient formula via the Envelope Theorem (Theorem 4.2) and the policy gradient theorem for dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2), are novel and significant contributions to the theory of risk-sensitive RL.

*   **Quality and Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-founded. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk is elegant and powerful, leading to a general and insightful gradient formula. The authors provide consistency results for their proposed SAA-based gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4), adding to the paper's rigor. The connection made between the dynamic risk gradient and a risk-adjusted MDP is a key insight that extends the classic policy gradient framework in a natural way.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts by clearly motivating the need for a general framework, introduces the necessary preliminaries on coherent risk, and then builds its contributions logically from the static case to the more complex dynamic case. The inclusion of concrete examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation helps to ground the general theory and demonstrates how the framework recovers and extends existing results.

### Weaknesses

*   **Practicality and Computational Cost:** The general algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem at each gradient estimation step. While convex, this could introduce significant computational overhead, potentially limiting the scalability of the approach compared to methods designed for simpler risk measures that have closed-form updates. This trade-off between generality and computational efficiency is not extensively discussed.

*   **Algorithmic Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is high-level. Key details about the critic (which leverages function approximation techniques from [31]) and the actor's two-phase sampling procedure are brief. The paper acknowledges that the algorithm requires a simulator capable of providing multiple next-state samples for any given state-action pair. This assumption is strong and may not hold in many real-world RL settings where learning must occur from single-trajectory rollouts. While noted as future work, this is a practical limitation of the proposed algorithm.

*   **Self-Containment:** Many essential details, including all proofs, the full actor-critic algorithm, and its convergence analysis, are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is common due to space constraints, it makes the main paper less self-contained and harder to evaluate thoroughly on its own.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  The paper provides the first general, unified policy gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures, a significant and novel contribution to risk-sensitive RL.
2.  The theoretical results, including the new gradient formulas for static and dynamic risk, are technically sound, elegant, and advance the state of the art.
3.  The framework offers valuable flexibility for practitioners to apply problem-specific risk criteria, moving beyond the standard, pre-defined risk measures.
4.  The work successfully generalizes and simplifies several recent results in the field, demonstrating its power and utility.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  The proposed general algorithms may have high computational and sample complexity (requiring solving a convex program and access to a generative model/simulator), potentially limiting their practical applicability in large-scale problems.
2.  The actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is described at a high level, with crucial implementation details and a key limiting assumption (simulator access) not fully explored in the main paper.
3.  The paper's reliance on the supplement for all proofs and detailed algorithmic descriptions makes a complete assessment of the work challenging based on the main text alone.
