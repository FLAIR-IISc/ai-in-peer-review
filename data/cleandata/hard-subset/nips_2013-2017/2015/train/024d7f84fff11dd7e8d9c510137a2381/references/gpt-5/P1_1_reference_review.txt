Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static coherent risk of trajectory returns and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. The key technical idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risks as worst-case expectations over a convex risk envelope and apply the envelope theorem plus likelihood-ratio tricks to derive gradient formulas.

For static risk, the authors:
- Derive a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) expressed in terms of the saddle point (primal-dual) solution of the risk-envelope Lagrangian, yielding an expectation weighted by the optimal density ξ* and corrections involving KKT multipliers.
- Show how known results for CVaR and mean-semideviation are special cases, yielding clean re-derivations and new formulas (e.g., CVaR gradient in discrete case without extra smoothness).
- Propose a sample-average approximation (SAA) approach that solves a finite convex program per batch to obtain ξ*, multipliers, and builds a consistent gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4).

For dynamic risk, they:
- Consider Markov coherent risk (time-consistent nested composition of a static coherent risk applied to next-state value distributions).
- Derive a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) expressing ∇θVθ(x) as an expectation over trajectories under ξ*-reweighted transitions, with a stage cost hθ involving value function, multipliers, and derivatives of envelope constraints with respect to transition probabilities.
- Outline an actor-critic algorithm: a critic computes Vθ using robust-MDP equivalence and function approximation; an actor estimates the gradient via two-phase sampling using ξ*-weighted transitions.

A small numerical example on portfolio selection illustrates that different coherent risk choices can yield qualitatively different policies.

Relation to prior work
The paper unifies and generalizes several strands: mean-variance and exponential-utility risk-sensitive RL, CVaR optimization in static MDPs, time-consistent dynamic risk and its equivalence to robust MDPs. The use of dual representations and envelope theorems follows classical convex risk optimization but is extended to policy-gradient settings where policies affect distributions. The dynamic gradient theorem extends the standard policy-gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk.

Strengths
- Generality and unification: a single framework and gradient formula covering a wide class of coherent risks (including CVaR, mean-semideviation, spectral measures) in both static and dynamic cases.
- Theoretical soundness: clear assumptions, coherent use of duality/KKT/envelope theorem, consistency of the SAA estimator. The dynamic theorem is novel and conceptually important.
- Recovering and simplifying known results: the CVaR gradient emerges transparently; the mean-semideviation gradient is derived with a sampling recipe.
- Practical algorithmic blueprint: sampling-plus-convex-program approach for static risk; actor-critic for dynamic risk via robust MDP connection.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is very limited. Only a small static trading example is shown; there are no dynamic experiments validating the actor-critic method, its stability, or performance versus baselines.
- Practicality concerns: each gradient estimate for general risks requires solving a convex program and accessing KKT multipliers; the dynamic method requires sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per visited state—implying a simulator and model knowledge. The approach seems difficult to deploy in purely model-free online RL.
- Strong assumptions: finite outcome/state spaces for exposition; existence of a strictly feasible point; bounded likelihood ratios; differentiability of envelope constraints with respect to transition probabilities; uniqueness/stability of saddle points. The dynamic theorem’s hθ depends on derivatives dfi/dp and dge/dp, which may be cumbersome or unavailable for some risk envelopes.
- Limited discussion of variance and sample complexity. Tail-sensitive criteria like CVaR can induce high-variance gradient estimates; while the paper mentions importance sampling as future work, there is no empirical/analytic variance study or finite-sample error bounds.
- Clarity/presentation: some notation is heavy and the dependence of the risk envelope on Pθ(·|x) could be further clarified. Concrete worked examples of hθ for specific risks (e.g., CVaR) in the dynamic setting would aid understanding. Many proofs and the full actor-critic algorithm are deferred to the supplement.

Quality
The theoretical contributions appear technically correct under stated assumptions. The SAA consistency is appropriate. However, the work would benefit from more comprehensive experiments (especially dynamic settings), complexity analysis, and variance/methodological robustness discussion.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, but dense. Suggestions: provide pseudocode for the SAA estimator and the actor-critic; add a worked dynamic example; discuss computational cost per iteration; explicitly list which common coherent risks satisfy Assumption 2.2 and provide the needed derivatives.

Originality
High. The extension of policy gradients to general coherent risks and the dynamic Markov coherent risk gradient theorem are novel and broadly useful. The unification across risk measures is a valuable contribution.

Significance
Potentially significant for risk-sensitive RL and robust control. If made practical (e.g., via variance reduction/model-free sampling), the framework could be adopted broadly. Current empirical evidence is insufficient to demonstrate practical impact.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide dynamic experiments (e.g., gridworld/control tasks) comparing against CVaR-PG, mean-variance PG, robust MDP baselines, and risk-neutral PG.
- Discuss model-free variants: how to approximate ξ*-weighted transitions via importance sampling from the nominal MDP? What bias/variance trade-offs arise?
- Detail computational complexity and scaling; report convex solver times and accuracy effects of inexact KKT multipliers.
- Clarify the handling of non-unique quantiles/saddle points (e.g., CVaR in discrete distributions) and whether gradients are subgradients; discuss smoothing if needed.
- Include examples of dfi/dp and dge/dp for CVaR, spectral risk.

Pros
- General, principled framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients.
- New dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Consistent SAA estimator; recovers/simplifies known special cases.

Cons
- Very limited empirical validation; no dynamic experiments.
- Practical deployment requires convex optimization and simulator access; unclear scalability.
- Technical assumptions may be restrictive; missing variance/sample complexity analysis.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are novel and likely to influence subsequent work; however, the paper would be substantially strengthened by dynamic empirical validation and more discussion of practicality and variance.

Confidence
High in assessment of theory and related work; lower in practical performance due to limited experiments.
