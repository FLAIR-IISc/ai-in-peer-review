

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a significant gap in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (RL) by developing policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk metrics like CVaR or mean-variance. The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to derive gradient formulas for both static and dynamic Markov coherent risk. Their approach generalizes and unifies previous work on risk-sensitive RL, as they explicitly demonstrate that existing methods for specific risk measures (e.g., [8, 30] for CVaR; [18, 29] for mean-variance) are special cases of their framework. This work builds on the theoretical foundations of coherent risk measures from finance [2, 25, 26] and extends the policy gradient theorem [28] to risk-sensitive settings.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL. The two key theorems (4.2 and 5.2) elegantly extend the policy gradient theorem to risk-sensitive settings, which is a substantial advance over prior work that treated each risk measure separately.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The authors carefully establish the connection between coherent risk theory and RL, leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures. The assumptions (particularly Assumption 2.2) are well-motivated and reasonable for the class of risk measures they consider.

3. **Practical Algorithm Design**: The proposed gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) combines sampling with convex programming, offering a practical method for estimating gradients when analytical solutions are unavailable. The consistency result (Proposition 4.4) provides theoretical justification for the approach.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-organized, with clear section headings and logical flow. The examples (Section 4.1-4.2) effectively demonstrate how the general framework recovers and simplifies previous results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity**: The proposed gradient estimation algorithm requires solving a convex program for each sample (Equation 8), which could be computationally prohibitive for large-scale problems. The paper does not adequately address the trade-off between the flexibility of the risk measure and the computational cost. For instance, how does the runtime scale with the number of constraints in the risk envelope? This is a critical practical concern for real-world applications.

2. **Limited Empirical Validation**: The numerical example (Section 6) is somewhat simplistic and doesn't demonstrate the approach on a challenging RL problem where risk-sensitive behavior would be particularly valuable. The comparison between different risk measures is qualitative rather than quantitative. A more thorough empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Assumption Limitations**: The key Assumption 2.2 requires the risk envelope to be known in explicit convex programming form. While the paper justifies this for common risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation), it doesn't address cases where the risk envelope might not be readily available or might require approximation. This limits the practical applicability of the framework.

4. **Dynamic Risk Implementation**: The actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk (Section 5) requires sampling multiple state transitions from each state, which is not feasible in many real-world RL settings where environment interaction is expensive. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but doesn't propose a solution or discuss how to handle this constraint.

5. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: While the paper claims to generalize previous work, it doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods on standard benchmarks. The authors should explicitly compare their approach with methods like [20, 21, 29, 30] in terms of performance and computational efficiency.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the connection to coherent risk theory is well-established. The work has significant potential to influence future research in risk-sensitive RL by providing a more flexible and principled approach to risk modeling.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the computational concerns and providing more substantial empirical validation. The proposed method's practical utility hinges on its computational feasibility, which is not thoroughly examined. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between risk measure flexibility and computational cost.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and novel, and it makes an important step toward unifying risk-sensitive RL. The weaknesses identified are primarily in the practical implementation and empirical validation aspects, which could be addressed with additional experiments and discussion.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a computational complexity analysis of the proposed algorithm
2. Including more substantial empirical results on standard RL benchmarks
3. Discussing the trade-offs between risk measure flexibility and computational cost
4. Providing a more detailed comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods

This paper is a solid contribution to the field that advances our understanding of risk-sensitive RL through a unified theoretical framework. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
