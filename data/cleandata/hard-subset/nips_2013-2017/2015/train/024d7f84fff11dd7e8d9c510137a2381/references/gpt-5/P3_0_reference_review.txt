Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives. It treats both static coherent risk of a random return and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk in MDPs. The key technical contributions are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risk measures using the dual “risk envelope” representation and the envelope theorem; (ii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) estimator that combines sampling with solving a convex program and uses KKT multipliers, with a consistency result; (iii) a policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk that expresses ∇θVθ in terms of an expectation under ξ*-reweighted dynamics and a stage-wise hθ, leading to an actor-critic scheme. Prior work has treated particular risks (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR) or specific dynamics; this paper unifies and generalizes to the full coherent class.

Relation to prior work
- The dual representation of coherent risk (Artzner; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) underpins the approach. The general static gradient subsumes known results for CVaR (e.g., Tamar et al.) and mean-variance/semi-deviation (Rockafellar-Uryasev; Tamar et al., Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh). The dynamic result builds on dynamic coherent risk and robust MDP equivalence (Ruszczyński; Osogami; Chow & Pavone; Petrik & Subramanian). Compared to Borkar’s exponential utility policy gradient and CVaR-specific methods (Chow & Ghavamzadeh; Tamar et al.), the novelty is a general envelope/KKT-based gradient applicable to any coherent risk with an explicit convex risk envelope, and an associated actor-critic for Markov coherent risk.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: a single framework for policy-gradient methods across the entire coherent risk class, bridging static and time-consistent dynamic settings.
- Clean derivation: Theorem 4.2 leverages the envelope theorem and likelihood ratios to yield an implementable gradient that uses saddle-point ξ* and multipliers. Recovering CVaR gradients without extra smoothness assumptions is elegant.
- Generality of SAA estimator: The convex-programming-plus-sampling recipe is broadly applicable; the consistency result is useful and the assumptions are reasonable for common risks (CVaR, spectral, mean-semideviation).
- Dynamic policy-gradient theorem: Extends the classic policy-gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk, clarifying the role of reweighted dynamics and the “risk-sensitive advantage” hθ.
- Connection to robust MDPs for the critic provides a principled path to value function approximation.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Proofs and several key details are deferred to the supplement. The main paper does not provide finite-sample error/variance bounds for the gradient estimators. This matters for risks sensitive to tail events (e.g., CVaR).
- The SAA-based gradient requires solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers at each batch; complexity and numerical stability (especially for large ω-supports) are not discussed. Alternatives (stochastic primal-dual) are not explored.
- Dynamic actor-critic feasibility: sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions requires constructing a modified kernel or implementing nontrivial importance sampling. The paper assumes a simulator that can generate multiple next-state samples per state and implicitly that ξ*-weighted sampling is possible; practical implementation details are sparse.
- Convergence guarantees with function approximation for the critic are not given in the main text; bias introduced by approximating Vθ affects the actor; only a brief mention of gradient error analysis is provided.

Clarity
- Overall clear and well-organized, but some assumptions and requirements deserve more upfront emphasis: finite probability space (with only a brief note on Lp extension), explicit differentiability of risk-envelope constraints w.r.t. p, availability of ∇θ log P(ω) and ∇θ log µθ.
- The dynamic theorem introduces expectations under Pθ(·|x)ξ*; highlighting normalization and conditions under which this defines a valid Markov kernel and retains ergodicity/mixing would aid readers.
- Providing pseudo-code for the static SAA gradient and the actor-critic would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The extension from specific risks to general coherent risks via the envelope/KKT route appears novel and valuable. The dynamic policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, to my knowledge, is new beyond specific utilities/risks.

Significance
- Potentially high impact as a unifying foundation for risk-sensitive RL, especially in domains where different coherent risks are appropriate. However, current empirical support is limited (a small static toy example) and there is no dynamic experiment demonstrating the actor-critic’s practicality, scalability, and performance vs. CVaR/mean-variance baselines.

Specific comments and questions
- How sensitive is the SAA estimator to non-unique saddle points (e.g., CVaR’s non-unique quantile)? Do different KKT selections induce variance/bias?
- For spectral risk measures, can you give the explicit form of fi/ge and their derivatives w.r.t. p to illustrate computational cost?
- Can the per-batch convex program be replaced by an online primal-dual update (stochastic mirror descent) to avoid solving a full program each iteration?
- In the dynamic algorithm, if only a single trajectory is available, can importance sampling correct for ξ*-reweighting? What are the variance implications?
- What conditions ensure that the ξ*-reweighted chain is ergodic and that the likelihood-ratio estimator has bounded variance?
- Please discuss continuous state/action extensions beyond “details omitted,” including function approximation choices and required regularity.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical contribution unifying risk-sensitive policy gradients for coherent risks.
- Recovers and simplifies known special cases; likely to be a useful reference for the community.
- Clear connection to robust control; expands the toolbox for risk-aware RL.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation, especially no dynamic/actor-critic experiments.
- Practicality concerns: requirement to solve convex programs and to sample under ξ*-weighted dynamics.
- Missing finite-sample analysis and variance reduction strategies for tail-sensitive risks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include at least one dynamic MDP experiment (e.g., inventory control, gridworld) comparing against CVaR and mean-variance baselines, with runtime and sample complexity.
- Add pseudo-code and a computational complexity discussion for the SAA and actor-critic steps, and report how KKT multipliers are obtained/stabilized.
- Provide a brief finite-sample error bound or variance discussion; consider control variates/baselines.
- Clarify how to implement ξ*-sampling in practice, or provide an importance-sampling alternative.

Overall recommendation
Promising and original theoretical work with clear potential but currently under-evaluated empirically. Strengthen the empirical section and clarify practical implementation to maximize impact.
