Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the broad class of coherent risk measures. The static part shows that any coherent risk ρ(Z) admits a dual representation as a worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope [2, 26], and derives a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) using the envelope theorem [17] and likelihood-ratio methods. This yields sampling-based estimators for ∇θρ(Z) that apply beyond specific risks like CVaR or mean-variance. Two concrete instances are given: (i) CVaR, recovering and simplifying prior gradient formulas (e.g., [30]) and extending them to discrete settings without additional smoothness; (ii) mean-semideviation, with an explicit estimator (Proposition 4.3). For general coherent risks, the authors propose a sample-average approximation (SAA) that combines sampling with a convex program to compute the saddle point and KKT multipliers, and prove consistency under mild assumptions (Proposition 4.4).

The dynamic part addresses time-consistent risk via Markov coherent risk measures [24]. The value function satisfies a risk-averse Bellman equation equivalent to a robust MDP [19]. The authors derive a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) that expresses ∇θVθ(x) as an expectation over trajectories of a modified Markov chain with transitions reweighted by worst-case densities, multiplied by a shaped stage-wise cost hθ that includes Lagrange multipliers and derivatives of envelope constraints. This extends the classic policy-gradient theorem [28, 14] to Markov coherent risk. An actor-critic algorithm is outlined: a critic estimates Vθ via function approximation leveraging robust MDP techniques [31], while an actor computes ξ∗ and gradient estimates using two-phase sampling. A small bandit-style experiment illustrates the impact of choosing different risk measures relative to mean-risk tradeoffs [29, 21], and highlights coherent measures that target downside risk.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Static case: The gradient formula follows appropriately from the coherent risk dual representation and envelope/Danskin-type results. Assumptions (finite Ω; Slater condition; smoothness of envelope constraints; bounded log-likelihood gradients) are clearly stated and standard in policy-gradient analyses. The SAA-based estimator and the consistency result (Proposition 4.4) are reasonable; the assumptions (compactness of saddle sets, continuity of constraints, uniform convergence of empirical distributions) are interpretable and are satisfied for common coherent risks (CVaR, mean-semideviation, spectral [1, 26]).
- Dynamic case: The Markov coherent Bellman equation is standard [24], and the form of the gradient (Theorem 5.2) is plausible: a REINFORCE-like sum with transitions reweighted by ξ∗ and a stage-wise signal hθ that correctly captures dependence on envelope constraints and the normalizing multiplier. The need to sample from ξ∗-weighted transitions is handled by computing ξ∗ from the critic’s Vθ and then simulating, which is consistent with robust MDP interpretations [19]. However, critical algorithmic and convergence details are deferred to the supplementary. The requirement to estimate and differentiate envelope constraints w.r.t. probabilities (d fi/dp, d ge/dp) is nontrivial but feasible for the listed risks. The actor-critic dependence on a simulator that allows multiple next-state samples per state is a limitation relative to standard on-policy settings.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. Assumptions are explicit; connections to coherent risk duality and robust MDPs are articulated. Notation is heavy but standard. Some parts crucial to reproducibility (full actor-critic algorithm, function approximation details, convergence proof) are in the supplement; brief summaries in the main text could be expanded. The computational footprint of the SAA step (solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers per batch) could be discussed more concretely.

Originality
The work unifies and generalizes policy gradients to the full class of coherent risk measures. The static gradient formula and the dynamic policy gradient theorem appear novel in their generality and derivation. Prior work has treated specific criteria (exponential utility [6], mean-variance [18, 29, 21], CVaR [8, 30]) or specialized dynamics [20, 9]. The extension to Markov coherent risk with an explicit gradient expression and a corresponding actor-critic framework seems new.

Significance
The approach provides a principled way to optimize a wide family of risk measures in RL, facilitating problem-dependent risk design. The dynamic result is especially relevant for time-consistent risk. The main limitations are computational (solving convex programs and simulating ξ∗-weighted transitions) and empirical (only a small illustrative experiment; no large-scale dynamic RL evaluation). Still, the theoretical framework can influence subsequent algorithm design and analysis for risk-sensitive RL and robust control.

Pros
- General, unified gradient formulas for coherent risks (static and dynamic), subsuming prior special cases [30, 29, 21].
- Sound use of convex duality and envelope theorems; clear, checkable assumptions.
- Consistency of the SAA estimator with practical recipe (sampling + convex programming), applicable to common risks (CVaR, spectral, semideviation).
- Dynamic policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk extends classic PG theory [28, 14] and leverages robust MDP connections [19].
- Highlights the importance of selecting appropriate risk measures via an illustrative example.

Cons
- Limited empirical validation; no experiments on dynamic MDPs to demonstrate the actor-critic method, variance, or convergence.
- Computational burden: each gradient estimate requires solving a convex program and obtaining KKT multipliers; scalability and wall-clock cost are not quantified.
- Practical constraints: need to sample multiple next states per state and to simulate ξ∗-weighted dynamics; may be restrictive in typical RL settings with limited simulators or online data.
- Assumptions of finite Ω and smoothness of envelope constraints; extensions to continuous spaces and function approximation are deferred and not demonstrated empirically.
- Some essential algorithmic and proof details relegated to the supplement, reducing self-contained clarity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., gridworlds, control tasks) for dynamic risk, comparing to CVaR/mean-variance baselines and robust MDP planning [31].
- Quantify computational overhead per update (size of convex programs, number of constraints) and runtime scaling with sample size and state-space.
- Analyze sensitivity to errors in Vθ and ξ∗ estimation (bias in the gradient) and explore variance reduction/importance sampling for rare-event risks [3].
- Discuss practical strategies when only single-trajectory data are available (off-policy corrections or importance sampling under ξ∗).
- Clarify which coherent risks lead to particularly simple envelopes and derivatives, enabling efficient implementations.

Overall assessment
The paper presents technically sound and broadly applicable gradient formulas that extend risk-sensitive RL beyond specific criteria, with a novel dynamic policy gradient theorem. The main weaknesses are empirical and practical. With stronger experimental validation and discussion of computational aspects, the contribution would be impactful for risk-sensitive RL.
