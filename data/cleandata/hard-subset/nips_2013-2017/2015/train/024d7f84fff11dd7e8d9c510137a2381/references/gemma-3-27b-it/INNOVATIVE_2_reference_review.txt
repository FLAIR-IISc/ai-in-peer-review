This paper presents a compelling and broadly applicable framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk criteria. The core idea – leveraging the duality representation of coherent risk to derive tractable gradient formulas – is genuinely novel and represents a significant step forward. While the technical details are dense, the authors successfully navigate a complex landscape and provide a unifying perspective on risk-sensitive RL. The generalization of prior work (CVaR, mean-variance) under a single coherent framework is a substantial achievement. The numerical example, though simple, effectively illustrates the practical implications of different risk preferences.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The work introduces a novel and powerful framework for applying policy gradients to a general class of coherent risk measures. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a significant conceptual advance.
*   **Generality:** The approach unifies previous results for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance) and provides a foundation for exploring new risk criteria.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas appears rigorous, building on established results in coherent risk theory and optimization. The connection to robust MDPs is insightful.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed algorithms, while requiring convex optimization as a subroutine, are amenable to sampling-based estimation, making them scalable to larger problems.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of coherent risk measures and the limitations of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Complexity:** The paper is mathematically dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with coherent risk theory to fully grasp. The notation is heavy.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example is illustrative but lacks the scale and complexity needed to convincingly demonstrate the performance of the proposed algorithms in realistic scenarios. More extensive experiments are needed.
*   **Implementation Details:** The actor-critic algorithm described is high-level. Crucial implementation details (function approximation, hyperparameter tuning, convergence criteria) are omitted, making it difficult to assess the practicality of the approach.
*   **Computational Cost:** The reliance on solving convex optimization problems within the policy iteration loop could be computationally expensive for very large-scale problems. Although sampling alleviates this, the trade-off is not fully discussed.
*   **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2) that may not hold in all practical settings. A discussion of the robustness of the approach to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound framework for risk-sensitive RL.
*   The generality of the approach is a major strength, unifying previous work and enabling the exploration of new risk criteria.
*   The potential impact on the field is significant, as it provides a principled way to incorporate risk preferences into reinforcement learning algorithms.
*   The authors address a challenging problem and provide a promising solution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The technical complexity and limited empirical evaluation may hinder the adoption of the proposed algorithms.
*   The computational cost could be a barrier for large-scale applications.
*   The lack of detailed implementation guidelines makes it difficult to reproduce and extend the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and potential significance of the work outweigh the limitations. While further empirical validation and implementation details would be desirable, the core contribution – a unifying framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk – is a substantial advance. The paper tackles a challenging problem with a creative and theoretically sound approach. It is a valuable addition to the NIPS literature and has the potential to inspire future research in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. I particularly appreciate the authors' willingness to take a bold, general approach rather than focusing on incremental improvements. The paper pushes the boundaries of the field and opens up exciting new avenues for exploration.
