- Paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering static and dynamic (time-consistent Markov) cases.  
- Key idea: use dual representation of coherent risks (risk envelope) plus envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio tricks to derive gradient formulas.  
- Static-risk results: general gradient formula (Thm 4.2) expressed via saddle-point solution, yielding ξ*-weighted expectations with KKT corrections.  
- Static-risk contributions: recovers CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases and provides new discrete-case CVaR gradient formulas.  
- Static algorithm: sample-average approximation (SAA) solving a convex program per batch to get ξ*, multipliers, and a consistent gradient estimator.  
- Dynamic-risk results: defines Markov coherent risk (nested static risk) and derives a policy gradient theorem (Thm 5.2) using ξ*-reweighted transitions and stage cost hθ.  
- Dynamic algorithm: actor-critic blueprint using robust-MDP equivalence for the critic and two-phase ξ*-weighted sampling for the actor.  
- Empirical validation is minimal: only a small static portfolio example; no dynamic experiments to validate actor-critic or baselines.  
- Relation to prior work: unifies mean-variance, exponential utility, CVaR, and robust MDP literature; extends dual/envelope techniques to policy-gradient settings.  
- Strength: generality and unification across many coherent risk measures in both static and dynamic settings.  
- Strength: solid theoretical grounding using duality, KKT, envelope theorem, and SAA consistency.  
- Strength: simplifies and recovers known results (e.g., CVaR) and provides actionable sampling recipes for some risks.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation, especially lacking dynamic experiments and comparisons to baselines.  
- Weakness: practical challenges—each gradient requires solving a convex program and KKT multipliers; dynamic method needs ξ*-weighted sampling and multiple next-state samples, implying simulator/model access.  
- Weakness: strong and potentially restrictive assumptions (finite spaces, strict feasibility, bounded likelihood ratios, differentiability of envelope constraints, uniqueness/stability of saddle points).  
- Weakness: limited discussion on variance, sample complexity, and high-variance issues for tail-sensitive criteria; no variance-reduction or finite-sample analysis.  
- Weakness: presentation density and heavy notation; some derivations and full actor-critic details deferred to supplement; need clearer worked dynamic examples.  
- Quality: theoretical contributions appear correct under assumptions; SAA consistency appropriate; lacks complexity and empirical robustness analysis.  
- Originality: high—novel extension of policy gradients to general coherent risks and new dynamic Markov coherent risk gradient theorem.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for risk-sensitive RL and robust control if made practical with variance-reduction or model-free methods.  
- Suggestions: add dynamic experiments comparing relevant baselines, discuss model-free approximations/importance sampling, analyze computational complexity and solver costs, and provide worked examples for hθ and derivatives (dfi/dp, dge/dp).  
- Pros: principled, general framework; novel dynamic gradient theorem; consistent SAA estimator that unifies special cases.  
- Cons: scant empirical validation, scalability/practicality concerns, and restrictive technical assumptions.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — strong theoretical novelty but needs more dynamic experiments and practical/variance analyses to demonstrate impact.  
- Confidence: high in theoretical assessment; lower regarding practical performance due to limited experiments.
