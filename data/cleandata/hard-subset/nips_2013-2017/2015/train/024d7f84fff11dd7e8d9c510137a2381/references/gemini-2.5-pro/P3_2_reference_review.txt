### Summary
This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The authors make two primary contributions. First, for static coherent risk measures (applied to the total discounted cost), they derive a novel and general policy gradient formula. This formula leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures and the envelope theorem, resulting in an estimator that combines sampling with convex programming. This approach unifies and generalizes previous results for specific risk measures like CVaR and Mean-Semideviation. Second, they extend this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. They derive a new policy gradient theorem for this setting, analogous to the standard one for expected returns, and outline a corresponding actor-critic algorithm suitable for large-scale MDPs.

### Relation to Prior Work
The paper is well-situated within the growing literature on risk-sensitive RL. It correctly identifies that prior work has largely focused on developing bespoke algorithms for specific risk measures, such as mean-variance, exponential utility, or CVaR. The key novelty here is the use of the axiomatic framework of coherent risk measures (Artzner et al., 1999) and their dual representation (Ruszczynski & Shapiro, 2006) to develop a single, principled method that applies to the entire class. The authors adeptly distinguish their work from the stochastic programming literature (e.g., Ruszczynski & Shapiro) by highlighting that, in RL, the policy parameters `θ` influence the underlying probability distribution, which their method handles via the likelihood-ratio trick. For the dynamic case, the work builds upon the theory of Markov coherent risk measures (Ruszczyński, 2010) and its connection to robust MDPs (Osogami, 2012), but shifts the focus from dynamic programming or value-based solutions to policy gradient methods, which are often more suitable for continuous or large state spaces.

### Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)
1.  **Generality and Unification:** The main strength of this paper is its theoretical elegance and generality. By tackling the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a unifying framework that subsumes and simplifies several previous, more specific results. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could guide future research in risk-sensitive RL.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the static risk gradient in Theorem 4.2 is novel and insightful. Using the envelope theorem on the dual formulation is a powerful technique that cleanly separates the optimization over the risk envelope from the gradient calculation with respect to the policy parameters. The re-derivation of the CVaR policy gradient is a compelling demonstration of the method's power and simplicity compared to prior proofs.

3.  **Principled Extension to Dynamic Risk:** The development of a policy gradient theorem for time-consistent Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) is a crucial step towards making these theoretically appealing risk measures practical in an RL context. The formulation connects neatly to the standard actor-critic framework and correctly identifies the need for a modified value function and a distorted state distribution for the gradient update.

4.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear introduction to coherent and dynamic risk measures, making the work accessible. The numerical example in Section 6, while simple, is very effective at illustrating why flexibility in choosing a risk measure is important, particularly highlighting the potential pitfalls of standard deviation in the presence of heavy-tailed distributions with positive skew.

### Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)
1.  **Practicality and Scalability of the General Algorithm:** The proposed general gradient estimator (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem over `N` samples at each step of the SGD update. While convex, the computational cost of this subproblem could be a significant bottleneck, especially if a large `N` is needed for low-variance gradient estimates. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the computational complexity and potential limitations of this inner loop.

2.  **Lack of Details on the Actor-Critic Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is a major contribution, but its details and analysis are deferred to the supplementary material. The main paper provides only a high-level sketch. Critical challenges, such as how to efficiently sample from the `ξ*`-weighted MDP or the specifics of the two-phase sampling procedure for `hθ(x, a)`, are not discussed. This makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess the feasibility and complexity of the proposed dynamic risk algorithm.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The single numerical experiment is a stateless bandit problem. While it serves its motivational purpose well, it does not validate the core algorithmic contributions, especially the actor-critic algorithm for sequential decision-making. Testing the method on a standard RL benchmark (e.g., a gridworld, inventory management problem, or a simple continuous control task) would provide much stronger evidence of its practical utility and performance compared to existing risk-sensitive methods.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. Its primary value lies in providing a unified and principled policy gradient framework for the broad and axiomatically sound class of coherent risk measures. The technical derivations are novel and elegant. While the paper's main weakness is the lack of substantial empirical validation and the relegation of key algorithmic details to the appendix, the quality and importance of the theoretical results are high enough to warrant acceptance. The work is likely to inspire further research into general risk-sensitive algorithms and the practical application of coherent risk in RL.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
