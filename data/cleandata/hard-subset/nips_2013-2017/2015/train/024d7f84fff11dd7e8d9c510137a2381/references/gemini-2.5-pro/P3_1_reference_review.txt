### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning for objectives based on coherent risk measures. It addresses both static risk of a total discounted cost and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The core contribution is a set of novel policy gradient theorems that unify and generalize previous results for specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. For static risk, the authors derive a gradient formula by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures and the envelope theorem. They propose a practical algorithm that estimates this gradient by solving a sample-average approximation (SAA) of a convex program. For dynamic risk, they derive a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem, analogous to the standard one, which relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. This leads to a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The work is well-situated within the growing literature on risk-sensitive RL, connecting it to the foundational theory of coherent risk from finance and operations research.

### Strengths
1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant generalization of prior work. Instead of developing bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), the authors provide a single, principled framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a major conceptual advance, moving the field towards a more systematic treatment of risk. The ability to re-derive and simplify existing results (like for CVaR) within their framework is a powerful demonstration of its utility.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the static policy gradient in Theorem 4.2 is novel and elegant. The combination of the dual representation of coherent risk (Theorem 2.1), the envelope theorem for constrained optimization, and the standard likelihood-ratio trick is a powerful technique. This provides a clean, interpretable formula for the gradient in terms of the optimal worst-case density `ξ*` and the Lagrangian multipliers.

3.  **Practical Algorithm for General Static Risk:** The SAA-based gradient estimation algorithm in Section 4.3 is a key practical contribution. It provides a concrete, implementable recipe for any coherent risk measure whose risk envelope can be described by a convex program: (1) draw samples, (2) solve the empirical convex optimization problem to find the worst-case density `ξ*_N` and KKT multipliers `λ*_N`, and (3) plug these into the gradient formula. The provided consistency result (Proposition 4.4) gives this practical method theoretical grounding.

4.  **Extension to Dynamic Risk:** The paper successfully extends its ideas to the more complex setting of dynamic, time-consistent risk. Theorem 5.2 presents a novel policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures. This result is a non-trivial generalization of the standard policy gradient theorem and correctly identifies the need to average over trajectories generated by a `ξ*`-distorted transition model. This properly connects the gradient to the underlying structure of the risk-sensitive Bellman equation.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration in Section 6 is more of a conceptual motivation for using different risk measures than a validation of the proposed algorithms. It convincingly argues why flexibility in choosing a risk measure is important (e.g., standard deviation's issue with heavy-tailed upside) but does not evaluate the performance of the proposed gradient estimators. There are no experiments assessing the convergence, sample efficiency, or variance of the general SAA estimator or the actor-critic algorithm. A stronger empirical section would have compared the proposed general algorithm (instantiated for CVaR) against a specialized CVaR algorithm (e.g., [8, 30]) on a standard RL benchmark to demonstrate its practical viability.

2.  **Practicality of the Dynamic Actor-Critic Algorithm:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case appears computationally demanding. The actor's gradient estimate requires sampling from a modified MDP with `ξ*`-weighted transitions, where `ξ*` itself depends on the critic's value function estimate `Vθ`. Furthermore, the term `hθ(x, a)` involves an inner expectation, which the authors suggest handling with a "two-phase" sampling procedure. This nested sampling structure could lead to high variance and poor sample complexity. The paper acknowledges the need for a simulator, which limits its applicability compared to single-trajectory on-policy methods. More discussion on the computational and sample complexity challenges would be welcome.

3.  **Completeness of the Main Paper:** Due to space limitations, key details, including most proofs and the full specification and analysis of the actor-critic algorithm, are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is common, it makes the dynamic risk portion of the paper in Section 5 feel somewhat incomplete and programmatic, leaving the reader to trust that the significant challenges of function approximation and convergence are adequately handled in the supplement.

### Detailed Comments and Questions
*   **Quality:** The theoretical development is of high quality. The claims are well-supported by mathematical derivations that build on solid foundations in convex analysis and RL theory. The connection between coherent risk and robust MDPs is correctly identified and leveraged.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It logically progresses from the static to the dynamic case, using clear examples to build intuition. The mathematical notation is consistent and precise.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While risk-sensitive PG methods exist, this is the first to provide a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The technical approach of combining the dual representation with the envelope theorem is novel in this context.
*   **Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant. It provides researchers and practitioners with a powerful and flexible toolkit for risk-sensitive RL. By abstracting away the specifics of the risk measure, it allows for principled exploration of different risk preferences, as motivated by the conclusion and the numerical example. This work is likely to be built upon and could influence how risk is handled in RL applications.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:
**Pro:**
*   Provides a highly significant theoretical unification of risk-sensitive policy gradient methods.
*   The technical contributions (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) are novel, elegant, and important.
*   The SAA-based algorithm for static risk is a practical and well-justified contribution.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and connects several fields (RL, optimization, finance) in a compelling way.

**Con:**
*   The empirical evaluation is weak and does not validate the proposed algorithms' performance.
*   The proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk seems complex and potentially sample-inefficient, with its practicality not being demonstrated.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

The theoretical contribution of this paper is strong, novel, and significant enough to warrant acceptance despite the limited empirical validation. The unification of policy gradient methods under the coherent risk framework is a major step forward for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The weaknesses, particularly regarding the empirical results, can be addressed in future work, and the current paper provides a solid and inspiring foundation for it.
