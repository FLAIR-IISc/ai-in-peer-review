- Proposes a unified policy-gradient framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures in RL, not just specific risks like CVaR.  
- Derives gradient formulas using the representation theorem that views coherent risk as a worst-case expectation over a risk envelope (Theorem 2.1).  
- Presents two algorithms: a sampling + convex programming method for static coherent risk and an extended policy-gradient/actor-critic approach for dynamic Markov coherent risk.  
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous, with detailed proofs (Theorems 4.2, 5.2) and consistency results (Proposition 4.4).  
- Connects the risk envelope to a practical gradient estimation procedure in an elegant, general way.  
- Practical algorithm design is reasonable: a two-step sampling then convex optimization procedure with a numerical portfolio example.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and balances theory with motivating examples (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation).  
- Significant contribution to risk-sensitive RL by generalizing previous work limited to specific risk measures.  
- Experimental validation is limited to a single numerical example and lacks broad empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmarks.  
- The computational cost—solving a convex program per gradient estimate—is not sufficiently analyzed and may be prohibitive for large-scale MDPs.  
- Lacks comprehensive empirical or empirical-methodological comparison with existing state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL approaches.  
- Some technical assumptions (risk envelope structure, smoothness) may not hold broadly and need fuller discussion.  
- Dynamic risk algorithm requires a simulator for multiple state-transition sampling; implementation details for simulator-free environments are not addressed.  
- Overall quality: technically sound, complete contribution, and honest about limitations.  
- Clarity: mathematical notation and exposition are consistent and reproducible; supplementary material contains proofs.  
- Originality: novel use of the coherent risk representation and convex optimization for gradient estimation; fills a gap beyond prior work.  
- Significance: highly significant theoretical advance with potential broad adoption, though stronger experiments would boost impact.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Suggested improvements: expand experiments on standard benchmarks, analyze computational complexity and approximations, provide thorough comparisons to related methods, discuss extensions to model uncertainty, and clarify dynamic implementation without simulators.
