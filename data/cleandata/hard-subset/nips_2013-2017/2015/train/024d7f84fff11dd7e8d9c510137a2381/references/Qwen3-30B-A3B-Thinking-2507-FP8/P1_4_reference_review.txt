

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary
This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (RL) by developing policy gradient algorithms for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance as previous work has done. The authors provide a unified framework that generalizes and simplifies prior approaches. They establish two key theoretical contributions: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk suitable for sampling-based estimation, and (2) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures that extends the classical policy gradient theorem to dynamic risk settings. The paper connects to important prior work on coherent risk measures (Artzner et al., 1999) and risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [18, 21, 29, 30]), while addressing limitations in previous approaches that were restricted to specific risk measures or system dynamics.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors establish a solid theoretical foundation by leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) and applying the envelope theorem to derive their gradient formulas. The proofs of the main theorems (4.2 and 5.2) are well-structured and supported by supplementary materials. The consistency result (Proposition 4.4) provides important theoretical guarantees for their gradient estimator. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of choosing appropriate risk measures for different problem characteristics. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the need for a simulator for sampling multiple transitions) and provides clear assumptions that are well-justified.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the authors carefully define all key concepts before using them. The connection between the general framework and specific examples (CVaR, mean-semideviation) is particularly helpful for readers. The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper would be even clearer if it included a high-level diagram illustrating the relationship between static and dynamic risk measures and their respective gradient formulas, but the current presentation is already quite accessible to experts in the field.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by unifying the treatment of risk-sensitive RL across the entire class of coherent risk measures. Previous work has focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR [8, 30], mean-variance [18, 21], exponential utility [6]), but this paper provides a general framework that encompasses all these approaches as special cases. The authors demonstrate this by showing how their gradient formulas recover known results for specific risk measures. This unification is novel and represents a meaningful advancement over prior work, which was limited to specific risk measures. The paper also extends the policy gradient theorem to dynamic coherent risk measures, which is a non-trivial contribution.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Risk-sensitive RL is increasingly important in applications where managing cost variability is as important as minimizing expected cost (e.g., finance, operations research). The authors' framework provides practitioners with flexibility to choose risk measures that best match their specific problem characteristics and risk preferences. The numerical example in Section 6 powerfully demonstrates how different risk measures can lead to dramatically different policy choices (e.g., selecting a heavy-tailed asset vs. a high-mean asset), which is a crucial insight for real-world applications. The paper also opens up new possibilities for connecting risk management with model uncertainty, as noted in the conclusion.

## Strengths
- Unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures
- Rigorous theoretical development with well-supported claims
- Practical numerical demonstration of the framework's utility
- Clear connections to prior work and demonstration of how previous results are special cases
- Provides a principled approach to selecting risk measures based on problem characteristics

## Weaknesses
- The algorithmic details for the actor-critic approach (Section 5) are somewhat brief due to space constraints, though the supplementary material appears to address this
- The paper assumes access to a simulator for sampling multiple state transitions from each state, which may limit applicability in some real-world settings where only single trajectories are available
- The empirical comparison between analytical-solution-based and SAA estimators (mentioned in Section 4.3) would benefit from more detailed results to help practitioners choose between methods

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by providing a unified framework for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific measures. The theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated, and the numerical example demonstrates practical significance. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Provides a unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL, generalizing previous work
- Theoretical results are rigorous with clear assumptions and proofs
- Demonstrates practical utility through a compelling numerical example
- Connects risk management to broader concepts like model uncertainty

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Space limitations prevent full algorithmic details for the actor-critic approach
- Requires access to a simulator for multiple state transitions (may limit some applications)

Overall, this paper is of high quality and makes a significant contribution to the field. It is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the theoretical foundations of reinforcement learning with clear practical implications. I recommend acceptance.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
