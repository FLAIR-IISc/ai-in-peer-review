Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies risk-sensitive reinforcement learning for the broad class of coherent risk measures. Using the dual representation of coherent risk (risk envelope), the authors derive (i) a general policy-gradient formula for static coherent risk measures where the return distribution depends on policy parameters, (ii) a sample-average approximation (SAA) based estimator that combines sampling with a small convex program and proves consistency, and (iii) a policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk in MDPs, leading to an actor–critic style algorithm. The static results unify and generalize prior work on specific risks (e.g., CVaR, mean–variance/semideviation) by giving a single gradient expression via the envelope theorem, and they recover known special cases (e.g., the CVaR gradient) with a simpler derivation that also handles discrete distributions. For dynamic risk, the paper builds on the equivalence between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs to derive a Bellman equation and a policy-gradient theorem with a ξ-weighted transition kernel. The empirical illustration is a simple bandit-like investment example showing how different risk measures induce different policies.

The paper relates to and extends prior work: risk-sensitive RL for exponential utilities, mean–variance/variance-related criteria, CVaR optimization via sampling, and robust/Markov coherent risk in MDPs (Ruszczyński; Petrik and Subramanian; Tamar et al.; Prashanth and Ghavamzadeh). The key novelty is the general coherent-risk PG framework (both static and dynamic) with a sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator, rather than focusing on a single risk.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The static gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) grounded in the risk-envelope representation and envelope theorem is well-motivated and aligns with standard likelihood-ratio techniques. The specialization to CVaR and mean-semideviation is correct and insightful. The SAA estimator and consistency result (Proposition 4.4) rely on reasonable compactness and smoothness assumptions for finite probability spaces and known risk envelopes; this matches the stochastic programming literature.
- For dynamic risk, the Bellman equation and the resulting policy-gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) appear technically sound and represent a meaningful extension of the policy gradient theorem to time-consistent coherent risk. The reliance on ξ-weighted transitions is natural from the dual viewpoint.
- However, key algorithmic and convergence details of the actor–critic method are deferred to the supplement; the main text lacks a clear, self-contained algorithm and rigorous convergence guarantees under function approximation. Moreover, the need to sample from or emulate ξ-weighted transitions is a nontrivial practical requirement; the proposed two-phase sampling requires multiple next-state samples at each state and a simulator, which limits applicability.
- Empirical validation is very limited (a toy bandit). There is no quantitative evaluation on MDP benchmarks, no comparison of estimator variance, and no dynamic-risk experiments to demonstrate the actor–critic approach.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear statement of assumptions (finite spaces; smooth envelopes; bounded likelihood ratios). The derivation of the static gradient and its examples are easy to follow.
- Some notational density may hinder accessibility (e.g., dfi/dp(x′) in Theorem 5.2, the use of ξPθ as densities, and multiple Lagrange multipliers per state). It would help to provide intuitive explanations for the terms in hθ(x, a), a brief complexity discussion for the convex subproblems, and explicit pseudocode for the SAA estimator and the actor–critic method in the main text.
- Several footnote artifacts and minor typos are present; cleaning would improve clarity.

Originality
- The unifying static gradient formula for general coherent risk measures, the SAA+convex program estimator with consistency, and the dynamic risk policy-gradient theorem are, to my knowledge, novel in the RL literature and go beyond prior works that focused on specific risk metrics. The derivation of the CVaR gradient as a special case without additional smoothness assumptions is a nice contribution. The dynamic result extends the PG theorem to Markov coherent risk in a principled way.

Significance
- If adopted, the framework could become a reference approach for risk-sensitive PG methods across a wide class of coherent risks, enabling principled tailoring of risk envelopes. The dynamic PG theorem is conceptually important.
- Practical impact is tempered by (i) the need to know and differentiate the risk-envelope constraints with respect to the transition probability vector, (ii) the requirement for a simulator that yields multiple next-state samples per state, and (iii) the lack of empirical evidence on nontrivial MDPs. Without experiments demonstrating stability, variance, and performance compared to specialized methods (e.g., CVaR PG, robust MDP planning with function approximation), it is hard to assess the approach’s effectiveness.

Pros
- Clear, principled unification of policy-gradient methods for coherent risks via duality and envelope theorem.
- General SAA-based estimator with consistency; recovers and simplifies known special cases (CVaR).
- New policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk; connection to robust MDPs.
- Good coverage of related work and positioning.

Cons
- Sparse empirical evaluation; no dynamic-risk experiments or comparisons on standard RL tasks.
- Practical limitations: need to compute Lagrange multipliers and gradients of envelope constraints; need to sample under ξ-weighted transitions or draw multiple next states per visited state.
- Algorithmic details and convergence for the actor–critic method under function approximation are deferred; no finite-sample analysis or variance characterization.
- Assumption of finite spaces and smooth envelopes may narrow immediate applicability; extensions to continuous control are not demonstrated.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Include at least one dynamic MDP experiment (e.g., gridworld/queueing/inventory) comparing your actor–critic to CVaR PG, robust MDP baselines, and variance-related criteria; report performance, variance, and computational cost.
- Provide pseudocode for the SAA gradient estimator and the actor–critic loop in the main text.
- Discuss computational complexity: per-iteration cost of the convex program (variables/constraints scale), and practical ways to obtain Lagrange multipliers from solvers.
- Elaborate on how to sample under ξ-weighted transitions in practice. If only a standard simulator is available, can importance sampling or doubly-robust estimators replace the two-phase sampling? Provide guidance or bounds on bias/variance.
- Clarify how the derivatives dfi/dp(x′) and dge/dp(x′) are computed for common envelopes beyond CVaR (e.g., spectral risk), with concrete formulas.
- If possible, provide finite-sample error bounds for the SAA gradient and variance reduction strategies (importance sampling, control variates).

Recommendation
Borderline. The paper offers solid and novel theory that unifies risk-sensitive PG for coherent risks and extends to dynamic Markov coherent risk, but the experimental validation and practical algorithmic details are insufficient for NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the actor–critic implementation would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
