- Presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in RL using coherent risk measures, including static and time-consistent Markov formulations.
- Main contributions: gradient formula for static coherent risk, sampling+convex-programming algorithm for static risk, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Generalizes prior work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance) to the full class of coherent risk measures and connects to robust MDPs.
- Theoretical contribution: novel, general gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) that subsume previous results.
- Technical soundness: rigorous proofs and well-justified assumptions, leveraging the representation theorem effectively.
- Broad applicability: framework covers the entire class of coherent risk measures, not just a few special cases.
- Practical relevance: numerical example shows different risk measures yield different policy choices, illustrating practical importance.
- Clear presentation: well-organized paper with precise definitions, consistent notation, and helpful examples.
- Limited experimental validation: only a single trading example with three assets; lacks tests on standard RL benchmarks.
- Missing computational complexity analysis and scalability discussion for the proposed sampling + convex programming approach.
- Potentially restrictive assumptions: Assumption 2.2 (canonical convex programming form of the risk envelope) may not hold for all practical risk measures.
- Lacks quantitative comparisons with existing risk-sensitive RL methods on performance, convergence, or computation.
- Insufficient discussion of limitations, especially for non-coherent risk measures or envelopes that violate assumptions.
- Quality: technically sound and complete work, though some proofs are relegated to supplementary material.
- Clarity: generally clear but would benefit from more detail on computational aspects and experiments.
- Originality: novel theoretical framework and gradient formulas; new perspective linking coherent risk and robust MDPs.
- Significance: important advance for risk-sensitive RL with wide applicability, but impact would increase with broader empirical validation.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical novelty, generality, rigorous results, and insightful connection to robust MDPs.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, missing complexity analysis, restrictive assumptions, and lack of quantitative baselines.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions due to strong theoretical contribution.
- Suggested improvements: expand experiments to standard RL benchmarks (MuJoCo, Atari), add computational complexity analysis, and compare quantitatively with existing methods.
- Suggested improvements: discuss and mitigate limitations of Assumption 2.2 and consider extensions for non-canonical risk envelopes.
- Suggested improvements: clarify and expand the connection to robust MDPs to draw out practical implications.
