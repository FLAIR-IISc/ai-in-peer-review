## NIPS Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable and technically sound contribution to the growing field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It successfully generalizes existing approaches by providing a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, addressing both static and dynamic risk settings. The theoretical results are strong, and the connections to existing literature (robust MDPs, stochastic programming) are well-articulated. While the numerical example is limited, it effectively illustrates the potential for nuanced risk preference modeling. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, primarily focused on clarity and expanding the experimental validation.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of optimizing policies in MDPs under coherent risk measures, a crucial extension of standard expected-cost RL.  Instead of focusing on a specific risk measure like CVaR or mean-variance, the authors cleverly leverage the unifying theory of coherent risk, providing a general framework. Their key contributions are: a novel gradient formula for static coherent risk suited for sampling; a sampling-based algorithm with consistency guarantees for static risk; and a policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk coupled with an actor-critic algorithm. This builds upon and generalizes numerous recent works.  The paper correctly positions itself relative to existing risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [6, 18, 29, 30]) and robust MDPs [19], highlighting the advantages of their sampling-based approach for scalability and flexibility.  The connection to stochastic programming [25, 26] is also clearly drawn, noting its limitations in the RL context.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Generality & Unification:** The coherent risk framework is a significant strength.  It allows the authors to encompass a wide range of risk preferences within a single theoretical and algorithmic structure.  This is far more elegant and extensible than approaches tailored to specific risk measures.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) is mathematically sound and leverages important results like the Envelope Theorem.  The consistency results (Proposition 4.4) are particularly valuable, providing justification for the sampling-based approach.
* **Sampling-Based Approach:** The proposed algorithms are well-suited for large-scale MDPs where exact dynamic programming is intractable. The use of SAA for static risk estimation is a practical and efficient approach.
* **Dynamic Risk Treatment:** The extension to dynamic Markov coherent risk is a significant contribution, addressing the critical issue of time consistency in multi-stage decision problems.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the related literature and clearly articulate how their work builds upon and advances the state of the art.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Numerical Evaluation:**  The numerical example (Section 6) is somewhat limited in scope.  While it demonstrates the qualitative impact of different risk measures, it lacks a comprehensive comparison against baseline algorithms (e.g., standard policy gradients, CVaR-based methods) on more challenging benchmark tasks. The figure is very small.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The proposed algorithms, particularly the actor-critic scheme for dynamic risk, involve multiple steps (convex programming, sampling, function approximation).  Implementing and tuning these algorithms could be challenging in practice.  More details on implementation choices and computational complexity would be helpful.
* **Assumption Dependency:** The reliance on Assumption 2.2 (the general form of the risk envelope) could be a limitation. While stated as general, the practical applicability depends on whether suitable convex programming formulations exist for a given coherent risk measure.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, some sections (particularly the discussion of Lagrangian multipliers) could benefit from more explicit explanations of the notation and concepts.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically strong, with rigorous derivations and clear justifications for the proposed methods. The consistency results add significant value.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally clear and well-organized, but some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples. The notation is sometimes dense.
* **Originality (8/10):** The unification of policy gradient methods under a coherent risk framework is a novel and significant contribution. While building upon existing concepts, the authors provide a substantial generalization and practical algorithmic framework.
* **Significance (8/10):** This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of risk-sensitive RL. The generality and scalability of the proposed methods make them applicable to a wide range of real-world problems where managing risk is crucial.

**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a theoretically sound and general framework for risk-sensitive policy gradients.
* Addresses both static and dynamic risk settings with novel algorithms.
* Offers a practical sampling-based approach suitable for large-scale problems.
* Fills a gap in the literature by unifying various risk-sensitive RL methods.

**Con:**

* Limited numerical evaluation.
* Implementation complexity could be a barrier to adoption.
* Dependence on the assumption regarding the risk envelope.




**6. Recommendations:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions:

* **Expand Numerical Validation:** Include a more comprehensive experimental evaluation on standard RL benchmarks, comparing the performance of the proposed algorithms against baseline methods (e.g., standard policy gradients, CVaR-based RL) across a range of risk preferences.
* **Address Implementation Complexity:** Provide more details on implementation choices, computational complexity, and potential challenges associated with tuning the algorithms.
* **Clarify Notation:** Review sections with dense notation (e.g., Lagrangian multipliers) and provide more detailed explanations and examples.
* **Discuss Practical Considerations:**  Expand the discussion on the practical applicability of Assumption 2.2 and potential strategies for handling cases where suitable convex programming formulations are unavailable.



This paper represents a significant advancement in the field of risk-sensitive RL. The authors have made a valuable contribution by providing a unified and scalable framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. Addressing the minor revisions outlined above will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
