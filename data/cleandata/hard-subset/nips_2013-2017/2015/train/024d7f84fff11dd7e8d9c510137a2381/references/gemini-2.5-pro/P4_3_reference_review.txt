This paper proposes a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The work generalizes and unifies previous approaches that have developed algorithms for specific risk measures, such as mean-variance or Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The authors address two settings: optimizing a static coherent risk of a total discounted return, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

The core technical contribution for the static case is a new formula for the policy gradient of any coherent risk measure (Theorem 4.2). This is derived by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk, which expresses the risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the "risk envelope"). By applying the envelope theorem to this dual formulation, the authors obtain a gradient expression that depends on the solution (both primal and dual variables) of the corresponding convex optimization problem. Based on this formula, they propose a general gradient estimation algorithm that uses Sample Average Approximation (SAA) to solve for the worst-case density and its associated Lagrange multipliers from sampled data, and then plugs these estimates into the gradient formula. For the dynamic case, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2). This theorem expresses the gradient of the risk-sensitive value function in a form analogous to the standard policy gradient theorem, involving a sum of discounted "risk-sensitive" advantage-like terms. This result forms the basis for a proposed actor-critic algorithm.

This work builds upon a rich literature in both risk-sensitive RL and stochastic programming. While previous NIPS papers have addressed policy gradients for specific risk criteria (e.g., [21, 29] for variance, [8, 30] for CVaR), this paper is the first to provide a unified treatment for the entire class of coherent risk measures defined by Artzner et al. [2]. The methodology relies heavily on the duality theory for coherent risk developed in stochastic programming [25, 26] and connects to the literature on robust MDPs [19, 24] for the dynamic case.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and unifying theoretical framework. By providing policy gradient formulas for the entire class of coherent risk measures, the work significantly advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL. It moves the field from a collection of special-case algorithms to a general, principled approach. This framework provides practitioners with the flexibility to select or design problem-specific risk measures that satisfy desirable axioms (coherence, time-consistency), as effectively argued in the numerical illustration.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical derivations are elegant and appear sound. The application of the envelope theorem to the dual representation of coherent risk is a powerful and insightful technique that yields a clean gradient formula (Theorem 4.2). This approach allows the authors to re-derive and generalize existing results, such as the gradient for CVaR, with a simpler proof. The policy gradient theorem for dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2) is a non-trivial and important extension of the classical result to the risk-sensitive setting.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It provides a good introduction to coherent risk measures and motivates their use in RL. The progression from the static case to the more complex dynamic case is logical and helps the reader build understanding. The examples of CVaR and mean-semideviation are well-chosen to demonstrate the practical utility of the general static gradient formula.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Algorithmic Details and Analysis:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case is overly brief. The paper states that the critic modifies an algorithm from [31] and the actor uses a "two-phase sampling procedure," but provides insufficient detail for a reader to understand the implementation, complexity, or potential challenges. While space is limited, key aspects of the algorithm, such as how the ξ-weighted transitions are sampled and how the function `hθ(x, a)` is estimated, should be elaborated in the main text. The full convergence analysis is deferred to supplementary material, making it difficult to assess the completeness of this part of the work from the main paper alone.

2.  **Experimental Validation:** The numerical illustration is effective for demonstrating the value of flexible risk measures but is too simple to validate the proposed algorithms' performance and scalability. The experiment is essentially a stateless bandit problem, which does not test the dynamic risk formulation or the proposed actor-critic algorithm. An evaluation on a standard RL benchmark (even a simple one like CartPole or a small gridworld) would significantly strengthen the paper by demonstrating the algorithm's practical viability in a sequential decision-making problem.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed methods. The general algorithm for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem (7) at each gradient estimation step. The size of this problem scales with the number of samples `N`. For the dynamic algorithm, the nested sampling procedure suggested for the actor could also be computationally intensive. A discussion of these costs and potential mitigation strategies would be valuable.

### Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly original and significant theoretical framework that unifies policy gradient methods for a broad and important class of risk measures.
*   The technical contributions, including the general gradient formulas for static and dynamic coherent risk, are novel and sound.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a clear motivation for a more general approach to risk-sensitive RL.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The practical algorithm for the dynamic setting is underdeveloped in the main paper, with critical details and analysis relegated to the appendix.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed algorithms for sequential decision-making problems.
*   The computational cost of the proposed methods is not analyzed.

Overall, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The weaknesses are primarily in the description of the practical algorithm and the limited empirical validation. However, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results are substantial enough to merit publication at NIPS. The proposed framework is likely to inspire future research in this area.
