

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary
This paper proposes policy gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, unifying previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance). The authors derive gradient formulas for both static coherent risk and dynamic Markov coherent risk using the representation theorem for coherent risk measures. They develop sampling-based algorithms that combine convex programming with likelihood-ratio estimation, extending risk-sensitive RL to the entire class of coherent risk measures.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, generalizing previous work on specific risk measures. The theoretical development is rigorous, with clear connections to the representation theorem for coherent risk measures. The numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical importance of flexibility in risk measure selection.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Formatting Issues
1. **Missing reference [13]**: The paper cites "Example 2.1 in [13]" but [13] is not present in the reference list. This is a serious formatting error that undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Inconsistent notation and definitions**: 
   - The paper states "B :={ ξ : ∫ ω∈Ω ξ(ω) = 1, ξ ≥ 0 }" but this definition is incorrect. B should be defined as the set of probability distributions, not density functions. The correct definition should specify that ξ is a density function such that ∫ ξ(ω)Pθ(ω) dω = 1.
   - In Assumption 2.2, "fi(ξ, p) and ge(ξ, p) are twice differentiable in p" is confusing. p is a probability distribution, and differentiating with respect to a distribution is not standard. The paper should clarify what this means.

3. **Ambiguous notation and definitions**:
   - The paper claims "the risk envelope U(Pθ) in Eq. 1 also depends on the state x" but fails to explain how this dependence manifests in the equations.
   - In Theorem 5.2, "Eξ∗θ [·] denotes the expectation w.r.t. trajectories generated by the Markov chain with transition probabilities Pθ(·|x)ξ∗θ,x(·)" is problematic because ξ∗θ,x is a density function, not a transition probability. This needs clarification.

4. **Inconsistent citations**:
   - The paper cites [19] as showing "an MDP with a dynamic coherent risk objective is essentially a robust MDP" but [19] in the references is "Robustness and risk-sensitivity in Markov decision processes, T. Osogami, NIPS, ," which is a different paper from the one cited in the text.
   - The citation for [24] is inconsistent with the reference list. The paper cites "Markov coherent risk" as [24], but [24] in the references is "Risk-averse dynamic programming for Markov decision processes, A. Ruszczyński, Mathematical Programming, 125(2):235–261, ," which is a different paper.

5. **Minor technical errors**:
   - In Section 4.1, the paper states "ξ∗θ (ω) = α −1 when Z(ω) > λ∗,Pθ" but the correct condition should be Z(ω) > λ∗,Pθ (not Z(ω) > λ∗,Pθ).
   - In Section 6, the Pareto distribution is defined as f(z) = αzα+1 ∀z > 1 with α = 1.5. This is incorrect; the standard Pareto density is f(z) = αzα+1 for z > 1, but the mean is α/(α-1) for α > 1, not αzα+1.

### Methodological Concerns
- The paper claims "the class of optimal policies is stationary Markov [24]" for Markov coherent risk, but then states "Our results can be extended to history-dependent policies or stationary Markov policies on a state space augmented with accumulated cost." This is contradictory since stationary Markov policies are already assumed to be optimal for Markov coherent risk.

- In Section 4.3, Proposition 4.4 states "the set SN is non-empty and SN ⊂ C w.p. 1" but SN is never defined. This is a minor but important inconsistency.

- The paper mentions "a standard 'likelihood-ratio' trick" in the proof of Theorem 4.2 but doesn't provide details. While this is common in policy gradient literature, the paper should at least reference the standard technique.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures, with clear connections to the literature. However, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and ambiguous notations significantly undermine the paper's credibility and clarity. These issues are not merely cosmetic but affect the paper's technical accuracy and reproducibility.

While the main contribution is significant and the paper is well-motivated, the number and severity of the technical inconsistencies (particularly the missing reference [13], inconsistent citations, and ambiguous notation) are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper would benefit from careful revision to address these issues before consideration for publication at NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper's technical content is promising, but the numerous minor flaws in the presentation and methodology make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The paper requires substantial revision to correct these issues before it can be considered for publication at NIPS.
