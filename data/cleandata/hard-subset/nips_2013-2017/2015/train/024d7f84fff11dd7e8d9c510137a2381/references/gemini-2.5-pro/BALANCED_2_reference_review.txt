This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. It generalizes and unifies a significant body of prior work that has focused on specific risk measures such as mean-variance, CVaR, or exponential utility. The core contribution is leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the risk envelope).

The authors make three main contributions. First, for the static risk setting (e.g., risk of the total discounted return), they derive a novel policy gradient formula using the Envelope Theorem on the dual formulation. This formula elegantly connects the gradient to the solution of the dual problem (the worst-case density and the Lagrange multipliers). Second, they propose a general gradient estimation algorithm for cases where the dual solution cannot be found analytically. This algorithm involves solving a sample average approximation (SAA) of the dual problem via convex programming. A consistency result for this estimator is provided. Third, for the dynamic, time-consistent setting, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures. This theorem is analogous to the standard policy gradient theorem but incorporates terms related to the worst-case density from the risk-sensitive Bellman equation. This leads to the proposal of a new actor-critic algorithm. The authors demonstrate their framework by re-deriving the known gradient for CVaR with a simpler proof and deriving a new gradient formula for the mean-semideviation risk measure.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Generalization and Unification:** The paper's primary strength is its generality. By tackling the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a unified theoretical lens through which to view and extend a fragmented landscape of risk-sensitive RL algorithms. This is a valuable conceptual contribution that could guide future research in this area.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Technical Approach:** The use of the dual representation of coherent risk combined with the Envelope Theorem is a powerful and elegant technique for deriving the gradient. Theorem 4.2 provides a clean and insightful formula that is the technical centerpiece of the paper. This approach is more fundamental than deriving gradients for each risk measure on a case-by-case basis.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations of the main gradient formulas in Theorem 4.2 and Theorem 5.2 appear correct and are well-motivated. The consistency analysis for the general SAA-based gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4), while having several assumptions, adds to the paper's theoretical rigor.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and logically structured. It effectively builds its case, starting from the preliminaries on coherent risk, moving to the simpler static risk case, and finally addressing the more complex dynamic risk setting.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Substantive Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the empirical evaluation. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is a simple multi-armed bandit problem. While it effectively illustrates the *motivation* for using different risk measures (i.e., that mean-variance can be suboptimal for heavy-tailed distributions), it does not validate the proposed *algorithms*. There are no experiments that:
    *   Demonstrate that the proposed gradient estimators lead to successful learning on a non-trivial RL benchmark.
    *   Analyze the sample complexity or variance of the general SAA-based estimator (Section 4.3).
    *   Compare the performance of the general estimator to specialized ones (e.g., for CVaR) in terms of computational time and learning speed.
    *   Provide any results for the proposed actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem, which is a major part of the paper's contribution.

2.  **Scalability Concerns of the General Algorithm:** The general gradient estimation algorithm requires solving a convex program at each step, where the number of variables and constraints is proportional to the batch size `N`. For low-variance gradient estimates, `N` might need to be large, potentially making this inner optimization loop a computational bottleneck. The paper does not discuss the practical scalability of this approach.

3.  **Incomplete Description of the Dynamic Risk Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem is described at a very high level, with most details deferred to supplementary material. Key challenges, such as sampling trajectories from the `ξ*`-weighted MDP and the two-phase sampling procedure for estimating `hθ(x, a)`, are mentioned but not elaborated upon. This makes it difficult to assess the practicality and complexity of the proposed algorithm from the main text alone.

**Summary of Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper makes a strong and elegant theoretical contribution by providing a unified policy gradient framework for all coherent risk measures. The technical approach is novel and the results generalize and simplify several prior findings. This is a significant conceptual advance for the field of risk-sensitive RL.
*   **Con:** The paper is very light on empirical validation. The proposed algorithms are not tested on any standard RL tasks, making it impossible to judge their practical utility, sample efficiency, or scalability. The key algorithm for the dynamic risk setting is only vaguely sketched.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**

Even if the decision is to reject, the paper has a strong theoretical core that is worth building upon. My primary suggestion is to significantly bolster the empirical section.

1.  **Validate the Static Risk Algorithm:** Implement the general SAA-based gradient estimator from Section 4.3 and test it on a classic control problem (e.g., CartPole, Acrobot) or a simple portfolio optimization problem with more assets. Compare its performance (convergence speed, final policy risk) and computational overhead against a specialized algorithm (e.g., the CVaR algorithm from Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014).
2.  **Demonstrate the Dynamic Risk Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm is a key contribution and needs to be demonstrated. Even a simple gridworld problem where different risk preferences (e.g., CVaR vs. mean-semideviation) lead to qualitatively different, interesting behaviors (e.g., avoiding states with low-probability-high-cost transitions) would make the contribution much more concrete.
3.  **Discuss Computational Complexity:** Add a discussion on the computational complexity of solving the convex program in the general estimator, and how it scales with the sample size `N`. This would provide readers with a better understanding of the algorithm's practical limitations.
4.  **Clarify the Actor-Critic Algorithm:** An algorithm box or a more detailed paragraph in the main paper describing the critic update (leveraging [31]) and the actor update (with its two-phase sampling) would greatly improve the paper's completeness and clarity.

In its current form, the paper is an excellent theoretical proposal but falls short of being a complete NIPS paper due to the lack of empirical evidence to support the proposed algorithms. With a stronger experimental section, this would be a very compelling submission.
