

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, addressing both static and dynamic risk formulations. The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to derive gradient formulas that can be used with sampling-based methods. The main contributions include a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm for general static coherent risk involving sampling with convex programming, and a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm.

The paper builds upon a growing line of work on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 18, 21, 29, 30]), but makes a significant contribution by unifying approaches that previously focused on specific risk measures. The authors demonstrate that their framework generalizes previous results while providing a more principled approach to risk-sensitive optimization in MDPs. The theoretical foundation is well-grounded in the literature on coherent risk measures [2, 26], and the paper provides a clear connection between risk measures and dynamic programming for MDPs.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive policy gradient methods, with well-justified assumptions and clear proofs (in the supplementary material). The representation theorem for coherent risk measures is effectively leveraged to derive the key gradient formulas.

2. **Unification of approaches:** The paper successfully unifies risk-sensitive RL approaches under a single coherent risk framework, demonstrating how previously studied specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation, etc.) are special cases of their general approach.

3. **Practical relevance:** The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of choosing appropriate risk measures for specific problems, particularly highlighting how different risk measures can lead to different policy choices in financial applications.

4. **Clear exposition:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate technical detail for the target audience. The connection between the representation theorem and the gradient formulas is particularly well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited experimental validation:** The paper lacks extensive empirical results comparing the proposed method with existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms. The numerical example is illustrative but does not provide a comprehensive comparison of performance metrics, computational efficiency, or scalability.

2. **Implementation details:** The actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is only briefly sketched, with limited details on how to implement the two-phase sampling procedure or handle the computational challenges of the convex programming component.

3. **Scalability concerns:** The paper mentions the use of convex programming for gradient estimation, but doesn't adequately address the computational complexity or scalability of this approach for large MDPs, which is a significant practical limitation for RL applications.

4. **Comparison with related work:** While the paper references relevant work, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL methods, making it difficult to assess the relative advantages of the proposed approach.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**
1. Add more extensive experimental results comparing the proposed method with existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms (e.g., [8, 21, 29, 30]) on standard benchmark tasks. This should include performance metrics, computational efficiency, and scalability analysis.

2. Provide more detailed implementation guidelines for the actor-critic algorithm, particularly addressing how to handle the convex programming component efficiently for large-scale problems.

3. Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method and explore potential approximations or alternative approaches to reduce the computational burden of the convex programming steps.

4. Include a more thorough comparison with related work, highlighting the specific advantages of the proposed framework in terms of flexibility, theoretical guarantees, and practical performance.

5. Address the practical limitations of the approach in real-world applications, particularly regarding the computational cost of the convex programming component, and suggest potential solutions or approximations.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The theoretical development is rigorous and well-motivated, and the numerical example demonstrates the practical importance of the approach. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and insufficient detail on implementation and scalability represent significant limitations for a NIPS submission.

The paper addresses a relevant problem in RL with clear significance for practitioners who need flexible risk management approaches. The unification of risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework is an important conceptual advance. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper would be substantially strengthened with more comprehensive empirical validation and implementation details.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper has significant theoretical merit and addresses an important problem in RL, but requires additional experimental results and implementation details to fully demonstrate its practical value and comparative advantages over existing approaches. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above before final acceptance.
