### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning for objectives based on coherent risk measures. The authors tackle both static risk, applied to the total discounted return of an episode, and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The core contribution is a unified approach that leverages the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a set of probability densities (the "risk envelope").

For the static risk problem, the authors derive a novel policy gradient formula using the Envelope Theorem. This formula elegantly expresses the gradient in terms of the optimal solution to the dual problem (i.e., the worst-case density and the Lagrange multipliers). This general formula is shown to cleanly re-derive and simplify existing results for specific risk measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). For cases where the optimal dual variables are not analytically available, the paper proposes a practical algorithm that uses Sample Average Approximation (SAA) to solve the dual problem via convex programming and then estimates the gradient.

For the dynamic risk problem, the paper extends the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures. This leads to a new risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem where the gradient is related to a risk-sensitive value function that obeys a risk-sensitive Bellman equation. This formulation naturally gives rise to an actor-critic algorithm, where the critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function and the actor updates the policy using the derived gradient.

### Review

This paper presents a significant and elegant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. By unifying policy gradient methods under the umbrella of coherent risk measures, it provides a principled foundation that generalizes many disparate, recent results.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this work is its generality. Instead of developing bespoke algorithms for each new risk measure (mean-variance, CVaR, etc.), this paper provides a single, powerful framework. The idea of using the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope Theorem to derive the policy gradient is novel in this context and provides deep insight into the structure of the problem. This unification advances the state of the art by moving from specific cases to a general theory.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound and rigorous. The derivation of the static gradient in Theorem 4.2 is a key result that is both powerful and cleanly presented. The extension to the dynamic case in Theorem 5.2, creating a risk-sensitive analogue of the standard policy gradient theorem, is a non-trivial and important step. The connection to robust MDPs is correctly identified and leveraged.

3.  **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself within the literature. It clearly demonstrates how its framework generalizes and simplifies previous findings, such as the gradient formula for CVaR from [30]. This not only validates the approach but also highlights its utility and elegance. The examples for CVaR and Mean-Semideviation effectively illustrate the application of the main theorem.

4.  **Conceptual Importance:** The work emphasizes the flexibility for a practitioner to *design* a risk measure tailored to their problem, rather than being confined to a few standard options. The numerical example, though simple, effectively demonstrates why this flexibility is crucial: a standard mean-variance objective fails on a heavy-tailed distribution where a mean-semideviation objective behaves more intuitively. This highlights the practical value of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / areas for improvement):**

1.  **Practicality and Computational Cost:** The general algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem for each gradient estimation step. The paper states this can be done with standard tools, but it does not discuss the computational complexity. For a large number of samples `N`, this inner optimization loop could become a significant bottleneck, potentially limiting the method's applicability compared to methods with analytical solutions. A discussion on the scalability of this SAA approach would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is limited. The single numerical illustration is a stateless multi-armed bandit problem. While it makes a valuable conceptual point, it does not demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms on a sequential decision-making task (an MDP with multiple states). In particular, the proposed actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is not evaluated at all. Demonstrating its performance on even a moderately complex benchmark (e.g., Gridworld, CartPole) would substantially increase the paper's impact and validate the practical viability of the dynamic risk framework.

3.  **Density and Missing Details:** The paper is very dense, and many critical details are deferred to the supplementary material. The actor-critic algorithm, a major contribution, is described only at a very high level in Section 5. Key aspects like the function approximation scheme for the critic and the specifics of the two-phase sampling for the actor are omitted. While this is understandable due to space constraints, it makes the second half of the paper difficult to fully assess and reproduce from the main text alone. The conditions for Proposition 4.4 on the consistency of the SAA estimator are also numerous and could benefit from more intuition.

4.  **Limitations of the Dynamic Approach:** The paper acknowledges that the proposed actor-critic algorithm requires a simulator to sample multiple transitions from each state to estimate `hθ(x, a)`. It notes that extending this to a single trajectory roll-out (more common in standard RL) is future work. This is a significant practical limitation that should be highlighted more prominently.

### Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a fundamental contribution to risk-sensitive RL. Its main value lies in the elegant unification of policy gradient methods for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are novel, significant, and well-presented.

The primary weaknesses are the limited empirical evaluation and the high-level treatment of the proposed actor-critic algorithm, which leaves questions about its practical implementation and performance unanswered. However, for a conference like NIPS, a strong theoretical contribution that opens up new avenues can be highly valuable, even if the practical aspects are not fully fleshed out. The framework presented here is likely to be built upon by future research.

Given the significance, originality, and technical quality of the theoretical framework, I recommend **acceptance**. The authors should be strongly encouraged to expand the discussion on computational complexity and, if possible, include at least a simple MDP-based experiment in the final version to demonstrate the dynamic risk algorithm.
