This paper tackles the challenging problem of policy gradient optimization for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors aim for a unified framework applicable to a broad class of risk measures, generalizing existing work focused on specific instances like CVaR. The core contribution lies in deriving gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, leveraging convex optimization and sampling techniques to address computational complexities.

The paper is well-motivated, correctly identifying the need for more general and theoretically sound approaches to risk-sensitive RL. The connection to robust MDPs and the discussion of time consistency are valuable. The derived gradient formulas, particularly for the static case (Theorem 4.2), are a significant technical achievement. The attempt to extend this to the dynamic case (Theorem 5.2) is commendable.

However, despite the technical sophistication, I find myself unconvinced of the overall significance and practical impact of this work. My primary concern lies with the lack of compelling empirical validation. While a numerical example is provided (Section 6), it's limited in scope and doesn’t demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing risk-averse RL algorithms (e.g., those based on CVaR). The example *highlights* the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure, which is a known issue, but doesn’t demonstrate that the proposed framework offers a principled way to *select* or *adapt* such a measure.

Furthermore, the proposed algorithms rely heavily on solving convex optimization problems (SAA in Section 4.3 and in the Critic step of the dynamic case). While the authors acknowledge this as a computational bottleneck, the scalability of these convex programs to large-scale MDPs remains a critical unanswered question. The references to [31] are insufficient to assuage this concern, and more detailed analysis or experimental results demonstrating scalability are needed. The discussion of function approximation in the dynamic case is similarly hand-wavy and lacks concrete details about implementation and error bounds.

The theoretical contributions, while technically sound, feel somewhat incremental. Theorem 4.2 largely serves to re-derive known gradient formulas for CVaR and mean-semideviation in a more general framework. The gain in generality doesn't seem to justify the added complexity. The dynamic case is more novel, but the reliance on the value function approximation limits its practical applicability without further analysis.

The writing is generally clear, but the paper suffers from a degree of mathematical density that could hinder accessibility. More intuitive explanations of the key concepts and a more streamlined presentation of the proofs would be beneficial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound derivation of gradient formulas for coherent risk measures.
*   Unified framework potentially generalizing existing risk-sensitive RL approaches.
*   Connection to robust MDPs and time consistency provides theoretical grounding.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of substantial empirical validation demonstrating practical advantages.
*   Scalability concerns related to the reliance on solving convex programs.
*   Incremental theoretical contributions; limited novelty beyond generalization.
*   Mathematical density and potential accessibility issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the technical work is sound and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature, the lack of compelling empirical results and the scalability concerns outweigh the theoretical contributions. The paper doesn’t present a convincing case for why the proposed framework would be preferred over existing risk-averse RL methods in practice. The authors need to provide more convincing evidence of scalability and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods before this work would be suitable for NIPS. I encourage the authors to focus on addressing these limitations in future work.
