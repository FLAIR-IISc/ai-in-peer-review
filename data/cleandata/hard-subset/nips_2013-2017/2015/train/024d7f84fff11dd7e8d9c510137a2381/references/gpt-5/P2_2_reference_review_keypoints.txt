- Derives a general policy-gradient formula for static coherent risk measures via the dual risk-envelope and envelope theorem.  
- Proposes an SAA-based gradient estimator that combines sampling with a small convex program and proves consistency under reasonable assumptions.  
- Develops a policy-gradient theorem for time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk, yielding an actor–critic–style algorithm.  
- Static results unify and generalize prior specific-risk analyses (e.g., CVaR, mean–semideviation) and recover known gradients more simply, including for discrete distributions.  
- Dynamic analysis leverages equivalence to robust MDPs to obtain a Bellman equation and a ξ-weighted transition kernel in the gradient.  
- Theorem 4.2 (static gradient) is technically sound and consistent with likelihood-ratio methods.  
- SAA consistency (Proposition 4.4) relies on compactness and smoothness assumptions appropriate for finite probability spaces.  
- The dynamic PG theorem (Theorem 5.2) appears technically valid and meaningfully extends PG to Markov coherent risk.  
- Key algorithmic and convergence details for the actor–critic method are deferred to the supplement, leaving the main text without rigorous implementation guarantees.  
- Practical implementation requires sampling or emulating ξ-weighted transitions, which typically needs a simulator and multiple next-state samples per state.  
- Empirical evaluation is very limited (toy bandit); lacks experiments on MDP benchmarks, variance analysis, and dynamic-risk validation.  
- Paper is generally well organized with clear assumptions, and static derivations/examples are easy to follow.  
- Notational density (e.g., dfi/dp, ξPθ, multiple per-state multipliers) hampers accessibility and needs more intuition and explanation.  
- Several minor typos and footnote artifacts should be cleaned up.  
- The unified static gradient, SAA+convex-program estimator, and dynamic PG theorem are novel contributions to RL literature.  
- Potential significance is high if adopted, but practical impact is limited by envelope-differentiation needs, sampling requirements, and sparse empirical evidence.  
- Pros: principled unification via duality/envelope theorem, consistent SAA estimator, dynamic PG extension, and good related-work coverage.  
- Cons: sparse empirical validation, need to compute Lagrange multipliers/gradients, costly sampling requirements, and deferred algorithmic/approximation guarantees.  
- Suggested revisions: add dynamic MDP experiments with baselines, include SAA/actor–critic pseudocode, analyze convex-program complexity, provide practical sampling strategies (importance sampling/doubly-robust), and give derivative formulas for common envelopes.  
- Recommendation: Borderline — strong and novel theory but insufficient experimental and practical algorithmic detail for clear acceptance.
