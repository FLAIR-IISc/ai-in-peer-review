Summary
The paper studies policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. Using the dual representation of coherent risk (as worst-case expectations over a risk envelope), the authors derive: (i) a new sampling-friendly gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2), leading to a general SAA-based estimator that uses convex optimization to obtain envelope densities and multipliers; (ii) specific derivations for CVaR and mean-semi-deviation; and (iii) a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2), enabling an actor-critic scheme that leverages the robust MDP connection to estimate the value function. A small static “asset selection” experiment illustrates how different risk criteria yield distinct policies.

Strengths
- Ambitious, unifying perspective: a single framework that covers a broad class of coherent risks (static and dynamic), subsuming several prior special cases.
- The static gradient formula via an envelope theorem plus likelihood ratios is elegant and broadly applicable, and it recovers known results (e.g., CVaR) cleanly.
- The SAA-based estimator with convex programming is principled and, under stated assumptions, consistent.
- The dynamic risk policy-gradient theorem is a natural extension of the standard PG theorem to Markov coherent risk and conceptually useful. The connection to robust MDPs for the critic is appropriate.
- Clear motivation for flexibility in risk criteria and discussion of time-consistency.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness and completeness
- The static theory is developed on finite probability spaces, with “extension to Lp” deferred; for RL (continuous spaces, infinite horizons), this is a major gap. Many key results (proofs of Theorem 4.2, Theorem 5.2, consistency of the actor-critic with function approximation) are deferred to the supplement, leaving insufficient detail to verify technical conditions (regularity, measurability, subdifferentials at non-smooth points like CVaR quantiles).
- The use of the envelope theorem with potentially non-unique saddle points requires care (selection, measurable dependence on θ, regularity for differentiating the value function). Assumptions ensuring uniqueness or appropriate subgradient use are not stated.
- For CVaR, the gradient at discrete distributions/atoms is delicate. The paper claims generality (discrete and continuous), but does not discuss subgradients when qα is not unique or when the tail set changes discontinuously with θ.
- Mean-semideviation: the expression divides by SD(Z). What if SD(Z)=0? No discussion of subgradients at this kink or regularization. The proposed “replace expectations with sample averages” estimator may be biased due to products of sample means; variance and bias properties are not analyzed.
- Dynamic PG theorem: Expectations are taken under a ξ-weighted kernel. The notation suggests reweighting Pθ(·|x), whereas hθ(x,a) depends on P(·|x,a). It is unclear whether the modified kernel is defined w.r.t. Pθ or action-conditional P, and whether the induced process is a valid Markov chain per action. Clarify normalization: is Pξ(x′|x,a) ∝ P(x′|x,a)ξ(x′) or w.r.t. Pθ(·|x)? This matters for the unbiasedness and implementability of the estimator.
- Sampling under weighted transitions requires a generative model able to sample from Pξ, not just from P. The paper acknowledges needing a simulator “for sampling multiple state-transitions,” but the ability to sample from the ξ-reweighted kernel is nontrivial; otherwise one needs importance sampling, which is not developed.
- Computational burden: The general static estimator requires solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers per batch; the dynamic actor needs solving per-state saddle-point problems. There is no complexity analysis, scalability discussion, or practical heuristics for large-scale or continuous MDPs.
- The critic uses function approximation for robust MDP value estimation (citing [31]), but no details on approximation architecture, projection method, error bounds, or conditions under which the actor’s gradient bias remains controlled.

Originality and relation to prior work
- The static gradient formula generality is a contribution, but several key special cases (CVaR, mean-variance, exponential utility) have been handled in prior work. The paper would benefit from sharper positioning vs. Tamar et al. (CVaR sampling), Chow et al. (CVaR in MDPs), Borkar/Prashanth (risk-sensitive actor-critic), and Ruszczyński/Shapiro (convex risk optimization). Some citations lack years/pages and are difficult to cross-check.
- The dynamic PG theorem for general Markov coherent risk appears new, but its assumptions and proof sketch are too thin to unequivocally establish novelty and correctness.

Significance and empirical evaluation
- Experiments are extremely limited: a single static bandit-style example with three assets. No dynamic MDP experiments, no evaluation of the dynamic actor-critic, no comparisons on benchmark tasks, no runtime/complexity metrics, no ablations, no sensitivity to α or envelope choices. This is a severe weakness for NeurIPS.
- Without empirical validation of the dynamic algorithm and scalability, it is hard to assess practical impact.

Clarity and presentation
- Several notational overloads (e.g., ξ∗θ used across static and dynamic, dependence on x vs. global), and footnotes interrupt the flow. The definition of Markov coherent risk could be more precise about state dependence and conditioning.
- Assumption 2.2 demands twice differentiability of constraints in p and bounded derivatives; concrete verification for common risk measures should be presented (not just asserted).
- References have missing years/pages and inconsistent formatting; some entries are incomplete.
- The paper frequently defers essential details to the supplement.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely define the reweighted transition kernel used in Theorem 5.2 and in the actor’s sampling. How is it sampled in practice without the ability to directly generate from Pξ? Do you propose an importance-sampling scheme?
2. How do you handle non-unique quantiles and non-differentiability in CVaR and other kinked envelopes (subgradients)? What are the implications for SGD?
3. Provide conditions ensuring existence/uniqueness/measurability of saddle points per θ,x and justify differentiation under the max.
4. Complexity: what is the per-iteration cost (static and dynamic)? How does it scale with sample size, state/action cardinality, and number of envelope constraints?
5. Can you provide at least one dynamic MDP experiment, with comparisons to CVaR-RL baselines?

Pros
- Conceptually unifying framework; general static gradient formula.
- Consistent SAA estimator; ties to robust MDPs.
- Potentially broad applicability to many coherent risks.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on restrictive assumptions (finite Ω, known envelopes, generative model); many proofs deferred.
- Technical gaps around non-smoothness and saddle-point differentiation.
- Ambiguities in the dynamic sampling and kernel reweighting.
- Very weak experimental validation; no dynamic results; no scalability analysis.
- Numerous formatting/reference issues.

Recommendation: Reject
Despite interesting ideas and a promising unification, the paper falls short on rigor (for the dynamic result), clarity on implementability, and especially empirical validation. Addressing the technical gaps, clarifying the reweighted dynamics and sampling, adding substantial dynamic experiments, and tightening presentation would significantly strengthen the submission.
