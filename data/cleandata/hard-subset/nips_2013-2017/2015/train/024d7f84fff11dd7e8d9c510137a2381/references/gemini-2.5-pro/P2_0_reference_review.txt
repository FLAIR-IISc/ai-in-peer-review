This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning, applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The key idea is to leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a convex set of probability densities (the risk envelope). By applying the Envelope Theorem to the Lagrangian of this dual formulation, the authors derive a general formula for the policy gradient. This framework is developed for two settings: (1) the static risk of a total discounted cost, and (2) the time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

For the static case, the paper shows that its general gradient formula can analytically recover and simplify existing results for specific measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). More importantly, for general coherent risk measures where an analytical solution is unavailable, it proposes a practical two-step algorithm: first, solve a sample average approximation (SAA) of the dual problem to find the worst-case density and Lagrange multipliers, and second, plug these estimates into the gradient formula. A consistency result for this gradient estimator is provided.

For the dynamic case, the authors derive a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk, which connects the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function. This theorem forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm, where the critic estimates the risk-sensitive value function and the actor updates the policy using the derived gradient.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The main contribution is the unification of policy gradient methods for a broad and axiomatically-justified class of risk measures. Prior work in risk-sensitive RL has often focused on developing bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures (e.g., mean-variance, CVaR). This paper provides a principled and general framework that subsumes many of these as special cases. This is a significant conceptual advance that could streamline and generalize future research in risk-sensitive RL.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Elegance:** The theoretical development is strong. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope Theorem is an elegant and powerful approach to derive the policy gradient. Theorem 4.2 is the cornerstone of the paper and provides a clean, general formula that is amenable to sampling-based estimation. Deriving a corresponding policy gradient theorem for the dynamic case (Theorem 5.2) is a non-trivial and valuable extension.

3.  **Practical General-Purpose Algorithm:** The proposed SAA-based gradient estimator for the static case is a novel and practical contribution. It provides a concrete recipe for applying policy gradient to *any* coherent risk measure, provided its risk envelope can be described by a convex program. This moves beyond theoretical representation to a plausible algorithmic implementation.

4.  **Clarity of Motivation and Structure:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts with clear motivation for using coherent and time-consistent risk measures, introduces the necessary preliminaries, and builds its case systematically from the static to the more complex dynamic setting. The connection to prior work is clearly articulated.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical illustration is insufficient to validate the proposed algorithms. The experiment is a one-step bandit problem, not a sequential decision-making problem (MDP). While it effectively illustrates the *motivation* for choosing different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation), it does not test the performance, sample efficiency, or stability of the proposed gradient estimation schemes (either the general SAA-based one or the actor-critic algorithm). A stronger evaluation on benchmark MDPs would be necessary to demonstrate the practical viability of the proposed methods. For example, how does the SAA-based gradient estimator's variance compare to analytical forms where they exist? How does the actor-critic algorithm perform on a standard RL task?

2.  **Incomplete Description of the Dynamic Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem is described at a very high level. Key details regarding the critic's implementation (based on a modification of [31]), the "two-phase sampling procedure" for the actor, and the handling of function approximation are deferred to a "full paper version" in the supplementary material. For a NIPS paper, the core algorithmic contribution should be understandable from the main text. This lack of detail makes it difficult to fully assess the practicality and complexity of the proposed solution for the dynamic case.

3.  **Potential Practical Hurdles:** The proposed general algorithm involves solving a convex optimization problem at each gradient step (or for each batch). While convex, the size of this problem scales with the number of samples `N`. For large `N`, this could become a computational bottleneck, potentially limiting the scalability of the approach compared to methods that have analytical gradient updates. The paper does not discuss this potential limitation. Furthermore, the actor-critic algorithm requires a simulator that can provide multiple samples of the next state for any given state, which is a stronger assumption than being able to roll out a single trajectory.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. Its main strength lies in providing a unified, principled framework for policy gradient methods across the entire class of coherent risk measures. The theoretical approach is elegant and sound.

The primary weaknesses are the lack of convincing empirical validation and the incomplete description of the actor-critic algorithm in the main text. The experiment serves as a good motivation but does not validate the proposed algorithms in a meaningful way.

Despite these weaknesses, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper introduces a powerful new lens through which to view and solve risk-sensitive RL problems. I recommend **Weak Accept**. I would strongly encourage the authors to include a more substantial empirical evaluation and a more detailed description of the actor-critic algorithm in the camera-ready version to strengthen the paper significantly.
