

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper presents a significant conceptual contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing a unified framework for policy gradient methods under coherent risk measures. The authors successfully generalize previous work on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.) into a single coherent theoretical framework, which is a major conceptual advance.

The key novelty lies in leveraging the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) to derive new gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk. The static risk gradient (Theorem 4.2) is particularly elegant, providing a general formula that encompasses previous results for specific risk measures as special cases. The authors demonstrate this by rederiving known results for CVaR and mean-semideviation with simpler proofs, showing how their framework unifies and simplifies previous work. The dynamic risk gradient (Theorem 5.2) extends the classic policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures, which is a significant conceptual leap.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual novelty and unification of disparate approaches. Previous work on risk-sensitive RL has largely focused on specific risk measures (CVaR [8, 30], mean-variance [18, 21], etc.), each requiring separate algorithmic development. This paper demonstrates that all these approaches are special cases of a single framework, which is both theoretically satisfying and practically useful. The authors provide a clear roadmap for implementing their approach through sampling-based algorithms that combine convex programming with policy gradient methods.

The technical quality is strong, with well-justified theoretical results supported by consistency proofs (Proposition 4.4). The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of the framework, showing how different risk measures (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard-deviation) lead to different policy choices in a financial trading problem. The connection to model uncertainty in Section 7 is particularly insightful, suggesting broader implications beyond risk-sensitive RL.

The paper's clarity is excellent. The mathematical exposition is well-structured, with clear definitions and logical progression from preliminaries to main results. The examples effectively illustrate the theoretical contributions, and the relation to prior work is well-articulated.

I see two minor limitations: (1) The computational complexity of the convex programming step may be a practical concern for large-scale problems, though the authors acknowledge this and suggest function approximation as a solution; (2) The numerical validation, while effective, is limited to a single trading example. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's primary contribution is conceptual and theoretical.

This paper stands out as a significant contribution to the field. While previous work on risk-sensitive RL has been incremental (focusing on specific risk measures), this paper provides a unifying framework that fundamentally changes how we approach risk-sensitive RL. The authors have successfully demonstrated how a single theoretical framework can encompass and simplify multiple previous approaches.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a principled foundation for risk-sensitive RL that is not limited to specific risk measures, giving practitioners the flexibility to choose risk criteria appropriate for their specific problem. The framework also opens new avenues for handling model uncertainty, as hinted in Section 7.

In the context of NIPS, this paper represents a bold theoretical advance that will likely influence future work in risk-sensitive RL. It's rare to see a paper that so clearly and elegantly unifies disparate approaches while providing a foundation for future research. The authors have made a major contribution to the field by showing that coherent risk measures provide a natural unifying framework for risk-sensitive RL.

I recommend acceptance. The novelty and conceptual significance of this work far outweigh the minor methodological limitations. This paper is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS should prioritize. The authors have not only solved a technical problem but have reframed an entire research direction in risk-sensitive RL.
