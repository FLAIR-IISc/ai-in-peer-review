This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as the objective. The authors generalize previous work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance) by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures. The main contributions are twofold: 1) For static risk measures, the paper derives a new policy gradient formula using the Envelope Theorem, leading to a general estimation algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. 2) For dynamic Markov coherent risk, the paper presents a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem, analogous to the standard one, and outlines an actor-critic algorithm to solve the problem.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical elegance and generality. By framing the problem within the well-established theory of coherent risk measures, the authors provide a unifying perspective that connects and generalizes several disparate results in the risk-sensitive RL literature. The derivation of the static risk gradient in Theorem 4.2, using the dual representation and the Envelope Theorem, is methodologically sound and insightful. It successfully re-derives the gradient for CVaR with considerable simplicity compared to previous work and provides a new, explicit formula for the mean-semideviation risk. This theoretical consolidation is a valuable contribution to the field. The extension to a dynamic policy gradient theorem in Section 5 is a logical and important step, providing a formal foundation for developing algorithms in the sequential setting.

**Weaknesses:**

While the theoretical foundation is strong, the paper is critically lacking in empirical validation, which significantly undermines its claims and practical significance. The proposed methods, while general, introduce new computational challenges and assumptions whose impact is not evaluated.

1.  **Insufficient and Inadequate Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's only numerical illustration (Section 6) is a simple, one-step bandit problem with three arms. This experiment does not evaluate the core algorithmic contributions of the paper.
    *   It does not test the proposed algorithm for the dynamic risk case (the actor-critic method), which is a major part of the paper's contribution. The problem is not a sequential decision-making task (MDP).
    *   It does not compare the performance or efficiency of the proposed general gradient estimator (Section 4.3) against established, specialized algorithms for specific risk measures like CVaR. The paper claims its framework is general, but provides no evidence that the general algorithm is competitive with, or even viable compared to, existing methods on problems where those methods apply.
    *   The experiment's purpose is merely to show that different risk measures lead to different policies. While this is true, it is a well-known concept and does not validate the proposed *optimization method*. A paper proposing a new algorithm must demonstrate that the algorithm works effectively.

2.  **Unaddressed Computational Concerns:** The general gradient estimation algorithm for static risk (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) to find the worst-case density `ξ*` and its corresponding multipliers *at each gradient estimation step*. This is a substantial computational burden that is not present in standard policy gradient methods. The authors do not discuss the scalability of this approach. How would this perform in a deep RL setting where millions of gradient steps are taken and the sample size `N` might be large? This practical consideration is crucial for a method intended for RL and is completely unaddressed.

3.  **Underdeveloped Dynamic Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem is only sketched. Key details, implementation challenges, and the convergence analysis are deferred to the supplementary material. More importantly, the actor update requires sampling from an MDP with modified transition probabilities (`Pθ(·|x)ξ*θ,x(·)`), which necessitates a generative model or a simulator that can be reset to any state. This is a very strong assumption that limits the applicability of the method, contrasting with standard actor-critic algorithms that can learn from a single stream of experience. This limitation is not sufficiently discussed or justified.

4.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** Proposition 4.4 provides a consistency result for the static gradient estimator under a set of assumptions. While the authors claim these are mild, the conditions on the set of saddle points (SN ⊂ C w.p. 1) for the empirical problem are non-trivial and may not be straightforward to verify for arbitrary new coherent risk measures, which slightly weakens the claim of broad, practical applicability.

**Summary of evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The theoretical work is of high quality and technically sound. However, the lack of rigorous empirical validation makes this an incomplete piece of work. Claims about having a scalable, sampling-based RL-style approach are not substantiated by evidence.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and the theoretical derivations are well-explained.
*   **Originality:** The approach of using the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope Theorem to derive a general policy gradient formula is novel and original.
*   **Significance:** The potential significance is high, as a unified framework for risk-sensitive PG methods would be a major advance. However, in its current state, the paper presents a theoretical idea without demonstrating its practical viability. Without evidence that the proposed algorithms are computationally feasible and performant on non-trivial RL problems, the work's impact remains speculative.

**Recommendation:**

I lean towards rejecting this paper in its current form. While the theoretical contributions are commendable, NIPS is a conference where algorithmic contributions must be supported by convincing empirical evidence. This paper proposes a general algorithmic framework but fails to demonstrate that it is a practical or effective tool for solving RL problems. The single, non-sequential numerical example is insufficient to support the paper's claims. To be acceptable, the paper would need to include experiments on standard RL benchmark problems that:
a) Demonstrate the performance of the dynamic actor-critic algorithm.
b) Compare the general SAA-based estimator against specialized, established baselines for well-known risk measures like CVaR, evaluating both solution quality and computational cost.
c) Provide an analysis of the computational overhead of the proposed method.

Without this validation, the paper remains a promising theoretical exercise rather than a demonstrated advance in reinforcement learning methodology.
