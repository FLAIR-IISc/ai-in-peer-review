## Peer Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel and comprehensive framework for applying policy gradient methods to reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. It generalizes existing approaches by focusing on the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic (time-consistent Markov coherent risk). The authors provide new gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, facilitating the development of sampling-based algorithms. They demonstrate the generality of their approach by re-deriving existing results as special cases and illustrating its application with a numerical example involving portfolio optimization.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work on risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. It acknowledges prior work on specific risk measures like mean-variance, CVaR, and exponential utility, highlighting its broader scope.  It also appropriately recognizes connections to robust MDPs and stochastic programming, distinguishing its approach by focusing on policy-space approximation, suitable for large-scale problems, and directly addressing the challenges of dynamic risk in an RL setting. The paper’s grounding in the work of Artzner et al. on coherent risk measures and Ruszczynski & Shapiro on stochastic programming is solid.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The core strength is the generalization to the entire class of coherent risk measures. This provides a unified framework and avoids the need for developing separate algorithms for each specific risk measure.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of gradient formulas based on the envelope theorem and convex optimization duality is rigorous and well-motivated. The consistency results for the sampling-based estimators are important for practical application.
* **Practicality:**  The proposed algorithms, while complex, are grounded in sampling and convex programming, making them potentially scalable to large problems.  The connection to existing actor-critic methods is beneficial.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are clearly defined.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example provides a concrete illustration of the potential benefits of using different coherent risk measures in a practical setting, demonstrating the framework’s flexibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical framework is quite dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with coherent risk measures and convex optimization to fully grasp. While clear, the mathematical derivations demand careful attention.
* **Computational Cost:** While sampling-based, the proposed algorithms still require solving convex optimization problems at each iteration. This could become computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional state and action spaces. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example, while insightful, is relatively simple. A more extensive empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks would strengthen the paper and demonstrate the scalability and performance of the proposed algorithms. Specifically, performance comparison against baseline risk-averse RL algorithms would be highly valuable.
* **Assumption 2.2:** While justified, the reliance on Assumption 2.2 regarding the structure of the risk envelope limits the applicability to risk measures that satisfy this condition. A discussion of the limitations and potential extensions to other risk measures would be beneficial.
* **Dynamic Risk Algorithm Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk is high-level, referring to a supplementary material. More details within the main paper would improve accessibility.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.
* The generality of the approach is a major strength, offering a unified framework for a wide range of risk measures.
* The derived gradient formulas are novel and provide a foundation for developing practical algorithms.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the proposed algorithms could be prohibitive for large-scale problems.
* The empirical evaluation is limited, lacking a thorough comparison with existing methods.
* The complexity of the theoretical framework may hinder its adoption by researchers and practitioners.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a solid and original contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The theoretical framework is well-developed, and the proposed algorithms have the potential to be valuable tools for solving complex decision-making problems under risk.  However, the computational cost and limited empirical validation are concerns.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  **Expand the discussion of computational complexity.** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the algorithms and potential strategies for improving scalability.
2.  **Strengthen the empirical evaluation.**  Include results on benchmark RL tasks and compare performance against existing risk-averse RL algorithms.
3.  **Provide more details on the dynamic risk actor-critic algorithm.** Expand the description of the algorithm within the main paper, moving essential components from the supplementary material.
4.  **Discuss the limitations of Assumption 2.2.** Briefly address the limitations of the assumption and potential directions for extending the framework to other risk measures.
