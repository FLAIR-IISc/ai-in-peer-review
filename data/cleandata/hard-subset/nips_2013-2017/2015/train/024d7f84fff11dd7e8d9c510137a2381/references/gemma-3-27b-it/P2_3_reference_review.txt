## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of applying policy gradient methods to reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. It provides a unifying framework for risk-sensitive RL, extending beyond specific risk measures like CVaR or variance. The core contribution lies in deriving novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, leveraging the duality representation of coherent risk measures and combining it with sampling techniques.  The authors also propose an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk optimization.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The work offers a significant generalization of existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms. By focusing on the broader class of coherent risk measures, it provides a more flexible and theoretically grounded approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas is well-motivated and relies on established results from coherent risk theory (Artzner et al., Ruszczynski & Shapiro). The proof sketches are provided and appear reasonable (full proofs are in supplementary material).
* **Practicality via Sampling:** Recognizing the intractability of direct optimization, the authors skillfully employ sampling-based methods, making the approach applicable to larger, more complex problems. The connection to stochastic programming and robust MDPs is insightful.
* **Algorithm Development:** The proposed actor-critic algorithm offers a concrete implementation strategy for dynamic risk optimization, building upon established RL techniques.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper carefully positions its contributions relative to existing literature in risk-sensitive RL and coherent risk optimization, highlighting how it generalizes and simplifies prior approaches.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example using different risk preferences (mean-semideviation vs. standard deviation) provides a concrete illustration of the flexibility of the approach and the impact of different risk measures on policy selection.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The method relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 regarding the structure of the risk envelope, smoothness of constraints, and Assumption 4.1/5.1 regarding likelihood ratios) that may not hold in all practical scenarios.  The robustness of the approach to violations of these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Computational Complexity:** While sampling makes the problem tractable, the algorithm still involves solving convex optimization problems (for the static risk case) and potentially complex estimations in the dynamic risk case. The computational cost of these steps is not fully analyzed, and scaling to very large state/action spaces remains a concern.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is limited in scope. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks with varying risk preferences would significantly strengthen the paper. Demonstrating performance gains over existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms would be particularly compelling.
* **Actor-Critic Details:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm is somewhat high-level. More details about the function approximation techniques, the learning rates, and the specific implementation details would be beneficial. The convergence guarantees are mentioned, but details are in supplementary materials.
* **Lack of Discussion on Risk Envelope Selection:** The paper focuses on *optimizing* given a coherent risk measure but doesn't offer much guidance on *selecting* an appropriate risk measure or defining its corresponding risk envelope for a specific application.  This is an important practical consideration.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Strong. The technical contribution is sound, and the analysis appears rigorous. The theoretical results are well-motivated and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections (especially those describing the actor-critic algorithm) could benefit from more detail.
* **Originality:** High. The unification of risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework is a novel and significant contribution. The derived gradient formulas and the proposed actor-critic algorithm are also original.
* **Significance:**  Good to Very Good. The work has the potential to advance the field of risk-sensitive RL and to provide practitioners with a more flexible and principled approach to managing risk in decision-making problems. It could be especially useful in applications where careful risk management is critical, such as finance, robotics, and healthcare.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to risk-sensitive RL.
* The proposed framework is general and unifies existing approaches.
* The sampling-based algorithms make the approach practical for larger problems.
* The numerical illustration provides a compelling demonstration of the flexibility of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on several assumptions might limit the applicability of the method.
* The computational complexity of the algorithm remains a concern.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and could be strengthened.
* The lack of guidance on risk envelope selection could be a practical drawback.


**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive RL. The theoretical results are sound, the approach is general and practical, and the potential impact is significant. However, addressing the weaknesses mentioned above (especially expanding the empirical evaluation and providing more details about the actor-critic algorithm) would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  I recommend accepting the paper with a request for minor revisions to address these points.  The reviewers should also carefully scrutinize the proofs in the supplementary materials.
