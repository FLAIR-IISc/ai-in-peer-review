- Summary: Presents a unifying framework and novel gradient formulas for static and dynamic coherent risk measures in policy-gradient RL, plus a sampling-based actor-critic algorithm.

Strengths:
- Generality: Extends risk-sensitive RL to the broad class of coherent risk measures, offering greater flexibility than prior work.
- Theoretical soundness: Gradient derivations are well-motivated and grounded in coherent risk theory with supporting proofs.
- Practicality via sampling: Uses sampling methods to make intractable optimizations practical and connects to stochastic programming and robust MDPs.
- Algorithm development: Proposes a concrete actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk optimization.
- Clear positioning: Carefully relates and generalizes existing risk-sensitive RL literature.
- Illustrative example: Numerical example demonstrates how different risk measures affect policy choices.

Weaknesses:
- Strong assumptions: Relies on structural and smoothness assumptions (risk envelope, likelihood ratios) whose violation implications are not explored.
- Computational complexity: Requires solving convex programs and complex estimations; scalability to large state/action spaces is unclear.
- Limited empirical evaluation: Only a small numerical illustration; lacks benchmarks and comparisons to existing risk-sensitive RL methods.
- Actor-critic details sparse: High-level algorithm description with limited implementation, function-approximation, and learning-rate details.
- No guidance on risk envelope selection: Offers little practical advice on choosing or defining coherent risk measures for applications.

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: Strong technical contribution and rigorous analysis.
- Clarity: Generally well-written but actor-critic section could use more detail.
- Originality: High — novel unification under coherent risk and new gradient formulas.
- Significance: Good to very good potential impact for risk-sensitive RL applications.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Significant theoretical contribution and unifying framework.
- Generalizes and subsumes existing approaches.
- Sampling-based methods increase practical applicability.
- Numerical example showcases flexibility of the approach.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Dependence on several potentially restrictive assumptions.
- Computational and scalability concerns remain.
- Empirical results are insufficiently comprehensive.
- Lack of practical guidance on selecting risk envelopes.

Overall recommendation:
- Accept with minor revisions: paper is valuable but should expand empirical evaluation, provide more actor-critic implementation details, and ensure supplementary proofs are carefully checked.
