- Presents a novel framework for applying policy-gradient methods to the full class of coherent risk measures (static and dynamic), with new gradient formulas and an actor-critic algorithm.  
- Provides a gradient formula for static coherent risk and an estimation algorithm that uses convex programming and sampling.  
- Derives a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk and proposes an actor-critic method for the dynamic case.  
- Unifies prior risk-sensitive RL results, subsuming CVaR and mean-variance as special cases.  
- Builds on solid theoretical foundations (coherent risk theory and envelope theorem) with apparently rigorous proofs.  
- Derivations lead to sampling-amenable gradient estimates applicable to large-scale and continuous MDPs.  
- Connects clearly to existing work in risk-sensitive RL and robust MDPs.  
- Includes an illustrative numerical example showing the effect of different coherent risk measures.  
- Tackles the practical challenge of making theoretically appealing coherent risk measures usable in policy optimization.  
- Method is relatively complex, adding convex programming overhead that may affect scalability.  
- Results depend on assumptions (e.g., risk envelope form, bounded likelihood ratio) without analysis of sensitivity to violations.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited; more benchmarks and comparisons to existing risk-sensitive methods are needed.  
- Dynamic (actor-critic) algorithm description is high-level; more details on value-function approximation and sampling are required.  
- Quality assessment: high — technically sound with correct derivations pending fuller supplemental review.  
- Clarity assessment: good — generally well-written but needs elaboration in dynamic algorithm sections.  
- Originality assessment: high — novel generalization to the whole class of coherent risk measures.  
- Significance assessment: good — enables more sophisticated, risk-aware RL agents and opens new research directions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: unifying framework, novel theory, practical sampling-based algorithm, addresses a major challenge.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential computational complexity, limited empirical validation, and incomplete dynamic-algorithm details.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — add broader empirical benchmarks, flesh out dynamic actor-critic details, and discuss impact of assumption violations.
