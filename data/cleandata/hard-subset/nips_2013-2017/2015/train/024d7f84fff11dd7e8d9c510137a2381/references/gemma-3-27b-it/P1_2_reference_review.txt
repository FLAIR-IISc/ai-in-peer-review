## NIPS Paper Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of policy gradient optimization in reinforcement learning (RL) settings where the objective function incorporates a coherent risk measure. Existing work often focuses on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance), while this paper aims to provide a unified framework applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic (time-consistent).  The authors derive novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, utilizing a sampling-based approach suitable for large-scale problems. They demonstrate the generality of their approach by showing that existing results are special cases and provide a numerical illustration showcasing the impact of different risk preferences.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing field of risk-sensitive RL. It acknowledges prior work on specific risk measures and highlights the limitations of those approaches – namely, their lack of generality. It also acknowledges connections to robust MDPs and stochastic programming, explaining why those approaches are less suitable for typical RL scenarios where policy choices directly influence the underlying distribution.  The references are comprehensive and accurately represent the state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The most significant strength is the development of a unified framework for coherent risk measures. This moves beyond the ad-hoc treatment of specific risk functions and offers a principled way to explore different risk preferences within RL.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of gradient formulas, particularly for the static case (Theorem 4.2), is mathematically sound and leverages key concepts from coherent risk theory (e.g., the representation theorem). The consistency results for the sampling-based estimation (Proposition 4.4) provide a theoretical foundation for practical implementation.
* **Sampling-Based Approach:** The reliance on sampling and convex programming makes the approach scalable to large MDPs, addressing a key limitation of dynamic programming-based methods.
* **Dynamic Risk Handling:** Extending the gradient calculation to dynamic, time-consistent risk measures (Theorem 5.2) is a substantial contribution, opening the door to more sophisticated risk-aware control policies.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical notation is consistent.  The numerical illustration, while simple, helps to highlight the practical implications of different risk preferences.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** Despite the effort towards clarity, the mathematical formalism is dense and can be challenging to follow for readers not already familiar with coherent risk theory and stochastic optimization.  More intuitive explanations of the key concepts would be helpful.
* **Implementation Details:**  The paper mentions an actor-critic algorithm for dynamic risk, but provides limited details on its implementation. A more thorough discussion of the practical challenges and potential optimizations would be valuable. The reliance on a simulator for state transitions in the actor-critic algorithm is a limitation that should be acknowledged more explicitly.
* **Numerical Experiment Limited:** The numerical example, while illustrative, is relatively basic. A more extensive experimental evaluation on benchmark RL tasks would strengthen the claims of scalability and performance. The choice of assets and the specific policies tested could be justified more thoroughly.
* **Assumptions:**  The paper relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2, Assumption 4.1).  While these are standard in the field, a more detailed discussion of their limitations and potential impact on the results would be beneficial.  For example, the smoothness requirement in Assumption 2.2 might not hold for all coherent risk measures.
* **Connection to Robust MDPs:** While the paper acknowledges the link to robust MDPs, it doesn't fully explore the implications of this connection. A more detailed discussion of the similarities and differences between the two approaches could provide valuable insights.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in risk-sensitive RL, providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures.
* The derived gradient formulas are novel and potentially useful for a wide range of applications.
* The sampling-based approach addresses the scalability limitations of existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and logically organized, with a comprehensive literature review.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical complexity of the paper may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The lack of detailed implementation information and extensive experimental evaluation raises concerns about the practical feasibility and performance of the proposed approach.
* The reliance on several assumptions requires further justification and discussion of potential limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.  The generality of the proposed framework and the theoretical rigor of the derivations are particularly noteworthy.  While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly in terms of implementation details and experimental evaluation, I believe these can be addressed in a revised version.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest:

*   Providing more intuitive explanations of the key mathematical concepts.
*   Expanding the discussion of the actor-critic algorithm, including details on its implementation and potential optimizations.
*   Conducting a more comprehensive experimental evaluation on benchmark RL tasks.
*   Providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the key assumptions.
