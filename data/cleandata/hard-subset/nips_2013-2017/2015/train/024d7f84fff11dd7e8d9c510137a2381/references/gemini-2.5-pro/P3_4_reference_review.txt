### Paper Summary

This paper presents a novel and general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures as objectives. The authors' main contribution is a unified approach that applies to the entire class of coherent risk measures, thereby generalizing several previous works that focused on specific instances like CVaR or mean-variance. The work is divided into two main parts. First, for static coherent risk measures (applied to the total discounted cost), the paper derives a new formula for the policy gradient. This is achieved elegantly by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures and applying the envelope theorem. Based on this formula, the authors propose a practical estimation algorithm that combines sampling with convex programming. They provide a consistency proof for this estimator. Second, the paper extends this framework to dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. It introduces a new policy gradient theorem for this setting, which relates the gradient to a risk-sensitive value function, and outlines a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The work successfully re-derives and simplifies previous results (e.g., for CVaR) as special cases of its general framework.

### Strengths

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. By grounding the methodology in the theory of coherent risk measures, it provides a single, principled framework that encompasses a vast range of risk criteria. This is a significant conceptual and theoretical leap forward from the previous literature, which has largely focused on developing bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures. This unification is a substantial contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Methodology:** The derivation of the static risk gradient in Theorem 4.2 is novel and elegant. The use of the dual representation of coherent risk and the envelope theorem is a powerful technique that cleanly separates the expectation over the policy-induced distribution from the optimization over the risk envelope. This provides deep insight into the structure of the policy gradient for this class of objectives.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is theoretically rigorous. It provides a new policy gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), which is a non-trivial extension of the standard policy gradient theorem. Furthermore, the consistency result for the general gradient estimator (Proposition 4.4) provides solid theoretical justification for the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear introduction to coherent and dynamic risk measures, making the paper accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with the topic. The progression from the static case to the more complex dynamic case is logical and easy to follow. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation effectively illustrate the application of the general theory.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the numerical illustration in Section 6 is effective at demonstrating *why* flexibility in choosing a risk measure is important, it is too simplistic to validate the proposed algorithms. The problem is a stateless multi-armed bandit, which does not test the dynamic risk formulation or the actor-critic algorithm. It would significantly strengthen the paper to include experiments on a standard RL benchmark (e.g., from OpenAI Gym or DeepMind Control Suite) to assess:
    *   The sample efficiency and computational performance of the general SAA-based gradient estimator (Section 4.3).
    *   The performance of the full actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem.
    *   A comparison against existing specialized algorithms (e.g., the CVaR algorithm from Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014) on a problem where both are applicable.

2.  **Practicality of the Dynamic Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm proposed for the dynamic risk case has some practical limitations that are not fully addressed.
    *   The algorithm appears to require a simulator, as the actor update involves sampling from a modified MDP with ξ*-weighted transitions and estimating hθ(x, a) by sampling multiple next states for each state-action pair. The paper notes that extending this to a single-trajectory setting is future work, which limits its immediate applicability in many standard RL scenarios.
    *   The details of the actor-critic algorithm and its analysis are deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable due to space constraints, this makes the contribution in the main paper feel somewhat incomplete. Providing more intuition about the critic's function approximation and the two-phase sampling in the main text would be beneficial.

3.  **Computational Cost of the General Estimator:** The general gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex optimization problem (Eq. 7) for each gradient estimate. The size of this problem scales with the number of samples, N. While convex, this could become a computational bottleneck for large batch sizes (large N), which are often necessary for accurate estimation, especially for tail-sensitive risk measures. A discussion of the practical computational complexity and potential mitigation strategies would be valuable.

### Final Assessment

This is a strong, well-written, and theoretically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unified framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures is novel, elegant, and likely to be influential. It provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

The main weaknesses are the limited empirical validation and the practical limitations of the proposed actor-critic algorithm. While the theoretical contributions are strong enough to warrant publication on their own, the paper would be much more impactful with a more thorough experimental section that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods on a challenging control problem.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results are high. I recommend acceptance. The authors should be strongly encouraged to incorporate more substantial experiments and a clearer discussion of the practical aspects of the dynamic algorithm in the final version of the paper.
