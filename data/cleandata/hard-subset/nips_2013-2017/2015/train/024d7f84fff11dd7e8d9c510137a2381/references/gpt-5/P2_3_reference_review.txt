Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for optimizing coherent risk objectives in reinforcement learning. It treats both static coherent risk of random returns and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures. The core technical idea is to exploit the dual representation of coherent risk as a worst-case expectation over a convex risk envelope and to differentiate the associated Lagrangian via the envelope theorem and likelihood-ratio tricks.

For static risk, the authors derive a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) that expresses ∇θρ(Z) in terms of: (i) a likelihood-ratio expectation with respect to the worst-case density ξ* and (ii) contributions from KKT multipliers of the risk-envelope constraints. They show how this recovers known results for CVaR and yields a new formula for mean-semideviation. For general coherent risks where closed-form ξ* is unavailable, they propose a sample average approximation (SAA) that solves a finite convex program to obtain ξ* and multipliers, and prove consistency of the resulting gradient estimator under mild assumptions.

For dynamic risk, the paper considers time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures (nested static coherent risks), recalls the associated risk-sensitive Bellman equation, and proves a policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) that relates ∇θVθ(x) to on-trajectory likelihood ratios under ξ*-reweighted dynamics and a stage-wise cost hθ involving the value function, multipliers, and derivatives of the envelope constraints. They sketch an actor-critic algorithm: a critic estimates Vθ using the robust-MDP equivalence with function approximation, and an actor uses Theorem 5.2 to estimate gradients by sampling under ξ*-weighted transitions. A small bandit-like experiment illustrates that different coherent risk choices induce sensible behavior compared to mean-variance.

Relation to prior work
The paper unifies and generalizes prior RL results focused on specific risks: exponential utility, mean-variance, and CVaR. It leverages classical results in coherent risk (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and time-consistent Markov risk (Ruszczyński). It recovers known CVaR gradient formulas in a simpler, more general way and extends policy gradient to the full coherent class in both static and dynamic settings. The robust MDP link for the critic builds on prior work (Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al.). The scope and unification are well articulated and appropriately referenced.

Strengths
- Generality: A single gradient framework covering the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and time-consistent dynamic, is novel and valuable.
- Technical soundness: The envelope-theorem-based derivation is clean, avoids differentiating ξ* w.r.t. θ, and yields implementable estimators. The SAA approach with consistency is standard and appropriate.
- Conceptual clarity: The link between coherent risk and worst-case expectations, and between Markov coherent risk and robust MDPs, is exploited effectively to produce RL algorithms.
- Unification: Prior special-case results (e.g., CVaR policy gradient) fall out as corollaries; the dynamic policy gradient theorem appears new and broadly applicable.
- Practical pathway: The two-step estimator (sampling + convex program to get ξ* and multipliers) is clear; the actor-critic sketch identifies the main engineering challenges.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Empirical evaluation is minimal. There is no dynamic MDP experiment, no evaluation of the actor-critic method, and no comparison on standard RL benchmarks. The bandit-style illustration demonstrates qualitative flexibility but does not validate variance, sample efficiency, or computational cost.
- Practicality of dynamic gradient estimation: Theorem 5.2 requires sampling under ξ*-weighted transitions and access to derivatives of envelope constraints. In many environments one cannot alter transition dynamics; importance sampling or acceptance-rejection schemes would be needed. The paper acknowledges this and suggests future work, but practical recipes and variance properties are missing.
- Computational burden: Each gradient estimate requires solving a convex program (over O(N) variables) to obtain ξ* and multipliers. The paper should quantify runtime and scaling for typical risks (e.g., CVaR, spectral risk) and discuss warm-starting and solver choices.
- Assumptions and regularity: Finite Ω and differentiability/smoothness of risk-envelope constraints are reasonable starting points, but the extension to Lp spaces and continuous MDPs is deferred. Non-uniqueness of saddle points and quantiles (for CVaR) is glossed over; measurable selection and stability of ξ* as θ varies deserve discussion.
- Omitted details: The actor-critic algorithm, convergence analysis with function approximation, and estimator bias due to approximate Vθ are in the supplement. Including at least pseudocode and main convergence statements in the main text would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Quality
The theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated, with clear derivations and consistency guarantees for the SAA estimator. However, the lack of substantive experiments limits empirical support. Variance analysis and finite-sample error bounds are not provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a logical flow from static to dynamic settings. Some notation is heavy, and several important algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement. Clarifying how ξ* is computed in practice for common envelopes and providing pseudocode would help.

Originality
High. Extending policy gradient to general coherent risks (static and dynamic) via envelope duality and providing a dynamic PG theorem is novel. Recovering known special cases adds credibility.

Significance
Potentially high. A general risk-sensitive PG toolkit could influence RL practice and research, especially in safety-critical or finance settings. Realizing this potential will require clearer practical guidance and empirical validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on at least one nontrivial MDP (e.g., gridworld with risky transitions, portfolio management simulator) comparing risk-neutral, CVaR, spectral, and mean-semideviation criteria; include ablations on solver cost and sample efficiency.
- Provide pseudocode for the static SAA gradient and the dynamic actor-critic, and specify solver/hyperparameters.
- Discuss how to implement ξ*-weighted sampling when one cannot alter the simulator; outline importance sampling schemes and their variance.
- Comment on the stability/uniqueness of saddle points and the impact on gradient estimators.
- Include practical tips (e.g., warm starts, regularization of envelopes) and complexity estimates.

Pros
- General, unifying theory and algorithms for coherent risk in RL.
- Clean derivations; recovers and simplifies known results.
- Dynamic PG theorem is new and conceptually important.

Cons
- Very limited empirical validation; dynamic algorithm untested.
- Practical sampling and computational issues underexplored.
- Key details deferred to supplement.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is solid and of broad interest to the NeurIPS community, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by more substantial experiments and practical guidance.
