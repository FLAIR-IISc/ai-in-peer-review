

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper proposes a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors develop gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures, claiming to generalize previous work on specific risk measures like CVaR and mean-variance. The key theoretical contribution is a representation of coherent risk measures as the maximum expectation over a risk envelope (Theorem 2.1), which the authors leverage to derive sampling-based gradient estimators.

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the literature on risk-sensitive RL. The mathematical derivations are rigorous, and the connection between coherent risk measures and the risk envelope representation is well-established. The authors demonstrate that their framework generalizes previous work on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation) and provides a unified approach to risk-sensitive RL.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes empirical validation and practical significance over theoretical novelty, I have significant concerns about this paper's contribution to the field. The paper's main weakness is the lack of substantial empirical evidence demonstrating the practical advantages of the proposed framework over existing risk-sensitive RL methods. The numerical example in Section 6 is extremely limited - it involves only three assets with simple distributions and compares three policies trained with different risk objectives, but does not benchmark the proposed method against established risk-sensitive RL algorithms on standard RL benchmarks.

The paper claims to provide a "general method for the whole class of coherent risk measures," but fails to demonstrate how this generalization leads to meaningful practical improvements. The computational cost of the proposed approach (which involves sampling followed by convex programming for each gradient estimate) is not addressed, despite the fact that such two-step procedures can be significantly more expensive than existing methods. For instance, the CVaR policy gradient method of [30] is computationally simpler than the proposed approach, yet the paper doesn't demonstrate that the increased complexity of the proposed method leads to superior performance.

The paper also makes several overstated claims about the significance of the work. While the theoretical unification of risk measures is mathematically interesting, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this unification provides new insights or practical advantages. The claim that "our approach allows to rederive them in greater generality and simplicity" is not substantiated with evidence showing that the proposed method is simpler or more effective in practice.

The connection to prior work is generally adequate, but the paper doesn't sufficiently address how this work advances beyond existing risk-sensitive RL methods. For example, the paper cites [29] and [30] as prior work on CVaR, but doesn't clearly articulate how the proposed method improves upon these approaches. The authors claim their method "generalizes and unifies previous approaches," but without empirical evidence of superior performance, this claim lacks substance.

The paper's limited empirical validation is particularly concerning given the field's emphasis on empirical results. The single numerical example with three assets cannot demonstrate the approach's scalability or effectiveness on more complex problems. The paper would benefit from comparisons on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari, or other standard RL environments) that would demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed framework.

While the theoretical foundation is solid, the paper falls short of providing sufficient evidence to justify its claims of significance. The proposed method appears to be a theoretical extension of existing approaches rather than a practical advancement that would be valuable to the RL community. The lack of comprehensive empirical validation against established baselines makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed method.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically interesting generalization of risk-sensitive RL to the class of coherent risk measures. However, the lack of substantial empirical validation, the absence of clear practical advantages over existing methods, and the limited demonstration of scalability to more complex problems significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The theoretical framework is sound, but without evidence of practical utility, the paper does not meet the high standards of NIPS for empirical and practical significance.

**Recommendation**: Reject

**Strengths**: 
- Theoretical framework is mathematically sound and well-motivated
- Clear connection to existing work on coherent risk measures
- Provides a unified perspective on risk-sensitive RL

**Weaknesses**:
- Insufficient empirical validation on standard RL benchmarks
- No comparison against established risk-sensitive RL methods
- Limited demonstration of practical advantages over existing approaches
- No analysis of computational complexity or scalability
- Overstated claims about the significance and practical utility of the proposed method
