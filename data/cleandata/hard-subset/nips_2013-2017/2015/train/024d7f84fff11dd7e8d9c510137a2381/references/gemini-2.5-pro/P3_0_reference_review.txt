**Summary**

This paper introduces a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. The work's main contribution is to unify and generalize previous approaches, which have typically focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance), to the entire class of axiomatically-defined coherent risk measures. The authors tackle two distinct but related problems: (1) optimizing a static coherent risk of the total discounted return and (2) optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk.

For the static case, the authors derive a novel policy gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures and the envelope theorem. This formula expresses the gradient as an expectation with respect to a "worst-case" probability distortion. They propose a practical algorithm for estimating this gradient using Sample Average Approximation (SAA), which involves solving a convex program on a batch of sampled trajectories. A consistency result for this estimator is provided.

For the dynamic case, they present a new policy gradient theorem (Theorem 5.2) for Markov coherent risk, which extends the classic policy gradient theorem to this risk-sensitive setting. This leads to a proposed actor-critic algorithm where the critic estimates a risk-sensitive value function (by connecting it to robust MDPs) and the actor updates the policy using a gradient estimate derived from the new theorem.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this paper is its generality. By grounding the methodology in the axiomatic framework of coherent risk measures, it provides a single, elegant theory that encompasses a wide variety of risk preferences. This is a significant conceptual advance over prior work that developed bespoke algorithms for individual risk measures. The paper successfully demonstrates how its framework can re-derive and simplify previous results, for instance, for CVaR (Section 4.1).

2.  **Novel and Elegant Theoretical Contributions:** The paper presents two new policy gradient theorems (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) that are both technically sound and insightful. The derivation of the static risk gradient using the dual representation and the envelope theorem is particularly elegant. This provides a principled "first principles" foundation for risk-sensitive policy gradients that has been lacking in the literature.

3.  **Constructive Algorithmic Framework:** The proposed SAA-based gradient estimator for the static case is a valuable practical contribution. It provides a concrete recipe for applying the theoretical result to any coherent risk measure whose risk envelope can be described by convex constraints, even when closed-form solutions for the worst-case density are unavailable.

4.  **Addressing Time Consistency:** The paper correctly identifies the importance of time consistency for sequential decision problems and explicitly addresses it by analyzing dynamic Markov coherent risk measures. This is a crucial consideration for rational decision-making over time and distinguishes this work from many risk-sensitive RL formulations that apply static measures in a potentially inconsistent way.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the limited empirical evaluation. The numerical illustration is a simple one-step bandit problem, which only validates the static risk gradient estimator. There are no experiments for the dynamic case, which means the proposed actor-critic algorithm—a major part of the paper's contribution—is not empirically validated at all. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance on a standard RL benchmark (even a simple one like a gridworld or an inventory management task) would be essential to substantiate its practical utility.

2.  **Lack of Detail on the Actor-Critic Algorithm:** The description of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem is relegated to the supplementary material due to space limitations. Key components, such as the two-phase sampling procedure, the function approximation for the critic, and the convergence analysis, are central to the paper's claims. Without these details in the main text, it is difficult for a reader to fully assess the completeness and practicality of this part of the contribution.

3.  **Potential Computational Concerns:** The general gradient estimation algorithm requires solving a convex optimization problem at each step on a batch of `N` samples. The computational cost of this step is not discussed. For large `N` (which may be required for accurate gradient estimation, especially for tail-sensitive risk measures like CVaR), this could become a significant bottleneck, potentially limiting the scalability of the approach compared to standard policy gradient methods.

4.  **Practical Limitations of the Dynamic Algorithm:** The authors note that their actor-critic algorithm requires a simulator to sample multiple next-state transitions from each state in a trajectory. This limits its applicability to model-based or simulator-based settings and excludes a large class of RL problems where interaction is limited to single trajectory rollouts. While acknowledged as future work, this is a major practical limitation of the current proposal.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. It provides a much-needed unification of policy gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning under the principled framework of coherent risk measures. The derived gradient formulas are novel and insightful, representing a clear advance for the field.

The primary weaknesses are the almost complete lack of empirical validation for the dynamic setting and the high-level treatment of the proposed actor-critic algorithm. While the theoretical contributions alone are of high quality, a NIPS paper should ideally demonstrate that the proposed ideas lead to practical algorithms with measurable performance.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's originality, theoretical quality, and potential for future impact are substantial. The ideas presented here are likely to inspire a new line of research in developing more general and principled risk-sensitive RL agents.

**Decision:** Accept.

I strongly recommend that the authors use the rebuttal period and the camera-ready version to:
1.  Move more essential details of the actor-critic algorithm from the supplement into the main paper, perhaps by condensing the preliminaries.
2.  Add an experimental result for the dynamic risk case on a standard MDP benchmark to demonstrate the feasibility and behavior of the proposed actor-critic algorithm. This would substantially strengthen the paper.
