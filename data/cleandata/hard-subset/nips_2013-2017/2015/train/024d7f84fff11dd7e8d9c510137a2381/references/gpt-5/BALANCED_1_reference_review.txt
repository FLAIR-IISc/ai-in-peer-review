Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static coherent risk of trajectory return and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The key technical contributions are: (i) an envelope-theorem-based gradient formula for general static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2), which yields sampling-friendly estimators via likelihood ratios; (ii) a sample-average-approximation (SAA) gradient estimator that combines Monte Carlo with a convex program over the risk envelope and proves consistency; (iii) a policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) that expresses the gradient as an expectation under risk-distorted transitions and yields an actor-critic algorithm.

The work unifies and generalizes prior risk-sensitive RL results that focused on specific risks: exponential utility [6], mean-variance [18,29,21], CVaR [8,30] in static settings, and dynamic coherent risk for linear systems [20,9]. It leverages the coherent risk dual representation [25,26] and the equivalence between dynamic coherent risk and robust MDPs [19], and builds on approximate robust planning [31]. The CVaR gradient (Sec. 4.1) recovers and simplifies [30]; the mean-semideviation gradient (Sec. 4.2) extends [26] to the policy-dependent case. For dynamic risk, the gradient theorem complements dynamic-programming treatments of coherent risk [24] by enabling policy search and function approximation.

Strengths
- Generality and unification: The envelope representation is used to derive a single gradient formula applicable to any coherent risk with a tractable risk envelope, subsuming CVaR, mean-semideviation, spectral risk [1], etc.
- Technical clarity in the static case: The use of the Lagrangian saddle point and envelope theorem provides a concise, reusable recipe; closed-form derivations for CVaR and mean-semideviation are neat and instructive.
- SAA gradient estimator with consistency: The two-step sampling + convex optimization approach is well-justified, practical for risks without analytic saddle points, and connects to established stochastic programming theory [26].
- Dynamic risk policy-gradient theorem: Extends the classic policy-gradient theorem [28,14] to time-consistent Markov coherent risk; the expression highlights the role of risk-distorted transitions and connects to robust MDPs [19].
- Conceptual relevance: The paper articulates the importance of choosing a risk measure to match problem properties (e.g., tail sensitivity), and highlights links to model uncertainty.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation: The paper presents only a single static toy example; there is no empirical validation of the dynamic actor-critic, no comparisons on standard RL benchmarks, and no study of variance, bias, or sample efficiency of the proposed estimators.
- Practicality and computational overhead: Each gradient estimate requires solving a convex program to obtain the saddle point (and KKT multipliers); for dynamic risk, sampling from ξ-weighted transitions assumes a generative model and requires multi-sample next-state queries, which is stronger than typical online RL settings.
- Regularity assumptions and non-uniqueness: The gradient formulas rely on envelope-theorem conditions, Slater’s condition, differentiability of constraints with respect to Pθ, and a saddle-point selection; the impact of non-unique quantiles/saddle points (e.g., CVaR at flat regions) and subgradients is not fully discussed in the main text.
- Clarity/organization for the dynamic case: Many essential details (critic algorithm, convergence analysis, sampling procedures, complexity) are deferred to the supplement; readers cannot assess implementability from the main paper alone.
- Missing finite-sample/statistical guarantees: Beyond consistency, there are no variance analyses or finite-sample error bounds for the gradient estimators, which is especially important for tail-sensitive risks (CVaR).

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, building on solid convex analytic and stochastic approximation foundations. The static theory is careful, and the consistency of the SAA estimator is valuable. However, the dynamic actor-critic is undervalidated empirically and lacks finite-sample analysis; the assumptions for Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 should be more explicitly checked for canonical risks and realistic policy classes. The need for a generative model and per-iteration convex programs raises questions about scalability.

Clarity
Generally well-written, with helpful motivation and connections. The static section is clear and self-contained. The dynamic section would benefit from more detail in the main text: explicit pseudo-code, computational complexity, and clearer discussion of how ξ-weighted transitions are implemented and how value-function approximation errors propagate into the gradient.

Originality
The unifying gradient formula for coherent risks and the extension of the policy-gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk are original and useful. Recovering the CVaR gradient in a simpler derivation is a nice contribution. The general SAA-based gradient estimator for policy-dependent distributions appears novel in the RL context.

Significance
Potentially significant: provides a general framework to bring risk-sensitive policy gradients to a broad class of coherent risks and dynamic, time-consistent objectives. That said, the current empirical evidence is insufficient to demonstrate practical impact or superiority over specialized methods; more convincing experiments are needed to influence practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Broad, principled unification of risk-sensitive PG methods with clean, reusable formulas.
- New dynamic risk policy-gradient theorem with implications for robust control/RL.
- Solid integration of convex analysis and sampling; consistency guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient experiments (no dynamic results, no robust benchmarking, no ablations).
- Practicality concerns: reliance on a generative model and inner convex programs per update.
- Missing finite-sample analyses and detailed guidance on variance reduction and scalability.

Questions and requests for clarification
1. How are non-unique saddle points/quantiles handled in practice? Do results provide subgradient estimates, and how does this affect convergence of SGD?
2. What are the per-iteration computational costs (time/space) of solving the inner convex programs and obtaining KKT multipliers, as N grows?
3. Can you provide variance analyses or empirical variance measurements for the SAA and closed-form estimators (e.g., CVaR), and discuss baseline/control variate strategies?
4. How sensitive is the dynamic actor-critic to approximation error in Vθ? Can you quantify bias in ∇θ estimates induced by function approximation?
5. Can the dynamic method be adapted to single-trajectory data (without a generative model), e.g., via importance sampling or off-policy corrections?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add substantial experiments: (i) static: CVaR and mean-semideviation on synthetic heavy-tail tasks with ablations (SAA vs. analytic); (ii) dynamic: risk-sensitive gridworlds, queueing, and continuous-control (e.g., LQG with CVaR), comparing to [8,30,21,20,9] and robust MDP baselines [31]; report return distributions, constraint satisfaction, and computational overhead.
- Include pseudo-code and complexity analysis for both static SAA gradient and the dynamic actor-critic in the main text; discuss how to obtain KKT multipliers in practice.
- Present variance reduction techniques (baselines/control variates, advantage functions) tailored to the new gradient forms; explore importance sampling for tail events [3].
- Clarify assumptions and subgradient handling for non-unique saddle points; consider smoothing the envelope to ensure differentiability and study its bias.
- Discuss extensions to continuous state/action spaces (e.g., via reparameterization and differentiable risk envelopes) and off-policy settings.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper offers strong and potentially influential theory, but the experimental evaluation and practical guidance are too limited for NeurIPS at this stage, especially for the dynamic method. With more comprehensive empirical validation, clearer algorithmic details in the main text, and some finite-sample/variance analysis, this could become a strong contribution.
