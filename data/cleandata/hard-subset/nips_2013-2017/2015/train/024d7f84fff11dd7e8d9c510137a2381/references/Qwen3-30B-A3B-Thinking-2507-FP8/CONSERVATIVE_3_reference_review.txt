

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

This paper proposes policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, claiming to unify previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance, etc.) under a single framework. The authors present theoretical results for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures, including new gradient formulas and corresponding sampling-based algorithms. The work builds on the coherent risk framework from Artzner et al. (2002) and extends it to reinforcement learning.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a solid theoretical foundation. The connection between coherent risk measures and their representation via risk envelopes (Theorem 2.1) is well-established, and the derivation of the gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2) appears mathematically sound. The authors demonstrate how their approach generalizes previous results for specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-semideviation), which is a legitimate contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from significant weaknesses in empirical validation and comparative analysis, which are critical for a paper claiming to present a general method. The numerical illustration (Section 6) is extremely limited, featuring only a single small-scale trading example with three assets, without comparison to established risk-sensitive RL methods. The authors claim their approach "generalizes and unifies previous approaches," but provide no evidence that their method outperforms existing techniques on standard benchmarks or in more complex settings.

The paper makes unsubstantiated claims about the practical utility of their approach. For instance, they propose a two-step procedure involving both sampling and convex programming for gradient estimation, but provide no analysis of computational complexity or scalability to large-scale RL problems. The claim that their method "scales-up to large or continuous MDPs" is unsupported by any empirical evidence or complexity analysis.

The comparison with related work is superficial. The authors cite several papers on risk-sensitive RL (e.g., [8, 30] for CVaR; [29, 21] for mean-variance), but fail to demonstrate how their method improves upon these approaches. The numerical example compares three policies but does not show that their proposed algorithm for training the risk-averse policies outperforms standard methods for those specific risk measures.

The consistency result (Proposition 4.4) is theoretical but lacks concrete convergence rate analysis. The paper also makes no mention of the computational cost of solving the convex programming subproblems required for their gradient estimation, which could be prohibitive for large-scale problems.

**Significance and Contribution:**
While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper fails to demonstrate significant advances over existing methods. The "unified framework" claim is overstated - most previous risk-sensitive RL methods already operate within a similar theoretical framework (e.g., the CVaR approach in [8, 30] is based on the same risk envelope representation). The paper does not provide evidence that their sampling-based approach offers practical advantages over existing methods, such as better sample efficiency or computational tractability.

The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) is interesting but remains largely theoretical without empirical validation. The paper's claim that "by carefully shaping the risk-criterion, the decision maker may be able to take uncertainty into account in a broad sense" is intriguing but unsupported by data.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a theoretically sound extension of risk-sensitive RL to the class of coherent risk measures. However, as a scientific contribution, it falls short of the standards expected at NIPS due to the lack of comprehensive empirical validation and comparative analysis. The paper makes strong claims about generalization and unification but provides insufficient evidence to support these claims.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Extensive empirical evaluation on standard RL benchmarks (e.g., MuJoCo, Atari)
2. Detailed comparison with established risk-sensitive RL methods
3. Analysis of computational complexity and scalability
4. Demonstration of practical advantages over existing approaches

Without these elements, the paper does not sufficiently advance the state of the art in risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. While the theoretical framework is valid, the paper lacks the empirical rigor required to establish its practical significance.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension but fails to provide the necessary empirical evidence to support its claims of practical utility and generalization. The lack of thorough experimental validation against established baselines makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed methods.
