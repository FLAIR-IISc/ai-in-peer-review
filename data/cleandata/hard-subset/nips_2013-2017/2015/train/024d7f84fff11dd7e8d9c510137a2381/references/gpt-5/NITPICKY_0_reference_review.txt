Summary
The paper aims to develop policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive RL that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic (time-consistent Markov coherent risk). The key ideas are: (i) use the dual representation of coherent risk (as a worst-case expectation over a convex “risk envelope”) combined with an envelope theorem to derive a general gradient formula for static risk; (ii) propose a sampling-plus-convex-programming estimator that uses the Lagrangian saddle point and KKT multipliers; (iii) extend to dynamic, time-consistent risk via a Bellman equation with a max over the envelope at each state, yielding a policy-gradient theorem under ξ-weighted dynamics, and sketch an actor–critic algorithm. The paper shows CVaR and mean-semi-deviation as examples and provides a single toy “trading” experiment.

Strengths
- Ambitious unification: attempts to provide a single policy-gradient framework for all coherent risks (static and dynamic), subsuming CVaR, mean-semi-deviation, and spectral risks.
- The static-gradient derivation via the dual risk envelope and an envelope theorem is conceptually clean, and the CVaR and MSD examples connect to known results.
- The SAA-based gradient estimator that leverages convex dual variables is interesting and potentially useful when analytic saddle points are unavailable.
- The dynamic section connects to robust MDPs and proposes a PG-style theorem for Markov coherent risk, which is of independent interest.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and assumptions
- Differentiability vs subgradients: Theorems are stated for gradients, yet coherent risks (e.g., CVaR at mass points, spectral risks with kinks) are generally nonsmooth. With multiple optimal ξ, ρ can be nondifferentiable. Theorem 4.2 asserts a gradient for “any saddle point,” but does not justify independence from the choice of saddle point or specify that the result yields a subgradient. The same concern applies to the CVaR example where the quantile is nonunique in the discrete case; the claim that the result “holds regardless” of continuity assumptions is too strong without a subdifferential treatment.
- Hidden dependence of Z on θ: The static formula includes only likelihood-ratio terms and derivatives of envelope constraints with respect to p, but no explicit ∇θ Z term. The paper says it “suppresses the notation of θ in θ-dependent quantities,” but does not clarify whether Z(ω) can depend on θ beyond ω’s distribution. If Z depends on θ explicitly (e.g., parameterized costs), additional terms are missing.
- Assumption 2.2 is strong and nonstandardly stated. U(Pθ) is assumed given in a canonical convex form with twice differentiable dependence on p; many coherent risks satisfy this, but the paper provides no general recipe for constructing ge, fi for arbitrary coherent risk envelopes nor checks for nonsmooth envelopes.
- Proposition 4.4’s conditions (I)–(IV) are heavy. Convergence of gradients under SAA with θ-dependent feasible sets generally requires careful regularity (e.g., graphical convergence of argmax sets, calmness). The paper omits proofs and does not discuss cases where multipliers are not unique.
- The dynamic theorem uses expectations under ξ∗-weighted transitions. This requires that ξ∗(·|x)Pθ(·|x) defines a valid transition kernel; while the envelope includes normalization, this should be stated explicitly. Also, the notation “∇Vθ(x)” is ambiguous (should be ∇θ Vθ(x)).
- hθ(x,a) includes C(x) outside the expectation; it’s unclear whether this double-counts stage costs within the policy gradient sum or is intended as part of a “Q-like” term. A derivation sketch would help verify the baseline structure and variance-reduction properties.
- The likelihood-ratio boundedness assumptions (Assumptions 4.1, 5.1) are strong and typically violated for common policy classes unless soft constraints are imposed.

Algorithmic practicality
- The static estimator requires solving a convex program and extracting KKT multipliers at every gradient step. This can be expensive; no complexity or wall-clock discussion is provided, and it is unclear how stable multipliers are numerically.
- The dynamic actor–critic requires multiple transitions sampled per state and access to a simulator. The paper explicitly does not handle single-trajectory settings. This severely limits applicability to many RL scenarios.
- The robust-MDP value estimation step with function approximation is only sketched; key algorithmic and convergence details are deferred to the supplement.

Experiments
- Extremely limited empirical validation: only a small 3-armed “trading” example with static risks. No dynamic MDP results, no comparisons on standard RL benchmarks, no evaluation of the proposed SAA estimator versus analytic approaches for general risks, no variance/sample complexity analysis, and no ablation on solver accuracy or multiplier sensitivity.
- The example compares to mean-standard-deviation (non-coherent) to make a point about heavy tails, but does not demonstrate the benefits of the proposed general framework over existing CVaR or spectral-risk methods.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation is often overloaded or suppressed (θ-dependences suppressed throughout; ∇Vθ vs ∇θVθ; “MDPM” vs “MDP M”; “Eξ[·]” defined with ξPθ but later used as if ξ induces a probability directly). The set B is defined as probability distributions but later used for densities ξ; writing elements of U as “ξPθ” is confusing.
- Footnotes bleed into the main text; figure is referenced but not shown; several typographical errors.
- References are incomplete and inconsistent (missing years/pages; titles truncated; odd ordering; multiple entries with commas instead of years). This is below NeurIPS standards.

Pros
- Ambitious, unified view of risk-sensitive policy gradients for coherent risks.
- The static-gradient formula and SAA estimator are potentially useful and could inspire follow-on work.
- Connection to robust MDPs and time-consistent risks is timely.

Cons
- Overclaims of differentiability and generality; lack of subgradient treatment.
- Heavy and sometimes unclear assumptions; missing key proof details.
- Practicality concerns (solver dependence, simulator requirement).
- Very weak empirical section; no dynamic results; no thorough evaluation.
- Notational and formatting issues; incomplete references.

Questions for authors
- Does Theorem 4.2 yield a subgradient in general? Under what conditions is the gradient unique, and how do you handle nonunique quantiles for CVaR?
- Does Z depend on θ beyond through Pθ? If so, how does your formula change?
- Can you provide explicit ge/fi for several representative coherent risks beyond CVaR/MSD and verify Assumption 2.2?
- What is the computational cost per gradient step (static and dynamic)? How sensitive are KKT multipliers to solver tolerances?
- Can the actor–critic be adapted to single-trajectory on-policy sampling?

Recommendation: Reject
While the paper is conceptually interesting, the current version has multiple technical gaps (nonsmoothness/subgradients, strong unverified assumptions), lacks essential algorithmic and experimental validation (especially for the dynamic setting), and suffers from clarity/formatting issues and incomplete references. Strengthening the theory (with precise subgradient statements and proofs), providing solid empirical evidence on dynamic benchmarks, and improving presentation would substantially improve the submission.
