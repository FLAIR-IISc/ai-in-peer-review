## Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a general framework for applying policy gradient methods to reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. It extends existing work, which often focuses on specific risk measures, to the entire class of coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic (time-consistent). The core contributions include a novel gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm for its approximation using convex programming and sampling, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk leading to an actor-critic algorithm. The authors position their work as a unification and generalization of prior approaches, offering improved scalability and applicability to large/continuous MDPs.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The framework’s ability to handle *any* coherent risk measure is a significant strength. This avoids the limitations of existing methods tied to specific risk functions like CVaR or mean-variance.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, leveraging results from coherent risk theory and establishing clear connections to robust MDPs and dynamic programming. Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 are central to this and provide key insights.
* **Novel Gradient Formulas:** The derived gradient formulas, particularly Theorem 4.2, are elegant and appear well-suited for sampling-based estimation, a crucial aspect for practical application.
* **Algorithm Development:** The proposed sampling-based algorithms for both static and dynamic risk problems offer a pathway to scalability beyond traditional dynamic programming approaches.
* **Connection to Robust MDPs:**  Highlighting the link between coherent risk and robust MDPs provides a valuable perspective and opens avenues for leveraging techniques from that field.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example effectively demonstrates the impact of different risk criteria (mean-semideviation vs. mean-standard deviation) on policy selection, showcasing the practical relevance of the framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical formalism is quite dense and can be challenging to follow, particularly for readers unfamiliar with coherent risk theory. While comprehensive, the presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations of key concepts.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks detailed information on the actor-critic algorithm implementation. Key aspects like function approximation, hyperparameter tuning, and specific sampling strategies are not thoroughly discussed, making it difficult to assess the practicality of the approach.  While mentioning the use of a modified algorithm from [31], more specifics are needed.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical example is limited. A more extensive empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks with varying degrees of risk aversion would significantly strengthen the paper.  Comparing performance against existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms (e.g., those based on CVaR) is crucial.
* **Assumption Dependence:** The results rely on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 regarding the risk envelope).  A more detailed discussion of the practical implications and limitations of these assumptions would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:** The sampling and convex programming steps within the gradient estimation procedure could be computationally expensive, particularly for high-dimensional state/action spaces.  This potential bottleneck is not adequately addressed.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical justifications for the proposed methods. However, the lack of extensive empirical validation and discussion of computational costs tempers the overall quality assessment.
* **Clarity (6/10):** The writing is precise but dense.  More intuitive explanations and examples would improve clarity.  The notation is heavy and can be difficult to track.
* **Originality (9/10):**  The generalization to the entire class of coherent risk measures and the associated gradient formulas represent a significant contribution to the field.  The unification of previous approaches is valuable.
* **Significance (8/10):** The potential to apply risk-sensitive RL to a broader range of applications and the scalability offered by the sampling-based algorithms make this work highly significant. The connection to robust MDPs adds further value.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides a general and theoretically sound framework for risk-sensitive RL.
* Develops novel and potentially efficient gradient estimation algorithms.
* Unifies and generalizes existing approaches.
* Opens up new avenues for research in risk-aware decision making.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of extensive empirical evaluation.
* Limited discussion of implementation details and computational costs.
* Dense mathematical presentation can hinder accessibility.
* Reliance on potentially restrictive assumptions.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning.  The theoretical foundations are strong, and the proposed framework has the potential to be widely applicable.  However, the authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by:

1.  Providing more detailed implementation details for the actor-critic algorithm.
2.  Conducting a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on benchmark RL tasks.
3.  Discussing the computational costs and potential optimizations of the proposed algorithms.
4.  Clarifying the implications of key assumptions and exploring their robustness.
5.  Improving the clarity of the presentation with more intuitive explanations and examples.



Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper's impact and solidify its position as a significant contribution to NIPS.
