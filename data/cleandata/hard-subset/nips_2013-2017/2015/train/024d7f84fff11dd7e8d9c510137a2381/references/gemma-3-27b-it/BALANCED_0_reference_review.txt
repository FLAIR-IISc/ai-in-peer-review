This paper tackles the important problem of policy gradient optimization under coherent risk measures, a generalization of commonly used risk-sensitive RL objectives like CVaR and mean-variance. The authors provide novel gradient formulas for both static and dynamic coherent risk, alongside an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case. The work is well-motivated, builds upon a solid theoretical foundation, and offers a unifying framework for existing approaches. 

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the limitations of focusing on specific risk measures and leverages the concept of coherent risk measures, which possess desirable properties like monotonicity and translation invariance. It builds on Artzner et al.'s [2] definition of coherence and extends the recent work on dynamic Markov coherent risk measures [24]. The core contribution lies in formulating policy gradients applicable to the entire class of coherent risks, generalizing prior work (e.g., [6, 18, 29, 30]) restricted to particular instances. The authors skillfully connect their work to robust MDPs [19] and stochastic programming [25, 26], highlighting the interplay between risk aversion and uncertainty. The proposed approach utilizes sampling techniques, making it scalable to large or continuous MDPs, unlike dynamic programming approaches [24].

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The unified framework for coherent risk measures is a significant strength. It provides a systematic way to analyze and optimize a wide range of risk preferences.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the gradient formulas is rigorous, relying on established results like the Envelope Theorem [17] and careful application of the chain rule.
* **Practicality:** The introduction of sampling-based algorithms makes the approach applicable to large-scale problems where exact solutions are intractable. The algorithm utilizes a straightforward, two-step process of convex optimization and gradient estimation.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions itself within the existing literature, demonstrating an understanding of related approaches and highlighting the contributions of this work. The ability to re-derive previous results in a more general setting is valuable.
* **Illustrative Example:** The numerical example effectively demonstrates how different risk measures lead to different trading strategies, underscoring the importance of selecting an appropriate risk criterion.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions, including the boundedness of the cost function and the differentiability of the constraints in the risk envelope. While these are common in the literature, their impact on the applicability of the algorithms in more general settings is not fully discussed. Assumption 2.2, requiring smoothness of constraints, might be restrictive.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The actor-critic algorithm, while conceptually sound, appears quite complex to implement.  The need for nested sampling procedures (sampling trajectories *and* next states within each step) could be computationally expensive.
* **Convergence Analysis:** The convergence analysis of the actor-critic algorithm is mentioned but relegated to the supplementary material. Providing a more concise and clear summary of the convergence guarantees within the main paper would strengthen the contribution.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical illustration is valuable, but a more extensive empirical evaluation on a wider range of benchmark MDPs would bolster the claims of scalability and performance. Comparison to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms beyond the single example would be beneficial.
* **Clarity in Dynamic Case:**  The presentation of the dynamic case (Section 5) feels less intuitive than the static case (Section 4). The connection between the dynamic programming formulation and the policy gradient theorem could be made more explicit.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning – incorporating sophisticated risk preferences.
* The theoretical contributions are substantial, providing novel and general gradient formulas.
* The proposed algorithms offer a practical approach to tackling risk-sensitive optimization in large-scale settings.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented, demonstrating a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions, while standard, could limit the applicability of the algorithms.
* The complexity of the actor-critic algorithm may hinder its practical adoption.
* The empirical evaluation is limited, making it difficult to fully assess the performance and scalability of the approach.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The generality of the framework, the theoretical rigor of the derivations, and the practical potential of the algorithms are compelling. While the assumptions and implementation complexity are concerns, the strengths of the work outweigh these weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Discussion of Assumptions:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations imposed by the assumptions, and suggest potential avenues for relaxing them.
* **Simplification of Actor-Critic Algorithm:** Explore opportunities to simplify the actor-critic algorithm, potentially by reducing the number of sampling steps.
* **Expanded Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on a wider range of benchmark MDPs, including comparisons to existing risk-sensitive RL algorithms.
* **Clarify Dynamic Case:**  Improve the clarity of the presentation for the dynamic risk case, explicitly connecting the dynamic programming formulation with the policy gradient theorem.
* **Summary of Convergence Analysis:**  Include a concise summary of the convergence guarantees of the actor-critic algorithm in the main paper.
