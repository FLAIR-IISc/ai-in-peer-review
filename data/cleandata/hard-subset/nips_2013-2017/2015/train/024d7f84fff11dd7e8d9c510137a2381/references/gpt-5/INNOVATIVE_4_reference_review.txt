Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for risk-sensitive reinforcement learning that apply to the full class of coherent risk measures. For static risk, it derives a general gradient formula using the dual (risk-envelope) representation and the envelope theorem, enabling sampling-based estimators that combine likelihood ratios with solutions of a small convex program. It recovers known special cases (e.g., CVaR) and provides a general sample-average approximation (SAA) approach with a consistency result. For dynamic risk, the paper builds on Markov coherent (time-consistent) risk measures, relates the Bellman equation to robust MDPs, and proves a policy-gradient theorem expressing the gradient as an expectation under ξ-weighted transitions with a stage-wise term hθ. This yields an actor-critic algorithm that alternates between approximating the risk-sensitive value function and estimating the gradient via sampling and per-state convex programs. A small numerical illustration highlights how different risk measures induce qualitatively different policies.

This work unifies and generalizes several threads: risk-sensitive RL with specific criteria (exponential utility, mean-variance, CVaR) and dynamic coherent risk via robust MDPs. Unlike prior RL methods that handle one risk measure at a time, the proposed framework covers general coherent risks (including spectral and mean-semideviation) and both static and dynamic settings.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A general policy-gradient framework for coherent risk measures (static and dynamic). This is a clean and bold generalization beyond CVaR/mean-variance.
- Theoretical contributions: 
  - A concise gradient formula for static coherent risk via Lagrangian saddle points (Theorem 4.2) that recovers CVaR and new cases (e.g., mean-semideviation).
  - A practical SAA-based estimator with a consistency theorem under mild conditions.
  - A policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2) and an associated actor-critic scheme leveraging robust MDP equivalence.
- Methodological breadth: Works with general risk envelopes given in convex form; integrates sampling with convex optimization; aligns with existing policy-gradient machinery.
- Clarity of the main ideas: The role of the risk envelope and the envelope theorem is explained well; examples help anchor the general result.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality and scalability: The dynamic method requires (i) solving convex programs to obtain ξ and KKT multipliers per state (or per batch), and (ii) sampling from ξ-reweighted transitions and multiple next-state samples per state. This may be challenging in large/unknown-model, single-trajectory RL settings.
- Experimental validation: The empirical section is minimal and does not evaluate the dynamic actor-critic on standard RL benchmarks nor compare against robust MDP or CVaR-RL baselines. No wall-clock or sample-complexity evidence is provided.
- Assumptions and technical details: 
  - The finite probability space assumption simplifies analysis; extensions to general Lp spaces are claimed but not detailed.
  - Assumption 2.2 requires smooth dependence of constraints on Pθ; some coherent measures (e.g., CVaR at quantile points) can introduce nondifferentiabilities that deserve more discussion.
  - For the dynamic gradient theorem, conditions ensuring ergodicity/mixing under ξ-weighted transitions and variance control are not stated.
- Algorithmic details: Due to space, the actor-critic is only sketched; convergence guarantees with function approximation and the impact of errors in Vθ and ξ on the gradient estimator are relegated to the supplement.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound and is well-motivated. The use of the envelope theorem to pass gradients through the max over the risk envelope is appropriate, and the consistency of the SAA gradient estimator addresses potential bias from sample-based KKT multipliers. Some technical gaps (ergodicity, nondifferentiability) should be clarified. Empirical validation is currently too light to demonstrate practical viability for the dynamic case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with precise statements and helpful examples. The notation burden is nontrivial but standard for this area. Providing a concise pseudocode for the two-phase actor and the critic would improve readability.

Originality
High. The unified gradient calculus for coherent risks and the policy-gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures go beyond prior work focused on specific risk criteria.

Significance
High conceptually. If adopted, this framework could become a foundation for risk-sensitive RL across many choices of coherent risk. Practical impact will depend on addressing sampling and computational hurdles in large-scale, model-free settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add pseudocode and computational complexity for the static SAA estimator and the dynamic actor-critic (including per-iteration convex program sizes).
- Provide experiments on standard RL domains (e.g., gridworlds, inventory/queueing, control tasks) for dynamic risk, comparing against CVaR-RL and robust MDP baselines. Report sample efficiency and runtime.
- Discuss handling of single-trajectory, model-free settings; propose importance sampling or off-policy corrections to approximate ξ-weighted transitions without a generative model.
- Clarify conditions for existence/uniqueness/selectability of saddle points and behavior at nondifferentiable points (e.g., CVaR quantile ties).
- Analyze variance and stability of the gradient estimator; suggest variance reduction (e.g., baselines, control variates, likelihood ratio normalization).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, broadly applicable theoretical advance; unifies disparate risk-sensitive RL methods.
- Clean generalization with clear special cases (CVaR, mean-semideviation).
- Actor-critic path toward scalable algorithms and connection to robust MDPs.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation, especially for the dynamic case.
- Practical constraints (need for generative model, per-state convex programs) may hinder applicability.
- Some technical assumptions and conditions are stated but not explored in depth.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant, general framework likely to stimulate further research and practical methods for risk-sensitive RL. While experiments are light and some practical aspects remain open, the novelty and potential impact outweigh these limitations.
