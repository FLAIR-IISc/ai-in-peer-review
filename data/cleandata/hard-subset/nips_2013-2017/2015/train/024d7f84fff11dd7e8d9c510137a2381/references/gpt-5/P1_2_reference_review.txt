Summary
The paper develops policy-gradient methods for reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives, covering both static coherent risk of a random return and time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk in MDPs. The key technical ingredients are: (i) a general gradient formula for static coherent risk (Theorem 4.2) derived via the convex dual (risk-envelope) representation and Milgrom–Segal’s envelope theorem, yielding a likelihood-ratio estimator that uses the Lagrangian saddle point; (ii) a sample-average approximation (SAA) approach that computes the saddle point from a convex program and uses the associated multipliers to produce a consistent gradient estimator; (iii) a policy-gradient theorem for dynamic Markov coherent risk (Theorem 5.2), expressing ∇θVθ in terms of a value function Vθ and a modified trajectory distribution with worst-case reweighting ξ*, enabling an actor–critic scheme. The framework recovers existing special cases (e.g., CVaR, mean-semideviation), unifies them, and suggests practical sampling-based algorithms that combine estimation and convex optimization. A small portfolio-selection example illustrates the impact of choosing different coherent risk measures.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on the coherent risk representation (Artzner et al.; Ruszczyński & Shapiro) and the dynamic Markov coherent risk framework (Ruszczyński 2010). It generalizes specific RL treatments of risk such as mean-variance (Tamar et al.; Prashanth & Ghavamzadeh) and CVaR (Rockafellar & Uryasev; Tamar et al.) by providing a single derivation that applies to any coherent risk with a tractable risk envelope. The dynamic result connects to robust MDPs (Osogami; Petrik & Subramanian; Tamar et al. 2015) and leverages approximate dynamic programming for robust MDPs. The CVaR gradient formula obtained here subsumes prior derivations and holds under weaker conditions, including discrete distributions.

Strengths
- Generality and unification: The static and dynamic gradient formulas cover the entire class of coherent risk measures satisfying a mild risk-envelope structure. Previous results (e.g., CVaR) emerge as clean corollaries.
- Sound theory: The use of envelope theorem and dual representation is elegant; the SAA estimator’s consistency is established under reasonable assumptions; the dynamic policy-gradient theorem is a nontrivial extension of the classical theorem to time-consistent risk.
- Practical algorithmic path: The SAA approach gives a concrete procedure (sampling + convex program + multipliers) that is implementable with standard solvers. The dynamic actor–critic approach is well motivated and leverages robust MDP equivalence.
- Clarity of contribution: The paper clearly separates the static and dynamic cases, illustrates with CVaR and mean-semideviation, and discusses how the dynamic result can be used in an actor–critic.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and computational burden: The SAA gradient requires solving a convex program and retrieving KKT multipliers at each iteration (and per state in the dynamic case), which could be expensive for large problems and may limit scalability. Complexity and runtime trade-offs are not quantified.
- Sampling requirements: The dynamic theorem requires sampling from an MDP with ξ*-weighted transitions. This generally needs access to the model or a simulator capable of generating multiple next states per state-action and altering the transition kernel; this is stronger than the single-trajectory, model-free setting common in RL.
- Assumptions and scope: The exposition assumes a finite probability space and smooth dependence of constraints on Pθ; extensions to continuous spaces and function approximation are claimed but detailed proofs are deferred. Non-uniqueness of saddle points and the stability/continuity of selected ξ* and multipliers are not fully discussed.
- Limited empirical validation: Experiments are limited to a small static-risk portfolio example; there is no empirical demonstration of the dynamic actor–critic, its convergence, or comparisons with established baselines (e.g., CVaR policy gradient) on standard RL benchmarks.
- Missing details: Key algorithmic elements (critic with function approximation, two-phase sampling, importance sampling variants) are deferred to the supplement; pseudocode and ablations would enhance reproducibility.

Quality
The theoretical development appears technically sound and well grounded in convex analysis and policy-gradient methodology. Claims are appropriately scoped, and consistency guarantees are provided for the SAA estimator. However, empirical evidence is insufficient to assess practical performance and stability of the dynamic method.

Clarity
Overall, the paper is well-written and organized. Notation is heavy but standard. Suggestions:
- Provide high-level pseudocode for the static SAA estimator and the dynamic actor–critic (including how ξ* and multipliers are computed and used).
- Clarify computational complexity per gradient step and how often convex programs must be solved.
- Explain how to approximate sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions in model-free settings (e.g., importance sampling corrections).
- Discuss how non-unique saddle points are handled and whether the gradient is invariant to the choice.

Originality
High. The general policy-gradient expressions for coherent risk measures and the dynamic extension to Markov coherent risk appear novel and go beyond prior risk-specific treatments.

Significance
Moderate to high. The work provides a unifying theoretical framework that many researchers can build on. Its practical impact will depend on scalable implementations and broader empirical validation.

Questions for the authors
- How do you implement sampling from ξ*-weighted transitions without a generative model? Can off-policy corrections be used robustly?
- What are the computational costs (per iteration/state) for solving the convex programs and obtaining KKT multipliers in large MDPs?
- How sensitive is the SAA estimator to the sample size N and solver tolerances? Any variance-reduction strategies beyond importance sampling?
- Can you provide experiments for the dynamic actor–critic on standard benchmarks and comparisons to CVaR-specific methods?

Pros
- General, elegant theory that unifies risk-sensitive RL across coherent risks.
- Recovers and simplifies known results (e.g., CVaR gradient).
- Provides a consistent, implementable estimator via SAA.
- Extends policy-gradient theorem to time-consistent dynamic risk.

Cons
- Strong sampling/model-access assumptions; unclear feasibility in standard RL settings.
- Heavy computational overhead (convex programs, multipliers).
- Sparse empirical validation; dynamic method not demonstrated.
- Some assumptions (finite Ω, smooth envelopes) and algorithmic details deferred.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a solid, general theoretical contribution with clear novelty and potential impact, but empirical validation and practicality could be strengthened. I encourage acceptance provided the authors clarify computational aspects and, if possible, add experimental evidence for the dynamic algorithm.
