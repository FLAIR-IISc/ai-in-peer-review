

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

## Summary
This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient algorithms for the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (from Artzner et al.) to derive new gradient formulas for both static and dynamic risk measures. Their key contributions include: (1) a new gradient formula for static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, (2) a general algorithm for static risk involving sampling with convex programming, and (3) a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk with an actor-critic algorithm. The work generalizes and unifies previous approaches to risk-sensitive RL, as demonstrated through theoretical connections and a numerical example showing how different coherent risk measures lead to different policy choices in portfolio optimization.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations. The authors properly establish the connection between coherent risk measures and their representation as worst-case expectations (Theorem 2.1), then build upon this to derive their gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2). The consistency result (Proposition 4.4) provides a solid theoretical basis for their sampling-based approach. The numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical significance of their framework by showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a portfolio optimization setting. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the need for convex programming in the gradient estimation) and makes clear assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2.2 about the risk envelope), which is appropriate for a theoretical contribution. The work appears to be complete, not merely a position paper, with well-supported claims.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the broader context of risk-sensitive RL. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors use concrete examples (CVaR and mean-semideviation) to illustrate their general framework, making the theoretical contributions more accessible. The connection to prior work is clearly articulated, showing how their approach generalizes previous results. The paper is structured logically, progressing from problem formulation to theoretical results to numerical validation. The supplementary material appears to provide the necessary details for reproducibility.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by unifying risk-sensitive RL across the entire class of coherent risk measures. Previous work had focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR [Chow et al.], mean-variance [Tamar et al.]), requiring separate algorithmic development for each. The authors demonstrate that their framework generalizes and simplifies these previous approaches. The key novelty lies in their gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) and its extension to dynamic risk (Theorem 5.2), which were not previously known for the general class of coherent risk measures. The paper properly cites related work and clearly articulates how their contribution differs from prior art, making it distinct from previous NIPS publications on risk-sensitive RL.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. By providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures in RL, the paper enables researchers to design risk-averse policies without having to develop new algorithms for each specific risk measure. The numerical example effectively demonstrates the practical importance of selecting the appropriate risk measure for a problem (showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a portfolio optimization setting). The connection to model uncertainty (in the conclusion) suggests broader implications for robust decision-making under model misspecification, which could be a fruitful direction for future work. The paper advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL by providing a theoretically grounded, general approach that encompasses previous specific methods.

## Strengths
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-supported claims
- Clear unification of previous work on specific risk measures
- Practical demonstration of the framework's utility in a numerical example
- Proper acknowledgment of limitations and assumptions
- Well-written with logical organization and clear notation

## Weaknesses
- The computational cost of the convex programming step in the gradient estimation may be prohibitive for very large problems (though the paper acknowledges this as a limitation)
- The paper does not provide extensive empirical comparisons with state-of-the-art risk-sensitive RL methods on standard benchmarks
- The connection to practical applications beyond the portfolio example could be strengthened

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by providing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The theoretical results are sound, the presentation is clear, and the work represents a meaningful advance over previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures. While the computational cost of the convex programming step is a limitation, the paper properly acknowledges this. The significance of the work is high, as it enables practitioners to select and implement appropriate risk measures without having to develop new algorithms for each specific measure.

I recommend acceptance. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is of high quality with strong theoretical foundations, clearly written, original in its unified approach, and significant for the field. The work represents a meaningful step forward in risk-sensitive RL and will likely be influential for future research in this area.

## Pro/Con Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Unifies previous work on risk-sensitive RL under a single coherent framework
- Provides strong theoretical results with clear connections to prior work
- Demonstrates practical utility through a meaningful numerical example
- Makes clear theoretical contributions to risk measure theory in RL

**Con:**
- Computational cost of convex programming may limit scalability
- Limited empirical comparison with state-of-the-art methods
- Connection to broader practical applications could be strengthened

Overall, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of reinforcement learning.
