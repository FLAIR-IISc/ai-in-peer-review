This paper presents a general policy gradient framework for optimizing coherent risk measures in reinforcement learning. The core contribution is the derivation of gradient formulas for both static coherent risk and dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk. The authors leverage the dual representation of coherent risk measures, which expresses any such measure as a worst-case expectation over a set of test distributions (the risk envelope). By applying the envelope theorem to this dual formulation, they derive a general formula for the policy gradient. This framework is shown to unify and generalize previous work that developed specialized algorithms for individual risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance. For the general static case where an analytical solution is unavailable, the authors propose a two-step algorithm that combines sample average approximation (SAA) with convex programming to estimate the gradient. For the dynamic case, they present a risk-sensitive policy gradient theorem analogous to the standard one, leading to a proposed actor-critic algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Unification:** The primary strength of this work is its generality. It provides a single, principled framework that encompasses the entire class of coherent risk measures. This is a significant conceptual contribution that moves beyond the ad-hoc, measure-by-measure approach prevalent in the literature. The use of the dual representation and the envelope theorem is elegant and methodologically sound.

2.  **Novel Gradient Formulas:** Theorem 4.2 presents a novel and general formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure. The ability of this formula to cleanly re-derive the known gradient for CVaR (Section 4.1) serves as a strong validation of the framework's correctness and power. Similarly, Theorem 5.2 provides a valuable theoretical result by extending the policy gradient theorem to the dynamic Markov coherent risk setting.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem, introduces the necessary preliminaries on coherent risk, and builds its case from the static to the more complex dynamic setting.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of rigorous empirical evaluation. The claims of developing new, general *algorithms* are not substantiated by experiments that demonstrate their efficacy.
    *   The sole numerical illustration (Section 6) is a one-step bandit problem, not a sequential decision-making problem. This fails to test the main contribution for the dynamic setting (Section 5), including the proposed actor-critic algorithm.
    *   The experiment's goal is to highlight the importance of choosing an appropriate risk measure, which is a valid conceptual point. However, it does not benchmark the proposed algorithm's performance (e.g., sample efficiency, convergence speed, final policy quality) against any established risk-sensitive RL baselines on standard environments.
    *   The general gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3), which relies on solving a convex program at each step, is not evaluated at all in the main paper. Its computational cost, scalability with the number of samples (N), and practical performance compared to specialized algorithms remain entirely unaddressed.

2.  **Omission of Key Algorithmic Details:** The paper proposes an actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem but defers almost all details to the supplementary material due to "space limitation." The description of the critic (modifying an algorithm from [31]) and the actor (a two-phase sampling procedure) is too high-level to allow for a proper assessment of its novelty, complexity, or practicality. A paper introducing a new algorithm should provide sufficient detail for the reader to understand its mechanics and potential challenges.

3.  **Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The proposed general method for static risk requires solving a convex optimization problem to estimate the gradient at each policy update step. While convex programming is efficient in principle, embedding it within a stochastic optimization loop could be computationally prohibitive for large sample sizes (N) or complex risk envelopes. The paper does not discuss this crucial practical limitation or compare its computational overhead to existing methods.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The paper provides a strong, novel, and elegant theoretical framework that unifies policy gradient methods for a broad and important class of risk measures.
    *   The derived gradient formulas are significant theoretical results that advance our understanding of risk-sensitive optimization in RL.
    *   The work has the potential to be highly influential by providing a foundation for future research in general risk-sensitive algorithms.

*   **Arguments against Acceptance:**
    *   The empirical validation is critically weak and does not support the paper's algorithmic claims. The experiments do not test the dynamic risk algorithm and fail to benchmark against any relevant baselines.
    *   The paper feels incomplete as a contribution to an empirical field like RL. The lack of evidence makes it difficult to judge whether the proposed algorithms are practical or offer any advantage over existing, more specialized methods.
    *   Crucial details of the actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic case are omitted, preventing a full review of one of the paper's main contributions.

In its current form, the paper presents a compelling theoretical idea but falls short of the standards for a complete NIPS paper due to the profound lack of empirical validation. The theoretical contributions are sound, but without evidence of their practical utility, the significance of the work is diminished. The proposed algorithms, particularly the general SAA-based one and the dynamic actor-critic method, must be implemented and tested on non-trivial sequential decision-making problems against appropriate baselines to demonstrate their value.
