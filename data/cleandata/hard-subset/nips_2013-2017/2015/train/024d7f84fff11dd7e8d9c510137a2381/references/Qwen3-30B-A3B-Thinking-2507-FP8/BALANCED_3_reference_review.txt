

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper addresses risk-sensitive reinforcement learning by developing policy gradient algorithms for the entire class of coherent risk measures. The authors present a unified framework that generalizes previous approaches focusing on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance), providing theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms for both static and dynamic coherent risk measures. The work builds on the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) and leverages convex programming to derive sampling-based gradient estimators.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its theoretical unification of risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. The authors successfully derive a general gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) that encompasses specific risk measures like CVaR and mean-semideviation as special cases. This generalization is significant, as previous work has focused on individual risk measures without establishing a common theoretical foundation. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by extending the policy gradient theorem to Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2), which is particularly important for dynamic decision-making problems. The numerical example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates the practical implications of their framework, showing how different risk measures lead to different policy choices in a financial trading scenario. The connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (Section 7) is insightful and points to promising future research directions.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main limitations concern practical implementation and scalability. The proposed algorithms require solving convex optimization problems for each gradient estimate, which may be computationally intensive for large-scale problems. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity or sample complexity, making it difficult to assess how the approach scales compared to existing methods. While the authors mention that their approach can be applied to large MDPs, they do not provide empirical evidence of this scalability beyond the simple numerical example. Additionally, the paper restricts attention to finite probability spaces, which may limit applicability to continuous domains, though the authors note this can be extended (with omitted details).

The numerical illustration (Section 6) is somewhat limited in scope. It demonstrates how different risk measures lead to different policy choices but does not compare the computational efficiency or sample efficiency of the proposed method against existing approaches. The paper also doesn't address how the choice of risk envelope (U(Pθ)) affects the performance of the algorithms, which is crucial for practitioners.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs of the main theorems are rigorous (though relegated to the supplementary material), and the authors carefully address the assumptions required for their approach. The numerical example provides empirical validation of the framework's practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from preliminaries to problem formulation to results. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The connection between coherent risk measures and previous work is clearly articulated.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution by providing a unified framework for risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures. While previous work has considered specific risk measures (CVaR, mean-variance), this is the first to provide a general method applicable to the entire class of coherent risk measures.

**Significance:** The results are important for both researchers and practitioners in risk-sensitive RL. The framework provides flexibility in designing risk criteria tailored to specific problem characteristics, as demonstrated in the numerical example. The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) suggests broader applicability beyond risk-sensitive RL.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning and provides a valuable unified framework. However, the authors should address the following points to strengthen the paper:

1. Provide more detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability, including empirical comparisons with existing methods on larger problems.

2. Expand the numerical example to include a more comprehensive comparison with existing risk-sensitive RL methods, focusing on computational efficiency and sample complexity.

3. Discuss the practical considerations for selecting the risk envelope U(Pθ) in different application domains.

4. Address the finite probability space limitation more thoroughly, discussing potential extensions to continuous domains.

The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions, it would provide a valuable resource for researchers and practitioners working on risk-sensitive decision-making. The unification of risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures is a major step forward, and the proposed algorithms offer a practical pathway to implementing risk-sensitive policies in a wide range of applications.
