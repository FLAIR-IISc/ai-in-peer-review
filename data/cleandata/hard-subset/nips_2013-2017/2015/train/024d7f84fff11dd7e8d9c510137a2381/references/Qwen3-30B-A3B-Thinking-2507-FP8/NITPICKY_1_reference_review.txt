

# Review of "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary
This paper proposes policy gradient algorithms for reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, addressing both static coherent risk and dynamic Markov coherent risk. The authors derive a new gradient formula for static coherent risk, an algorithm for gradient estimation using sampling and convex programming, and a policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk. The paper claims to generalize previous work on risk-sensitive RL by unifying approaches for various risk measures.

## Strengths
The paper identifies an important gap in risk-sensitive RL research by proposing a unified framework for coherent risk measures. The theoretical foundation using the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) is well-chosen and appropriately cited. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the flexibility of risk criteria in selecting appropriate policies.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
1. **Critical error in Proposition 4.3**: The claim "with ∇θE[Z] = E[∇θ logP(ω)Z]" is mathematically incorrect. The correct identity should include an additional term: ∇θE[Z] = E[∇θ logP(ω)Z] + E[Z]∇θ log P(ω). This fundamental mistake undermines the validity of the gradient formula for the mean-semideviation risk measure.

2. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. In Theorem 4.2, the gradient formula contains "logP(ω)" without indicating the dependence on θ (it should be logPθ(ω)). In Section 4.1, the CVaR formula references [30] (Bardou et al.), but [30] appears to be a different paper than the one the authors intend to cite.

3. **Incorrect citation**: In Section 4.1, the authors claim their CVaR gradient formula "was recently proved in [30]" but [30] is cited as "Computing VaR and CVaR using stochastic approximation..." (Bardou et al., 2009), which is not a CVaR policy gradient paper. This misattribution creates confusion.

4. **Unclear definition**: The risk-sensitive value function is defined as Vθ(x) = ρ∞(M|x0 = x) (Section 5), but ρ∞(M) was previously defined for the whole MDP, not conditional on initial state. The paper fails to clarify this crucial distinction.

5. **Misleading claim about stochastic dominance**: In Section 6, the paper claims "A3 stochastically dominates A1" with reference [12], but the example doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. A3 has a Pareto distribution with infinite variance, while A1 is normal, making stochastic dominance non-trivial to establish.

### Methodological and Presentation Issues
1. **Incomplete algorithm description**: The paper claims to present a "sampling-based estimator" (Equation 8) but fails to provide a complete algorithm with pseudocode or clear implementation details. The "two-phase sampling procedure" described in Section 5 is vague and lacks critical implementation details.

2. **Missing theoretical guarantees**: The paper claims to provide a "consistency result" (Proposition 4.4) but lacks convergence rate analysis or sample complexity bounds, which are crucial for practical implementation.

3. **Inconsistent reference formatting**: The references contain numerous formatting errors, including missing years, extra commas, and inconsistent page number formats (e.g., "203– 228," with a space before the dash).

4. **Unclear contribution**: The paper claims to "generalize and unify previous approaches," but fails to provide a detailed comparison with existing work on dynamic coherent risk (e.g., [20], [9]) that addresses the same problem.

5. **Technical oversight**: The paper assumes "finite probability spaces" in Section 2 but later discusses "continuous distributions" in Section 4.1 without clarifying how the results extend to continuous cases.

### Minor but Significant Issues
1. **Typographical errors**: The paper contains numerous minor typographical errors, such as "policies on a state space augmented with accumulated cost" (should be "policy" or "policies" depending on context) and inconsistent use of "risk envelope" versus "risk-envelope."

2. **Supplementary material dependence**: The paper repeatedly references "the supplementary material" for proofs and algorithms, but the supplementary material is not provided, making it impossible to verify the claims.

3. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims to "rederive them in greater generality and simplicity" for previous results, but doesn't demonstrate this clearly through concrete examples or comparisons.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an important problem in risk-sensitive RL and has a theoretically sound foundation, the technical errors (particularly in Proposition 4.3), inconsistent notation, and unclear presentation significantly undermine the validity of the proposed methods. The paper contains multiple fundamental mathematical mistakes that would prevent replication of the results. The numerous formatting and citation errors further diminish the paper's credibility.

The paper's claim of unifying previous approaches is not substantiated by clear comparisons with existing work. The numerical example lacks sufficient analysis to support the main claims, and the proposed algorithms are not described with sufficient detail for implementation.

## Recommendation

**Reject**. The paper contains multiple fundamental technical errors that invalidate key results, particularly in Proposition 4.3. The numerous inconsistencies in notation, references, and presentation further reduce the paper's quality. While the problem addressed is important, the paper's technical shortcomings prevent it from making a valid contribution to the field. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to correct these fundamental errors before it could be considered for publication.
