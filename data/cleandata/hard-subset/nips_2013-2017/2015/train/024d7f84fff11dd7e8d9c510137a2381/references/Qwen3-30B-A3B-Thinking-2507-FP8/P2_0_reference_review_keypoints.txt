- Paper proposes a unified policy gradient framework for reinforcement learning under coherent risk measures, using the representation theorem to derive sampling-based gradient estimators for static and dynamic settings.
- Introduces new gradient formulas and sampling-with-convex-programming algorithms for static risk and a generalized policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk.
- Theoretical development is rigorous, leveraging the representation and envelope theorems with clear assumptions and complete proof sketches.
- Original contribution: unifies prior risk-sensitive RL work (CVaR, mean-variance, mean-semideviation) under a general coherent risk framework and connects to robust MDPs.
- Significance: addresses important real-world risk-sensitive RL needs and enables practitioners to choose problem-dependent risk measures; numerical example illustrates differing policy outcomes.
- Clarity: paper is well-written, well-organized, and uses consistent notation with helpful concrete examples.
- Weakness: empirical validation limited to a single numerical example; lacks broader experiments and comparisons of sample efficiency or computational cost versus existing methods.
- Weakness: practical limitations not fully explored, notably the simulator requirement for multiple transitions and scalability to large RL problems.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of computational complexity and how convex optimization costs scale with problem size and samples.
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution that generalizes prior work and merits acceptance despite limited empirical evaluation.
- Recommendation: accept — theoretical advances and unification justify publication even though more experiments would strengthen practical relevance.
- Suggested improvements: add broader empirical comparisons, analyze computational complexity and scalability, address simulator/feasibility limitations, and discuss integration with deep or model-based RL.
- Pro: solid, general theoretical framework that unifies prior specialized results and includes a clear numerical demonstration.
- Con: limited empirical validation, incomplete treatment of computational feasibility, and lack of comparisons to existing methods.
