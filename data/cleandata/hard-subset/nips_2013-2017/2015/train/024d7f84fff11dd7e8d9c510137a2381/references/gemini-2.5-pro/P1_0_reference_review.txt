### **Summary**

This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk objectives. The authors tackle two distinct but related problems: optimizing a static coherent risk measure of the total discounted return, and optimizing a time-consistent dynamic Markov coherent risk measure. The core contribution is a novel formula for the policy gradient of a static coherent risk measure (Theorem 4.2), derived by leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk and the Envelope Theorem. This formula expresses the gradient in terms of the optimal solution to a dual problem.

Based on this theorem, the paper proposes a general, two-step gradient estimation algorithm for static risk: first, use sample average approximation (SAA) to solve a convex program that approximates the risk and its dual variables; second, plug these estimates into the new gradient formula. The authors provide a consistency result for this estimator. They demonstrate that their framework elegantly recovers and simplifies previous results for specific risk measures like Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) and provides a new gradient formula for mean-semideviation.

For the dynamic risk setting, the paper derives a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (Theorem 5.2), which generalizes the standard policy gradient theorem. This result forms the basis of a proposed actor-critic algorithm where the critic estimates a risk-sensitive value function (by solving a robust Bellman equation) and the actor updates the policy using a gradient estimate that involves sampling from a modified MDP.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Generality:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. By moving from specific risk measures (like variance or CVaR) to the entire class of coherent risk measures, it provides a powerful and unified framework. This is a major step forward, as coherent risk measures are axiomatically well-founded and widely used in other fields like finance. The ability to handle any coherent risk measure given its dual representation is a substantial increase in flexibility for RL practitioners.

2.  **Technical Quality and Novelty:** The theoretical development is elegant and sound. The key insight of using the dual representation of coherent risk combined with the Envelope Theorem to derive the policy gradient is novel and powerful. This approach not only yields a general formula but also provides a principled way to re-derive and simplify existing results for specific measures like CVaR, as demonstrated in Section 4.1. The extension to dynamic risk via a new policy gradient theorem is also a strong theoretical contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides a clear motivation, introduces the necessary mathematical preliminaries on coherent risk measures, and logically builds its case from the static risk problem to the more complex dynamic risk problem. The examples for CVaR and mean-semideviation are effective in illustrating the power and application of the main theoretical result (Theorem 4.2).

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness is the lack of compelling experimental results. The numerical illustration in Section 6 is a simple stateless bandit problem. While it effectively demonstrates *why* different risk measures are useful (the conceptual motivation), it does not validate the proposed *algorithms*. There are no experiments showing the learning dynamics, convergence, or sample efficiency of the proposed SAA-based gradient estimator or, more importantly, the full actor-critic algorithm in an actual MDP setting. The claim of similar performance between the SAA and analytical estimators for CVaR is relegated to the supplement, weakening the main paper's empirical support.

2.  **Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The proposed gradient estimation algorithm for the general case requires solving a convex optimization problem at each step of the SGD update (or for each batch). The complexity of this step scales with the number of samples `N` and the complexity of the risk envelope `U`. For large `N` (needed for accurate gradient estimates, especially for tail-sensitive risks), this could be a significant computational bottleneck. The paper does not discuss this potential scalability issue.

3.  **Incomplete Description of the Dynamic Risk Algorithm:** The actor-critic algorithm for the dynamic risk problem is only sketched out, with crucial details deferred to a "full paper version" in the supplementary material. For instance, the actor requires sampling from an MDP with "twisted" transition probabilities `P(·|x)ξ*`, and the `hθ(x,a)` term involves further expectations. The practical implementation, challenges, and variance properties of such a procedure are non-trivial and their omission from the main text makes it difficult to fully assess the contribution for the dynamic case. The algorithm also relies on a simulator that can provide multiple next-state samples from any state, which is a stronger assumption than standard single-trajectory RL settings.

### **Evaluation and Justification**

*   **Quality:** The theoretical work is of high quality. The derivations are sound and the connections to duality theory are elegant. However, the work feels incomplete due to the lack of sufficient algorithmic details (for the dynamic case) and convincing experimental validation.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow for a reader with the necessary background.
*   **Originality:** High. The core idea of a general policy gradient for any coherent risk measure is novel and represents a significant generalization of prior art.
*   **Significance:** High. The results are important and likely to be built upon by researchers in risk-sensitive RL, robust optimization, and related fields. It provides a foundational tool for a much broader exploration of risk in sequential decision-making.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a strong, novel, and elegant theoretical foundation for policy gradients with a very general and important class of risk measures.
*   Unifies and simplifies several disparate prior results, demonstrating the power of the proposed framework.
*   The work is likely to be highly influential and spur further research in risk-sensitive RL.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is minimal and does not support the efficacy of the proposed algorithms in a standard RL task.
*   Key details of the main algorithm for the dynamic case are omitted from the paper, making the contribution feel preliminary.
*   Potential computational issues with the proposed methods are not addressed.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the weaknesses in the experimental section and the incomplete algorithmic description for the dynamic case, the theoretical contribution of this paper is strong, novel, and significant enough to warrant acceptance. The unified framework for coherent risk is a major step forward for the field. The authors should be strongly encouraged to expand the experimental section and bring more algorithmic details into the main paper for the final version.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  Strengthen the experimental section by applying the proposed algorithms (both the static SAA-based one and the dynamic actor-critic one) to a standard benchmark MDP (e.g., Gridworld, CartPole). This would demonstrate the algorithms' learning behavior and provide a much-needed validation of the theoretical results.
2.  Provide more detail on the actor-critic algorithm in the main text. Briefly explain how the critic is trained and how the sampling for the actor update is practically implemented, and discuss the associated challenges (e.g., variance).
3.  Add a brief discussion on the computational complexity of solving the convex program in the SAA estimator and potential ways to manage it (e.g., mini-batching, stochastic convex optimization methods).
