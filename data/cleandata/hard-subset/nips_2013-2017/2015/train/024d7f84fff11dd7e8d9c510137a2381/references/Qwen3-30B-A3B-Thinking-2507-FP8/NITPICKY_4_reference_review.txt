

# Review: Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures

This paper proposes a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures, both static and dynamic. The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures to derive gradient formulas that can be implemented via sampling and convex programming. The work generalizes previous approaches that focused on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance, and provides a principled way to handle the whole class of coherent risk measures.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a theoretical foundation that unifies risk-sensitive RL under the coherent risk framework. The gradient formulas derived in Theorems 4.2 and 5.2 are theoretically sound and build upon solid foundations (e.g., the representation theorem for coherent risk measures). The numerical illustration in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the importance of flexible risk criteria for different problem characteristics. The paper is well-organized and the technical exposition is generally clear.

## Weaknesses

Despite the strong theoretical contribution, the paper contains several critical issues that undermine its technical quality and reproducibility:

1. **Notational inconsistencies**: Multiple instances of missing spaces in mathematical expressions (e.g., "ξPθ" instead of "ξPθ"), inconsistent use of brackets (e.g., "E [Z]" vs. "E[Z]"), and missing closing parentheses in Theorem 4.2. These minor errors create confusion and suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

2. **Critical error in numerical example**: The paper states that "Policy π2 is risk-averse and had a mean-semideviation objective maxθ E[Z] - SD[Z]". However, the mean-semideviation risk measure is defined as ρMSD(Z;α) = E[Z] + αSD[Z] (Proposition 4.3), so the objective should be minθ ρMSD(Z;α), not maxθ E[Z] - SD[Z]. This is a fundamental error that misrepresents the approach.

3. **Unjustified assumptions**: In Section 4.3, the paper assumes "a solution to (7) may be computed efficiently using standard convex programming tools" without justification. For large-scale problems (which the paper claims to address), solving the convex optimization problem (7) with O(N) variables and constraints would be computationally prohibitive, contradicting the paper's claim of scalability.

4. **Ambiguous notation**: The definition of the Markov coherent risk measure in Equation (3) is ambiguous. It's unclear what ρ represents in each iteration of the nested expression. The paper should clarify whether ρ is the same static risk measure at each time step, or if it varies.

5. **Inconsistent risk measure formulation**: In Section 2.1, the paper states "ρ(Z) = max ξ : ξPθ∈U(Pθ) Eξ[Z]" but the notation should be "ρ(Z) = max_{ξ: ξPθ ∈ U(Pθ)} Eξ[Z]" with proper subscript notation. The missing space in "ξPθ∈U(Pθ)" creates confusion.

6. **Mischaracterization of risk measures**: The paper states "For Markov coherent risk, the class of optimal policies is stationary Markov [24], while this is not necessarily true for static risk." This is misleading because Markov coherent risk is a specific type of dynamic risk measure, not a separate risk measure. The paper should clarify this distinction.

7. **Missing technical details**: The paper claims "we show that sampling may indeed be used in both cases to devise suitable estimators" but provides no detailed analysis of the computational complexity or sample complexity of the proposed estimators.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting unification of risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures, the technical quality is undermined by multiple notational inconsistencies, a critical error in the numerical example, and unjustified assumptions about computational scalability. The paper's claim of handling "large or continuous MDPs" is not supported by the proposed algorithm's computational complexity.

The paper would be significantly stronger with careful attention to these technical details. As it stands, the inconsistencies and errors make it difficult to evaluate the proposed methods' practical utility and theoretical soundness. These issues are not trivial and would likely cause confusion for readers attempting to implement or build upon the work.

## Recommendation

**Reject** - The paper has a strong theoretical foundation but contains multiple critical technical errors and inconsistencies that prevent it from being a quality NIPS contribution. The numerical example's fundamental error in the risk objective formulation, combined with the unjustified computational assumptions, significantly undermine the paper's credibility. While the core idea is interesting, the execution is flawed and would require substantial revision to be acceptable for publication.
