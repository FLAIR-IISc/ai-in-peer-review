This paper presents a general framework for policy gradient algorithms in reinforcement learning problems with coherent risk objectives. It generalizes and unifies prior work that has focused on specific risk measures. The main contributions are: (1) a novel formula for the gradient of a static coherent risk measure, derived using the dual representation of such measures and the envelope theorem; (2) a sample-based algorithm for estimating this gradient that combines sample average approximation with convex programming; (3) a policy gradient theorem for dynamic, time-consistent Markov coherent risk measures; and (4) an outline of an actor-critic algorithm based on this theorem.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generality and Unification:** The primary strength of this work is its ambition and success in providing a unified theoretical framework for a broad and important class of risk measures. By leveraging the dual representation of coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1), the authors derive results applicable to any risk measure within this class, provided its risk envelope is known. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous papers that developed bespoke methods for individual measures like CVaR or mean-variance.
2.  **Novel Theoretical Approach:** The application of the envelope theorem to the Lagrangian of the dual formulation of the risk measure (Theorem 4.2) is an elegant and powerful technique. It provides a clean, general formula for the policy gradient that neatly separates the policy-dependent parts from the risk-measure-specific parts (which are captured by the saddle point of the Lagrangian). This is a solid theoretical contribution.
3.  **Significance:** Risk-sensitive RL is a crucial area with applications in finance, robotics, and other domains where worst-case performance or cost variability matters. This paper makes this class of methods more accessible by providing a single algorithmic template, potentially accelerating research and application in this area.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising theoretical core, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws, omissions, and inconsistencies that collectively undermine its quality and completeness.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:**
    *   The notation surrounding the risk envelope is confusing. Theorem 2.1 states `max_{ξ: ξPθ ∈ U(Pθ)}`, while Assumption 2.2 defines `U(Pθ)` as `{ ξPθ : ... }`. This seems circular or redundant. It should be clarified whether the set `U` consists of densities `ξ` or measures `ξPθ`. This ambiguity makes the subsequent formulas harder to parse.
    *   Several important claims are made in footnotes without justification. For instance, Footnote 1 claims results "may easily be extended" to more general cost structures, and Footnote 2 claims extension to history-dependent policies. These are non-trivial claims that require more than a passing mention.
    *   Footnote 3 mentions that the risk envelope `U` depends on the state `x` in the MDP setting. This is a critical detail for the dynamic risk section but is relegated to a footnote, making the transition from static to dynamic risk less clear than it should be.
    *   In the definition of `hθ(x, a)` in Theorem 5.2, the arguments of the derivatives `dfi/dp` and `dge/dp` are not specified. One must infer that `p` refers to the transition probabilities `P(x'|x,a)`. Such imprecision hinders reproducibility.

2.  **Omission of Critical Details:**
    *   The paper heavily relies on the supplementary material for nearly all proofs, algorithmic details, and analysis. While this is common, the extent here is excessive. The entire actor-critic algorithm for the main dynamic risk problem is only sketched at a high level, with the full details, including the critic's function approximation scheme and the actor's two-phase sampling procedure, deferred. This makes the second half of the paper difficult to evaluate as a self-contained contribution.
    *   The proposed general gradient estimation algorithm (Section 4.3) requires solving a convex program (Eq. 7) for *every gradient step*. The computational complexity of this inner loop, which depends on the number of samples `N`, is not discussed. This is a major practical concern that is completely ignored. A statement that it can be solved "efficiently" is insufficient.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:**
    *   The paper's single numerical illustration (Section 6) is on a stateless multi-armed bandit problem, not an MDP. Its purpose is to make a conceptual point about the choice of risk measures, not to validate the proposed algorithms.
    *   There is absolutely no empirical evaluation of the main algorithmic contribution for the dynamic setting—the actor-critic algorithm. Without an experiment showing that this algorithm can successfully solve a risk-sensitive MDP, the practical utility of Theorem 5.2 remains purely theoretical.
    *   For the static case, the paper mentions in the text that a comparison between the SAA estimator and an analytical one for CVaR is in the supplement. This is a key validation of the general approach and should have been included in the main paper. As it stands, there is no evidence in the paper itself to demonstrate that the SAA-based estimator is effective or efficient.

4.  **Strength of Assumptions:**
    *   Assumption 2.2 is quite strong. It requires an explicit convex programming formulation of the risk envelope and twice differentiability of the constraint functions with respect to the underlying probabilities. The authors should discuss the scope and limitations of these assumptions more thoroughly.
    *   The list of conditions for the consistency of the SAA estimator (Proposition 4.4) is long and technical. Describing them as "rather mild" seems optimistic without more justification or examples of when they might fail.

**Summary of Pros and Cons**

*   **Pro:** Provides a highly general and elegant unifying framework for policy gradients with coherent risk measures.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical derivation based on the dual representation and the envelope theorem is novel and insightful.
*   **Con:** The paper is marred by numerous instances of imprecise notation and unclear writing.
*   **Con:** Critical algorithmic details and all proofs are deferred to supplementary material, making the paper incomplete.
*   **Con:** The computational cost of the proposed general algorithm is not analyzed.
*   **Con:** The experimental validation is critically insufficient, with no results for the main dynamic risk algorithm and only a conceptual example for the static case.

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is built on a strong and elegant theoretical foundation, the execution is not up to the standards of NIPS. The lack of clarity in several key areas, the omission of crucial algorithmic details, and, most importantly, the near-complete absence of meaningful empirical validation make this feel like an incomplete piece of work. The combination of many "minor" flaws amounts to a major problem in assessing the paper's contribution and reproducibility. The ideas are certainly promising, but the paper requires a major revision to address these significant shortcomings before it is ready for publication.
