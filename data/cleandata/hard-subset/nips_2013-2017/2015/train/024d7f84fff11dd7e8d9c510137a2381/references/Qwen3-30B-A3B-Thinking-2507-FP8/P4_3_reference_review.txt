

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors generalize previous work on risk-sensitive RL, which has typically focused on specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR, mean-variance), to the entire class of coherent risk measures. The paper makes two main theoretical contributions: (1) a new formula for the gradient of static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation, and (2) a new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures, along with a corresponding actor-critic algorithm. The work builds on the theoretical foundation of coherent risk measures (Artzner et al., 1999) and extends it to sequential decision problems.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide rigorous mathematical formulations, theorems, and proofs for their gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2). The consistency of their sampling-based gradient estimators is established under reasonable assumptions (Proposition 4.4), and the paper carefully addresses the relationship between their approach and previous work. The theoretical framework is well-constructed, and the authors demonstrate how previous results (e.g., for CVaR) are special cases of their general approach. The numerical illustration in Section 6 provides a practical demonstration of the framework's utility, showing how different risk measures lead to different optimal policies in a financial trading scenario. However, the paper lacks detailed implementation details for the proposed algorithms, particularly the actor-critic method for dynamic risk, which limits the practical reproducibility of the approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the theorems are well-motivated with concrete examples (CVaR and mean-semideviation). The authors effectively demonstrate how their general approach unifies previous work. The writing is accessible to experts in RL and risk theory, and the paper provides sufficient information for a reader to understand the theoretical contributions. The only significant clarity issue is the omission of implementation details for the proposed algorithms, which would be valuable for practitioners.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by generalizing risk-sensitive RL to the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific risk metrics. The key novelty lies in the theoretical gradient formulas (Theorems 4.2 and 5.2), which are not present in previous work. The authors correctly acknowledge that previous results are special cases of their framework and demonstrate this through examples. The paper's connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty (Section 7) is also novel and potentially impactful for future work. While the concept of coherent risk measures is not new, their application to RL in this unified framework represents a meaningful advance.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in RL: the need for flexible risk-sensitive decision making. The ability to handle a broad class of risk measures rather than being limited to specific ones provides practitioners with greater flexibility in designing risk preferences for their applications. The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of this flexibility, showing how different risk measures lead to different optimal policies in a realistic financial setting. The connection to model uncertainty (Section 7) suggests potential broader applications beyond the specific RL context. While the paper does not claim to solve all challenges in risk-sensitive RL, it advances the state of the art by providing a general framework that encompasses previous approaches.

**Strengths:**
- Provides a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL with coherent risk measures
- Rigorous mathematical development of gradient formulas for static and dynamic risk
- Demonstrates how previous results are special cases of the general approach
- Offers a practical numerical illustration showing the value of risk measure flexibility
- Makes a promising connection between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty

**Weaknesses:**
- Lacks detailed implementation description for the proposed algorithms (particularly the actor-critic method)
- Makes strong assumptions about the form of the risk envelope (Assumption 2.2)
- Limited empirical validation with only a single numerical example
- The connection to model uncertainty remains largely theoretical without concrete examples

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a unified theoretical framework for risk-sensitive RL that generalizes previous approaches. The theoretical results are sound and well-supported, and the practical significance of the framework is demonstrated through a compelling numerical example. The main limitations are the lack of algorithmic implementation details and limited empirical validation. Given the importance of risk-sensitive decision making in real-world applications, this work represents a meaningful advancement in the field. The paper would benefit from more detailed algorithmic descriptions and additional empirical validation, but the core theoretical contribution is strong and well-justified.
