

# Peer Review: "Policy Gradient for Coherent Risk Measures"

## Summary

This paper presents a unified framework for policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning with coherent risk measures. The authors address the important problem of risk-sensitive optimization in sequential decision-making, which has received increasing attention in RL for managing cost variability beyond expected value. The paper makes a significant contribution by generalizing risk-sensitive RL to the entire class of coherent risk measures (satisfying convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity), rather than focusing on specific risk measures like CVaR or mean-variance as previous works have done.

The key technical contributions are:
1. A new gradient formula for static coherent risk that enables sampling-based estimation
2. A general gradient estimation algorithm combining sampling with convex programming
3. A new policy gradient theorem for Markov coherent risk measures (time-consistent dynamic risk)

The authors leverage the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (ρ(Z) = max_ξ E_ξ[Z] over a convex risk envelope) to derive their gradient formulas, which generalizes and unifies previous approaches. The paper connects to important concepts in risk theory (Artzner et al.'s coherent risk measures) and dynamic programming (time consistency), and relates their approach to existing work in risk-sensitive RL.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Rigor**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation, with well-motivated assumptions (2.2 and 4.1) and carefully derived theorems. The connection to the representation theorem for coherent risk measures (Theorem 2.1) is particularly strong and well-explained.

2. **Novel Unification**: The paper's main strength is its unification of risk-sensitive RL under a single coherent risk framework. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous work that focused on specific risk measures. As the authors correctly note, their approach generalizes and simplifies previous results (e.g., for CVaR, mean-semideviation).

3. **Clear Examples and Empirical Validation**: The numerical example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical importance of risk measure selection. The comparison between different risk-averse policies (π2 and π3) showing how different risk criteria lead to different decisions (particularly the counter-intuitive choice of A1 by π3 despite A3's higher mean) is compelling.

4. **Relevant Connections to Risk Theory**: The paper makes valuable connections between coherent risk measures and model uncertainty, noting that "the representation duality of coherent-risk... naturally relates the risk to model uncertainty." This provides a deeper conceptual understanding of risk-sensitive decision making.

5. **Practical Algorithmic Approach**: The proposed two-step procedure (sampling + convex programming) for gradient estimation is well-motivated and practical, with a consistency result (Proposition 4.4) that provides theoretical justification.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comprehensive Empirical Comparison**: While Section 6 provides a numerical illustration, the paper doesn't compare the performance of their general approach against specialized algorithms for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR algorithms from [8, 30]). It would strengthen the paper to include such a comparison showing that their general approach achieves comparable or better performance.

2. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions that their approach "scales-up to large or continuous MDPs" but doesn't adequately discuss the computational complexity of the convex programming step, especially as the number of constraints grows with the size of the risk envelope. This is critical for practical application.

3. **Incomplete Algorithm Details**: The statement "Space limitation restricts us from specifying the full details of our actor-critic algorithm" is problematic for a NeurIPS paper. The authors should provide a clear, concise description of their algorithm, possibly in the supplementary material.

4. **Weakness in Convergence Analysis**: The convergence analysis for the dynamic risk case is limited to the supplementary material, and the paper doesn't discuss how the approximation error in the critic (value function estimation) affects the overall convergence of the actor-critic algorithm.

5. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation Challenges**: The paper assumes that the risk envelope is known in canonical convex programming form (Assumption 2.2), but doesn't discuss how to obtain this form for more complex risk measures or how to handle cases where the envelope is unknown.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- **High Theoretical Quality**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by unifying risk-sensitive RL under coherent risk measures, with rigorous derivations and proofs.
- **Strong Conceptual Contribution**: The paper provides deeper insight into the relationship between risk measures and model uncertainty, which is valuable for real-world applications.
- **Clear Practical Implications**: The numerical example effectively demonstrates the importance of flexible risk criteria in decision making.
- **Novelty**: The paper's approach is novel in its unification of the entire class of coherent risk measures, rather than focusing on specific measures.
- **Relevance to RL Community**: Risk-sensitive RL is a growing area of interest in RL, with increasing applications in finance, robotics, and other safety-critical domains.

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- **Limited Empirical Validation**: The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing specialized algorithms for specific risk measures.
- **Incomplete Algorithm Description**: The lack of detailed algorithmic description for the dynamic risk case is a significant drawback for a NeurIPS paper.
- **Underdeveloped Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational complexity of the convex programming step, which is crucial for practical application.
- **Overstated Claims**: The claim that their approach "scales-up to large or continuous MDPs" without discussing the computational burden of the convex programming step may be misleading.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically sound and conceptually important contribution to risk-sensitive reinforcement learning. The unification of the entire class of coherent risk measures under a single policy gradient framework is a significant advance that simplifies and generalizes previous work. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed methods.

While the paper has some weaknesses in empirical validation and algorithmic detail, these are not fatal flaws given the strong theoretical contribution. The numerical example in Section 6 is compelling and demonstrates the practical value of the authors' approach.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and would be of significant interest to the NeurIPS community. The paper advances the state of the art in risk-sensitive RL by providing a unified framework that generalizes previous approaches, while also offering deeper theoretical insights into the relationship between risk measures and model uncertainty.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and novel, with significant implications for risk-sensitive decision making in reinforcement learning. While there are some weaknesses in the empirical validation and algorithmic detail, these are outweighed by the paper's strong theoretical foundation and conceptual contributions. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a comparison with specialized algorithms for specific risk measures (e.g., CVaR) to demonstrate that the general approach achieves comparable or better performance.

2. Provide a more detailed description of the actor-critic algorithm, particularly the critic's function approximation method for estimating the risk-sensitive value function.

3. Discuss the computational complexity of the convex programming step in more detail, including how it scales with the size of the problem and the number of risk envelope constraints.

4. In the numerical example, include a more detailed analysis of the convergence properties and final performance metrics of the different policies.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the risk envelope might be learned or approximated for risk measures where it's not known in canonical form.
