This paper addresses the problem of estimating degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network setting using gossip algorithms. While gossip algorithms for computing simple averages (separable sums) are well-established, their extension to U-statistics, which involve averages over pairs of observations, is a less explored and more challenging problem. The authors propose two novel algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for the synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively. The core idea is to have each node maintain a local estimate of the U-statistic and an auxiliary data observation. At each step, a pair of neighboring nodes average their local estimates and swap their auxiliary observations. This allows for simultaneous estimate consensus and data propagation throughout the network. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of these algorithms, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. The analysis introduces a novel "phantom network" formulation to decouple the data propagation and estimate averaging processes. The proposed methods are shown to improve upon the only existing work on this specific problem, U2-gossip [17], in terms of memory requirements, communication costs, and convergence speed. Experimental results on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important problem that significantly extends the scope of standard gossip algorithms. U-statistics encompass a wide range of useful metrics (e.g., sample variance, AUC, Gini mean difference), and the ability to compute them efficiently in a decentralized manner is highly valuable. The proposed approach is novel and elegant, combining a random walk of data points with a standard averaging gossip process on the estimates. This is a clear and substantial improvement over the prior state-of-the-art [17].

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength of the paper. The convergence proofs, based on the creative "phantom network" construction, are rigorous and provide insightful, explicit bounds. The rates depend on both the spectral gap of the network graph (characterizing connectivity) and data-dependent terms (characterizing the hardness of the estimation problem), which is a desirable feature. The analysis is also used to provide a refined convergence rate for the competing U2-gossip algorithm, putting the comparison on firm theoretical footing.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically organized. The problem statement is precise, the algorithms are presented clearly, and the theoretical results are stated formally. The inclusion of Figure 1 to illustrate the phantom network concept is helpful for understanding the proof technique.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and support the paper's claims. The comparison with U2-gossip on two different U-statistics and across three network topologies convincingly demonstrates the practical advantages of the GoSta algorithms in terms of both mean error and variance across nodes. The experiment showing the widening performance gap with increasing network size (Figure 3a) is particularly effective at illustrating the asymptotic advantage predicted by the theory.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Gap Between Asynchronous Theory and Practice:** The derived theoretical rate for GoSta-async (O(log t/t)) is worse than for GoSta-sync (O(1/t)). However, the empirical results in Figure 3b show that their performance is nearly identical. This is an interesting finding that deserves some discussion. The authors should comment on whether they believe the logarithmic factor in the asynchronous bound is an artifact of the proof technique or if the experimental settings are not sufficient to reveal the asymptotic difference. A brief discussion on this point would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Lack of a Simple Baseline:** The experiments focus exclusively on comparing GoSta with U2-gossip. While this is the most relevant competitor, the inclusion of a simpler, non-gossip baseline could provide valuable context. For instance, a baseline could involve each node simply gathering all data within a k-hop neighborhood and computing a local U-statistic, or routing all data to a root node along a spanning tree. Comparing against such a method would help to better quantify the trade-offs and advantages of the gossip-based approach for this problem.

3.  **Clarity of the U2-gossip Comparison:** The paper states that U2-gossip has higher memory and communication costs, but the algorithm itself is only described in the supplementary material. For self-containment and to make the comparison more direct for the reader, it would be helpful to include a brief, high-level description of the U2-gossip algorithm in Section 3 or 4.1 of the main paper.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of distributed computation and gossip algorithms. The proposed methods are elegant, the theoretical analysis is strong, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem with a superior solution compared to existing work. The identified weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to opportunities for additional discussion and experimental context. I strongly recommend acceptance.
