This paper addresses the problem of estimating degree-two U-statistics (e.g., AUC, sample variance) in a decentralized network. The authors propose GoSta, a new family of randomized gossip algorithms in both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The core idea is to combine two iterative steps: a data propagation step where neighboring nodes swap an auxiliary data observation, and a consensus step where they average their local estimates. This work builds upon the literature of gossip algorithms for simple averaging and directly improves upon U2-gossip [17], which is the primary prior work on gossip-based U-statistic estimation.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Quality & Originality:** The main contribution is a pair of novel algorithms that are more efficient in terms of communication and memory than the previous state-of-the-art. The introduction of a fully asynchronous algorithm is a key innovation, making the approach far more practical for real-world distributed systems that lack a global clock. The theoretical analysis is a major strength; the use of a "phantom network" construction to derive convergence rates is an elegant and original analytical tool. The derived rates of O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous) clearly depend on network connectivity (spectral gap) and data properties, providing valuable theoretical insight.

**Significance & Clarity:** The problem is highly significant, as U-statistics are fundamental quantities in machine learning, and their efficient computation in a decentralized manner is crucial for large-scale applications. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making the motivation, algorithms, and analysis easy to follow. The claims of superiority over U2-gossip are well-supported by thorough experiments on real-world datasets for two distinct U-statistics, demonstrating faster convergence and lower variance.

The paper has very few weaknesses. One minor point is that the theoretical bound for the asynchronous algorithm is slightly weaker than for the synchronous version, while their empirical performances are shown to be quite comparable. A brief discussion on this small gap between theory and practice could further strengthen the paper.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, sound, and significant contribution to the field of distributed computation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes novel, efficient synchronous and asynchronous algorithms for an important problem.
*   Provides a rigorous and original theoretical analysis with clear convergence bounds.
*   Strong empirical validation demonstrates clear superiority over the existing state-of-the-art.
*   High clarity in writing and presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None of significance.
