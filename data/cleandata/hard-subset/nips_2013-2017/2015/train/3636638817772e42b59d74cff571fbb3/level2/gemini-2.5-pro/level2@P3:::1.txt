This paper presents novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for the distributed estimation of U-statistics. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations, are crucial for many machine learning tasks (e.g., calculating AUC, sample variance, or within-cluster scatter) but are not directly computable by standard gossip algorithms designed for separable sums. The proposed approach, named GoSta, cleverly combines two steps: a local averaging of estimates among neighboring nodes and a data propagation step where nodes swap auxiliary data observations. This allows information about all data points to spread throughout the network over time, enabling each node to compute an increasingly accurate estimate of the global U-statistic.

The work's main contributions are twofold. First, it introduces the GoSta algorithms, which are shown to be more efficient in terms of memory and communication than the primary existing method, U2-gossip [17]. Second, it provides a rigorous convergence analysis for both the synchronous and asynchronous settings, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t), respectively. The analysis introduces a novel and insightful reformulation of the process using a "phantom network" to decouple the data propagation and estimate averaging dynamics. The theoretical results are supported by experiments on real datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation, demonstrating superior performance over the previous state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem. While gossip for distributed averaging is a well-studied area, the distributed computation of U-statistics is a less-explored but equally relevant problem for the machine learning community. The proposed method is a novel and elegant combination of existing ideas that leads to a substantial improvement over prior work. The analytical technique based on a "phantom network" is also a creative and powerful tool that may be of independent interest for analyzing other complex distributed processes.

*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The convergence analysis is thorough, providing explicit rates that depend on both data properties (via norms of data-dependent vectors) and network topology (via the spectral gap). This provides a deep understanding of the algorithm's behavior. The claims of superiority over U2-gossip are well-supported by both a refined theoretical analysis of that algorithm and strong empirical evidence.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem is well-motivated, the notation is consistent, and the proposed algorithms are described clearly. The experimental section is easy to follow, and the results are presented effectively through informative plots.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is a strong contribution, and the following points are primarily suggestions for minor revisions to further improve its clarity and completeness.

1.  **Clarification of Algorithm 2 (Asynchronous):** In Algorithm 2, step 5 updates `Zi` and `Zj` via averaging. Steps 6 and 7 then perform another update on `Zi` and `Zj`. It is slightly ambiguous whether the updates in steps 6 and 7 use the original values of `Zi` and `Zj` from before the averaging step, or the new, averaged values from step 5. Clarifying this sequence of operations is essential for the algorithm's reproducibility. Assuming the averaged values are used, the algorithm description could be made more explicit.

2.  **Intuition for the Phantom Network:** The phantom network is a central and elegant concept for the analysis. While Figure 1 provides a helpful visual, the text could benefit from a few more sentences of high-level, intuitive explanation before diving into the block matrix formulation. This would make this powerful analytical tool more accessible to a broader audience.

3.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** The problem statement assumes that the kernel `H` satisfies `H(X, X) = 0`. While this holds for many common U-statistics (e.g., those based on squared distances), it is not universally true. A brief comment on the implications of this assumption, and how the algorithm or analysis might change if it were relaxed, would add completeness.

4.  **Asynchronous Rate Constant:** The convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm in Theorem 2 contains a constant `c'(G)`. While a full characterization might be beyond the scope of this work, any additional insight into how this constant depends on the graph properties (e.g., degrees, spectral gap) would strengthen the theoretical result. This is a "nice-to-have" suggestion that could be addressed if space permits.

In summary, this paper provides an elegant, efficient, and well-analyzed solution to the important problem of decentralized U-statistic estimation. The work represents a significant advance over existing methods. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to points of clarification that can be addressed in a final version.
