## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networks, a task less explored than distributed mean estimation despite its relevance to various applications like variance calculation and AUC estimation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, providing convergence rate bounds and demonstrating empirical superiority over a prior approach (U2-gossip).

The core idea of leveraging “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem and facilitate analysis is clever and allows for a rigorous convergence analysis. The theoretical results – O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous algorithms – are well-presented and contextualized with data and network-dependent terms, highlighting the impact of network connectivity. The comparison to U2-gossip is a strong point, demonstrating both faster convergence and reduced communication/memory costs. The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real datasets convincingly support the claims, showing consistent performance gains across different network topologies. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature on gossip algorithms. The notation is clearly defined, and the mathematical derivations appear sound (though a more detailed proof sketch in the main body, rather than solely in the supplementary material, would be beneficial). The acknowledgement of limitations, particularly regarding the scalability of the phantom network approach for analysis, is commendable. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the data-dependent terms in the convergence rates. While acknowledged, a deeper understanding of how these terms influence performance in different scenarios would be valuable.  Additionally, while comparisons to U2-gossip are strong, a comparison to simpler distributed estimation methods (even if less efficient) could provide a broader context for the proposed algorithms’ performance.  Finally, the discussion of extending the approach to scenarios with multiple observations per node feels somewhat brief and could be expanded.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
*   Presents novel algorithms with rigorous convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrates empirical superiority over existing approaches.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the practical implications of data-dependent convergence terms.
*   Lack of comparison to simpler baseline distributed estimation methods.
*   Brief discussion of extending the approach to multiple observations per node.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of distributed estimation. Despite minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation make it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
