This paper introduces novel randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a distributed setting, addressing a gap in the existing literature which primarily focuses on averaging. The work provides convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous scenarios, supported by numerical experiments demonstrating superior performance compared to a previous approach [17]. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, related work, proposed algorithms, and experimental results. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, highlighting the importance of U-statistics in various applications and the limitations of existing gossip algorithms. The use of “phantom nodes” to analyze the synchronous case is a clever technique. The related work section is comprehensive, referencing key papers in gossip algorithms and U-statistics. The clarity of mathematical notation and algorithmic descriptions is commendable. The numerical experiments are well-designed, utilizing real-world datasets and different network topologies.

A significant strength of the work lies in the theoretical analysis, providing convergence rates that depend on both data and network characteristics. This allows for a nuanced understanding of algorithm performance in different scenarios. The comparison with [17] is particularly valuable, demonstrating concrete improvements in both convergence speed and resource usage. The inclusion of an asynchronous algorithm addresses a practical concern, as synchronization is often challenging in distributed systems.

However, several areas could be improved. While the paper establishes convergence rates, the constants hidden within the O-notation are not explicitly characterized. Providing more concrete bounds, even approximate ones, would be beneficial. The discussion of the asynchronous algorithm’s convergence rate (O(log t/t)) feels less detailed than the synchronous case. A more thorough analysis, or a justification for the brevity, would be appreciated. 

The experimental section could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the parameter settings and sensitivity analysis. For example, the choice of network parameters (k and p for the Watts-Strogatz graph) and their impact on performance are not fully explored. Furthermore, the comparison to other distributed estimation algorithms (beyond [17]) would strengthen the conclusions. While the authors acknowledge the potential for adaptive communication schemes, exploring these briefly in the discussion section could offer valuable insights. The supplementary material is mentioned frequently, but it is crucial that all key details needed to understand the analysis (like the proofs) are present there.

The phantom node technique, while clever, adds complexity to the analysis. A more intuitive explanation of how this reformulation relates to the actual algorithm execution could enhance understanding. It’s also important to emphasize that this is purely an analytical tool, as noted in the paper, but this point could be made more prominent.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The work addresses an important gap in the literature by extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The convergence rate analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding algorithm performance.
*   Empirical Validation: The numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms compared to existing approaches.
*   Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of concrete bounds: The constants hidden within the O-notation are not explicitly characterized.
*   Limited experimental comparison: The comparison is primarily limited to a single baseline algorithm.
*   Asynchronous analysis: The analysis of the asynchronous algorithm is less detailed than the synchronous case.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some improvements could be made, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh its weaknesses. The paper is of high quality and is suitable for acceptance to NIPS. A major revision addressing the issues outlined above, especially concerning the clarity of the constant factors and further experimental analysis, is recommended.
