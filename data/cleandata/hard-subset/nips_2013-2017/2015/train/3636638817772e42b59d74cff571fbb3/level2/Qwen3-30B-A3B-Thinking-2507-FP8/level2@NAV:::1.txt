

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a significant gap in the literature as existing gossip algorithms are limited to separable statistics like sample means. The main claim is that the proposed GoSta algorithms achieve faster convergence rates (O(1/t) and O(log t/t)) with lower memory and communication costs compared to the previous U2-gossip approach.

The theoretical support is strong, with rigorous convergence proofs for both settings using a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation that enables clear analysis of data propagation and averaging. The convergence bounds explicitly incorporate data-dependent terms and network-dependent spectral gap terms, demonstrating how well-connected networks accelerate convergence. The theoretical comparison with U2-gossip (equation 10) correctly identifies the √n factor disadvantage in the prior approach, which is empirically validated in the experiments.

The paper demonstrates solid field knowledge, correctly positioning itself within the gossip algorithm literature and properly citing relevant work. The applications to AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation are highly relevant to practical machine learning problems. The experimental results on real datasets consistently show GoSta's superiority across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), with lower average error and reduced variance in estimates.

The work presents a meaningful advancement over U2-gossip, addressing its key limitations in communication cost and asynchronicity. While the paper could better acknowledge limitations (e.g., performance under node failures or in highly dynamic networks), the theoretical and empirical evidence is compelling.

The paper excels in quality, clarity, and significance. It makes a substantial contribution to distributed computing by enabling efficient decentralized computation of important statistical measures that were previously challenging to compute. The technical depth, clear organization, and strong empirical validation make this a valuable addition to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the minor limitations mentioned.
