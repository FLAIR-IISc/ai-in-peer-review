- Introduces novel gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) for decentralized estimation of U-statistics, filling a gap vs. existing separable-only gossip methods.
- Targets U-statistics (AUC, variance, within-cluster scatter) that require pairwise averaging and were previously inefficiently handled in decentralized settings.
- Proposes synchronous and asynchronous algorithms that maintain local estimates while propagating data across the network.
- Key analytical innovation: a "phantom nodes" reformulation that enables tractable convergence analysis and explicit bounds.
- Synchronous algorithm achieves O(1/t) convergence with constants depending on the network spectral gap.
- Asynchronous algorithm achieves O(log t / t) convergence, slower than synchronous but practically comparable in experiments.
- Theoretical bounds improve on U2-gossip’s O(1/t) rate while reducing memory and communication costs.
- Provides refined convergence-rate comparisons and analysis of U2-gossip to highlight advantages of the proposed methods.
- Empirical evaluation on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter demonstrates superior average error and variance across nodes.
- Experiments on complete graphs, 2D grids, and Watts–Strogatz networks show consistent performance gains and agree with theoretical predictions.
- Performance gap increases with network size, matching the dependence predicted by the convergence bounds.
- Inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous variants addresses practical implementation concerns.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise mathematical formulations and reproducible technical detail.
- Background and related work sections effectively contextualize the contribution and identify limitations of prior methods.
- Minor weakness: phantom node concept is complex and would benefit from a brief intuitive explanation in the main text.
- Minor weakness: asynchronous convergence bound is less tight than synchronous; authors provide a reasonable explanation.
- Minor weakness: experiments could include more network topologies to further illustrate network-dependent behavior.
- Overall judgment: significant, original contribution with strong theoretical and empirical support, low resource costs, and high relevance to NIPS — suitable for publication.
