This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for the decentralized estimation of degree-two U-statistics. Standard gossip algorithms are typically used for separable functions like sample means, and are not directly applicable to U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations. The key idea of the proposed algorithms, named GoSta, is to combine two iterative processes: 1) a data propagation step, where an auxiliary data observation at each node performs a random walk on the network, and 2) an estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic with their neighbors. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of their methods, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t / t) for the asynchronous one. A notable contribution of the analysis is a novel proof technique based on a "phantom network" construction, which elegantly decouples the two processes for the analysis. The paper compares GoSta to the main prior work in this area, U2-gossip [17], both theoretically and empirically. The authors provide a new analysis of U2-gossip showing its convergence rate is worse by a factor of sqrt(n), and demonstrate through experiments on real-world datasets that their proposed methods are superior in terms of accuracy, convergence speed, and resource requirements (memory and communication).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem. U-statistics (e.g., AUC, sample variance, within-cluster scatter) are fundamental in machine learning, and their efficient estimation in decentralized settings is a valuable contribution. The proposed approach of simultaneously propagating data and averaging estimates is novel for this problem and significantly improves upon the state-of-the-art.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength of the paper. The convergence proofs are non-trivial, and the "phantom network" abstraction is a clever and powerful tool that could be of independent interest for analyzing other complex distributed algorithms. The derived convergence rates are insightful, explicitly showing the dependency on network connectivity (via the spectral gap) and data properties.

3.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the algorithms are presented concisely, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The authors also provide a new, more refined analysis of the competing U2-gossip algorithm within their framework, which allows for a direct and fair theoretical comparison. This is a strong point, as it provides a clear reason for the empirical outperformance of their method.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. They are performed on real-world datasets for relevant U-statistics (AUC and within-cluster scatter) and across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz). The results strongly support the theoretical findings, clearly showing that GoSta converges faster and with lower variance across nodes than U2-gossip, and that the performance gap widens with network size as predicted by the theory.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Assumptions in the Asynchronous Algorithm:** The asynchronous algorithm (Algorithm 2) requires each node `k` to know `pk = 2dk/|E|`, which implies knowledge of its own degree `dk` (local information) but also the total number of edges `|E|` (global information). The need for global network information seems to run counter to the ethos of fully decentralized gossip algorithms. The authors should discuss this assumption. Can `|E|` be pre-computed or estimated via another gossip protocol? How sensitive is the algorithm to an inexact value of `|E|`?

2.  **Clarification of Node Activation Probability:** There appears to be a small inconsistency in Section 4.2. The paper states that an edge `(i, j)` is drawn uniformly at random from `E`. In this case, the probability of a specific node `k` being involved in the communication (i.e., being activated) is the sum of probabilities of picking any edge connected to it, which is `dk/|E|`. However, the paper defines `pk = 2dk/|E|`. Please clarify the process by which this probability is derived.

3.  **Synchronous Update Model:** In Algorithm 1 (lines 3-5), all `n` nodes update their local estimates `Zp` before a single pair of nodes `(i, j)` communicates. This is a strong synchrony assumption, requiring a global clock for all nodes to perform this update step simultaneously. It would be valuable to discuss the practicality of this model and how it relates to more standard gossip settings where only active nodes perform computations.

4.  **Theory vs. Practice for Asynchronous Rate:** The experiments in Figure 3b show that GoSta-async performs comparably to GoSta-sync. This is an interesting result, especially since the proven theoretical rate for the asynchronous version (O(log t / t)) is slightly worse than the synchronous one (O(1/t)). It would be helpful if the authors could provide some intuition for this observation. Perhaps the constants hidden in the O() notation play a significant role, or the logarithmic factor is very small in the regimes tested. A brief discussion on this would strengthen the connection between the theory and the empirical results.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on distributed computation. The proposed algorithms are simple and effective, the theoretical analysis is novel and rigorous, and the experimental results are compelling. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed through clarifications in the text. The paper is of high quality and significance, and should be of great interest to the NIPS community.
