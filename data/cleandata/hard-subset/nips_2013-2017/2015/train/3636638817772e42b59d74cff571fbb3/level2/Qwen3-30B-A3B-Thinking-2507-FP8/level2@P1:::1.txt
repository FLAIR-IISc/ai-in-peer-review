

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are statistics depending on pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, empirical variance, Gini mean difference). The authors address an important gap in the literature, as previous gossip algorithms could only handle statistics depending on single observations. The key innovation is a reformulation using "phantom nodes" to model data propagation separately from averaging, enabling convergence rate analysis.

Strengths:
The paper makes a clear contribution to distributed computation by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with explicit convergence rates (O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous) that depend on both data properties and network connectivity (through the spectral gap). The authors carefully compare their approach to the previous U2-gossip algorithm [17], showing significant advantages in communication and memory complexity, as well as faster convergence rates in practice. The empirical validation on real datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC and Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter) is comprehensive, with comparisons across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) and multiple performance metrics (average error, variance, time to reach target accuracy).

Clarity:
The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear notation, well-structured arguments, and precise pseudocode for the algorithms. The "phantom nodes" reformulation is elegantly presented, and the connection between network properties and convergence rates is well-explained. The figures effectively illustrate the empirical results, particularly showing how the performance gap between GoSta and U2-gossip widens with network size.

Originality:
This work addresses a clear gap in the gossip literature, as U-statistics have been largely overlooked in distributed settings. The phantom node technique for analysis is novel and enables the convergence rate derivation. While the paper acknowledges related work on gossip for single-observation statistics, it clearly establishes how their approach differs from and improves upon the only prior work on U-statistics (U2-gossip).

Significance:
The significance of this work is substantial for distributed machine learning and statistics. Many important metrics in machine learning (AUC, Gini coefficient, within-cluster scatter) are U-statistics, and the paper demonstrates how to compute them efficiently in decentralized settings. The practical advantages in communication and memory costs make the approach immediately applicable to real-world distributed systems, as evidenced by the empirical results.

Minor weaknesses:
The comparison to U2-gossip could be slightly strengthened by providing more detailed analysis of the memory and communication complexity differences. While the paper mentions that GoSta has lower memory requirements, a more explicit comparison of the exact memory footprint (e.g., number of variables stored per node) would be helpful. Additionally, the paper could briefly discuss potential limitations, such as how the algorithms perform when the underlying graph is disconnected or when observations are not uniformly distributed across the network.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses an important problem in distributed computation with clear theoretical and practical contributions. The work builds on but significantly extends the gossip algorithm literature, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

The authors have carefully addressed the key challenges in extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics, and their theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by experiments. The paper is technically strong, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
