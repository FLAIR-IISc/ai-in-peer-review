Summary
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. While standard gossip algorithms efficiently compute separable statistics (e.g., means), U-statistics involve pairwise interactions and have seen limited attention in decentralized settings. The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node maintains a single auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via swaps and a local estimate that is repeatedly averaged both temporally (running average) and spatially (pairwise averaging upon edge activations). A key analytical device is a “phantom graph” construction used to decouple data propagation and averaging. The authors prove convergence in expectation to the exact U-statistic and derive rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with explicit dependence on the data dispersion and the spectral gap of the network. They compare against U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2011], claiming better rates and lower memory/communication. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter on multiple graph topologies support the claims.

Relation to prior work: The work is positioned relative to classic randomized gossip and consensus averaging (e.g., Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.) and the lone prior proposal for U-statistics (U2-gossip). The proposed algorithms combine standard consensus averaging with a data propagation mechanism and provide refined rates dependent on the spectral properties of the communication graph, consistent with consensus literature. The paper also discusses an asynchronous model akin to the one used in randomized gossip.

Quality
- Theory: The analysis is technically sound at a high level. The phantom graph reformulation neatly separates mixing of estimates (W2(G) operator) and data propagation (block-diagonal W1(G) operators), enabling bounds on expected error. The stated rates are plausible: O(1/t) in synchronous settings with averaging, and O(log t/t) under asynchronous activation due to uneven updates and the need to correct with local counters. The comparison bound for U2-gossip elucidates the √n factor penalty from not leveraging spatial averaging, which is insightful.
- Assumptions and caveats:
  - Convergence is shown in expectation. There are no high-probability, MSE, or almost sure convergence guarantees, which limits practical interpretability in stochastic environments. Given the linear dynamics, extending to mean-square bounds or concentration (e.g., via mixing/contraction plus martingale arguments) seems feasible and would strengthen the paper.
  - Asynchronous algorithm requires each node to know pk = 2dk/|E| and thus |E|. This global quantity is often unavailable in realistic decentralized settings; please discuss how pk can be estimated online, or whether replacing 1/(pk mk) by 1/(visit_count) preserves unbiasedness or only affects constants.
  - Both algorithms assume undirected, connected, non-bipartite graphs with uniform random edge activations. For bipartite graphs, a lazy variant (self-loops) could relax the assumption; it would be helpful to state this. Directed graphs (push-sum) and time-varying networks are out of scope; a brief discussion would clarify applicability.
  - Synchronous algorithm uses a globally uniform random edge each iteration; who implements this scheduler? Clarifying how this maps to standard synchronous protocols (e.g., randomized matchings or simultaneous independent edge activations without conflicts) would help.
- Correctness details: The identity 1 − λ2(W2(G)) = β2/|E| (β2 the second smallest Laplacian eigenvalue) should be checked; the manuscript uses βn−1 as the “second smallest,” which is atypical. Please standardize notation to avoid confusion. The dependence on spectral gap is otherwise consistent with consensus literature.
- Experiments: The empirical evaluation demonstrates faster convergence of GoSta-sync vs U2-gossip on complete, grid, and Watts-Strogatz graphs, and comparable performance of async vs sync. However:
  - Fairness of comparison: U2-gossip activates two edges per iteration and stores two auxiliary observations. Consider normalizing by number of edge communications or total words transmitted to compare communication efficiency, not just iteration count.
  - Variance across nodes is shown empirically; a theoretical result on consensus variance decay would be useful.
  - Asynchronous results are relatively limited (single figure). Additional networks and problem sizes would support the O(log t/t) behavior.
  - Sensitivity to graph size and spectral gap is convincingly illustrated for complete graphs; adding grids/WS graphs would reinforce the spectral-gap narrative.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The phantom graph construction is clear, and the transition matrix blocks are explicit. Minor issues:
- Clarify eigenvalue notation and the relationship between Wα(G) and L(G) to avoid index ambiguity.
- State more clearly the computational cost per iteration and memory (one auxiliary vector and a scalar per node; for async also a counter).
- Make explicit the deviation from the classical U-statistic normalization and its impact on interpretation.

Originality
Extending gossip to U-statistics with a single auxiliary datum and coupling it with consensus averaging is a novel and meaningful refinement over U2-gossip. The phantom-graph analysis, while reminiscent of augmented-state techniques in consensus, appears original in this context. The refined comparison bound for U2-gossip is a useful contribution.

Significance
Estimating U-statistics in a decentralized setting is relevant for several ML tasks (AUC, scatter, Gini, Kendall tau). The algorithms reduce memory and communication relative to prior work and provide better rates, making them practically attractive. The main limitation is that results are in expectation and require some global knowledge in the asynchronous case. Nonetheless, the contribution is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on distributed learning and networked systems.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled algorithms for an important but underexplored problem (distributed U-statistics).
- Solid analysis with explicit dependence on data and spectral gap; improved rates over prior art.
- Lower memory and communication per iteration than U2-gossip.
- Empirical results across multiple graph families and applications support claims.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Convergence guarantees only in expectation; no finite-sample high-probability or MSE bounds.
- Asynchronous algorithm needs knowledge of |E|; practicality not fully addressed.
- Synchronous edge selection model may require centralized scheduling; deployment details unclear.
- Experimental comparison does not normalize for communication budget; limited async evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide mean-square error bounds or high-probability concentration results for both algorithms.
- Remove or mitigate the need for |E| in GoSta-async (online estimation, step-sizes based on local visit counts) and quantify the impact on rates.
- Discuss bipartite graphs (lazy updates), directed/time-varying networks (push-sum variants), and robustness to message delays and losses.
- Normalize empirical comparisons by total messages or words communicated; include wall-clock or communication-cost plots.
- Expand async experiments across more graphs and sizes; report the effect of spectral gap empirically.
- Clarify eigenvalue notation and standardize the spectral gap definition (β2 vs βn−1).
