Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in peer-to-peer networks via randomized gossip. While classical gossip methods efficiently compute separable statistics (e.g., means), they do not directly handle pairwise functionals such as variance, AUC, Gini mean difference, Kendall tau, and within-cluster scatter. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) that (i) propagate raw observations via token swaps along randomly selected edges and (ii) average local estimates to drive consensus. A key analytical device is a “phantom network” construction that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging, enabling convergence analysis. Theoretical results give convergence in expectation with rates O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O((log t)/t) for the asynchronous case; the bounds include data-dependent dispersion terms and network-dependent spectral terms (via the Laplacian spectral gap through Wα(G)). The paper carefully contrasts with U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), deriving a refined bound for U2-gossip that reveals a √n penalty and higher per-iteration memory/communication.

This work builds on and connects to literature on gossip/consensus (Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Dimakis et al.), geographic gossip, and distributed optimization (Duchi et al.; Nedic & Ozdaglar), while extending the scope to U-statistics with theoretically grounded rates.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: Extends gossip to non-separable U-statistics with both synchronous and fully asynchronous algorithms, addressing a gap left by U2-gossip.
- Theory: Clear O(1/t) and O((log t)/t) rates with explicit dependence on problem hardness (dispersion of H and h) and network connectivity (spectral gap). The phantom-node reformulation is elegant and may be of independent interest.
- Practicality: Lower memory and communication than U2-gossip (a single auxiliary observation and a single edge activation per iteration). Asynchronous variant uses only local activations and simple counters.
- Empirics: Demonstrates improved accuracy and lower across-node variance versus U2-gossip on two relevant tasks (AUC and within-cluster scatter), across multiple graph topologies; trends match theory (benefit increases with n and with better connectivity).

Weaknesses and questions
- Convergence mode: Results are in expectation. High-probability or almost-sure convergence/finite-sample concentration for the error would make the guarantees more actionable, especially to quantify variability across nodes/time.
- Knowledge of |E|: The asynchronous algorithm requires pk = 2dk/|E| and thus knowledge of |E|. In decentralized settings this may be unavailable; could the method be adapted to unknown |E| (e.g., via local Poisson clocks per node/edge or online estimation of pk)?
- Communication model and cost: Results are given in iterations. A more detailed accounting of message size (swapping a full data vector Yk) and total communication until a target error would facilitate comparisons. For high-dimensional Xi, swapping raw data could be expensive.
- Privacy/data movement: Since raw observations are swapped, the approach may not suit privacy-constrained networks. Any possibility of using randomized sketches, hashing, or secure aggregation to approximate H(Xi, Yj) without revealing raw data?
- Optimality and lower bounds: Are the O(1/t) and O((log t)/t) rates minimax optimal for pairwise functionals under this communication model? A discussion or lower-bound reference would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental breadth: Datasets are moderate-scale; larger networks, more diverse topologies (e.g., scale-free), and experiments varying the spectral gap would better illustrate the network-dependence predicted by the bounds. Also, a study with multiple observations per node (as in Remark 2) would be helpful.
- Robustness: Assumptions include connected, non-bipartite graphs and uniform random edge selection. How sensitive are rates to non-uniform edge selection, time-varying graphs, or occasional packet loss?

Quality
The algorithms are well specified; proofs are sketched with clear intuition and deferred details. The rate expressions are interpretable. The empirical study, while not exhaustive, is consistent with theory. Clarifying the requirement on |E| and offering alternatives would improve practical relevance.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The phantom-network analysis is explained carefully. Minor suggestions: (i) briefly summarize the proof ideas for Theorem 2 in the main text; (ii) explicitly state the assumptions on edge selection in both settings; (iii) add a paragraph on communication/memory complexity per iteration and to reach ε-accuracy.

Originality
Combining averaging with single-token data propagation for U-statistics and the phantom-node analysis appear novel relative to U2-gossip and standard consensus methods. The refined comparison bound for U2-gossip is a useful contribution.

Significance
U-statistics are widely used in ML; enabling their decentralized estimation with rigorous rates is valuable. The algorithms are simple and likely to be adopted/extended (e.g., as building blocks for decentralized optimization with pairwise losses).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem formulation and practical algorithms (sync/async).
- Theoretical rates with explicit data/network dependence.
- Lower memory/communication and faster convergence than prior U2-gossip.
- Empirical validation aligning with theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Only expectation bounds; no concentration results.
- Requires knowledge of |E| in the async variant.
- Limited exploration of large-scale settings and multipoint-per-node cases.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide high-probability bounds or variance decay across nodes; at least report empirical confidence intervals over runs and nodes as a function of t.
- Discuss/implement an async variant that does not require |E| (e.g., edge-based Poisson clocks, push-sum normalization, or adaptive estimates of pk).
- Report communication complexity (number and size of messages) to reach a target error, and assess the impact of data dimension d.
- Explore sketching-based or privacy-preserving extensions to avoid raw data swaps.
- Add experiments varying the spectral gap and with multiple observations per node.

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely contribution with clear theoretical and empirical advances over prior art. I lean toward acceptance.
