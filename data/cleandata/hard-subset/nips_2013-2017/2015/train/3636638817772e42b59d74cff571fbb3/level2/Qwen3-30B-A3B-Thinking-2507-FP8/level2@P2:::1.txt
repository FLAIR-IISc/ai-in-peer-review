

This paper proposes novel gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are quantities depending on pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, variance). The authors introduce "GoSta" algorithms (both synchronous and asynchronous) that maintain local estimates while propagating data across the network. The main contribution is the theoretical convergence analysis showing O(1/t) rate for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings, along with empirical evidence demonstrating superiority over the previous U2-gossip approach [17].

Strengths:
1. The problem is well-motivated: U-statistics are important in many machine learning applications, but distributed estimation has been largely unaddressed in the gossip literature. The paper effectively identifies this gap.
2. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with the novel "phantom node" construction providing an elegant way to separate data propagation and averaging steps. The convergence bounds explicitly incorporate data-dependent and network-dependent terms, which is valuable for practical implementation.
3. The experimental results on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) convincingly demonstrate practical advantages over U2-gossip, particularly in terms of convergence speed and variance reduction across nodes.
4. The paper clearly positions its contribution against prior work, particularly U2-gossip [17], and explains both theoretical and practical advantages.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could better contextualize its work within the broader literature on distributed statistics. For instance, the work by Dimakis et al. (2010) on "Gossip-Based Computation of Aggregate Information" addresses distributed computation of more complex statistics, but the authors don't discuss how their work relates to or extends this line of research.
2. The experimental section is somewhat limited. The authors compare only to U2-gossip, but it would be valuable to include comparisons to baseline methods (e.g., centralized computation, simple averaging of pairwise statistics) to better establish the practical advantages in real-world settings.
3. The theoretical analysis of the asynchronous algorithm (Theorem 2) is less detailed than the synchronous case. The constant c'(G) is mentioned but not characterized, making it difficult to assess how the convergence rate depends on specific network properties.
4. The paper doesn't discuss practical implementation considerations for real-world networks (e.g., handling node failures, network partitioning, or time-varying topologies), which would be valuable for practitioners.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The convergence proofs are rigorous (though some details are relegated to supplementary material), and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The authors carefully compare their approach to the previous state-of-the-art (U2-gossip) and acknowledge limitations.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The algorithms are presented in pseudocode, and key concepts (like "phantom nodes") are well-explained. The notation is consistent throughout. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality: The paper identifies an important gap in the gossip literature (U-statistics haven't been addressed before in this context) and provides a novel solution. The phantom node construction is particularly elegant and seems to be a new technique for analyzing distributed algorithms. The work is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers on gossip algorithms.

Significance: U-statistics are important in many machine learning applications, and the proposed algorithms offer practical advantages over the previous state-of-the-art. The lower communication and memory costs, combined with faster convergence, make this work significant for real-world distributed systems. The experimental results on real datasets further strengthen the practical significance.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed algorithms. The main weakness is the limited contextualization within the broader literature on distributed statistics. Addressing this (and providing more details on the asynchronous convergence rate) would strengthen the paper significantly. The empirical comparison to additional baselines would also be helpful.

Overall, I recommend acceptance. The paper is technically strong, addresses an important problem, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses are easily fixable and don't undermine the main contribution.
