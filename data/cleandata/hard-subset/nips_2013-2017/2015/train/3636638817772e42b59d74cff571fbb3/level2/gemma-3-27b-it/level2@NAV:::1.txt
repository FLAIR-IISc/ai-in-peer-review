## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networked systems, a task less explored than distributed mean estimation despite its relevance in various applications like variance calculation, AUC estimation, and Gini mean difference. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for this purpose, providing convergence rate bounds and empirical validation through numerical experiments. 

The core contribution lies in extending the existing gossip framework to handle the pair-wise averaging inherent in U-statistic calculation. The proposed algorithms cleverly combine data propagation and local estimate averaging. The synchronous algorithm achieves a convergence rate of O(1/t), while the asynchronous version reaches O(log t/t), with bounds explicitly dependent on both data characteristics and network topology (spectral gap). The “phantom node” reformulation used in the analysis is a clever technique to tackle the complexity of the problem.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of gossip algorithms and U-statistics. They acknowledge the limitations of previous work [17] and demonstrate improvements in both convergence rate and computational cost. The numerical experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation using real datasets support the claims of superior performance compared to U2-gossip. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing distributed estimation techniques beyond U2-gossip. While the authors mention these algorithms are not directly applicable, a discussion of why they fall short and a potential comparison with adapted variants would be valuable. Furthermore, the constant factors hidden within the O() notation of the convergence rates are not discussed, making it difficult to assess practical performance differences. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem in distributed computation.
*   Proposes novel and well-motivated algorithms with theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrates improvements over existing approaches through experiments.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to alternative distributed estimation techniques.
*   Lack of discussion on constant factors within the convergence rates.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed computation. The proposed algorithms offer a promising approach to estimating U-statistics in networked systems. While some refinements would strengthen the paper, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more comprehensive comparison to alternative approaches and a discussion of constant factors influencing convergence.
