This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for efficiently computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of these algorithms, establishing bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively. The paper also presents numerical experiments demonstrating the superiority of the proposed algorithms over existing approaches, such as U2-gossip.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear and concise presentation of the problem statement, the proposed algorithms, and their analysis. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing gossip algorithms and the need for new approaches. The paper's technical contributions are significant, as it introduces a novel reformulation of the problem using "phantom nodes" to model data propagation and averaging steps separately. The convergence analysis is rigorous and well-presented, and the numerical experiments are convincing in demonstrating the practical interest of the proposed algorithms.

One of the weaknesses of the paper is that it assumes a relatively high level of mathematical sophistication from the reader. While the authors provide a clear presentation of the technical details, some readers may find the notation and mathematical derivations challenging to follow. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the proposed algorithms, such as their scalability and robustness to network failures.

In terms of originality, the paper makes significant contributions to the field of decentralized computation and estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel and well-motivated, and the convergence analysis is rigorous and thorough. The paper's significance lies in its potential to enable efficient and robust computation of U-statistics in decentralized settings, which has important applications in machine learning, data mining, and other fields.

Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes significant contributions to the field of decentralized computation and estimation. While it may be challenging for some readers to follow, the paper's technical details are well-presented and rigorous, and the numerical experiments are convincing in demonstrating the practical interest of the proposed algorithms.
