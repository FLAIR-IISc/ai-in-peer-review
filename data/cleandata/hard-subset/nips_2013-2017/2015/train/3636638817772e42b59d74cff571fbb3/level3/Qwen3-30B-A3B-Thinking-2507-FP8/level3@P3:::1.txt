

# Review: "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computation: while gossip algorithms for computing separable statistics (like sample means) are well-studied, computation of U-statistics—which require averaging over pairs of observations—has received far less attention. The authors propose novel synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) gossip algorithms for distributed U-statistic estimation, building upon the foundational work on gossip algorithms for mean computation [2, 10, 15, 18, 19].

The key innovation lies in combining two processes: (1) propagation of local observations across the network using auxiliary variables, and (2) averaging of local estimates. The authors introduce a clever "phantom nodes" reformulation to analyze the convergence, which enables them to establish O(1/t) convergence for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings—both faster than the O(1/t) rate of the prior U2-Gossip algorithm [17]. Crucially, their algorithms achieve these improvements with lower memory and communication costs, as U2-Gossip requires maintaining two auxiliary observations per node and exchanging two pairs of observations per iteration. The theoretical analysis properly accounts for data-dependent terms (reflecting the difficulty of the estimation problem) and network-dependent terms (related to the spectral gap of the network graph).

The paper excels in all four evaluation criteria. For quality, the theoretical analysis is sound and well-motivated, with clear convergence bounds that are validated by extensive experiments on two real-world applications: AUC estimation (using SMVguide3 dataset) and within-cluster point scatter (using Wine Quality dataset). The empirical results consistently demonstrate superior performance of GoSta over U2-Gossip across various network topologies (complete, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz), with the performance gap widening as network size increases. For clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-written with precise pseudocode, clear figures, and a well-structured presentation that makes the technical content accessible. The originality is strong: the problem of U-statistic estimation in distributed settings is novel in the gossip literature, and the phantom nodes technique for analysis represents a creative contribution. For significance, the paper addresses an important class of statistics (AUC, variance, Gini mean difference, etc.) that are essential for many machine learning applications, while providing a practical algorithm with demonstrable advantages over prior work.

The paper is properly positioned relative to prior work, with a thorough related work section that correctly identifies U2-Gossip as the closest prior approach and clearly articulates the limitations of that method. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work (e.g., the slightly worse theoretical rate for the asynchronous algorithm) and provide a compelling argument for why the practical performance remains comparable.

The only minor weakness is that the paper doesn't explore potential extensions to higher-order U-statistics (beyond degree two), though the authors mention this as future work in their conclusion. This limitation is not substantial given the paper's clear focus and scope.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel problem formulation addressing an important gap in distributed estimation
- Superior theoretical convergence rates with practical advantages
- Rigorous analysis using the phantom nodes technique
- Comprehensive empirical validation on two meaningful applications
- Well-written, well-organized, and properly contextualized

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope to degree two U-statistics (though this is a reasonable focus)
- Minor theoretical gap in the asynchronous rate compared to synchronous case

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization and estimation. The proposed algorithms advance the state of the art in both theoretical guarantees and practical performance, with clear applications to important statistical measures. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
