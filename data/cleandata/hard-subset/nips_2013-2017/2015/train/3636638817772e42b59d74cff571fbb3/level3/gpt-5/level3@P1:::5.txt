This paper proposes two randomized gossip algorithms—GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous)—for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in a network where each node holds one observation. The key idea is to combine, at each iteration, (i) propagation of auxiliary observations through swaps along randomly selected edges and (ii) averaging of local estimates. A central technical contribution is an analysis via a “phantom nodes” construction that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging. Theoretical results establish convergence in expectation to the desired U-statistic with explicit network- and data-dependent constants: O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t / t) for the asynchronous version, with rates governed by the spectral gap of the communication graph. Empirically, the methods are evaluated on AUC and within-cluster scatter across three graph families (complete, grid, Watts–Strogatz), consistently outperforming the prior U2-gossip algorithm in both speed and across-node variance of estimates.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on randomized gossip/averaging (Boyd et al., Kempe et al.) and leverages known spectral-gap dependencies from spectral graph theory (Chung). It targets a gap in the literature: distributed computation of pairwise statistics (U-statistics), which existing gossip methods for separable sums do not address. The closest prior is U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), which propagates two observations without averaging. The authors clarify differences: GoSta uses one auxiliary observation per node and explicit averaging, leading to tighter rates and lower communication/memory per step. The analysis further refines U2-gossip’s bound, explaining its slower practical convergence due to a √n factor despite a favorable squared spectral term.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: U-statistics underpin widely used measures (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall’s tau, cluster scatter) yet are not directly supported by standard gossip.
- Solid theoretical guarantees: Explicit finite-time bounds with network (spectral gap) and data dispersion terms; expectation convergence for both sync and async settings; careful modeling of asynchronous clocks and node selection probabilities.
- Elegant analysis: The phantom network reformulation cleanly separates propagation and averaging, enabling tractable bounds and insights into constants.
- Practical benefits: Lower per-iteration memory and communication than U2-gossip; simple implementation primitives (pairwise averaging and swap).
- Empirical validation: Consistent improvements across diverse graph topologies and two application metrics; reduced across-node variance thanks to averaging; scaling trends consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Convergence only in expectation: High-probability or almost-sure convergence and concentration rates (e.g., via martingale bounds or mixing-time arguments) would strengthen the guarantees and facilitate stopping criteria.
- Assumptions about edge selection and global knowledge: The async algorithm requires pk = 2dk/|E| and uses 1/pk for unbiased iteration counting. Knowing |E| may be unrealistic; discuss how to estimate |E| on the fly (e.g., via a preliminary consensus) and robustness to estimation errors.
- Uniform edge sampling: The model draws edges uniformly at random; in practice, node-driven Poisson activations may induce degree-biased edge activations. Clarify implementation to realize uniform edge sampling in a fully decentralized way, or analyze non-uniform sampling.
- Tightness of the log t / t rate: Is the logarithmic factor intrinsic to asynchrony here, or could alternative step-size schedules or variance-reduction (e.g., Polyak–Ruppert averaging, accelerated consensus) recover O(1/t)?
- Scope limitations: Only degree-2 symmetric U-statistics are analyzed. Discuss extensions to higher-order U-statistics (m > 2) and non-symmetric kernels via symmetrization, and the computational/communication implications.
- Experimental breadth: Results focus on iteration counts; add wall-clock/communication metrics (messages, words transmitted), and stress tests on sparse/heterogeneous graphs (e.g., scale-free) and highly skewed degrees where pk varies widely. Including the “multiple observations per node” extension in experiments would improve practical relevance.
- Practicalities of H evaluation: Some kernels H can be costly in high dimension; discuss caching or mini-batching strategies and their effect on bias/variance.
- Bipartite graphs: The analysis assumes connected, non-bipartite graphs. Provide a simple fix (e.g., lazy updates/self-loops) and confirm empirically.

Quality
- Technically sound with careful derivations and explicit constants. Comparison to U2-gossip is both analytical and empirical. Some assumptions (global |E|, uniform edge sampling) could be better justified or relaxed, and stronger probabilistic guarantees would improve rigor.

Clarity
- Well organized and clearly written. Algorithm descriptions are precise; the phantom-node analysis is explained accessibly. Minor suggestions: summarize communication/memory costs per iteration in a compact table; explicitly discuss how to realize uniform edge sampling in practice.

Originality
- Novel problem framing for gossip (U-statistics) with an original analytical device (phantom networks). The asynchronous scheme with unbiased iteration tracking is a neat twist. To my knowledge, this is significantly different from prior NIPS work focused on separable sums/averaging.

Significance
- U-statistics are ubiquitous; enabling their decentralized estimation with provable rates is valuable to distributed learning on sensor/peer-to-peer networks. The methods and analyses should be useful to researchers building distributed algorithms for ranking, clustering, and testing.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with principled algorithms.
- Strong, interpretable convergence guarantees and insight into network/data dependencies.
- Empirically superior to the main prior method, with lower variance and overhead.
- Techniques (phantom networks, analysis) are of independent interest.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of high-probability bounds and limited discussion of implementation realism (|E| knowledge, uniform edge sampling).
- Experimental evaluation could be broader (communication/time, additional graph families, multiple-observation setting).
- Asynchronous rate has a log factor; discussion on tightness/acceleration is limited.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide high-probability bounds or concentration results for both algorithms?
- How sensitive is GoSta-async to misspecification of |E| in pk and 1/pk? Any adaptive estimators?
- Can the approach be extended to degree-m U-statistics and what are the resulting costs/rates?
- Would adding laziness/self-loops handle bipartite graphs without changing the rates?
- Any prospects for acceleration (e.g., Chebyshev/accelerated consensus) in the averaging step?
