

This paper addresses a significant gap in decentralized estimation by extending gossip algorithms to compute U-statistics, which are essential for key metrics like AUC, variance, and Gini mean difference but previously unaddressed in gossip frameworks. While NIPS has seen extensive work on gossip for mean/sum estimation (e.g., [2, 10, 15]), U-statistics require pairwise averaging, a problem only partially tackled by U2-gossip [17]. The authors propose GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, novel algorithms that simultaneously propagate data and maintain local U-statistic estimates. The core innovation lies in reformulating the problem using "phantom nodes" to decouple data propagation from averaging, enabling rigorous convergence analysis. The synchronous algorithm achieves O(1/t) convergence with network-dependent constants tied to the graph Laplacian's spectral gap, while the asynchronous variant converges at O(log t/t), both significantly improving upon U2-gossip's rate and reducing memory/communication costs (which scale with dimensionality in [17]).

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The phantom node construction (Section 4.1) is elegant and enables tight rate bounds that explicitly quantify the interplay between data complexity (via ∥h−Ûn(H)1n∥) and network topology (via spectral gap βn−1). The proofs are thorough, with supplementary material providing necessary lemmas (e.g., relating λ2(2) to βn−1). Experiments on AUC (SMVguide3 dataset, n=1260) and within-cluster scatter (Wine Quality, n=1599) convincingly demonstrate superiority over U2-gossip across three network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), with GoSta-sync achieving lower average error and reduced variance. Crucially, the performance gap widens with network size (Fig. 3a), aligning with the theoretical analysis. The asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) matches synchronous performance in practice despite the looser theoretical bound (Fig. 3b), validating the practicality of the approach.

Originality is strong: U-statistics in gossip settings are a clear NIPS gap, and GoSta’s combination of data propagation via auxiliary observations and local averaging is novel. The comparison to [17] is precise—U2-gossip’s √n factor in its convergence bound (Eq. 10) is rigorously explained as a consequence of lacking averaging, which GoSta avoids. This distinguishes the work from prior gossip extensions (e.g., [15] for separable functions) and situates it within the broader NIPS literature on decentralized computation.

Significance is high. U-statistics underpin critical ML tasks (e.g., AUC for fairness-aware classifiers, within-cluster scatter for distributed clustering), and the paper enables their efficient decentralized computation without centralized coordination—addressing real-world constraints in sensor networks or privacy-sensitive applications. The algorithms’ low per-iteration memory (O(1) auxiliary observations vs. U2-gossip’s O(2)) and communication (single edge swap vs. two) make them practical for high-dimensional data, a key advantage over prior work.

Minor weaknesses include: (1) The asynchronous rate bound O(log t/t) is less sharp than the synchronous O(1/t) or standard gossip mean rates (e.g., O(e−ct) in [2]), though the authors correctly attribute this to the absence of global synchronization; (2) Experiments exclusively compare to U2-gossip and lack baselines for decentralized mean estimation (e.g., [2]) to contextualize the speedup; (3) The phantom node technique, while powerful for analysis, is not immediately intuitive—adding a small diagram would aid clarity (a minor note for revision).

The paper is well-structured, with clear problem formulation (Section 2.2), concise related work (Section 3), and focused experiments (Section 5). It avoids overclaiming, explicitly acknowledging U2-gossip’s prior contribution while demonstrating clear improvements. The writing is precise, and the supplementary material adequately supports the claims.

In summary, this work makes a rigorous, impactful contribution to decentralized estimation. It fills a meaningful gap in gossip literature, provides tight convergence guarantees with practical implications, and delivers strong empirical validation. The technical depth, relevance to active NIPS themes (distributed ML), and clear presentation make it a compelling candidate for acceptance. The minor weaknesses are readily addressable and do not undermine the core advance. This paper advances the state of the art in distributed U-statistic computation and will be valuable to researchers working on decentralized ML systems.
