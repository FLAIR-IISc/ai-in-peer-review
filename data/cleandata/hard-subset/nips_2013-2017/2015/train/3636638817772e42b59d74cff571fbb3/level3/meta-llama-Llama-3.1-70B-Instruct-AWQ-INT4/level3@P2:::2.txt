This paper proposes a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing pairwise U-statistics across networked nodes. The algorithm, referred to as GoSta, relies on a novel reformulation of the problem using "phantom nodes" to model data propagation and estimate updates separately. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's convergence rate, establishing a bound of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of decentralized computation, and the reviewer finds the approach to be innovative and well-motivated. However, to further strengthen the paper, the reviewer suggests establishing stronger connections to existing gossip literature and related ideas. In particular, the key algorithmic trick of swapping a secondary state (initial observation) during averaging, allowing observations to perform coupled random walks, should be highlighted in the main text.

The reviewer also notes that the induced edge-distribution from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" model could be clarified, as "drawing a random edge" is ambiguous. Furthermore, a comparison to a flooding/memory-heavy alternative (nodes store received tagged observations) would help to demonstrate the tradeoffs of the proposed algorithm and whether the 1/t convergence rate is good or bad.

The related work section could be expanded to include citations to Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) and other gossip approaches omitted from the current references. Additionally, the reviewer suggests investigating lifting constructions (e.g., LADA, lifted Markov chains) as they appear closely related and may accelerate mixing.

The theorems and analysis should state assumptions on the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and make error guarantees explicit as a function of initial data. The reviewer was unable to verify the supplemental proofs due to time constraints.

Several small language, notation, and typo fixes are suggested, including a clarification on bipartite directed-graphs. The experiments should include baseline comparisons to demonstrate empirical benefits.

Overall, the reviewer finds the paper to be well-written and the contribution valuable. With some revisions to address the suggested comments, the reviewer raises their score from 7 to 8 and recommends acceptance.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The algorithm is carefully designed, and the convergence rate bounds are established through a rigorous analysis. However, the reviewer suggests clarifying the assumptions on the initial data distribution and making error guarantees explicit as a function of initial data.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear. However, some minor language and notation fixes are suggested to improve readability. The reviewer also recommends highlighting the key algorithmic trick of swapping a secondary state during averaging.

**Originality:** The paper proposes a novel approach to computing pairwise U-statistics in a decentralized setting. The use of "phantom nodes" to model data propagation and estimate updates separately is innovative and well-motivated.
