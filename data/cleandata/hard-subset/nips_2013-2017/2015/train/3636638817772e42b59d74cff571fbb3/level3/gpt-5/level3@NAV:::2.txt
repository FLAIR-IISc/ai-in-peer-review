This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous gossip-style algorithms to compute pairwise U-statistics over a network by combining local averaging with a clever “state swap” in which nodes exchange an auxiliary observation. The key idea is that each node maintains Xi and an auxiliary Yi; upon edge activation, the two endpoints average their estimates and swap Yi, so that observations execute coupled random walks over the graph. The analysis uses a “phantom network” construction to decouple averaging and data propagation and proves convergence in expectation at O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async), with explicit data- and graph-dependent constants. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter compare favorably to U2-gossip.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on classical and modern gossip literature (e.g., Tsitsiklis; Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE; geographic gossip), but the connection could be tightened, especially wrt mixing, edge sampling models, and accelerated consensus.

Quality: The technical development is interesting and appears sound, but I could not verify the supplemental proofs due to time constraints. Please make assumptions on data explicit in the theorems (worst-case fixed sample vs probabilistic Xi; boundedness or moment conditions on H), and express error bounds directly as functions of those assumptions. Clarify the edge-activation model: “draw a random edge” vs “a node wakes up and picks a neighbor” induce different edge distributions (uniform over E vs degree-weighted); ensure pk, Wα(G), and constants are consistent, and discuss bipartite graphs (e.g., laziness). Also highlight prominently in the main text that swapping Yi is the core trick enabling coupled random walks.

Originality: Extending gossip to exact U-statistics with one auxiliary state per node is novel and practically relevant. The phantom-node analysis is nice.

Significance: Useful for decentralized AUC/variance-like metrics. To strengthen impact, compare against a flooding/memory-heavy baseline where nodes store tagged observations (trading O(n d) memory for faster accuracy) and discuss whether the O(1/t) rate is optimal under memory/traffic constraints.

Clarity: Generally clear. Minor fixes: use “non-bipartite” consistently; fix typos (“perfomance”); clarify Eq. (6) constants (check 1−λ2(2) = βn−1/|E| scaling). Expand related work: include Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) and accelerated mixing via lifting (e.g., LADA; lifted/nonreversible Markov chains) which could speed the Yi random walks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple algorithmic idea; reduced memory/communication vs U2-gossip
- Convergence guarantees with data/graph dependence
- Empirical gains

Cons:
- Limited baselines; edge-model ambiguity
- Missing assumptions/explicit error–data linkage
- Potential improvements via lifting not explored

After revisions addressing these points, I raise my score from 7 to 8 and recommend acceptance.
