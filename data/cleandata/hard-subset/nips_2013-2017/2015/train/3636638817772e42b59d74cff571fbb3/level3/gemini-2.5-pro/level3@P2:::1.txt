This paper tackles the problem of estimating U-statistics of degree two in a decentralized network setting using gossip algorithms. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations, are fundamental in many machine learning tasks (e.g., calculating AUC, sample variance, or within-cluster scatter), but cannot be computed by standard gossip algorithms designed for separable sums like sample means. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, named "GoSta," which efficiently compute these statistics. The core idea is to have each node maintain a local estimate and a single auxiliary data observation. At each step, the algorithms combine a local estimate update, a standard gossip averaging step on the estimates between neighboring nodes, and a data propagation step where the auxiliary observations are swapped.

This work builds upon the literature of gossip algorithms for distributed averaging [2, 10] but extends it to the more complex, non-separable case of U-statistics. To the best of my knowledge, the only direct prior work on this specific problem is Pelckmans and Suykens [17], who proposed the U2-gossip algorithm. The present work provides a significant improvement over [17] by proposing algorithms that are not only more efficient in terms of memory and communication costs but also achieve faster theoretical convergence rates.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of their proposed methods. By reformulating the problem using an elegant "phantom network" construction, they derive convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. These bounds clearly articulate the dependency on both network topology (via the spectral gap) and data properties. The paper's contributions are further substantiated by a strong set of experiments on real-world datasets, evaluating the estimation of AUC and within-cluster point scatter on various network graphs.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically very solid. The proposed algorithms are simple and elegant, yet the convergence analysis is non-trivial and carefully executed. The use of the "phantom network" is a creative tool for decoupling the data propagation and estimate averaging dynamics, leading to a clear proof. The claims are well-supported by both this theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimental results, which demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed methods over the existing state-of-the-art. The authors are also careful to refine the analysis of the competing U2-gossip algorithm, providing a clear and fair comparison.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction provides excellent motivation for the problem and clearly situates the work within the existing literature. The problem statement, algorithms, and theoretical results are all presented with precision. The flow from the synchronous to the more complex asynchronous setting is logical and helps build the reader's understanding.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While gossip algorithms are a well-established field, their application to U-statistics is a relatively unexplored and important direction. The proposed GoSta algorithms represent a novel and effective combination of data propagation (via random walks) and estimate averaging. The development and analysis of a fully asynchronous algorithm for this problem is a particularly significant contribution, as it addresses a key requirement for many real-world decentralized systems.

**Significance:** This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed machine learning. U-statistics are a broad and important class of functions, and this work provides a practical, efficient, and theoretically-grounded tool for computing them in a decentralized manner. The demonstrated improvements over the prior state-of-the-art in convergence rate, communication overhead, and memory usage are substantial. The ideas presented here are likely to be influential and could serve as a foundation for future work, for instance, in the development of distributed optimization algorithms for objectives based on U-statistics.

### Weaknesses

It is difficult to find significant weaknesses in this paper. One minor point for discussion could be the theoretical gap between the synchronous O(1/t) rate and the asynchronous O(log t/t) rate. The experiments in Figure 3b show that the practical performance of the two algorithms is quite comparable. It would be interesting if the authors could briefly comment on whether they believe this theoretical gap is fundamental to the asynchronous setting or potentially an artifact of the analysis that could be tightened in future work. This, however, is a minor point and does not detract from the overall quality of the contribution.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem: distributed computation of U-statistics.
*   Proposes novel, simple, and efficient synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms.
*   Provides a rigorous and insightful theoretical analysis with strong convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrably improves upon the prior state-of-the-art (U2-gossip) in terms of convergence speed, memory, and communication costs.
*   The contribution is validated with thorough experiments on real-world machine learning tasks.
*   The paper is excellently written and presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   I find no compelling arguments against accepting this paper.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research. It solves an important problem with both theoretical rigor and practical relevance. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
