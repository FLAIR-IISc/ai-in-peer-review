Summary
This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous gossip-style algorithms (GoSta-sync/GoSta-async) for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a connected network. The key idea is to combine (i) local running averages of H(Xi, Yk), and (ii) a per-iteration swap of an auxiliary “secondary state” (initial observation) between the two communicating nodes. This swap makes the observations perform coupled random walks over the network, enabling each node to sample diverse partners for its own Xi and thereby approximate hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi, Xj). The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple averaging from data propagation, and yields rates O(1/t) (sync) and O((log t)/t) (async), with explicit constants depending on the graph spectral gap and data dispersion terms. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter show improvements over U2-gossip with lower memory and communication costs.

Relation to prior work: The problem extends classical randomized gossip for separable sums (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE 2010). The algorithmic trick of swapping an auxiliary state is reminiscent of multi-token random walks and rumor spreading (Karp et al. 2000) but tailored to U-statistics. Compared to U2-gossip (Pelckmans and Suykens, 2009), the proposed methods use less memory/communication and empirically converge faster.

Strengths
- Clear decentralization of non-separable U-statistics with constant memory per node and simple per-iteration updates.
- Elegant analysis via a phantom network; explicit dependence on spectral gap and data dispersion is insightful.
- Both synchronous and asynchronous variants with rigorous (expected) convergence guarantees.
- Practical and relevant applications (AUC, within-cluster scatter) with consistent empirical gains over U2-gossip.

Weaknesses and requested revisions
- Make stronger connections to the gossip literature:
  - Expand related work to include Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE 2010) in more detail, geographic gossip (Dimakis et al. 2006), push-sum and averaging on directed/time-varying graphs (e.g., Kempe et al. 2003; Nedić and Ozdaglar 2009), and accelerated consensus (Chebyshev/polynomial filtering).
  - Discuss multi-message rumor spreading and multi-token random walks as conceptual relatives to the auxiliary-state swapping.
- Highlight the key algorithmic trick prominently in the main text: explicitly frame “swap of auxiliary observations so that observations perform coupled random walks,” and explain why coupling plus averaging yields the 1/t behavior.
- Clarify the edge-selection model:
  - “Drawing a random edge” is ambiguous. Distinguish clearly:
    1) Edge-centric: pick an undirected edge uniformly; then Pr(node k participates) = d_k/|E|.
    2) Node-centric: a node wakes (uniformly or by independent Poisson clocks) and contacts a neighbor (uniformly or via P); the induced distribution over undirected edges is generally non-uniform, Pr({i,j}) ∝ 1/d_i + 1/d_j if wake-ups are uniform over nodes and neighbor choice is uniform.
  - In Algorithm 2 you set pk = 2 d_k/|E| under “uniform over edges.” If edges are undirected and sampled uniformly, pk should be d_k/|E|; if you work with ordered edges or a node-centric model, derive and state the exact pk. Please settle the factor-of-two and unify the definition of |E|.
- Compare to a flooding/memory-heavy baseline:
  - Baseline: nodes store all tagged observations they receive; with push–pull flooding each Xi is disseminated network-wide (multi-rumor spreading). This yields exact U-statistics once all tokens arrive but requires O(nd) memory per node and high communication. Time scales with network mixing/diameter and congestion (on complete graphs, O(log n) for a single rumor; for n rumors congestion dominates).
  - Discuss the tradeoff: your O(1/t) (or O((log t)/t)) with O(d) memory vs. exact but memory-heavy flooding. Is 1/t “good”? For constant memory and local communication, 1/t is expected and competitive; your empirical results support favorable constants.
- Lifting/nonreversible Markov chains:
  - Investigate and cite lifting constructions (e.g., LADA; nonreversible/lifted Markov chains) that accelerate mixing for consensus by breaking reversibility. Because your data propagation is a random walk on G, nonreversible lifts could reduce cover/mixing times and improve constants in the bounds. Please discuss feasibility of replacing Wα(G) with a lifted/nonreversible kernel.
- Assumptions and guarantees:
  - State clearly whether the bounds are worst-case over fixed data or in expectation over a data-generating distribution. The current theorems are in expectation over algorithmic randomness; please make error terms explicit in norms of H and h, and consider high-probability bounds via martingale/concentration arguments.
  - For bipartite graphs, clarify the non-bipartite requirement and propose standard fixes (e.g., lazy updates/self-loops) to remove periodicity; relate to push-sum for directed graphs.
- Experiments:
  - Add baselines beyond U2-gossip: (i) flooding with tagged observations (report memory/communication), (ii) centralized oracle, (iii) node-centric vs edge-centric selection, (iv) an accelerated/chebyshev-filtered consensus step if possible.
  - Report message complexity and memory per node to reach a target error, and sensitivity to spectral gap (vary Watts–Strogatz rewiring).
- Proofs: I could not verify the supplemental proofs due to time constraints. Please ensure the final version is self-contained, fixes any ambiguities (e.g., pk, |E|), and includes all lemmas used in the main bounds.
- Language/notation/typos (selected):
  - “Nodes need not to be aware” -> “need not be aware.”
  - Be consistent about undirected vs directed edges; if you treat ordered edges, state it once and keep |E| consistent.
  - When defining bipartite graphs, specify undirected bipartite; for directed, clarify strongly connected/aperiodic assumptions.
  - Define Wα(G) once and recall its relation to L(G); avoid overloading α.
  - Explicitly define the norms used in bounds and their interpretation (data dispersion).

Evaluation
- Quality: Strong. Sound algorithms and analyses with explicit constants; asynchronous variant is valuable. Please add clarity on activation models and assumptions, and strengthen experiments with baselines and complexity reporting.
- Clarity: Generally good, but the edge-selection model and pk need precise, unambiguous definitions. Bring the key swap/coupled-walk insight forward.
- Originality: Novel extension of gossip to U-statistics with a neat auxiliary-state mechanism and phantom-graph analysis.
- Significance: Useful for decentralized evaluation of common ML metrics (AUC, scatter). Likely to be adopted and extended; connections to accelerated mixing open a promising direction.

Pros for acceptance
- New problem formulation with practical importance.
- Simple, scalable algorithms with rigorous rates and explicit dependencies.
- Clear empirical improvements over prior U2-gossip with lower resource use.

Cons / concerns
- Ambiguity in activation/edge distribution and pk.
- Limited experimental baselines and lack of message/memory accounting.
- Related work on accelerated/lifted gossip not yet connected.

Recommendation
Score: 8 (accept). After author revisions addressing the clarifications, related work, and experimental baselines, this will be a strong NIPS paper.
