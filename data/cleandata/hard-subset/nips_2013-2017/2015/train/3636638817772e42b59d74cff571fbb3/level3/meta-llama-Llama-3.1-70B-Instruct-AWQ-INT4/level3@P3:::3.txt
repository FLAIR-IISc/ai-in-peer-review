This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, highlighting the limitations of existing gossip algorithms in handling quantities that depend on pairs of observations.

The proposed algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, are well-motivated and clearly explained. The synchronous algorithm, GoSta-sync, is shown to converge to the true U-statistic at a rate of O(1/t), with a data-dependent term that reflects the hardness of the estimation problem and a network-dependent term related to the spectral gap of the network graph. The asynchronous algorithm, GoSta-async, achieves a slightly worse convergence rate of O(log t/t).

The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the algorithms, including a reformulation of the problem using "phantom nodes" to model the data propagation and averaging steps separately. The convergence bounds are derived using a transition matrix that accounts for the averaging step, data propagation, and estimate update.

The paper also presents numerical experiments on two real-world datasets, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed algorithms over the U2-gossip baseline. The results show that GoSta-sync outperforms U2-gossip on various network types, with a lower variance of estimates across nodes.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed algorithms are novel and well-motivated, addressing a gap in existing gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics.
* The analysis is comprehensive and rigorous, providing insights into the convergence rates and dependencies on data and network properties.
* The numerical experiments are thorough and well-executed, demonstrating the practical interest of the proposed algorithms.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing algorithms, including a discussion of the trade-offs between convergence rates, memory, and communication costs.
* The authors mention that the algorithms can be extended to the situation where nodes contain multiple observations, but this is not fully explored in the paper.
* The reviewer requests per-iteration run-times for both the proposed method and U2-gossip, as this would provide a more complete understanding of the practical utility of the algorithms.

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with gossip algorithms and U-statistics. The contributions are significant, and the paper has the potential to make a valuable impact in the field of decentralized estimation and machine learning.
