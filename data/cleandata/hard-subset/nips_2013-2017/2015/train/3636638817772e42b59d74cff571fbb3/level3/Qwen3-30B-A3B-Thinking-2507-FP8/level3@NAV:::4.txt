

This paper addresses the important problem of computing U-statistics in distributed networks, extending gossip algorithms to handle statistics that depend on pairs of observations rather than individual data points. The authors propose "GoSta" algorithms (both synchronous and asynchronous) for decentralized estimation of U-statistics, which they theoretically analyze with convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. The approach is intuitive, using phantom nodes to reformulate the problem and enabling simultaneous data propagation and local estimation. The work builds on prior gossip algorithms for mean estimation [2, 10, 15] but extends them to handle more complex statistics like AUC and within-cluster point scatter.

Strengths include the clear problem formulation, comprehensive theoretical analysis with explicit convergence bounds, and practical relevance to important statistical measures. The paper demonstrates empirical superiority over U2-gossip [17] in experiments on real datasets, showing faster convergence and lower variance. The problem is significant as U-statistics are essential for describing global population properties in distributed settings.

However, the paper has a major weakness: it lacks comparison to intuitive or randomized baselines beyond U2-gossip. The authors claim their method "surpasses the previously introduced approach" without providing meaningful benchmarks against simpler alternatives (e.g., naive distributed computation, other gossip variants, or centralized implementations). Without such comparisons, the claimed superiority is difficult to substantiate, and the experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical significance of the theoretical improvements.

The novelty is unclear; while the paper extends gossip algorithms to U-statistics, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work on distributed U-statistics [17]. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work differs from and improves upon existing methods.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting problem with sound theoretical foundations but is weakened by inadequate experimental validation. I recommend rejection due to the lack of proper baseline comparisons, which undermines the significance claim. To strengthen the paper, the authors should include comparisons to intuitive baselines and provide more detailed experimental analysis.
