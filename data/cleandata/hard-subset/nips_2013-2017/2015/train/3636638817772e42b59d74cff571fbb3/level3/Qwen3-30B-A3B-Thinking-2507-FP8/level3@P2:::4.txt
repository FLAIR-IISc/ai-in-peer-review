

# Review for "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computing literature: the efficient computation of U-statistics in decentralized networks. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for computing separable statistics (like the sample mean), the paper recognizes that U-statistics—which require averaging over pairs of observations—are fundamentally different and more challenging to compute in a distributed setting. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) for estimating U-statistics, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively, and demonstrating their superiority over the prior U2-gossip approach [17].

The paper's main contribution lies in extending gossip algorithms to handle pairwise statistics through a clever "phantom nodes" reformulation that enables clean convergence analysis. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with explicit bounds showing how convergence rates depend on both data properties and network topology (via spectral gap). The authors clearly demonstrate that their approach achieves faster convergence than U2-gossip while requiring lower memory and communication costs, particularly important for high-dimensional data.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough introduction that contextualizes the problem within existing gossip literature [2, 10, 18, 19] and clearly articulates the limitations of prior work on U-statistics [17]. The experimental evaluation on two important applications—AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter—using real datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) provides practical evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness. The results consistently show that GoSta outperforms U2-gossip across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), with the performance gap widening as network size increases.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. The most critical issue is the lack of comparison to baseline methods beyond U2-gossip. The authors acknowledge that U2-gossip is the only prior work on this problem, but they fail to include intuitive randomized baselines (e.g., naive distributed computation where each node computes the full U-statistic locally) or standard gossip-based approaches that might be adapted to this problem. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed superiority is truly significant or merely a consequence of U2-gossip's specific limitations.

The novelty claim is also underdeveloped. While the phantom nodes approach is clever, it's unclear how much the proposed algorithms differ from U2-gossip in terms of conceptual contribution. The paper states that GoSta "largely improves upon" U2-gossip but doesn't clearly articulate what the key conceptual differences are. The experimental results are limited to two applications, and the paper doesn't explore how the algorithms perform on other important U-statistics (e.g., Gini mean difference or Kendall tau).

The paper demonstrates good scientific quality with sound theoretical analysis and meaningful experiments, but the lack of comprehensive baselines significantly weakens the claims of superiority. The significance is clear (U-statistics are widely used in machine learning), but the contribution would be stronger with more thorough experimental validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses an important problem in distributed learning that has been largely overlooked
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with explicit convergence rates
- Demonstrates practical relevance through experiments on real datasets
- The phantom nodes technique is a novel analytical approach

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Lacks comparison to meaningful baseline methods beyond U2-gossip
- Novelty over prior work is not clearly articulated
- Experimental evaluation is limited to two applications
- Claims of superiority are not sufficiently supported by comparative evidence

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper addresses an interesting problem with sound theoretical contributions, but the lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons significantly undermines the empirical claims. I recommend the authors strengthen the experimental section by including additional baselines and more thorough comparisons to demonstrate the true advantages of their approach.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**
1. Add comparison to a naive distributed implementation (each node computes the full U-statistic locally)
2. Include comparison to a simple adaptation of standard gossip algorithms to this problem
3. Expand the experimental section to include more U-statistic applications
4. Clarify the conceptual novelty relative to U2-gossip in the introduction and related work
5. Discuss potential limitations of the approach (e.g., sensitivity to network topology) in more detail

The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of distributed learning, but requires significant strengthening of the empirical evaluation to support the claims of superiority.
