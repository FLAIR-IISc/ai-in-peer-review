- Addresses computing degree-two U-statistics in decentralized networks via new GoSta gossip algorithms (synchronous and asynchronous).  
- Core algorithmic idea: nodes maintain a local estimate and an auxiliary observation, averaging estimates and swapping auxiliaries to induce coupled random walks that pair data points.  
- Theoretical results: convergence rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous), depending on graph spectral gap and data-dependent variance.  
- Analysis uses a novel "phantom network" construction to decouple data propagation and estimate averaging.  
- Empirical validation: experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter show GoSta outperforms prior U2-gossip in practice.  
- Strength: tackles a novel and significant problem—decentralized computation of U-statistics relevant to ML/statistics.  
- Strength: elegant, simple mechanism (swap + averaging) that avoids global knowledge and excessive communication.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis with strong rates for both sync and async settings.  
- Strength: practical advantages over prior work—lower memory (one auxiliary per node) and less communication per iteration.  
- Weakness: the swap mechanism is not highlighted early enough; introduction should emphasize it as the central contribution.  
- Weakness: related work section is narrow; should connect to broader gossip literature and cite surveys (e.g., Dimakis et al. 2011) and lifting/LADA-like techniques.  
- Weakness: need baseline comparison to a flooding/full-communication strategy to contextualize the O(1/t) rate in terms of total communication/time to accuracy.  
- Weakness: ambiguity in the communication model—clarify whether edges are sampled uniformly or nodes wake and pick neighbors, especially for irregular graphs.  
- Weakness: experiments should include simple additional baselines (e.g., swapping auxiliaries without averaging) to isolate the averaging step's contribution.  
- Question: theoretical bounds depend on norms of data-dependent terms—are these worst-case over X, and can probabilistic data assumptions yield tighter bounds?  
- Question: is the non-bipartite graph assumption strictly necessary for data propagation convergence, given averaging alone needs only connectivity?  
- Question: in GoSta-async, the ordering of averaging and local update seems counterintuitive—clarify whether the update ordering is intentional and motivated.  
- Conclusion: strong, novel paper with convincing theory and experiments; recommend acceptance if authors clarify mechanism, broaden literature context, and add convergence/benchmarking context.
