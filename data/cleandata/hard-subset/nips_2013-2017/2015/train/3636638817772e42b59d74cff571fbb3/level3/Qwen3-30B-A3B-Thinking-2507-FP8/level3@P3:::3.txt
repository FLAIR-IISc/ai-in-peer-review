

# Review: Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics

This paper addresses a significant gap in decentralized computation: the efficient estimation of U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations) in distributed networks. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for mean estimation, they have not been well-suited for U-statistics, which require averaging over pairs of observations. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, establishing convergence rate bounds and demonstrating empirical superiority over the previous U2-gossip baseline.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, effectively positioning its contribution within the context of existing gossip algorithms for distributed computation. The authors correctly identify that prior work (notably U2-gossip [17]) has addressed this problem but with limitations in memory requirements, communication costs, and asynchrony support. The paper's core innovation is the introduction of a "phantom node" technique to reformulate the problem, enabling the analysis of data propagation and estimation updates separately. This clever analytical approach allows the authors to establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, with explicit dependence on both data properties and network topology (through the spectral gap).

The paper's strengths are substantial. It makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis that improves upon previous work in convergence rates and resource requirements. The empirical evaluation on two important applications (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) using real datasets across three distinct network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz) provides compelling evidence that the proposed algorithms outperform U2-gossip. The results show both lower average relative error and reduced variance across nodes, with the performance gap widening as network size increases. The paper also provides valuable theoretical insights about how network connectivity affects convergence rates.

The paper is exceptionally well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors thoughtfully acknowledge the limitations of their approach compared to U2-gossip, particularly regarding the potential trade-off between convergence rate and per-iteration computational cost.

However, I have one significant concern that needs addressing before the paper can be accepted. The authors claim their algorithms "surpass[es] the previously introduced approach" but do not provide per-iteration run-times for their method versus U2-gossip. This is critical because the paper's practical utility depends on whether the improved convergence rate outweighs any potential slowdown in individual iterations. If the proposed method's iterations are substantially slower than U2-gossip (due to the additional bookkeeping required for the auxiliary observations and data propagation), the overall runtime advantage could be negated, especially in resource-constrained networks.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on decentralized computation. The problem of U-statistics estimation is important in machine learning (AUC, variance, Gini mean difference are all widely used metrics), and the authors successfully address a gap in the gossip literature. The work extends beyond previous gossip algorithms by handling more complex statistical functionals while maintaining the simplicity and robustness that make gossip algorithms appealing for distributed systems.

Regarding the four review criteria:

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims through both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The convergence bounds are properly derived and include meaningful data and network-dependent terms. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The phantom node technique is well-explained, and the algorithms are precisely described. The supplementary material provides additional details that would be helpful for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper identifies and addresses a clear gap in the literature (U-statistics estimation via gossip algorithms), with a novel algorithmic approach that improves upon previous work. The combination of the phantom node reformulation with gossip algorithms for U-statistics represents a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:** U-statistics are widely used in machine learning applications, and the paper demonstrates practical improvements over previous methods. The algorithms could be valuable in distributed sensor networks, social media analysis, and other large-scale distributed systems where communication efficiency is critical.

**Pro arguments for acceptance:** 
- Addresses a meaningful gap in distributed computation literature
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates
- Demonstrates empirical superiority over previous methods on real datasets
- Well-written, clear, and technically sound contribution

**Con arguments for acceptance:**
- Requires additional experimental data on per-iteration run-times to fully assess practical utility
- While the convergence rates are improved, the practical benefit depends on iteration speed

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to distributed computation. The minor concern regarding per-iteration computational cost can be addressed with additional experimental results, which would significantly strengthen the paper's practical relevance. With these results, the paper would represent a significant advance in the area of gossip algorithms for distributed estimation.
