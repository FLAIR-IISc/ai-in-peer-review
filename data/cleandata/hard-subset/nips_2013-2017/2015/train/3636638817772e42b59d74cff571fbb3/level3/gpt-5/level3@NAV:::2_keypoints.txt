- Proposal: synchronous and asynchronous gossip-style algorithms to compute pairwise U-statistics using local averaging plus an auxiliary Yi that is swapped between nodes.  
- Core idea: swapping Yi is the crucial trick that creates coupled random walks for data propagation.  
- Analysis: uses a “phantom network” construction to decouple averaging and propagation; proves convergence in expectation O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async) with explicit data- and graph-dependent constants.  
- Experiments: AUC and within-cluster scatter tasks show favorable performance compared to U2-gossip.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on classical/modern gossip literature but needs tighter connections (mixing, edge sampling models, accelerated consensus).  
- Edge-activation ambiguity: clarify “draw a random edge” vs “node wakes and picks neighbor” (uniform over E vs degree-weighted); ensure pk, Wα(G), and constants are consistent and address bipartite graphs/laziness.  
- Assumptions: make data assumptions explicit in theorems (fixed worst-case samples vs probabilistic Xi; boundedness or moment conditions on H) and express error bounds as functions of those assumptions.  
- Constants/details: clarify constants in equations (e.g., scaling of 1−λ2(2) = β n−1/|E|) and check Eq. (6).  
- Baseline comparison: add a flooding/memory-heavy baseline (nodes store tagged observations) to compare trade-offs between memory/traffic and accuracy.  
- Optimality discussion: discuss whether the O(1/t) rate is optimal under memory/communication constraints and traffic models.  
- Potential improvements: consider accelerated mixing techniques (lifting/nonreversible chains, LADA) to speed Yi random walks.  
- Clarity/edits: paper is generally clear but fix minor typos (e.g., “perfomance”), use “non-bipartite” consistently, and prominently highlight the Yi-swap trick in the main text.  
- Pros for acceptance: simple and novel algorithm, reduced memory/communication vs U2-gossip, data/graph-dependent convergence guarantees, and empirical improvements.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited baselines, edge-model ambiguity, missing explicit assumptions/error–data linkage, and unexplored improvements via lifting.  
- Recommendation: after addressing these revisions the reviewer raises score from 7 to 8 and recommends acceptance.
