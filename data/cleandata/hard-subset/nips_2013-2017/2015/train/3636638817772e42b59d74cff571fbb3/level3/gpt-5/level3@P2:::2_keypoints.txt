- Tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau, etc.) in networks.
- Algorithm: each node keeps one auxiliary observation Yk, updates running average H(Xk,Yk), and on edge activation nodes average estimates and swap Y’s.
- Observations perform coupled random walks while estimates mix via standard gossip (propagation + averaging).
- Analysis uses a “phantom network” to decouple propagation and averaging, yielding clear convergence analysis.
- Convergence rates: O(1/t) synchronous and O(log t / t) asynchronous, with constants depending explicitly on the graph spectral gap.
- Contrasts favorably with prior U2-gossip (better rates and memory/communication trade-offs); supported by experiments.
- Well situated in randomized gossip/consensus literature; potential links to lifting/nonreversible walks and location-aided consensus not exploited.
- Phantom-node analysis is elegant and appears sound; spectral-gap dependence matches intuition.
- Asynchronous scheme is unbiased with per-node counters mk, but constant c′(G) is not explicit and should be specified.
- Need explicit assumptions on data: clarify whether results are worst-case over fixed X,H or in expectation over data distribution.
- Recommend high-probability bounds (e.g., bounded/sub-Gaussian H) and discussion of whether O(1/t) is minimax or provide lower bounds.
- Activation/edge-sampling model is ambiguous; specify induced edge distribution in async model and reconcile pk = 2dk/|E|.
- For bipartite graphs, state lazy modification explicitly and clarify whether it removes non-bipartite requirement and its effect on constants.
- Empirical comparison should include stronger baselines: flooding/caching (various cache sizes), push-sum, and centralized reference with communication normalization.
- Report communication and memory per iteration, message complexity to reach fixed error, wall-clock where possible, and node-wise convergence variability.
- Vary spectral gap systematically to demonstrate how empirical rates track 1−λ2.
- The central mechanism (swapping auxiliary observation) should be highlighted earlier; include a small illustrative example for intuition.
- Theorems should state data assumptions (fixed X, bounded H), define all data-dependent norms, and specify all constants in bounds (e.g., c′(G)) and their spectral dependence.
- Unify activation model notation (uniform edge sampling vs. node activation) and consistent use of symbols like Wα(G).
- Pros: important problem, simple practical algorithm with low memory/communication, clean analysis, explicit spectral-gap dependence, improved empirical performance.
- Cons: activation/edge-selection ambiguities, unspecified async constants, missing stronger baselines and communication/memory-vs-accuracy study, incomplete data-assumption and probabilistic-guarantee discussion, limited exploration of acceleration via lifting.
- Key questions for authors: precise edge sampling distributions; effect of laziness on bipartite graphs; existence of high-probability bounds and tightness of O(1/t); impact of lifting/nonreversible schemes; quantitative communication/memory comparisons vs U2-gossip and flooding.
- Minor fixes: unify Wα(G) notation, define norms and constants on first use, fix typos/hyphenation/phrasing, and clarify directed/bipartite remarks.
- Overall recommendation: accept (rating 8) pending clarifications, more explicit constants/assumptions, and stronger empirical baselines.
