This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner using gossip algorithms. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations (e.g., sample variance, AUC, within-cluster scatter), are not computable by standard gossip algorithms that focus on separable functions like the sample mean. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, named GoSta, where nodes in a network iteratively perform two actions: they average their local estimate of the U-statistic with a neighbor, and they swap an "auxiliary" data observation with that neighbor. This dual mechanism ensures that both the estimates and the raw data propagate through the network. The main contribution is a thorough theoretical analysis of these algorithms, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. The analysis, which relies on an interesting "phantom network" construction, shows an explicit dependency on the network's spectral gap and data-dependent variance terms. The proposed method is positioned as a significant improvement over the only known prior work in this area, U2-gossip by Pelckmans & Suykens [17], in terms of convergence rate, memory, and communication costs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. As distributed systems and large-scale machine learning become more prevalent, the need for decentralized algorithms that can compute complex statistics beyond simple averages is clear. U-statistics represent a broad and useful class of such measures. Applying gossip-style algorithms to this problem is a natural but non-trivial extension of the existing literature, and this work appears to be one of the first to provide a rigorous and efficient solution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is the main strength of this paper. The proofs are comprehensive, and the derivation of explicit convergence rates is a solid contribution. The use of the "phantom network" G̃ is a clever analytical device that allows for a clean decomposition of the data propagation and estimate averaging steps. The resulting bounds, which connect the convergence speed to both the network topology (via the spectral gap) and the data properties (via variance-like terms), are insightful and align with the known behavior of gossip algorithms.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, the proposed algorithms are presented intuitively, and the main theoretical results are stated precisely. The distinction between the synchronous and asynchronous settings is handled well.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper is the limited scope of its experimental evaluation. The authors only compare their GoSta algorithms against the U2-gossip algorithm [17]. While this is the most direct competitor, the comparison feels insular. To establish the practical significance of the proposed approach, it is crucial to compare it against simpler, more intuitive baseline methods. The absence of such baselines makes it difficult to judge whether the complexity of the GoSta algorithm is justified. For instance, what would be the performance of the following naive strategies?
    *   **Data-Passing Gossip:** Each node `i` simply sends its observation `Xi` on a random walk. Each node `k` maintains a list of unique observations it has seen so far. After some number of iterations `t`, node `k` computes its estimate of the U-statistic based on the data it has collected. This decouples data collection from estimation.
    *   **Randomized Pairwise Calculation:** At each step, a random edge `(i, j)` is chosen. Nodes `i` and `j` exchange their observations `Xi` and `Xj`, and both update a running average with the new term `H(Xi, Xj)`. This is a much simpler randomized approach.
    Comparing against such baselines would provide a much stronger argument for the paper's contribution.

2.  **Originality of Algorithmic Components:** While the application and analysis are novel, the core algorithmic building blocks—pairwise averaging for consensus and data swapping via random walks—are standard primitives in the gossip literature. The contribution is primarily in the effective combination of these primitives for the U-statistic problem and the rigorous analysis thereof. The paper would be strengthened if it more clearly articulated the key algorithmic insight that makes this combination successful, beyond simply stating that it works.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in distributed machine learning.
*   Provides a novel and technically sound solution with rigorous convergence analysis.
*   The theoretical results are strong, with clear rates and insightful dependencies on network and data properties.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental evaluation is weak due to the lack of simple, intuitive baselines, making it hard to assess the practical value of the proposed algorithm.
*   The algorithmic novelty is somewhat limited to a new combination of existing techniques.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, this is a good paper with a strong theoretical contribution to an important problem. The main drawback is the unconvincing experimental section. I believe the strengths, particularly the quality of the theoretical analysis, outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to strengthen their experimental evaluation in the final version by including comparisons to the simple baselines suggested above. This would significantly improve the paper and make a much more compelling case for the practical utility of their proposed algorithms.
