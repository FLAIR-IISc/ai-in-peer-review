This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the decentralized estimation of U-statistics, a class of functionals requiring averaging over pairs of observations. While distributed estimation of the sample mean is well-studied, computing U-statistics – crucial for metrics like Area Under the Curve, empirical variance, and Gini mean difference – has received less attention. The authors establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively, dependent on both data and network characteristics, and demonstrate empirical superiority over a previous approach [17].

The paper builds on the established field of gossip algorithms [19, 18, 5], which offer simple yet effective decentralized computation. However, standard gossip algorithms are not directly applicable to U-statistics due to their reliance on averaging over pairs rather than individual observations. This work directly addresses this limitation. Related work in distributed computation, such as [10, 2, 15, 11, 9], primarily focuses on separable functions, highlighting the novelty of tackling the U-statistic problem within the gossip framework. The authors appropriately cite [12] as a foundational reference for U-statistics. However, the connections to more recent and broadly applicable gossip algorithms could be strengthened.  Specifically, the work of Dimakis et al. [Dimakis et al., 2006, *Proceedings of the IEEE*] provides a comprehensive overview of gossip algorithms, and its inclusion would contextualize the contribution more effectively. 

A key strength of the proposed approach is the clever introduction of “auxiliary observations” that are swapped alongside the U-statistic estimates.  This allows the original data to propagate through the network while enabling local averaging, effectively implementing coupled random walks. This mechanism, and its advantages over simpler alternatives, is not sufficiently highlighted in the main text and warrants greater emphasis.  The description of how the random edge is drawn – stating nodes “wake up” and contact a neighbor – is somewhat ambiguous.  Clarifying the precise induced edge distribution is necessary for full understanding.  

A crucial aspect missing is a comparison to a more naive approach, such as a flooding algorithm where each node stores and propagates all received observations (perhaps tagged with source node IDs). This comparison would help establish the tradeoffs in terms of memory usage and convergence rate. While the authors claim lower memory cost than [17], demonstrating this advantage relative to a broader class of potential solutions would be more convincing. Furthermore, the rate of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) should be benchmarked against what one might expect from a direct application of established gossip algorithms, even if it requires more memory.  

The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution.  However, the assumptions regarding the initial data distribution are not explicitly stated. Specifying whether the analysis holds for worst-case data or under probabilistic assumptions, and providing explicit error guarantees as a function of the initial data, would strengthen the results.  The “phantom nodes” used in the proof are a creative technique to reformulate the problem, but the connection between the phantom network and the original graph could be made clearer.  

The experimental evaluation, using AUC and within-cluster point scatter on real datasets, provides compelling evidence of the algorithm's performance. The comparison to [17] is valuable. However, including comparisons to simpler baselines, as suggested above, would further solidify the empirical claims.  

Originality is high, as the paper tackles a previously underexplored problem within the well-established field of gossip algorithms. The proposed solution is novel and effectively addresses the challenges posed by U-statistics.  The significance of the work lies in its potential to enable decentralized computation of important statistical measures in large-scale distributed systems. The convergence guarantees and empirical results demonstrate a tangible advancement in the state of the art. The work provides unique results with demonstrable improvement over existing methods. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to decentralized U-statistic estimation.
*   Solid theoretical convergence analysis with bounds dependent on network properties.
*   Empirically demonstrated performance improvement over existing methods.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with broad applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to alternative approaches (e.g., flooding).
*   Ambiguous description of the random edge selection process.
*   Insufficient discussion of the initial data distribution's impact on convergence.
*   Missing connections to broader gossip algorithm literature.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of decentralized computation. While there are areas for improvement, particularly in contextualizing the work within the broader literature and providing more comprehensive comparisons, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical results justify acceptance. I initially assigned a score of 7, but after considering the potential impact and addressing the concerns, I would raise it to 8 and recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the review comments. Specifically, the authors should expand the related work section, clarify the edge selection process, add comparisons to baseline algorithms, and explicitly state the assumptions on the initial data distribution.
