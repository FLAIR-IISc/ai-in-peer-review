This paper tackles the problem of computing degree-two U-statistics in a distributed network using gossip-based communication. This is an important generalization of the well-studied distributed averaging problem, as U-statistics like sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster scatter depend on pairs of data points, which are initially located at different nodes. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, named GoSta, where nodes iteratively combine two steps: 1) propagating data across the network by swapping auxiliary observations with a neighbor, and 2) averaging their local U-statistic estimate with that neighbor. This approach is positioned as an improvement over the only known prior work on this problem ([17] Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010), offering lower resource requirements and an asynchronous variant. The primary contribution is a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively. The analysis, which relies on a clever reformulation using "phantom nodes", connects the convergence speed to properties of the network graph, such as its spectral gap.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The problem statement is clear, and the proposed algorithms are presented intuitively and formally. The theoretical contribution is the paper's main strength; the convergence proofs are detailed, and the resulting bounds explicitly show the dependency on both the data distribution and the network topology, which is insightful. The use of a "phantom network" as an analytical tool to decouple data propagation and estimate averaging is elegant.

However, the experimental quality is a major weakness that significantly undermines the paper's claims. The evaluation compares the proposed GoSta algorithm solely against the U2-gossip algorithm from [17]. While this demonstrates superiority over a single, specific predecessor, it fails to provide a broader context for the algorithm's performance. A strong experimental section should convince the reader of a method's practical utility, which requires comparison against reasonable and simple baselines. Without these, it is difficult to ascertain whether the proposed algorithm's complexity is justified.

**Originality and Significance**

The problem of distributed U-statistic estimation is significant and relatively under-explored compared to simple averaging. This paper provides one of the first rigorous analyses of a practical gossip algorithm for this task, which is a valuable contribution. The originality of the theoretical analysis, particularly the proof technique, is high.

The originality of the algorithm itself is more modest. The core mechanism—swapping data packets to perform a random walk combined with local averaging—is an intuitive and natural design for this problem. While the specific combination and its analysis are novel, the conceptual building blocks are familiar in the gossip literature.

The significance of the work is currently limited by the weak experimental validation. The paper addresses a difficult and important problem, but it does not provide sufficient evidence that the proposed solution is demonstrably better than simpler, practical alternatives one might design. If GoSta significantly outperforms intuitive baselines, its significance would be much higher. As it stands, the contribution is primarily theoretical.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting, a necessary step beyond simple distributed averaging.
2.  **Strong Theory:** It provides a comprehensive and novel theoretical analysis, deriving explicit convergence rates for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The proof technique is clever.
3.  **Intuitive Algorithm:** The proposed GoSta algorithm is simple, elegant, and practical, offering low memory and communication overhead compared to prior work.

**Arguments for Rejection/Major Revisions:**
1.  **Critically Weak Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is insufficient. By only comparing to a single, fairly obscure method ([17]), the paper fails to establish the practical value of its contribution. The central question of "how does this compare to a simple, common-sense approach?" is left unanswered.
2.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** While the analysis is novel, the algorithm itself is a rather intuitive combination of existing gossip primitives (swapping and averaging).

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper would be strengthened considerably by a more thorough experimental evaluation. I strongly urge the authors to include comparisons to several baseline methods. For instance:
*   **Random Sampling Baseline:** Each node could periodically broadcast its observation `Xi` to a randomly chosen neighbor. After `t` iterations, each node would have a small, random collection of other nodes' data and could compute a U-statistic based on this local sample. This is a very simple randomized approach that would serve as an excellent point of comparison.
*   **Naive Propagation Baseline:** A simpler gossip approach could involve nodes trying to collect all data points. In each round, a node could exchange its current set of known observations with a neighbor. This is communication-heavy but provides a performance baseline for a method that eventually computes the exact U-statistic locally.

Including such baselines would contextualize the performance of GoSta and demonstrate whether its careful design provides a tangible benefit over simpler strategies. This would significantly bolster the claims of practical significance and make a much stronger case for the paper's contribution to the field.
