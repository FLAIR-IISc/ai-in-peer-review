This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for computing pairwise U-statistics across networked nodes. The authors demonstrate the efficiency and robustness of their approach through theoretical analysis and numerical experiments. The key idea behind the algorithm is to have nodes swap a secondary state (initial observation) during averaging, allowing observations to perform coupled random walks on the network graph.

The contribution of this paper is valuable, as it addresses the problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics, which is essential in many distributed systems. However, the authors could strengthen the connection to existing gossip literature and related ideas. For instance, they could discuss the relationship between their approach and other gossip algorithms, such as those proposed by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE).

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and its analysis. However, there are some minor issues with notation and language. For example, the authors use the term "phantom nodes" to describe the additional nodes that account for data propagation in the network, but they do not provide a clear definition of this term. Additionally, the authors could clarify the induced edge-distribution from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" model, as the phrase "drawing a random edge" is ambiguous.

The authors compare their algorithm to a flooding/memory-heavy alternative, which is a good way to demonstrate the trade-offs between different approaches. However, they could also compare their algorithm to other gossip algorithms, such as U2-gossip, to show the advantages of their approach.

The related work section is a bit limited, and the authors could expand it to include other relevant papers on gossip algorithms and decentralized estimation. For instance, they could cite the work of Kempe et al. (In Symposium on Foundations of Computer Science) and Mosk-Aoyama and Shah (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory).

The theorems and analysis could be improved by stating assumptions on the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and making error guarantees explicit as a function of initial data. Additionally, the authors could investigate lifting constructions, such as LADA or lifted Markov chains, which may accelerate mixing.

The experimental results are promising, but the authors could provide more baseline comparisons to demonstrate the empirical benefits of their approach. For instance, they could compare their algorithm to a centralized approach or to other decentralized algorithms.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of decentralized estimation, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their algorithm and its analysis. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the NIPS conference.
