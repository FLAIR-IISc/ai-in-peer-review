## Review of “Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics”

This paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in a network setting, a less explored area compared to distributed mean estimation. The authors propose new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, providing convergence rate bounds and empirical evidence of improved performance compared to a prior approach [17]. The problem is well-motivated, as U-statistics are fundamental to describing global properties of statistical populations and appear in diverse applications like AUC calculation and variance estimation.

The core idea of the proposed algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) is to combine data propagation across the network with averaging of local estimates. The authors cleverly reformulate the problem using “phantom nodes” to facilitate the convergence analysis, resulting in rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases respectively. These bounds are explicit and depend on both data and network characteristics, notably the spectral gap of the network graph. The theoretical analysis appears comprehensive and demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying principles of gossip algorithms and spectral graph theory.

A significant strength of this work lies in the theoretical rigor. The paper provides a detailed mathematical framework, including convergence proofs, and connects the performance to key network properties. This analytical depth allows for a clear understanding of the algorithm's limitations and potential for improvement. The introduction of phantom nodes is a particularly clever technique for simplifying the analysis. The extensions to the asynchronous setting are also valuable, addressing a more realistic scenario where global synchronization is unavailable.

However, the paper suffers from a notable weakness: the experimental evaluation is insufficient. While the authors claim empirical evidence of superiority over [17], the comparison is limited to two specific applications and lacks comparison to any intuitive or randomized baselines. For instance, a simple distributed average calculation without the specialized U-statistic approach would provide a crucial point of reference to understand the gains achieved by the proposed algorithms. Including such baselines would significantly strengthen the argument for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.  Further, the presentation of experimental results is relatively limited; more comprehensive visualizations, such as learning curves across different network sizes and topologies, would be beneficial.

Regarding originality, while extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is a natural progression, the paper doesn't fully articulate how it differs significantly from existing work beyond the specific algorithmic approach and analysis. A more thorough discussion of related work in distributed estimation and U-statistic computation, beyond the citation of [17], would contextualize the contribution more effectively. Checking for related work in the recent NIPS proceedings is crucial here.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written, although the notation can be dense, particularly in the theoretical sections. Providing more intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts and their connection to the algorithm would improve accessibility. The use of the "phantom node" concept, while insightful, requires careful explanation for readers unfamiliar with the technique. 

The significance of this work is moderate. The problem addressed is relevant to a variety of distributed systems, and the theoretical results provide valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms. The practical impact, however, is currently limited by the lack of comprehensive experimental validation.  If the authors can convincingly demonstrate the performance gains over simpler baselines and across a wider range of scenarios, the significance would be greatly enhanced.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundations and rigorous convergence analysis.
*   Addresses an important and less-explored problem in distributed estimation.
*   Novel algorithmic approach with explicit convergence rate bounds.
*   Clear connection between algorithm performance and network properties.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient experimental evaluation, lacking comparison to basic baselines.
*   Limited discussion of originality and related work.
*   Dense notation and potential lack of intuitive explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. The theoretical contributions are valuable and demonstrate a strong understanding of the field. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation significantly weakens the paper. Addressing the weaknesses in the experimental evaluation and strengthening the discussion of related work would substantially improve the paper’s impact and justify acceptance. I recommend the authors be given the opportunity to revise and resubmit, contingent on addressing the aforementioned concerns.
