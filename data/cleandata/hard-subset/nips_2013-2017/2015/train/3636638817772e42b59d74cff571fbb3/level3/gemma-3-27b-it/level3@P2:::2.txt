## Review of “Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics”

This paper presents new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of U-statistics, a class of data functionals important in various machine learning applications. The core idea is to combine propagation of local observations with averaging of local estimates, achieving convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively. The contribution is timely, as distributed computation is increasingly relevant, and extending gossip algorithms beyond simple averages is a significant step. While the paper is technically sound and presents a valuable extension to existing literature, some improvements in clarity, related work discussion, and experimental validation are needed.

The paper effectively introduces the problem of distributed U-statistic estimation and motivates its importance with examples like AUC and within-cluster point scatter. The authors correctly position this work within the context of gossip algorithms, noting the limitations of existing approaches for non-separable functions. The proposed algorithms are clearly described, and the convergence analysis appears rigorous (though I was unable to fully verify the proofs in the supplemental material due to time constraints). The core algorithmic insight – maintaining and swapping auxiliary observations to facilitate the coupled random walk of data – is clever and should be more prominently highlighted in the main text as a key contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is a valuable contribution, addressing a gap in the literature.
* **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, establishing theoretical guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The algorithms are well-described, and the problem is clearly motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Related Work:** The discussion of related work feels incomplete. While key papers are cited (e.g., Boyd et al., Dimakis et al.), the paper lacks a broader discussion of gossip algorithms, particularly variations beyond simple averaging. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive overview of the landscape of distributed computation techniques. Specifically, citing Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) would be a significant addition.
* **Clarity of Edge Selection:** The description of the edge selection process ("Draw (i, j) uniformly at random from E") is ambiguous. It’s crucial to explicitly state how the edge is chosen when a node awakens and contacts a neighbor. Is it uniformly random among its neighbors? This needs clarification.
* **Trade-off Analysis:** The paper doesn’t adequately position the proposed algorithms in terms of tradeoffs. A comparison to a naive approach – where each node stores and forwards *all* received observations – would help to understand the benefits of the proposed algorithm in terms of memory and communication cost. Analyzing the convergence rate relative to this baseline would provide context for whether the achieved O(1/t) and O(log t/t) rates are favorable.
* **Potential Connections:** The paper doesn’t explore connections to related techniques like lifting schemes (e.g., LADA, lifted Markov chains). These methods often accelerate mixing in networked systems and might be relevant for improving convergence rates.
* **Assumptions and Error Guarantees:** The theorems should explicitly state the assumptions on the initial data distribution (e.g., worst-case, probabilistic). Moreover, the error guarantees should be more explicit as functions of the initial data, providing tighter bounds on the convergence behavior.
* **Minor Issues:** The statement “a bipartite directed-graph” in Section 2.1 seems to contain an error; bipartite graphs are undirected.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in distributed computation.
* The proposed algorithms are novel and offer a practical solution for estimating U-statistics in a decentralized setting.
* The theoretical analysis provides rigorous convergence guarantees.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The related work section is incomplete, potentially overlooking relevant literature.
* The clarity of the edge selection process needs improvement.
* The lack of a comparative analysis against a baseline algorithm limits the understanding of the algorithm’s benefits.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed computation. While some weaknesses exist, the strengths outweigh the concerns. I initially scored this paper a 7, reflecting the need for substantial revisions. However, after careful consideration of the contributions and the potential impact of this work, I raise my score to 8. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly expanding the related work discussion, clarifying the edge selection process, providing a comparison to a relevant baseline, and exploring potential connections to related techniques like lifting schemes. If these comments are adequately addressed, this paper will be a significant addition to the NIPS proceedings.
