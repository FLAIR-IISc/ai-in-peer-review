- Introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in distributed networks.  
- Problem is well-motivated: U-statistics are important for global data properties and arise in many ML applications (AUC, variance, etc.).  
- Correctly identifies limitations of existing gossip algorithms (designed for averaging) and proposes propagation of local observations plus averaging local estimates.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with sound theoretical analysis and promising empirical results.  
- Builds on prior gossip and U-statistics literature and clearly positions its contributions relative to related work.  
- Addresses limitations of Pelckmans and Suykens [17], reducing memory/communication costs and enabling asynchronicity.  
- Key theoretical contribution: convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms that incorporate data and network dependencies.  
- Empirical strength: experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter show superior performance to U2-gossip across multiple network topologies (complete, grid, Watts–Strogatz).  
- Technical novelty: use of “phantom nodes” to model data propagation in the theoretical analysis.  
- Major practical weakness: lacks per-iteration run-time comparisons with U2-gossip, leaving practical runtime benefits unclear.  
- Experiments report better final accuracy but do not show time-to-accuracy, which is critical for iterative gossip methods.  
- Additional baseline comparisons (e.g., centralized computation or parameter servers) would provide useful context.  
- Scalability to much larger networks and datasets is not explicitly addressed.  
- Analysis assumes connected, non-bipartite networks; behavior on more general graph structures is not discussed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, novel, well-motivated, shows clear improvements over U2-gossip, and is clearly presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing per-iteration runtime data, limited baseline comparisons, and unclear scalability.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper recommended for acceptance contingent on adding per-iteration run-time comparisons to strengthen practical relevance.
