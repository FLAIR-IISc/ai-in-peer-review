This paper addresses the important and relatively under-explored problem of computing U-statistics of degree two in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip protocols are designed for separable functions, like simple averages, and do not directly apply to U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations across the network. The authors propose two new randomized gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively. The core idea is to combine a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary observations in a random-walk-like fashion, with an estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic. This work builds upon and aims to improve the only known prior method, U2-gossip (Pelckmans et al., 2011), which relies solely on data propagation without estimate averaging.

The main contributions of the paper are:
1.  The proposal of novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for U-statistic estimation.
2.  A theoretical analysis that establishes convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous one. The convergence bounds insightfully depend on both data-dependent terms and network-dependent terms (the spectral gap), highlighting the influence of network topology.
3.  An empirical evaluation on two real-world tasks (AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation) across various network structures, demonstrating superior performance over the U2-gossip baseline in terms of convergence per iteration.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Motivation:** The paper tackles a well-motivated problem. U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and the ability to compute them efficiently in a decentralized manner is highly relevant for applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale data analysis. The paper does an excellent job of positioning the problem and highlighting its importance with concrete examples like AUC and within-cluster scatter.

*   **Quality and Originality:** The technical contribution is strong. The proposed algorithms are a novel and intuitive extension of gossip protocols. The theoretical analysis, which uses a "phantom network" construction to decouple data propagation and averaging, is clever and appears sound. The resulting convergence bounds improve upon the analysis for the prior state-of-the-art and correctly capture the dependencies on network connectivity. The development of a fully asynchronous version is a significant practical contribution, as it removes the often unrealistic requirement of a global clock.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the algorithms are presented with clarity, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The authors effectively contrast their approach with previous work on standard gossip averaging and the U2-gossip algorithm, making their contributions easy to understand.

*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims. By testing on three distinct network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), the authors demonstrate that their method's superiority holds under varied communication scenarios. The results in Figure 2 clearly show that GoSta-sync converges faster and with lower variance than U2-gossip in terms of iteration count.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

The primary concern with the current manuscript is that the experimental evaluation, while compelling, may not tell the whole story regarding practical performance.

1.  **Per-Iteration Complexity:** The experiments compare algorithms based on the number of communication rounds (iterations). However, the practical efficiency is determined by wall-clock time, which depends on both the number of iterations and the computational/communication cost per iteration. In GoSta-sync (Algorithm 1), each iteration involves an update at every node (line 4) followed by a single communication event (lines 7-8). In contrast, the U2-gossip baseline involves an update at every node and then two communication events. It is not immediately clear how the overall per-iteration complexity compares. A more detailed comparison of the computational steps and communication load per iteration for both GoSta and U2-gossip would be very valuable.

2.  **Wall-Clock Time Evaluation:** The central claim of superiority would be significantly strengthened if the experimental results were presented in terms of wall-clock time in addition to iteration count. If the per-iteration time of GoSta is substantially higher than that of U2-gossip, its advantage in iteration count might be diminished or nullified in practice. I would strongly encourage the authors to add plots of "average relative error vs. time" to Figure 2. This would provide a more complete and practically relevant comparison, directly addressing whether the proposed method offers a true speedup.

3.  **Asynchronous Algorithm Performance:** In Figure 3b, the asynchronous GoSta-async algorithm performs comparably to its synchronous counterpart, despite having a theoretically slower convergence rate (O(log t/t) vs. O(1/t)). While this is a positive result, a brief discussion on this observation would be insightful. Is the O(log t/t) bound potentially loose, or are the constants in the O(1/t) bound such that the difference is negligible in this practical regime?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed computation. It is well-written, theoretically sound, and empirically supported. The proposed algorithms advance the state-of-the-art for an important problem. My main reservation is the evaluation being solely based on iteration counts. Addressing the question of per-iteration complexity and wall-clock time would make this an even stronger and more impactful contribution.
