Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics (e.g., AUC, variance, Gini mean difference, within-cluster scatter) over a network using gossip-style communication. The authors propose two algorithms: a synchronous method (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous method (GoSta-async). Both combine two mechanisms at each iteration: (i) propagation of auxiliary data via random edge swaps, enabling each node to approximate its local partial average hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi,Xj), and (ii) averaging of node estimates to reach consensus on Ûn(H) = (1/n)∑i hi. A key technical ingredient is a “phantom nodes” reformulation that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging and supports the convergence analysis.

The paper provides convergence bounds: O(1/t) in the synchronous case with constants depending on data dispersion and the spectral gap of the communication graph, and O((log t)/t) in the asynchronous case. Compared to the prior U2-gossip method of Pelckmans & Suykens (IFAC 2009), the proposed algorithms use fewer auxiliary variables and fewer communications per iteration, and achieve more favorable bounds (removing a √n factor and leveraging averaging). Empirically, on two applications—AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter—across three graph families (complete, 2D grid, Watts–Strogatz), the proposed methods outperform U2-gossip, with results reported as average relative error (for AUC, average relative AUC error) over nodes.

Strengths:
- Well motivated problem with clear relevance to sensor/peer-to-peer systems where pairwise functionals matter.
- Well written and well organized; the “phantom nodes” device makes the analysis accessible.
- Solid theory: unbiasedness plus explicit-rate bounds with interpretable constants (spectral gap; data-dependent dispersion).
- Both synchronous and fully asynchronous variants are proposed and analyzed.
- Memory and communication advantages vs. U2-gossip are clearly articulated.
- Empirical results on two datasets and varied graph topologies show consistent gains in average relative error and reduced cross-node variance.

Weaknesses and requests:
- Missing per-iteration run-time comparisons. Practical utility hinges on the relative cost per iteration/activation. If GoSta’s iterations are substantially slower than U2-gossip’s, the wall-clock advantage may narrow or invert. Please report:
  - Wall-clock time per iteration/activation for both methods, across d and n.
  - Breakdowns: number of H evaluations per global iteration, bytes communicated per iteration (and per node), number of nodes activated, and size of messages (O(d) vectors).
  - Convergence vs. wall-clock curves and also vs. total number of H evaluations and total bytes sent (cost-normalized comparisons).
- Experimental scope is modest: two tasks/datasets, three graph types. Additional tests (e.g., larger d, non-Euclidean H such as kernelized statistics, and more realistic sparse/scale-free graphs) would strengthen claims.
- The asynchronous bound O((log t)/t) seems loose relative to practice; high-probability bounds and finite-time constants would help interpretability.
- Assumption that each node knows |E| to set pk may be strong; please clarify how |E| is obtained in a decentralized setting, or provide a variant that estimates pk locally.
- Robustness aspects (link failures, delays, time-varying graphs) are not explored.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound and careful, and claims are supported by proofs and experiments. The comparative bound against U2-gossip is informative. The main gap is the absence of timing/complexity measurements that connect theory to practical runtime.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, with precise algorithms and a clean analysis. Minor suggestions:
- Add a concise table contrasting per-iteration compute/memory/communication between GoSta-sync, GoSta-async, and U2-gossip.
- State explicitly the per-iteration global cost: GoSta-sync computes H(Xk,Yk) at all nodes each tick (O(n·cost(H))) and communicates along one edge; U2-gossip similarly computes at all nodes but communicates along two edges.
- Clarify how labels are propagated for AUC and whether they travel with Y in asynchronous swaps.

Originality:
The formulation and analysis for U-statistics with gossip are novel beyond U2-gossip. The phantom-node analysis and asynchronous construction are original contributions.

Significance:
U-statistics are widely used; enabling their decentralized estimation with provable rates and good empirical performance is valuable. The methods could become building blocks for decentralized optimization of U-statistics (e.g., AUC maximization, rank-based learning).

Specific suggestions for the authors:
- Provide per-iteration and per-activation runtime results, scaling with d and n, for both methods.
- Report cost-normalized convergence (error vs. total H evaluations and vs. total bytes communicated).
- Include experiments with more complex H (e.g., Kendall tau, RBF-kernel MMD) to test the impact of heavier H evaluations.
- Explore non-uniform edge sampling or accelerated gossip (e.g., Chebyshev acceleration) to leverage spectral information.
- Discuss how to handle unknown |E| and degree heterogeneity in practice.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, well-motivated problem with practical relevance.
- Strong theoretical guarantees with interpretable constants.
- Both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms presented.
- Empirical superiority over U2-gossip across diverse graphs.
- Reduced memory and communication footprint.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- No per-iteration runtime/communication cost comparisons; practical gains are not quantified in wall-clock terms.
- Limited experimental breadth (two datasets, two statistics).
- Asynchronous bound is loose; lack of high-probability or robustness results.

Overall recommendation:
Leaning accept. The paper is technically solid, well written, and advances decentralized estimation of U-statistics with both theory and practice. Including per-iteration runtime and cost-normalized convergence analyses, as requested above, would significantly strengthen the practical case and address the key concern about iteration speed relative to U2-gossip.
