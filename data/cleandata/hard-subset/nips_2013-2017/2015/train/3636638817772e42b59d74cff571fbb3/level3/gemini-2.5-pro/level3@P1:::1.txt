### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. While standard gossip algorithms are well-suited for computing simple averages of functions separable across observations (e.g., the sample mean), they are not directly applicable to U-statistics, which involve averages over pairs of observations. U-statistics represent a broad and important class of estimators, including sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster point scatter.

The authors propose a novel family of algorithms, named "GoSta" (Gossip for Statistics), in both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The core idea is to combine two iterative processes at each step: 1) a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary data observations with neighbors to disseminate information across the network, and 2) an estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic with their neighbors. This dual mechanism allows each node to gradually build an accurate global estimate.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms. Using a clever reformulation based on "phantom networks," the authors derive convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. These rates explicitly depend on the network topology (via the spectral gap) and data-dependent terms, providing clear insight into the algorithm's behavior. The proposed methods are positioned as a significant improvement over the primary prior work in this area, U2-gossip [17], by offering faster theoretical convergence, lower memory and communication costs, and support for asynchronous operation. The theoretical claims are substantiated through experiments on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation across various network topologies.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Important and Under-Explored Problem:** The decentralized estimation of U-statistics is a practical and significant problem that has received surprisingly little attention compared to distributed averaging. This paper makes a strong contribution to an important niche.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Algorithm:** The GoSta algorithm's design, which simultaneously propagates data and averages estimates, is intuitive and effective. It elegantly solves the core challenge of U-statistics, where each node needs information about pairs of data points from across the network.
3.  **Rigorous Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a thorough convergence analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The rates are state-of-the-art for this problem, and the analysis technique using phantom networks is original. The derived bounds clearly show the dependence on network connectivity and data properties, which is highly valuable.
4.  **Clear Superiority Over Prior Art:** The paper makes a compelling case for GoSta's superiority over the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17]. GoSta is better on multiple practical axes: it requires less memory (one auxiliary observation vs. two), less communication per iteration (one swap vs. two), and crucially, it offers a fully asynchronous version, which is more realistic for many decentralized systems. The refined analysis of U2-gossip provided by the authors further solidifies this claim.
5.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, using real datasets for relevant U-statistics (AUC, within-cluster scatter) and testing on diverse network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz). The results consistently demonstrate that GoSta outperforms U2-gossip, empirically confirming the theoretical advantages.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Practicality of the Asynchronous Algorithm's Assumptions:** The asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) requires each node `k` to know `pk = 2dk/|E|`, which in turn requires knowledge of the total number of edges `|E|` in the network. While the local degree `dk` is readily available, `|E|` is a global property. Computing it might require a preliminary consensus/gossip phase, adding complexity. It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss the practical implications of this assumption.
2.  **Gap Between Synchronous and Asynchronous Rates:** The theoretical rate for the asynchronous version (O(log t/t)) is slightly worse than for the synchronous one (O(1/t)). While the experiments show comparable performance, it would be interesting if the authors could comment on whether this gap is fundamental to the asynchronous nature of the problem or an artifact of the analysis that could potentially be tightened.

Overall, the weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's significant contributions. They are primarily points for clarification or future exploration.

**Detailed Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithms are well-defined, and the theoretical claims are supported by detailed proofs (referenced to supplementary material). The experimental methodology is sound and the results support the paper's conclusions. The work is complete and presents a self-contained, solid contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated in the introduction, related work is adequately summarized, and the proposed GoSta algorithms are presented with clarity. The "phantom network" concept, while abstract, is used purely as an analytical tool and is explained well enough not to confuse the reader about the actual algorithm's implementation. The figures and tables are clear and effectively illustrate the experimental results.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon the extensive literature on gossip algorithms, it tackles a novel problem variant (U-statistics) that existing methods cannot handle efficiently. The proposed algorithm, combining data swapping with estimate averaging, is a novel design. The theoretical analysis framework is also original. The work is a significant step beyond the only directly related prior work [17].

*   **Significance:** The results are significant and likely to be impactful. U-statistics are fundamental tools in statistics and machine learning. As distributed systems and large-scale data processing become more prevalent, the need for efficient, decentralized algorithms for these fundamental quantities will grow. This paper provides a practical, robust, and theoretically-grounded solution that is superior to the previous state-of-the-art. Researchers and practitioners working on distributed machine learning, sensor networks, and large-scale data analysis are likely to use these ideas or build upon them, for instance, in developing decentralized optimization algorithms for objectives based on U-statistics.
