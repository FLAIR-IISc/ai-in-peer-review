## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networked systems, a less explored area compared to average computation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, providing theoretical convergence rate bounds and empirical validation through experiments. The core idea of leveraging “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem and facilitate analysis is clever and contributes to a comprehensive theoretical treatment. 

The work builds upon the existing literature on gossip algorithms (e.g., [19, 2, 5]) and acknowledges the limitations of prior work on U-statistic estimation [17]. The proposed algorithms aim to improve upon [17] by achieving faster convergence with lower communication and memory costs. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for synchronous and asynchronous cases respectively, is a significant strength. The inclusion of data and network-dependent terms in the bounds demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the problem's complexities. 

However, a major weakness lies in the complete lack of comparison to baseline methods beyond [17]. While the authors claim superiority, this claim isn’t substantiated by comparing against simpler, intuitive baselines. For instance, a naive approach of each node independently estimating the U-statistic and then applying a standard gossip averaging algorithm, or even a randomized baseline where nodes randomly exchange estimates without the specific U-statistic update rule, would provide valuable context. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvements over [17], would be significantly strengthened by such comparisons. 

Furthermore, while the theoretical analysis is thorough, the practical implications of the data-dependent terms could be better explored. A discussion on how these terms influence performance in different scenarios would be beneficial. The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though some of the notation (particularly in Section 2) could be streamlined for improved readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an interesting and relevant problem in distributed computation.
*   Proposes a novel approach with a comprehensive theoretical analysis.
*   Demonstrates improvements over existing work on U-statistic estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks comparison to basic baseline methods, hindering a thorough evaluation of the proposed algorithms.
*   Limited discussion on the practical implications of the data-dependent convergence terms.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of distributed estimation. However, the lack of baseline comparisons significantly weakens the empirical validation. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after incorporating these comparisons and strengthening the experimental evaluation. Addressing this key weakness would substantially improve the paper’s impact and justify its inclusion in NIPS.
