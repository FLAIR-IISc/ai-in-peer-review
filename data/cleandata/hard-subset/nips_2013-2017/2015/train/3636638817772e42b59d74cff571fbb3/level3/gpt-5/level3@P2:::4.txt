Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in networks, a setting where standard gossip algorithms for separable functions do not apply. The authors propose two algorithms: a synchronous method (GoSta-sync) and an asynchronous variant (GoSta-async). Both maintain at each node a running estimate Zk and a single auxiliary observation Yk that performs a random walk via pairwise swaps. Each iteration consists of (i) updating local running averages using H(Xk, Yk), and (ii) averaging Z-values across a randomly selected edge while swapping Y-values. The analysis shows convergence in expectation to the global U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous), and constants depending on data dispersion terms and the spectral gap of the communication graph. A “phantom nodes” construction enables a clean decomposition of averaging versus data propagation in the proof. The paper contrasts its approach with U2-gossip (Pelckmans and Suykens, 2010), arguing better rates and lower memory/communication; experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter on several graph classes show improved empirical performance.

This work builds on a rich literature on gossip/consensus and averaging in networks, including randomized pairwise averaging and its spectral-gap dependence (e.g., Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Dimakis et al.), geographic gossip (Dimakis et al.), and asynchronous randomized gossip. It targets the less explored case of pairwise-dependent functionals, where the most directly related prior is U2-gossip. The U-statistics background is classical (Lee), and the application examples (AUC, scatter) are well-motivated in ML.

Quality
- Theory: The convergence analyses for both variants are carefully developed and technically sound to the best of my assessment. The spectral-gap dependence is plausible and aligns with consensus theory. The phantom-graph technique is elegant and clarifies the coupling between data propagation and averaging. However:
  - Guarantees are in expectation; there are no high-probability or almost-sure convergence guarantees, nor variance bounds to quantify concentration across runs/nodes.
  - The asynchronous bound hides a graph-dependent constant and scales with ||H|| but does not expose the same data-dependent structure as in the synchronous case; it would help to make c′(G) explicit and discuss tightness.
  - The async algorithm requires nodes to know pk = 2dk/|E| (i.e., |E|) to maintain unbiased counters. This assumption may be strong in decentralized settings; the paper should discuss how to relax/estimate |E|, or analyze bias/variance if pk is estimated online.
- Experiments: The empirical section illustrates error vs. iterations on AUC and scatter across three topologies and shows consistent improvements over U2-gossip. That said, the evaluation is narrow:
  - There are no comparisons to intuitive baselines beyond U2-gossip (see suggestions below).
  - Communication, memory, and computation costs per node are not measured empirically (e.g., bytes sent, number of H evaluations, wall-clock under a realistic MAC), even though these are central to the claimed advantages.
  - Asynchronous results are minimal; the setting assumes uniform random edge activations and does not explore heterogeneity or time-varying graphs.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Algorithms are clearly specified. The phantom-network construction is a helpful proof device, though the exposition is dense; adding more intuition (e.g., a short verbal summary of why averaging plus random walks yields unbiasedness and faster mixing) would aid readers. Define pk and the need for |E| earlier in the async section and discuss practicality. A brief complexity table (state size, messages, H-evals per iteration) would improve readability.

Originality
The problem (decentralized U-statistic estimation) is interesting and underexplored, and the combination of averaging gossip with coupled random-walk propagation is intuitive and, to my knowledge, not previously analyzed with these rates. The phantom-node proof technique is a nice contribution. That said, algorithmically the approach is a relatively natural extension of known gossip ideas, and novelty over U2-gossip is incremental rather than conceptual breakthrough.

Significance
U-statistics cover many important metrics (AUC, variance, rank correlations), so a practical distributed estimator is potentially impactful. The spectral-gap dependence informs network design. With a stronger empirical evaluation—including communication efficiency and broader baselines—the work could be of high practical interest. As it stands, significance is moderate.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored distributed estimation problem.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with low state (one auxiliary observation).
- Solid theoretical guarantees with clear dependence on spectral gap and data dispersion.
- Phantom-graph analysis is elegant and could be reused elsewhere.

Cons
- Limited experimental baselines and lack of communication/computation measurements.
- Only expectation bounds; no concentration/variance analysis.
- Async variant requires knowledge of |E|; robustness to misspecification not studied.
- Novelty is somewhat incremental relative to prior gossip and U2-gossip.

Questions and suggestions
- Baselines to include:
  - Naive Monte Carlo: each node forms a local mini-batch estimate of hi by sampling remote observations via random walks, then runs standard average gossip; compare error vs. H-evaluations and messages.
  - Token-based all-pairs sampling without averaging: random pair selection computes H once and broadcasts partial sums via spanning tree or push-sum; serves as a communication-heavy upper bound.
  - Flooding/aggregation on a spanning tree (exact but expensive) to provide a “gold” communication baseline.
  - Random pairing with averaging but without running-average weighting to show the effect of the 1/t schedule.
- Report for all methods: number of messages, total bytes, H-evaluations, memory per node, time-to-ε, and sensitivity to d (costly H).
- Provide high-probability bounds or at least variance bounds across nodes; experimentally, plot variance decay and concentration across runs.
- Discuss extensions/robustness: directed/time-varying graphs, message loss, heterogeneous activation rates, privacy constraints, and the case of multiple observations per node with load imbalance.
- Clarify whether λ2 and constants in the async bound can be made explicit; if not, discuss empirically how the rate matches theory.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper tackles an interesting problem and presents a clean, technically solid approach with compelling intuition. However, the novelty over existing gossip ideas is modest, and the experimental evaluation is too narrow to substantiate practical advantages (no baselines beyond U2-gossip, no communication/computation metrics). Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the async assumptions would significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence
High on the assessment of the literature and empirical shortcomings; moderate on the fine details of the proofs.
