The paper "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics" proposes a novel gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing pairwise U-statistics across networked nodes. The algorithm, dubbed GoSta, is designed to efficiently estimate U-statistics, which are essential in various machine learning and data mining applications.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's convergence rate, showing that it achieves a rate of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous versions, respectively. The convergence bounds feature data-dependent terms that reflect the hardness of the estimation problem and network-dependent terms related to the spectral gap of the network graph.

The paper also presents numerical experiments that demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over existing approaches, including U2-gossip. The experiments show that GoSta achieves better performance on various network topologies, including complete graphs, two-dimensional grids, and Watts-Strogatz networks.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposal of a novel gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing pairwise U-statistics.
* A thorough analysis of the algorithm's convergence rate, including data-dependent and network-dependent terms.
* Numerical experiments that demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over existing approaches.

However, there are some areas that require improvement:

* The paper could benefit from stronger connections to existing gossip literature and related ideas.
* The key algorithmic trick, where nodes swap a secondary state (initial observation) during averaging, should be highlighted in the main text.
* The induced edge-distribution from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" model could be clarified, as "drawing a random edge" is ambiguous.
* A comparison to a flooding/memory-heavy alternative (nodes store received tagged observations) would be useful to show tradeoffs and whether the 1/t convergence rate is good or bad.
* The related work section could be expanded to include other gossip approaches, such as those by Dimakis et al.
* The theorems/analysis should state assumptions on the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and make error guarantees explicit as a function of initial data.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of decentralized estimation and gossip algorithms. With some revisions to address the above points, the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.
