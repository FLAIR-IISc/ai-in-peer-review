

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for decentralized computation of U-statistics, which are pairwise averages over observations distributed across a network. The authors address an important gap in the literature, as existing gossip algorithms primarily handle separable statistics (like means), but not U-statistics that require pairwise interactions. The key insight is that nodes maintain a secondary state (auxiliary observation) that they swap during averaging, allowing observations to perform coupled random walks across the network. This clever trick enables efficient computation of U-statistics with convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to the decentralized computation literature, addressing an important gap with practical relevance for applications like AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter. The algorithmic insight of using coupled random walks through auxiliary state swapping is elegant and well-justified. The theoretical analysis is generally sound, with convergence rates explicitly depending on both network connectivity (through spectral gap) and data characteristics. The experiments on real datasets demonstrate clear empirical advantages over the only prior approach (U2-gossip), particularly in terms of convergence speed and variance reduction.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. **Connections to Gossip Literature:** The paper lacks sufficient connection to the rich gossip literature. The authors should cite Dimakis et al.'s "Gossip Algorithms for Distributed Signal Processing" (Proceedings of the IEEE, 2010), which is foundational to the field, as well as other key works like "Geographic Gossip" (Dimakis et al., IEEE Transactions on Signal Processing, 2008). The paper should better position itself relative to these works.

2. **Clarification of Edge Distribution:** The asynchronous model states "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" but does not clarify the induced edge distribution. The paper mentions "drawing a random edge" as ambiguous; the authors should explicitly state whether edges are drawn uniformly from E or with probabilities proportional to node degrees. This is critical for the theoretical analysis.

3. **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The paper should explicitly compare to a flooding approach where nodes store all received tagged observations (which would require O(n) memory per node). This would clarify the memory tradeoff between the proposed method (O(1) memory) and the flooding alternative, and demonstrate why the O(1/t) rate is advantageous despite the theoretical slower rate than some gossip algorithms.

4. **Lifting Constructions:** The paper should investigate whether lifting constructions (e.g., LADA or lifted Markov chains) could accelerate mixing. These techniques have been successfully applied to speed up gossip algorithms and could provide additional insights into the network-dependent terms in the convergence rates.

5. **Theoretical Assumptions and Guarantees:** The theorems should explicitly state assumptions about the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and make error guarantees more explicit as functions of initial data. The current analysis lacks these details, making it difficult to assess the practical implications of the convergence rates.

6. **Supplemental Proofs:** The reviewer could not verify the supplemental proofs due to time constraints. The authors should ensure these proofs are complete and well-documented in the main text or supplementary materials.

7. **Minor Issues:** Several small language/notation issues need attention: 
   - In Section 4.1, the phrase "the observations are thus each performing a random walk" is misleading; they perform coupled random walks.
   - The bipartite directed-graph point in the notation section needs clarification.
   - The paper should use consistent notation for the design matrix (X vs. X).

8. **Experiments:** The experiments would benefit from additional baseline comparisons beyond U2-gossip, such as a naive flooding approach with explicit memory tradeoffs. The current comparison to U2-gossip is good but would be stronger with additional baselines.

**Recommendation:** The paper addresses an important problem with a clever algorithmic solution, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The weaknesses identified are fixable with minor revisions. After addressing these comments, the paper would make a strong contribution to the literature. I have raised my score from 7 to 8 (strong accept) and recommend acceptance pending these revisions. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and advances the state of the art in decentralized computation for U-statistics.
