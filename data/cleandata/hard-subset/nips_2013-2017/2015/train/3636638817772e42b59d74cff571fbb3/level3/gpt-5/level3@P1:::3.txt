Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over networks, a setting where standard gossip (which estimates separable sums/means) is not directly applicable. The authors propose two algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, that combine (i) data propagation via random walks of auxiliary observations and (ii) averaging of local estimates. A key analytical tool is a “phantom nodes” reformulation that decouples data propagation from averaging. The paper proves convergence in expectation with rates O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, with explicit dependence on data dispersion and the network spectral gap. Empirically, the methods outperform U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010] on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs.

Relative to prior work on averaging gossip (e.g., Boyd et al., Kempe et al.) and variants using geographic acceleration, this paper focuses on non-separable functionals (pairs) and provides an alternative to U2-gossip that uses one auxiliary observation per node (versus two), requires fewer exchanges, supports asynchrony without a global clock, and yields improved rates and constants. The comparison to U2-gossip is strengthened by a refined bound for U2 showing a √n penalty due to lack of averaging.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated problem: many practical U-statistics (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter) arise in distributed settings.
- Algorithmic novelty: simple synchronous and asynchronous protocols that interleave averaging with randomized data propagation; elegant phantom-network analysis.
- Theoretical guarantees: expectation convergence with explicit network- and data-dependent terms; dependence on spectral gap matches consensus intuition and is useful for practitioners.
- Practical relevance: asynchronous variant with local Poisson clocks and per-node counters; reduced memory/communication versus U2-gossip.
- Empirical validation: consistent gains over U2-gossip across network topologies; lower across-node variance due to averaging; scaling experiment suggests widening advantage with n.

Weaknesses and questions
- Per-iteration runtime and communication cost are not reported. One GoSta iteration involves averaging and one swap; U2-gossip triggers two swaps and all nodes update. If GoSta iterations are significantly slower in wall-clock time (e.g., due to additional computations or synchronization in certain implementations), the practical advantage might shrink. Please report:
  - CPU time per iteration and per edge-activation for both methods.
  - Messages and bytes communicated per iteration (swapping one vs two d-dimensional vectors).
  - Wall-clock time to reach target error across graph types and dimensions d.
- Assumptions/robustness:
  - Non-bipartite connectivity is assumed. A standard fix is to add laziness (self-loops) or edge sampling with probability 1/2; please discuss how to adapt GoSta to bipartite graphs and whether guarantees transfer.
  - Algorithm 2 requires knowledge of pk = 2dk/|E|. Is |E| assumed known globally? If not, can pk be estimated online (e.g., via local degree and an unbiased estimator for |E|) without biasing mk?
  - What conditions on H are required for the bounds (boundedness, sub-Gaussian tails)? The statements use norms of H; please clarify assumptions ensuring finite constants.
  - Asynchronous bound only states existence of c′(G). Can it be expressed via spectral quantities (e.g., Laplacian gap) to parallel the synchronous case?
- Scope of experiments:
  - Only two datasets and degree-2 U-statistics are evaluated. Additional tasks (e.g., Kendall tau) and larger-scale n,d would strengthen significance.
  - Results are reported vs iterations; please include wall-clock plots and communication-normalized comparisons.
  - Memory advantage is asserted; quantify memory in bytes/node for both methods across d.
- Concentration: The results are in expectation. High-probability or mean-square bounds (even with logarithmic factors) would be valuable, especially for practitioners concerned with variability.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful modeling of synchronous/asynchronous dynamics and clear dependence on spectral gap. The phantom-network proof technique is insightful. Some constants and assumptions could be made more explicit, and empirical methodology would benefit from reporting computational/communication budgets.

Clarity
Well written and well organized. Algorithm descriptions are clear. Suggestions:
- Summarize assumptions on H and graph properties upfront.
- Make explicit the computational and communication costs per iteration for all methods.
- Clarify how uniform random edge selection is implemented in distributed settings (node-activated model vs explicit edge sampling) to match practice.

Originality
Good. Extends gossip to non-separable U-statistics with new analysis and asynchronous design, improving over U2-gossip in both theory and practice.

Significance
Moderate to strong. U-statistics are widely relevant, and a practical, provably convergent asynchronous algorithm is useful. The empirical section could be broadened and strengthened with wall-clock and communication-normalized comparisons.

Requested additions before acceptance
- Report per-iteration runtime, messages/bytes, and wall-clock to target accuracy; include scaling with d.
- Provide an explicit expression or bound for c′(G) in Theorem 2, preferably in terms of spectral gap.
- Discuss adaptation to bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy updates) and impact on constants.
- Clarify assumptions on H (boundedness or moments) ensuring finite norms; if possible, add high-probability bounds.
- Quantify memory costs and compare to U2-gossip.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important gap: decentralized estimation of U-statistics.
- Simple algorithms with solid theory leveraging spectral properties.
- Asynchronous operation without global clock; practical design.
- Consistent empirical improvements over prior art; lower variance due to averaging.
- Analysis technique (phantom nodes) may be useful elsewhere.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Missing wall-clock and communication-normalized comparisons could limit practical conclusions.
- Asynchronous constant c′(G) not explicit; assumptions on H not fully specified.
- Limited experimental breadth (tasks, datasets, scales).
- Dependence on global |E| for pk in async algorithm may be restrictive without a practical workaround.

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward accept, contingent on adding the requested practical comparisons and clarifying assumptions/constants. The contribution is novel, the theory is sound, and the algorithms are simple and useful for decentralized estimation.
