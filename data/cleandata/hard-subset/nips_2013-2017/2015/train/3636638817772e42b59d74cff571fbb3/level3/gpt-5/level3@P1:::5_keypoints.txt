- Proposes two randomized gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics where each node holds one observation.  
- Core idea: combine propagation of auxiliary observations via random edge swaps with averaging of local estimates at each iteration.  
- Introduces a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from estimate averaging for analysis.  
- Theoretical guarantees: expectation convergence to the true U-statistic with explicit network- and data-dependent constants.  
- Convergence rates: O(1/t) for synchronous GoSta and O((log t)/t) for asynchronous GoSta, governed by the graph spectral gap.  
- Empirical evaluation on AUC and within-cluster scatter across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs.  
- Empirically outperforms prior U2-gossip in speed and across-node variance of estimates.  
- Builds on randomized gossip/averaging and spectral-gap dependencies from spectral graph theory.  
- Targets a gap: decentralized computation of pairwise U-statistics not handled by standard gossip methods for separable sums.  
- Distinguishes from U2-gossip: uses one auxiliary observation per node plus explicit averaging, reducing communication/memory and improving rates.  
- Analysis refines U2-gossip’s bounds and explains its practical slowdown due to a √n factor.  
- Strength: clear problem motivation linking U-statistics to common measures (AUC, variance, Kendall’s tau, etc.).  
- Strength: solid finite-time bounds and careful modeling of asynchronous clocks and node selection probabilities.  
- Strength: elegant phantom-node analysis that yields tractable bounds and interpretable constants.  
- Strength: practical advantages—lower per-iteration memory/communication and simple primitives (pairwise average and swap).  
- Weakness: only expectation convergence; lacks high-probability or almost-sure concentration bounds and stopping criteria.  
- Weakness: async algorithm requires knowledge of |E| (pk = 2dk/|E|); practicality and robustness to estimation errors need discussion.  
- Weakness: assumes uniform edge sampling; practical decentralized realizations or analysis of non-uniform/degree-biased activations are missing.  
- Weakness: log factor in asynchronous rate—unclear if intrinsic or removable via step-size schedules or variance-reduction/acceleration.  
- Weakness: limited scope to degree-2 symmetric U-statistics; extensions to m>2 or non-symmetric kernels are not analyzed.  
- Weakness: experiments focus on iteration counts; missing wall-clock/communication metrics, scale-free/skewed-degree graphs, and multi-observation-per-node tests.  
- Weakness: practical costs of evaluating costly kernels H in high dimension (caching, mini-batching) are not discussed.  
- Weakness: analysis assumes non-bipartite graphs; a simple fix (lazy updates/self-loops) should be stated and validated.  
- Quality: technically sound with explicit constants and careful derivations; comparisons to U2-gossip are analytical and empirical.  
- Clarity: well organized and clearly written; algorithms and phantom-node analysis are accessible; recommend summarizing communication/memory per iteration.  
- Originality: novel framing of gossip for U-statistics and a new phantom-network analytical device; asynchronous unbiased iteration tracking is a notable twist.  
- Significance: enabling decentralized U-statistic estimation is valuable for distributed ranking, clustering, and testing applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an underexplored problem with principled algorithms, interpretable guarantees, and strong empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks probabilistic concentration results, realistic implementation discussion (|E|, sampling), and broader experiments; async rate has a log factor.  
- Questions for authors: can you provide high-probability bounds or concentration results for both algorithms?  
- Questions for authors: how sensitive is GoSta-async to misspecification of |E| and are there adaptive estimators?  
- Questions for authors: can the approach extend to degree-m U-statistics and what are the costs/rates?  
- Questions for authors: would adding laziness/self-loops handle bipartite graphs without changing rates?  
- Questions for authors: are there prospects for accelerating the averaging step (e.g., Chebyshev/accelerated consensus)?
