

This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a significant gap in decentralized computation literature. The authors extend gossip algorithms beyond mean estimation to handle more complex statistics like AUC, variance, and within-cluster point scatter, which require pairwise data interactions. Previous work (e.g., U2-gossip) suffered from high memory/communication costs and lacked asynchronicity, while this work achieves O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence rates for synchronous and asynchronous settings respectively, with explicit data and network-dependent terms.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear theoretical foundation. The key innovation—using "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem—provides a clever and rigorous approach to separate data propagation from averaging steps. The convergence proofs are well-executed and significantly improve upon U2-gossip's theoretical guarantees. Experimental validation on real datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) consistently shows superior performance in terms of both convergence speed and estimation accuracy across various network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz).

Clarity is excellent: the paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, intuitive algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2), and effective visualizations (Figures 2-3). The connection to prior work is appropriately contextualized, particularly in Section 3 where the limitations of U2-gossip are thoroughly analyzed.

Originality is strong—the application of gossip algorithms to U-statistics represents a meaningful extension of decentralized computation, and the phantom node technique offers a novel analytical framework. The significance is high, as U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning applications where centralized computation is impractical.

Strengths: Theoretical contribution is rigorous, experimental validation is comprehensive, and the algorithms offer practical advantages in memory/communication efficiency. Weakness: The practical implementation details (e.g., handling node failures) could be expanded, and the discussion of how network topology parameters affect the constants in convergence rates could be more detailed.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to distributed optimization literature with clear theoretical advances and empirical validation. The work is technically sound, well-presented, and addresses an important problem with significant practical implications. I strongly recommend acceptance.
