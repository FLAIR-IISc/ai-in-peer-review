This paper introduces GoSta, a family of synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the decentralized estimation of U-statistics. This problem extends the well-studied topic of distributed averaging (e.g., Boyd et al., NIPS 2004) to a more complex class of statistics that depend on pairs of observations, such as AUC and within-cluster scatter. The work builds directly upon Pelckmans et al. (2010), which to our knowledge is the only prior work on gossip algorithms for U-statistics, by proposing methods with improved convergence, memory, and communication efficiency.

The paper's primary strengths lie in its technical quality, clarity, and significance.

**Quality:** The work is technically sound. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. The use of a "phantom network" construction is an elegant tool for the proof. These theoretical claims are thoroughly validated by experiments on real-world datasets and various network topologies, which clearly demonstrate the proposed algorithms' superior performance and convergence behavior compared to the previous state-of-the-art approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The motivation is well-established, the algorithms are presented concisely, and the connection between the theoretical analysis and the practical results is explicit. The authors clearly articulate how their method improves upon prior art.

**Originality:** The proposed algorithms are novel. By combining a data propagation step (swapping auxiliary observations) with an estimate averaging step, the authors present a new and effective technique for this problem. This represents a significant and non-trivial extension of existing gossip-based methods to a more challenging computational setting.

**Significance:** The contribution is highly significant. U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning, and the need for efficient, decentralized estimation methods is growing. The proposed GoSta algorithms provide a practical and theoretically-grounded solution that is likely to be used and built upon by researchers in distributed and large-scale machine learning.

The paper has no major weaknesses. It is a complete and polished piece of work. One minor suggestion for future work, as the authors themselves note, could be to investigate adaptive communication schemes to further accelerate convergence on poorly connected networks.

In summary, this paper presents a strong contribution with a novel method, solid theoretical backing, and convincing empirical validation for an important problem. It clearly advances the state of the art and should be of great interest to the NIPS community.
