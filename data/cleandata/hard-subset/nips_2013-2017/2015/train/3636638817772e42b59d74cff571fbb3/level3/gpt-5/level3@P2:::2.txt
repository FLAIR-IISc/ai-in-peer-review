Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in networks, a class that includes AUC, variance, Gini mean difference, Kendall tau, and within-cluster scatter. The key idea is to combine (i) averaging of local estimates with (ii) propagation of raw observations so that each node can approximate its local hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi,Xj) and then average these across the network. Concretely, each node keeps a single auxiliary observation Yk (initialized to Xk), updates its estimate with a running average of H(Xk,Yk), and when a random edge is selected, the two incident nodes average their estimates and swap auxiliary observations. Thus, observations perform coupled random walks over the network while estimates mix via standard gossip.

Analysis uses a “phantom network” construction to decouple averaging and propagation and yields convergence in expectation at rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants that depend explicitly on the spectral gap of the graph. The paper contrasts this with U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), showing better rates/communication-memory tradeoffs, and supports the claims with experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several graph families.

This work is well situated within the literature on randomized gossip/consensus (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al. Proc. IEEE 2010; geographic gossip; rumor spreading). It goes beyond separable functions to pairwise U-statistics. Related acceleration ideas such as location-aided consensus and lifting/nonreversible random walks (e.g., LADA, lifted Markov chains) seem closely connected and are worth discussing as potential speed-ups for the data-propagation component.

Quality
- Theory: The phantom-node analysis is elegant and appears sound; the rates and their dependence on spectral gap are plausible and match intuition. The asynchronous construction with per-node counters mk gives unbiased updates and achieves O(log t/t), though the constant c′(G) is not explicit. Please make assumptions on data explicit: are results worst-case over fixed X and H or in expectation over a data distribution? High-probability bounds (e.g., under bounded/sub-Gaussian H) would strengthen the results. Clarify whether the O(1/t) rate is minimax for this decentralized setting, or provide a lower bound.
- Model clarity: “Draw (i,j) uniformly from E” is ambiguous in asynchronous settings where a node wakes and contacts a neighbor via a stochastic matrix P. Please specify the induced edge distribution (e.g., pij ∝ degree-based selection leads to uniform over edges only under specific choices) and reconcile with the pk = 2dk/|E| choice. For bipartite graphs, add a standard lazy modification and state explicitly whether this removes the non-bipartite requirement.
- Comparison: The refined bound for U2-gossip is helpful. A stronger empirical baseline would be a flooding/memory-heavy scheme in which nodes cache tagged observations and compute hi locally; comparing convergence vs. memory/communication (words per edge per iteration) would illuminate trade-offs and contextualize the O(1/t) rate.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The core trick—swapping the auxiliary observation so that observations execute coupled random walks—should be highlighted earlier and more prominently as the central mechanism. The phantom-network construction is clear, but a small illustrative example would aid intuition. Please:
- State explicitly in the theorems whether X is fixed and H bounded, and define all data-dependent norms in the bounds.
- Specify all constants (e.g., c′(G)) and their dependence on spectral parameters if possible.
- Unify the activation model notation (uniform edge sampling vs. node activation with neighbor selection).
- Minor issues: “non-bipartite” (hyphenation), “performance” (typo), ensure consistent use of Wα(G), and fix small phrasing glitches (e.g., directed/bipartite mention).

Originality
Extending gossip to non-separable U-statistics with a single auxiliary sample per node and an averaging step is a novel and simple design that improves over prior U2-gossip. The analytical device via phantom nodes and the explicit spectral-gap dependence are original and useful. The connection to lifting/nonreversible schemes is not exploited here and could open an avenue for further novelty.

Significance
U-statistics are ubiquitous; enabling their decentralized estimation with low memory/communication and provable rates is valuable. The results can serve as a building block for decentralized learning/optimization involving pairwise losses (e.g., AUC, metric learning). The bounds’ dependence on spectral gap provides design guidance for network architectures.

Empirical evaluation
The experiments on AUC and cluster scatter across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs show consistent improvements over U2-gossip, and synchronous vs. asynchronous variants perform similarly in practice. To strengthen the empirical case:
- Add baselines: flooding/caching with various cache sizes; push-sum variants; naive centralized (for reference) with communication budget normalization.
- Report communication/memory per iteration and wall-clock or message complexity to a fixed error.
- Vary spectral gap systematically and show how rates track 1−λ2.
- Report variability and perhaps node-wise convergence histograms.

Pros
- Addresses an important and underexplored decentralized estimation problem.
- Simple, practical algorithms with low memory/communication.
- Clean analysis with explicit spectral-gap dependence; improved rates over U2-gossip.
- Empirical results on real datasets and multiple graph families.

Cons
- Ambiguities in the activation/edge-selection model; constants in asynchronous bound not explicit.
- Missing stronger baselines and communication/memory-vs-accuracy trade-off study.
- Assumptions on data and types of guarantees (worst-case vs probabilistic, high-probability) not fully spelled out.
- Limited discussion of potential accelerations (lifting/nonreversible dynamics).

Questions for authors
1) Precisely specify the edge sampling distribution in both sync/async models and conditions under which it is uniform. How sensitive are guarantees to deviations from uniform edge sampling?
2) Can adding laziness remove the non-bipartite assumption? Any impact on constants?
3) Are there high-probability bounds under bounded or sub-Gaussian H? Is the O(1/t) rate tight?
4) How would lifting/nonreversible random walks or location-aided schemes impact data propagation and constants?
5) Provide quantitative communication/memory comparisons vs. U2-gossip and flooding baselines.

Minor comments
- Unify notation for Wα(G); define all norms used in bounds; ensure all constants are defined on first use.
- Fix typos (e.g., “perfomance”→“performance”), hyphenation, and a few punctuation/spacing issues in algorithms.
- Clarify the remark about directed/bipartite graphs.

Overall rating: 8 (accept)
Confidence: Moderate. I could not verify all supplemental proofs due to time; however, the approach is sound, the contributions are clear and useful, and the paper should be accepted provided the clarifications and additional comparisons are addressed.
