This paper addresses the problem of estimating U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance, within-cluster scatter), are fundamental quantities in machine learning and statistics. However, their non-separable nature makes them incompatible with standard gossip algorithms designed for simple averaging. The authors propose two new randomized gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for the synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively. The core idea is to combine two iterative steps: a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary observations to gain global information, and an estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic. This work builds upon the literature of gossip algorithms for averaging (e.g., Boyd et al., 2006) and directly improves upon the only prior work on gossip for U-statistics, the U2-gossip algorithm of Pelckmans & Suykens (2011).

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The main claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical contribution is strong. The authors provide convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous one. The analysis, based on a novel "phantom network" reformulation, is elegant and clearly shows how the convergence rate depends on both data-dependent terms and the spectral gap of the network graph. The experimental evaluation is also convincing, demonstrating that the proposed GoSta algorithm converges faster in terms of iterations than the U2-gossip baseline across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) and for two different U-statistics (AUC and within-cluster scatter).
*   **Weaknesses:** The experimental results are presented in terms of the number of iterations. While this is appropriate for evaluating the convergence rate of the algorithm, it may not fully capture the practical performance. The paper claims lower communication costs than U2-gossip (one swap vs. two swaps per iteration), but it is not clear what the difference in computational cost per iteration is. If one iteration of GoSta is significantly slower than one iteration of U2-gossip, the practical advantage shown in the plots might be less pronounced.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation for the problem is clearly articulated in the introduction. The problem statement, notation, and proposed algorithms are defined precisely. The "phantom network" concept used for the analysis, while abstract, is explained well and its purpose as an analytical tool is made clear. The figures are informative and support the paper's claims effectively.

**Originality**
The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** While the problem of decentralized estimation has been studied extensively, the specific focus on U-statistics is a less investigated area. This paper presents a novel approach that differs significantly from the only existing competitor, U2-gossip. The combination of data propagation with explicit estimate averaging is a key new idea that leads to improved performance. Furthermore, the development of a fully asynchronous algorithm for this problem is a novel and important contribution, as the asynchronous setting is more realistic for many practical distributed systems.

**Significance**
The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful.

*   **Strengths:** U-statistics are widely used, and the ability to compute them efficiently in a decentralized manner has broad applications in large-scale machine learning, sensor networks, and peer-to-peer computing. The proposed GoSta algorithms demonstrably advance the state of the art by providing a method that is provably faster and requires less memory and communication overhead than the previous best approach. The work could also serve as a foundational component for more complex tasks, such as decentralized optimization of objective functions involving U-statistics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles an important and practical problem in distributed machine learning.
*   It introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous algorithms with strong theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   The proposed method is shown to be superior to the existing state-of-the-art both theoretically (better bounds, lower overhead) and empirically (faster convergence per iteration).
*   The paper is very clearly written and presents a complete, high-quality scientific contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental comparison relies solely on the number of iterations, omitting wall-clock time comparisons. This leaves a minor question about the true practical speed-up, as the per-iteration computational complexity is not analyzed.

**Conclusion and Suggestions for Authors**
Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance on an important problem. The strengths—a novel and well-motivated approach, strong theoretical analysis, and compelling empirical results—far outweigh the minor weakness in the experimental evaluation. I strongly recommend acceptance.

For the final version, I would encourage the authors to add a brief discussion on the per-iteration computational complexity of GoSta versus U2-gossip. If possible, including an experiment that compares the methods based on wall-clock time would further strengthen the paper's claims of practical superiority and address the only notable concern.
