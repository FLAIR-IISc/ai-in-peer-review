## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics in networks, a less explored area compared to average estimation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, providing convergence bounds and demonstrating superior performance to the existing U2-gossip baseline across various graph scenarios. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a relevant problem with a solid theoretical and empirical foundation. 

The core contribution lies in extending the well-established gossip framework to handle the pair-wise averaging inherent in U-statistics computation. The introduction of “phantom nodes” to facilitate analysis is a clever technique, and the resulting convergence bounds (O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous) are valuable. The comparison against U2-gossip is well-justified, highlighting the benefits of the proposed approach in terms of convergence rate, memory usage, and applicability to asynchronous settings. The experimental evaluation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real datasets further strengthens the claims. The paper appropriately references relevant work in gossip algorithms and U-statistics, demonstrating a good understanding of the existing literature.

However, a key practical consideration is missing: per-iteration run-times for both the proposed method and U2-gossip. While the theoretical convergence analysis shows faster rates for the proposed algorithms, if each iteration is significantly slower than U2-gossip, the overall practical utility could be limited. The authors acknowledge the communication and memory costs are lower, but computational complexity per iteration is not discussed. This is a crucial point, as the constant factors hidden within the asymptotic notation can dominate performance in practice. 

Furthermore, while the convergence analysis is rigorous, the assumptions regarding network connectivity (connected and non-bipartite) might be restrictive in some real-world scenarios. Exploring the algorithm’s behavior under weaker connectivity assumptions could be a valuable direction for future work. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem.
*   Provides novel algorithms with theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrates superior performance compared to the existing baseline.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of per-iteration runtime comparison with U2-gossip.
*   Restricted network connectivity assumptions.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of distributed estimation. Addressing the runtime concern would further solidify its impact and increase its practical relevance. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors providing per-iteration runtime results in the final version.
