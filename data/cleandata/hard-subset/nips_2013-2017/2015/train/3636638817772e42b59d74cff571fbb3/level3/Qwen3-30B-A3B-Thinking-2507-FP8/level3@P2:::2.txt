

# Review for "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are quantities depending on pairwise interactions between observations (e.g., AUC, within-cluster scatter). The authors address an important gap in decentralized computation, as existing gossip algorithms primarily handle separable statistics (like averages) but not pairwise statistics requiring cross-observation averaging.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:** The paper extends the gossip framework to handle U-statistics by introducing a clever "phantom network" construction to decouple data propagation from averaging. This approach builds on classic gossip work by Boyd et al. (2006) and Dimakis et al. (2010) but addresses a significant limitation in existing literature. While previous work (e.g., [17]) has considered U-statistics with U2-gossip, the current paper's approach achieves better convergence rates with lower memory and communication costs. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing body of decentralized optimization literature, though it could better contextualize its relationship to recent advances in gossip algorithms for more complex statistics.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality:** The key innovation of using coupled random walks for data propagation via secondary state swapping is novel and elegantly solves the U-statistic estimation problem. This differs significantly from existing approaches like U2-gossip, which requires storing two auxiliary observations and has higher communication costs.
2. **Significance:** The paper addresses a practically important problem in decentralized machine learning (e.g., distributed AUC estimation) with clear applications. The theoretical convergence rates (O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous) improve upon prior work.
3. **Clarity:** The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are well-structured, and the phantom network construction provides a useful conceptual framework. The experimental section demonstrates clear empirical advantages over U2-gossip across multiple network topologies and real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Related Work:** The related work section omits key papers in gossip algorithms, particularly Dimakis et al.'s "Gossip Algorithms for Distributed Signal Processing" (Proc. IEEE, 2010) and "Geographic Gossip" (IEEE Trans. Signal Processing, 2008). This weakens the paper's positioning within the broader gossip literature.
2. **Analysis Depth:** The convergence analysis (Theorems 1-2) lacks explicit assumptions about the initial data distribution and doesn't clearly state error guarantees as a function of initial conditions. The "phantom network" construction, while clever, isn't sufficiently motivated in the main text.
3. **Algorithmic Clarity:** The edge-drawing mechanism ("drawing a random edge uniformly at random") is ambiguous. The paper should clarify whether this corresponds to a node waking up and selecting a neighbor (as in classic gossip) or an edge-based selection model, as these have different convergence properties.
4. **Experimental Comparison:** While the paper compares to U2-gossip, it lacks comparison to a simple flooding-based baseline (where nodes store all received observations), making it difficult to assess the true communication/memory efficiency gains.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand related work to include Dimakis et al.'s foundational gossip papers and recent advances in decentralized U-statistics.
2. Clarify the edge-drawing mechanism and explicitly state the probability model used (e.g., "each edge is selected with probability 1/|E| per iteration").
3. Add a comparison to a simple flooding baseline to better demonstrate the communication/memory efficiency of the proposed approach.
4. Discuss whether the convergence rates can be improved using lifting techniques (e.g., LADA or lifted Markov chains), which are relevant to the phantom network construction.
5. Explicitly state assumptions about the initial data distribution and provide error bounds as a function of initial conditions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a meaningful gap in decentralized computation.
- The proposed algorithms are theoretically sound with better convergence rates than prior work.
- Experimental results consistently show superior performance across multiple datasets and network topologies.
- The key innovation (coupled random walks via secondary state swapping) is novel and well-executed.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The related work section omits critical papers in the gossip literature.
- The algorithmic description lacks clarity on the edge selection model.
- The theoretical analysis would benefit from more explicit assumptions and error guarantees.
- The experimental section could better demonstrate the communication/memory advantages.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong contribution to the field of decentralized computation with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over existing methods. The paper's main weaknesses lie in its incomplete contextualization within the gossip literature and some ambiguities in the algorithm description. With careful revisions addressing the specific suggestions above, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's technical quality is high, the significance is clear, and the originality is well-justified. The suggested revisions are primarily about strengthening the paper's connection to the broader literature and clarifying certain technical aspects, rather than addressing fundamental flaws. After addressing these points, the paper would make a compelling contribution to the NIPS community.
