This paper introduces new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in distributed networks. The motivation stems from the limitations of existing gossip algorithms, which struggle with computations requiring averaging over pairs of observations, essential for quantities like empirical variance, AUC, and Gini mean difference. The authors address this gap by proposing algorithms that propagate data and maintain local estimates, providing convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case.  These bounds are explicitly data and network dependent, demonstrating faster convergence on well-connected networks. The paper builds upon prior work in gossip algorithms [Boyd et al., 2005; Kempe et al., 2006] and U-statistic theory [Lee, 1995], while distinguishing itself by tackling the challenging problem of distributed U-statistic estimation.  The work is presented clearly and is well-structured.

A key strength of this paper lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide convergence guarantees, tying the rate to both data characteristics and network topology (spectral gap [Chung, 1997]). The clever reformulation of the problem using “phantom nodes” is a notable contribution, enabling a clean and insightful analysis.  Furthermore, the experimental validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation using real datasets convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed algorithms over the existing U2-gossip approach [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2015]. The comparisons across different network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz [Watts & Strogatz, 1998]) provide a robust assessment of performance. The paper also clearly articulates the advantages of their approach, including lower memory and communication costs. 

However, some aspects could be strengthened. While the theoretical analysis is thorough, the practical implications of the convergence rates aren't fully explored. The paper demonstrates that the algorithms converge, but lacks a detailed discussion of the constants involved and their impact on runtime in realistic scenarios.  Specifically, including per-iteration run-times for both the proposed method (GoSta) and U2-gossip would be highly valuable. The reviewer’s concern is valid: if GoSta’s iterations are significantly slower than U2-gossip, the theoretical improvements in convergence rate may not translate to a practical advantage.  A more detailed complexity analysis, particularly regarding the computational cost of the averaging steps, would also be helpful. 

Regarding originality, the paper presents a novel combination of gossip algorithms and U-statistic estimation, addressing a previously under-explored area. The introduction of phantom nodes and the explicit network-dependent convergence bounds constitute original contributions. The related work section is comprehensive, appropriately citing relevant literature. 

In terms of significance, this work has the potential to impact a range of applications where decentralized estimation of U-statistics is crucial, including sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale data analysis.  The algorithms provide a practical solution for computing important statistical properties in a distributed manner. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods suggest a tangible advancement in the state-of-the-art.  The inclusion of real-world datasets strengthens the impact of the findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem in distributed estimation.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Strong empirical validation demonstrating superior performance over existing methods.
*   Well-written and clearly organized.
*   Potential for broad applicability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed runtime analysis and complexity assessment.
*   The theoretical improvements in convergence rate need to be balanced against practical iteration costs.
*   While well-written, the convergence analysis could be made more accessible to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper.  While a more detailed runtime analysis would be beneficial, the novel approach, rigorous theoretical foundation, and compelling empirical results make this a significant contribution to the field. The paper addresses an important problem with a well-designed solution, and the potential impact is substantial. Addressing the reviewer’s concerns about per-iteration runtimes in a revised version would further strengthen the paper.
