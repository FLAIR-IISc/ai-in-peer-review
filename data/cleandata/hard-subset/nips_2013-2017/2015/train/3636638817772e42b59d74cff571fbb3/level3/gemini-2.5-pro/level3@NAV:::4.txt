This paper addresses the important problem of computing U-statistics in a distributed, decentralized environment using gossip-based communication. The authors propose GoSta, a novel family of synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms. The core idea is for each node to maintain both a local estimate of the U-statistic and an auxiliary data observation. At each step, nodes combine local estimate averaging with a data propagation step, where auxiliary observations are swapped between neighbors. This extends the canonical gossip averaging framework, which typically handles sums of separable functions, to the more complex domain of statistics based on pairs of observations, such as AUC or sample variance.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and significant problem. The ability to efficiently compute complex, non-separable statistics in a decentralized manner is highly relevant for modern large-scale machine learning, sensor networks, and peer-to-peer systems.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, presenting a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms. The authors provide rigorous proofs for the convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous versions, clearly linking performance to network topology via the spectral gap.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the proposed algorithm is intuitive and easy to understand. The problem statement and methodology are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Evaluation:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While the paper provides a comparison against the U2-gossip algorithm from prior work, it critically lacks comparisons to simpler, more intuitive baseline methods. This makes it difficult to fully assess the practical contribution and efficiency of the proposed method. Without knowing how GoSta performs against a naive random sampling or a simple data flooding approach, the claimed superiority is only contextualized against one other specialized algorithm.
*   **Originality:** While the application and theoretical analysis are novel, the algorithmic components themselves are extensions of familiar gossip primitives (averaging and random walks). The conceptual leap is straightforward, with the main novelty resting in the rigorous formal analysis.

In summary, this is a solid paper with strong theoretical results on an important problem. To be considered for acceptance, the authors should significantly strengthen the experimental section. I strongly recommend including comparisons to at least one or two simple baselines (e.g., a "random query" baseline where nodes periodically pull a data point from a random peer). The paper mentions such comparisons exist in the supplementary material; these results should be summarized in the main paper to provide a more convincing and complete evaluation of the algorithm's performance.
