## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper tackles the important problem of distributed U-statistic estimation in networks, a less explored area compared to average computation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, and provide convergence rate bounds for both. The work is well motivated, clearly written, and addresses a gap in the existing literature. It builds upon the established foundation of gossip algorithms (e.g., [19, 2, 5]) and extends them to handle the more computationally demanding task of U-statistic calculation, which has relevance to a variety of machine learning problems like AUC estimation and variance computation. 

A key strength lies in the theoretical analysis, establishing convergence bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous one, with explicit dependencies on data and network characteristics. This is a significant improvement over the existing U2-gossip baseline [17], and the authors convincingly demonstrate this both theoretically and empirically. The innovative use of “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem and facilitate the analysis is particularly clever. The experiments on two datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) consistently show superior performance of GoSta algorithms compared to U2-gossip, especially as the network size increases.

However, a crucial point that needs clarification is the per-iteration runtime of both the proposed algorithms and U2-gossip. While the paper demonstrates superior convergence rates, a slower runtime per iteration for GoSta could limit its practical utility. The authors acknowledge this concern implicitly, but a direct comparison of runtimes (even with a smaller network size) would significantly strengthen the evaluation. If the iterations are substantially slower, the theoretical gains in convergence rate may be offset. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous convergence analysis.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Convincing empirical results demonstrating superior performance over a baseline.
*   Provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between convergence rate and network characteristics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of per-iteration runtime comparison with U2-gossip, which is critical for assessing practical viability.



**Overall:** I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors providing per-iteration runtime comparisons in the revised version. Addressing this concern will allow for a more complete assessment of the algorithm’s practicality and impact.
