Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles decentralized computation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network, a problem that standard gossip algorithms for separable functions cannot handle efficiently. Building on classical randomized gossip for means (e.g., Kempe et al. 2003; Boyd et al. 2006) and the prior U2-Gossip method (Pelckmans & Suykens 2013), the authors propose two new algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node keeps a single auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via pairwise swaps, while nodes also average local estimates when an edge is activated. A “phantom nodes” analytical device allows separation of data propagation and averaging, yielding convergence-in-expectation rates of O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async), with explicit dependence on data dispersion and the graph spectral gap. Compared to U2-Gossip, GoSta uses less memory and communication (one auxiliary sample and one edge activation per iteration) and achieves faster rates. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter across three graph families (complete, grid, Watts–Strogatz) demonstrate consistent improvements.

Strengths:
- Novel algorithms for decentralized U-statistic estimation with both synchronous and fully asynchronous operation, the latter matching realistic settings without global clocks.
- Faster convergence than U2-Gossip: O(1/t) in synchronous mode; O(log t/t) in asynchronous mode; and a refined analysis showing a dominating √n factor in U2-Gossip’s bound.
- Lower memory and communication cost than U2-Gossip: one auxiliary observation per node and a single edge activation per iteration, beneficial in high-dimensional settings.
- Theoretical guarantees with clean network dependence via spectral gap and data-dependent terms quantifying estimation hardness; the phantom-network proof technique is insightful.
- Solid empirical study on two representative U-statistics (AUC, within-cluster scatter) and three network topologies; results align with theory and show clear superiority to baselines.
- Clear, well-structured writing, good positioning within the gossip literature, and appropriate citations.

Weaknesses:
- Guarantees are in expectation; high-probability or almost-sure convergence bounds are not provided.
- Empirics use two datasets of moderate size; no large-scale or high-dimensional stress tests, nor real asynchronous delays/packet loss.
- Comparison set is limited (mainly U2-Gossip). Additional baselines (e.g., push-sum variants, mini-batch or sketch-based methods, or centralized approximations) would strengthen claims.
- Some practical details are assumed: knowledge of |E| for pk; handling of bipartite graphs (though standard laziness fixes may apply); overhead of computing H at each step.
- Communication/computation trade-offs are not quantified in wall-clock or message counts to a fixed accuracy ε.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound. The use of the phantom network to decouple mixing and propagation is clever and leads to transparent bounds with correct dependence on the spectral gap. The asynchronous scheme’s unbiased iteration estimator is well motivated. Proof details are delegated to the supplement; providing a succinct intuition for c′(G) and conditions for bounded H would help. Experiments are carefully run with multiple runs and different topologies; scaling plots further support the analysis.

Clarity:
The paper is well organized and easy to follow. Algorithms are clearly specified. The derivation of Wα(G) and its spectral properties are explained with enough context. Minor suggestions: explicitly summarize per-iteration compute/memory/communication costs in a small table; add a short remark on handling bipartite graphs via lazy updates; clarify how AUC denominators (class counts) are handled in a decentralized manner.

Originality:
The problem (distributed U-statistics) is underexplored; this is, to my knowledge, the first asynchronous algorithm with provable rates for this setting. The phantom-node analysis and the refined comparison to U2-Gossip are novel and insightful.

Significance:
U-statistics underpin many important metrics (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall’s tau, within-cluster scatter), so enabling their decentralized estimation with rigorous guarantees is impactful. The methods are simple to implement and should be useful in sensor networks and distributed ML systems. The improved rates and lower resource requirements over the prior state-of-the-art advance the area.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Can you provide high-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration) or almost-sure convergence under bounded H?
- How does GoSta behave on time-varying or directed graphs (e.g., push-sum adaptations)?
- Can the edge-selection distribution be optimized (non-uniform sampling) to improve constants, especially on irregular graphs?
- Please quantify message sizes and total communication to reach ε-accuracy across methods.
- For AUC, how are class counts estimated/distributed; do you run a separate gossip for denominators?
- Could the phantom-network idea extend to higher-order U-statistics (order > 2) or M-estimators built from pairwise losses?

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with practical relevance.
- Strong theoretical contributions with clear, interpretable rates.
- Asynchronous algorithm suitable for realistic deployments.
- Lower memory/communication costs than prior work.
- Consistent empirical improvements across datasets and graph families.
- Clear, well written, and well positioned relative to prior art.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lack of high-probability/AS convergence guarantees.
- Limited scale and scope of experiments; few comparative baselines beyond U2-Gossip.
- Practical assumptions (e.g., knowledge of |E|, static graphs) not stress-tested.

Overall assessment:
A strong, well-executed paper that advances decentralized estimation beyond separable functions. The combination of novel algorithms, solid theory, and corroborating experiments makes it a valuable contribution to NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance after minor clarifications, especially on communication costs, decentralized handling of denominators (for AUC), and discussion of concentration guarantees.
