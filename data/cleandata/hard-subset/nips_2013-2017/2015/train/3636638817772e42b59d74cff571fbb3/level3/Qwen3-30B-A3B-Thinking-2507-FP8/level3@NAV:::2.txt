

This paper introduces GoSta algorithms for decentralized computation of U-statistics in networked systems, addressing a significant gap in gossip-based distributed estimation. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous methods that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local U-statistic estimates, achieving O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence rates respectively. The key innovation involves nodes swapping a secondary state (initial observation) during averaging, enabling observations to perform coupled random walks—a crucial technique that should be more prominently highlighted in the main text.

The contribution is valuable for extending gossip algorithms to handle pairwise statistics, but the paper requires stronger connections to existing gossip literature. The authors should explicitly cite Dimakis et al.'s seminal Proc. IEEE work on gossip algorithms and discuss how their phantom network construction relates to lifting techniques like LADA or lifted Markov chains, which could potentially accelerate mixing. The induced edge-distribution from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" model is currently ambiguous; the paper should clarify whether "drawing a random edge" refers to uniform sampling over edges or a different process.

A comparison to a flooding alternative (where nodes store received tagged observations) would better demonstrate the communication-memory tradeoffs, as the current analysis doesn't address whether the 1/t rate is favorable compared to memory-intensive approaches. The theorems lack explicit assumptions about initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and fail to make error guarantees explicit as functions of initial data. Minor language/notation issues (e.g., bipartite directed-graph point) require correction.

Strengths include a novel phantom network reformulation, strong theoretical convergence rates, and compelling experiments showing GoSta's superiority over U2-gossip in AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation. However, experiments would benefit from additional baseline comparisons to more fully demonstrate empirical advantages.

Pro: Novel approach to decentralized U-statistics, elegant theoretical framework, strong empirical results.
Con: Weak connections to gossip literature, insufficient comparison to alternatives, unclear analytical assumptions.

After addressing these concerns, the paper would make a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (score 8).
