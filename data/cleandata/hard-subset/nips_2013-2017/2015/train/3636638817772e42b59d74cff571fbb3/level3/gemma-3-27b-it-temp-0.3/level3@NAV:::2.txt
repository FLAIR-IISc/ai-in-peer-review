## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized U-statistic computation in networked systems, extending the well-studied area of gossip algorithms beyond simple averages. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and provide convergence rate bounds. The core idea – nodes swapping a secondary state (initial observation) during averaging to induce coupled random walks – is a clever algorithmic trick that allows for efficient U-statistic estimation. This is a valuable contribution, as U-statistics are fundamental to many machine learning tasks and decentralized settings.

The paper is generally well-written and the problem statement is clearly motivated. However, the connection to the broader gossip literature could be strengthened. While references to foundational gossip work are present, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of recent advancements and variations in gossip protocols (e.g., Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE, as rightly pointed out).  The algorithmic novelty of the coupled random walk induced by the secondary state swapping should be more prominently highlighted in the main text, as it's the key to the approach. 

A significant weakness is the lack of a comparative analysis against a more straightforward, albeit potentially less efficient, alternative. Comparing against a flooding-based approach where nodes store all received tagged observations would clearly demonstrate the trade-offs and justify the 1/t (or log t/t) convergence rate. Furthermore, the authors should explore related work on lifting constructions (LADA, lifted Markov chains) as these techniques could potentially accelerate mixing and improve convergence. 

The theoretical analysis would be more robust by explicitly stating assumptions on the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and providing error guarantees as a function of the initial data.  I was unable to verify the supplemental proofs due to time constraints. Minor language and notation issues (e.g., the bipartite directed-graph point) should be addressed.  Finally, the experimental evaluation lacks baseline comparisons; demonstrating empirical benefits against existing methods is crucial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The problem is relevant, the proposed algorithms are novel and theoretically sound, and the initial experimental results are promising. The core idea of using coupled random walks is insightful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to related work and alternative approaches, and the experimental evaluation is incomplete. The theoretical analysis could be strengthened with more explicit assumptions and error bounds.

**Overall:**  I initially scored this paper a 7. After considering the potential of the approach and the authors' willingness to address the concerns raised, I raise the score to 8 and recommend acceptance *if* the identified weaknesses are adequately addressed during the revision process. Specifically, strengthening the related work section, adding a comparative analysis against a flooding approach, and expanding the experimental evaluation are critical for acceptance.
