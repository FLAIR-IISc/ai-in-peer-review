

This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for decentralized computation of U-statistics, a significant extension beyond existing gossip-based mean estimation. The authors address an important gap in distributed computing literature, as U-statistics (e.g., AUC, variance, Gini mean difference) require pairwise data interactions that prior gossip algorithms cannot efficiently handle. Their approach, named GoSta, maintains local estimates while simultaneously propagating data across the network, with convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings—improving upon the O(1/t) rate of U2-Gossip [17] while achieving lower memory and communication costs.

The paper excels in all four key criteria. Quality is strong with rigorous theoretical analysis using a clever "phantom node" reformulation to separate data propagation and averaging steps, complemented by thorough empirical validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation across three network topologies. Clarity is excellent—well-organized, well-motivated, and properly positioned relative to prior work, with clear comparisons to U2-Gossip. Originality is evident in the novel algorithm design addressing an underexplored problem, distinct from previous gossip approaches. Significance is high as U-statistics are fundamental to many machine learning applications, and the algorithms offer practical advantages in resource-constrained networks.

The only minor limitation is that the phantom network analysis, while mathematically elegant, may require careful explanation for readers unfamiliar with graph-theoretic reformulations. However, this does not undermine the paper's contributions. The authors appropriately acknowledge U2-Gossip's limitations while demonstrating superior performance in both theory and practice.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, making a clear, significant contribution to distributed optimization. The combination of strong theory, practical implementation, and thorough empirical evaluation meets all conference standards. I strongly recommend acceptance with no major concerns. The work advances the state-of-the-art in decentralized estimation and provides valuable tools for practitioners dealing with U-statistics in distributed settings.
