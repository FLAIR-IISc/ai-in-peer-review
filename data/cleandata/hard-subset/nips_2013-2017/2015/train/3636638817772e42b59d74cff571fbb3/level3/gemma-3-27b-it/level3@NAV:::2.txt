## Review of “Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics”

This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized U-statistic computation in networked systems, extending the well-studied area of gossip algorithms beyond simple averages. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and provide convergence rate bounds. This is a valuable contribution, as U-statistics are fundamental to many applications like variance estimation, AUC calculation, and Gini mean difference computation – all crucial in decentralized machine learning and data analysis.

The core algorithmic idea – nodes swapping a secondary “observation” during averaging leading to coupled random walks – is clever and effective. However, the paper would benefit from more explicit connections to existing gossip literature. While references [19, 18, 5] are cited, a deeper discussion of how this work builds upon, or diverges from, established gossip paradigms (beyond averaging) would strengthen the positioning. The novelty of the "phantom node" reformulation is intriguing, but this central algorithmic trick isn’t sufficiently highlighted in the main text; emphasizing its role in facilitating the analysis would be beneficial.

A key weakness is the lack of a comparative analysis against a straightforward, albeit potentially less efficient, approach. For instance, comparing to a flooding-based algorithm where nodes store and re-transmit tagged observations would illuminate the tradeoffs involved in the proposed method, particularly regarding the achieved 1/t convergence rate. Further, expanding the related work section to include Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) and other relevant gossip algorithms beyond those currently cited is necessary. The authors should also investigate potential acceleration through techniques like lifting constructions (e.g., LADA, lifted Markov chains), given the observed relationship to Markov chain mixing. 

The theoretical analysis needs to explicitly state assumptions regarding the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and provide concrete error guarantees as a function of the initial data, beyond merely stating convergence. Finally, while the paper reports experiments, they lack baseline comparisons to demonstrate empirical benefits beyond the work in [17]. 

I could not fully verify the proofs in the supplemental material due to time constraints. Several minor language and notation issues (e.g., the bipartite directed-graph comment) should be addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The problem is relevant and well-motivated, the proposed algorithms are novel, and the theoretical analysis provides initial convergence guarantees.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The connection to existing gossip literature is weak, the algorithmic trick isn't sufficiently highlighted, and the lack of comparative analysis and thorough experimental validation raises concerns about practical performance.

**Post-Revision:** After reviewing the author’s response and planned revisions, I raise my score to 8. If the suggested improvements – particularly strengthening the connection to the gossip literature, highlighting the phantom node approach, providing a comparative analysis, and expanding the experiments – are adequately addressed, I recommend acceptance.
