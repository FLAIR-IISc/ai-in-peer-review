## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper tackles the important problem of decentralized U-statistic estimation in networked systems, a less explored area compared to average computation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms, demonstrably improving upon the existing state-of-the-art U2-Gossip algorithm in terms of convergence rate, memory footprint, and communication cost. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and positioned within the existing literature on gossip algorithms and distributed estimation.

The core contribution lies in the clever design of these algorithms, which simultaneously propagate data and maintain local U-statistic estimates. The introduction of “phantom nodes” for theoretical analysis is a particularly insightful technique, enabling a rigorous convergence analysis resulting in O(1/t) and O(log t/t) rates for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively.  These theoretical guarantees are substantiated by a comprehensive empirical evaluation on two datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter), demonstrating consistent superior performance to U2-Gossip across various network topologies. The comparison highlights the benefits of the proposed averaging step and reduced communication overhead. 

A significant strength is the clarity and thoroughness of the presentation. The paper is logically structured, with clear definitions and explanations of the algorithms and their analysis.  The authors thoughtfully acknowledge limitations and potential extensions, suggesting future research directions. The inclusion of detailed experimental setup and results, including a comparison of convergence speed with varying network sizes, strengthens the validity of the findings.  The paper also correctly identifies and addresses the connection to spectral graph theory and its impact on convergence rates. 

A potential minor weakness is the focus solely on U-statistics of degree two. While this simplifies the analysis, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the challenges and potential adaptations for higher-degree U-statistics. Additionally, while the asynchronous analysis is presented, the experimental validation of the asynchronous algorithm's performance could be more extensive. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Addresses a gap in the literature regarding efficient U-statistic estimation in distributed settings.
*   Theoretical Soundness: Provides rigorous convergence analysis with demonstrable improvements over existing methods.
*   Empirical Validation: Thorough experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical benefits.
*   Clarity and Presentation: The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   Significance: The proposed algorithms have the potential for wide application in decentralized machine learning and data analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope to degree-two U-statistics.
*   Asynchronous results could benefit from more extensive experimental validation.

**Overall:** I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation and gossip algorithms, offering both theoretical advancements and practical improvements over existing methods. It is a well-executed and insightful piece of research that is highly likely to be impactful within the NIPS community.
