

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networked systems, extending gossip algorithms beyond simple averages to handle more complex statistics like AUC, variance, and Gini mean difference. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms that maintain local estimates of U-statistics while propagating data across the network. The key innovation lies in the dual-step approach: data propagation followed by averaging, with the theoretical analysis using a novel "phantom node" reformulation to establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings.

The paper's strengths are significant. The problem is well-motivated and relevant to real-world applications in sensor networks, social media analysis, and distributed machine learning. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with careful proof of convergence rates that explicitly incorporate data-dependent and network-dependent terms, revealing how connectivity (via spectral gap) affects performance. The phantom node approach is elegant and enables clean analysis of the coupled data propagation and averaging steps. The authors also provide a thorough comparison to U2-gossip [17], demonstrating superior convergence rates and lower memory/communication costs, with the key insight that U2-gossip's √n factor in its convergence bound makes it substantially slower than their approach.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance. The most serious issue is the lack of comparison to baseline methods. The authors claim their algorithms "surpass" U2-gossip, but provide no comparison to simpler alternatives like running a standard gossip algorithm on transformed data or using other distributed optimization approaches for U-statistics. For instance, they could have compared to a naive distributed approach where each node computes its local U-statistic and then gossips these values. Such comparisons would better establish the novelty and practical advantages of their approach.

The novelty claim is also not sufficiently clear. While the paper distinguishes their work from U2-gossip, it doesn't adequately contextualize how their approach differs from other gossip-based methods for related problems. For example, the paper cites [10, 2, 15, 11, 9] for gossip algorithms for separable functions, but doesn't explicitly discuss how their algorithm differs from adapting those methods to U-statistics. The statement that "this problem has only been investigated in [17]" is questionable given the extensive literature on gossip algorithms for distributed optimization.

The experimental evaluation needs strengthening. While the paper compares to U2-gossip on AUC and within-cluster point scatter, it lacks comparison to intuitive baselines that would better demonstrate the value of their approach. For example, they could have included a simple "naive" method where each node computes the entire U-statistic locally (which is computationally expensive) or a method that uses a central coordinator for reference. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient detail on the network topologies used in experiments beyond listing three types (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), making it difficult to assess generalizability.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is strong, but the experimental evaluation is weak due to missing baselines.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with the phantom node concept being particularly well-explained.
- Originality: The core idea has merit, but the novelty is not sufficiently established relative to the broader gossip literature.
- Significance: The problem is important, but the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of comprehensive experimental validation against alternatives.

The authors have made a solid contribution to distributed estimation of U-statistics, but the paper would be significantly improved with additional baseline comparisons and clearer articulation of novelty. The theoretical results are compelling, but without stronger empirical validation against alternative approaches, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the work.

Pro: Strong theoretical analysis, clear problem statement, well-motivated applications, comprehensive comparison to U2-gossip.
Con: Lacks comparison to intuitive baselines, novelty claim is not fully established, experimental evaluation needs strengthening.

The paper addresses an interesting problem with sound theory, but the lack of baseline comparisons and insufficient novelty justification prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. With the suggested revisions, it could be a valuable contribution. As it stands, it's borderline, but the weakness in the experimental evaluation tips the balance toward rejection.
