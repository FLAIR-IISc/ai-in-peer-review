This paper introduces a novel gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing pairwise U-statistics, a class of statistics essential for tasks like calculating variance or AUC. This work extends the well-studied problem of decentralized averaging of separable functions to the more complex setting of pairwise interactions across a network. The proposed synchronous and asynchronous algorithms enable nodes to compute a global U-statistic using only local communication, with theoretical convergence guarantees.

The paper's main strength is its elegant and original solution to this important problem. The key algorithmic insight—having nodes swap a secondary state variable (their initial observation) while simultaneously averaging local estimates—is particularly clever. This mechanism effectively makes the observations perform coupled random walks, allowing every pair of initial data points to interact indirectly over time. This provides a novel and principled way to handle non-separable computations in a gossip framework, representing a significant contribution.

However, the paper could be significantly improved in several areas. The core mechanism of observation-swapping should be highlighted more clearly as the central contribution. The communication model is ambiguous; the authors should clarify the edge distribution induced by the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" model. The theoretical analysis would benefit from explicitly stating assumptions on the initial data distribution (e.g., worst-case or probabilistic) and making the error guarantees a more explicit function of this data. To better situate the O(1/t) convergence rate, a comparison to a memory-intensive flooding baseline where nodes store all received observations would be insightful. The related work is sparse and should be expanded to include foundational reviews (e.g., Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE) and investigate connections to related concepts like lifted Markov chains, which may suggest avenues for acceleration. The experiments, while promising, lack comparisons to relevant baselines. Due to time constraints, I was unable to verify the supplementary proofs. There are also several minor notational and language issues to be addressed.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors address the points above, particularly regarding clarity, theoretical precision, related work, and experimental baselines. These revisions would substantially increase the paper's quality and impact.
