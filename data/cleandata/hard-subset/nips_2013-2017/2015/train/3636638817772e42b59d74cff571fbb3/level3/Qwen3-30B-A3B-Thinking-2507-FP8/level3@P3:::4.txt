

# Review: "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper introduces GoSta algorithms for distributed computation of U-statistics in networked systems. U-statistics, which require averaging over pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, variance, Gini mean difference), present a significant challenge for distributed computation since standard gossip algorithms can only handle separable statistics (averaging over individual observations). The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms that maintain local estimates of U-statistics while simultaneously propagating data across the network. The key innovation lies in their "phantom node" reformulation, which enables them to analyze the convergence of these algorithms. The paper establishes convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings, with explicit dependence on data and network properties.

The paper relates well to prior work on gossip algorithms for distributed averaging (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006, Dimakis et al. 2008), but extends this literature to handle the more complex U-statistic case. It properly cites the related U2-gossip algorithm (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2013) which this work improves upon. The paper also connects to the broader literature on distributed computation of non-separable functions (e.g., Mosk-Aoyama & Shah, 2006).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-executed. The phantom node reformulation is elegant and enables rigorous convergence proofs that establish explicit rate dependencies on network topology (through the spectral gap) and data properties. The proofs are thorough and address both synchronous and asynchronous settings.

2. **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses an important gap in distributed statistics. U-statistics are widely used in machine learning and data analysis (e.g., AUC for classifier evaluation, within-cluster scatter for clustering), yet their distributed computation has been largely overlooked. The authors demonstrate this with compelling examples from real applications.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise, and the theoretical results are presented logically. The connection between the phantom node construction and the actual algorithm execution is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Experimental Evaluation:** The most significant weakness is the lack of meaningful baseline comparisons. The paper only compares to U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2013), which is itself a prior work on the same problem. The authors should have included comparisons to more intuitive baselines, such as:
   - A naive distributed approach where nodes exchange all observations before computing the U-statistic
   - Randomized gossip variants that don't specifically target U-statistics
   - A simple centralized implementation as an upper bound

2. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments only show comparisons to U2-gossip on two specific datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) across three network types. The paper doesn't explore how the algorithms perform on more challenging data distributions, different network topologies, or larger-scale problems.

3. **Practical Considerations:** While the paper mentions communication costs, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the proposed algorithms scale with network size or dimensionality of the data, which would be important for practitioners.

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The theoretical contribution is strong and well-justified, providing meaningful convergence rate bounds with explicit dependencies on problem parameters.
- The problem addressed is significant and fills an important gap in distributed statistics literature.
- The algorithmic approach is elegant and extends the gossip framework to a substantially more challenging class of statistics.

*Con:*
- The lack of meaningful baseline comparisons significantly weakens the empirical validation of the approach.
- The experimental section fails to demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms beyond the narrow comparison to U2-gossip.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the proposed algorithms would perform in real-world scenarios with noisy communication or dynamic network topologies.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on distributed algorithms for statistics computation. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-executed, addressing an important problem that has been overlooked in prior work. However, the lack of meaningful baseline comparisons in the experimental section significantly undermines the paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed approach.

I recommend the paper be accepted with major revisions. The authors should strengthen the experimental evaluation by adding intuitive baselines and conducting more thorough comparisons across a wider range of datasets and network topologies. They should also provide a more detailed analysis of the practical communication and memory costs of their approach relative to alternatives. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
