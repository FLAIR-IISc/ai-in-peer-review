This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in distributed networks. The problem is well-motivated, as U-statistics are crucial for describing global properties of data and arise in various machine learning applications (AUC, variance, etc.). The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing gossip algorithms, which are primarily designed for averaging, and propose a solution based on propagating local observations and averaging local estimates. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, presenting a sound theoretical analysis and demonstrating promising empirical results.

The work builds upon the existing literature on gossip algorithms [19, 18, 5], particularly the work on distributed averaging [2, 10, 15]. It effectively addresses a gap in the field by extending gossip algorithms to handle the more complex computation of U-statistics, which requires averaging over pairs of observations. The connection to previous work on U-statistics [12] is clearly established. The paper also correctly identifies and builds upon the limitations of the prior approach by Pelckmans and Suykens [17], specifically addressing the higher memory and communication costs and lack of asynchronicity. 

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The theoretical analysis is a key contribution, providing convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms. The derived bounds, incorporating data and network dependencies, are insightful and demonstrate the algorithm’s efficiency on well-connected networks. The numerical experiments on two datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) provide strong evidence supporting the superior performance of the proposed algorithms compared to the U2-gossip baseline. The inclusion of different network topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts-Strogatz) further strengthens the empirical evaluation. The introduction of “phantom nodes” for the theoretical analysis is a clever technique to model the data propagation process effectively.

However, there’s a notable weakness regarding the practical utility of the proposed method. While the theoretical convergence rate is favorable, the reviewer requested, and the authors did not provide, per-iteration run-times for both the proposed method and U2-gossip. This information is crucial for assessing the practical benefits of the algorithm. As the reviewer correctly points out, if the iterations of the proposed method are significantly slower than U2-gossip, the overall runtime may be longer, diminishing its practical value despite the faster convergence rate. The experiments demonstrate better *final* accuracy, but don’t show how long it takes to *achieve* that accuracy. This is a critical gap, particularly given the iterative nature of gossip algorithms.

Furthermore, while the paper rigorously compares against U2-gossip, a comparison with simpler baselines like centralized computation (for smaller datasets) or parameter servers might offer valuable context. It’s also worth considering the scalability of the proposed algorithms to even larger networks and datasets, which isn't explicitly addressed. The analysis, while thorough, relies on certain assumptions about the network structure (connected and non-bipartite). A discussion of the algorithm's behavior in more general graph settings would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-supported claims through theoretical analysis and empirical results.
*   Addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods (U2-gossip) in terms of convergence rate and, empirically, final accuracy.
*   Well-written and clearly organized, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.
*   Includes a thorough related work section and appropriately cites relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of per-iteration run-time comparisons hinders assessment of practical utility.
*   Limited comparison with alternative baselines (e.g., centralized computation).
*   Scalability to very large networks and datasets is not explicitly addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the missing run-time analysis, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The theoretical results are solid, and the empirical evaluation is compelling. The clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the related work section further enhance the quality of the paper. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors providing per-iteration run-time comparisons in a revised version. Addressing this concern would significantly strengthen the practical relevance of the work and solidify its contribution to the NIPS community.
