## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper presents novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a challenging problem with applications in various domains like machine learning and data mining. The authors address a gap in the existing literature where gossip algorithms were primarily focused on simpler statistics like the sample mean. They provide theoretical convergence rate analysis – O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings – and demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithms through numerical experiments, showcasing improvements over a previously introduced approach [17].

The problem tackled is indeed important. Distributed estimation is crucial in many modern applications dealing with large-scale decentralized data.  U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are foundational to a wide range of statistical measures (variance, AUC, Gini index, etc.). While distributed estimation of the mean has been well-studied (e.g., [19, 18, 5, 10, 2, 15, 11, 9]), the extension to U-statistics has remained relatively unexplored. The authors appropriately position their work within the context of gossip algorithms and highlight the limitations of existing techniques for handling such data functionals.

The strengths of this paper lie in its solid theoretical foundation and well-designed experimental evaluation.  The convergence analysis, utilizing a clever reformulation of the problem with "phantom nodes", is particularly noteworthy. The convergence rates are clearly stated and presented with explicit data and network-dependent terms, allowing for a nuanced understanding of performance.  The paper's organization is excellent; it systematically introduces the problem, related work, proposed algorithms, theoretical analysis, and experimental results.  The clarity of writing is also commendable, facilitating understanding of the complex concepts involved. The numerical experiments on both AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation, using real datasets, provide convincing evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness. The comparison against the existing work [17] demonstrates a tangible improvement in convergence and efficiency.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is comprehensive, the constant factors hidden within the Big-O notation are not explored. A more detailed examination of these constants could provide further insights into the practical performance of the algorithms. The comparison to U2-gossip is quite thorough, but a wider range of baseline methods, particularly those adapted from other distributed estimation techniques, would strengthen the evaluation. Although the authors mention the possibility of applying adaptive communication schemes [6, 13], they don’t explore this direction in the experiments.  The asynchronous algorithm’s performance is only briefly touched upon and further investigation to validate the practical benefits of the asynchronous approach would be valuable. Finally, while the authors state their results generalize to cases where nodes hold multiple observations, the discussion is rather concise. A more detailed explanation or a brief experiment demonstrating this generalization would enhance the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The paper addresses a largely unexplored problem – distributed estimation of U-statistics – using gossip algorithms.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis is well-developed and provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.
* **Experimental Validation:** The numerical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms compared to existing approaches.
* **Significance:** The algorithms have the potential to be widely used in various applications dealing with decentralized data and U-statistic computations.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison is primarily focused on a single related work [17].
* **Constant Factors:** The theoretical analysis lacks discussion of constant factors within the Big-O notation.
* **Asynchronous Algorithm:** Further exploration of the asynchronous algorithm's performance is needed.
* **Generalization Discussion:**  The extension to multiple observations per node is only briefly mentioned.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The novel algorithms, rigorous theoretical analysis, and compelling experimental results make it a valuable addition to NIPS. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths significantly outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the suggestions regarding broader baseline comparisons, a discussion of constant factors, and expanded validation of the asynchronous algorithm. The authors should also consider elaborating on the generalization to scenarios with multiple observations per node.
