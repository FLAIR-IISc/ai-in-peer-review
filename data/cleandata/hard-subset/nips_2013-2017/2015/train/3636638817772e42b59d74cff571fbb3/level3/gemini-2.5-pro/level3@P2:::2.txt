This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics of degree two in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. While gossip methods for simple averaging are well-studied, computing U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations, is a more challenging and less-explored problem. The authors propose novel synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) algorithms. The core idea is to have each node maintain a local estimate of the U-statistic and an auxiliary data observation. At each step, nodes average their local estimates with neighbors while simultaneously swapping their auxiliary observations, causing the data points to propagate through the network as coupled random walks. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, improving upon the only known prior work in this area [17]. The analysis relies on an interesting "phantom network" construction. Experiments on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation demonstrate the proposed method's superiority over the previous approach [17].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and developing efficient, decentralized algorithms for their computation is a valuable contribution. The proposed method is a novel and elegant extension of gossip algorithms to this setting, clearly improving upon the prior state-of-the-art [17] in terms of convergence rate, memory, and communication costs.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms. The convergence proofs, based on the clever "phantom network" reformulation, establish explicit rates that depend on both data properties and network topology (specifically, the spectral gap). The analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings is a solid technical contribution.

3.  **Algorithmic Design:** The core mechanism of combining estimate averaging with observation swapping is simple and effective. It elegantly solves the problem of propagating pairwise information across the network without requiring nodes to store the entire dataset.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Framing:**
    *   The central algorithmic insight—that the auxiliary observations (Y_k) are effectively performing coupled random walks on the graph—is a powerful idea but is somewhat understated. Highlighting this more explicitly in the introduction and method description would improve the paper's narrative and help readers grasp the intuition behind the algorithm's success.
    *   The communication model assumes an edge is drawn uniformly at random from E at each step. This is a common model for analysis but differs from the more practical asynchronous model where a node wakes up and contacts a random neighbor. Please clarify the precise model and discuss the implications of this choice. For instance, in the node-centric model, the edge sampling distribution is not uniform unless the graph is regular.

2.  **Contextualization and Related Work:**
    *   The paper would be stronger if it better contextualized its O(1/t) convergence rate. Is this rate fast or slow for this problem? A simple comparison to a baseline, such as a flooding algorithm where nodes broadcast their observations and store all received data, would be very useful. While memory-intensive, such a method would converge quickly and provide a valuable reference point for the trade-offs made by your algorithm.
    *   The related work section could be expanded. The current review is quite focused on the direct predecessor [17]. A broader discussion of the gossip literature, such as the survey by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, 2011), would be beneficial.
    *   The data propagation mechanism via swaps seems closely related to "lifting" techniques used to accelerate mixing in Markov chains (e.g., LADA). A discussion of these connections would strengthen the paper's contribution by linking it to another established line of work and could potentially suggest avenues for further speed-ups.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The experiments convincingly show that GoSta outperforms U2-gossip. However, the lack of other baselines (e.g., a simplified flooding approach) makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's absolute performance. Including such a baseline, even if it's not scalable, would provide a more complete empirical picture.

4.  **Theoretical Analysis:**
    *   The theorems provide guarantees on the expected error. It would be helpful to explicitly state any assumptions made about the initial data distribution (e.g., worst-case analysis) and to make the dependence of the error bounds on the initial data `H` and `h` more transparent in the main text.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Section 3, when discussing convergence for non-bipartite graphs, it is worth noting that for bipartite graphs, convergence is typically to a periodic or two-time-scale stationary distribution.
*   Please check for minor typos and grammar throughout the manuscript to improve readability.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses a significant and under-explored problem with a novel and technically sound solution. The proposed algorithms are simple, efficient, and demonstrably better than the previous state-of-the-art. The theoretical analysis is solid and provides valuable insights.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The work could be better contextualized within the broader literature on gossip algorithms and Markov chain mixing. The experimental evaluation, while positive, would be more compelling with additional baselines.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed computation. The weaknesses identified are largely related to positioning and context rather than fundamental flaws in the method or its analysis. These are addressable in a revision. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the comparison to baselines and the discussion of related work on lifting.
