This paper presents new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are crucial for describing global statistical properties, yet their decentralized computation has received limited attention compared to sample mean estimation. The authors address this gap by proposing algorithms that simultaneously propagate data and maintain local estimates, achieving convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively. The paper is well-structured, clearly explains the proposed methods, and provides a comprehensive analysis supported by numerical experiments.

The work builds upon the existing literature on gossip algorithms, particularly extending the capabilities beyond simple averaging, a limitation of prior approaches [19, 18, 5, 2, 15, 11, 9].  The authors correctly identify the challenges in applying standard gossip protocols to U-statistics, as highlighted by the work of Pelckmans and Suykens [17], which their approach significantly improves upon. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, outlining its importance in various applications such as AUC calculation, empirical variance estimation, and clustering analysis. The paper appropriately references related work, including foundational texts on spectral graph theory [3] and U-statistics [12].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed algorithms extend the applicability of gossip algorithms to a broader class of distributed estimation problems – those involving U-statistics. This is a significant contribution as it tackles a previously under-explored area.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide rigorous convergence rate analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The bounds are explicit and incorporate dependencies on both data characteristics and network topology, providing valuable insights into the algorithm's performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the algorithms are described in a concise and understandable manner. The introduction of “phantom nodes” is a clever technique for simplifying the analysis and is explained effectively.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. The comparisons to U2-gossip [17] highlight the advantages of the GoSta algorithms in terms of both convergence speed and variance reduction. The use of real datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) adds to the practical relevance of the work.
* **Practical Relevance:** The proposed algorithms have potential applications in diverse fields such as sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and distributed machine learning. The ability to efficiently compute U-statistics in a decentralized manner is valuable for analyzing large-scale datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The paper assumes an undirected graph and uniform data distribution across nodes.  While reasonable for many scenarios, the performance under more complex network topologies or non-uniform data distributions could be explored in future work.
* **Asynchronous Complexity:** While the asynchronous algorithm is presented, the theoretical convergence rate (O(log t / t)) is less favorable than the synchronous rate (O(1/t)). The practical trade-offs between synchronization overhead and convergence speed are not thoroughly discussed.
* **Network Model:** The assumption of known network degree (pk calculation in the asynchronous setting) may not always hold in dynamic network environments. Addressing this limitation would enhance the practical applicability.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While comparisons to U2-gossip are provided, a more comprehensive comparison to other relevant distributed estimation algorithms (even if not specifically designed for U-statistics) would further strengthen the results.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by extending gossip algorithms to U-statistic estimation.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the algorithm's convergence behavior.
* The numerical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
* The work has the potential to impact various fields dealing with decentralized data analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The assumptions made regarding the network topology and data distribution might limit the applicability of the algorithms in certain scenarios.
* The asynchronous convergence rate is less favorable than the synchronous rate, and the trade-offs are not fully explored.
* The comparison to other relevant distributed estimation algorithms is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some limitations exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.
