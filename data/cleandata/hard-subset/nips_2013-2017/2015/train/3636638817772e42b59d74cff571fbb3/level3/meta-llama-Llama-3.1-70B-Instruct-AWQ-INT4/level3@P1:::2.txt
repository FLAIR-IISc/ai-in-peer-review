This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, to efficiently compute U-statistics in a decentralized setting. U-statistics are essential in describing global properties of a statistical population, and they have numerous applications in machine learning and data mining.

The authors provide a thorough background on gossip algorithms, which have been extensively studied in the context of decentralized averaging in networks. However, existing gossip algorithms cannot be used to efficiently compute U-statistics as they depend on pairs of observations. The authors highlight the limitations of the previously introduced U2-gossip algorithm, which achieves O(1/t) convergence rate but has several drawbacks, including high memory and communication costs.

The proposed algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, are designed to address these limitations. They combine two steps at each iteration: a data propagation step to allow each node to estimate the U-statistic, and an averaging step to ensure convergence to the desired value. The key algorithmic trick is that nodes swap a secondary state (initial observation) during averaging, so observations perform coupled random walks.

The authors provide a detailed analysis of the convergence rates of the proposed algorithms. They establish that the local estimates generated by GoSta-sync converge to the U-statistic at a rate O(1/t), while those generated by GoSta-async converge at a rate O(log t/t). The analysis relies on an original reformulation of the problem using "phantom nodes," which allows the authors to model the data propagation and the averaging steps separately.

The paper also presents numerical experiments on two applications: the decentralized estimation of the Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) and the within-cluster point scatter. The results show that GoSta-sync outperforms U2-gossip on every network, both in terms of average error and variance. The performance gap between the two algorithms increases early on, presumably because the exponential term in the convergence bound of GoSta-sync is significant in the first steps.

The authors conclude that their algorithms are faster, more efficient, and more robust than existing approaches, making them suitable for decentralized estimation in networks. They also highlight the potential of their contribution as a building block for decentralized optimization of U-statistics.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes new algorithms for computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting, which is an important problem in machine learning and data mining.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of the proposed algorithms, which is essential for understanding their performance.
* The numerical experiments demonstrate the practical interest of the proposed algorithms and their superiority over existing approaches.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from stronger connections to existing gossip literature and related ideas.
* The induced edge-distribution from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" model could be clarified.
* A comparison to a flooding/memory-heavy alternative (nodes store received tagged observations) could be useful to show tradeoffs and whether the 1/t convergence rate is good or bad.
* The related work section could be expanded to include other gossip approaches omitted from the current references.
* The theorems/analysis could state assumptions on the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and make error guarantees explicit as a function of initial data.
