

# Review for "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on distributed computation by extending gossip algorithms to estimate U-statistics, which are statistics that depend on pairs of observations (e.g., Area Under the Curve, empirical variance, Gini mean difference). While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for distributed averaging of individual observations, they cannot efficiently compute U-statistics that require pairwise interactions. The authors propose two novel randomized gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous), which simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of distributed optimization and estimation, extending the applicability of gossip algorithms to a wider class of statistics.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement in Section 2.2 is precise, and the authors provide an excellent motivation for why U-statistics are important in machine learning applications. The related work in Section 3 properly contextualizes the paper within the gossip algorithm literature, particularly highlighting the limitations of the recent U2-gossip approach [17]. The technical development in Section 4 is rigorous, with the "phantom nodes" approach providing a novel and elegant way to analyze the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms. The theoretical results are sound, with clear convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, featuring explicit data- and network-dependent terms. The experiments in Section 5 provide strong empirical validation, demonstrating that the proposed algorithms consistently outperform U2-gossip across multiple network topologies and real-world datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation).

The paper excels in quality, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive experiments. The convergence bounds explicitly account for network connectivity through the spectral gap of the graph Laplacian, which is a significant improvement over previous work. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly the additional memory requirements compared to simpler gossip algorithms (though they argue this is justified by the increased functionality).

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem is well-defined, the algorithms are clearly presented with pseudocode, and the technical developments are logically structured. The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the figures effectively illustrate the experimental results.

The originality of this work is strong. While gossip algorithms for distributed averaging have been studied extensively, the extension to U-statistics represents a meaningful advance. The phantom nodes approach for analysis is novel and provides a clean way to separate the data propagation and averaging components. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work, particularly U2-gossip [17], by demonstrating superior convergence rates, lower memory requirements, and the ability to operate in asynchronous settings.

The significance of this work is substantial. U-statistics are widely used in machine learning and data analysis, and the ability to compute them efficiently in decentralized networks has important practical implications for sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale distributed learning. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over the previous state-of-the-art, with faster convergence and lower variance across nodes. The authors also correctly identify potential extensions to adaptive communication schemes and decentralized optimization of U-statistics.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a meaningful gap in the literature on distributed computation
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence rates
- Introduces a novel "phantom nodes" approach for analysis
- Demonstrates practical advantages through comprehensive experiments
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Extends the applicability of gossip algorithms to a wider class of statistics

Arguments against acceptance:
- The additional memory requirements for the auxiliary observations (though justified by the increased functionality) may be a concern in extremely resource-constrained networks
- The theoretical convergence rate for the asynchronous case (O(log t/t)) is slightly slower than some recent gossip algorithms for simpler statistics, though the experimental results show practical competitiveness

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed computation. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical implications are clear. The paper is well-written and properly situates itself within the existing literature. While the additional memory requirements for the auxiliary observations may be a minor concern in some resource-constrained scenarios, the authors' analysis of this trade-off is thorough and justified. The work represents a meaningful extension of gossip algorithms that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on distributed machine learning and optimization.

The paper meets all four key criteria: it is of high quality with rigorous analysis, exceptionally clear in its presentation, highly original in its approach to extending gossip algorithms, and of significant importance to the field of distributed learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
