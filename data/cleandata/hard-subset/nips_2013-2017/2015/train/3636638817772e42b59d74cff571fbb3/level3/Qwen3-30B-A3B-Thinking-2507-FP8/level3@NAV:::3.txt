

This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of U-statistics, addressing a gap in existing gossip-based methods that primarily handle mean statistics. The authors introduce "GoSta" algorithms that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local U-statistic estimates, with convergence bounds of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings. The work builds on prior gossip literature [2, 5, 18, 19] but extends it to handle pairwise data dependencies, which U2-gossip [17] previously addressed with higher communication and memory costs.

Strengths: The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, with strong theoretical contributions including explicit convergence bounds that incorporate data and network-dependent terms. Empirical results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter across three network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) consistently demonstrate superior performance over U2-gossip, with lower error rates and reduced variance. The problem is significant as U-statistics underpin key applications like AUC estimation and clustering quality measurement.

Weaknesses: While the theoretical and empirical results are compelling, the paper lacks per-iteration runtime comparisons between GoSta and U2-gossip. This is a critical omission because if GoSta's iterations are substantially slower due to additional computation required for the two-step update (data propagation + averaging), its practical utility could be limited despite better convergence rates. The authors should address this concern in the revision.

Quality: High (solid theory and experiments), but missing runtime analysis weakens practical assessment.  
Clarity: Excellent (well-organized with clear algorithms and proofs).  
Originality: Strong (novel algorithms for U-statistics in gossip frameworks).  
Significance: High (addresses important distributed estimation problem with real-world applications).

Pro: Novel approach, strong theoretical guarantees, compelling empirical results across diverse networks.  
Con: Missing runtime comparison undermines practical utility assessment.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-executed contribution that advances distributed computation for U-statistics. With the runtime clarification, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
