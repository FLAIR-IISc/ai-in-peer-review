

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics in networked systems. The work extends gossip algorithms, which have been extensively studied for estimating separable statistics like sample means, to handle pairwise U-statistics such as AUC, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter. The key innovation lies in maintaining auxiliary observations that perform coupled random walks across the network while simultaneously averaging local estimates. The authors establish O(1/t) convergence for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings, with explicit dependencies on data and network properties.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on distributed computation in networks. It builds upon seminal work by Boyd et al. (2006) on randomized gossip algorithms and extends the framework to handle non-separable statistics. However, the connection to related work could be strengthened: the authors should cite Dimakis et al.'s "Gossip Algorithms for Distributed Signal Processing" (Proc. IEEE, 2010) which provides a comprehensive overview of gossip methods, and reference the more recent "Geographic Gossip" approach (Dimakis et al., 2008) that addresses similar communication constraints. The discussion of "phantom nodes" for convergence analysis bears resemblance to lifting techniques used in Markov chain Monte Carlo and could be explicitly connected to works like "Lifting Markov Chains" by Diaconis et al.

The algorithmic insight is well-motivated: by having nodes swap auxiliary observations during averaging steps, the method enables distributed propagation of pairwise data while maintaining local estimates. This is a clever solution to the challenge of computing statistics depending on pairs of observations. The authors should emphasize this key insight more prominently in the main text rather than burying it in the algorithm description. However, the paper's description of the communication model is ambiguous: "drawing a random edge" is unclear in the context of asynchronous settings where nodes wake up independently. The authors should clarify whether this corresponds to selecting a random edge uniformly at random or selecting a random node that then selects a random neighbor.

The convergence analysis is theoretically sound but could be strengthened. Theorems 1 and 2 should explicitly state assumptions about the data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic) and make error guarantees more transparent as functions of initial data. The authors claim "explicit data and network dependent terms" in their convergence rates, but these terms are not sufficiently unpacked for the reader to understand their practical implications. The comparison to U2-gossip is valuable, but the paper would benefit from a direct comparison to a flooding-based baseline that stores all received observations with tags, to better demonstrate the memory and communication advantages.

The experimental section provides reasonable evidence of the algorithm's superiority for AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation. However, the comparison is limited to U2-gossip, and the paper should include additional baselines to more convincingly demonstrate the empirical advantages. The figure showing performance scaling with network size (Figure 3a) is particularly compelling and supports the theoretical claims.

The paper has several minor issues that should be addressed: the definition of "bipartite" in Section 2.1 contains a typo (it should be "bipartite" not "bipartite"), the notation for the graph Laplacian could be more consistent, and the reference to "Theorem 1" in Section 4.1 should be "Theorem 1" for consistency with the main text.

Strengths:
- Novel extension of gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, addressing an important gap in distributed estimation
- Clear theoretical convergence rates with meaningful network and data dependencies
- Practical algorithms with demonstrable empirical advantages over the only prior approach (U2-gossip)
- Well-structured experimental validation on real-world datasets

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient connection to existing gossip literature and related work
- Ambiguous communication model description
- Limited baseline comparisons in experiments
- Convergence rate analysis could better clarify assumptions and practical implications
- Minor notation and language issues

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, but the current version would benefit from the suggested revisions. The theoretical analysis is sound, the algorithms are well-motivated, and the experiments support the claims. The main issues are presentation-related rather than substantive.

Pro: Novel problem extension, strong theoretical foundation, clear empirical validation
Con: Insufficient literature connections, ambiguous communication model, limited baselines

After addressing these points, the paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS. The work advances distributed computation for networked systems by solving a meaningful problem that has been largely overlooked in the gossip literature. The algorithms have clear practical advantages in memory and communication efficiency over the only prior approach, and the theoretical analysis provides meaningful insights into the convergence properties. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
