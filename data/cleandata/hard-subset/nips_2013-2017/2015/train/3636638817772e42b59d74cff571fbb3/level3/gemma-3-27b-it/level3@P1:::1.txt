## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the efficient and decentralized computation of U-statistics, a class of data functionals crucial in various machine learning applications. While distributed estimation of means has been extensively studied, the computation of U-statistics has received comparatively less attention. The authors effectively address this gap, presenting algorithms with provable convergence guarantees and demonstrating superior performance compared to the existing state-of-the-art, U2-Gossip [17], through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

The core idea revolves around propagating local observations and averaging local estimates iteratively. The authors cleverly reformulate the problem using "phantom nodes" to facilitate the convergence analysis. They establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with bounds explicitly dependent on data and network characteristics. This dependency on the network’s spectral gap highlights the algorithm’s efficiency on well-connected networks.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The problem addressed is important and under-explored. Extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics unlocks possibilities for distributed computation in applications like AUC estimation, variance calculation, and Gini mean difference estimation – areas where decentralized approaches are highly valuable.  The theoretical contribution of establishing convergence rates and identifying the dependency on network properties is significant.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms are well-defined and the convergence analysis appears rigorous. The use of phantom nodes is a clever trick to simplify the analysis and provides a clear path to the theoretical results. The authors carefully consider both synchronous and asynchronous settings, expanding the practical applicability of their approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper includes a comprehensive empirical evaluation on two real-world datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) demonstrating the superiority of the proposed algorithms over U2-Gossip [17]. The experiments cover different network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, and Watts-Strogatz) providing robustness to the findings.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors effectively motivate the problem and clearly present their approach. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2, along with the description of the experimental setup, enhances reproducibility. 
* **Comparison to Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough review of related work in gossip algorithms and clearly distinguish their contribution from existing methods.  The detailed comparison with U2-Gossip highlights the advantages of their approach in terms of convergence rate, memory usage, and communication cost.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The asynchronous convergence rate of O(log t/t) is slightly weaker than the synchronous rate of O(1/t).  While a weaker rate is often expected in asynchronous settings, further analysis or techniques to improve this rate could be valuable.
* **Network Model Simplification:** The assumption of a static network topology might be limiting. In many real-world scenarios, network topologies evolve over time. Investigating the robustness of the algorithms to dynamic network changes would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the comparison to U2-Gossip is crucial, including comparisons to other distributed estimation methods, even if they are not directly applicable to U-statistics, could provide a broader context for evaluating the performance of the proposed algorithms.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and previously under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
* The proposed algorithms are technically sound and offer significant improvements over existing approaches.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior and the impact of network characteristics.
* The empirical evaluation validates the effectiveness of the algorithms in practical scenarios.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the contribution in relation to prior work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The asynchronous convergence rate is slightly weaker than the synchronous rate.
* The network model is simplified and may not fully reflect real-world scenarios.
* The comparison to other distributed estimation methods is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel, technically sound, and empirically validated.  The paper’s strengths clearly outweigh its weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider briefly discussing the limitations of the static network model and potential future work to address dynamic topologies.  A slightly more comprehensive comparison to other relevant distributed estimation methods, even if requiring adaptation, could further strengthen the paper.
