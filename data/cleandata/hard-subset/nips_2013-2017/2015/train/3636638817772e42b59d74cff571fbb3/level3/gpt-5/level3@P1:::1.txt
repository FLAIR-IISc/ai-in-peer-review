This paper tackles the decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network, a gap in the gossip/consensus literature that has largely focused on separable aggregates (sums/means). The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms: a synchronous version (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous version (GoSta-async). Each node keeps a single auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via edge swaps and forms a running local estimate by averaging H(Xi, Y) values over time; local estimates are also averaged via standard pairwise gossip. The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple averaging and data propagation, yielding explicit convergence rates in expectation: O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous). The bounds depend on data dispersion terms and on the network spectral gap through the second eigenvalue of a weighted averaging operator, matching the intuition that better-connected graphs mix faster. The paper refines and improves over the prior U2-gossip method (Pelckmans & Suykens), both theoretically (tighter rates; elimination of a √n factor) and practically (only one auxiliary sample, fewer messages). Empirically, on AUC and within-cluster scatter, across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs, the proposed methods converge faster and with lower across-node variance than U2-gossip.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well grounded in the gossip literature on distributed averaging and acceleration (e.g., Boyd et al., Dimakis et al., Kempe et al.) and connects to U-statistics foundations (Lee; Clémençon). To my knowledge, U2-gossip is the closest prior specifically targeting U-statistics; this submission improves both the algorithmic design (averaging plus single random walk) and the analysis. The asynchronous setting aligns with classic randomized gossip models (Poisson clocks), and the spectral-gap dependence parallels standard consensus results.

Quality: The technical development is sound and carefully executed. The phantom-graph block-matrix analysis gives a clean decomposition: Wα(G) drives averaging, while a Kronecker structure captures propagation. The comparison to U2-gossip is fair and informative, with a refined bound that clarifies when the proposed approach dominates. The asynchronous algorithm’s debiasing via node-specific counters mk(t) is simple and effective. Assumptions (connected, non-bipartite graphs; symmetric H with H(x,x)=0; uniform random edge draws) are standard. That said, the guarantees are in expectation; the paper lacks high-probability or almost-sure convergence and does not quantify the variance across nodes or runs. The empirical section is solid but modest in scale and variety; communication/compute costs versus accuracy are not explicitly profiled.

Clarity: The paper is well written, with clear problem setup and algorithm descriptions. The phantom-nodes intuition is compelling, though a condensed figure-plus-intuition subsection before the formal block-matrix derivation would help non-experts. Notationally, Wα(G) is used consistently, but it would help to restate the relation between 1 − λ2(2) and the Laplacian spectral gap and to explicitly define all constants in the asynchronous bound (c′(G)) in the main text. The asynchronous requirement that nodes know pk = 2dk/|E| (hence |E|) should be highlighted as a modeling assumption and discussed operationally.

Originality: The problem is important and underexplored; the algorithmic idea of combining standard gossip averaging with a single propagating auxiliary observation is simple yet novel in this context. The phantom-graph analysis technique is a nice contribution that may be of independent interest. The asynchronous extension, with unbiased debiasing via local activation counts, is practical and goes beyond prior synchronous-only designs.

Significance: U-statistics (AUC, Gini difference, pairwise scatter, Kendall tau, rank tests) are central in ML and statistics. Providing decentralized estimators with finite-time rates and low per-node memory/communication can enable applications in sensor networks, edge analytics, and privacy-aware distributed systems. The spectral-gap dependence aligns with established theory and should guide system designers. The practical impact would be strengthened by larger-scale experiments, explicit communication–accuracy tradeoffs, and robustness studies.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a meaningful, underexplored problem (decentralized U-statistics) with clear applications.
- Solid theory with explicit spectral-gap–dependent rates; improves over the main prior (U2-gossip).
- Fully asynchronous algorithm with simple, local debiasing.
- Lower memory and communication than U2-gossip; single auxiliary observation per node.
- Empirical results on two tasks and multiple topologies corroborate theory; reduced cross-node variance.

Cons / weaknesses:
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation only; no high-probability bounds or concentration.
- Asynchronous algorithm assumes nodes know |E| (or pk); practicality under partial topology knowledge is not discussed.
- Evaluation scale is moderate (n up to ~1.6k, low d); no tests on highly sparse, large, or time-varying graphs.
- No comparison to approximate U-statistic estimators (e.g., pair subsampling) under equal communication budgets.
- Only degree-2 U-statistics considered; extensions to higher-order U-statistics are not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide high-probability and/or almost-sure convergence via martingale concentration or mixing-time arguments; bound the variance across nodes.
- Remove or relax the need to know |E| by estimating pk online, or discuss practicality when only local degree is known.
- Report communication and computation per node versus error curves; include dimensionality scaling and bandwidth constraints.
- Explore acceleration (Chebyshev-polynomial or over-relaxed gossip) to tighten constants, and robustness to packet loss/quantization.
- Compare against subsampled pairwise estimators under fixed communication budgets.
- Discuss extensions to higher-order U-statistics and to time-varying/directed graphs.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, technically solid, and practically relevant contribution to decentralized statistics and gossip algorithms. I recommend acceptance after minor clarifications and with the above suggestions considered.
