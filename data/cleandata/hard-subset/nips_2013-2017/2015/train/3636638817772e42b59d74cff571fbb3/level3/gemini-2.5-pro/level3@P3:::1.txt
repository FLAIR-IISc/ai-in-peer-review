### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the important but relatively under-explored problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip protocols are well-suited for separable functions like sample means, but they cannot be directly applied to U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance, within-cluster scatter). The authors propose novel synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) algorithms to tackle this challenge. The core idea is to have each node maintain both a local estimate of the U-statistic and an auxiliary data observation. At each step, two communicating nodes average their local estimates while simultaneously swapping their auxiliary observations. This elegant mechanism combines the well-understood principles of gossip averaging for consensus with a random walk-like process for data propagation, allowing each node to eventually "see" all other data points.

This work is well-positioned relative to prior art. It directly improves upon the only existing gossip-based method for this problem, U2-gossip [17]. The authors provide a compelling theoretical and empirical case that their proposed GoSta algorithms are superior, achieving faster convergence rates with significantly lower memory and communication overhead.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments.

*   **Theory:** The authors provide convergence proofs for both the synchronous and asynchronous algorithms. The convergence rates of O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous) are formally established. A key strength of the analysis is that the bounds explicitly characterize the dependency on network topology (via the spectral gap) and data properties (via variance-like terms), providing valuable insight into the algorithm's behavior. The use of a "phantom network" is an original and effective analytical tool for decoupling the data propagation and estimate averaging steps in the proof.
*   **Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough. The algorithms are tested on two relevant U-statistic estimation tasks (AUC and within-cluster scatter) using real-world datasets. The comparison against U2-gossip across three different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) convincingly demonstrates the practical superiority of the proposed methods, corroborating the theoretical findings. The experiments clearly show that GoSta converges faster and results in lower variance across node estimates, especially as the network size increases.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.

*   The problem statement and motivation are laid out clearly in the introduction.
*   The related work section effectively situates the paper, explaining the limitations of existing methods and establishing the novelty of the contribution.
*   The proposed algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2).
*   The theoretical results are stated precisely in Theorems 1 and 2, and the experimental results are presented with informative plots that are easy to interpret.

A minor suggestion for improvement: The transition from the algorithm's description to the "phantom network" analysis in Section 4.1 is quite abrupt. While this is a clever analytical device, a sentence or two explaining *why* this reformulation is necessary (e.g., to cleanly separate the dynamics of estimate averaging from data propagation for the proof) could further enhance readability for readers less familiar with such techniques.

**Originality**

The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on distributed computation and gossip algorithms. While gossip averaging and data propagation via random walks are known concepts, their combination in this specific manner to solve the U-statistic estimation problem is novel. This work is a substantial advancement over the previous state-of-the-art [17]. The proposed approach is not an incremental improvement but a fundamentally different and more efficient algorithm that simultaneously reduces computational, communication, and memory requirements while also being applicable in a more realistic asynchronous setting. The refined convergence analysis for the competing U2-gossip algorithm (Eq. 10) is also a novel contribution in its own right.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a considerable impact.

*   **Practical Importance:** U-statistics are fundamental in statistics and machine learning. This paper provides a practical, efficient, and robust method for their computation in decentralized environments like sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, or large-scale distributed learning settings. The asynchronous nature of GoSta-async makes it particularly relevant for real-world deployment where global synchronization is infeasible.
*   **Advancement of State of the Art:** The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art. GoSta is superior to the existing U2-gossip baseline on every important metric: convergence speed, memory footprint (one auxiliary point vs. two), and communication cost (one data swap per round vs. two).
*   **Future Work:** The ideas presented here provide a strong foundation for future research. As the authors suggest, this work could serve as a key building block for more complex tasks, such as the fully decentralized optimization of machine learning models whose objectives are U-statistics (e.g., optimizing for AUC).

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
*   Proposes novel, efficient synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for the important problem of U-statistic estimation.
*   Achieves theoretically and empirically superior performance compared to the prior state-of-the-art (U2-gossip).
*   Operates with lower memory and communication costs than its predecessor.
*   The asynchronous variant is highly practical for real-world decentralized systems.
*   Provides rigorous convergence analysis with insightful, explicit dependencies on network and data characteristics.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, motivated, and positioned.

**Weaknesses:**
*   (Minor) The theoretical convergence rate for the asynchronous case (O(log t / t)) is slightly worse than the synchronous case (O(1/t)), though this gap is not apparent in the experiments. A brief discussion on this discrepancy could be insightful.
*   (Minor) The constant `c'(G)` in the asynchronous convergence bound (Theorem 2) is not explicitly characterized in the main text, which slightly obscures its dependency on the network topology.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a complete and high-quality piece of work that solves an important problem with an elegant and effective solution. It represents a clear and significant contribution to the field.
