This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the decentralized estimation of U-statistics, a class of data functionals often encountered in machine learning and data mining. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as existing gossip algorithms struggle with computations requiring averaging over pairs of observations. The proposed algorithms, termed GoSta, achieve improved convergence rates compared to the previous state-of-the-art (U2-Gossip) while also offering advantages in terms of memory and communication costs. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and appropriately positioned within the existing literature on gossip algorithms and U-statistics.

The core idea revolves around propagating data across the network and maintaining local estimates of the U-statistic. The synchronous algorithm updates estimates based on running averages and edge-based information exchange, while the asynchronous version adapts to a more realistic setting with nodes operating on local clocks. A clever reformulation of the problem using "phantom nodes" facilitates the convergence analysis, yielding bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with terms dependent on both data characteristics and network topology. Extensive experimental validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation using real datasets corroborates the theoretical findings and demonstrates superior performance compared to U2-Gossip.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper tackles an important and relatively unexplored problem in decentralized computation – efficient estimation of U-statistics. The proposed algorithms represent a significant advancement over existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The use of phantom nodes is a clever technique to facilitate this analysis.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The algorithms are designed with practical considerations in mind, operating effectively in asynchronous settings and reducing memory/communication costs.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The comprehensive experimental evaluation on real-world datasets convincingly demonstrates the superiority of GoSta over U2-Gossip. The inclusion of different network topologies in the experimental setup adds to the robustness of the findings.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistently used, and the explanations are easy to follow. The connection to related work is well established.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to U2-Gossip is thorough, a more extensive comparison to other distributed estimation techniques, even those not specifically designed for U-statistics, might provide further context. For instance, exploring the applicability of techniques from federated learning could be valuable.
*   **Complexity of Analysis:** The proof of Theorem 1, while theoretically sound, relies on a relatively complex analysis involving phantom nodes and spectral graph theory.  While this is arguably necessary, it might make the paper less accessible to researchers unfamiliar with these tools.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The asynchronous algorithm's performance might be sensitive to the choice of the probabilities *pk*. While the paper addresses this by relating them to node degree, a more detailed discussion of practical parameter tuning strategies could be beneficial.
*   **Scalability Discussion:** While the experiments show promising results, a deeper discussion of the scalability of the algorithms to very large networks (e.g., millions of nodes) would be valuable. The memory footprint of the phantom node representation, even if theoretical, could become a bottleneck.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in distributed computation.
*   The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
*   The convergence analysis provides valuable insights into the performance of the algorithms.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and contributes meaningfully to the literature on gossip algorithms and U-statistics.
*   The results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working in distributed systems, machine learning, and data mining.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is complex and may limit the accessibility of the paper.
*   The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited.
*   The scalability of the algorithms to very large networks remains an open question.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed computation. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach, and the empirical validation convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Quality:** Excellent – The paper is technically sound, claims are well-supported, and it represents a complete piece of work.
**Clarity:** Excellent – The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
**Originality:** Excellent – The problem addressed and the proposed approach are novel.
**Significance:** High – The results are important and have the potential to be widely used by researchers and practitioners.
