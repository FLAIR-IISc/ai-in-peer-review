This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics, with applications including AUC and within-cluster scatter. The key idea is to combine (i) propagation of data via random neighbor exchanges and (ii) averaging of local estimates, enabling each node to track a global U-statistic. The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple propagation and averaging, yielding explicit convergence rates: O(1/t) for the synchronous variant and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous one, with constants tied to data dispersion and the graph spectral gap. Empirically, on complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs, the proposed method consistently outperforms the U2-gossip baseline. Results are reported on two datasets, with AUC evaluated via average relative AUC error; performance improves as connectivity increases, aligning with the theory. The work relates well to classic randomized gossip for separable functions (e.g., Boyd et al.) and directly advances prior work on U-statistics gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens).

Quality: The theory appears sound and carefully tied to graph spectra; the comparison to U2-gossip clarifies where averaging helps. Experiments are clean and show consistent gains, though the empirical scope is modest (two datasets) and focuses on relative error rather than absolute accuracy or wall-clock efficiency.

Clarity: The paper is well written, well motivated, and organized; the phantom-node analysis is explained clearly. Some technical details defer to the supplement, which is acceptable.

Originality: Extending gossip to U-statistics with both synchronous and fully asynchronous protocols and deriving explicit rates is a meaningful and novel contribution over U2-gossip.

Significance: U-statistics cover many important measures (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau); the proposed methods are likely to be useful in decentralized ML systems.

Main concern: Practical utility depends on per-iteration costs. In GoSta-sync, every node computes H each iteration; in GoSta-async, only two nodes do. U2-gossip exchanges two auxiliaries per iteration. Without per-iteration run-times and communication/memory budgets, it is hard to assess wall-clock advantages. I strongly recommend adding: (i) per-iteration run-times for GoSta-sync/async and U2-gossip; (ii) bytes communicated and H-evaluations per iteration; (iii) wall-clock time to reach a target relative error on all graphs.

Pros:
- Clear problem motivation; strong, explicit convergence bounds.
- Consistent empirical gains over U2-gossip across graph families.
- Asynchronous algorithm addresses realistic settings.

Cons:
- No per-iteration run-time/communication analysis; wall-clock performance unclear.
- Limited experimental breadth (two datasets) and metrics.
