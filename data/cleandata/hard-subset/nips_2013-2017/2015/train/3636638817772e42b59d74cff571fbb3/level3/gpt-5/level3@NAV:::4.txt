This paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in peer-to-peer networks, proposing synchronous and asynchronous gossip-style algorithms that maintain a per-node auxiliary sample and average local estimates during random pairwise exchanges. The authors prove convergence in expectation with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants that expose data dispersion and the network spectral gap; the “phantom nodes” reformulation is used to separate data propagation from averaging in the analysis. The work targets important functionals (AUC, variance, Gini difference, within-cluster scatter) frequently encountered in ML. It relates naturally to prior NeurIPS work on gossip and decentralized computation/optimization (e.g., Kempe et al. 2003; Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al. 2010; Duchi et al. 2012) and to U-statistics/U-processes in learning (Clémençon 2008). The main comparator is U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens 2010).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound; convergence proofs and the dependence on spectral gap are clearly articulated. However, the empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to simple and informative baselines, making it difficult to assess practical gains. Suggested baselines include: (i) randomized subsampling of pairs (Monte Carlo U-statistic estimators); (ii) naive data-shuffling with push-sum/averaging of per-node estimates of hi via short random walks; (iii) reservoir/sketch-based pair sampling; (iv) centralized/MapReduce “oracle” (for calibration); and (v) trivial local baselines (no propagation). Report communication, memory, and wall-clock under equal budgets.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written. It would help to (a) make the per-iteration computation and communication costs explicit; (b) clarify assumptions (connected, non-bipartite graphs) and discuss directed/time-varying cases (e.g., push-sum compatibility); (c) better motivate the asynchronous weighting pk and provide intuition for its unbiasedness.

Originality: The algorithmic idea is intuitive and builds on standard gossip/averaging with coupled random walks; novelty relative to existing randomized gossip and push-sum paradigms is modest, though the phantom-node analysis is a nice contribution.

Significance: The problem is timely and relevant; if validated empirically against reasonable baselines, the approach could be useful to practitioners.

Pros:
- Addresses an important and underexplored distributed estimation problem.
- Simple algorithm; comprehensive and clear theory with spectral-gap dependence.
- Asynchronous variant is practical.

Cons:
- Novelty over prior gossip frameworks is unclear.
- No comparisons to intuitive/randomized baselines; limited empirical rigor and metrics.
- Scope limited to degree-2 U-statistics and undirected, non-bipartite graphs.

Overall: Promising theory and interesting problem; acceptance would require a strengthened experimental section with baseline comparisons and budgeted communication analyses.
