## Review of “Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics”

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics, a class of statistical functionals frequently encountered in machine learning that are challenging for traditional gossip algorithms. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for this task, providing theoretical convergence rate bounds and empirical validation through experiments. The motivation is clear; efficiently computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting has applications in areas like variance estimation, AUC calculation, and clustering analysis. This work builds on the extensive literature surrounding gossip algorithms (e.g., [19, 5, 2]) by extending their applicability beyond simple averages.

The proposed algorithms are intuitively designed, leveraging both data propagation and local estimate averaging. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing convergence guarantees for both synchronous (O(1/t)) and asynchronous (O(log t/t)) settings, with bounds dependent on both data characteristics and network topology. The use of "phantom nodes" for analysis is a clever technique. The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter provide preliminary evidence of the algorithms' effectiveness.

However, the most significant weakness of this paper is the complete lack of comparison to any baseline methods. While the authors claim to surpass a previously introduced approach ([17]), they do not present a comparative analysis within the paper itself. It’s crucial to demonstrate performance gains *relative* to existing methods, even simple ones like naive averaging or parallel computation without gossip. Including intuitive or randomized baselines would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation. Additionally, the experimental section could be improved by exploring a wider range of network topologies and dataset sizes. The effect of network connectivity (as predicted by the theory) isn't fully demonstrated. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an interesting and relevant problem.
*   Proposes an intuitive and potentially useful algorithm.
*   Provides comprehensive theoretical analysis with insightful bounds.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks comparison to baseline methods, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of the results.
*   Experimental evaluation could be more thorough, with broader network and dataset exploration.



**Overall:** The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution but is hampered by a lack of empirical comparison. Addressing the baseline comparison and strengthening the experimental evaluation would significantly improve the paper’s quality and justify acceptance. I recommend **rejection** in its current form, but encourage resubmission after incorporating these suggestions.
