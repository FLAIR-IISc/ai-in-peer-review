Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style protocols. Unlike classic gossip, which computes averages of values separable across nodes, U-statistics depend on pairs of observations and include important functionals such as AUC, variance, Gini mean difference, Kendall tau, and within-cluster scatter. The authors propose two algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node maintains a local auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via pairwise swaps, while local estimates are averaged across communicating nodes. The analysis uses an elegant “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from averaging. The authors establish convergence in expectation to the desired U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with explicit dependence on data dispersion and network spectral gap.

The work is positioned against classic randomized gossip for means (Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Tsitsiklis) and the closest prior method for U-statistics, U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens). Compared to U2-gossip, GoSta requires only one auxiliary observation per node, one edge activation per iteration, and achieves stronger bounds (removing a √n factor and providing more favorable network dependence), while also supporting a realistic asynchronous setting. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across three network topologies empirically validate improved convergence and lower across-node variance.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with strong motivation; U-statistics are widely relevant yet underexplored for decentralized settings.
- Novel algorithmic design that couples random-walk-based data propagation with consensus-style averaging; asynchronous variant addresses realistic deployment.
- Solid theoretical analysis leveraging a clean block-triangular state evolution on a phantom network, yielding rates with interpretable data- and graph-dependent constants.
- Improved communication and memory costs over U2-gossip, with careful comparison and refined rate analysis for the baseline.
- Empirical results on two tasks and multiple topologies consistently show better accuracy and stability; trends match theory (spectral gap effects).

Weaknesses
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation only; no high-probability or almost-sure rates, which are common in gossip literature.
- Asynchronous bound is O(log t/t) with an unspecified c′(G); the looseness of constants and gap to synchronous O(1/t) is not explored empirically beyond a single plot.
- Experimental scope is modest: two datasets, moderate n, and no stress tests on higher dimensions d, lossy links, delays, or directed/asymmetric communication.
- Assumes nodes know their degree and total number of edges to compute pk and the per-node counter normalization; this can be unrealistic without an initial discovery phase.
- Uniform random edge activation is assumed; no results for Metropolis weights, non-uniform or time-varying activation, or push-sum variants for directed graphs.
- Only degree-2 U-statistics are considered; discussion of extension to higher orders is brief.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The state evolution and spectral analysis appear correct, and the comparison to U2-gossip is carefully derived. The main results are convergence in expectation with explicit network terms. The asynchronous analysis is less tight (unspecified constant, weaker rate), and a high-probability analysis would strengthen the contribution. Experiments are well executed but could be broader.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The algorithms are straightforward, and the phantom network device makes the analysis accessible. Minor issues: some typos (e.g., “1 Poisson process”), occasional missing reference details, and footnote placement. A small table summarizing per-iteration memory/communication for all methods would help.

Originality
Computing U-statistics via gossip with a combined propagate-and-average mechanism and the phantom-node proof technique is novel relative to prior work. The asynchronous algorithm and refined comparison to U2-gossip add new insights.

Significance
Decentralized estimation of pairwise functionals is important in sensor networks and distributed ML. The improved rates and reduced costs over U2-gossip make this a meaningful advance. With broader experimental validation and strengthened probabilistic guarantees, this could become a standard approach.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide high-probability bounds using martingale concentration and spectral gap arguments?
- How sensitive are results to non-uniform edge activation (e.g., Metropolis weights) and to time-varying/erroneous degree estimates for pk?
- Can the asynchronous rate be sharpened and constants made explicit?
- Please report communication bits per iteration and total to reach a target accuracy, and scaling with d.
- Any robustness to message loss/delays? Could push-sum enable directed/asymmetric communication?
- Briefly discuss extension to higher-order U-statistics and whether multiple auxiliary observations or stratified propagation would help.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with practical relevance.
- Conceptually clean algorithms with improved theory and practice over prior art.
- Realistic asynchronous model and lower resource usage.
- Clear writing and analysis with interpretable dependence on spectral gap.

Cons for acceptance
- Only expectation guarantees; asynchronous analysis less tight.
- Experimental scope limited; no communication-efficiency study or robustness tests.
- Some modeling assumptions (global |E| knowledge, uniform edge sampling) may be strong in practice.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a solid and novel contribution with improved theory and convincing empirical trends; tightening the asynchronous analysis and broadening experiments would make it a clear accept.

Reviewer confidence
High in the assessment of the algorithmic novelty and relation to prior gossip work; moderate on the tightness of the asynchronous bound due to omitted proof details.
