This paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. The authors propose two algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, that interleave (i) propagation of observations via swapping an auxiliary datum along random edges with (ii) averaging of local estimates. A key idea is that each node k maintains a secondary state Yk (initialized to Xk) and updates Zk with a running average of H(Xk, Yk); swapping Y’s makes observations perform coupled random walks, enabling each node to approximate hk = (1/n)∑j H(Xk, Xj). They provide convergence in expectation with explicit data- and graph-dependent bounds: O(1/t) for synchronous and O((log t)/t) for asynchronous updates, where the rates depend on the spectral gap through λ2(Wα(G)). The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple estimation and propagation. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs show empirical improvements over U2-gossip in error and across-node variance, with reduced memory/communication per iteration.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to classical gossip/consensus (Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al. 2010) and separable-function aggregation (Mosk-Aoyama & Shah 2008), and directly improves upon U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens). The lifting/phantom construction is reminiscent of lifted chains and location-aided/accelerated consensus ideas (e.g., geographic gossip; lifted Markov chains/LADA) and could be positioned more explicitly in that literature.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The linear-time-varying system analysis via phantom networks is elegant and appears correct, yielding clear constants with spectral-gap dependence. The asynchronous scheme uses local counters mk to debias updates; the pk = 2dk/|E| choice is consistent with uniform-over-edges activation. I could not fully verify the supplemental proofs due to time constraints; high-level steps are plausible.
- Assumptions and guarantees: Convergence is in expectation over algorithmic randomness with fixed data; this should be stated up front. The synchronous bound cleanly separates data terms (∥h − Û1∥, ∥H − h1⊤∥) from a graph term c(G) = 1 − λ2(W2(G)); the asynchronous bound is looser (c′(G)·‖H‖·(log t)/t). A variance or high-probability bound would substantially strengthen the claims.
- Comparison baselines: Beyond U2-gossip, a “flooding/accumulate” baseline that stores tagged observations (or Bloom-filtered sketches) would clarify memory/communication vs. speed trade-offs. It would also be helpful to include a centralized oracle (for normalization) and cost-to-accuracy plots (messages, words, wall-clock) to complement iteration counts.

Clarity:
- Edge selection model: The text alternates between “draw an edge uniformly at random” and the node-activation view. Please make the induced edge distribution explicit and consistent, and clarify the relation between the two models. In the async algorithm, justify pk = 2dk/|E| under uniform-over-edges selection, and state what nodes must know (dk, |E|). If |E| is unknown, discuss how to estimate pk online and the effect on bias.
- Algorithmic emphasis: The coupled random-walk data propagation (via Y-swaps) is the central trick; highlight this in the introduction and discussion.
- Notation/typos: Numerous minor fixes would improve readability. Examples: “nodes need not be aware” (not “need not to”), “rate-1 Poisson process,” “non-bipartite” consistently hyphenated, “performance” (typo), “d ≥ 1,” ensure consistent use of Wα(G) and λ2(α), and explicitly state that expectations are over the edge-selection randomness. Clarify H(X, X) = 0 assumption and when it is needed.

Originality:
- The problem—distributed estimation of U-statistics—is important and underexplored relative to separable aggregates. The combination of single-auxiliary swapping with local averaging, and the phantom-node analysis, is a neat and practically meaningful refinement over U2-gossip. While related in spirit to lifted chains and location-aided schemes, the application to U-statistics with explicit bounds appears novel.

Significance:
- Many global metrics in ML (AUC, Kendall’s tau, Gini, scatter) are U-statistics; enabling them in decentralized settings is impactful. The explicit dependence on spectral gap makes the insights actionable for network design. The lower memory/communication footprint (one auxiliary vector, one edge activation) versus U2-gossip is persuasive. The empirical section is solid but could be more comprehensive: include (i) asynchronous results across graph families, (ii) scaling with d and H-evaluation cost, (iii) communication-normalized accuracy, and (iv) sensitivity to spectral gap (e.g., vary Watts–Strogatz rewiring).

Constructive suggestions:
- Related work: Expand to include accelerated consensus (Chebyshev/Polynomial acceleration), push-sum for directed graphs, geographic gossip/location-aided averaging, and lifted Markov chains (e.g., LADA-style liftings) that might accelerate the propagation component.
- Tightness/acceleration: Discuss whether polynomial acceleration on Wα(G) (Chebyshev) or non-backtracking/lifted walks could improve constants or yield o(1/t) in practice. Can Polyak–Ruppert averaging reduce constants?
- Assumptions and bounds: State clearly whether bounds are worst-case over initial data or depend on norms of H/h as in Theorem 1; consider providing concentration bounds. In async, specify c′(G) and how it scales with spectral gap and degree heterogeneity.
- Practicalities: The mk counters require knowledge of |E|; discuss distributed estimation of |E| or alternatives (per-node Robbins–Monro steps) and robustness to pk mis-specification.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with clear motivation.
- Simple algorithms with provable convergence and explicit graph/data dependence.
- Lower memory/communication and better empirics than prior U2-gossip.
- Analysis technique (phantom nodes) is insightful and could be reused.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited baselines; no communication-normalized comparisons.
- Expectation-only guarantees; no variance/concentration bounds.
- Some ambiguity in edge-selection modeling and required global knowledge (|E|).
- Related work on lifted/accelerated consensus could be better integrated.

Minor editorial fixes (non-exhaustive): “non-bipartite” (hyphen), “rate-1 Poisson,” “need not be aware,” unify notation for vectors (1n vs 1), define norms used in bounds, fix typos (“perfomance”), and ensure consistent assumptions on H and graph class.

Overall recommendation: Accept (8). The contribution is technically sound, practically relevant, and improves the state of the art; addressing the clarity and experimental breadth issues above will further strengthen the paper.
