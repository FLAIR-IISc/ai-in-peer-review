Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in networks, a problem that cannot be addressed efficiently by classical gossip averaging because the target functional couples pairs of observations. The authors introduce two randomized gossip algorithms: (i) a synchronous variant (GoSta-sync) that alternates per-node running-average updates with pairwise averaging and auxiliary-observation swaps; and (ii) a fully asynchronous variant (GoSta-async) that updates only the activated pair, using per-node counters to maintain an unbiased running average. A key analytical device is a “phantom node” construction that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging, enabling a clean linear-operator analysis. They prove convergence in expectation with explicit, data- and network-dependent constants: O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t / t) for asynchronous. The rates depend on the spectral gap of the network graph and on dispersion terms in H. Empirically, on AUC and within-cluster scatter across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs, the proposed methods converge faster and with lower cross-node variance than the prior U2-gossip approach of Pelckmans & Suykens (IFAC 2009).

This work builds on and extends a rich line of research on gossip and distributed averaging (e.g., Tsitsiklis; Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Dimakis et al.; Nedic & Ozdaglar; Duchi et al.). Relative to previous work, it targets non-separable pairwise functionals and improves over U2-gossip by combining data propagation with consensus averaging, yielding better rates and lower memory/communication per iteration.

Quality
- Theory: The analysis appears sound. The GoSta-sync rate O(1/t) with constants depending on 1 − λ2(W2) (i.e., the spectral gap) and problem-dependent terms is carefully derived via a block-triangular transition matrix and phantom graph argument. For GoSta-async, the O(log t / t) expectation bound is plausible given partial updates and unbiased local counters. The refined comparison bound for U2-gossip (scaling as √n/t times spectral-gap factors) is insightful and supports the empirical findings.
- Assumptions and scope: Graphs are undirected, connected, and non-bipartite; H is symmetric with H(X, X) = 0; convergence is in expectation. The asynchronous method requires nodes to know their degree and |E| to compute pk, which is reasonable but should be discussed explicitly as a requirement. Extensions to multiple observations per node are sketched.
- Experiments: Real datasets (AUC, cluster scatter) on three network families; 50 runs; error trajectories and scaling with n are shown. Results consistently favor the proposed methods. It would be helpful to also report communication complexity to ε-accuracy (messages or words sent) and wall-clock proxies, especially as d grows, to substantiate “lower cost” claims.
- Limitations: No high-probability or almost-sure rates; asynchronous constants are coarse and depend on ||H||; no study of directed/time-varying graphs or robustness to delays/failures; comparisons limited to U2-gossip and baselines in the supplement.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and clearly written. Algorithms are easy to follow; the phantom network device is explained cleanly as an analysis tool rather than an implementation requirement. Connections between spectral gap and convergence are made explicit and match the empirical section.
- Minor clarity issues/suggestions:
  - Clarify the relation between 1 − λ2(W2) and Laplacian eigenvalues; the statement “1 − λ2(2) = βn−1/|E|” may conflict with standard notation where the spectral gap is typically β2. A brief derivation or pointer would help.
  - State more explicitly why using 1/n^2 with H(X, X) = 0 matches the usual U-statistic definition (n/(n − 1) factor).
  - Summarize the per-iteration communication and memory costs (in words/bits) for GoSta vs U2-gossip in the main text.

Originality
- The problem setting—distributed estimation of U-statistics via gossip—is relatively underexplored. The simultaneous “propagate-and-average” design and the phantom-node analysis are novel in this context. The asynchronous variant with unbiased local counters is a practical and nontrivial extension. Relative to U2-gossip, the approach is a meaningful advance rather than a minor tweak.

Significance
- U-statistics underpin widely used metrics (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter). Efficient decentralized estimation is relevant to sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale graph analytics. The theoretical rates with explicit network/data dependence and the demonstrated empirical gains make this a useful contribution that others are likely to build on (e.g., towards decentralized optimization of pairwise losses, higher-order U-statistics, or accelerated schemes).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important and under-studied decentralized estimation problem.
- Clear algorithms with both synchronous and asynchronous settings.
- Solid theory with explicit O(1/t) and O(log t / t) rates and insightful comparison to U2-gossip.
- Empirical results on real data and diverse graphs corroborate theory; lower variance across nodes.
- Well written and organized; techniques likely to be useful to researchers.

Cons:
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation only; no high-probability bounds.
- Asynchronous bound is looser and constants depend on ||H||; limited guidance on practical constants.
- Experimental scope could include larger n, higher-dimensional d, communication/wall-clock metrics, and more baselines.
- Requires knowledge of degrees and |E|; no results for directed or time-varying networks, delays, or failures.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide high-probability (e.g., Azuma/Hoeffding-based) bounds or variance bounds across nodes, especially for the asynchronous setting.
- Report communication complexity and memory usage to reach a target ε, and plot error vs messages/bytes rather than only vs iterations.
- Discuss extensions to directed/time-varying graphs (e.g., push-sum or column-stochastic variants) and robustness to delays or packet loss.
- Explore acceleration (Chebyshev/Polya, geographic gossip) to improve spectral-gap dependence.
- Clarify the spectral gap notation and the U-statistic normalization choice.
- Consider additional tasks (e.g., Kendall tau, Gini) and larger networks to stress-test scalability.

Overall assessment
A strong, well-executed paper that advances the state of the art on decentralized estimation of pairwise statistics with both theoretical and empirical support. I lean toward acceptance after minor clarifications and with the above suggestions considered.
