Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. While classical randomized gossip algorithms efficiently compute separable sums/averages (Kempe et al., Boyd et al., Dimakis et al.), they do not directly handle pairwise quantities such as AUC, variance, Gini mean difference, Kendall tau, and within-cluster scatter. The closest prior work is U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), which swaps two auxiliary observations per node but converges slowly and incurs higher memory/communication costs. The authors propose two algorithms:
- GoSta-sync: synchronous pairwise gossip that maintains one auxiliary observation per node. Each step averages the two selected nodes’ estimates and swaps their auxiliary observations; each node also performs a running average with H(Xk, Yk).
- GoSta-async: fully asynchronous variant where nodes use per-node activation counters to maintain unbiased running averages under random edge activations.

Key theoretical contributions include convergence in expectation to the global U-statistic with explicit graph- and data-dependent rates: O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous. The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple averaging and data propagation and yields bounds in terms of the spectral gap (via eigenvalues of Wα(G)), clarifying the benefit of well-connected graphs. The authors also refine U2-gossip’s rate, highlighting a detrimental √n factor due to lack of estimate averaging.

Experiments on AUC (svmguide3) and within-cluster scatter (Wine Quality) across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs demonstrate faster convergence and lower across-node variance than U2-gossip; synchronous and asynchronous variants perform similarly in practice.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithms are well-defined and the convergence analysis is careful. The phantom-network reformulation is a neat analytical device; the rate dependencies on both data dispersion terms and spectral gap are informative. The asynchronous construction with mi ← mi + 1/pi yields unbiased 1/ri step sizes (ri = activations), which is appropriate.
- Theorems provide expectation bounds; the synchronous bound includes both O(1/t) and a beneficial exp(−ct) transient. The asynchronous bound O(log t/t) is plausible given partial updates.
- Comparisons to U2-gossip include both theory and experiments; communication/memory advantages are clear (one auxiliary observation and one edge activation per iteration).
- Suggestions to strengthen: provide high-probability (or mean-square) bounds; make constants in c′(G) explicit; discuss robustness to message loss and delays; and include wall-clock and communication-complexity comparisons.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. Algorithms are clearly presented, and the role of Wα(G), spectral gap, and data-dependent terms is explained.
- Minor suggestions: 
  - Summarize the phantom-node proof ideas in the main text with a small illustrative example.
  - Clearly state the norm used in bounds (it appears to be ℓ2); give dimensions for H and h to avoid ambiguity.
  - Briefly discuss how to make the method work on bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy updates/self-loops).
  - Add a concise per-iteration cost table (messages, words, and memory per node) vs U2-gossip.

Originality
- Extending gossip to U-statistics with simultaneous averaging and data propagation, coupled with the phantom-node analysis, is original relative to prior work on separable functions and to U2-gossip. The refined analysis of U2-gossip and practical asynchronous variant are also novel contributions.

Significance
- Many decentralized learning/monitoring tasks require pairwise functionals (AUC, scatter, variance). The methods and rates are likely to be used or adapted in practice. The explicit spectral-gap dependence is useful for network design. The asynchronous algorithm broadens applicability. The work could serve as a building block for decentralized optimization of U-statistics.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: decentralized computation of pairwise statistics.
- Clear, simple algorithms with low memory/communication footprint.
- Solid analysis with explicit graph/data dependence; improved over U2-gossip.
- Empirical validation on realistic graphs and tasks; consistent performance gains; reduced variance due to averaging.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Bounds are in expectation only; high-probability or MSE bounds would be valuable (especially for stopping criteria).
- Asynchronous bound is looser (log t/t) than observed; could you provide tighter analysis under additional mixing assumptions or show finite-time constants?
- Non-bipartite assumption: discuss a lazy variant to cover bipartite graphs; consider directed/time-varying graphs via push-sum.
- Experiments: 
  - Report communication complexity (words sent) and wall-clock per method.
  - Explore degree heterogeneity (e.g., scale-free networks) and non-uniform edge sampling.
  - Include larger graphs and additional U-statistics (e.g., Kendall tau) to test versatility.
- Privacy/robustness: swapping raw data may be problematic in some settings; briefly discuss sketching or privacy-preserving variants.

Questions for the authors
- Can the phantom-node technique extend to higher-order U-statistics (order m > 2)? What is the expected rate/overhead?
- Could mini-batch swapping (multiple auxiliary observations per activation) improve constants without growing memory too much?
- How sensitive is performance to inaccurate pk in the asynchronous method (e.g., partial knowledge of |E|)?
- Can you derive non-asymptotic, graph-dependent constants for c′(G)?

Pros for acceptance
- Original, well-motivated problem with broad relevance.
- Simple, practical algorithms; asynchronous variant is valuable.
- Strong theory with explicit spectral-gap dependence.
- Clear empirical gains over the best available baseline (U2-gossip).

Cons for acceptance
- No high-probability guarantees; asynchronous bound may be conservative.
- Limited exploration of heterogeneous and dynamic networks; no communication/wall-clock reporting.
- Bipartite and directed graphs not handled explicitly (beyond assumptions).

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution that advances decentralized computation of U-statistics with both theoretical and practical impact. With minor clarifications and additional empirical characterization of communication costs and robustness, this would be a strong NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
