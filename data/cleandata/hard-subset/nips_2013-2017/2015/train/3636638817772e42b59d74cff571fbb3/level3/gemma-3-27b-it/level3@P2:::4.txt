This paper introduces new randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for the distributed estimation of U-statistics, a class of data functionals appearing in various machine learning and statistical applications (e.g., AUC, variance). The authors establish convergence rate bounds for both synchronous (O(1/t)) and asynchronous (O(log t/t)) settings, with terms dependent on both data characteristics and network topology. They demonstrate empirically that their algorithms outperform a previously introduced approach [17] on real datasets.

The core idea builds upon existing gossip algorithms for simpler averaging problems but extends them to handle the pair-wise averaging inherent in U-statistic computation. The synchronous algorithm (GoSta-sync) iteratively updates local estimates and exchanges information with neighbors. A clever reformulation using “phantom nodes” allows for a relatively clean convergence analysis. The asynchronous version (GoSta-async) adapts the synchronous algorithm for environments without a global clock.

This work builds on a substantial body of literature concerning gossip algorithms, notably [19, 18, 5, 2, 10, 15, 11, 9]. These earlier works focused primarily on computing averages or sums of separable functions, whereas this paper tackles the more challenging problem of U-statistics. The authors correctly position their work within this context and acknowledge relevant prior contributions. The connection to spectral graph theory [3] through the use of spectral gap bounds is also well-established within the gossip algorithm literature.  The problem of U-statistic estimation itself is rooted in established statistical theory [12].  The most directly related work, [17], is also appropriately discussed and serves as the primary point of comparison.

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The paper presents a solid theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms. The convergence proofs appear rigorous and the derived rates are clearly stated. The use of phantom nodes is a creative approach to facilitate the analysis.
* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Computing U-statistics in a distributed manner is a relevant and challenging problem with applications in several domains.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the algorithm descriptions are concise.
* **Explicit Rate Analysis:** The convergence rates are expressed with explicit dependence on both data and network parameters, providing valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Positive Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithms outperform the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17] in terms of both error and variance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of comparison to more basic or intuitive baseline methods. While comparing to [17] is valuable, it would be beneficial to see how the proposed algorithms perform relative to simpler alternatives. For example, a naive distributed implementation of the U-statistic computation without gossip (e.g., simply exchanging all pairwise differences) could provide a useful benchmark. Randomized averaging schemes would also be helpful to include.
* **Originality - Incremental:**  While the problem setting is interesting, the novelty of the approach feels somewhat incremental.  The core idea of extending gossip algorithms to handle pairwise interactions is a natural extension, and the theoretical results, while solid, largely follow established patterns in this field.  A stronger articulation of the key innovation(s) would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Depth:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvement over [17], could be more thorough. Exploring a wider range of network topologies and datasets would strengthen the empirical validation. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different network parameters (e.g., connectivity, degree distribution) on performance would also be valuable.
* **Asynchronous Rate:** While a rate is provided for the asynchronous case, it's significantly weaker than the synchronous case (log t/t vs 1/t) and the impact of that weaker rate isn’t deeply explored, especially regarding practical implications.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound solution to a relevant problem.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
* The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in gossip algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comparison to basic baseline methods weakens the empirical evaluation.
* The incremental novelty might not fully justify acceptance to a highly selective conference like NIPS.
* The weaker convergence rate in the asynchronous setting warrants further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and technically competent paper. However, the limited baseline comparisons and somewhat incremental novelty raise concerns about its suitability for NIPS. I recommend **weak accept** with a request for the authors to:

1.  **Include comparisons to more basic baseline methods** (e.g., naive distributed U-statistic calculation, randomized averaging).
2.  **Expand the experimental evaluation** by exploring a wider range of datasets and network topologies.
3.  **Provide a more in-depth analysis of the asynchronous algorithm's performance** and the implications of its weaker convergence rate.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of making a substantial contribution to the field.
