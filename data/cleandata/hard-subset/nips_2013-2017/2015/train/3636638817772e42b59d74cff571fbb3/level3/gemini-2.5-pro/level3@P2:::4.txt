This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics of degree two in a decentralized network using gossip-based algorithms. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations, are crucial for many machine learning tasks (e.g., calculating sample variance, AUC, or within-cluster scatter) but cannot be computed by standard gossip algorithms designed for separable functions like the sample mean. The authors propose a new family of algorithms, named GoSta, for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The core idea involves each node maintaining a local estimate of the U-statistic and an auxiliary data observation. In each iteration, a randomly selected pair of connected nodes averages their local estimates and swaps their auxiliary observations. This dual mechanism allows for both the propagation of data points across the network (enabling the computation of pairwise functions) and the averaging of estimates to accelerate convergence.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of their proposed methods. They establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. The analysis, which relies on a clever "phantom network" construction, shows that these rates depend explicitly on the network's connectivity (via its spectral gap) and on data-dependent terms. These results represent a significant improvement over the only prior work on this specific problem, Pelckmans and Suykens (2011) [17], which the authors show has a slower convergence rate and higher memory and communication costs. The theoretical findings are supported by experiments on real datasets for AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation, demonstrating the practical advantages of the proposed approach over [17].

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem. While decentralized averaging is a mature field, the distributed estimation of non-separable statistics like U-statistics is far less explored yet highly relevant for modern large-scale machine learning. The applications discussed, such as AUC and clustering evaluation, are compelling.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical contribution is the main strength of this work. The convergence proofs are comprehensive and provide sharp, interpretable rates. The analysis technique using a "phantom network" is elegant and could potentially be of independent interest. The paper presents a complete piece of work with both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, backed by solid theory.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, the proposed algorithms are intuitive, and the main results are stated precisely. The authors do a good job of positioning their work relative to the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper is the limited scope of its experimental comparison. The proposed algorithms are only compared against the U2-gossip algorithm from [17]. While this is the most direct competitor, the lack of comparison to any other baselines makes it difficult to fully appreciate the practical performance of GoSta. The field would benefit from understanding how GoSta fares against simpler, perhaps more naive, distributed strategies. For instance, a baseline where each node's data packet performs a random walk until all data is collected at each node, or a simple flooding protocol, could provide valuable context on the trade-offs between convergence speed, communication cost, and memory. While the paper mentions additional comparisons in the supplementary material, the main text would be significantly strengthened by including and discussing at least one such baseline.

**Originality:**

The proposed algorithm is an intuitive and effective combination of two standard gossip primitives: pairwise averaging and random swapping (equivalent to a random walk for the data packets). The novelty lies not in the invention of a fundamentally new mechanism, but in the successful application and adaptation of these ideas to the challenging problem of U-statistic estimation, and especially in the rigorous analysis that proves the efficiency of this combination. The work is a clear and significant advance over the previous state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and relatively under-explored problem in distributed machine learning.
*   It provides novel synchronous and asynchronous algorithms that are simple and practical.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous, insightful, and represents a significant technical contribution.
*   The work clearly improves upon the only existing direct competitor in terms of convergence rate, memory, and communication efficiency.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is narrow, comparing only against one other specialized algorithm. This makes it difficult to gauge the algorithm's performance in a broader context of distributed computation strategies.

**Conclusion and Suggestions:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms and distributed computation. The theoretical results are solid and the problem is of high practical relevance. The main weakness is the limited experimental comparison. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to strengthen the experimental section in the final version. Including comparisons to one or two simple, intuitive baselines would provide a more complete picture and make the practical contribution of the paper even clearer. For example, a baseline could involve each node broadcasting its data to neighbors until all data has been propagated throughout the network, followed by local computation. Analyzing the convergence time versus the communication overhead for such an approach would provide a very useful point of reference for GoSta.
