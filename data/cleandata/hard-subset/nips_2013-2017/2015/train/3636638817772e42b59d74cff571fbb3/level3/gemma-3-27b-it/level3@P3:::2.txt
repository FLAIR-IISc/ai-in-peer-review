This paper proposes gossip-style decentralized algorithms for computing pairwise U-statistics across networked nodes, a problem less explored than distributed average estimation despite its importance in various applications. The authors present both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, providing convergence rate bounds and demonstrating empirical improvements over a previously introduced approach [17]. Overall, the contribution is valuable, and the paper addresses an important problem in decentralized computation. However, strengthening connections to existing gossip literature and clarifying certain algorithmic details would improve the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** Computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner is a significant problem with applications in diverse areas like machine learning and data mining.
*   **Algorithmic Innovation:** The core idea of swapping secondary state (initial observation) during averaging, inducing coupled random walks, is clever and addresses the limitations of existing gossip algorithms for U-statistics.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, offering insights into the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms compared to [17].
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Connection to Gossip Literature:** While references to gossip algorithms are provided, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this work builds upon or deviates from existing gossip protocols. Specifically, the authors should more explicitly connect their approach to related ideas like consensus-based averaging and distributed optimization. A citation of Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, 2006) on gossip algorithms for distributed signal processing would be valuable. Other relevant gossip approaches should also be included in the related work.
*   **Algorithm Detail – Edge Distribution:** The description of drawing a random edge in the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” model is ambiguous. It’s crucial to clarify whether each node uniformly selects an *existing* neighbor or randomly *attempts* to connect to any other node (potentially creating temporary edges). The induced edge distribution needs explicit definition.
*   **Comparative Analysis:** A more thorough comparison to a flooding-based approach (where nodes store received tagged observations) is needed. This would help demonstrate the trade-offs between the proposed approach (with its 1/t convergence) and a potentially memory-intensive alternative. Is 1/t a good rate in practice, or are there other algorithms achieving better convergence, albeit with higher memory cost?
*   **Related Work Expansion:** The related work section feels incomplete. Several recent advances in gossip algorithms, especially those dealing with heterogeneous networks or constrained resources, are not mentioned.
*   **Lifting Constructions:** The paper should explore potential connections to lifting constructions like LADA or lifted Markov chains. These techniques have shown promise in accelerating mixing times for similar problems and could potentially be applied to improve the convergence rate of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Assumptions & Error Guarantees:** The theorems and analysis should clearly state the assumptions on the initial data distribution (worst-case vs. probabilistic). Furthermore, error guarantees should be explicitly expressed as a function of the initial data, providing a more practical understanding of the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Supplemental Proofs:** The reviewer was unable to verify the supplemental proofs due to time constraints. Increased clarity and self-containment would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Issues:** A few minor language/notation issues were identified (e.g., clarifications regarding the bipartite directed-graph terminology) and should be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a genuinely important and under-explored problem in distributed computation.
*   The proposed algorithms offer a practical and efficient solution for computing U-statistics in decentralized settings.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the algorithm's convergence behavior.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the empirical effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a more comprehensive connection to existing gossip literature weakens the paper's positioning within the field.
*   The ambiguity surrounding the edge selection process hinders a complete understanding of the algorithm.
*   The absence of a detailed comparison to alternative approaches (like flooding) makes it difficult to assess the trade-offs of the proposed solution.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of decentralized computation. The authors have developed novel algorithms for a challenging problem and provided theoretical and empirical evidence of their effectiveness. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. Given the authors’ willingness to address the reviewer’s comments (as evidenced by the score increase from 7 to 8), I recommend acceptance *conditional on* addressing the points raised in this review. Specifically, a more comprehensive related work section, clarification of the edge distribution, a comparison to flooding, and a discussion of lifting constructions are crucial for acceptance.
