### Paper Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of computing degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. While standard gossip methods excel at computing separable sums or averages (like the sample mean), they are not directly applicable to U-statistics, which involve averages over pairs of data points distributed across the network. The authors propose a new family of algorithms, called GoSta (Gossip for Statistics), in both synchronous and asynchronous variants.

The core idea is to have each node maintain not only a local estimate of the U-statistic but also an "auxiliary observation." In each round, two connected nodes average their local estimates and also *swap* their auxiliary observations. This swap mechanism induces a coupled random walk of the data points across the network, allowing each node's static observation `X_k` to be paired with a traveling observation `Y_k` that eventually represents the entire dataset. This clever combination of estimate averaging and data propagation allows the local estimates to converge to the global U-statistic.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis of their algorithms, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. The analysis, based on a novel "phantom network" construction, shows that the rates depend on the spectral gap of the network graph and data-dependent variance terms. The paper compares GoSta to the only known prior work in this area, U2-gossip [17], demonstrating superior theoretical rates and lower memory and communication costs. Experimental results on real datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation validate the theoretical claims and show that GoSta outperforms U2-gossip in practice.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem:** The paper tackles an important and previously under-explored problem in the gossip literature. U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and developing efficient, fully decentralized algorithms for their computation is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Elegant Algorithmic Idea:** The mechanism of combining local estimate averaging with the swapping of auxiliary observations is simple, elegant, and effective. It neatly solves the challenge of creating pairwise interactions between distributed data points without requiring global knowledge or excessive communication.
3.  **Thorough Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The O(1/t) rate is a strong result. The use of the "phantom network" is an interesting analytical tool for decoupling the data propagation and estimate averaging processes.
4.  **Practical Advantages:** Compared to the prior work [17], the proposed GoSta algorithm requires less memory (one auxiliary variable per node instead of two) and less communication per iteration (one edge activation instead of two), making it more practical for resource-constrained settings.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of the Core Mechanism:** The key algorithmic innovation—the swapping of auxiliary observations to create coupled random walks—is a fantastic idea but is somewhat buried in the algorithm description. The introduction and motivation would be significantly strengthened by highlighting this mechanism upfront as the central contribution that enables the computation of pairwise statistics.

2.  **Context within Broader Gossip Literature:** The related work section is a bit narrow, focusing primarily on the direct predecessor [17]. The paper would benefit from connecting to the broader literature on gossip algorithms. A citation to a comprehensive survey like Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, 2011) would be appropriate. Furthermore, the "phantom network" analysis is reminiscent of "lifting" techniques used to analyze and accelerate mixing in Markov chains (e.g., in LADA). Discussing this connection could provide deeper insight and potentially suggest avenues for future work, such as using lifted constructions to accelerate convergence.

3.  **Benchmarking the Convergence Rate:** The O(1/t) convergence rate is presented as a key result, but its significance is hard to gauge in isolation. A useful point of comparison would be a simple, memory-intensive baseline. For instance, consider a "flooding" approach where each node broadcasts its observation `X_i` to all other nodes. Once a node receives all `n` observations, it can compute the U-statistic exactly. This has a high upfront communication cost but is finite-time. How does the convergence of GoSta compare to such a strategy in terms of total communication or time to reach a certain accuracy? Providing this context would help readers better understand the trade-offs and the practical utility of the O(1/t) rate.

4.  **Clarification of the Communication Model:** The paper states that an edge `(i, j)` is drawn "uniformly at random from E." This is ambiguous. A more common model in the asynchronous gossip literature is that a node `i` wakes up and chooses a neighbor `j` uniformly at random. This induces a different distribution on the edges than uniform sampling from `E`, especially in irregular graphs. The authors should clarify their precise model and its practical justification.

5.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experiments compellingly demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over U2-gossip. However, the inclusion of other simple baselines would strengthen the results. For example, a "random walk" baseline where only the auxiliary observations are swapped (without the averaging step `Z_i, Z_j <- 1/2 (Z_i + Z_j)`) could show the importance of the averaging step.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   The theoretical bounds in Theorem 1 and Eq. (10) depend on terms like `||h - U_n(H)1_n||` and `||H - h1_n^T||`. The analysis appears to be worst-case over the initial data `X`. Could the authors clarify this and perhaps comment on whether any probabilistic assumptions on the data would lead to tighter or more intuitive bounds?
*   The condition for convergence is that the graph `G` is connected and non-bipartite. This is standard for random walks on undirected graphs to ensure convergence to a unique stationary distribution. However, the use of `W_2(G)` for the averaging step does not require the non-bipartite assumption for convergence of the average, only connectivity. Is the non-bipartite condition strictly necessary for the data propagation part of the analysis?
*   (Minor) In Algorithm 2 (GoSta-async), the update for `Z_i` and `Z_j` (lines 6-7) happens *after* the averaging step (line 5). This means the newly averaged values are immediately altered. Is this ordering intentional? It seems more natural to perform the local update using `H(X_i, Y_i)` first and then average the resulting estimates. Please clarify the motivation for this specific order.

**Conclusion**

This is a strong paper that makes a novel and valuable contribution to the field of decentralized computation. The problem is significant, the proposed algorithm is elegant, and the theoretical and empirical results are convincing. The primary weaknesses relate to positioning the work within the broader literature and providing sufficient context to fully appreciate the significance of the results. I believe the paper is a clear candidate for acceptance. My recommendation would become even stronger if the authors can address the points above, particularly by clarifying the core mechanism in the introduction and providing better context for the convergence rate via baseline comparisons.
