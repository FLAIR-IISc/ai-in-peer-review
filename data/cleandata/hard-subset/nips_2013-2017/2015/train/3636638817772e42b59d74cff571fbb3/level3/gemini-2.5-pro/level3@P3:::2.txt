This paper proposes a novel decentralized algorithm for computing degree-two U-statistics over a network. This is an important and challenging problem, as U-statistics involve averages over pairs of data points, which standard gossip algorithms for separable sums (like sample means) cannot handle efficiently. The core idea is to have each node maintain not only a local estimate of the U-statistic but also a secondary state variable, an "auxiliary observation," which is initialized to its own data point. The proposed algorithm, GoSta, combines two steps: a standard gossip averaging step on the local estimates, and a data propagation step where neighboring nodes *swap* their auxiliary observations. This clever swapping mechanism induces coupled random walks of the initial observations across the network, allowing each node to eventually "see" a representative sample of other nodes' data to form pairwise comparisons. The authors provide both synchronous and asynchronous versions of the algorithm and prove convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t / t), respectively. The analysis connects the convergence speed to the spectral gap of the network graph and data-dependent variance terms.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper addresses a significant and relatively under-explored problem in decentralized computation. The proposed algorithmic technique of combining averaging with the swapping of auxiliary observations is simple, elegant, and novel. It provides a memory-efficient way to propagate data for pairwise computations without resorting to expensive flooding or storing large histories.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms. The use of a "phantom network" is an interesting proof technique to decouple the analysis of the averaging and data propagation dynamics. The derived convergence rates explicitly show the dependence on network topology (via the spectral gap) and data properties, which is insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the main algorithm is easy to understand. The experiments on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation clearly demonstrate the empirical benefits of the proposed method over the main existing competitor (U2-gossip).

**Weaknesses**

*   **Context and Related Work:** The paper could do a better job of positioning its contribution within the broader literature on gossip algorithms and related decentralized methods. The related work section is somewhat brief and misses some key references and conceptual connections.
*   **Lack of Baselines:** The experimental comparison is limited to the U2-gossip algorithm. While this is the most direct competitor, the inclusion of other natural, albeit perhaps naive, baselines would provide a much clearer picture of the algorithm's tradeoffs and performance. Without this context, it is difficult to judge whether the O(1/t) convergence rate is "good" for this problem class.
*   **Clarity of Assumptions:** Some details regarding the communication model and theoretical assumptions could be clarified to improve reproducibility and rigor.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Highlighting the Core Mechanism:** The key algorithmic idea—having observations perform coupled random walks by swapping a secondary state variable—is the paper's most elegant contribution. I would encourage the authors to state this more explicitly and prominently in the introduction and methods sections. It is a powerful and potentially generalizable trick for other non-separable distributed computations.

2.  **Strengthening Connections to Related Work:**
    *   The related work section should cite the comprehensive survey by Dimakis et al. ("Gossip Algorithms for Distributed Signal Processing," Proc. IEEE, 2014), which is a standard reference in this area.
    *   The mechanism of propagating information on an expanded state space (the original node plus its auxiliary observation) seems conceptually related to "lifting" techniques used to accelerate mixing in Markov chains (e.g., Lifted Metropolis-Hastings, LADA). It would be valuable to discuss this potential connection, even if just to contrast the goals and methods. Could such ideas be used to accelerate the data propagation step?

3.  **Clarification of the Communication Model:** The algorithm description states "Draw (i, j) uniformly at random from E." This is ambiguous. Does this mean an edge is sampled uniformly from the set E (an edge-centric model)? Or does a node wake up and choose a neighbor uniformly (a node-centric model)? These models lead to different transition matrices and have different practical implications. Please clarify this in the text and ensure the analysis matches the stated model precisely.

4.  **Establishing Stronger Baselines:** To better situate the performance of GoSta, please consider comparing it to a memory-intensive baseline. For example, a "flooding-based" algorithm where nodes broadcast their initial observation (Xi, i) and store all unique observations they receive. Once a node has collected all `n` observations, it can compute the U-statistic exactly. This algorithm would be communication- and memory-heavy but would serve as a valuable benchmark to understand the speed-memory tradeoff. How does the convergence of GoSta compare to the time it takes for a flooding protocol to terminate? This would help answer whether O(1/t) is a fast or slow rate for this problem.

5.  **Assumptions and Guarantees:** The theorems provide error bounds in terms of norms of `H` and `h`. Could you be more explicit about the assumptions on the initial data `(X1, ..., Xn)`? Are the bounds worst-case over any data configuration, or do they rely on implicit assumptions? Making the dependence of the error on the properties of the initial data sample `X` more explicit would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

6.  **Minor Points:**
    *   I was not able to verify the proofs in the supplementary material due to time constraints, so my evaluation relies on the correctness of the claims in the main text.
    *   In Section 2.1, the definition of a bipartite graph seems to assume the graph is undirected. However, the text says `E ⊆ (S × T )∪ (T × S)`, which is correct. A small point, but please ensure all definitions and notation are precise throughout. A final proofreading pass for language and typos is recommended.

**Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on decentralized algorithms. The core idea is clever and the analysis is solid. My primary concerns relate to positioning the work within the broader literature and strengthening the experimental evaluation with more informative baselines. I believe these points can be addressed in a revision. I recommend acceptance.
