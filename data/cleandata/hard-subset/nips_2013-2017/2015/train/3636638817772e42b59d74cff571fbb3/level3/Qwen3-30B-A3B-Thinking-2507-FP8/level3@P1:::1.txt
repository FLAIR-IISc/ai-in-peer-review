

This paper addresses the important problem of efficiently computing U-statistics in distributed networks, a problem that has received limited attention compared to distributed averaging of sample means. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic, which is defined as Ûn(H) = (1/n²)∑i,j H(Xi,Xj). The work builds on the extensive literature on gossip algorithms for distributed averaging [2, 10, 15, 18, 19], but extends it to handle the more complex case of U-statistics that depend on pairs of observations. The authors correctly identify that existing gossip algorithms cannot efficiently compute U-statistics, and they note that the only prior work in this area (U2-gossip [17]) has significant drawbacks in terms of memory and communication costs.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it provides theoretically rigorous convergence rate guarantees: O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit dependence on data and network properties. The authors establish these results using an elegant "phantom node" reformulation that allows them to separate data propagation and averaging steps in the analysis. Second, they demonstrate that their approach achieves better convergence rates with lower memory and communication costs compared to U2-gossip [17], with the theoretical analysis showing that their method avoids the √n factor present in U2-gossip's convergence bound. Third, they conduct thorough empirical validation on two important applications (AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter) across multiple network topologies (complete, grid, and Watts-Strogatz), demonstrating consistent superiority over U2-gossip.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear definitions and a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm design to theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The key insights are well-explained, and the paper properly positions itself relative to related work, particularly by clearly articulating the limitations of U2-gossip.

The main strengths of the paper include:
- Novel contribution to an important problem (distributed U-statistic estimation)
- Strong theoretical guarantees with explicit convergence rates
- Significant practical improvements over prior work (lower memory/communication costs)
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on real-world applications
- Clear writing and proper contextualization within the literature

Potential weaknesses include:
- The paper focuses on the specific case of degree-2 U-statistics, which limits the scope of applicability
- The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, is somewhat complex and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory
- The paper does not address how their approach would scale to very large networks with millions of nodes

The paper excels across all four review criteria. For quality, the theoretical results are sound, well-supported by analysis, and the empirical evaluation is thorough. For clarity, the paper is well-structured and well-written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. For originality, the work represents a significant extension of gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, which is novel compared to previous NIPS work on gossip algorithms. For significance, the problem addressed is important for many distributed machine learning applications, and the proposed approach offers practical advantages that could be widely adopted.

The paper's main limitation is that it doesn't address whether the approach can be extended to higher-order U-statistics (beyond degree-2), which would be valuable for certain applications. However, this is a reasonable scope for a single paper, and the authors have clearly identified the specific contribution they're making.

The authors' comparison to U2-gossip is particularly compelling, with both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence demonstrating superior performance. The experimental results consistently show that GoSta algorithms outperform U2-gossip across different network topologies and problem instances, with the performance gap widening as network size increases.

Given the strength of the theoretical contribution, the clear demonstration of practical advantages, and the thorough empirical validation, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field of distributed learning. The work is clearly positioned within the literature, addresses an important problem with a novel solution, and provides both theoretical guarantees and practical evidence of effectiveness. The paper is of high quality, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the literature on distributed algorithms.

Pro: Novel extension of gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics with better theoretical guarantees and practical performance than prior work.  
Con: Focus on degree-2 U-statistics limits scope; theoretical analysis may be challenging for some readers.  

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the field of distributed learning with significant theoretical and practical implications.
