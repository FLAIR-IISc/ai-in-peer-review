- Proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of U-statistics (pairwise-average functionals).  
- Proves convergence rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous), dependent on data and network, and shows empirical improvement over [17].  
- Tackles an underexplored problem important for metrics like AUC, empirical variance, and Gini mean difference.  
- Extends gossip algorithms to non-separable U-statistics, addressing a limitation of standard gossip methods.  
- Builds on established gossip literature but should better cite and connect to broader work (e.g., Dimakis et al. 2006).  
- Introduces “auxiliary observations” swapped with estimates to propagate data and implement coupled random walks — a clever mechanism needing more emphasis.  
- The description of random edge selection is ambiguous; the induced edge distribution must be clarified.  
- Lacks comparison to naive baselines (e.g., flooding where nodes store/propagate all observations) to demonstrate memory vs convergence tradeoffs.  
- Claimed lower memory cost than [17] should be shown relative to a broader set of potential solutions.  
- Convergence rates should be benchmarked against what standard gossip algorithms would yield (even if they use more memory).  
- Theoretical analysis is a significant contribution but omits explicit assumptions on initial data distribution (worst-case vs probabilistic).  
- Should provide explicit error guarantees as a function of the initial data.  
- The “phantom nodes” proof technique is creative but the connection between the phantom network and the original graph needs clarification.  
- Experimental evaluation (AUC and within-cluster scatter) supports algorithm performance and comparison to [17], but lacks simpler baselines.  
- Originality and significance are high: novel solution, theoretical guarantees, and empirical gains for decentralized U-statistics.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, solid theoretical analysis, empirical improvement, and practical relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited baseline comparisons, ambiguous edge-selection description, unclear impact of initial data, and incomplete literature context.  
- Recommendation: accept conditional on addressing concerns — expand related work, clarify edge selection, add baseline comparisons, and state initial-data assumptions.
