

# Review for "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses an important gap in decentralized computation: the distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are fundamental for many statistical applications including AUC, variance, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for computing separable statistics (like averages), they have not been adapted to handle U-statistics that require pairwise averaging. The authors build upon prior work (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010) which introduced U2-gossip but had limitations in memory requirements, communication complexity, and asynchronicity.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of U-statistics. The authors establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit data and network-dependent terms. The key innovation is the use of "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem, enabling a clean convergence analysis that reveals how the rate depends on the spectral gap of the network graph. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed, with the convergence bounds showing clear improvements over U2-gossip.

The paper is well-motivated and properly positioned within the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous work (U2-gossip) and demonstrate how their approach overcomes these limitations in terms of convergence rate, memory usage, and communication cost. The experimental section provides compelling empirical evidence of the superiority of the proposed algorithms across multiple network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz) and two real-world applications (AUC estimation on SVMguide3 dataset and within-cluster point scatter on Wine Quality dataset). The results consistently show that GoSta algorithms achieve lower average error and variance compared to U2-gossip, with the performance gap widening as network size increases.

Regarding the four review criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The convergence proofs are complete, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to address both strengths (convergence rates, memory efficiency) and weaknesses (theoretical limitations of the asynchronous case) of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, algorithms, and theoretical analysis are clearly presented with appropriate notation. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, and the figures effectively illustrate the experimental findings.

Originality: The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a previously unexplored problem (distributed U-statistic estimation) with a novel algorithmic approach. The use of phantom nodes for convergence analysis represents an interesting technical contribution. The work is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers on gossip algorithms (e.g., Boyd et al., 2006; Kempe et al., 2003) and from the related U2-gossip work.

Significance: U-statistics are fundamental to many machine learning and statistical applications, and the proposed algorithms have practical implications for decentralized systems where communication and memory are constrained. The work provides a new building block for decentralized optimization of U-statistics, with potential applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and distributed learning. The improved convergence rates and reduced communication costs make the approach particularly valuable for large-scale distributed systems.

The paper's strengths are substantial: novel contribution to a meaningful problem, strong theoretical guarantees, thorough experimental validation, and clear exposition. The main weakness is that the work is limited to degree-two U-statistics, though the authors note that their framework could be extended to higher-order statistics. However, this limitation is reasonable given the paper's scope and the importance of the degree-two case.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap in distributed computation literature
- Provides significant theoretical improvements over prior work
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages through comprehensive experiments
- Well-written and well-organized with proper positioning relative to related work
- Offers a new tool for practitioners working with distributed systems

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited to degree-two U-statistics (though the authors note potential extensions)
- Theoretical analysis of the asynchronous case has a weaker rate (O(log t/t) vs O(1/t))

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of distributed algorithms. It advances the state of the art in gossip algorithms by extending them to handle U-statistics, which are crucial for many applications. The theoretical results are strong, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. I strongly recommend acceptance.
