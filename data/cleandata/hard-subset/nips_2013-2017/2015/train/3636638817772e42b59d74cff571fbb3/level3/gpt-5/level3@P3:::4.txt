Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics using gossip-style communication. Unlike classical gossip that computes averages of node-local quantities, U-statistics average over all pairs of observations, which breaks separability and is not handled by standard consensus/averaging techniques. The authors propose two intuitive algorithms:
- GoSta-sync: a synchronous procedure where, at each iteration, all nodes update a running average using H(Xk, Yk); a random edge averages local estimates Zi and swaps auxiliary observations Yi ↔ Yj to propagate data.
- GoSta-async: an asynchronous variant where only the selected edge updates; nodes keep a local counter mk and use importance-weighted running averages to debias updates under non-uniform activation.

They analyze convergence in expectation, establishing O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit constants that depend on data dispersion terms and the spectral gap of the network graph. The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple estimation and data propagation dynamics.

The work is positioned against (i) classical randomized gossip for means (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006; Kempe et al. 2003) and (ii) U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), the only prior algorithm targeting U-statistics, which requires two auxiliary observations per node and two concurrent swaps per iteration. The paper claims lower memory/communication, faster rates, and asynchrony.

Strengths
- Interesting and relevant problem: U-statistics include AUC, variance, Gini difference, Kendall tau, and within-cluster scatter—metrics of practical importance in decentralized ML and sensing.
- Clear and intuitive algorithms: the “swap+average” scheme is easy to implement; the asynchronous variant is practical for real networks.
- Rigorous analysis: convergence rates with explicit network and data dependence; the phantom-network proof technique is well-executed. The comparison to U2-gossip is theoretically informative.
- Empirical results corroborate faster convergence on several network topologies and tasks; the observed benefit grows with n, consistent with theory.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines: experiments mainly compare to U2-gossip. There is no thorough evaluation against simple intuitive or randomized baselines, nor against generic dissemination/consensus strategies adapted to U-statistics.
- Experimental depth: results report relative error vs. iterations but provide limited accounting of communication, memory, and wall-clock cost. Realistic factors (message delays, loss, degree heterogeneity) are not explored, and network classes are restricted.
- Novelty is incremental: algorithmically, the approach is a natural blend of value averaging and token-swapping random walks. The key conceptual step (propagate auxiliary samples while averaging local estimates) is intuitive; the main novelty lies in the analysis and asynchronous design.
- Theory is in expectation only: no high-probability or mean-square error bounds; no finite-time concentration across nodes.

Quality
- Soundness: The proofs appear careful, with explicit constants capturing spectral gap effects. The O(1/t) vs. O(log t/t) rates are reasonable given synchronous vs. asynchronous updates. The comparison bound for U2-gossip is useful.
- Completeness: The work covers both synchronous and asynchronous settings and two representative applications. However, evaluation lacks ablations (e.g., spectral gap variation) and stress tests.
- Evaluation honesty: The paper fairly discusses assumptions (connected, non-bipartite graph; nodes know degrees and |E| in async). Still, sensitivity to misspecification of pk and robustness to asynchrony beyond Poisson clocks are not examined.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear and well organized. Algorithms are stated explicitly. The phantom-node construction is explained carefully. Minor suggestions:
  - Provide more intuition behind the log t/t rate in the async case.
  - Summarize per-iteration computation, memory, and message sizes in one place.
  - Clarify practical means to estimate |E| for pk in distributed settings, or suggest substitutes (e.g., Metropolis weights).

Originality
- Extends gossip beyond separable sums to U-statistics with a simple algorithmic recipe and a neat analytical framework. Relative to U2-gossip, the asynchronous variant and reduced resource footprint are valuable, but the core mechanism is not a large conceptual leap.

Significance
- Practical impact could be meaningful because U-statistics are common in ML evaluation and clustering. The explicit dependence on spectral gap ties nicely to network design. Broader adoption would be strengthened by a more comprehensive empirical study and comparisons.

Suggestions for improving the experimental evaluation and baselines
- Add intuitive/randomized baselines:
  1) Pair-subsampling baseline: at each step sample a random edge and compute H(Xi, Xj), aggregate via standard average gossip; this estimates the U-statistic via unbiased pair sampling without data propagation.
  2) Flooding/broadcast baseline: periodically flood auxiliary observations (or compact sketches) to neighbors and run standard average consensus on accumulated local H terms; compare communication vs. error.
  3) Push-sum two-phase baseline: first estimate per-node hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi,Xj) via random walks or randomized routing to pull samples from j, then average hi via consensus.
  4) Single-random-walk without averaging: a token carrying a running average of H(Xtoken, Xi) visits nodes; compare mixing-time-driven convergence.
  5) For AUC: stratified pair sampling across classes with reservoir sampling at nodes to ensure balanced positive-negative comparisons.
- Report communication/memory/runtime: messages per iteration, total transmitted features, memory footprint per node, and wall-clock on simulated asynchronous networks.
- Stress tests: vary spectral gap (e.g., barbell, line, scale-free), n and d, message delays/drops, degree heterogeneity, and multiple observations per node.
- Metrics: include mean-square error and high-probability quantiles across nodes; show variance reduction due to averaging.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses a timely and practically relevant decentralized estimation problem.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with solid theory linking data and network properties.
- Asynchronous variant and lower resource usage compared to prior U2-gossip.
- Empirical results consistent with theory.

Cons:
- Novelty is moderate; the main advance is analytical and engineering rather than conceptual.
- Experimental comparison set is narrow; lacks intuitive baselines and comprehensive cost reporting.
- No concentration or high-probability guarantees; robustness not explored.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-argued paper on an interesting problem with practical implications. I lean toward borderline accept if the authors strengthen the empirical section with simple randomized baselines and thorough communication/runtime analyses; otherwise, this risks being viewed as an incremental improvement over U2-gossip with insufficient experimental evidence.
