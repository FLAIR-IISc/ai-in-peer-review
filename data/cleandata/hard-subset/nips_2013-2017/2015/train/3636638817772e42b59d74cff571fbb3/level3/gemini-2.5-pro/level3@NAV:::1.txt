This paper introduces GoSta, a family of novel randomized gossip algorithms for the distributed estimation of degree-two U-statistics. The problem is significant, as U-statistics encompass important metrics like AUC and sample variance, which are more complex than the simple averages typically handled by gossip algorithms. The authors position their work as a direct and substantial improvement over the prior state-of-the-art, U2-gossip [17], by addressing its shortcomings in communication cost, memory requirements, and reliance on a synchronous setting.

The paper's primary strength lies in its comprehensive and rigorous contribution.
**Quality & Significance:** The proposed synchronous and asynchronous algorithms are backed by solid theoretical convergence analyses, establishing rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t), respectively. These theoretical claims are convincingly validated through an extensive empirical evaluation on two real-world datasets, demonstrating a clear advance over existing baselines. GoSta is demonstrably superior to U2-gossip in convergence speed, memory usage, and communication overhead. The development of a practical asynchronous variant is a particularly significant step forward for real-world applicability in decentralized systems where global clocks are unavailable.

**Originality & Clarity:** The approach is novel, cleverly combining a data propagation step (via auxiliary observation swapping) with an estimate averaging step, which proves more effective than prior methods. The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-motivated, and organized, making the technical details easy to follow. The "phantom network" construction used for the convergence analysis is an elegant and insightful analytical tool.

The paper has very few weaknesses. However, for future improvement, the authors could briefly discuss the challenges of extending the GoSta framework to U-statistics of a degree higher than two. While the empirical evaluation is solid, testing on a wider variety of network topologies or larger-scale datasets could further strengthen the claims of robustness. Finally, a minor point of discussion could be the gap between the O(log t/t) theoretical bound for the asynchronous case and its strong empirical performance, which appears comparable to the synchronous version.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of distributed machine learning. It solves an important problem more effectively than previous methods and is presented with outstanding clarity. I strongly recommend acceptance.
