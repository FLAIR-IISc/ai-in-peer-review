This paper addresses the important problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. While gossip-based methods for simple averages are well-studied, computing U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations, is a more challenging and less explored area. The authors propose novel synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) randomized gossip algorithms for this task. The core idea is to combine a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary observations, with an estimate averaging step. This work directly extends the line of research on gossip algorithms and aims to improve upon the U2-gossip algorithm, which is the primary prior work on this specific problem.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical relevance, as U-statistics like AUC and sample variance are fundamental in machine learning. The proposed algorithms represent a novel and meaningful contribution to the field of decentralized computation, advancing the state of the art beyond simple averaging.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically strong. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous versions. The "phantom network" technique used for the analysis is an elegant and insightful theoretical tool.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and clearly written. The algorithms and their underlying intuition are explained effectively, and the manuscript is well-organized.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results are convincing. The proposed GoSta algorithm is shown to outperform the U2-gossip baseline across two different U-statistics (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) on real-world datasets and under varied network topologies, confirming its theoretical advantages.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**
The primary weakness lies in the evaluation of computational efficiency. While the paper compellingly demonstrates faster convergence in terms of the number of iterations, it does not report on the wall-clock time per iteration. The abstract claims lower communication and memory costs, but the complexity of the local updates in Algorithm 1 (a loop over all `n` nodes before the communication step) raises questions. It is not immediately clear how the per-iteration run-times of GoSta and U2-gossip compare. If an iteration of the proposed method is substantially slower than an iteration of U2-gossip, the observed gains in iteration-based convergence might not translate to faster real-world performance.

To strengthen the paper's claims of practical utility, I would request that the authors provide a comparison of the per-iteration run-times. Ideally, the existing plots could be supplemented with new ones showing the average relative error versus wall-clock time, which would provide a more complete and impactful assessment of the algorithm's practical advantages.
