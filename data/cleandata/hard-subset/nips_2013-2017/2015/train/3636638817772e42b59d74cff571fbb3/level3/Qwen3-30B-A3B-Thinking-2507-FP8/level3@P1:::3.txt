

This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a significant extension of existing gossip-based distributed computation methods. The authors address a well-motivated problem: while gossip algorithms are established for estimating separable statistics (like sample means), they cannot efficiently compute U-statistics, which require averaging over pairs of observations. The authors note that U-statistics are essential for important applications including Area Under the Curve (AUC), empirical variance, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter. The paper builds on previous work in gossip algorithms (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006, Kempe et al. 2003) and the U2-gossip algorithm (Pelckmans and Suykens 2010), which the authors identify as having limitations in memory, communication costs, and asynchronicity.

The key contributions are two novel algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic. The authors provide theoretical convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit data and network-dependent terms. These results significantly improve upon U2-gossip, which achieves O(1/t) but with higher memory and communication costs per iteration. The authors' convergence analysis is technically sound, featuring a clever reformulation using "phantom nodes" to separate data propagation and averaging steps. The proofs are well-structured and provide meaningful insights into how network connectivity (via the spectral gap) and data dispersion affect convergence rates.

The paper is well-written with clear exposition of the problem, algorithms, and analysis. The experimental section provides strong empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims, comparing GoSta against U2-gossip on two real-world applications (AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter) across three network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, and Watts-Strogatz). The results consistently show that GoSta outperforms U2-gossip in both convergence speed and variance reduction, with the performance gap widening as network size increases. The authors demonstrate the practical significance of their work by addressing important machine learning metrics that were previously difficult to compute in distributed settings.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, which are critical for many applications. This work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on gossip algorithms (e.g., Kempe et al. 2003, Boyd et al. 2006) and related work on U-statistics (e.g., Pelckmans and Suykens 2010). The authors adequately reference related work and clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior methods.

However, a critical concern remains regarding the practical utility of the proposed algorithms. The paper does not provide per-iteration run-times for both GoSta and U2-gossip, which is essential for assessing real-world applicability. U2-gossip requires two pairs of nodes to exchange observations at each iteration, while GoSta requires only one pair. If GoSta's iteration is significantly slower due to additional computations (e.g., maintaining and updating auxiliary observations), its practical advantage might be limited despite better asymptotic convergence rates. The authors should address this in the revision by including detailed computational complexity analysis and per-iteration timing measurements.

The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims and thorough experimental validation. The convergence rate analysis provides valuable insights into the interplay between network structure and estimation difficulty. The clarity of writing is excellent, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with distributed algorithms. The significance of the work is high, as it enables efficient distributed computation of important statistical measures that were previously challenging to compute in decentralized settings.

Strengths:
- Novel extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics
- Rigorous convergence rate analysis with meaningful data/network-dependent terms
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets and network topologies
- Clear writing and well-structured presentation
- Practical relevance to important machine learning metrics (AUC, within-cluster scatter)

Weaknesses:
- Lack of per-iteration computational complexity analysis
- Insufficient discussion of practical implementation considerations
- Limited comparison to other baseline methods beyond U2-gossip

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to distributed estimation. The technical content is sound, the results are compelling, and the work has clear practical relevance. The main concern regarding computational efficiency per iteration should be addressed in a revision, but this does not detract from the paper's scientific merit. The authors have successfully extended gossip algorithms to a new class of problems with important applications, and their theoretical and empirical results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of their approach. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the computational complexity concerns.
