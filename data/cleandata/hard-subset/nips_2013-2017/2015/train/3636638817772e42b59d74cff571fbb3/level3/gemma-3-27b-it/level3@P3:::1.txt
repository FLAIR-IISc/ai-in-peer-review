This paper proposes novel gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, a challenging problem in decentralized estimation. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as existing gossip algorithms primarily focus on simpler statistics like the sample mean. The proposed algorithms, both synchronous and asynchronous, achieve faster convergence rates than the prior state-of-the-art (U2-Gossip) and offer improvements in memory and communication cost. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and thoroughly positioned within the context of existing research in gossip algorithms and U-statistics. The theoretical guarantees and convergence analysis are a strong point, and the empirical evaluation on two datasets convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis, while complex, appears rigorous and provides concrete bounds on the convergence rates. The theoretical results are appropriately supported by the experimental findings. The paper is a complete piece of work; it clearly defines the problem, presents novel algorithms, analyzes their properties, and validates them empirically. The authors are careful to compare their approach with U2-Gossip, highlighting both the advantages and potential trade-offs. The "phantom node" technique is clever and effectively used to analyze the synchronous algorithm. The acknowledgement of the limitations and potential areas for future work (e.g., adaptive communication schemes) demonstrate intellectual honesty.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the algorithms are presented in a concise and understandable manner. The introduction provides a strong motivation for the work, and the related work section effectively contextualizes the contribution. The descriptions of the algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) are sufficiently detailed to allow for implementation. The use of Algorithm 1 and 2 is helpful for understanding the procedural aspects. The explanations surrounding the "phantom network" concept are clear, though potentially challenging for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory.

**Originality:** The problem of applying gossip algorithms to U-statistics is itself a novel contribution, as explicitly stated by the authors. While gossip algorithms are well-established, their extension to this more complex class of statistics is not trivial. The specific algorithms proposed, particularly the combination of data propagation and averaging steps, represent a novel approach. The use of a “phantom network” to analyze the synchronous algorithm is an original technique. The paper properly references relevant prior work, including U2-Gossip and other related research in distributed estimation. The comparison to U2-Gossip, and the detailed explanation of its shortcomings, further establishes the originality of the work.

**Significance:** This work is significant. U-statistics arise in a wide range of applications, including machine learning (AUC, Gini mean difference) and statistics (variance).  Efficient decentralized computation of these statistics is crucial for handling large datasets distributed across multiple nodes. The proposed algorithms offer a substantial improvement over existing methods, both in terms of convergence rate and resource requirements. This makes them potentially useful for practitioners and researchers working in areas such as distributed machine learning, sensor networks, and data mining.  The theoretical analysis and empirical validation demonstrate a demonstrable advancement in the state-of-the-art.  The provided datasets and potential for extending the algorithms to decentralized optimization further highlight the practical impact of this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and important problem addressed.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees with rigorous convergence analysis.
*   Empirically validated superiority over existing methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Significant potential impact on distributed estimation and machine learning.
*   Addresses weaknesses of prior work (U2-Gossip).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis, while thorough, might be challenging for readers without a strong background in spectral graph theory. (This is a minor point, as the presentation is generally clear).
*   The asynchronous algorithm's practical performance is comparable to the synchronous algorithm, despite a slightly worse theoretical rate. More investigation into conditions favoring one over the other could be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:** This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The authors have presented a novel and well-motivated solution to a challenging problem, backed by strong theoretical analysis and empirical validation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
