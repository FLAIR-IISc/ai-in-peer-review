This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for estimating U-statistics, a class of data functionals less explored than sample means but crucial for describing global population properties. The authors derive theoretical convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t / t) for the asynchronous version, and demonstrate empirically that their algorithms outperform a previous approach (U2-gossip). The paper is well-organized, clearly presented, and addresses a relevant problem with potentially broad applicability.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of decentralized U-statistic estimation in networked systems. Prior work in gossip algorithms, extensively reviewed, primarily focused on computing averages and sums of separable functions (e.g., [19, 2, 15]). These existing methods are insufficient for U-statistics, which require averaging over pairs of observations. The work builds upon the earlier, but less efficient, U2-gossip algorithm [17], improving both convergence rates and communication/memory costs.  The authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of distributed estimation and gossip protocols, acknowledging relevant literature on spectral graph theory [3] and network connectivity.  The connection to broader statistical concepts like AUC and within-cluster point scatter grounds the theoretical contribution in practical applications.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The derivation of convergence rates appears rigorous, although the supplementary material is essential for a full assessment. The use of "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem is a clever and insightful technique that simplifies the analysis. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of both theoretical aspects and practical performance. The comparison to U2-gossip, and the analysis showing improved convergence, strengthens the claims. However, the discussion of limitations is somewhat brief. While they acknowledge potential improvements with adaptive communication schemes, further discussion of scenarios where these algorithms might *not* perform well (e.g., highly heterogeneous networks) would enhance the paper. The proofs, while alluded to as being in the supplementary material, are crucial for verifying the claims and should be assessed with equal scrutiny.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. Notation is well-defined, and the algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) are presented in a concise and understandable manner. The use of Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 is helpful.  The rationale behind the phantom node construction is clearly explained, making a potentially complex idea accessible. The discussion of convergence rates, while mathematically dense, is well-explained in relation to network connectivity (spectral gap).

**Originality:**

The core contribution – extending gossip algorithms to efficiently estimate U-statistics – is original. While building on existing gossip principles, the authors devise novel algorithms and convergence analysis tailored to the specific challenges posed by U-statistics. The combination of synchronous and asynchronous approaches adds to the originality. The use of phantom nodes for analysis is a novel technique in this context. The authors acknowledge and differentiate their work from U2-gossip, highlighting the improvements.  A deeper look at related work *outside* of gossip algorithms, potentially in distributed statistical estimation more broadly, might further solidify the originality claim.

**Significance:**

The results are significant. U-statistics are fundamental to various machine learning and data mining applications (AUC, Gini mean difference, etc.). Efficiently computing them in a distributed manner has broad implications. The algorithms presented could be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with large-scale, decentralized datasets. The demonstrated performance advantages over existing approaches and the theoretical convergence guarantees establish a clear advance in the state of the art. The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter provide compelling evidence of practical applicability. The potential for future work extending these ideas to decentralized optimization of U-statistics further highlights the significance of this contribution.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees (convergence rates).
*   Empirical validation demonstrating performance advantages.
*   Addresses a relevant problem with broad applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion of limitations could be more extensive.
*   Dependence on supplementary material for complete proof verification.
*   A more comprehensive comparison to alternative distributed statistical estimation methods might be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The technical soundness, clarity, originality, and significance all support acceptance.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to expand the discussion of limitations and perhaps include a brief exploration of potential extensions beyond the scope of the current work. The supplementary material should be thoroughly reviewed by the program committee to ensure the rigor of the proofs.
