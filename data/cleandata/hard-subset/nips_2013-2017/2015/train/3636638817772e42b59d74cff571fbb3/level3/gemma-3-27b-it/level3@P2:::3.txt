This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. The motivation is strong – while distributed estimation of averages is well-studied, computing U-statistics (requiring averaging over pairs of observations) is less explored, yet critical for many applications like AUC calculation, variance estimation, and more. The authors establish convergence rate bounds and demonstrate, through numerical experiments on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter), that their algorithms outperform a previously introduced approach (U2-gossip) in terms of convergence speed.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively lays out the problem and its relevance, connecting it to existing work in decentralized computation and gossip algorithms. The related work section provides a good overview of existing gossip algorithms and highlights the gap this paper addresses. The technical details of the proposed algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) are presented clearly, and the convergence analysis, while dense, appears rigorous. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over U2-gossip across different network topologies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem of efficiently computing U-statistics via gossip algorithms is relatively unexplored, making this a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis provides theoretical guarantees for the performance of the algorithms, with bounds dependent on both data and network characteristics. The phantom node reformulation is a clever technique for proving these bounds.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on real datasets provide strong evidence supporting the claims of improved performance. The comparison to U2-gossip is well-justified, and the consistent outperformance across different network topologies is encouraging.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity. The organization is logical, and the notation is consistent.
* **Practical Relevance:** The applications highlighted (AUC, within-cluster scatter) are relevant to a wide range of machine learning and data mining tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost per Iteration:** While the convergence rate of GoSta-sync is faster (O(1/t)) than U2-gossip (and GoSta-async has a logarithmic factor), the paper doesn’t provide a detailed comparison of the *per-iteration* computational cost. If each iteration of GoSta-sync or GoSta-async is significantly slower than an iteration of U2-gossip, the overall runtime advantage might be diminished, especially for smaller datasets or well-connected networks where U2-gossip might converge reasonably quickly.  Run-time measurements, ideally broken down by component (communication, computation), would be a valuable addition.
* **Network Model Simplification:** The assumption of uniformly random edge selection might not be realistic in many practical scenarios. Exploring the performance under more realistic network models (e.g., considering node degree distributions) could strengthen the robustness of the results.
* **Asynchronous Algorithm Analysis:** While GoSta-async is presented, its convergence analysis is less detailed than that of the synchronous version. A more comprehensive analysis, or a clearer justification for the assumed conditions, would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and important problem in distributed computation.
* The theoretical results are sound and provide valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
* The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed per-iteration runtime comparison raises a concern about the practical efficiency of the proposed algorithms.
* The reliance on a simplified network model might limit the generalizability of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While the lack of per-iteration runtime comparison is a concern, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.  I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors consider including a more detailed runtime analysis in the camera-ready version, comparing per-iteration cost for both algorithms, perhaps as an appendix.  Addressing this point would further solidify the claim of practical superiority.



**Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.
**Clarity:** Very Good. The paper is well-written and organized, although some sections are dense.
**Originality:** Excellent. The problem and approach are novel.
**Significance:** Good. The results are important and could have a significant impact on applications requiring distributed U-statistic estimation.
