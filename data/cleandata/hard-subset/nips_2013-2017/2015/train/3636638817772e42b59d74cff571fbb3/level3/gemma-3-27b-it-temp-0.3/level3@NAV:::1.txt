## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networks, a less explored area compared to average estimation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms, demonstrating improvements over the existing U2-Gossip approach in terms of convergence rate, memory, and communication cost. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and appropriately positioned within the existing literature.

The core contribution lies in the design of these new gossip algorithms, leveraging both data propagation and local estimate averaging. The theoretical analysis provides convergence rate bounds (O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous) with insightful dependencies on data and network characteristics. The use of “phantom nodes” to facilitate the analysis is a clever technique. Importantly, the authors don’t just present theoretical results; they back them up with extensive empirical evaluation on two datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter), consistently demonstrating superior performance compared to U2-Gossip across different network topologies. 

A significant strength is the thorough comparison to U2-Gossip, highlighting the advantages of the proposed algorithms, particularly regarding memory and communication efficiency. The analysis also reveals the impact of network connectivity on convergence, linking it to the spectral gap of the network graph. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous versions broadens the applicability of the work to more realistic network settings. The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the algorithms and their analysis.

However, while the empirical evaluation is strong, it could be further strengthened by comparing against more diverse baseline methods beyond U2-Gossip.  Exploring the sensitivity of the algorithms to different network structures and data distributions would also be valuable.  The discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical convergence rates could be expanded, offering guidance on parameter tuning and expected performance in real-world scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees and convergence analysis.
*   Superior empirical performance compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   Well-written, clear, and well-motivated.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to baseline methods beyond U2-Gossip.
*   Further exploration of sensitivity to network and data characteristics would be beneficial.



**Overall:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance to the conference.
