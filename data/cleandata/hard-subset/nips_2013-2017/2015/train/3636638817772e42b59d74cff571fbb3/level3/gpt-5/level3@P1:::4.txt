This paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style protocols. The authors propose synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) algorithms in which each node holds one data point, maintains a single auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via edge swaps, and maintains a running local estimate that is also averaged when edges activate. A key modeling device is a “phantom nodes” construction that decouples data propagation from averaging and enables a clean linear-algebraic analysis. The main results show convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous). The bounds expose both data-dependent terms (dispersion of H and h) and network-dependent terms via the spectral gap (1 − λ2 of suitable Wα(G)). The memory and communication footprint are reduced relative to U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010], which requires two auxiliary observations and two edge activations per iteration. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over complete graphs, grids, and Watts–Strogatz networks indicate better empirical error and smaller across-node variance for GoSta compared to U2-gossip.

Relation to prior work: Classic randomized gossip achieves exponential rates for separable sums/averages [Boyd et al. 2006; Kempe et al. 2003], including geographic variants for poor connectivity [Dimakis et al.]. Those approaches do not directly handle pairwise functionals. U2-gossip is the closest antecedent; the present work adds an averaging step coupled with a single auxiliary observation and provides sharper rates that depend on the spectral gap and data dispersion, plus an asynchronous variant. The “phantom network” analysis is reminiscent of Markovian coupling arguments and lazy random walk analyses in spectral graph theory [Chung], but applied in a novel way to U-statistics.

Quality: The technical development is solid. The convergence proofs are carefully structured, with explicit dependence on network connectivity and data characteristics. The synchronous bound is informative, while the asynchronous bound is somewhat coarser (log t/t with an unspecified c′(G)). A few aspects could be strengthened:
- Only expectation bounds are provided; high-probability or MSE bounds would be more informative for practitioners, especially given stochastic activation.
- The non-bipartite assumption in Theorem 1 should be reconciled with experiments on grids (which are bipartite). If laziness from W2(G) suffices to break periodicity, please state and prove the relaxed condition.
- The asynchronous result hides constants in c′(G) and uses ‖H‖ alone; can the bound be made parallel to the synchronous case to reflect data dispersion and spectral gap separately?
- The requirement that nodes know pk = 2dk/|E| presupposes knowledge of |E|. Discuss how to estimate |E| (and impacts on bias/variance), and robustness to degree/edge-count estimation errors.

Clarity: The algorithms are simple and well presented. The phantom construction is clever; moving more intuition into the main text (with a figure) would help readers not versed in Kronecker formulations. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., remind readers of h and H−h1^T where used). The discussion contrasting GoSta with U2-gossip is helpful; an explicit per-iteration communication/memory/compute table would further clarify the practical trade-offs.

Originality: The overall approach—combining data propagation via random-walk swaps with consensus averaging to estimate U-statistics in a decentralized manner—appears to be a meaningful and nontrivial extension over U2-gossip. The phantom-node analytical device is a nice contribution. The problem is timely and underexplored relative to separable-function gossip.

Significance: U-statistics (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter) are widely used; a decentralized estimator with explicit network- and data-dependent rates is potentially impactful. The empirical results suggest material practical gains over U2-gossip with lower resource use. Broader adoption would benefit from stronger experimental baselines and scaling studies.

Experimental evaluation: The experiments are informative but limited in scope.
- The only direct competitor shown is U2-gossip; the paper mentions “additional baselines” in the supplement, but none are discussed in the main text. Including simple and strong baselines would make the case more compelling: centralized compute (oracle), flooding/aggregation (where feasible), token-passing random walks, subsampling-based approximate U-statistics (e.g., pair sampling per node), push-sum variants, and geographic gossip on location-aware networks.
- Report performance against communication budget (bytes or messages) and wall-clock time, not just iterations; per-iteration costs differ between methods.
- Provide ablations: with/without averaging; varying number of auxiliary observations; biased edge activation; robustness to packet loss and asynchrony jitter.
- Scale: results are up to n ≈ 1.6k; larger synthetic networks (e.g., 10k–100k) and more diverse topologies (barbell, expander-like) would better probe spectral-gap effects.
- Since theory addresses expectation, report MSE and quantiles across runs/nodes; include confidence intervals and convergence of across-node variance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem (distributed U-statistic estimation).
- Simple, implementable algorithms with lower memory/communication than prior art.
- Rigorous analysis with interpretable, network- and data-dependent rates.
- Both synchronous and asynchronous variants; clear improvement over U2-gossip empirically.

Cons for acceptance:
- Novelty over U2-gossip, while meaningful, is incremental for some readers.
- Experimental evaluation lacks broader baselines and communication/time accounting.
- Some theoretical assumptions and constants (non-bipartite, c′(G), knowledge of |E|) need clarification and practicalization.
- No high-probability or finite-sample MSE guarantees.

Suggestions to the authors:
- Add baselines as above and report error vs. messages/bytes and wall-clock.
- Clarify how the theory applies to bipartite graphs used in experiments or make Wα lazy and relax the assumptions accordingly.
- Provide high-probability or variance bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration).
- Discuss how to estimate |E| and degrees in decentralized settings and the impact on bias.
- Include resource accounting (message sizes in d, computation per step) and a table summarizing costs across methods.
- Explore approximate variants that trade accuracy for lower communication (pair subsampling), and compare.

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper on a timely topic. With stronger experimental baselines and clarified assumptions, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
