This paper addresses the problem of computing higher-order statistics—including sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients—measured over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. The authors propose a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm to compute these statistics. 

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, but the authors could strengthen the connection to existing approaches and related work.

This is a detailed review, and I did not have sufficient time to verify the correctness of all material presented in the supplement.

**Comments:**

1) A key aspect of the algorithm is that, during estimate exchange and averaging, nodes also swap a secondary state variable representing their initial observation. This creates a coupled random walk for the initial observations. Highlighting this more explicitly in the manuscript would improve clarity and motivate the analysis technique using multiple copies of the graph.

2) The authors cite limited related work on gossip approaches for computing statistics. A relevant snapshot of the state-of-the-art can be found in the Dimakis et al. paper from *Proc. IEEE*. Situating the current work within the context of this and similar work would be more helpful than the current, somewhat brief discussion.

3) On page 4, it would be beneficial to clarify the induced distribution on edges resulting from the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” does not fully specify the distribution.

4) A natural alternative approach to this problem involves allowing nodes to store more memory and use the same contact process to flood their own values across the network. Nodes would store received observations tagged with the originating node's identity, and at each time step, they could calculate the U-statistic on the subset of data they possess. Comparing this approach to the proposed method would help demonstrate the benefits of averaging, or reveal potential performance losses due to limited node memory. Without this comparison, it is unclear whether the achieved rate of 1/t is competitive. For expander graphs, the flooding method may perform quite well.

5) The authors should investigate relevant work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, as their construction shares similarities. Consider the following papers:
    * K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in *Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp.*, Urbana-Champaign, IL, Sep. 2007.
    * W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in *IEEE Transactions on Information Theory* 56(12), 2010.
    * F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in *Proceedings of the thirty-first annual ACM symposium on Theory of computing*. ACM, 1999, pp. 275-281.

6) Analyses of gossip algorithms typically make worst-case assumptions regarding the initial data distribution, providing guarantees over all possible initial data vectors *X*. This assumption isn't clearly stated in the problem statement or the theorem. Clarifying the assumption on the data distribution and specifying the error guarantees as a function of the initial data is crucial.

**Small corrections:**

*   p4: “let us stress out that” --> “we stress that”
*   p4: “awakes and contact” --> “awakes and contacts”
*   p6: “requires to initiate” --> “requires initiating”
*   p7: “allows to create” --> “allows us to create”

**After Author Feedback:**

I find the clarifications and modifications suggested by the authors to be well-addressed. I am revising my initial score from a 7 to an 8. 

However, the authors should still carefully examine the lifting constructions (especially LADA) and revise the related work accordingly. 

Furthermore, the experimental results should include comparisons to relevant baseline methods.

In conclusion, this is a well-presented approach to extending gossip methods for computing more complex statistics in distributed settings, such as sensor networks. The mathematical formulation and connection to prior work could benefit from further clarification. Assuming the authors make substantial revisions addressing the points raised in this review, I recommend accepting this paper.
