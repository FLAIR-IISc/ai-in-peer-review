This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for the distributed estimation of U-statistics. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t / t) for the asynchronous version. These theoretical findings are substantiated by empirical results that demonstrate the proposed methods' practical advantages over alternative approaches.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of estimating U-statistics in a decentralized setting. The proposed gossip-based algorithms are novel and offer an elegant solution that avoids the need for a central coordinator. This work is a valuable contribution to the literature on distributed and decentralized machine learning.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** A key strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions. The derivation of O(1/t) and O(log t / t) convergence rates for the synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively, provides strong performance guarantees. These rates are favorable and rigorously establish the efficiency of the proposed estimators.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The problem is clearly motivated, the proposed algorithms are described in sufficient detail, and the theoretical analysis is presented in a clear, coherent manner. The flow from theory to experimental validation is easy to follow.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and effectively supports the paper's claims. The authors compare their algorithms against relevant baselines, clearly showcasing their superior performance in terms of both convergence speed and final estimation accuracy. The empirical results align well with the theoretical predictions.

**Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** While the theoretical analysis is comprehensive, the paper could be improved by more explicitly stating and discussing the underlying assumptions. A dedicated paragraph outlining the assumptions regarding the network topology (e.g., connectivity), communication model, and properties of the U-statistic's kernel function would enhance the clarity and self-containedness of the paper.

2.  **Scalability Analysis:** The experiments are convincing for the tested network sizes. However, a discussion on the scalability of the proposed methods would strengthen the paper. For instance, how is the convergence impacted by the number of nodes, network diameter, or sparsity? Including either larger-scale experiments or a theoretical discussion on these aspects would provide a more complete picture of the algorithms' behavior in massive decentralized systems.

3.  **Context within Broader Literature:** The paper provides a good comparison to the direct baselines. It could be further strengthened by positioning the work within the broader context of distributed statistical estimation. A brief discussion of how these gossip-based methods compare or relate to other paradigms, such as those based on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) or other consensus protocols, would be insightful for the reader.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a solid contribution to an important area of research. The proposed algorithms are novel, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results are compelling. The work is of clear interest to researchers in distributed systems, statistics, and machine learning. With minor revisions to clarify assumptions and expand the discussion on scalability, this paper would be an excellent addition to the conference proceedings.
