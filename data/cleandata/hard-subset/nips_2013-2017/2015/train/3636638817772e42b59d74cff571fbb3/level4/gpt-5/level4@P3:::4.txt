Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-two U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. Unlike standard gossip algorithms that compute separable aggregates (e.g., sums/means of local functions), U-statistics average over pairs of observations, making them non-separable and challenging in distributed settings. The authors propose two algorithms:

- GoSta-sync: a synchronous randomized gossip algorithm that, at each iteration, performs (i) a running-average update of the local estimate using H(Xk, Yk), where Yk is an auxiliary observation that diffuses via swaps along randomly chosen edges, and (ii) average-consensus on the local estimates across the selected pair.
- GoSta-async: a fully asynchronous variant where only the selected pair updates, and each node uses a local counter with normalization by pk to correct for non-uniform activations.

A key analytical device is a “phantom network” construction that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging, enabling convergence analysis via block matrix recurrences. The paper proves convergence in expectation at O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous one, with constants that depend explicitly on data dispersion terms and the graph spectral gap. The approach improves on U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010) in rate constants and communication/memory costs. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter over multiple network topologies show faster convergence than U2-gossip.

This work is grounded in the literature on randomized gossip and consensus (e.g., Tsitsiklis; Boyd et al.; Dimakis et al.) and addresses a gap for non-separable statistics, where prior work is scarce beyond U2-gossip.

Evaluation:

Quality:
- The algorithms are simple and implementable: a single auxiliary sample per node, swapping along randomly sampled edges, and local running averages. The asynchronous variant accounts for node selection probabilities via pk = 2dk/|E|.
- The analysis is technically sound and clearly identifies the roles of data-dependent dispersion and network connectivity (through the spectral gap). The phantom-node construction is original and helpful for proof organization.
- Convergence is shown in expectation, with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async). The comparison bound for U2-gossip is sharpened and highlights its √n disadvantage due to lack of averaging.
- On the empirical side, the experiments are consistent with the theory and cover different network topologies. However, measurements focus on relative error vs iterations; message complexity, wall-clock time under asynchrony, and sensitivity to graph spectral gap could be quantified more explicitly.

Clarity:
- The paper is overall well-organized. The algorithm descriptions are clear and concise.
- The phantom network is used only for analysis, but this could be emphasized earlier to avoid the impression of quadratic storage.
- Some notation is heavy (e.g., block matrices M1/M2/M3, Wα(G)), and the intuition behind the constants and their dependence on the Laplacian could be briefly summarized in the main text (with detailed algebra in the supplement).
- In the asynchronous section, assumptions about nodes knowing pk (requiring degrees and |E|) should be stated upfront and discussed in terms of realism and alternatives.

Originality:
- Extending gossip to degree-two U-statistics is nontrivial. The combination of data propagation and estimate averaging with a single auxiliary sample per node, plus the phantom-node proof technique, appears novel relative to U2-gossip.
- The asynchronous algorithm with unbiased normalization and the explicit rate bounds with spectral-gap dependence add value beyond prior work.

Significance:
- Many important statistics (AUC, variance, Gini difference, within-cluster scatter) are U-statistics, so enabling their decentralized estimation is significant for sensor networks and federated or peer-to-peer analytics.
- The improved communication/memory footprint and faster convergence compared to U2-gossip are practically meaningful.
- The results may serve as a foundation for decentralized optimization of pairwise objectives.

Pros:
- Clear problem formulation for non-separable U-statistics in distributed settings.
- Simple, practical algorithms with low memory/communication overhead.
- Solid theoretical analysis with explicit, interpretable constants.
- Meaningful comparison to the main prior method (U2-gossip) and consistent empirical gains.
- Both synchronous and asynchronous variants are developed.

Cons:
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation; high-probability or variance bounds are not provided.
- Experimental evaluation could report message complexity and runtime, not just iterations, and include larger graphs and higher dimensions.
- Assumptions in the asynchronous algorithm (knowledge of degrees and |E|) may be strong in some settings; robustness to mis-specified pk is not studied.
- The study is limited to degree-two U-statistics; discussion on extending to higher-order U-statistics is brief.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide concentration results (e.g., high-probability bounds) or variance decay analysis for the estimates across nodes.
- Report communication and memory costs per algorithm explicitly, and compare time-to-accuracy under asynchronous event-driven simulations.
- Explore robustness to approximate pk and scenarios with time-varying or directed graphs.
- Add baselines such as: (i) naive random-pair sampling without consensus averaging; (ii) push-sum or accelerated consensus adaptations; (iii) geographic/accelerated gossip when location or conductance information is available.
- Discuss extension to higher-order U-statistics (k > 2) and non-symmetric kernels, and clarify the role of H(X, X) = 0.

Recommendation:
This is a solid, well-motivated contribution that advances decentralized computation for non-separable statistics with both theoretical and empirical support. Despite some missing empirical metrics and concentration results, the paper meets NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the experimental reporting and clarifying asynchronous assumptions.
