Summary
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree‑2 U‑statistics over networks, where each node holds a single observation and communication is restricted to pairwise gossip. U‑statistics capture important global quantities such as variance, AUC, Gini mean difference, Kendall tau, and within‑cluster scatter. The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms: a synchronous version (GoSta‑sync) and an asynchronous version (GoSta‑async). Each node maintains a running estimate and an auxiliary observation; whenever two neighboring nodes communicate, they average their estimates and swap their auxiliary observations. This makes the raw data perform coupled random walks while estimates are averaged. Using a “phantom nodes” construction to separate averaging from data propagation, the paper proves convergence in expectation: O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O((log t)/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit dependence on the network’s spectral gap and problem-dependent data dispersion terms. Compared to U2‑gossip (Pelckmans and Suykens), the proposed methods reduce memory and communication per iteration and achieve faster practical convergence. Experiments on AUC estimation and within‑cluster scatter over several network topologies support the claims.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on randomized gossip for averaging (e.g., Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.), geographic/accelerated gossip (Dimakis et al.; Li and Dai), and prior attempts to compute non‑separable statistics via gossip (U2‑gossip). The analysis highlights spectral gap dependence consistent with consensus literature (e.g., Chung; Bollobás). The “phantom nodes” view connects to lifted Markov chains and consensus acceleration (Jung, Shah, and Shin; Chen, Lovász, and Pak; Li and Dai’s LADA), which merits more explicit discussion.

Quality
- Theory: The convergence analysis by decoupling averaging and data propagation via a graph lifting is elegant. Rates and constants reflect both network connectivity (spectral gap) and data difficulty. However, guarantees are in expectation; high‑probability or almost‑sure convergence statements and variance bounds would strengthen the results. The asynchronous rate is weaker; a discussion of whether this is an artifact of analysis would help.
- Correctness: I did not verify the supplementary proofs in detail. The results are plausible and standard tools appear to be applied appropriately.
- Empirics: Experiments on real data and several graph families are appropriate and show consistent gains over U2‑gossip. Including stronger baselines (e.g., flooding) and more detailed communication/memory cost comparisons would improve the evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but several points need clarification:
- Make explicit that auxiliary observations execute random walks, which motivates the phantom‑graph analysis.
- Precisely define the edge selection distribution. If edges are sampled uniformly, say so; if the model is “node wakes, picks neighbor,” give the resulting edge distribution and its relation to P being stochastic/bi‑stochastic.
- State assumptions on the data sequence clearly (adversarial/ arbitrary vs. random), and specify whether results hold uniformly over data or under a probabilistic model.
- Minor wording issues are listed below.

Originality
Extending gossip to U‑statistics with a single auxiliary sample per node and combining averaging with data propagation is a neat, simple idea. The phantom‑node analysis for coupled data propagation and averaging is a useful contribution. The asynchronous variant with local clocks is also valuable. While related to lifted Markov chains, the specific construction for U‑statistics appears novel.

Significance
U‑statistics are widely used; providing decentralized estimators with explicit network‑dependent rates is practically relevant for sensor and peer‑to‑peer settings. The methods reduce memory/communication relative to U2‑gossip and outperform it empirically. If further connected to acceleration techniques and equipped with stronger concentration guarantees, the approach could see broad use.

Detailed comments and suggestions
1) Algorithmic mechanism: Emphasize earlier that swapping auxiliary observations makes each datum perform a random walk. This intuition directly motivates the phantom‑graph analysis and helps the reader understand why averaging plus swapping works.

2) Related work: The survey by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) provides a broader context for gossip beyond averaging, including geographic and accelerated variants. Please position your contribution relative to that body of work, not just the subset cited.

3) Edge selection: Clarify the induced edge distribution under the “node wakes and contacts a neighbor” model versus uniform edge sampling. If using pk = 2dk/|E|, define it up front and ensure consistency between the model and analysis.

4) Alternative baseline (flooding): Discuss and, if possible, evaluate a memory‑rich baseline where nodes flood and store tagged observations, computing the U‑statistic on the subset they have received. On expanders, flooding can be fast; this comparison would help quantify the benefits of averaging when memory is constrained and contextualize the O(1/t) rate.

5) Lifting/acceleration: Your phantom construction parallels lifted Markov chains used to speed mixing and consensus (Jung, Shah, and Shin; Chen, Lovász, and Pak; Li and Dai’s LADA). Please discuss these connections and whether analogous acceleration can further improve your rates.

6) Assumptions and guarantees: Specify whether guarantees hold for all fixed datasets or under a distributional assumption. If possible, add concentration bounds (e.g., variance or high‑probability) to complement in‑expectation results.

Small wording/typos
- Bipartite definition: Using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) suggests directed edges; adjust wording for undirected graphs.
- p.4: “we stress that” (instead of “let us stress out that”).
- p.4: “awakes and contacts” (instead of “awakes and contact”).
- p.6: “requires initiating” (instead of “requires to initiate”).
- p.7: “allows us to create” (instead of “allows to create”).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, implementable algorithms with clear intuition.
- Theoretical rates with explicit dependence on data difficulty and network connectivity.
- Handles both synchronous and asynchronous settings.
- Lower memory and communication than U2‑gossip; consistent empirical gains.

Cons for acceptance
- Results are in expectation; lack of concentration/high‑probability guarantees.
- Asynchronous rate is weaker; unclear if improvable.
- Related work on lifting/acceleration and broader baselines not fully covered.
- Some clarity issues around edge selection and assumptions on data.

After author feedback
The authors’ clarifications and planned revisions address several concerns. I encourage a careful integration of lifting‑based acceleration literature (especially LADA) and inclusion of baseline comparisons. With these refinements, I recommend acceptance.
