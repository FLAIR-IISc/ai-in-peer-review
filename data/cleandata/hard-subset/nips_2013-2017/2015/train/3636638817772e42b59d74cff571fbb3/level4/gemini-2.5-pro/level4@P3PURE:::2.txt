This paper proposes a decentralized, gossip-style aggregation algorithm for computing higher-order statistics over pairs of observations (U-statistics) distributed across a network. The target quantities of interest include sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients, among other aggregate measures.

Overall, this is a nice contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, the paper's impact could be strengthened by providing a more thorough connection to existing approaches and a clearer exposition of its core ideas and assumptions. This is a detailed review; due to time constraints, the correctness of the material in the supplement has not been verified.

**Major Comments**

1.  A key algorithmic mechanism involves nodes not only averaging their estimates but also swapping a secondary state variable corresponding to their initial observation. This effectively causes the initial observations to perform a (coupled) random walk on the network. Highlighting this intuition more clearly in the main text would improve readability and better motivate the analysis, which appears to rely on multiple copies of the graph.

2.  The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work on gossip algorithms for statistical computation. The literature in this area is extensive, as exemplified by the survey from Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE). Situating the present work within the context of the state-of-the-art described in such surveys would be more insightful than the current, somewhat cursory, treatment.

3.  The communication model on page 4 is not fully specified. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous; the authors should clarify the underlying probability distribution on the edges induced by the "node wakes up and contacts a neighbor" process.

4.  A natural baseline algorithm is not considered, which would be to allow more memory at each node. In such a scheme, nodes could use the same contact process to flood their initial observations throughout the network. Each node would store the observations it receives (tagged by their originating node) and compute the U-statistic locally on its available subset of data. A comparison to this approach is crucial. It would help demonstrate the advantages of the proposed averaging method, particularly in memory-constrained settings, and provide context for its performance. Without this baseline, it is difficult to judge whether the stated 1/t convergence rate is competitive, especially on well-connected graphs like expanders where a flooding approach might perform very well.

5.  The proposed construction appears closely related to "lifting" techniques used to accelerate gossip algorithms. We strongly recommend the authors discuss this connection and cite relevant work, for example:
    *   K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., 2007.
    *   W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, 2010.
    *   F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in STOC, 1999.

6.  The theoretical guarantees could be stated more precisely. Typically, the analysis of gossip algorithms provides worst-case guarantees over the initial data distribution. It is not clear from the problem statement or the theorem what assumptions are made on the data distribution and how the error bounds depend on the initial data vector.

**Minor Comments**
*   p1: The definition of a bipartite graph using `(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)` seems to imply a directed graph. Please clarify.
*   p4: "let us stress out that" should be "we stress that".
*   p4: "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts".
*   p6: "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating".
*   p7: "allows to create" should be "allows us to create".

**Comments After Author Feedback**
I find the clarifications and modifications proposed by the authors to be satisfactory, and I have raised my score from 7 to 8. I still strongly encourage the authors to integrate a detailed discussion of lifting constructions (especially LADA) into the related work section. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation should be updated to include the baseline comparisons suggested in this review.

**Conclusion**
This paper presents a nicely motivated approach for extending gossip methods to compute complex statistics in distributed settings. However, the mathematical formulation and connection to prior work are somewhat unclear in the current draft. Assuming the authors undertake a thorough revision to address the points raised in this review, I recommend accepting the paper.
