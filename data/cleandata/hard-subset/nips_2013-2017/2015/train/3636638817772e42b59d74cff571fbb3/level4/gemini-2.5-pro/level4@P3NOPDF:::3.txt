This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the distributed computation of U-statistics. The problem is well-motivated, as U-statistics are fundamental in many areas of statistics and machine learning, and their efficient computation in decentralized settings is a significant challenge. The paper is clearly written, and the proposed methods are explained in a coherent manner.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. When compared to the U2-gossip baseline on two datasets, the new methods exhibit substantially better performance, as measured by the average relative AUC error. The authors show this advantage holds across varied network topologies, which strengthens the claim of the method's robustness.

2.  **Theoretical Guarantees:** A key strength of this work is the inclusion of theoretical convergence guarantees. Providing these bounds adds significant rigor to the proposed algorithms and helps in understanding their behavior and limitations.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** The paper thoughtfully considers both synchronous and asynchronous settings, which broadens the applicability of the proposed methods to a wider range of real-world distributed systems where strict synchronization is not always feasible.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

My primary concern revolves around the practical computational cost and runtime of the proposed algorithms, which is not fully addressed in the current evaluation.

The paper presents its performance evaluation in terms of convergence with respect to the number of iterations. While the proposed method shows superior convergence in this regard, this view might be incomplete without an analysis of the per-iteration complexity. The overall utility of an algorithm depends not only on how many iterations it takes to converge but also on how long each iteration takes.

Could the authors provide a comparison of the wall-clock time per iteration for their method versus the U2-gossip baseline? Alternatively, a detailed analysis of the computational complexity (e.g., number of floating-point operations) per iteration for both methods would be highly valuable. If each iteration of the proposed algorithm is significantly more computationally expensive than an iteration of U2-gossip, the observed faster convergence in terms of iterations might not translate to faster convergence in terms of total runtime. Clarifying this point is crucial for assessing the practical utility of the work and would make the paper's contribution much stronger.
