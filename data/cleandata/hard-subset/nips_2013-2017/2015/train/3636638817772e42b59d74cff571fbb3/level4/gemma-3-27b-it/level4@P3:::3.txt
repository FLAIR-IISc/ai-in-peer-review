This paper presents new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. The motivation is clear – existing gossip algorithms struggle with U-statistics, which are crucial for representing global properties like variance and AUC. The authors address this gap with algorithms that propagate data and maintain local estimates, providing convergence rate bounds and demonstrating improvements over a prior approach (U2-gossip) through numerical experiments on both AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem and proposed solution.

The core idea of using “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem and analyze convergence is clever and well-executed. The convergence analysis, providing rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, is a significant theoretical contribution. The experimental results on real datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms compared to U2-gossip across different network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, and Watts-Strogatz). The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous versions expands the applicability of the work to a wider range of practical scenarios. 

However, a key weakness is the lack of detailed runtime analysis. While the paper demonstrates faster convergence in terms of iterations, it does not provide information on the computational cost *per* iteration for the proposed algorithms versus U2-gossip.  If each iteration of the proposed methods is significantly slower than an iteration of U2-gossip, the overall practical benefit might be diminished, especially for large networks. Providing a comparison of iteration runtime would significantly strengthen the practical implications of the results.  Additionally, the discussion of the theoretical convergence rates could be further enhanced by relating the data-dependent terms to specific characteristics of the U-statistic being estimated (e.g., the range of H(X,X')). While the authors acknowledge these terms reflect the “hardness” of the problem, a more concrete connection would be valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses an important gap in distributed estimation – efficient computation of U-statistics.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** Provides convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates clear performance improvements over a baseline algorithm on real datasets and different network topologies.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the technical details are presented in a clear and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Missing Runtime Analysis:** Lacks a comparison of iteration runtime between the proposed algorithms and U2-gossip.
*   **Limited Insight into Data-Dependent Terms:** The connection between the data-dependent terms in the convergence bounds and the difficulty of the estimation problem could be more clearly articulated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
*   The proposed algorithms offer a significant improvement over the existing state-of-the-art (U2-gossip) in terms of convergence rate.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of runtime analysis prevents a complete assessment of the practical utility of the proposed algorithms. 
*   The paper does not thoroughly explore the implications of the data-dependent terms in the convergence bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The novel approach, strong theoretical foundation, and compelling experimental results outweigh the identified weaknesses. While the missing runtime analysis is a concern, the demonstrated convergence improvements are substantial. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors address the runtime analysis in a revised version. Providing this information will further solidify the practical value of their work.
