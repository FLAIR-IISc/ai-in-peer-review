Summary
The paper studies decentralized computation of pairwise U-statistics (e.g., sample variance, correlation, and other second-order aggregates) when observations are distributed across a network. It proposes a simple gossip-style algorithm that maintains at each node both an estimate of the global statistic and a secondary state representing an initial observation. When two nodes communicate, they average their estimates and swap their secondary states. This induces random walks of the initial observations across the network and enables estimation of statistics that depend on pairs of data points. The analysis leverages this structure to establish convergence rates. Overall, the paper makes a clear and interesting contribution to extending gossip methods beyond averages.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant extension of gossip algorithms to higher-order statistics.
- Algorithm is simple, communication-efficient, and easily implementable.
- Analysis connects naturally to random-walk and lifted-graph viewpoints.
- Empirical results are promising and the presentation is generally clear.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) Clarify the core algorithmic idea. The key mechanism is not only averaging the local estimates upon contact, but also swapping a secondary state that contains an initial observation. This implies that initial observations perform (coupled) random walks over the network. Making this mechanism explicit in the main text would (i) aid intuition, and (ii) naturally motivate the analysis via multiple copies or lifted versions of the graph.

2) Strengthen the connection to prior work. There is a rich literature on gossip and decentralized computation beyond the cited references. A more thorough positioning relative to the survey by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, 2010) would be particularly helpful. For example, clarify similarities and differences from push-sum, randomized incremental methods, and token-based or random-walk-based schemes for distributed statistics.

3) Specify the edge-selection distribution. On page 4, the process “a node wakes up and contacts a neighbor” induces a specific distribution over edges. Please spell out the induced edge distribution explicitly. Phrases like “drawing a random edge of the network” are ambiguous: is the edge selected uniformly at random, proportional to node degrees, or according to an activation clock per node/edge? Because convergence rates depend on the underlying Markov chain, this detail matters.

4) Compare with a flooding/memory baseline. A natural alternative is to allow more memory per node and use the same contact process to flood each node’s observation through the network. Nodes store received observations (tagged by their origin) and compute the U-statistic over the subset they have seen. For expanders and moderate memory, flooding could yield fast mixing and rapid approximation. A discussion (and ideally experiments) comparing:
   - your averaging-with-swap method,
   - pure flooding with bounded memory per node,
   - and a hybrid (averaging plus caching a few tokens),
would clarify when averaging is beneficial and how memory constraints trade off with convergence. This would also contextualize whether the 1/t rate you report is competitive in practice.

5) Relate to lifting and accelerated consensus. Your construction is closely related to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gossip:
   - Jung, Shah, Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Allerton 2007.
   - Li, Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE Trans. Info. Theory, 2010 (LADA).
   - Chen, Lovász, Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” STOC 1999.
Please explain how your multiple-copies viewpoint and coupling compare to these lifting constructions, whether your process can be interpreted as a lift, and if known acceleration guarantees might apply or be adapted for U-statistics.

6) Clarify assumptions and guarantees. Analyses of gossip often provide worst-case guarantees over all initial data vectors, with performance depending on spectral gaps and initialization norms. The problem statement and main theorem should clearly state:
   - whether guarantees are worst-case over initial data or in expectation over a data distribution,
   - the precise error metric (e.g., mean-squared error of the estimate),
   - how constants depend on network properties (spectral gap, degree distribution) and on the initial data (e.g., variance or boundedness assumptions).
This will improve interpretability of rates and enable fair comparisons.

Experimental suggestions
- Include the flooding/memory and hybrid baselines mentioned above.
- Report dependence on network topology (paths, grids, expanders, small-world) and on degree heterogeneity.
- Provide ablations on memory per node and communication budget.
- Clarify how edge-selection is implemented in simulations to match the theoretical model.

Minor presentation issues
- Page 1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) describes a directed edge set; clarify if the graph is directed or undirected.
- Page 4: “we stress that” (instead of “let us stress out that”).
- Page 4: “awakes and contacts” (instead of “awakes and contact”).
- Page 6: “requires initiating” (instead of “requires to initiate”).
- Page 7: “allows us to create” (instead of “allows to create”).
- Please define all notation upon first use and keep consistent symbols between the main text and supplement.

Limitations of this review
This is a heavy review. I did not have time to verify the correctness of the supplemental proofs and technical lemmas.

Overall assessment
This is a nicely presented approach to computing higher-order statistics via gossip in decentralized settings such as sensor networks. The algorithmic idea is elegant, and the analysis is promising. The primary weakness is the limited connection to prior work (especially lifting/accelerated consensus) and the absence of baseline comparisons that would contextualize the reported rates and constants.

After author feedback
The authors’ clarifications and proposed revisions address several concerns. With an improved related-work section (including lifting/LADA) and added baseline experiments, the paper will be significantly stronger. I raise my preliminary score from 7 to 8 and recommend acceptance, contingent on integrating these revisions.
