This paper studies the decentralized computation of higher-order statistics over pairs of observations (U-statistics) when the observations are distributed across a network. These include sample variance, correlation and scatter coefficients, and other aggregate measures. The main contribution is a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm for computing these statistics.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the gossip literature, but the connection to existing approaches and ideas should be strengthened.

This is a heavy review. I did not have time to verify the correctness of the supplemental material.

Comments
1) Algorithmic mechanism: when two nodes exchange their estimates and average them, they also swap a secondary state variable that carries their initial observation. Consequently, the initial observations each perform (coupled) random walks over the network. Making this explicit in the main text would clarify the method and motivate the analysis via multiple copies of the graph.
2) Related work: there has been extensive prior work on gossip for computing statistics beyond the references cited. A useful snapshot from a few years ago is the Proc. IEEE survey by Dimakis et al. Positioning the present work within the context of the papers discussed there would be more informative than the current cursory treatment.
3) Protocol clarification: on page 4, please clarify the edge-selection distribution induced by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” does not specify the distribution.
4) Baseline via flooding with memory: a natural alternative is to allocate more memory at each node and use the same contact process to flood each node’s own value across the network. Nodes store received observations (tagged by their origin) and, at any time, compute the U-statistic on the subset they possess. How does this compare to the proposed method? This could show why averaging helps, or quantify the performance loss when per-node memory is limited. Without this comparison, it is not clear whether the 1/t rate is good or bad; on expanders, the flooding method may perform quite well.
5) Connections to lifting: please consider the literature on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip, which seems closely related to your construction. For example: K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., Urbana-Champaign, IL, Sep. 2007; W. Li and H. Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 56(12), 2010; F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” Proceedings of STOC, 1999, pp. 275–281.
6) Assumptions and guarantees: analyses of gossip algorithms typically provide worst-case guarantees over all initial data vectors X. This is not clear in your problem statement or theorem. What are the assumptions on the data distribution, and how does the error depend on the initial data?

Small things
- p1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies a directed graph.
- p4: “let us stress out that” -> “we stress that”
- p4: “awakes and contact” -> “awakes and contacts”
- p6: “requires to initiate” -> “requires initiating”
- p7: “allows to create” -> “allows us to create”

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK: The authors’ clarifications and revisions are helpful. I am raising my initial score from 7 to 8. They should still take a close look at lifting constructions (especially LADA) and update the related work. The experiments should also include baseline comparisons.

This is a nicely presented approach to extending gossip methods to compute more complex statistics in distributed settings such as sensor networks. The mathematical formulation and connection to prior work are still somewhat unclear. Assuming the authors revise the paper to address the points raised above, I recommend acceptance.
