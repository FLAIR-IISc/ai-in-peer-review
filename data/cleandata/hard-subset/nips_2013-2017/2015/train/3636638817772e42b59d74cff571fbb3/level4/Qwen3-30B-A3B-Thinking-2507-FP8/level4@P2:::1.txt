

This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a significant extension to the existing literature on decentralized computation. The authors address an important gap in the literature: while gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for estimating sample means, computation of U-statistics—which require averaging over pairs of observations—has received considerably less attention. The paper makes a strong contribution by proposing GoSta algorithms that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of U-statistics, with convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide a novel reformulation using "phantom nodes" to separate data propagation and averaging steps, enabling clean convergence proofs. The convergence bounds explicitly incorporate data-dependent terms reflecting estimation difficulty and network-dependent terms related to spectral gap, showing the algorithms' efficiency on well-connected networks. The comparison with U2-gossip [17] is particularly compelling, as the authors demonstrate that their approach achieves faster convergence with lower memory and communication costs, especially for high-dimensional data. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with detailed proofs provided in the supplementary material.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, a problem not adequately addressed in previous work. The authors effectively position their work against existing literature, particularly contrasting their methods with U2-gossip [17] and explaining why their approach overcomes key limitations of prior work. The authors also demonstrate the applicability of their algorithms to important machine learning problems like Area Under the Curve (AUC) estimation and within-cluster point scatter, showing the practical relevance of their theoretical contributions.

The significance of the work is evident in both the theoretical advances and practical applications. U-statistics are fundamental to many statistical analyses, and the authors successfully demonstrate how their algorithms can be applied to important machine learning problems. The experimental results on real datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC and Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter) provide strong empirical validation, showing consistent superiority of GoSta algorithms over U2-gossip across different network topologies (complete graphs, 2D grids, and Watts-Strogatz networks). The results are well-presented, with clear metrics showing both average error and variance across nodes, and the performance gap widens as network size increases, confirming the theoretical predictions.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistently used, and the supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors make an effort to clarify technical points, such as the phantom network construction, which is crucial for understanding the convergence analysis.

Regarding weaknesses, the paper could provide slightly more discussion on practical implementation considerations, particularly regarding the assumptions about network knowledge (e.g., degree information for asynchronous algorithms). However, this is a minor point given the paper's strong theoretical and empirical contributions. The authors have thoroughly addressed related work, including the important distinction between their approach and U2-gossip, and have properly cited relevant literature.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization and statistical learning. It successfully extends gossip algorithms to a broader class of problems (U-statistics), provides rigorous theoretical guarantees with practical implications, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages. The work advances the state-of-the-art in decentralized computation by addressing a fundamental limitation in existing gossip algorithms.

In terms of the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with thorough theoretical analysis and strong experimental validation.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and precise mathematical notation.
- Originality: The extension to U-statistics represents a meaningful novel contribution to the gossip algorithm literature.
- Significance: The approach addresses important statistical problems with practical applications in distributed machine learning.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a substantial theoretical contribution with clear practical relevance to a growing area of interest in distributed machine learning. The work demonstrates how to extend distributed algorithms to handle more complex statistical functions, which is increasingly important as data becomes more distributed across networks.

The work has clear potential for impact in both theoretical distributed computation and practical applications of distributed machine learning.
