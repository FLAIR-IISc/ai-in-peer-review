This paper proposes a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm to compute higher-order statistics, specifically U-statistics, measured over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. These U-statistics encompass various quantities of interest, including sample variance, correlation and scatter coefficients, and other aggregate measures.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, the authors should strengthen the connection to existing approaches and ideas to enhance the paper's clarity and impact.

I did not have the opportunity to verify the correctness of the material in the supplement.

Comments:

1. The algorithm's key innovation lies in the exchange of estimate values and averaging between nodes, accompanied by the swapping of a secondary state variable representing their initial observation. This results in the initial observations performing a random walk, albeit a coupled one. It would be beneficial to explicitly mention this in the main manuscript, as it would facilitate understanding and motivate the analysis method via the multiple copies of the graph.

2. The authors should situate their work within the broader context of gossip approaches for computing statistics, referencing works beyond those currently cited. For instance, Dimakis et al.'s Proc. IEEE paper provides a snapshot of the state of the art from a few years ago. This would be more helpful than the current manuscript's cursory explanation.

3. On page 4, it would be worth clarifying the induced distribution on edges resulting from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" does not specify the distribution.

4. An alternative approach to this problem is to allocate more memory at each node and utilize the same contact process to flood each node's value across the network. Nodes would store received observations, tagged with the node that initially possessed them. At each time step, nodes could calculate the U-statistic on the subset of data they possess. Comparing this method to the current approach would help demonstrate the benefits of averaging and the potential performance loss when memory at each node is limited. Without this comparison, it is unclear whether the rate of 1/t is good or bad. For expanders, the flooding method may be quite effective.

5. The authors should examine the work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, as it appears closely related to their construction. Notable examples include:
K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., Urbana-Champaign, IL, Sep. 2007.
W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 56(12), 2010.
F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in Proceedings of the thirty-first annual ACM symposium on Theory of computing. ACM, 1999, pp. 275-281.

6. Typically, gossip algorithm analyses make worst-case assumptions about the initial data distribution, providing guarantees over all initial data vectors X. However, this is not explicitly stated in the problem statement or theorem. The authors should clarify their assumption on the data distribution and the guarantees on the error as a function of the initial data.

Minor corrections:
- p1: The bipartite definition using (S \times T) \cup (T \times S) implies directed graphs.
- p4: "let us stress out that" should be "we stress that"
- p4: "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts"
- p6: "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating"
- p7: "allows to create" should be "allows us to create"

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK:
I think the clarifications and modifications suggested by the authors are good. I am revising my initial score of 7 up to an 8.
I still recommend that the authors take a close look at the lifting constructions (especially LADA) and modify the related work.
Furthermore, the experiments should include baseline comparisons.

This paper presents a nicely structured approach to extending gossip methods for computing more complex statistics in distributed settings such as sensor networks. The mathematical formulation and connection to prior work could be improved. Assuming the authors make a nice revision and address the comments raised in this review, I recommend accepting this paper.
