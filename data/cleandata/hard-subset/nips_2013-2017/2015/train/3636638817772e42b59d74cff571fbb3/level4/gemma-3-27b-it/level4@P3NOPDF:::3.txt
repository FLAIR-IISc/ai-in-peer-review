This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, a challenging problem with applications in machine learning and statistics. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors present results on two datasets, evaluating performance using average relative AUC error. Their proposed method significantly outperforms the U2-gossip baseline across a variety of graph structures, demonstrating its robustness and effectiveness. Furthermore, the paper provides theoretical convergence bounds, adding to the rigor of the work.

The core strength of this paper lies in its practical improvement over existing methods for distributed U-statistic computation. U2-gossip, while a prior approach, is demonstrably surpassed by the proposed algorithms, particularly in more complex network topologies. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous variants expands the applicability of the work, allowing for flexibility depending on the network environment. The theoretical analysis, guaranteeing convergence, is a valuable contribution, solidifying the reliability of the proposed algorithms.

However, a crucial aspect missing from the evaluation is a detailed analysis of computational cost. While reporting AUC error provides a measure of accuracy, it does not reveal the practical feasibility of the proposed approach. Specifically, I would like to see run-times *per iteration* for both the proposed methods (synchronous and asynchronous) and U2-gossip. Understanding the time complexity is critical. If each iteration of the proposed algorithms is substantially slower than an iteration of U2-gossip, even significant gains in AUC error may be offset by the increased computational burden, limiting the paper’s practical utility. A comparison of wall-clock time to convergence, beyond per-iteration timing, would further strengthen the evaluation.

Several minor points could be addressed to enhance clarity. While the paper states the algorithms are evaluated on "varied scenarios (graphs)," a more detailed description of the graph characteristics used in the experiments would be beneficial. Providing statistics on graph diameter, degree distribution, and clustering coefficient would allow for a better understanding of the conditions under which the proposed methods excel. Additionally, a brief discussion of the parameter sensitivity of the algorithms – how performance changes with different parameter settings – would be helpful for potential users.

Despite these minor concerns, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-designed and theoretically sound approach. The significantly improved performance over the U2-gossip baseline is compelling. Addressing the runtime concern raised above is, in my opinion, the most important step towards elevating this paper from good to excellent.



**Overall Score:** 7/10 (Strong Accept, contingent on addressing runtime concerns)

**Strengths:**

*   Clear motivation and well-written presentation.
*   Significant performance improvement over the baseline (U2-gossip).
*   Theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms are proposed.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of runtime analysis (per iteration and total).
*   Limited details on graph characteristics used in the evaluation.
*   No discussion of parameter sensitivity.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the runtime per iteration for the proposed synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, compared to U2-gossip?
*   Can you provide more details about the graph structures used in the experiments (e.g., diameter, degree distribution)?
*   How sensitive is the performance of your algorithms to the choice of hyperparameters?
