- Proposes an intuitive distributed averaging algorithm with comprehensive theoretical analysis for large-scale/resource-constrained systems.  
- Strength: Clear, practically relevant problem setting (distributed systems, sensor networks).  
- Strength: Algorithm is conceptually simple and appears easy to implement.  
- Strength: Theoretical section and proofs are thorough and carefully constructed.  
- Strength: Focus on rigorous guarantees is commendable and potentially valuable with empirical validation.  
- Weakness: Novelty is unclear; method appears closely related to randomized gossip, push-sum, Metropolis-weighted consensus, and accelerated gossip.  
- Weakness: Paper fails to convincingly differentiate the approach or demonstrate a distinct advantage.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is weak—no comparisons to baseline methods.  
- Weakness: Experiments do not explore diverse network topologies, communication models, or failure modes, so generality and scalability are unproven.  
- Weakness: Assumptions and practical constraints (asynchrony, packet loss, quantization, communication costs) are insufficiently discussed.  
- Quality: Solid theoretical work, but lack of comparative experiments limits significance in a mature area with many baselines.  
- Clarity: Writing and proofs are clear, but the paper needs more explicit positioning against classical methods (randomized gossip, Metropolis, push-sum, accelerated gossip, tree-based aggregation).  
- Suggested experiments: include baselines—randomized pairwise gossip, Metropolis consensus, push-sum, accelerated gossip, centralized aggregator, and spanning-tree/flooding.  
- Suggested metrics: convergence vs. communication rounds, total message complexity, per-node volume, wall-clock time with realistic latencies, robustness to loss/delays/asynchrony, topology sensitivity, and quantization impact.  
- Suggested ablations: vary algorithm parameters, test heterogeneous vs. homogeneous data, and simulate stragglers/partial participation.  
- Positioning/Theory: If novelty is analytical, explicitly state the theoretical improvements (tighter bounds, constants, relaxed assumptions) and provide a succinct proof roadmap.  
- Questions: What exactly is novel—mechanism, analysis, or assumptions—and under what conditions are provable improvements obtained?  
- Questions: How does the method perform with asynchrony, message loss, time-varying graphs, and what are per-iteration computational/memory costs vs. established methods?  
- Questions: Can the approach be extended to constrained averaging, privacy/noise, or quantization while preserving guarantees?  
- Overall: Important problem with strong theory but limited contribution due to weak empirical evaluation and unclear distinctiveness; recommend thorough experiments and clearer novelty claims.
