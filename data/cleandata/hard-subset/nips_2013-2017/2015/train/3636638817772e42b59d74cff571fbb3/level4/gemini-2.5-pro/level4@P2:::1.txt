This paper introduces GoSta, a new family of randomized gossip algorithms for the distributed estimation of U-statistics. While standard gossip algorithms are well-suited for computing separable functions like sums and means, they are not directly applicable to U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations. The authors correctly identify this as an important and under-explored problem, with key applications including the calculation of sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster point scatter in a decentralized setting.

The proposed method cleverly combines two iterative steps: a data propagation step, where auxiliary observations perform a random walk on the network via swaps, and an averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates with their neighbors. The authors present both synchronous and asynchronous versions of their algorithm. A key theoretical contribution is the convergence analysis, which establishes rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. This analysis, which relies on a novel reformulation using "phantom networks," improves significantly upon the O(1/√t) rate of the main prior work, U2-gossip by Pelckmans and Suykens (2009). The theoretical claims are validated by experiments on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in decentralized machine learning. Extending the powerful and lightweight gossip framework to U-statistics is a valuable and non-trivial contribution. The proposed approach is novel and provides a clear conceptual and practical improvement over existing methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful. The derivation of explicit convergence rates is a major strength, clearly linking performance to both data-dependent factors (variance of the kernel function) and network topology (the spectral gap). The "phantom network" construction used in the proofs is an elegant analytical tool. The claims of superiority over U2-gossip are well-supported by both a refined theoretical analysis of the prior work and strong empirical evidence.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** The development of a fully asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) is a significant practical advantage. This makes the method applicable to more realistic network settings where nodes lack a global clock for synchronization. The proposed algorithms are also shown to have lower memory and communication costs per iteration compared to the state-of-the-art.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the algorithms are presented cleanly, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the existing literature on gossip algorithms.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Global Information in Asynchronous Algorithm:** The asynchronous algorithm (Algorithm 2) requires each node `k` to know its probability of being picked, `pk = 2dk/|E|`. While the node's own degree (`dk`) is local information, the total number of edges in the network (`|E|`) is global information. This assumption could be made more explicit, as computing or estimating `|E|` might require a separate preliminary gossip consensus protocol, adding a slight overhead that is not discussed.

2.  **Discussion of the Logarithmic Factor:** The theoretical rate for the asynchronous algorithm (O(log t/t)) is slightly worse than the synchronous one (O(1/t)). However, the experiments in Figure 3b suggest their practical performance is very similar. It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss this gap. Is the logarithmic factor an artifact of the proof technique, or is there a fundamental reason for the slower theoretical convergence in the asynchronous setting?

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are well-executed and convincing. To further strengthen the paper, the authors could consider testing on a wider range of network topologies, particularly those with known bottlenecks or community structures, to more thoroughly probe the impact of the spectral gap on practical convergence speed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of decentralized computation and machine learning. It presents novel, efficient, and practical algorithms for an important problem, backs them with rigorous theoretical analysis, and demonstrates their superiority through solid experiments. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are mainly suggestions for further discussion and experimentation. The work is significant and is likely to be built upon by other researchers in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
