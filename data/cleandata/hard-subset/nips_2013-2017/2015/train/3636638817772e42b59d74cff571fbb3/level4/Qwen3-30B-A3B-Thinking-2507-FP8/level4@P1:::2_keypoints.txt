- Addresses efficient computation of U-statistics in distributed networks, extending gossip algorithms beyond simple averages.  
- Proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms that propagate data while maintaining local U-statistic estimates.  
- Establishes convergence rates: O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t / t) for asynchronous, with explicit dependence on data and network structure.  
- Empirical results show the proposed methods outperform the U2-gossip algorithm in convergence speed and communication efficiency.  
- Novel technical insight: use of "phantom nodes" to decouple data propagation from estimation in the convergence analysis.  
- Mathematical formulation and proofs appear sound; experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter convincingly support claims.  
- Contribution is valuable for distributed estimation by enabling more complex statistics to be computed in decentralized settings.  
- Related work section is too cursory and should better situate the paper within the broader gossip and distributed estimation literature (e.g., Dimakis et al. 2010).  
- Should clarify how the proposed algorithms differ from and improve upon foundational works in gossip algorithms.  
- Ambiguity in technical descriptions (e.g., "drawing a random edge") — need to specify selection distribution (uniform, degree-proportional, etc.).  
- Missing comparison to a natural alternative: nodes storing multiple observations and flooding data before computing local U-statistics, especially under memory constraints.  
- Limited engagement with lifting constructions literature (e.g., Jung–Shah–Shin 2007; Li–Dai 2010; Chen–Lovász–Pak 1999) which may relate to the phantom nodes approach.  
- Analysis appears to assume a worst-case over initial data distributions; the paper should explicitly state data-distribution assumptions and error-bound dependencies.  
- Minor technical/typo issues: bipartite graph edge definition implies directed edges; several phrasing and grammar fixes suggested.  
- Overall assessment: well-organized, solid theoretical and empirical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve contextualization and clarify technical points.
