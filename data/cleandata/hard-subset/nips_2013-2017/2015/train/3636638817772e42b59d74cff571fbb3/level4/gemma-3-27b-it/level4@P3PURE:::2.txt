This paper addresses the problem of computing higher-order statistics—including sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients—measured over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. These statistics are captured by U-statistics, and the paper proposes a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm to compute them.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, the authors could strengthen the paper by establishing a clearer connection to existing approaches and related work. I did not have time to verify the correctness of the material in the supplement.

Here are specific comments:

1) The key innovation in the algorithm is the exchange of both estimate values *and* the associated initial observation when two nodes communicate and average. This effectively induces a random walk on the initial observations (albeit a coupled one). Explicitly highlighting this in the manuscript would enhance clarity and motivate the analysis method leveraging multiple copies of the graph.

2) A significant body of work exists on gossip approaches for computing statistics beyond the references cited. A relevant snapshot of the state-of-the-art can be found in Dimakis et al.’s Proc. IEEE paper. Situating the current work within this broader context, potentially referencing the works discussed therein, would be more informative than the current cursory discussion.

3) On page 4, clarifying the induced distribution on edges arising from the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process would be beneficial. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” lacks sufficient specificity regarding the distribution.

4) A natural alternative approach involves increasing memory at each node and utilizing the same contact process to flood each node's value across the network. Nodes would store received observations, tagged with the originating node. At each time step, nodes could calculate the U-statistic on their local dataset. Comparing the performance of this approach to the current method—which appears to be memory-constrained—would clarify the benefits of averaging. Does the averaging provide improved performance, or is there a loss associated with the limited memory? For example, on expander graphs, the flooding method might perform well. Understanding this trade-off is crucial for evaluating the achieved rate of 1/t.

5) The authors should investigate related work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, as these techniques appear relevant to their construction. Relevant papers include: K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., Urbana-Champaign, IL, Sep. 2007; W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 56(12), 2010; and F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in Proceedings of the thirty-first annual ACM symposium on Theory of computing. ACM, 1999, pp. 275-281.

6) Analysis of gossip algorithms typically relies on a worst-case assumption regarding the initial data distribution, guaranteeing performance over all possible initial data vectors $X$. This assumption is not explicitly stated in the problem statement or the theorem statement. Clarifying the assumptions on the data distribution and the resulting guarantees on the error as a function of the initial data is essential.

Minor edits: p1: The bipartite definition using $(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)$ implies directed graphs. p4: "let us stress out that" --> "we stress that". p4: "awakes and contact" --> "awakes and contacts". p6: "requires to initiate" --> "requires initiating". p7: "allows to create" --> "allows us to create".

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK: I find the clarifications and modifications provided by the authors to be good and constructive. I am revising my initial score of 7 to an 8.

However, the authors should still carefully consider the lifting constructions (especially LADA) and revise the related work section accordingly. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation should include baseline comparisons.

This is a well-presented approach to extending gossip methods for computing more complex statistics in distributed settings like sensor networks. The mathematical formulation and connection to prior work could be more clearly articulated. Assuming the authors address the concerns raised in this review with a thorough revision, I recommend accepting this paper.
