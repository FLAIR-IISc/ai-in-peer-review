- Proposes GoSta-sync and GoSta-async randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics using one auxiliary observation per node and swap-and-average on random edges.  
- GoSta-sync converges in expectation at O(1/t); GoSta-async at O((log t)/t); convergence constants depend on data and network spectral gap via a novel phantom node construction.  
- Algorithmic improvement over U2-gossip: single auxiliary and single swap per iteration, reducing memory and communication while achieving faster convergence.  
- Experiments (AUC, within-cluster scatter) across multiple network topologies show lower error and variance than U2-gossip, matching theoretical predictions.  
- Extends gossip averaging literature from separable statistics to pairwise U-statistics, addressing a gap where decentralized methods were scarce.  
- Theory: clear, technically sound convergence guarantees with explicit data and network dependence; phantom network analysis is original and motivated.  
- Algorithms: simple, practical design; asynchronous variant removes global clock assumption and preserves convergence in expectation.  
- Experiments: strong empirical validation on real datasets and diverse network types, supporting theoretical advantages.  
- Clarity: well written and organized, with clear motivation, precise algorithm descriptions, and intuitive analysis (spectral gap effects, phantom nodes).  
- Originality: novel one-auxiliary swap-and-average mechanism and phantom node proof technique; asynchronous extension with explicit rates is new.  
- Significance: enables decentralized computation of important U-statistics with rigorous guarantees and lower resource use, likely to influence decentralized learning/statistics.  
- Pros: principled synchronous/asynchronous algorithms, interpretable convergence rates, lower memory/communication, strong empirical results, good exposition.  
- Cons: no major issues identified.  
- Overall: a solid, novel, well-analyzed, and practically relevant contribution with theoretical and empirical improvements over prior work.
