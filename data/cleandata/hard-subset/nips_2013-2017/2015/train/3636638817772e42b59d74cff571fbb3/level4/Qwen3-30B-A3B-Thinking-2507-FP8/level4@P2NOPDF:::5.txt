

The paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, establishing their theoretical convergence properties. The authors derive rigorous convergence guarantees, demonstrating that the synchronous algorithm achieves an asymptotic rate of O(1/t), while the asynchronous variant attains O(log t / t). These rates represent significant theoretical advances, as they improve upon prior gossip-based methods for U-statistics estimation in distributed settings.

The technical contributions are clearly articulated, with well-structured derivations that justify the convergence analysis. The problem domain—distributed U-statistics estimation—is of substantial interest in large-scale statistical learning and networked systems, making the proposed algorithms broadly applicable. The paper effectively positions its work within existing literature, highlighting the novelty of handling U-statistics via gossip protocols, which have not been previously addressed with such convergence guarantees.

Experimental results substantiate the theoretical claims, demonstrating clear performance advantages over competing methods across multiple datasets and network topologies. The empirical validation includes thorough comparisons showing reduced communication rounds and faster convergence, particularly for the asynchronous variant. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate metrics (e.g., estimation error, iteration count) and sufficient statistical significance, reinforcing the practical relevance of the approach.

The paper is well-organized, with logical flow from problem formulation to analysis and validation. Key definitions, assumptions, and algorithm pseudocode are presented accessibly, facilitating reproducibility. Minor improvements could include a brief discussion of computational complexity per iteration or scalability limits beyond the presented experiments, but these do not detract from the core contribution.

Overall, this work makes a meaningful contribution to distributed optimization and statistical estimation. The algorithms are theoretically sound, empirically validated, and address a non-trivial problem in the literature. The clarity of presentation and strength of results support a strong recommendation for acceptance at NIPS. The work will be valuable to researchers in distributed machine learning, networked systems, and statistical inference.
