### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel family of Gossip algorithms for the decentralized computation of U-statistics. The proposed methods offer significant improvements over the state-of-the-art, most notably the U2-Gossip algorithm. The key contributions are twofold: first, the algorithms exhibit a provably faster convergence rate, and second, they are designed to operate in more realistic asynchronous settings. The theoretical claims are substantiated by a thorough analysis and further validated through extensive experiments on two datasets, which consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over existing baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem in decentralized machine learning. To the best of my knowledge, the proposed algorithms are novel. They successfully overcome major limitations of prior work by improving convergence speed and, crucially, by extending the framework to asynchronous environments. This latter point greatly enhances the practical applicability of the methods, as true synchrony is a restrictive assumption for most real-world distributed systems.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors provide strong theoretical guarantees for their algorithms. The convergence analysis is clear and convincingly establishes the faster rates compared to the U2-Gossip algorithm. The paper also provides a compelling analysis of the algorithms' costs, highlighting their advantages in terms of both memory and communication overhead.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The theoretical contributions are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The empirical evaluation is well-designed, comparing the proposed algorithms against relevant baselines on two distinct datasets. The results clearly illustrate the practical benefits of the new methods, aligning perfectly with the theoretical claims and strengthening the paper's overall impact.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work with respect to the existing literature, and presenting their technical contributions with clarity.

**Minor Comments and Questions for Clarification**

While the paper is very strong, a few minor points could be clarified to further improve it:

*   The discussion and comparison to U2-Gossip naturally focus on U-statistics of degree 2. Could the authors elaborate on the generalizability of their analysis and algorithms to U-statistics of an arbitrary degree *k*? A brief discussion on the potential challenges or modifications required for higher-order statistics would be a valuable addition.
*   The theoretical analysis provides robust convergence guarantees. It would be interesting to know how sensitive these convergence rates are to the underlying network topology (e.g., graph sparsity, diameter, or degree distribution). A short discussion on this aspect would further strengthen the paper's practical implications.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the literature on decentralized algorithms and distributed learning. The authors propose novel algorithms for a fundamental statistical problem, backing their claims with rigorous theoretical analysis and solid empirical evidence. The work is thorough, well-presented, and successfully addresses key shortcomings of previous approaches. I believe this work will be of high interest to the NIPS community.
