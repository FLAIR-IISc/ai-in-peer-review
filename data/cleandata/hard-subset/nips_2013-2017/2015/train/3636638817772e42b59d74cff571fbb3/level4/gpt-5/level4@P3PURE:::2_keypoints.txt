- Main contribution: Proposes a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing pairwise U-statistics (variance, correlation, etc.) over networked, distributed observations.
- Overall assessment: Solid contribution but needs clearer connection to prior work and fuller experimental/analytical positioning.
- Algorithmic mechanism: Make explicit that nodes swap a secondary state (their initial observation), causing initial observations to perform coupled random walks—this clarifies the algorithm and motivates the analysis.
- Related work/context: Broaden and deepen related-work discussion (e.g., cite Dimakis et al. survey) to better situate the paper among existing gossip methods for nontrivial statistics.
- Edge selection distribution: Clarify the exact edge-selection distribution induced by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” protocol (degree-proportional vs. uniform-over-edges vs. other).
- Flooding-with-memory baseline: Compare to a baseline where nodes store/tag received observations and flood values—this would show when averaging is advantageous and how memory limits affect performance (e.g., 1/t rate vs. flooding on expanders).
- Connection to lifting constructions: Discuss and compare to lifted Markov chain/consensus acceleration work (e.g., LADA and related lifting papers) which appear closely related.
- Assumptions and guarantees: Explicitly state assumptions on initial data/distribution and provide error guarantees as functions of the initial data (clarify worst-case vs. distributional guarantees).
- Small issues — bipartite definition: Clarify whether (S × T) ∪ (T × S) intends directed or undirected bipartite edges.
- Small issues — wording/typos: Fix phrasing and typos (examples given: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”, “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”, etc.).
- After author feedback: Authors’ revisions helped; reviewer raised score from 7 to 8 but still requests deeper engagement with lifting-based methods (especially LADA) and inclusion of the baseline comparisons in experiments.
- Conclusion: Approach is clearly presented and promising for distributed higher-order statistics; recommend acceptance conditional on careful revisions addressing the points above.
