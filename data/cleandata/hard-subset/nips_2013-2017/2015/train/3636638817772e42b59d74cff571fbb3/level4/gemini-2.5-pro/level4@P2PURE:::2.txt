This paper proposes a decentralized, gossip-style averaging algorithm for computing higher-order statistics (U-statistics) over pairs of observations distributed across a network. This class of statistics includes many important quantities, such as sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, though the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough connection to existing approaches and ideas.

This review is based on a detailed reading of the main paper; due to time constraints, the supplementary material was not verified for correctness.

**Comments**

1.  The key algorithmic mechanism involves two nodes not only averaging their current estimates but also swapping a secondary state variable corresponding to their initial observation. This effectively causes the initial observations to perform coupled random walks on the network. Highlighting this perspective more explicitly in the main manuscript would clarify the algorithm's mechanics and better motivate the chosen analysis method, which relies on multiple copies of the graph.

2.  The literature on gossip algorithms for statistical computation is extensive, and the paper could better position its contribution within this broader context. A comprehensive survey, such as the one by Dimakis et al. in Proceedings of the IEEE, provides a snapshot of the state of the art. Situating the current work relative to the methods discussed therein would offer more context than the current, somewhat cursory, review of related work.

3.  On page 4, the communication model is described as a node waking up and contacting a neighbor. This process is said to induce a distribution on edges, summarized as "drawing a random edge of the network." This phrasing is ambiguous; the authors should clarify the precise probability distribution over the edges that this process generates.

4.  A natural alternative approach exists for this problem. One could allow each node more memory and use the same contact process for each node to flood its initial value across the network. In this scheme, nodes would store all received observations, tagged by their originating node. At any time, a node could compute the U-statistic on the subset of data it has received. A comparison to this baseline method is needed. Such a comparison would help contextualize the performance of the proposed algorithm—for instance, clarifying whether a 1/t convergence rate is strong or weak—and would illuminate the trade-offs between memory constraints and convergence speed. For certain graph topologies, such as expanders, this flooding approach may be highly effective.

5.  The proposed construction appears closely related to "lifting" techniques used to accelerate gossip algorithms. The authors should review and discuss this line of work, as it seems highly relevant. See, for example:
    *   K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., 2007.
    *   W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 56(12), 2010.
    *   F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in STOC, 1999.

6.  The analysis of gossip algorithms typically provides worst-case guarantees over the initial distribution of data across the nodes. It is unclear from the problem formulation and theorem statements what assumptions are made about the initial data in this paper. The authors should explicitly state their assumptions and clarify how the error guarantees depend on the initial data vector $X$.

**Minor Comments**
*   p1: The definition of a bipartite graph using $(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)$ implies a directed graph, which may be unintentional.
*   p4: "let us stress out that" should be "we stress that".
*   p4: "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts".
*   p6: "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating".
*   p7: "allows to create" should be "allows us to create".

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment**
The clarifications and modifications proposed by the authors in their response are excellent, and I have raised my score from 7 to 8.

However, I maintain that the authors should more thoroughly integrate the literature on lifting constructions (especially LADA) into the related work section. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation should be updated to include the baseline comparisons suggested in this review.

**Final Recommendation**
This paper presents a nicely motivated approach for extending gossip methods to compute more complex statistics in distributed settings like sensor networks. The initial submission was somewhat unclear in its mathematical formulation and its connection to prior work. Assuming the authors make a thorough revision addressing the points raised in this review and their rebuttal, I recommend accepting this paper.
