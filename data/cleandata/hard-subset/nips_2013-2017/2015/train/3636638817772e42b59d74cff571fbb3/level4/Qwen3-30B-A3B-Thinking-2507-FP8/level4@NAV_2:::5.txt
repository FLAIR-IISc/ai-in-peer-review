

The paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing an important gap in the literature. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for decentralized averaging of sample means [19, 18, 5, 2, 10, 15], this work extends these techniques to handle U-statistics, which require pairwise averaging and are essential for important statistical measures including Area Under the Curve (AUC), empirical variance, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter. The authors propose "GoSta" algorithms that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit data and network-dependent terms. These results improve upon the previously proposed U2-gossip algorithm [17], which requires higher memory and communication costs and lacks asynchrony.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous convergence proofs using a novel "phantom node" reformulation of the problem, carefully addressing both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The quality of the work is evident in the thorough comparison to U2-gossip, where the authors provide refined convergence rate analysis showing GoSta's advantages, particularly the absence of the √n factor that plagues U2-gossip's convergence. The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-organized sections, precise mathematical notation, and clear algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2). The paper also properly relates to previous work by acknowledging U2-gossip [17] as the only prior attempt at this problem while highlighting its limitations.

The originality of the work is significant, as it addresses a previously unexplored area in gossip algorithms for distributed statistics. The phantom node technique for analysis represents a novel approach that enables the convergence proofs, and the algorithms themselves are the first to efficiently compute U-statistics in a distributed setting. The significance of the results is substantial, as U-statistics are widely used in machine learning applications, and the proposed algorithms offer practical advantages in memory, communication, and convergence rate compared to existing methods.

Strengths: The paper successfully addresses an important gap in distributed estimation with theoretically sound algorithms that demonstrate clear empirical advantages. The convergence analysis is comprehensive, and the experimental results on real datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over U2-gossip across different network topologies. The authors are transparent about their contributions relative to prior work.

Weaknesses: While the phantom node technique is elegant for the analysis, it might be challenging for some readers to grasp without the supplementary material. The paper could provide more discussion about how to handle cases where nodes contain multiple observations (though this is noted as future work). The experiments, while well-conducted, are limited to two specific applications (AUC and within-cluster point scatter).

Arguments for acceptance: The work addresses a meaningful problem with significant practical implications for distributed machine learning. The algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated with clear advantages over prior work. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and makes a substantive contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance: The phantom node analysis, while clever, may require additional explanation for broader accessibility. The scope of the experimental validation could be expanded to other U-statistics applications to further demonstrate the generality of the approach.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed algorithms for statistical estimation, with clear theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods.
