This paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networks, an area less explored than distributed mean estimation despite its importance for characterizing global statistical properties. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms that simultaneously propagate data and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic. They establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively, with terms dependent on both data characteristics and network topology. Numerical experiments demonstrate performance exceeding a previously introduced approach.

The core idea of the proposed algorithms is intuitive: combine data propagation with averaging of local estimates. The authors' approach of reformulating the problem using “phantom nodes” appears clever and allows for a rigorous analysis. The convergence rate analysis is comprehensive, and the inclusion of data- and network-dependent terms is a strength, indicating the algorithms will perform better on well-connected networks and for less challenging estimation problems. The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical framework.

However, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to relevant baselines. While the authors mention a previous work [17], the experimental evaluation would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons with simpler, intuitive baselines—such as straightforward averaging schemes or variations on existing gossip algorithms adapted to the U-statistic problem. This would provide a clearer understanding of the gains achieved by the proposed approach.  Further, the phantom node construction, while providing analytical clarity, could benefit from a more intuitive explanation for readers unfamiliar with this technique. While the authors mention extending their results to nodes holding multiple observations, elaborating on the implications and potential performance trade-offs could be valuable. Finally, the discussion of the asynchronous setting and its convergence rate could be further clarified with respect to the practical considerations of implementation and the impact of clock modeling assumptions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant and under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
*   The proposed algorithms are well-motivated and theoretically sound, with rigorous convergence analysis.
*   The inclusion of data- and network-dependent terms in the convergence bounds demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the problem.
*   Numerical results support the theoretical findings and show improvement over an existing approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited by the lack of comparison to a wider range of baseline methods.
*   The phantom node construction could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.
*   The discussion of the asynchronous setting could be more detailed and practical.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the theoretical results and the novelty of the approach warrant consideration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly the inclusion of baseline comparisons in the experimental section.
