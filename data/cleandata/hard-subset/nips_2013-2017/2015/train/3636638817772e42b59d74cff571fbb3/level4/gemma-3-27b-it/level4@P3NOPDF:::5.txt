This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, and provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both. The authors demonstrate that the synchronous algorithm achieves a convergence rate of 1/t, while the asynchronous variant converges at a rate of log(t)/t. This work addresses an important problem in distributed machine learning and statistics, where efficient and scalable computation of U-statistics is frequently required.

The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the theoretical derivations supporting the proposed algorithms. The authors effectively connect the algorithms to existing gossip-based methods while highlighting their specific adaptations for U-statistic estimation. The presentation of the convergence rates is concise and easy to follow.  The experimental results provide compelling evidence of the algorithms’ performance, demonstrating advantages over existing approaches in terms of both convergence speed and accuracy. 

The core contribution lies in the design and analysis of these gossip algorithms tailored for U-statistics. Existing distributed U-statistic estimation often relies on parameter server architectures or synchronous averaging, which can suffer from communication bottlenecks or sensitivity to stragglers. Gossip algorithms offer a potentially more robust and scalable alternative, and this paper makes a valuable contribution by providing rigorously analyzed algorithms in this space. The derived convergence rates are particularly noteworthy; a 1/t rate for the synchronous algorithm is competitive, and the log(t)/t rate for the asynchronous version is a strong result given the challenges inherent in asynchronous computation.

Several aspects of the paper are particularly strong. The theoretical analysis appears sound, and the derivation of the convergence rates is clearly explained. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the comparisons to baseline methods are fair and informative. The authors appropriately choose relevant U-statistic examples for evaluation, showcasing the algorithms’ utility in practical scenarios. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous variants broadens the applicability of the work, catering to different network and computational constraints.

However, a few areas could benefit from further discussion. While the paper demonstrates performance advantages, the practical impact of the logarithmic factor in the asynchronous convergence rate (log(t)/t) is not fully explored. A more detailed analysis of the constant factors hidden within the asymptotic notation could provide valuable insights into the algorithm’s performance in realistic settings.  

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis. For example, the authors assume knowledge of the U-statistic’s underlying function.  How sensitive are the algorithms to inaccuracies or approximations in this function?  Addressing such questions would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

Finally, it would be valuable to briefly discuss the limitations of the approach. Are there specific types of U-statistics for which these algorithms perform poorly?  Acknowledging these limitations would demonstrate a balanced and thorough understanding of the method.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the area of distributed U-statistic estimation. The proposed algorithms are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While a few minor points could be addressed, the paper is of high quality and is suitable for acceptance to NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding the practical impact of the logarithmic factor, sensitivity to assumptions, and limitations of the approach.
