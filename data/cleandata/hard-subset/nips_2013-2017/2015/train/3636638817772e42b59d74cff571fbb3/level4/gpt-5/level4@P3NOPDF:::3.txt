Summary
The paper tackles the problem of computing U-statistics in decentralized settings via randomized gossip. It proposes both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms and provides convergence guarantees. Empirically, the method is evaluated on two datasets using average relative AUC error and achieves substantially better accuracy than the U2-gossip baseline across several graph topologies.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and writing; the problem is important for decentralized learning where pairwise statistics are central (e.g., AUC).
- Proposes both synchronous and asynchronous variants, which broadens practical applicability.
- Provides convergence bounds, which is valuable given the nontrivial structure of U-statistics and network effects.
- Empirical results indicate consistent gains over U2-gossip across multiple graph structures, suggesting robustness to topology.

Weaknesses and questions
- Lack of runtime and communication analysis. The paper reports error vs. iterations but does not report per-iteration wall-clock time, computational cost, or communication volume. If each iteration of the proposed method is significantly more expensive than U2-gossip, the practical utility may be limited despite better per-iteration accuracy.
- Limited experimental scope. Only two datasets and a single metric (relative AUC error) are shown. It would be helpful to validate on additional datasets and tasks where U-statistics are relevant, and to include error bars across random seeds and graph samples.
- Missing baselines and ablations. Beyond U2-gossip, comparisons to other decentralized aggregation schemes (e.g., push-sum variants, different mixing strategies, or mini-batch approximations of U-statistics) would help position the contribution. An ablation analyzing the impact of the key design choices in the proposed method would clarify where the gains come from.
- Insufficient characterization of asynchrony. The asynchronous setting is important in practice; please clarify the clock model (e.g., Poisson), whether delays and message drops are permitted, and how these factors affect the bounds and empirical performance.
- Assumptions and tightness of bounds. It would help to state clearly the assumptions on the kernel function, boundedness, independence, and connectivity/mixing of the graph. Are the rates dimension-free and how do constants scale with network size, spectral gap, and the variance of the U-statistic? Any evidence on the tightness of the bounds (e.g., lower bounds or empirical scaling) would be useful.
- Memory/computation per node. Computing U-statistics naively is quadratic. What is the per-update computational complexity and memory footprint at each node? Do nodes store a buffer of pairs, and how is sampling over pairs implemented to remain unbiased?

Requests for additional results (important for assessing practical utility)
- Report per-iteration wall-clock time and total time to reach a target accuracy (e.g., 1% relative AUC error), for both the proposed method and U2-gossip. Include hardware details and implementation notes.
- Report communication cost: messages per iteration, bytes per message, and total communication to reach a target error.
- Provide convergence plots vs. wall-clock time in addition to iterations.
- Sensitivity analyses: effect of network size, degree/spectral gap, and asynchrony (delay distributions, message drops) on convergence speed and final accuracy.
- Compare against a centralized estimator to quantify absolute bias/variance and to calibrate the error scale.
- Evaluate on at least one additional dataset and include error bars over multiple random graph initializations.

Clarity and presentation
- Please provide explicit pseudocode for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, with a clear statement of per-step computational and communication costs.
- Define the graph models used (e.g., Erdos–Rényi, ring, grid) and report their sizes and spectral properties to contextualize the results.
- Unify terminology and notation (e.g., “synchronous/asynchronous,” “U2-gossip”) and fix minor grammar (“results are,” “synchronous,” etc.).

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated and technically sound paper addressing an important and underexplored problem: decentralized computation of U-statistics. The proposed algorithms and convergence guarantees are valuable, and the empirical results are promising across multiple graph structures. However, the current experimental section does not sufficiently characterize runtime and communication overhead, which is crucial for judging practical relevance. I would be inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors can provide comprehensive runtime/communication analyses, broaden the empirical evaluation, and clarify the assumptions and costs of the algorithms.
