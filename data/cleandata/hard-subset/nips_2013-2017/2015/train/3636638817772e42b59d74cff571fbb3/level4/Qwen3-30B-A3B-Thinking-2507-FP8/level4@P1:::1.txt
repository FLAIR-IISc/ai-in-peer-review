

This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) for distributed computation of U-statistics in networked systems. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for separable statistics like sample means, their application to U-statistics—which require pairwise interactions between data points—has received far less attention. The paper builds on Pelckmans and Suykens' (2009) U2-gossip algorithm but significantly improves upon it in key aspects.

The main contribution is a framework for computing U-statistics that combines data propagation with local averaging. The authors introduce an elegant "phantom node" technique to reformulate the problem, enabling them to establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous setting and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous setting. These rates surpass U2-gossip's O(1/t) rate, with the authors demonstrating that their approach achieves faster convergence while using less memory and communication per iteration (each node maintains a single auxiliary observation versus two in U2-gossip).

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical and empirical results. The convergence analysis is technically sound, with clear dependencies on both data properties (through norm terms) and network topology (via the spectral gap of the graph). The proofs in the supplementary material appear rigorous. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with experiments on two real-world applications—Area Under the Curve (AUC) and within-cluster point scatter—across three network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, and Watts-Strogatz). Results consistently show GoSta algorithms outperform U2-gossip in convergence speed and stability across nodes.

The paper excels in quality, with well-supported claims that carefully address both strengths (faster convergence, lower memory/communication costs) and weaknesses of the approach. Clarity is excellent—the paper is well-organized, progressing logically from problem formulation to algorithm design, analysis, and experiments. The phantom node technique is particularly well-explained, making a complex analysis accessible.

Originality is strong: the problem of distributed U-statistics computation is novel in gossip literature, and the proposed algorithms represent a significant advance over the only prior work. The authors clearly position their work against related literature, including foundational gossip papers (Boyd et al., 2006) and U-statistics literature (Lee, 1989).

Significance is high: U-statistics are widely used in machine learning for critical metrics like AUC, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster scatter. The proposed algorithms make these computations feasible in distributed settings where centralized computation is impractical, with potential applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale data analysis. The empirical results confirm that theoretical advantages translate to practical performance gains.

Strengths:
- Novel approach to distributed U-statistics computation
- Superior theoretical convergence rates compared to prior work
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and network topologies
- Clear exposition of complex theoretical concepts
- Practical improvements in memory and communication efficiency

Weaknesses:
- The phantom node technique, while elegant, may require careful reading for full comprehension
- The asynchronous analysis, while rigorous, is more complex than the synchronous case
- The paper could better elaborate on practical implications of spectral gap dependence

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to distributed optimization and statistical learning. The proposed algorithms are both theoretically sound and practically useful, addressing an important gap in the literature. The work is well-positioned within the NIPS community, with clear relevance to current trends in distributed machine learning. The quality, clarity, originality, and significance all meet the highest standards for NIPS.

I recommend acceptance.
