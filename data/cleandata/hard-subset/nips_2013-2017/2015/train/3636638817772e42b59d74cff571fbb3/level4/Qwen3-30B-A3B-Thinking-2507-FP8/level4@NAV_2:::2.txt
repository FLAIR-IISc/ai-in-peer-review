

This paper extends gossip algorithms to compute U-statistics in distributed networks, addressing an important gap in the literature where most gossip-based approaches focus on averages rather than statistics requiring pairwise observations. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms that maintain local estimates of U-statistics while propagating data across the network. They establish convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively, with explicit dependence on data and network properties. The work is significant as U-statistics include important measures like Area Under the Curve (AUC), empirical variance, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter, which are essential for distributed statistical analysis.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending gossip algorithms to handle more complex statistics. The theoretical analysis is sound, with convergence bounds that properly reflect both data-dependent and network-dependent factors. The experimental results on real datasets (AUC estimation with SVMguide3 and within-cluster point scatter with Wine Quality) demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over the U2-gossip algorithm from [17], particularly in terms of convergence speed and communication efficiency.

However, the paper would benefit from a stronger connection to the broader literature on gossip algorithms. While the authors cite some relevant work, they do not adequately situate their contribution within the extensive literature on gossip algorithms for distributed computation. The Dimakis et al. IEEE Transactions on Information Theory paper provides an excellent overview of the state-of-the-art in gossip-based distributed computation, and situating this work within that context would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should also address the lifting constructions for gossip algorithms (e.g., LADA [Li & Dai, 2010] and "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains" [Jung et al., 2007]), which are highly relevant to their phantom network approach.

Specifically, the manuscript would be improved by:
1) Clarifying the distribution over edges in the algorithm description (the phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous without specifying the probability distribution).
2) Better explaining the core insight that the initial observations perform coupled random walks across the network, which motivates the phantom network analysis.
3) Providing a more detailed comparison with the flooding approach (where nodes store and propagate all observations they receive), which would help establish why the proposed averaging approach is advantageous when memory is limited.
4) Explicitly stating the assumptions on the data distribution in the convergence analysis (the paper currently presents worst-case guarantees, but this should be clearly specified).
5) Fixing minor grammatical issues (e.g., "stresses out" → "stresses," "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts," "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating").

The paper is well-written overall, with a clear structure and appropriate mathematical notation. The experimental results are convincing and support the theoretical claims. While the analysis in the supplementary material appears technically sound, the main manuscript would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work to better establish the novelty and significance of the contribution.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the literature on distributed estimation. With the suggested revisions addressing the connections to prior work and clarifying key aspects of the methodology, the paper would be a valuable addition to the conference proceedings. The significance of the work for distributed systems applications is clear, and the technical quality is high. I recommend acceptance after addressing the minor concerns outlined above.
