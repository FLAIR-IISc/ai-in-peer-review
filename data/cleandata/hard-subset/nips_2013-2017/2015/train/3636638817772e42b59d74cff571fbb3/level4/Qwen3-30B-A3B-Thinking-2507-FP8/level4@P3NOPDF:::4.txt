

This paper addresses the distributed computation of aggregate statistics (e.g., averages), a problem of practical relevance in large-scale systems. The core algorithm proposed is straightforward and well-motivated, leveraging a simple iterative scheme to converge to the global average. However, the work’s primary contribution remains unclear. While the theoretical analysis is thorough and the proofs are technically sound, the paper fails to articulate how the proposed method advances beyond existing approaches. The algorithmic design resembles known techniques in distributed optimization (e.g., consensus-based averaging), and the absence of a clear novelty statement or comparison to foundational methods weakens the contribution. For instance, the approach does not differentiate itself from standard randomized gossip protocols or periodic synchronization schemes in terms of communication complexity, convergence rate, or robustness to network dynamics.

The most significant weakness lies in the empirical evaluation. The authors present results only on synthetic datasets without comparing against any baselines. This omission is critical: without benchmarking against reasonable alternatives (e.g., randomized sampling, periodic synchronization, or existing gossip algorithms), it is impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers tangible improvements. Several intuitive baselines exist that could be easily implemented and compared. For example, a baseline using uniform random node selection for communication would provide a meaningful reference point for evaluating the efficiency gains claimed. The paper’s assertion that the method "significantly reduces communication overhead" lacks empirical support without such comparisons. The current evaluation merely demonstrates convergence on a single dataset, failing to establish the method’s relative performance or scalability advantages.

Furthermore, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail to reproduce or critically assess the results. Key parameters (e.g., network size, topology, noise levels) are not fully specified, and the reported metrics (e.g., "number of communication rounds") do not contextualize the method’s efficiency relative to alternatives. For instance, if the method requires 10% fewer rounds than a baseline but incurs higher per-round communication costs, the net benefit is unclear. Without baselines, the paper cannot substantiate its claims about practical utility. This is particularly problematic for NIPS, where empirical rigor is paramount. The absence of comparative experiments undermines the paper’s core argument and leaves the reader unable to gauge the method’s real-world relevance.

The theoretical contribution is also underdeveloped. While the convergence proofs are comprehensive, they rely on standard assumptions (e.g., doubly stochastic weights, connected networks) without addressing practical limitations. For example, the analysis does not consider node failures, heterogeneous communication delays, or asynchronous updates—common challenges in distributed systems. A more compelling contribution would have integrated these considerations or provided a tighter theoretical bound that directly motivates the algorithm’s design.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) clearly articulate the novelty by contrasting their approach with key prior work (e.g., [cite relevant gossip algorithms]); (2) include rigorous comparisons against at least two strong baselines (e.g., randomized gossip and periodic synchronization) across diverse network topologies and noise levels; and (3) provide detailed experimental setup and metrics that enable direct performance assessment. Without these, the work remains descriptive rather than contributive.

In summary, while the problem addressed is relevant and the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper’s lack of novelty justification and empirical baselines constitutes a major weakness. The current evaluation fails to demonstrate the method’s superiority, making it difficult to assess the work’s impact. The authors are encouraged to address these gaps before the paper can be considered for publication. Given the absence of comparative evidence and unclear differentiation from existing methods, I recommend rejection.
