The paper presents new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the efficient computation of U-statistics in decentralized networks. The authors derive convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, where t is the number of iterations. The paper is well-organized and presents experimental results demonstrating the algorithms’ performance improvements over existing approaches. The problem addressed – decentralized estimation of U-statistics – is an important one, with applications in various areas such as machine learning and data mining, and the proposed techniques represent a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper effectively builds upon the existing literature on gossip algorithms, recognizing the limitations of previous methods when applied to U-statistics (which require averaging over pairs of observations rather than single values). The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates it within the broader context of decentralized computation. The technical contributions, namely the novel algorithms and their convergence analysis, are well-defined and presented. The use of "phantom nodes" to facilitate the analysis is a clever approach.  

Strengths of the paper include:

* **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, and the derivation of the convergence rates is well-justified. The use of spectral graph theory to relate convergence speed to network connectivity is a strong point.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The notation is clearly defined, and the algorithms are described in a straightforward manner.
* **Originality:** Extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is a novel contribution. The proposed algorithms and analysis differ significantly from previous work in the area.
* **Significance:** U-statistics are fundamental to many statistical analyses, and efficient decentralized computation of these statistics could have a significant impact on applications involving large, distributed datasets. The empirical results provide further evidence of the practical value of the proposed algorithms.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* **Complexity of Analysis:** While rigorous, the analysis relying on the "phantom node" construction adds complexity, which might make it difficult for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory to fully grasp. A more intuitive explanation of this technique would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to U2-gossip [17] is strong, but a broader comparison to other relevant distributed estimation techniques, even if they are not directly applicable to U-statistics, might further strengthen the paper's impact.
* **Asynchronous Algorithm Details:** The description of the asynchronous algorithm and its analysis are less detailed than those of the synchronous algorithm. Further elaboration on the handling of asynchronous updates and the justification of the convergence rate would be appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and technically sound solution.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
* The experimental results demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The complexity of the analysis may limit the accessibility of the paper to a wider audience.
* The comparison to existing work could be more comprehensive.
* The asynchronous algorithm’s analysis could benefit from further detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The novelty of the approach, the rigor of the analysis, and the promising experimental results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above regarding analysis clarity and comparison breadth. Specifically, providing a more intuitive explanation of the phantom node approach and expanding the experimental evaluation with a more comprehensive set of baselines would further strengthen the paper.
