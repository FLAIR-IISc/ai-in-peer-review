

This paper addresses the problem of efficiently computing U-statistics in distributed networks, extending gossip algorithms beyond simple averages to handle more complex statistics that depend on pairs of observations. U-statistics, such as the sample variance, Area Under the Curve (AUC), Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter, are important for describing global properties of statistical populations but have received less attention in decentralized settings. The authors propose new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms that maintain local estimates of U-statistics while simultaneously propagating data across the network. They establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit dependencies on data characteristics and network structure. The paper demonstrates through experiments that their approach outperforms the previously proposed U2-gossip algorithm in both convergence speed and communication efficiency.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on distributed estimation, particularly extending gossip algorithms to handle more complex statistics. The authors' use of "phantom nodes" for convergence analysis is a novel technical insight that helps decouple data propagation from estimation, making the analysis more transparent. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter provide convincing empirical evidence for the practical advantages of their approach.

However, the paper could significantly improve its connection to the broader literature on gossip algorithms. The related work section is somewhat cursory and fails to situate the current contribution within the extensive body of work on distributed estimation. A more thorough comparison with key papers such as Dimakis et al.'s "Gossip Algorithms for Distributed Signal Processing" (Proceedings of the IEEE, 2010) would help contextualize the novelty and significance of the proposed algorithms. The authors should explicitly address how their work differs from and improves upon these foundational contributions.

The technical presentation would benefit from greater clarity in several key areas. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" on page 4 is ambiguous—it should specify whether the edge is selected uniformly at random or according to some other distribution (e.g., proportional to node degrees). The paper would be stronger if it explicitly compared their approach to a natural alternative: allowing nodes to store multiple observations and flood their data across the network, then computing U-statistics on local subsets. This comparison would help demonstrate why the authors' averaging approach is advantageous, particularly when memory is limited at each node.

I also note that the paper could better engage with the literature on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms. Work by Jung, Shah, and Shin (Allerton 2007), Li and Dai (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, 2010), and Chen, Lovász, and Pak (STOC 1999) on "lifting" Markov chains to accelerate mixing might provide valuable context for the authors' "phantom nodes" approach. The authors should consider incorporating these references and discussing how their construction relates to this line of work.

The analysis makes a worst-case assumption over initial data distributions, but this is not clearly stated in the problem statement. The paper should explicitly clarify the assumptions about data distribution and the nature of the error bounds as a function of initial data.

Minor issues that should be addressed include:
- The bipartite graph definition on page 1 uses (S × T) ∪ (T × S), which implies directed edges rather than the standard undirected definition
- "Let us stress out that" should be "We stress that"
- "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts"
- "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating"
- "allows to create" should be "allows us to create"

The paper is well-organized and clearly written overall, with strong experimental results that support the theoretical claims. The mathematical proofs appear sound, though I was unable to verify all details in the supplementary material due to time constraints. The authors have made good progress in addressing previous concerns, and their clarifications have significantly improved the paper's presentation.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms extend gossip methods to handle more complex statistics in a theoretically grounded and practically effective manner. With the suggested revisions to better contextualize the work within the literature and clarify key technical points, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Strengths:
- Novel extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics
- Theoretical convergence guarantees with explicit dependencies on network structure
- Empirical results demonstrating clear advantages over existing approaches
- Well-structured presentation of the mathematical framework

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient connection to existing gossip algorithm literature
- Ambiguous descriptions of key technical aspects (e.g., edge selection process)
- Missing comparison with natural alternative approaches
- Limited engagement with lifting constructions literature

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the contextualization issues and clarify the technical points.
