Summary
This paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over networks by extending randomized gossip methods beyond separable sums. The authors propose synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) algorithms that simultaneously propagate data and average local estimates. They prove convergence in expectation at rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants that depend explicitly on data dispersion and network connectivity (via the spectral gap). The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from averaging. Empirically, on tasks such as AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter across several network topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts–Strogatz), the proposed methods outperform the prior U2-gossip algorithm, both in convergence speed and stability across nodes. Compared to U2-gossip, GoSta requires less memory and communication per iteration (one pairwise swap vs. two), and avoids global synchronization through an asynchronous variant.

Relation to prior work
Prior gossip algorithms focus on averaging or separable functions; they do not directly extend to U-statistics that couple pairs of observations. U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens) is the most relevant predecessor. The present work improves on U2-gossip by incorporating averaging, reducing auxiliary state, supporting asynchronous operation, and tightening convergence rates with network- and data-dependent constants.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: U-statistics include widely used metrics (AUC, variance, Gini difference, within-cluster scatter).
- Technical contribution: novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms with convergence guarantees that reflect both data difficulty and network spectral properties.
- Analytical tools: the phantom network reformulation cleanly separates propagation and averaging and leads to explicit bounds.
- Practical relevance: lower memory and communication than U2-gossip; asynchronous implementation removes reliance on a global clock.
- Empirical validation: consistent improvements over U2-gossip across diverse graphs and tasks; reduced variance across nodes due to averaging.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Runtime and cost reporting: The paper lacks wall-clock and per-iteration runtime comparisons. Because GoSta and U2-gossip differ in per-iteration communication and local computation, reporting (i) time-to-accuracy, (ii) messages/bytes per iteration, and (iii) memory per node would strengthen the practical conclusions.
- Fair budgeting: Consider comparing methods under matched communication/computation budgets (e.g., equal number of pair communications or bytes sent), not just iterations.
- Scalability with dimension and network size: Theoretical and empirical discussion of how costs scale with the data dimension d and large n would be useful, especially when H is expensive to compute.
- Assumptions in the asynchronous setting: GoSta-async requires nodes to know degrees and the total number of edges to form pk. Please clarify how this information is obtained in fully decentralized settings and the impact of estimation errors.
- Additional metrics: High-probability bounds (beyond in-expectation) or finite-time variance bounds could further support robustness claims, especially for asynchronous execution.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with clear proofs and well-argued bounds; experiments support claims. Additional cost metrics would improve the practical evaluation.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The phantom-node analysis is explained clearly. Adding a succinct complexity table (per iteration: local compute, messages, memory) would improve readability.
- Originality: Extends gossip to U-statistics with a novel combination of propagation and averaging, and provides both synchronous and asynchronous variants. Improves on U2-gossip theoretically and empirically.
- Significance: Estimating U-statistics is broadly useful in decentralized ML and sensing. The methods are likely to be adopted, especially the asynchronous variant.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical guarantees with insightful dependence on spectral gap and data.
- Practical algorithms that reduce communication/memory and support asynchrony.
- Consistent empirical gains over the main prior method across tasks and networks.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing wall-clock and per-iteration cost comparisons may limit practical impact assessment.
- Limited exploration of scalability with high-dimensional data and very large networks.
- Reliance on global graph information (for pk) needs clarification in fully decentralized deployments.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly presented contribution that advances decentralized estimation of U-statistics. Including detailed runtime, communication, and memory comparisons to U2-gossip would significantly strengthen the paper’s practical relevance.
