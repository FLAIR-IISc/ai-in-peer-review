The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. The authors present results for their approach on two datasets, evaluating performance using the average relative AUC error. Their method significantly outperforms the U2-gossip baseline across a variety of graph structures and scenarios. Notably, the paper provides convergence bounds for the proposed method. 

I would appreciate the inclusion of run-times per iteration for both the proposed method and U2-gossip. If each iteration of the proposed method is substantially slower than U2-gossip, the practical utility of the work would be limited. 

The paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics. Specifically, the core contribution lies in developing and analyzing these algorithms, which aim to improve the efficiency and scalability of U-statistic estimation in decentralized settings. The theoretical analysis demonstrating convergence is a strong aspect of the paper.

The experimental evaluation effectively demonstrates the superior performance of the proposed algorithms compared to the baseline. The choice of datasets appears reasonable, and the use of average relative AUC error is a suitable metric for evaluating performance. However, a more detailed discussion of the dataset characteristics, and how these characteristics might influence the observed performance differences, would be beneficial. For example, are the graphs sparse or dense? What is the degree distribution?

Regarding the theoretical contributions, the convergence bounds are valuable. It would be helpful to understand how these bounds scale with the problem size (number of nodes, dimension of the data, etc.). A discussion of the practical implications of these bounds – i.e., how many iterations are typically required to achieve a desired level of accuracy – would also strengthen the paper. 

The asynchronous algorithm is particularly interesting, as it offers the potential for faster computation. However, the analysis of asynchronous algorithms can be challenging. The authors should clearly articulate any assumptions made regarding the asynchrony model (e.g., bounded delay, independent and identically distributed delays).

Minor points: 

*   The notation could be slightly improved for readability. For instance, defining variables more explicitly when they are first introduced would be helpful.
*   A brief discussion of related work, particularly other approaches for decentralized U-statistic estimation, would provide valuable context. 

Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear contribution. Addressing the run-time concerns and providing more detailed insights into the scaling of the theoretical results would significantly strengthen the work. I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
