This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics – quantities requiring averaging over pairs of observations – in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. This is a significant extension to traditional gossip-based averaging, as U-statistics encompass important measures like variance, AUC, and Gini mean difference. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and provide convergence rate bounds, demonstrating improvements over a previously introduced approach. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on distributed estimation, but strengthening the connection to existing work and clarifying certain aspects of the presentation would enhance its impact.

The core idea of the algorithm – exchanging not only estimates but also auxiliary state variables representing initial observations, effectively allowing each observation to perform a random walk – is clever. However, this crucial aspect could be highlighted more prominently in the manuscript to better motivate the analysis, which utilizes a reformulation involving “phantom nodes.” 

The related work section could be expanded. While the cited papers are relevant, a broader overview of gossip algorithms for statistical computation, such as the comprehensive survey by Dimakis et al. (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, 2010), would provide valuable context. Situating the current work within this broader landscape would clarify its contributions.

On page 4, specifying the distribution used when “drawing a random edge of the network” would improve clarity.  Is each edge selected with uniform probability, or is the probability weighted by edge properties? 

A compelling comparison point would be an algorithm where nodes maintain and flood all received observations. This would allow the authors to demonstrate the benefits (or trade-offs) of their averaging approach, especially regarding memory and communication costs.  Without such a comparison, it’s difficult to assess whether the achieved O(1/t) convergence rate is optimal or competitive.  For instance, in expander graphs, a flooding approach might achieve comparable or superior performance.

The authors should consider related work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms (e.g., Jung et al., Allerton 2007; Li & Dai, IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 2010; Chen, Lovász, & Pak, STOC 1999).  These techniques appear relevant to the “phantom node” approach and could provide valuable insights.

The analysis appears to assume a worst-case initial data distribution, guaranteeing convergence for *any* initial data vector.  Clarifying this assumption and explicitly stating the guarantees on the error as a function of the initial data would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Minor points for improvement include replacing "let us stress out that" with "we stress that," “awakes and contact” with “awakes and contacts,” “requires to initiate” with “requires initiating,” and “allows to create” with “allows us to create.”  Additionally, the definition of a bipartite graph using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies a directed graph; this should be clarified.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper extends gossip algorithms to a challenging problem – computing U-statistics – that is not readily addressed by existing methods.
*   **Theoretical soundness:** The authors provide convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, supported by a non-trivial analysis.
*   **Empirical validation:** The numerical experiments demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms compared to a related approach on real datasets.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work opens avenues for decentralized computation of various important statistical quantities in distributed systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited connection to existing work:** The related work section could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding gossip algorithms for general statistical computation and lifting techniques.
*   **Lack of baseline comparison:** The absence of a comparison to a flooding-based approach makes it difficult to assess the efficiency of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Clarity of presentation:** Some aspects of the explanation, such as the motivation behind the “phantom node” approach, could be more clearly articulated.



Following the author feedback, the clarifications and modifications are a positive step. However, a more detailed discussion of lifting constructions and the inclusion of baseline comparisons would further strengthen the paper. 

**Overall Score:** 8/10.

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the remaining concerns regarding related work and baseline comparisons. This is a well-presented and promising approach to extending gossip methods for distributed statistical computation.
