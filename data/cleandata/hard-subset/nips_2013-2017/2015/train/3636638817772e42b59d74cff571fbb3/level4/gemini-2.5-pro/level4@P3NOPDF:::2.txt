### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing U-statistics, which are higher-order statistics measured over pairs of observations distributed across a network. The target statistics include important quantities like sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients. The core contribution is a novel decentralized averaging protocol designed for this class of problems.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper addresses the important and relevant problem of computing complex statistics in a fully decentralized manner. The proposed method is an interesting extension of standard gossip protocols to a more challenging class of functions.

While the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form would benefit significantly from improvements in clarity, a more thorough engagement with related literature, and a comparison to natural baseline methods. The mathematical formulation and its underlying assumptions also require clarification. Nevertheless, the contribution is potentially significant. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors thoroughly addressing the concerns detailed below.

*Please note: Due to time constraints, I was unable to verify the correctness of the proofs in the supplementary material.*

### Major Comments

**1. Clarity of the Algorithmic Mechanism:**
The key algorithmic innovation appears to be that when two nodes average their estimates, they also swap a secondary state variable representing their initial observation. This mechanism effectively makes the initial observations perform coupled random walks on the network. This is a crucial and elegant insight, but it is not explained with sufficient clarity in the main text. The authors should explicitly describe this process and highlight the random walk perspective. Doing so would not only make the algorithm more intuitive for the reader but would also provide a stronger motivation for the analysis technique, which relies on constructing multiple copies of the graph.

**2. Positioning within Existing Literature:**
The discussion of related work is insufficient and does not adequately position the paper within the broader literature on gossip algorithms.
*   A more thorough comparison with existing approaches is needed. For instance, the survey by Dimakis et al. ("A Survey of Gossip Algorithms for Distributed Signal Processing," Proc. IEEE) provides a comprehensive overview of the field, and contextualizing this work against the methods described there would be highly beneficial.
*   Furthermore, the proposed analysis technique seems closely related to "lifting" methods used to accelerate gossip algorithm convergence. The construction of a larger state space to analyze the process is reminiscent of these techniques. The authors should explicitly discuss and differentiate their work from lifting constructions, such as those proposed by Chen et al. (STOC 1999), Jung et al. (Allerton 2007), and Li and Dai (IEEE T-IT 2010).

**3. Lack of Comparison to a Natural Baseline:**
The paper lacks a comparison to a natural and important baseline algorithm, making it difficult to evaluate the performance of the proposed method. An alternative approach to this problem would be to use the gossip protocol to flood the initial observations across the network, assuming nodes have sufficient memory to store them. Each node could then compute the U-statistic locally on the ever-growing subset of data it has received.
A theoretical and/or empirical comparison against this flooding-based approach is essential. Such a comparison would help contextualize the algorithm's performance, clarify the trade-offs between memory, communication, and convergence speed, and justify the benefits of the proposed averaging scheme. Without this baseline, it is difficult to assess whether the stated 1/t convergence rate is competitive, especially on well-connected graphs like expanders where flooding can be very efficient.

**4. Ambiguity in Theoretical Assumptions:**
The theoretical analysis needs to be more precise about its assumptions. Typically, convergence guarantees for gossip algorithms are provided for the worst-case initial data distribution across the nodes. The paper does not clearly state whether its guarantees hold for any initial data vector. Please clarify the assumptions on the initial data and explicitly state how the error bounds depend on this initial configuration.

### Minor Comments and Typos

*   **Page 1:** The definition of a bipartite graph using `(S x T) U (T x S)` appears to imply a directed graph. Please clarify if this is intended.
*   **Page 4:** The communication model states a "random edge of the network" is drawn. Please specify the exact probability distribution over the edges induced by the "node wakes up and contacts a neighbor" process (e.g., is it uniform over all edges, or is it based on node degrees?).
