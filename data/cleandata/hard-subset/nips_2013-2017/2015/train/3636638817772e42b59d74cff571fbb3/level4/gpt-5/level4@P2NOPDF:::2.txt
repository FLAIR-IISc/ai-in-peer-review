This paper studies decentralized computation of pairwise U-statistics when observations are distributed across a network. The target statistics include sample variance, correlations, scatter coefficients, and related aggregates. The authors propose a gossip-style decentralized averaging scheme to estimate these quantities.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the gossip literature, but the paper should draw stronger connections to prior work and clarify several modeling and analytical choices.

This is a heavy review; I did not verify the supplemental proofs.

Comments
1) Algorithmic mechanism: When two nodes interact, they not only average their current estimates but also swap an auxiliary state that stores their initial observation. As a result, initial observations perform (coupled) random walks over the network. Stating this explicitly in the main text would improve intuition and motivate the analysis via multiple copies of the graph.

2) Related work: Beyond the cited references, there is substantial literature on gossip for computing statistics. A useful survey is the Proc. IEEE article by Dimakis et al., which consolidates many relevant approaches. Positioning this work within that landscape would be more informative than the current, relatively brief discussion.

3) Edge selection distribution: On page 4, please clarify the edge distribution induced by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” is ambiguous without specifying whether the selection is uniform over nodes, neighbors, or edges, and whether it is push, pull, or push-pull.

4) Baseline with larger memory (flooding): A natural alternative is to allow each node more memory and use the same contact process to flood its own observation through the network. Nodes retain received observations (tagged by source) and compute the U-statistic on their current subset. How does this compare to the proposed method in terms of accuracy and time to a desired error? This comparison would clarify when averaging is beneficial and how performance degrades with limited memory. Without it, it is hard to judge whether the observed 1/t convergence rate is favorable; on expander graphs, flooding may be highly competitive.

5) Lifting and accelerated gossip: The construction appears closely related to lifted Markov chains used to speed up mixing and consensus. Please discuss connections to:
- Jung, Shah, and Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Allerton, 2007.
- Li and Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE Trans. IT, 56(12), 2010.
- Chen, Lovász, and Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” STOC, 1999.

6) Assumptions and guarantees: Analyses of gossip often provide worst-case guarantees over all initial data vectors X. The paper’s statement of the problem and main theorem does not clearly specify the data model. Please state the assumptions on the data (deterministic vs. stochastic, boundedness, independence), and whether the error guarantees hold uniformly over X or in expectation over a distribution on X. Explicit dependence of the error on the initial data would be valuable.

Minor issues
- p.1: The bipartite graph definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) suggests directed edges; clarify whether the graph is directed or undirected.
- p.4: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”
- p.4: “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”
- p.6: “requires to initiate” → “requires initiating”
- p.7: “allows to create” → “allows us to create”

After author feedback: The authors’ clarifications and revisions address many concerns; I raise my score from 7 to 8. I still encourage a careful integration of lifting-based acceleration methods (especially LADA) into the related-work discussion, and inclusion of baseline experiments (e.g., flooding with larger local memory) for empirical comparison.

In sum, this is a well-presented extension of gossip methods to more complex distributed statistics. With clearer ties to prior work and the suggested baselines, I recommend acceptance.
