

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a significant gap in the literature on decentralized computation. The authors recognize that while gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for estimating separable statistics (e.g., sample means), computation of U-statistics—which require pairwise averaging over observations—has received much less attention. The paper's main contribution is a new approach that simultaneously propagates data across the network and maintains local estimates of the U-statistic, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case.

The work builds upon foundational gossip algorithms for distributed averaging [2, 10, 19] while addressing a critical limitation: existing approaches like U2-gossip [17] require significant memory and communication overhead (storing two auxiliary observations per node) and cannot operate asynchronously. The authors' key insight is to reformulate the problem using "phantom nodes" to separate data propagation and averaging steps, enabling a more efficient algorithm with lower memory requirements (only one auxiliary observation per node) and support for asynchronous operation.

Quality: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous convergence analysis. The authors provide detailed proofs for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, establishing convergence rates with explicit data- and network-dependent terms. The convergence bounds reveal important insights about the relationship between network connectivity (via the spectral gap) and estimation performance. The experimental section validates the theoretical claims with compelling results on real-world datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC estimation and Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter), showing consistent performance advantages over U2-gossip across different network topologies (complete graphs, 2D grids, and Watts-Strogatz networks). The paper carefully addresses the limitations of prior work and provides a refined rate analysis for U2-gossip that explains its inferior performance.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide precise mathematical definitions, intuitive explanations of the algorithmic approach, and a helpful figure illustrating the phantom network construction. The transition from problem statement to algorithm design to convergence analysis is logical and easy to follow. The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, though the main paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the phantom network's role in the algorithm's design. The notation is consistent throughout, and the writing is precise without being overly technical.

Originality: This work makes a significant contribution to the literature by extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics, a problem previously unaddressed in the decentralized setting. The phantom node technique represents a novel approach to analyzing and solving this problem, and the algorithms themselves represent a meaningful improvement over the only existing solution (U2-gossip). The paper demonstrates sufficient differentiation from previous NIPS work, particularly in addressing the limitations of U2-gossip while maintaining theoretical rigor. The authors appropriately cite related work in distributed computation and U-statistics [12, 14, 17], though they might consider referencing more recent work on decentralized U-statistics if available.

Significance: The paper addresses an important practical problem in decentralized systems where pairwise statistics (such as AUC, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter) are essential for many applications. The proposed algorithms reduce communication and memory requirements while achieving faster convergence, making them more suitable for resource-constrained networks. The experimental results demonstrate tangible performance improvements that would be valuable to practitioners working with distributed data. The work opens new possibilities for decentralized optimization of U-statistics and could be integrated into broader distributed learning frameworks.

Strengths:
1. Novel solution to a significant gap in decentralized computation (U-statistics)
2. Rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates
3. Practical implementation with lower memory/communication costs than prior work
4. Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and network topologies
5. Clear writing and well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
1. The phantom node construction, while clever, is somewhat abstract and might benefit from additional intuitive explanation
2. The comparison to U2-gossip could be strengthened with more detailed analysis of the communication complexity
3. The paper doesn't explore potential applications beyond AUC and within-cluster point scatter
4. The asynchronous algorithm's theoretical rate (O(log t/t)) is slower than the synchronous case (O(1/t)), though experiments show comparable practical performance

Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:
For acceptance: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to decentralized computation with practical implications. The algorithms are novel, well-analyzed, and empirically validated. The work advances the state-of-the-art in distributed estimation of non-separable statistics and would be valuable to the NIPS community.

Against acceptance: While the contribution is meaningful, the problem of U-statistics in decentralized settings is somewhat specialized. The paper could strengthen its significance argument by demonstrating broader applications beyond the two specific examples provided.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a clear contribution to the literature on distributed algorithms. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the writing is excellent. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions to the field. I recommend acceptance.
