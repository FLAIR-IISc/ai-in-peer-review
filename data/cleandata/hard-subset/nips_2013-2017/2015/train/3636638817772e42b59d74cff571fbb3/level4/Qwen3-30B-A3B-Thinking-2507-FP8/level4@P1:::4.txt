

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networked systems. U-statistics, such as area under the curve (AUC), empirical variance, and Gini mean difference, are crucial for describing global properties of statistical populations but have received less attention in distributed settings compared to simpler statistics like the sample mean. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) that enable decentralized computation of U-statistics by simultaneously propagating data across the network and maintaining local estimates. The paper establishes convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with explicit dependence on data and network properties. The authors demonstrate through experiments on real datasets that their approach outperforms the previously proposed U2-gossip algorithm [17].

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, including convergence proofs that reveal how the rate depends on both the data and network properties. The use of "phantom nodes" as an analytical tool is particularly elegant and insightful. Second, the paper addresses an important gap in the literature on distributed estimation, as existing gossip algorithms primarily handle statistics that are separable across observations but not those that require pairwise computations. Third, the experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation provide empirical evidence of the proposed algorithms' superiority over the U2-gossip baseline.

However, the evaluation of the proposed methods has several limitations that need to be addressed. The primary weakness is the lack of comparison with meaningful baselines beyond U2-gossip [17]. While the authors demonstrate that GoSta outperforms U2-gossip, they do not compare against more intuitive baseline approaches that could be implemented with minimal modifications to existing gossip algorithms. For example, a straightforward baseline would involve having each node compute the U-statistic locally using all observations (which would require a centralized computation), or using a simple gossip-based approach that averages the observations first and then computes the U-statistic (which would require each node to maintain the entire dataset). The authors should provide a more comprehensive comparison to these intuitive baselines to better establish the relative advantages of their approach.

Another concern is that the experimental section does not clearly quantify the communication and memory costs of the proposed algorithms compared to the baselines. The paper mentions that GoSta has "lower memory and communication costs" than U2-gossip, but this claim is not substantiated with actual measurements. The authors should include tables or figures showing the memory footprint and communication complexity per iteration for both algorithms across different network sizes.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, how does the algorithm scale to extremely large networks or very high-dimensional data? What are the trade-offs between the convergence rate and the communication overhead? The authors should address these questions to better contextualize the practical applicability of their method.

Strengths:
- Novel problem formulation for distributed U-statistics estimation
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rate bounds
- Elegant use of "phantom nodes" for the analysis
- Empirical evidence of superiority over U2-gossip on real datasets

Weaknesses:
- Lack of meaningful baselines beyond U2-gossip
- Insufficient empirical evidence regarding memory and communication costs
- Limited discussion of practical limitations and scalability

The paper addresses an important problem in distributed computation with a theoretically sound approach, but the evaluation is insufficient to fully validate the claimed advantages. With additional baselines and more thorough experimental comparisons, this work could make a strong contribution to the field.
