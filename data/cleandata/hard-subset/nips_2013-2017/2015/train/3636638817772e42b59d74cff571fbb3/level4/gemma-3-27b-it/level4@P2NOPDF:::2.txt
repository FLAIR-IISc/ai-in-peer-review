This paper presents a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing higher-order statistics – including variance, correlation, and other aggregate measures – measured over pairs of observations distributed across a network. These statistics are framed as U-statistics, motivating the algorithm’s design. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of gossip algorithms. However, strengthening the connection to existing literature would significantly enhance the work.

My assessment is based on a review of the main manuscript; I did not have sufficient time to fully verify the correctness of the supplemental material.

Here are several points for the authors to consider during revision:

1) The algorithm’s core mechanism – exchanging both estimate values *and* initial observation data between nodes – effectively induces a random walk for each initial observation, albeit a coupled one. Explicitly highlighting this point in the manuscript would clarify the algorithm’s behavior and motivate the analytical approach using multiple graph copies.

2) The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive review of related work. While the cited references are relevant, a significant body of research exists on gossip algorithms for statistical computation. Specifically, the work of Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) provides a valuable snapshot of the state-of-the-art several years ago, and positioning this work within that context would be beneficial. The current discussion of related work feels somewhat limited.

3) On page 4, clarifying the distribution induced on network edges by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process is crucial. The statement “drawing a random edge of the network” lacks specificity regarding the underlying distribution.

4) A compelling comparison point is missing: a flooding-based approach. Allowing nodes to store received observations (tagged by their origin) would enable each node to independently calculate the U-statistic on its local dataset. Comparing the performance of this approach to the proposed averaging method would illuminate the advantages of the current strategy, or reveal potential performance limitations. Without this benchmark, it is difficult to evaluate whether the observed rate of 1/t is competitive. For example, on expander graphs, flooding may perform remarkably well.

5) The authors should investigate and discuss the connection to existing work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms. These constructions appear highly relevant to the presented approach. Relevant references include: Jung, Shah, and Shin (Allerton 2007); Li and Dai (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 2010); and Chen, Lovasz, and Pak (STOC 1999).

6) Typically, the analysis of gossip algorithms assumes a worst-case initial data distribution, guaranteeing performance across *all* possible initial data vectors X. This assumption is not clearly stated in the problem definition or the theorem statement. The authors should explicitly clarify the assumptions regarding the data distribution and the corresponding error guarantees.

Minor points requiring attention:
* p1: The bipartite graph definition (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies a directed graph. This should be clarified.
* p4: Replace “let us stress out that” with “we stress that.”
* p4: Correct “awakes and contact” to “awakes and contacts.”
* p6: Replace “requires to initiate” with “requires initiating.”
* p7: Replace “allows to create” with “allows us to create.”

**Following Author Feedback:**

The authors’ clarifications and modifications in response to initial feedback are well-considered and address several of my concerns. I am therefore revising my initial score from a 7 to an 8.

However, I still recommend a closer examination of lifting constructions, particularly Location-Aided Distributed Averaging (LADA), and a corresponding revision of the related work section. Furthermore, including baseline comparisons – specifically, the flooding approach discussed above – in the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper.

In conclusion, this is a well-presented approach to extending gossip methods for complex statistical computations in distributed settings. While the mathematical formulation and connection to prior work could be clarified, I recommend acceptance upon satisfactory revision addressing the points raised in this review.
