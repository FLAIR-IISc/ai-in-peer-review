### Summary

This paper introduces novel randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, for the distributed estimation of U-statistics. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance, within-cluster point scatter), are crucial for many machine learning tasks but are not efficiently computable with standard gossip algorithms designed for separable sums. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous versions of their algorithm. The core idea is to combine two processes at each iteration: 1) the propagation of data observations across the network via a random walk (swapping), and 2) the averaging of local U-statistic estimates between neighboring nodes.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. A key element of their proof is an original reformulation using "phantom nodes" to decouple the analysis of data propagation and estimate averaging. This work directly builds upon and improves the state-of-the-art in this area, specifically the U2-gossip algorithm [17], by offering faster convergence, lower memory and communication costs, and, critically, an extension to the more practical asynchronous setting. The theoretical claims are substantiated by comprehensive experiments on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation tasks.

### Evaluation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed computation and gossip algorithms. It is well-written, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the convergence proofs are well-structured. The introduction of the "phantom network" as an analytical tool is particularly clever and provides a clear path to deriving the convergence bounds. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical rates derived and the extensive experimental results. The authors are careful to compare their proposed methods against the most relevant prior work, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach across different network topologies. The work is a complete contribution, presenting the algorithms, their theoretical justification, and their empirical performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem of U-statistic estimation in a decentralized setting is clearly motivated with relevant examples. The proposed GoSta algorithms are presented in a clean, algorithmic format. The distinction between the synchronous and asynchronous settings is explained well. The theoretical sections, while dense, are laid out logically, and the phantom network construction is well-illustrated. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, with informative plots that effectively communicate the performance comparison.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While gossip algorithms for averaging are well-studied, their extension to non-separable functions like U-statistics is a less explored and more challenging area. To my knowledge, [17] is the primary prior work directly addressing this problem. The proposed GoSta algorithms are novel in their approach of simultaneously swapping data observations and averaging local estimates, which proves to be more effective than the data-swapping-only approach of U2-gossip. Furthermore, the development and analysis of a fully asynchronous algorithm for this task is a significant and novel contribution, greatly enhancing the practical relevance of the work.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful. U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and the ability to compute them efficiently in large, decentralized networks (e.g., sensor networks, federated learning settings) is of great practical importance. This paper advances the state-of-the-art by providing algorithms that are not only faster and more resource-efficient than the previous best but also operable in asynchronous environments. Researchers and practitioners working on distributed machine learning are likely to use these ideas or build upon them. The work could also serve as a foundation for more complex distributed optimization problems where the objective function is a U-statistic.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Tackles the challenging but important problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized network, a problem with broad applications in machine learning.
*   **Novel and Effective Algorithms:** Proposes new synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) that are simple and effective. The combination of data propagation and estimate averaging is a key and novel insight.
*   **Rigorous Theory:** Provides a strong theoretical analysis with explicit convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t), which clearly improve upon existing work. The analytical technique using a "phantom network" is also a novel contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The superiority of the proposed algorithms is demonstrated through extensive experiments on real datasets and various network structures, confirming the theoretical findings.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The asynchronous algorithm is a major contribution that makes the approach applicable to a wider range of realistic, large-scale distributed systems where global synchronization is not feasible.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

*   (Minor) The theoretical convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm (O(log t/t)) is slightly weaker than for the synchronous one (O(1/t)). While this is expected, it constitutes a minor limitation. However, the empirical results show that the practical performance is comparable, mitigating this concern.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It presents a clear advance in an important area with solid theory and compelling experiments.
