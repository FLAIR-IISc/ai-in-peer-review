

This paper addresses computing higher-order statistics over pairs of observations distributed across a network, where these statistics encompass quantities like sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients. The primary contribution is a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for aggregating these U-statistics.  

Overall, this constitutes a valuable addition to gossip algorithm literature, though the authors should strengthen connections to prior work.  

Comments:  
1) The algorithm’s key innovation involves nodes exchanging estimates while swapping a secondary state variable tracking initial observations, effectively enabling coupled random walks of initial data. Explicitly highlighting this mechanism in the main text would clarify the analysis approach via multiple graph copies.  
2) The manuscript overlooks substantial prior work on gossip-based statistics computation. Situating this contribution within the context of Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, a state-of-the-art snapshot from several years ago) would be more informative than the current superficial treatment.  
3) On page 4, the induced edge distribution from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process requires clarification. The phrase "drawing a random edge" lacks specification of the distribution.  
4) An alternative approach—using node memory to flood initial values (with node tags) and locally compute U-statistics on received data—deserves comparison. This would clarify whether the averaging method’s rate $O(1/t)$ is advantageous, particularly since flooding may perform well for expander networks.  
5) The authors should incorporate related work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms (e.g., Jung et al., Allerton 2007; Li & Dai, IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 2010; Chen et al., STOC 1999), which directly relates to their approach.  
6) The analysis assumes worst-case initial data distributions (over all $X$), but this is not explicitly stated in the theorem setup. Clarify the data distribution assumption and error guarantees as a function of initial data.  

Minor corrections:  
- p1: Bipartite definition $(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)$ implies directed graphs.  
- p4: "stress out" → "stress"; "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts".  
- p6: "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating".  
- p7: "allows to create" → "allows us to create".  

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK: The authors’ clarifications and modifications are satisfactory. I revise my initial score from 7 to 8. They should further examine lifting constructions (particularly LADA) and strengthen the related work section. Additionally, experiments must include baseline comparisons.  

This is a well-presented approach extending gossip methods to compute complex statistics in distributed settings like sensor networks. While the mathematical formulation and prior work connections require refinement, addressing the reviewer’s points would warrant acceptance.
