Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes two randomized gossip algorithms—GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous)—for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network. Each node i holds one observation Xi and maintains an auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via pairwise swaps. At each iteration, nodes update local estimates by a running average of H(Xi, Yi) and use a standard gossip averaging step to reduce disagreement across nodes. The authors prove convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) in the synchronous case and O((log t)/t) in the asynchronous case. The bounds include explicit data-dependent terms (capturing the difficulty of estimating the local hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi, Xj)) and network-dependent terms via the spectral gap (1 − λ2) of the underlying graph. The analysis uses a “phantom node” construction to decouple data propagation from averaging, leading to clean matrix formulations.

The work is positioned against classical gossip algorithms for separable aggregates (e.g., averages) [Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al. 2010; Kempe et al. 2003] and geographic/accelerated variants [Dimakis et al. 2008; Li et al. 2010], which do not apply directly to pairwise functionals. It most closely relates to U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens 2009], which also targets U-statistics but requires two auxiliary observations per node and two simultaneous edge activations per iteration. The authors provide refined rate comparisons suggesting GoSta’s advantage, and their experiments (AUC and within-cluster scatter) on multiple graph topologies show superior empirical performance and lower across-node variance.

Quality:
- Theory: The convergence results are clearly stated and technically plausible. The rate dependence on the spectral gap is standard for gossip and is appropriately highlighted. The phantom network analysis is elegant and appears sound (details in the supplement).
- Algorithms: GoSta-sync needs only one auxiliary observation per node and one edge activation per iteration; GoSta-async accounts for non-synchronized updates via local counters mk(t) designed so that E[mk(t)] = t, keeping the running averages unbiased.
- Empirics: Experiments on AUC (svmguide3) and within-cluster scatter (Wine Quality), across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs, substantiate the theoretical claims and show clear improvements over U2-gossip in accuracy and variance.

Areas where quality could be strengthened:
- Convergence is proved in expectation; high-probability or mean-square error (MSE) bounds are not provided. For practical stopping rules and robustness claims, variance or concentration results would be valuable.
- The asynchronous scheme assumes each node knows pk = 2dk/|E|. In many decentralized settings, knowing |E| is nontrivial. Discussing how to estimate |E| online, or designing an update rule that avoids global quantities, would improve practicality.
- Communication and memory advantages are argued qualitatively but not quantified experimentally. Reporting messages/bytes per node to reach a target error, and memory per node, would make the cost comparison to U2-gossip more compelling.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The algorithms are clearly described, and the role of the spectral gap is well explained. The phantom node construction is a nice expository device; emphasizing earlier that it is only an analytical tool avoids confusion about computational complexity. Minor suggestions:
- Explicitly state early that the algorithms require the graph to be connected and non-bipartite, and discuss how to handle bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy random walks).
- Clarify the practical requirement in GoSta-async that nodes know degrees and |E|, and whether approximations are acceptable.
- Provide a concise table summarizing per-iteration memory and communication costs for GoSta-sync, GoSta-async, and U2-gossip.

Originality:
The combination of (i) a single-auxiliary observation with (ii) a coupled data-propagation and averaging step, and (iii) the phantom node analysis for U-statistics, is novel relative to U2-gossip. The asynchronous variant is a meaningful extension for realistic settings. The refined comparison bound for U2-gossip is also a useful contribution.

Significance:
Estimating U-statistics in decentralized networks is important for tasks such as AUC computation, variance, Gini difference, and within-cluster scatter. The algorithms advance the state of the art theoretically and practically, and the dependence on spectral gap provides actionable insights for network design. The methods are likely to be used or extended, including as primitives within decentralized learning and optimization frameworks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled algorithms for an important non-separable aggregate.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with explicit data/network dependence.
- Practical asynchronous variant.
- Empirical validation on multiple graphs and tasks, with consistent improvements over prior work.
- Elegant and clear analysis via phantom networks.

Cons and questions:
- Only expectation convergence is proved; lack of MSE/high-probability bounds limits guidance on runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs.
- GoSta-async’s reliance on knowledge of |E| may hinder deployment; can pk be estimated or can updates be adapted to local information?
- Experimental scope is modest (two datasets, moderate n and d); include larger-scale, higher-dimensional tests and report communication/memory to reach fixed error targets.
- Extensions to higher-order U-statistics are not discussed; is the approach scalable to degree > 2?
- Robustness to time-varying, directed, or lossy networks is not addressed.

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper tackles a well-motivated and underexplored problem, presents novel and efficient algorithms with theoretically grounded rates, and demonstrates clear empirical gains over the best prior method. Addressing the noted concerns—especially communication/memory quantification and stronger stochastic convergence guarantees—would further enhance the contribution.
