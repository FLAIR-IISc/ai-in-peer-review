This paper studies the decentralized computation of higher-order statistics defined over pairs of observations, where the observations are distributed across a network. These U-statistics include many commonly used quantities such as sample variance, correlation and scatter coefficients, and related aggregate measures. The main contribution is a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm to compute these statistics.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, the manuscript would benefit from a clearer connection to existing approaches and a more thorough positioning within the broader body of related work. This is a heavy review; I did not have time to verify the correctness of the supplementary material.

Comments
1) Algorithmic mechanism and analysis: The key idea is that when two nodes exchange and average their estimate values, they also swap a secondary state variable representing their initial observation. As a result, the initial observations perform (coupled) random walks on the network. Making this mechanism explicit in the main text would both clarify the algorithm and motivate the analysis approach via multiple copies of the graph.

2) Related work and context: There is substantial prior work on gossip-based computation of nontrivial statistics beyond what is cited. A good snapshot is provided by the survey of Dimakis et al. in Proceedings of the IEEE. Situating the present work more thoroughly in that context would be more informative than the current cursory treatment.

3) Edge selection distribution: On page 4, please clarify the edge distribution induced by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” protocol. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” is ambiguous without specifying the distribution (e.g., whether edges are chosen proportional to degree, uniformly over nodes then uniformly over their neighbors, etc.).

4) Comparison to a flooding-with-memory baseline: A natural alternative is to allow more memory at each node and use the same contact process to flood each node’s value across the network. Nodes store received observations (tagged by their source) and, at each time, compute the U-statistic over the data they currently hold. How does this baseline compare to the proposed averaging method? This comparison could clarify when averaging provides an advantage, and how performance degrades under memory constraints. In particular, without such a comparison it is not clear whether the 1/t rate is good or bad; on expander graphs, flooding may perform quite well.

5) Connection to lifting constructions: Please consider related work on lifted Markov chains for accelerating gossip/consensus, which appears closely related to your construction:
- K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Proc. Allerton Conf. on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2007.
- W. Li and H. Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, 56(12), 2010.
- F. Chen, L. Lovász, and I. Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” STOC, 1999, pp. 275–281.

6) Assumptions and guarantees: Analyses of gossip algorithms often make worst-case guarantees over all initial data vectors X. The problem statement and theorem statements do not make these assumptions or the scope of guarantees fully clear. Please specify the assumptions on the data distribution (if any) and state the error guarantees as a function of the initial data.

Small issues
- p.1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) suggests directed graphs; please clarify intent.
- p.4: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”
- p.4: “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”
- p.6: “requires to initiate” → “requires initiating”
- p.7: “allows to create” → “allows us to create”

After author feedback
The clarifications and modifications proposed by the authors are helpful. I am revising my initial score from 7 to 8. I still encourage the authors to engage more deeply with lifting-based constructions (especially LADA) and to revise the related work accordingly. In addition, the experimental section should include the baseline comparisons discussed above.

Conclusion
This is a clearly presented approach to extending gossip methods for computing more complex statistics in distributed settings (e.g., sensor networks). Some aspects of the mathematical formulation and the connection to prior work remain unclear, but assuming the authors make a careful revision addressing the points above, I recommend acceptance.
