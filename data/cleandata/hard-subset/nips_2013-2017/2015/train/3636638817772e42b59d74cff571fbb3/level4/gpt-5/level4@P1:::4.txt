Summary:
The paper studies decentralized computation of statistics that depend on pairs of observations (U-statistics) in networked settings. It proposes synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms that propagate data across the network while maintaining running local estimates. The algorithms are conceptually simple: nodes swap auxiliary observations along randomly selected edges and continuously average local estimates. The authors provide convergence guarantees with rates of O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), supported by comprehensive proofs.

Strengths:
- The problem is important in distributed settings, where quantities like AUC, variance, and within-cluster scatter arise naturally but are not amenable to standard gossip averaging.
- The algorithms are intuitive and easy to implement.
- The theoretical analysis is thorough; the convergence proofs are detailed and appear technically sound.

Weaknesses:
- The novelty of the algorithmic contribution is unclear. While the approach is intuitive and well motivated, it may be viewed as an incremental extension of standard randomized gossip ideas to pairwise statistics rather than a fundamentally new technique.
- The experimental evaluation is weak. There are no comparisons against baseline methods. Several natural baselines could be devised using straightforward randomized strategies, and at least some of these should be included to contextualize performance.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The claims are supported by detailed proofs, which is a strong point. However, the empirical evaluation does not adequately validate the practical benefits of the proposed methods because there are no baseline comparisons.
- Completeness: The theoretical component feels complete; the experimental component is not.
- Honesty about limitations: The paper does not sufficiently address how the proposed methods compare to simple alternatives in practice.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, and the presentation of the algorithms is understandable. The proofs are comprehensive.
- It would help to more explicitly position the contribution relative to straightforward randomized baselines and to clarify the practical trade-offs (e.g., memory/communication vs. convergence speed) in experiments.

Originality:
- The core algorithmic ideas are intuitive and echo standard gossip/averaging mechanisms. The perceived novelty may therefore be limited. A stronger empirical comparison could help establish distinctiveness and practical advantage.

Significance:
- The problem is interesting and relevant to distributed learning and inference, and practical impact could be significant if the methods demonstrably outperform reasonable baselines. As it stands, the lack of comparative baselines weakens the case for impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include comparisons with intuitive baseline methods (e.g., adaptations of randomized averaging to pairwise computations, simple sampling-based estimators, or variants with different communication schedules).
- Provide a discussion of computational, memory, and communication costs in practice alongside convergence behavior, and quantify the trade-offs experimentally.
- Clarify the specific novelty over existing gossip approaches in the literature to better establish the contribution.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a meaningful and underexplored problem in distributed estimation.
- Algorithms are simple and practically implementable.
- Theoretical analysis is comprehensive and appears correct.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Algorithmic novelty is not clearly established; contribution may be incremental.
- Experimental evaluation lacks baseline comparisons and does not convincingly demonstrate practical advantages.
