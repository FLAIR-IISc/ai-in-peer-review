This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics (statistics based on pairs of observations) in a decentralized network. U-statistics represent a broad and important class of measures, including sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster scatter, which are more complex than the simple averages typically handled by standard gossip algorithms. The authors propose GoSta, a new family of synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms designed specifically for this task. The core idea involves nodes not only averaging their local estimates but also swapping their initial data observations with neighbors. This mechanism effectively creates a random walk of the data points across the network, allowing each node's estimate to eventually be influenced by all pairs of observations. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, showing convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous versions, respectively. They also demonstrate empirically that their method outperforms the prior U2-gossip algorithm.

Overall, this paper presents a nice contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms by extending their applicability to a more complex class of statistics. The proposed approach is elegant and the analysis appears sound. However, the paper could be significantly strengthened by making a clearer connection to existing ideas and alternative approaches.

(Note: This review is based on the main manuscript; the correctness of the supplementary material has not been verified.)

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Qualitative Evaluation)**

*   **Quality:** The paper appears technically sound, providing convergence rate guarantees for the proposed algorithms. The experimental results support the theoretical claims by showing favorable performance against the main competitor from prior work. The main weakness is the lack of comparison against other natural, albeit potentially more memory-intensive, baseline methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written. However, the intuition behind the core algorithmic mechanism—the swapping of observations to create a random walk—could be explained more clearly in the main text. This would also serve to better motivate the "phantom network" construction used in the analysis.
*   **Originality:** The application of a gossip-style algorithm to the computation of U-statistics is novel. The analytical technique using a "phantom network" is interesting, though it bears a strong resemblance to existing theoretical concepts like "lifting" in Markov chain analysis, a connection that is currently missing from the paper.
*   **Significance:** The ability to efficiently compute U-statistics in a decentralized manner is a significant problem with applications in sensor networks and large-scale data analysis. The proposed algorithm is a valuable contribution. However, its practical significance is difficult to fully assess without comparison to a wider range of baselines.

**Detailed Comments for the Authors**

1.  **Algorithmic Intuition:** The core algorithmic mechanism relies on swapping initial observations between nodes during an averaging step. This causes the observations themselves to perform a coupled random walk on the network. This is a clever idea, but its motivation and intuition could be highlighted more clearly in the main text. Doing so would also better motivate the "phantom network" construction used in the analysis.

2.  **Related Work Context:** The literature review on gossip algorithms could be strengthened. The current discussion is somewhat cursory. Situating the work within the broader context established by comprehensive surveys, such as the Dimakis et al. (2010) Proceedings of the IEEE paper, would help readers better understand its specific contribution to the state of the art.

3.  **Ambiguity in Edge Selection:** On page 4, the description "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous. Please clarify the precise distribution over edges that is assumed (e.g., uniform over all edges in E?). This is particularly important for the asynchronous model and the definition of `pk`.

4.  **Comparison to a Flooding Baseline:** A key comparison is missing. A natural alternative approach is a "flooding-based" method, where each node broadcasts its observation, and other nodes store all observations they receive. Each node could then compute the U-statistic on its locally available subset of data. Comparing GoSta to this baseline would be highly informative. It would help demonstrate the trade-offs between memory constraints and convergence speed and provide crucial context for whether the O(1/t) rate is truly efficient. For well-connected graphs like expanders, the flooding approach might be very competitive.

5.  **Connection to Lifting Constructions:** The analytical technique involving "phantom nodes" appears closely related to the concept of "lifting" Markov chains to accelerate mixing, a well-established idea in theoretical computer science and information theory. The authors should investigate this connection and discuss it in the paper, as it would properly position the paper's theoretical contribution. Relevant works include:
    *   Chen, F., Lovasz, L., & Pak, I. (1999). Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing. STOC.
    *   Jung, K., Shah, D., & Shin, J. (2007). Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains. Allerton.
    *   Li, W., & Dai, H. (2010). Location-aided fast distributed consensus. IEEE T-IT.

6.  **Dependence on Initial Data:** The theoretical guarantees in Theorem 1 are presented without explicitly stating the assumptions on the initial data vector X. Gossip algorithm analyses typically provide worst-case guarantees over all possible initial data distributions. Please clarify if that is the case here and make the dependence of the error bounds on the initial data more explicit in the problem statement and theorem.

**Minor Typos and Phrasing**

*   p1: The definition of a bipartite graph using `(S x T) U (T x S)` could be interpreted as implying directed edges. Consider using `E ⊆ {(s, t) | s ∈ S, t ∈ T}` for an undirected graph.
*   p4: "let us stress out that" -> "we stress that"
*   p6: "requires to initiate" -> "requires initiating"
*   p7: "allows to create" -> "allows us to create"

**Summary and Recommendation**

This paper presents a well-motivated and interesting extension of gossip algorithms to the important problem of computing U-statistics. The proposed GoSta algorithms are elegant and supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in decentralized computation.
*   Proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous algorithms.
*   Provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees.
*   Demonstrates superior performance over the most directly related prior work.

**Arguments for Rejection (or conditional acceptance):**
*   The manuscript in its current form fails to connect the work to important related concepts (e.g., lifting constructions) and alternative approaches (e.g., flooding).
*   The context provided in the related work section is insufficient.
*   Some key details regarding the algorithm and analysis lack clarity.

The manuscript could be significantly improved by better contextualizing its contributions. Addressing the points raised above—particularly strengthening the related work section, comparing against a flooding-based baseline in the experiments, and explicitly discussing the connection to lifting constructions—would substantially increase the paper's impact and clarity.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

Based on the author's feedback indicating a willingness to incorporate these changes, I am confident that a revised version of this paper will represent a strong contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, contingent on a thorough revision that addresses the points detailed in this review.
