This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics in networks, a less explored area compared to distributed average estimation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and provide convergence rate bounds – O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case – with explicit dependence on data and network properties. The motivation is clear, as U-statistics are fundamental for describing global population properties and appear in applications like AUC calculation, variance estimation, and clustering analysis. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through numerical experiments on two real datasets (AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter), showing superior performance compared to a previously introduced baseline method (U2-gossip) across different network topologies. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms broadens the applicability of the proposed techniques. The convergence analysis is a significant contribution, providing theoretical guarantees for the algorithm's performance and insight into the factors influencing convergence speed (data dependency and network spectral gap).  This work builds on a substantial body of research in gossip algorithms [19, 18, 5] and extends them to handle the more complex computation of U-statistics, directly addressing a limitation of existing methods [17]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The application of gossip algorithms to U-statistic estimation is a novel contribution, filling a gap in the existing literature.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The provided convergence rate bounds are a strong point, demonstrating a rigorous analysis of the proposed algorithms.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms over the baseline U2-gossip method. The use of multiple network topologies adds to the robustness of the evaluation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical foundations.
* **Practical Relevance:**  U-statistics are important in various machine learning and data mining applications, making this work potentially impactful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Runtime Analysis:** While the paper presents convergence rates, it lacks a detailed comparison of the *runtime per iteration* between the proposed algorithms and U2-gossip. If each iteration of the proposed method is significantly slower, the overall practical benefit could be diminished, especially for large networks.  Providing this information would significantly strengthen the practical relevance of the results.
* **Limited Baselines:** While a comparison to U2-gossip is present, comparing to other relevant distributed estimation techniques, even if not specifically designed for U-statistics, would further establish the value of the proposed algorithms.
* **Network Model Simplification:** The paper assumes undirected graphs. Exploring the performance of the algorithms in directed graphs or networks with more complex structures might be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms.
* The convergence analysis provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior and performance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed runtime analysis per iteration raises concerns about the practical efficiency of the proposed algorithms.
* The limited comparison to other baselines could leave the reader wondering about the relative advantages of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation are all commendable. While the lack of detailed runtime analysis is a concern, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with a minor revision request to include a more thorough comparison of the runtime per iteration for both proposed algorithms and the baseline method.
