

This paper addresses the problem of computing higher-order network statistics (e.g., variance, correlation) in a decentralized setting via gossip algorithms, where observations are distributed across nodes. The authors propose a novel gossip-based method for aggregating U-statistics by exchanging both estimates and secondary state variables representing initial observations. While the core idea is valuable for extending gossip to complex statistics, the manuscript requires significant revisions to clarify its contributions relative to prior work and strengthen the technical exposition.  

**Major Strengths**  
The approach is conceptually sound and addresses a relevant gap in decentralized computation for sensor networks. The algorithm’s design—where secondary states enable coupled random walks of initial observations—provides a natural mechanism for convergence. The mathematical formulation is generally clear, and the experimental results demonstrate feasibility.  

**Key Concerns**  

1. **Contextualization with Prior Work**: The manuscript inadequately situates the work within the broader literature on gossip-based statistics. The Dimakis et al. (2010) survey on distributed computation over networks is foundational and should be explicitly engaged with, particularly regarding methods for computing non-linear statistics. The current discussion of related work is superficial; a detailed comparison (e.g., how this approach differs from linear averaging or entropy estimation techniques) would clarify the novelty. Additionally, the lifting constructions for accelerating gossip (e.g., Jung et al., 2007; Chen et al., 1999; Li & Dai, 2010) are highly relevant. The authors should explicitly discuss how their coupling mechanism relates to these lifting techniques, as this could significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.  

2. **Algorithmic Motivation and Baseline Comparison**: The manuscript does not sufficiently justify why averaging (over flooding) is preferable under memory constraints. A natural alternative—where nodes store received observations (tagged by source node) and compute U-statistics locally—deserves analysis. For expander graphs, flooding may achieve faster convergence with sufficient memory. Without comparing the 1/t convergence rate of the proposed method to this baseline (or to memory-constrained variants), it remains unclear whether the rate is optimal or merely adequate. The authors should quantify the trade-off between memory usage and convergence speed.  

3. **Technical Clarity**:  
   - *Page 4*: The description of the edge-drawing process ("drawing a random edge of the network") lacks specification of the distribution. Clarify whether edges are sampled uniformly from the graph’s edge set or via a node-based activation process.  
   - *Theorem Assumptions*: The worst-case analysis (over initial data vectors) is not stated in the problem setup. Explicitly define the data distribution assumption and the error guarantee (e.g., "the error decays as O(1/t) for all initial data vectors X").  
   - *Page 1*: The bipartite graph definition (S × T ∪ T × S) implies directed edges; this should be corrected to match the undirected context.  

4. **Minor Presentation Issues**:  
   - Page 4: "let us stress out that" → "we stress that"  
   - Page 4: "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts"  
   - Page 6: "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating"  
   - Page 7: "allows to create" → "allows us to create"  

**Author Response and Recommendation**  
The authors’ revisions addressing the above points (e.g., clarifying the secondary state mechanism, adding context for Dimakis et al.) are commendable. The score is raised from 7 to 8. However, two critical gaps remain:  
- The lifting constructions (e.g., LADA in Li & Dai, 2010) must be explicitly discussed in the related work to avoid underplaying connections to accelerated gossip methods.  
- The manuscript must include experiments comparing the proposed method to the flooding baseline under varying memory constraints, as this is essential for justifying the algorithm’s design.  

**Conclusion**  
This paper presents a promising extension of gossip algorithms to higher-order statistics. With the requested revisions—particularly deepening the comparison to lifting-based gossip methods, adding the flooding baseline, and clarifying technical assumptions—the contribution will be significantly strengthened. As it stands, the current manuscript lacks sufficient contextualization to fully establish its novelty. Addressing these points would elevate it to a strong acceptance.  

**Final Recommendation**: **Accept (after revisions)**. The authors have responded well to initial feedback, but the lifting constructions and baseline comparison remain essential for a definitive contribution.
