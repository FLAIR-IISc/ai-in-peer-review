This paper is about computing high-order statistics measured over pairs of observations, where the observations themselves are distributed across a network of nodes. These U-statistics include several quantities of interest such as sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients, and other aggregate measures. The main goal of this paper is to propose a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm for computing these statistics.

Overall, this is a good contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, but the authors should make better connections to existing approaches and ideas.

This is a "heavy" review. I did not have time to verify the accuracy of the material in the supplement.

Comments:

1) The main trick on the algorithm's side is that when two nodes exchange their predicted values and take their average, they also swap a secondary state variable representing their initial observation. This means that the initial observations are each making a random move (although paired). It would help to mention this more explicitly in the main manuscript and would also motivate the analysis method through several copies of the graph.
2) There has been a lot of work on gossip approaches to computing statistics beyond the references cited by the authors. A snapshot of the state of the art a few years ago is the Dimakis et al. Proc. IEEE paper. Positioning the current work in relation to the works mentioned there may be more helpful than some superficial explanations in the current manuscript.
3) It may be appropriate to clarify on page 4 what the inspired distribution on the edges is from the "node wakes up, contacts a neighbor" process. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" does not specify the distribution.
4) A natural other approach to this problem is to allow more memory at each node and essentially use the same contact process to fill each node with its value in the network. Nodes store the observations they receive (tagged with the node that initially had it). Each time, nodes can calculate the U statistic on the subset of data they have. How will this compare to the current method? It may help to show why averaging may be helpful, or show the loss in performance when memory is limited at each node. Without this comparison, it is not clear whether the rate 1/t is good or bad. The flood method may be quite good for expanders.
5) The authors should take a look at the work on lifting construction to speed up the gossip algorithm, as it seems quite related to their construction in this paper. See for example:

K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast Gossip via Lifted Markov Chains," Proc. IEEE paper. Alerton Conference on Com, Control, and Comp., Urbana-Champaign, IL, September 2007. W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-Assisted Fast Distributed Consensus," IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 56(12) in 2010. F. Chen, L. Lovaz, and I. Pak, "Lifting Markov Chains to Speed Up Mixing," Proceedings of the Thirty-First Annual ACM Conference on Theory of Computing. ACM, 1999, pp. 275-281.
6) Analyzing the gossip algorithm leads to the notion of worst-case scenarios on the initial data distribution. That is, the guarantee is on all initial data vectors $X$. This is not clear in the problem statement in the statement of the theorem. What is the notion on the data distribution and what is the guarantee on the error as a function of the initial data?

After the author's response: I think the explanations and revisions suggested by the authors are good. I am revising my initial score of 7 to 8.
I think they should still keep a closer eye on lifting construction (especially LADA) and revise the related work.
In addition, experiments should include baseline comparisons.
This is a well-presented, promising approach to extending gossip methods for computing more complex statistics in distributed settings such as sensor networks. The mathematical formulation and connection to prior work are a bit unclear. Assuming that the authors make a good revision and address the comments raised in this review, I recommend accepting this paper.
