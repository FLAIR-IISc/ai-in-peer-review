This paper addresses the problem of computing higher-order statistics – including variance, correlation, and other aggregate measures – from pairs of observations distributed across a network. The core contribution is a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for averaging and aggregating these statistics. 

Overall, this is a valuable addition to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough contextualization within existing work in the field.

Please note: Due to time constraints, a detailed verification of the supplementary material was not possible.

**Specific Comments:**

1) A key aspect of the proposed algorithm is the exchange of both estimate values *and* the associated initial observation between nodes during averaging. This induces a coupled random walk for the initial observations. Explicitly highlighting this in the manuscript would improve clarity and motivate the subsequent analysis utilizing multiple graph copies.

2) The related work section could be strengthened by acknowledging the broader landscape of gossip approaches for statistical computation. The work of Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) provides a useful snapshot of the state-of-the-art and offers a relevant context for positioning this contribution. A more detailed comparison to these existing approaches would be beneficial.

3) On page 4, the induced distribution on edges arising from the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process warrants clarification. The statement "drawing a random edge of the network" lacks sufficient specificity regarding the underlying distribution.

4) A valuable comparison point would be to consider an alternative approach leveraging increased memory at each node. Allowing nodes to flood their initial values across the network, storing received observations tagged with their origin, would enable local U-statistic computation. Comparing the performance of this flooding method to the current averaging approach could illuminate the benefits of the latter, or reveal potential performance limitations, particularly given the reported rate of 1/t. For example, on expander graphs, the flooding method might prove highly competitive.

5) The authors should investigate the relevant literature on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, as there appears to be a strong connection to their proposed approach. Relevant works include those by Jung et al. (Allerton 2007), Li and Dai (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 2010), and Chen, Lovasz, and Pak (STOC 1999).

6) The analysis of gossip algorithms typically operates under a worst-case assumption regarding the initial data distribution – guarantees are usually provided across all possible initial data vectors *X*. This assumption should be explicitly stated in the problem statement and theorem statement, along with a clear articulation of the error guarantees as a function of the initial data.

**Minor Points:**

*   p1: The bipartite definition (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs.
*   p4: “let us stress out that” --> “we stress that”
*   p4: “awakes and contact” --> “awakes and contacts”
*   p6: “requires to initiate” --> “requires initiating”
*   p7: “allows to create” --> “allows us to create”

**Post-Rebuttal:** The authors' clarifications and modifications are well-executed, prompting a revision of the initial score from a 7 to an 8.

However, a closer examination of lifting constructions (specifically LADA) and a revision of the related work section are still recommended. Furthermore, the inclusion of baseline comparisons within the experimental results would strengthen the paper. 

In conclusion, this is a well-presented approach to extending gossip methods for complex statistical computation in distributed environments. While the mathematical formulation and connection to prior work could be clarified, I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the raised comments and incorporating the suggested revisions.
