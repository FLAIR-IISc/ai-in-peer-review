This paper addresses the important but relatively under-explored problem of estimating U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. While standard gossip methods excel at computing separable functions like averages, they are not directly applicable to U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations. Such statistics are crucial for many machine learning tasks, including calculating sample variance, Area Under the Curve (AUC), and within-cluster point scatter.

The authors propose novel synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) randomized gossip algorithms to compute degree-two U-statistics. The core idea is to combine two iterative processes at each node: a data propagation step, where auxiliary observations perform a random walk on the network, and an averaging step, where nodes share their local estimates. This approach allows each node's estimate to converge to the global U-statistic.

A key contribution is the rigorous theoretical analysis of these algorithms. The authors establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. The analysis, based on a clever reformulation using "phantom nodes," reveals how the convergence rates depend explicitly on both data-dependent terms (quantifying the estimation difficulty) and network-dependent terms (related to the spectral gap of the network graph). The proposed methods are shown to improve upon the prior work of Pelckmans & Suykens [17] by achieving faster convergence with lower memory and communication costs. The theoretical claims are well-supported by experiments on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation across various network topologies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a significant problem in distributed machine learning. The proposed "GoSta" algorithms provide a novel and practical solution for decentralized U-statistic estimation, substantially advancing the state of the art beyond the only known previous work [17]. The ability to handle this important class of statistics opens up new possibilities for decentralized analysis.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical contribution is strong. The derivation of convergence rates is non-trivial, and the use of the "phantom network" abstraction is an elegant analytical tool that effectively decouples the data propagation and estimate averaging processes. The resulting bounds clearly connect the algorithm's performance to fundamental properties of the network and the data, which is a desirable feature for such analyses.

3.  **Practical Advantages:** Compared to the U2-gossip algorithm [17], the proposed methods offer clear practical benefits. GoSta-sync requires storing only one auxiliary observation per node (instead of two) and involves only one pair of nodes communicating at each step (instead of two). This reduces memory and communication overhead, which is especially important for high-dimensional data. The introduction of a fully asynchronous version is also a major practical advantage, as it removes the need for a global clock.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the algorithms are presented precisely, and the main theoretical results are stated clearly. The experimental section is well-designed and convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed algorithms over the baseline.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is a solid contribution, and the following points are minor suggestions for potential improvement:

1.  In the asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async), each node `k` needs to know its probability `pk = 2dk/|E|` to compute an unbiased estimate of the current iteration number. This implies that each node must know not only its own degree `dk` (which is local information) but also the total number of edges `|E|` in the network (which is global information). While `|E|` can often be computed with a separate gossip-based averaging protocol, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the practical implications of this requirement.

2.  The experimental results for the asynchronous algorithm show performance that is nearly identical to the synchronous version. This is an excellent practical result, but it contrasts slightly with the theoretical rates (O(log t/t) vs. O(1/t)). A brief comment on why this gap between theory and practice might exist would be insightful. For instance, is the `log t` factor in the bound loose, or are the constants such that the difference is negligible in these experimental regimes?

3.  Remark 2 briefly mentions extending the method to handle multiple observations per node. This is a very relevant scenario for practical applications. While a full analysis is likely beyond the scope of this paper, expanding this remark with a little more detail or intuition—for example, on how one might "judiciously" set edge weights in the conceptual sub-graph—would add value for practitioners seeking to implement the algorithm in such settings.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-analyzed, and empirically validated solution to an important problem. It is a clear advance over previous work and will likely be of interest to researchers in distributed optimization, gossip algorithms, and large-scale machine learning.
