This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics in distributed networks, a less explored area than distributed mean estimation. The authors propose new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for this task, providing convergence rate bounds and empirical validation. The core idea – combining data propagation with local averaging – is intuitive and builds upon the well-established field of gossip algorithms [18, 19, 5]. This work extends the applicability of gossip algorithms to a broader class of statistical computations crucial for various applications like AUC calculation, variance estimation, and clustering analysis [12, 14].

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. The theoretical analysis, including the convergence rate proofs and the use of “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem, demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. The convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case are valuable contributions. The explicit data and network dependency in these bounds provide a nuanced understanding of algorithm performance. The comparison to the existing approach in [17] is positive, highlighting improvements in convergence, memory usage, and communication cost. 

However, the paper suffers from a significant weakness in its experimental evaluation. While the authors present results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter, they lack comparison to relevant baselines. Simple randomized algorithms or existing decentralized estimation techniques adapted for U-statistics would serve as appropriate benchmarks. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to fully assess the significance of the proposed algorithms’ improvements. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, addressing it would substantially strengthen the paper.  

Furthermore, the presentation could be improved by including a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed algorithms. For example, the authors mention the potential for performance degradation with poorly connected networks, but a deeper exploration of this sensitivity and potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial.  The notation is dense in certain sections (e.g., Section 4.1), and simplifying it could improve readability. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Decentralized U-statistic estimation is a relevant and under-explored area.
*   **Sound theoretical analysis:** The convergence rate analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
*   **Clear algorithm description:** The proposed algorithms are presented in a clear and concise manner.
*   **Positive comparison to existing work:**  Demonstrates improvement over the method in [17].

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of baseline comparisons:** The absence of comparison to relevant baselines hinders assessment of the algorithms’ significance.
*   **Limited discussion of limitations:**  A more thorough discussion of the algorithms' weaknesses and potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial.
*   **Dense notation:** Some sections are difficult to follow due to the complex notation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The convergence rate analysis provides valuable guarantees on algorithm performance.
*   The experimental results, while limited, suggest the algorithms are effective in practice.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of baseline comparisons significantly weakens the evaluation.
*   The limitations of the algorithms are not thoroughly discussed.
*   The dense notation hinders readability in some sections.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The theoretical contributions are solid, and the problem addressed is important. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly the absence of baseline comparisons, is a significant concern. If the authors can address this weakness by including comparisons to relevant baselines, the paper’s value would be substantially increased and I would recommend a stronger acceptance.  As it stands, the paper presents a worthwhile contribution but falls short of demonstrating its full potential impact.
