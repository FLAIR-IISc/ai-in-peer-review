This paper studies the decentralized computation of higher-order statistics measured over pairs of observations in a networked setting. These U-statistics include many quantities of interest, such as sample variance, correlation, scatter coefficients, and related aggregate measures. The main contribution is a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm to compute these statistics.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the gossip literature, but the connection to prior approaches and known ideas should be strengthened.

This is a heavy review. I did not have time to verify the correctness of the material in the supplement.

Comments
1) Algorithmic insight: When two nodes exchange and average their estimates, they also swap a secondary state variable containing their initial observation. Thus, the initial observations themselves execute (coupled) random walks over the network. Stating this more explicitly in the main text would clarify the intuition and naturally motivate the analysis via multiple copies (lifts) of the graph.

2) Related work: There is substantial prior work on gossip for computing statistics beyond the references cited. A useful overview is Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE (2010). Positioning the present work within that broader context—rather than the somewhat cursory discussion in the current draft—would help readers understand what is new here and how it relates to existing techniques.

3) Edge selection distribution: On page 4, please clarify the distribution over edges induced by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” is ambiguous without specifying the exact edge-selection distribution (e.g., whether edges are chosen proportional to node degrees, uniformly over edges, etc.).

4) Alternative baseline with more memory: A natural comparison is a flooding-based approach that uses more memory per node. Each node could use the same contact process to flood its observation across the network, storing received observations tagged by their origin. At any time, nodes could compute the U-statistic on the subset they have collected. How does this compare to your method? Such a comparison would clarify when averaging provides advantages, and what performance loss occurs when memory is limited. Without this, the 1/t rate is hard to interpret—on expander graphs, the flooding baseline might be very competitive.

5) Lifting constructions: Please discuss prior work on lifting Markov chains to accelerate gossip/consensus, which appears closely related to your construction. Relevant references include:
- K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., 2007.
- W. Li and H. Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE Trans. Info. Theory, 56(12), 2010.
- F. Chen, L. Lovász, and I. Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” STOC, 1999, pp. 275–281.

6) Data assumptions and guarantees: Analyses of gossip algorithms typically provide guarantees that are worst-case over all initial data vectors X. This is not clear in your problem setup or the theorem statements. What are the assumptions on the data distribution, and what are the error guarantees as a function of the initial data? Please state whether guarantees are worst-case over X, in expectation over X, or under some distributional assumptions.

Small things
- p1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs.
- p4: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”
- p4: “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”
- p6: “requires to initiate” → “requires initiating”
- p7: “allows to create” → “allows us to create”

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK: The clarifications and modifications proposed by the authors are helpful. I am increasing my initial score from 7 to 8. I still recommend that the authors closely engage with the lifting constructions (especially LADA) and revise the related work accordingly. In addition, the experiments should include the baseline comparisons discussed above.

This is a clear and well-motivated approach to extending gossip methods to compute more complex statistics in distributed settings such as sensor networks. However, the mathematical framing and connection to prior work need to be sharpened. Assuming a careful revision that addresses the points above—particularly the relation to lifted Markov chain methods, the precise edge-selection process, the data assumptions in the guarantees, and the inclusion of strong baselines in experiments—I recommend accepting this paper.
