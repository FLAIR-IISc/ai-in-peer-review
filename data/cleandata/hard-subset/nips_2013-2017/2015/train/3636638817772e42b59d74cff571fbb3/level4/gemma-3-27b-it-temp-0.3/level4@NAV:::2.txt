This paper addresses the problem of decentralized computation of higher-order statistics – including variance, correlation, and other aggregate measures – over pairs of observations distributed across a network. The core contribution is a gossip-style algorithm for averaging and aggregating these statistics. 

Overall, this is a valuable addition to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, the paper would benefit from a stronger connection to, and discussion of, existing related work. I did not have time to fully verify the correctness of the material presented in the supplementary materials.

Here are some specific comments:

1) The algorithm’s key innovation – exchanging both estimate values *and* initial observation data during averaging – effectively induces a coupled random walk for the initial observations. Explicitly highlighting this aspect in the manuscript would improve clarity and motivate the analysis using multiple graph copies.

2) The related work section could be significantly strengthened by acknowledging the broader landscape of gossip approaches for statistical computation. The work of Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) provides a useful snapshot of the state-of-the-art a few years ago, and positioning this work within that context would be beneficial.

3) On page 4, the description of the induced distribution on edges resulting from the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process lacks specificity. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” doesn’t fully define the distribution.

4) A compelling comparison point would be to consider an alternative approach utilizing increased memory at each node to flood individual values across the network, storing received observations tagged with their origin. This would allow nodes to calculate the U-statistic on their local data subset. Comparing the performance of this flooding method to the proposed averaging approach could clarify the advantages of the latter, or reveal potential performance limitations, and contextualize the achieved rate of 1/t. For example, expander graphs might favor the flooding approach.

5) The authors should investigate and discuss the relevance of lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, as they appear closely related to their approach. Relevant works include Jung et al. (Allerton 2007), Li & Dai (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 2010), and Chen, Lovasz, & Pak (STOC 1999).

6) The analysis of gossip algorithms typically assumes a worst-case initial data distribution, providing guarantees across all possible initial data vectors *X*. This assumption should be clearly stated in the problem statement and theorem statement, along with a specification of the error guarantees as a function of the initial data.

Minor points: p1: The bipartite definition (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs. p4: “let us stress out that” --> “we stress that”. p4: “awakes and contact” --> “awakes and contacts”. p6: “requires to initiate” --> “requires initiating”. p7: “allows to create” --> “allows us to create”.

**Following Author Feedback:** The authors’ clarifications and modifications are well-received, and I am revising my initial score from a 7 to an 8. 

However, a more thorough examination of lifting constructions (particularly LADA) and an update to the related work section remain necessary. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation should include baseline comparisons.

In conclusion, this is a well-presented approach to extending gossip methods for computing complex statistics in distributed settings like sensor networks. While the mathematical formulation and connection to prior work could be clearer, I recommend acceptance pending a strong revision addressing the points raised in this review.
