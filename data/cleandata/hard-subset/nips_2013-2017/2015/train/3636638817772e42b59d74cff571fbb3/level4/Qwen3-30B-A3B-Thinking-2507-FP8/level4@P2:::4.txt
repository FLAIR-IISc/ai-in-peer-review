

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networked systems, which generalizes the more commonly studied problem of estimating separable statistics (like averages) via gossip algorithms. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics, which are crucial for various statistical measures including Area Under the Curve (AUC), empirical variance, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter. The work extends the literature on gossip algorithms [19, 18, 5] by addressing a significant gap: existing methods cannot efficiently compute quantities that depend on pairs of observations, while the proposed GoSta algorithms simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic.

The paper's main strengths include a clear problem formulation, comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rate bounds (O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous cases), and thorough experimental validation. The theoretical contributions are substantial, with the authors introducing an original "phantom node" construction to reformulate the problem and prove convergence. The convergence rates explicitly incorporate data-dependent terms (reflecting estimation difficulty) and network-dependent terms (related to the spectral gap), demonstrating that the algorithms perform better on well-connected networks. The experimental section provides compelling evidence of the proposed algorithms' superiority over the only existing method for this problem (U2-gossip [17]), with significant improvements in both convergence speed and memory/communication efficiency across multiple real-world datasets and network topologies (complete graphs, 2D grids, and Watts-Strogatz networks).

The paper demonstrates strong quality through its rigorous mathematical analysis and thorough experimental validation. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation that provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The originality is clear: while gossip algorithms for separable statistics have been well-studied, this work addresses a fundamentally different problem class requiring novel algorithmic approaches. The significance is also well-established through the importance of U-statistics in machine learning applications and the demonstrated practical advantages of the proposed methods.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. The most significant issue is the lack of comparison with intuitive baseline methods that could be constructed for distributed U-statistic estimation. For instance, one could consider:
1. A naive distributed implementation where each node computes its local U-statistic and then averages these values
2. A simple modification of standard gossip algorithms that maintains pairwise observations
3. A centralized implementation that serves as an upper bound

The authors focus exclusively on comparison with U2-gossip [17], but do not consider these more straightforward baselines that would better demonstrate the relative advantages of their approach. While U2-gossip is the only prior work on this specific problem, the absence of these intuitive baselines weakens the experimental evaluation. The paper could be strengthened by including such comparisons to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed methods' advantages.

Another minor weakness is the somewhat limited discussion of how the phantom node construction (used in the theoretical analysis) relates to practical implementation. While the authors clarify this is purely a theoretical tool, a brief discussion of why this construction is necessary and how it connects to the actual algorithm would enhance the paper's accessibility.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed learning, addressing an important gap in the literature. The theoretical and experimental results are strong, though the evaluation could be more comprehensive with additional baseline comparisons. The algorithms are practical and demonstrate clear advantages over the only existing method for this problem.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant gap in distributed learning literature (U-statistics vs. separable statistics)
- Provides comprehensive theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages over the only existing method for this problem
- Strong experimental validation across multiple applications and network topologies
- Well-written and clear presentation

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks comparison with intuitive baseline methods that could be constructed for distributed U-statistic estimation
- Limited discussion of how the theoretical phantom node construction relates to practical implementation
- Could better emphasize the significance of the results in the context of broader distributed learning challenges

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The theoretical advances are significant, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms. While the evaluation would be strengthened by including additional baseline comparisons, the paper's core contribution is valuable and well-supported. I recommend acceptance with the suggestion that the authors consider adding comparisons with intuitive baseline methods in a revised version.
