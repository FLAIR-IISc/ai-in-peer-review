This paper introduces novel randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in distributed networks. U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are crucial for characterizing global properties in various applications—including Area Under the Curve (AUC) calculation, variance estimation, and quantifying clustering quality—but are challenging to compute in a decentralized manner. The authors establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous variant, where 't' denotes the number of iterations, and provide explicit dependencies on data and network characteristics.  Numerical experiments demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed algorithms compared to a previously introduced approach [17].

The work builds upon the well-established field of gossip algorithms [18, 19, 5], which have become a cornerstone of distributed computation due to their simplicity and robustness. Existing gossip algorithms are well-suited for computing averages or sums of functions separable across observations [10, 2, 15, 11, 9]. However, they struggle with U-statistics requiring averaging over pairs. This paper directly addresses this limitation. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the broader literature on distributed estimation and highlight the relevance of U-statistics to diverse machine learning and data mining tasks [12, 14].  The related work section is concise but effective in situating the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The algorithms presented offer a new approach to computing U-statistics in a distributed setting, extending the capabilities of traditional gossip algorithms.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence rate analysis provides valuable theoretical insights into the performance of the algorithms, considering both data-dependent and network-dependent factors. The connection to the spectral gap of the network graph is a notable strength [3].
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The numerical experiments using real-world datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) provide compelling evidence of the algorithms' effectiveness and superiority over existing methods [17].
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, facilitating understanding of the proposed algorithms and their analysis.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed—efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed environment—has significant practical implications for applications involving large-scale datasets and decentralized computation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Phantom Node Concept:** While the use of "phantom nodes" is clever for the theoretical analysis, it adds a layer of complexity to the explanation.  A more intuitive explanation of why this reformulation is necessary might benefit the reader.
*   **Asynchronous Convergence:** The convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm (O(log t/t)) is slightly weaker than the synchronous rate (O(1/t)). A deeper exploration of the trade-offs between synchrony and asynchrony, and potentially strategies for improving the asynchronous rate, could be valuable.
*   **Network Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the assumption of a connected and non-bipartite graph.  Discussing the algorithms' behavior or potential modifications for other network topologies (e.g., disconnected graphs or bipartite graphs) would broaden the applicability of the work.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the comparison to [17] is valuable, including a comparison to more standard distributed estimation techniques (even if they don’t directly address U-statistics) could provide a more comprehensive benchmark.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem with clear practical relevance.
*   The proposed algorithms are novel and offer significant advantages over existing methods.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the algorithms’ convergence behavior.
*   The empirical results validate the algorithms’ effectiveness on real-world datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The “phantom node” concept might be difficult for some readers to grasp.
*   The asynchronous convergence rate is relatively weak.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The algorithms presented are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors consider clarifying the explanation of the "phantom node" concept and potentially expanding the experimental evaluation to include comparisons with a broader range of baseline algorithms.
