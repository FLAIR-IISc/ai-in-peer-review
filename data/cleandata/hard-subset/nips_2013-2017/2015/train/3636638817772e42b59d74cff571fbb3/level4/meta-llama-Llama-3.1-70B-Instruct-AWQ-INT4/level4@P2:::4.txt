This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics in a decentralized setting. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the convergence rates of their algorithms and demonstrate their practical interest through numerical experiments.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction provides a thorough background on decentralized computation and estimation, highlighting the importance of gossip algorithms in this context. The authors also provide a clear problem statement and a detailed review of related work, which helps to situate their contribution.

The proposed algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, are intuitive and well-motivated. The use of "phantom nodes" to model data propagation and estimate updates is a clever idea that facilitates the convergence analysis. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the convergence rates of their algorithms, including a comparison with the U2-gossip algorithm.

The numerical experiments are convincing and demonstrate the practical interest of the proposed algorithms. The authors consider three types of networks (complete graph, two-dimensional grid, and Watts-Strogatz) and two applications (AUC measure and within-cluster point scatter). The results show that GoSta-sync outperforms U2-gossip on every network, with a lower variance of estimates across nodes.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One major weakness is the lack of comparison with baseline measures. The authors should consider including some intuitive baselines based on randomized algorithms to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their algorithms. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results and the potential applications of the proposed algorithms.

In terms of novelty, the paper may not be highly innovative, as the algorithm itself is quite intuitive. However, the comprehensive analysis and the numerical experiments make a significant contribution to the field.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.

Strengths:

* Comprehensive analysis of convergence rates
* Convincing numerical experiments
* Clear and well-organized writing

Weaknesses:

* Lack of comparison with baseline measures
* Limited novelty of the algorithm
* Need for a more detailed discussion of implications and applications
