This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics—statistics defined over pairs of observations—in a decentralized network where data is distributed among nodes. These quantities, which include sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster scatter, cannot be computed by standard gossip algorithms designed for separable functions like the sample mean. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms that enable nodes to collaboratively estimate U-statistics.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on distributed computation. The proposed algorithms are elegant and the analysis appears sound. However, the paper could be significantly strengthened by improving its clarity, providing a more thorough comparison with alternative approaches, and better situating the work within the existing literature.

**Comments**

1.  **Algorithmic Intuition:** The core mechanism of the proposed algorithm is that when two nodes average their local estimates, they also swap an auxiliary variable that represents their initial observation. This clever design ensures that the initial observations effectively perform a random walk across the network. Highlighting this intuition more explicitly early in the paper would provide readers with a clearer understanding of the algorithm's design and better motivate the subsequent analysis, which relies on multiple 'phantom' copies of the graph.

2.  **Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be expanded to better position this contribution within the broader literature on gossip algorithms. The current survey is somewhat brief. For instance, consulting a comprehensive survey such as the Proceedings of the IEEE paper by Dimakis et al. would provide a valuable snapshot of the state of the art and help contextualize the paper's novelty and significance more effectively.

3.  **Connection to Lifting Constructions:** The analytical technique involving 'phantom nodes' and multiple graph copies appears closely related to the literature on 'lifting' Markov chains to accelerate mixing and consensus in gossip algorithms. This connection is worth exploring and discussing in the paper. We encourage the authors to review this line of work (e.g., Jung et al., 2007; Li and Dai, 2010; Chen et al., 1999) and comment on the relationship between their analytical framework and these existing constructions.

4.  **Baseline Comparison:** A comparison to a natural baseline algorithm would help demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method. One such baseline would be a flooding-based approach: using the same communication process, each node could broadcast its initial observation to its neighbors, and nodes would store all unique observations they receive. Each node could then compute the U-statistic locally on its growing subset of the data. Comparing against this baseline would provide a clearer context for the O(1/t) convergence rate and help quantify the benefits of the proposed averaging scheme, particularly in relation to memory limitations. Such a comparison may be especially insightful on well-connected networks like expanders, where flooding can be very efficient.

5.  **Clarification of the Communication Model:** The description of the communication model needs clarification. On page 4, Algorithm 1 states to "Draw (i, j) uniformly at random from E," but the text also mentions processes where a node "wakes up" and contacts a neighbor. The authors should specify the exact probability distribution over the edges E used in the algorithms and analysis.

6.  **Assumptions on Initial Data:** The theoretical guarantees should be clarified with respect to the initial data. Typically, analyses of gossip algorithms provide worst-case bounds that hold for any initial data vector. It is not entirely clear if that is the case here. The authors should explicitly state what assumptions, if any, are made on the data and clarify how the convergence bounds depend on the initial data values held by the nodes.

**Minor Points**

*   Please proofread the manuscript for minor grammatical errors. For example:
    *   (p. 4) "we stress that" is preferable to "let us stress out that".
    *   (p. 6) "requires initiating" is preferable to "requires to initiate".
    *   (p. 7) "allows us to create" is preferable to "allows to create".

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a well-motivated and elegant approach to a challenging problem in decentralized computation. The proposed algorithms are a significant extension of gossip methods to a new class of statistics. With revisions to address the points raised above—particularly regarding the connection to related work, the comparison with a flooding-based baseline, and clarification of the technical details—this paper would be a strong contribution to the conference. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, contingent on a thorough revision.
