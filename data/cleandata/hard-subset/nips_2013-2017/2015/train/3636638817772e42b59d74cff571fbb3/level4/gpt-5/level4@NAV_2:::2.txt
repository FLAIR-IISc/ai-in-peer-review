This paper tackles decentralized computation of pairwise (degree-2) U-statistics when observations are distributed across a network. These statistics capture many global quantities of interest (e.g., variance, rank correlation, AUC, within-cluster scatter). The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip methods that maintain local estimates while propagating data through the network. They prove convergence in expectation at rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants that explicitly depend on both the data and the spectral properties of the network, and report empirical improvements over U2-gossip.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the gossip literature, though a tighter connection to existing methods and ideas would strengthen the paper.

This is a “heavy” review. I did not have time to check the supplemental material for correctness.

Comments
1) Algorithmic mechanism. A key idea is that when two nodes average their local estimates, they also swap an auxiliary state holding their original observation. Consequently, the original observations execute coupled random walks over the graph. Making this mechanism more explicit in the main text would improve readability and help motivate the analysis via multiple graph copies (“phantom nodes”).

2) Positioning within prior work. There is a substantial body of work on gossip for computing nontrivial aggregates beyond the references currently cited. The survey by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) gives a broad view; positioning this work against that landscape—rather than the current brief related-work section—would clarify what is new here relative to established techniques.

3) Edge-selection distribution. On page 4, please clarify the induced edge-selection distribution under the “node wakes up and contacts a neighbor” model. The phrase “draw a random edge” is ambiguous without specifying the distribution over edges.

4) Alternative baseline via flooding. A natural comparator is to grant more memory per node and use the same contact process to flood each node’s sample throughout the network. Nodes store received observations (tagged by origin) and compute the U-statistic on what they have at each time. How would this approach compare, especially on well-connected graphs (e.g., expanders)? This comparison could illustrate when averaging is beneficial and quantify the cost of memory constraints; otherwise, it is hard to assess whether a 1/t rate is optimal or suboptimal in practice.

5) Relation to lifted Markov chains. There is relevant work on lifting to accelerate mixing/consensus that seems connected to your construction (e.g., the “phantom” graph). See:
- K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Allerton 2007.
- W. Li and H. Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE Trans. Info. Theory, 2010.
- F. Chen, L. Lovász, and I. Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” STOC 1999.
Discussing similarities/differences and the potential to incorporate lifting ideas could broaden the impact.

6) Data assumptions and guarantees. Gossip analyses often provide worst-case guarantees over all initial data vectors X. The problem setup and theorems here could more clearly state assumptions on the data and whether the error bounds are uniform over X or distributional. Please make explicit what randomness is averaged over (graph edge draws vs. data) and how the bounds scale with the data-dependent terms.

Small things
- p1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) suggests directed edges; please clarify for undirected graphs.
- p4: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”
- p4: “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”
- p6: “requires to initiate” → “requires initiating”
- p7: “allows to create” → “allows us to create”

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK: The authors’ clarifications and planned revisions address several of the concerns. I am raising my score from 7 to 8. I still recommend incorporating the lifting literature (especially LADA) into related work and adding the baseline flooding-style comparisons in the experiments.

This is a well-presented extension of gossip to U-statistics in distributed settings (e.g., sensor networks). With the suggested clarifications and additions, I recommend acceptance.
