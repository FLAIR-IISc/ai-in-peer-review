This paper proposes a decentralized, gossip-style algorithm for computing higher-order statistics over pairs of observations distributed across a network. The target U-statistics include many important quantities, such as sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by better situating the work within the context of existing approaches.

This is a detailed review. Please note that I did not have time to verify the correctness of the supplementary material.

**Comments**

1) The core algorithmic mechanism involves nodes not only averaging their current estimates but also swapping a secondary state variable that represents their initial observation. This effectively causes the initial observations to perform a coupled random walk on the network. Highlighting this mechanism more explicitly in the main text would improve clarity and provide a stronger motivation for the analysis, which relies on multiple copies of the graph.

2) A significant body of work exists on gossip-based computation of statistics beyond the references cited. For instance, the survey by Dimakis et al. in *Proceedings of the IEEE* provides a comprehensive overview of the state of the art from several years ago. This paper would be improved by situating its contributions within the broader context established by that survey and related works. Such a discussion would be more informative than the current, somewhat cursory, treatment of the literature.

3) On page 4, the authors should clarify the induced distribution over edges that results from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous and does not sufficiently specify this distribution.

4) A natural alternative approach to this problem would be to use a flooding-based method, where each node broadcasts its initial value across the network. If nodes have sufficient memory, they can store all received observations (tagged by their originating node) and compute the U-statistic locally on the subset of data they have accumulated. A comparison to this baseline would be highly informative. It could help demonstrate the benefits of the proposed averaging scheme or quantify the performance trade-offs under memory constraints. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the stated convergence rate of 1/t is competitive. On well-connected graphs like expanders, a flooding approach might perform very well.

5) The proposed construction appears highly relevant to the work on "lifting" for accelerating gossip algorithms. The authors should consult this line of work and discuss the connections. See, for example:
    *   K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in *Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp.*, 2007.
    *   W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in *IEEE Transactions on Information Theory*, 56(12), 2010.
    *   F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in *Proceedings of the thirty-first annual ACM symposium on Theory of computing*, 1999.

6) The analysis of gossip algorithms is typically performed under worst-case assumptions on the initial data; that is, performance guarantees should hold for any initial data vector X. This aspect is not clearly specified in the problem statement or in the theorem itself. The authors should explicitly state their assumptions regarding the data distribution and clarify how the error guarantees depend on the initial data.

**Minor Points**
*   p1: The definition of a bipartite graph using `(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)` implies a directed graph, which may be unintentional.
*   p4: "let us stress out that" should be "we stress that".
*   p4: "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts".
*   p6: "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating".
*   p7: "allows to create" should be "allows us to create".

***

**AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK:** The clarifications and modifications proposed by the authors in their rebuttal are satisfactory. I am revising my initial score of 7 up to an 8.

I still recommend that the authors take a close look at the lifting constructions (especially LADA) and revise the related work section accordingly. Furthermore, the experiments should be updated to include the requested baseline comparisons.

**Summary Recommendation**

This is a nicely presented approach for extending gossip methods to compute complex statistics in distributed settings. However, the mathematical formulation and its connection to prior work are somewhat unclear. Assuming the authors undertake a thorough revision that addresses the comments raised in this review, I recommend accepting this paper.
