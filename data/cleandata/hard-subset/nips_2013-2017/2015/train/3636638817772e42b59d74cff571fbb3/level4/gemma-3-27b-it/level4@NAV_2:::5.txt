The paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networks, an area less explored than distributed mean estimation despite the importance of U-statistics in describing global population properties (e.g., AUC, variance). The authors propose new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively, with explicit dependencies on data and network characteristics. Empirical results demonstrate superior performance compared to a previously introduced approach [17].

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. It effectively situates the problem within the broader context of gossip algorithms and highlights the limitations of existing methods when applied to U-statistics. The technical contributions – the proposed algorithms and their convergence analyses – are significant. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, including references to foundational papers in gossip algorithms [18, 19, 5] and relevant applications of U-statistics [12, 14]. The introduction of “phantom nodes” for analysis is a clever technique. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized, with appropriate notation and definitions (Section 2). 

A key strength is the theoretical analysis providing convergence rate bounds. The bounds are not just asymptotic but include terms dependent on the data and network graph, which is valuable. The comparison to the U2-gossip algorithm [17] is well-justified, and the analysis highlighting the trade-offs (memory, communication, convergence rate) is insightful. The empirical evaluation using AUC and within-cluster point scatter on real datasets further strengthens the claims. The exploration of different network topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts-Strogatz) provides a comprehensive assessment of algorithm performance.

However, there are some minor weaknesses. While the phantom node approach is well-explained, the detailed mathematical derivations in the convergence proofs could benefit from further clarity, potentially through more detailed explanations of key steps. The discussion of related work, while thorough, could benefit from referencing more recent advancements in distributed optimization and machine learning that might be relevant, even if not directly comparable. For example, work on federated learning or decentralized stochastic gradient descent could offer related perspectives. The discussion of the asynchronous algorithm (Section 4.2) feels slightly less detailed than the synchronous case and could benefit from a more in-depth comparison of the theoretical and practical implications of the different convergence rates.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper tackles an important and relatively unexplored problem – distributed U-statistic estimation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms are well-defined, and the convergence analyses appear rigorous (though the proofs could benefit from further clarity).
*   **Significance:** U-statistics are fundamental in various fields, and efficient distributed estimation has broad applicability.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Proof Clarity:** The detailed mathematical derivations could be more accessible.
*   **Related Work:** A more comprehensive review of recent advancements in distributed optimization might be beneficial.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
