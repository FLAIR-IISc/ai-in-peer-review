This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics in a decentralized setting. The authors extend gossip algorithms to handle quantities that depend on pairs of observations, which is a less investigated area. They establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively, with explicit data and network-dependent terms.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear background on the problem and related work. They introduce their approach, including the algorithms and convergence analysis, in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The numerical results demonstrate the performance advantages of the proposed algorithms over existing approaches, such as U2-gossip.

The strengths of the paper include its novel contribution to the field of gossip algorithms, its rigorous theoretical analysis, and its empirical evaluation on real-world datasets. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with existing work, highlighting the advantages and limitations of their approach.

One of the weaknesses of the paper is that it assumes a relatively simple network model, where each node has a single observation. While the authors mention that their results can be extended to the case where nodes contain multiple observations, this extension is not fully explored in the paper. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the proposed algorithms, such as their potential applications and limitations in real-world scenarios.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of gossip algorithms by extending them to handle U-statistics. The authors' approach is novel, and their convergence analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The paper also demonstrates the practical relevance of the proposed algorithms through numerical experiments.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important problem in decentralized estimation, and its results have the potential to impact various fields, such as machine learning, data mining, and sensor networks. The proposed algorithms could be used in a variety of applications, including distributed computing, clustering, and hypothesis testing.

In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of gossip algorithms, and its results demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach for decentralized estimation of U-statistics. While there are some limitations and areas for improvement, the paper is well-written, and its contributions are substantial.
