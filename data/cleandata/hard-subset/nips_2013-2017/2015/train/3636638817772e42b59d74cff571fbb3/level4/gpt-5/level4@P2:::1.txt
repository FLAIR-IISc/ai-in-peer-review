Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes two randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network: a synchronous version (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous version (GoSta-async). Each node maintains a local estimate and a single auxiliary observation that diffuses through the network via random edge selections. The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from averaging. The authors prove convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous). The bounds make explicit the dependence on the data (dispersion around local means) and on the network (spectral gap). Compared to U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2009), the proposed methods use less memory and communication (only one auxiliary observation per node and one edge activation per iteration), and achieve better theoretical and empirical performance. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across several network topologies support the claims.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: many relevant statistics (AUC, variance, Gini difference, within-cluster scatter) are U-statistics and not separable, making standard gossip averaging inapplicable.
- Novel methodology: the combination of data propagation with local averaging and the phantom-network analysis are original and insightful.
- Theoretical guarantees: explicit finite-time bounds with network-dependent rates tied to the spectral gap; improved rates over U2-gossip and careful comparison.
- Practical relevance: an asynchronous algorithm compatible with realistic, unsynchronized networks.
- Empirical validation: consistent gains over U2-gossip across tasks and graphs; lower variance across nodes due to averaging.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Guarantees are in expectation; high-probability or almost-sure convergence bounds and variance control across nodes would strengthen the contribution.
- The asynchronous bound depends on a global norm term and is looser than the synchronous one; discussing whether data-dependent refinements analogous to the synchronous case are possible would be helpful.
- The model assumes connected, non-bipartite undirected graphs and uniform random edge activation. Practical robustness to message loss, delays, time-varying or directed topologies is not discussed.
- Computing pk = 2dk/|E| requires knowledge of |E|; clarify how nodes obtain or estimate |E| locally, and the impact of misspecification.
- Experiments use two datasets and a limited set of network sizes; include communication/iteration complexity to reach a target error ε, wall-clock measurements, and sensitivity to network size/structure.
- Comparison set is narrow (primarily U2-gossip). Baselines such as push-sum adaptations, randomized pairing with reservoir sampling, or mini-batch pairwise estimators would add context.
- Some proofs are deferred; sketching the key steps (beyond the phantom construction) in the main text would improve readability.

Quality
The algorithms are well specified and the analysis is careful, with clear dependence on problem and network parameters. The comparison to U2-gossip is theoretically and empirically sound. That said, the guarantees are expectation-based, and the asynchronous bound is conservative. Experimental methodology is reasonable, though broader baselines and metrics (e.g., communication per ε-accuracy) would make the case stronger.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Algorithms are clearly described. Suggestions:
- Make explicit how nodes learn |E| in Algorithm 2, or provide a variant that does not require it.
- Briefly describe how to handle bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy randomization) to avoid periodicity.
- Clarify memory/communication costs per iteration and per node side-by-side with U2-gossip.
- Add a short intuition for the constants in the rates and their practical magnitude.

Originality
The problem—distributed estimation of U-statistics via gossip—is underexplored. The proposed synchronous and asynchronous schemes, together with the phantom-network analysis and improved bounds, represent a genuine advance over prior work.

Significance
Estimating U-statistics arises in ranking, clustering, and correlation tasks across sensor and peer-to-peer networks. The algorithms are simple, scalable, and likely to be reused, especially the asynchronous variant. Broader empirical validation and stronger probabilistic guarantees would further increase impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide high-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale techniques) for both algorithms?
- Is there a version of GoSta-async that avoids dependence on |E|, perhaps using local counters only?
- How sensitive is performance to non-uniform edge activation or degree heterogeneity?
- Could mini-batch swaps (multiple auxiliary observations) accelerate convergence in sparse graphs?
- Do your methods extend to time-varying or directed graphs?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: decentralized U-statistics.
- Sound theory with explicit dependence on data and network properties.
- Practical asynchronous algorithm with lower memory/communication than prior art.
- Consistent empirical improvements across tasks and topologies.

Cons for acceptance
- Expectation-only guarantees; conservative asynchronous rate.
- Limited baseline comparisons and experimental scope.
- Assumptions on graph and activation model may limit immediate applicability.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution with clear advances over U2-gossip, strong potential utility, and good clarity. With additional empirical breadth and strengthened probabilistic guarantees, the paper would be even more compelling.
