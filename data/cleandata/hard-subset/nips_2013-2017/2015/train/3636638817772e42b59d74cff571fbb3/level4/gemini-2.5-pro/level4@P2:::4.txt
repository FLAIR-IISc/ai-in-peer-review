This paper addresses the problem of estimating U-statistics in a decentralized network setting using gossip algorithms. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance, within-cluster scatter), cannot be computed by standard gossip algorithms designed for separable sums like the sample mean. The authors propose two new randomized algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for synchronous and asynchronous environments, respectively. The core idea is to have each node simultaneously propagate an auxiliary data observation through the network via random swaps with neighbors, while also averaging its local estimate of the U-statistic with that of its neighbor. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, using a novel "phantom network" construction, to establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. This work directly builds upon and aims to improve the U2-gossip algorithm of Pelckmans et al. [17], which is, to my knowledge, the only other work directly addressing this problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Many crucial metrics in machine learning are U-statistics, and developing efficient, robust methods for their computation in decentralized settings (like sensor networks or federated systems) is a valuable contribution. The problem is demonstrably more difficult than standard distributed averaging, making a specialized solution relevant.

*   **Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong. The convergence analysis is comprehensive and the use of the "phantom network" framework to decouple data propagation and estimate averaging is an elegant analytical tool. The derived rates are explicit, showing dependence on both data properties and the network's spectral gap, which aligns with established theory in the gossip literature.

*   **Originality:** While the algorithmic components (gossip averaging, random swaps) are familiar primitives, their combination to create a dual-purpose algorithm for U-statistic estimation is novel. The primary contribution lies in this specific algorithmic design and its accompanying rigorous analysis, which clearly advances the state-of-the-art over the prior U2-gossip method by reducing memory and communication costs.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, the algorithms are presented concisely, and the main theoretical results are stated upfront. The connection to and improvement over prior work is made explicit.

**Weaknesses:**

The main weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation. While the comparison against U2-gossip is necessary and effectively demonstrates the superiority of the proposed GoSta algorithm, the evaluation lacks a broader context that could be provided by including simpler baseline methods. The performance of a sophisticated algorithm is best understood when compared not only to its direct competitor but also to more naive or intuitive approaches. The absence of such baselines makes it harder to judge the practical necessity of the proposed method's complexity.

For instance, several simple baselines could have been implemented:
1.  **Random Sampling:** Each node, at each iteration, could query a small, random set of other nodes, receive their data points, and compute an updated local estimate of the U-statistic based on its own point and the newly received points.
2.  **Cumulative Data Propagation:** A simpler gossip protocol where nodes only propagate and accumulate data points from their neighbors over time. Each node would then compute the U-statistic on its locally aggregated (and growing) dataset.

Including such comparisons would significantly strengthen the paper by demonstrating not just that GoSta is better than U2-gossip, but also that it outperforms simpler heuristics, thereby justifying its specific design choices (e.g., the combination of swapping and averaging).

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a significant and practical problem in distributed machine learning.
    *   Presents novel synchronous and asynchronous algorithms with clear advantages (lower memory/communication) over the existing state-of-the-art.
    *   Provides a rigorous and non-trivial theoretical analysis with clear convergence rate guarantees.
    *   The paper is well-written and the contributions are clearly articulated.

*   **Con-Acceptance:**
    *   The experimental evaluation, while showing a clear improvement over the single existing competitor, is narrow. The lack of comparison to simpler, intuitive baselines makes it difficult to fully assess the practical significance and necessity of the proposed algorithm's design.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with solid theoretical contributions to an important problem. The weaknesses in the experimental section are notable but do not invalidate the core contributions. The theoretical improvements over prior work are significant enough to warrant interest from the community. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to strengthen their experimental section in the final version by including comparisons to the types of simple baselines mentioned above. This would make the paper's practical impact much clearer.
