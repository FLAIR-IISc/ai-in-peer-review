This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics in distributed networks, a less explored area compared to sample mean estimation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and establish convergence rate bounds, offering improvements over existing work like [17]. The core idea of propagating data and maintaining local estimates is intuitive and well-motivated, as U-statistics are fundamental for describing global statistical properties, with applications in areas like AUC, variance, and Gini mean difference. The proofs appear comprehensive and rigorously establish the theoretical properties of the proposed algorithms.

A key strength of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis, providing convergence rates with explicit data and network dependency. The reformulation of the problem using “phantom nodes” is a clever technique enabling this analysis. The favorable comparisons to the existing approach in [17] – achieving faster convergence with lower memory and communication costs – are significant. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms broadens the applicability of the proposed methods.

However, the experimental evaluation, while present, could be strengthened. While the authors compare against [17], the inclusion of simpler baseline methods for comparison would be beneficial. For example, a straightforward averaging approach or adaptations of existing gossip algorithms for mean estimation [19, 2] could serve as valuable benchmarks to better contextualize the performance gains achieved by the proposed algorithms. The experimental section focuses on two specific applications (AUC and within-cluster point scatter); exploring a wider range of U-statistics or datasets would enhance the generalizability of the results. Additionally, while the authors mention the dependency of convergence on the spectral gap of the network graph, a more detailed analysis of the impact of different network topologies on performance would be insightful. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem in distributed estimation.
*   Proposes novel algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees (convergence rates).
*   Demonstrates improvements over existing work [17] in terms of convergence and resource usage.
*   Provides a clear and comprehensive analysis, including a clever reformulation using phantom nodes.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation could be more thorough, including comparison with simpler baseline methods.
*   The evaluation focuses on a limited number of applications and datasets.
*   A more in-depth analysis of the impact of network topology on performance would be valuable.

Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the proposed algorithms offer promising improvements over existing methods. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the paper is of sufficient quality for acceptance, particularly given the novelty of the problem addressed and the rigor of the analysis.
