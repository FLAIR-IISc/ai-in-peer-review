Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in networks, a class that includes AUC, empirical variance, Gini mean difference, Kendall’s tau, and within-cluster scatter. The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms—GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous)—that maintain a single auxiliary observation per node, propagate these observations via swaps, and average local estimates. A key idea is that auxiliary observations perform coupled random walks while estimates are repeatedly averaged, leading to convergence in expectation to the desired U-statistic. The analysis introduces a “phantom network” construction that decouples data propagation from averaging and facilitates convergence-rate bounds. The authors derive rates of O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t / t) (async) with explicit constants depending on data dispersion terms and the network spectral gap. Empirically, the methods outperform the prior U2-gossip approach in memory/communication and convergence speed on AUC and within-cluster scatter tasks across several network topologies.

The work connects to classic and modern gossip literature on averaging and separable functions (e.g., Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Dimakis et al.; Nedić and Ozdaglar), and to U-statistics. It contrasts with U2-gossip, which propagates two auxiliary observations without averaging and has higher memory/communication costs and a weaker rate bound.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea: pairwise averaging combined with swapping a single auxiliary observation, which implements coupled random walks over the network.
- Convergence guarantees with explicit network and data dependence; the spectral gap connection aligns with established results in gossip/consensus.
- Asynchronous variant that uses local counters to maintain unbiased updates without a global clock.
- Comparison to U2-gossip with improved memory and communication complexity and refined rate analysis.
- Experiments on real datasets across diverse network structures support the theoretical claims.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Clarify the random-walk perspective. The “swap auxiliary observations” step implies each initial observation performs a random walk on the network. Stating this up front would better motivate both the algorithm and the phantom-graph analysis.

2) Broaden the related work discussion. Beyond the citations included, the Proc. IEEE survey by Dimakis et al. provides a comprehensive view of decentralized averaging and function computation; situating this work against that landscape would strengthen the narrative.

3) Specify the edge-selection distribution. In the asynchronous model, a “node wakes up and contacts a neighbor” induces an edge distribution proportional to node degrees. Please explicitly state the resulting edge-selection probabilities and how they map to the stochastic matrix assumptions.

4) Compare to a flooding baseline. A natural alternative is to allow nodes to flood/tag observations, store received data (subject to memory limits), and compute partial U-statistics locally. On expanders, flooding mixes rapidly. A discussion and, if possible, an empirical/analytical comparison would contextualize the O(1/t) and O(log t / t) rates and highlight the advantage of averaging when memory is limited.

5) Discuss lifted Markov chains and acceleration. Your phantom-node construction resonates with lifting ideas used to speed up mixing and consensus:
- Jung, Shah, Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Allerton 2007
- Li and Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE TIT 2010
- Chen, Lovász, Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” STOC 1999
Positioning your construction relative to this line of work and clarifying similarities/differences (e.g., whether your swaps implicitly realize a lifted chain) would be valuable.

6) State data assumptions and guarantees. Gossip analyses often provide worst-case guarantees over initial data vectors, while your bounds include data-dependent terms. Please make explicit:
- Whether guarantees hold uniformly over datasets or in expectation over randomization only.
- Any assumptions on H or X (e.g., boundedness) needed for the rates.
- How the data-dependent norms scale with n and typical problem instances.

Quality
The technical development appears sound, with careful matrix formulations and spectral-gap-based rates; however, I did not verify the supplementary proofs. The refined comparison to U2-gossip is convincing. Clarifying the probabilistic model for edge selection and the scope of the data-dependent guarantees would improve rigor.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The phantom-network device is clever; making its role explicit earlier would help. Clarify the asynchronous model, the induced edge distribution, and the interpretation of constants in the bounds. Minor language edits are listed below.

Originality
Extending gossip to U-statistics with a single auxiliary observation, combined with averaging and a phantom-graph analysis, is novel relative to U2-gossip and prior separable-function gossip methods. The asynchronous unbiasedness trick via local counters is a neat practical addition.

Significance
U-statistics are widely used; enabling their decentralized estimation with provable rates and low per-node cost is valuable for sensor, peer-to-peer, and federated settings. The spectral-gap dependence is standard and informative. The empirical results show clear practical gains.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: decentralized U-statistics with low memory/communication.
- Solid theory with explicit network/data dependence; improved rates over prior art.
- Practical asynchronous algorithm.
- Empirical validation on real tasks and diverse topologies.

Cons for acceptance
- Related work coverage could be deeper, especially on lifted chains and broader gossip literature.
- Missing comparisons to flooding/memory-rich baselines leave open how close the rates are to the best achievable under different resource constraints.
- Some modeling/assumption details (edge distribution, data conditions) need clarification.

Minor comments and typos
- Define bipartite graphs without implying directed edges; the expression (S × T) ∪ (T × S) needs careful wording for undirected graphs.
- p.4: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”
- p.4: “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”
- p.6: “requires to initiate” → “requires initiating”
- p.7: “allows to create” → “allows us to create”

After author feedback
The clarifications and modifications the authors propose address key points above. Assuming the revision strengthens the related work (esp. on lifting/LADA) and adds baseline comparisons, I recommend acceptance.
