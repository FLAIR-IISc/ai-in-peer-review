Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over networks via gossip. The authors propose two algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node maintains a local estimate Zk and a single auxiliary observation Yk that is swapped along randomly selected edges; the estimate is updated by an averaging step. In expectation, the synchronous algorithm converges at O(1/t) and the asynchronous at O(log t/t), with explicit constants that depend on (i) data terms capturing dispersion of H(Xi, Xj) and (ii) the spectral gap of the network. The analysis leverages an elegant “phantom nodes” construction that decouples data propagation from averaging. The paper also refines the rate for the prior U2-gossip algorithm [17], showing a √n/t dependence that explains its poorer behavior empirically.

This work builds on a rich literature on randomized gossip for averaging and separable aggregates [2, 10, 15] and is, to my knowledge, the first to provide both synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms with rates and explicit network/data dependencies for U-statistics, beyond the earlier U2-gossip approach. The experimental evaluation on AUC and within-cluster scatter over complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs shows consistent gains over U2-gossip.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: Extends gossip beyond separable sums to pairwise U-statistics (e.g., AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter), a practically important class.
- Theory: Clear, interpretable convergence guarantees with data- and network-dependent constants; insightful use of spectral graph theory. The phantom-network analysis is original and illuminating.
- Algorithmic design: Uses only one auxiliary observation per node and a single edge activation per iteration (vs. two in U2-gossip), reducing memory and communication. The asynchronous variant is practically relevant.
- Empirics: On two real datasets and several network topologies, GoSta-sync converges faster and with lower across-node variance than U2-gossip; GoSta-async performs comparably to GoSta-sync in practice.

Weaknesses and suggested improvements
- Practical cost metrics: Figures report error versus iteration count. To assess practical utility, please also report:
  - Wall-clock time and per-iteration runtimes (including computing H, message passing, and synchronization overhead).
  - Communication complexity (messages and words transmitted) and memory per node.
  - Time/messages to reach a target error ε. This is crucial because GoSta-sync updates all nodes each iteration (each computing one H), which can be costly when H is expensive or d is large. The asynchronous variant mitigates this, but the trade-off should be quantified.
- Knowledge of |E| in the asynchronous algorithm: GoSta-async assumes each node knows pk = 2dk/|E|, hence |E|. This may be unrealistic in large or dynamic networks. Please discuss:
  - How to estimate |E| online or remove the need for it (e.g., push-sum/ratio consensus, lazy random walks, or local sampling schemes).
  - Robustness to inaccurate pk.
- Bipartite graphs and laziness: The theory assumes connected, non-bipartite graphs. A standard fix is to use “lazy” updates (self-loops) to avoid periodicity. Please comment on such a variant and whether the current analysis extends directly.
- Edge selection model: Synchronous experiments draw edges uniformly at random; implementing this without a central scheduler can be nontrivial. Please clarify how the model maps to local Poisson clocks and neighbor selection (as in [2]), and whether the same guarantees hold.
- Privacy and data movement: The method propagates raw observations (via Y swaps). In privacy-sensitive settings, this may be problematic. A brief discussion of possible privacy-preserving variants (e.g., sketches, secure aggregation, or random features) would broaden impact.
- Empirical breadth: Results focus on AUC and within-cluster scatter with n ≈ 10^3–10^3.5. Additional experiments would strengthen the case:
  - Larger graphs (scaling of time/messages to ε versus n and spectral gap).
  - Sensitivity to graph connectivity (vary rewiring probability p in Watts–Strogatz).
  - Multi-observation-per-node setting (beyond the remark), with empirical validation.
  - Additional U-statistics (e.g., Kendall tau) to demonstrate generality.

Quality
The algorithms and analyses are technically sound and well connected to spectral graph theory. The proofs are deferred; based on the presentation, the results appear correct and the constants are interpretable. A few assumptions (non-bipartite, knowledge of |E|) should be discussed more explicitly for practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The phantom-network construction is explained cleanly. Minor suggestions: provide more intuition for Wα(G) and its role; summarize per-iteration computation/communication costs of GoSta versus U2-gossip in the main text; fix small typographical issues.

Originality
High. The combination of data propagation with averaging for U-statistics, the phantom-node analysis, and the asynchronous variant with rates go beyond prior work.

Significance
Moderate to high. U-statistics are pervasive, and decentralized computation is increasingly relevant. The improved rates and lower per-iteration resource demands over U2-gossip are meaningful, especially on well-connected graphs.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem with a simple, general method.
- Solid theory with explicit, interpretable convergence bounds.
- Clear improvements over the most relevant prior algorithm in both analysis and experiments.
- Practical asynchronous algorithm.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing wall-clock and communication-cost evaluations; iteration counts alone can be misleading.
- Assumes knowledge of |E| in the asynchronous algorithm; unclear robustness if unavailable.
- Limited empirical breadth (two tasks, moderate n) and no multi-observation-per-node experiments.
- No discussion of privacy implications of data propagation.

Overall evaluation
A well-motivated and carefully analyzed contribution to decentralized estimation with promising empirical evidence. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying practical cost metrics and the |E| assumption, and, if space permits, expanding the empirical evaluation.
