- Paper proposes a decentralized gossip-style algorithm to compute higher-order U-statistics (variance, correlation, scatter) across networked observations.
- Contribution is valuable for extending gossip methods to complex distributed statistics but needs stronger links to prior work and clearer exposition.
- Core algorithm exchanges current estimates plus an auxiliary state holding initial observations; this enables coupled random walks that should be explicitly explained in the main text.
- The multiple-graph-copy analysis should be motivated by clarifying how each initial observation performs the coupled random walk across the network.
- Related work section is weak: missing key surveys and foundational references (e.g., Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE 2010) that contextualize gossip-based aggregation.
- Must incorporate literature on accelerating gossip via lifting constructions (e.g., Jung et al. Allerton 2007; Li and Dai IEEE Trans. IT 2010; Chen et al. STOC 1999) as they relate to the auxiliary-state mechanism.
- Ambiguity in the model: specify the exact edge-selection distribution induced by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process (uniform edges vs degree-weighted).
- Missing comparison to an alternative flooding/complete-history strategy; experiments should evaluate the communication vs memory trade-off (e.g., flooding may outperform on expanders).
- Error analysis implicitly uses worst-case initial data distributions but this assumption is not stated; clarify data-distribution assumptions and their effect on bounds.
- Minor corrections: clarify bipartite edge definition (directed vs undirected) and fix grammatical issues (“let us stress out that” → “we stress that”, “awakes and contact(s)”, “requires initiating”, “allows us to create”).
- After revisions addressing core technical points, reviewer raised score from 7 to 8 and found the changes satisfactory.
- Required next steps before final acceptance: add lifting-construction citations (LADA, Li & Dai 2010) and include baseline experimental comparisons (e.g., flooding) to demonstrate trade-offs.
