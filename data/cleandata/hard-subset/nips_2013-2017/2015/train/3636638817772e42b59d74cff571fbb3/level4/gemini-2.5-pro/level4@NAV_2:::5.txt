This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the distributed estimation of degree-two U-statistics. Whereas gossip algorithms are well-established for decentralized averaging of separable functions like the sample mean, U-statistics—which include important metrics like AUC, empirical variance, and within-cluster scatter—pose a greater challenge due to their dependence on pairs of observations across the network. The authors build upon the gossip paradigm by proposing a method that combines a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary observations, with a local estimate averaging step. This work directly improves upon the approach in [17], aiming for faster convergence with lower memory and communication overhead. The theoretical analysis, which uses an original "phantom network" reformulation, establishes convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and less-investigated problem in distributed computation. The proposed algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, represent a novel and valuable contribution for computing a broad class of useful statistics in a fully decentralized manner. The extension to an asynchronous setting is particularly relevant for practical applications where global synchronization is not feasible.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical claims are strong and well-supported. The authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis with explicit data- and network-dependent terms, clearly showing how connectivity (via the spectral gap) and data dispersion affect performance. The "phantom network" construction used in the proofs is a clever and insightful analytical tool.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are convincing and well-designed. Using real-world datasets and relevant U-statistics (AUC and within-cluster point scatter), the authors clearly demonstrate that their proposed algorithms outperform the main competitor from prior work ([17]) across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz). The results align with the theoretical analysis, showing lower average error and reduced variance across nodes.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides the necessary background, situates the work within the literature, and presents the algorithms and their analysis in a clear, systematic fashion. The exposition is sufficient for an expert to understand the contributions and potentially reproduce the results.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

*   **Asynchronous Rate Analysis:** The theoretical convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm (O(log t/t)) is slightly weaker than for the synchronous version (O(1/t)). While the experiments show comparable empirical performance, a brief discussion on whether this logarithmic factor is an artifact of the proof technique or reflects a fundamental limitation would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Impact of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on standard assumptions for gossip algorithms (connected, non-bipartite graph). It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly comment on the robustness of the algorithms if these assumptions are relaxed, for example, in networks that are nearly bipartite or have disconnected components.
*   **Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments effectively compare performance in terms of iterations, a discussion of wall-clock time or computational complexity per iteration would better substantiate the claims of lower communication and memory costs compared to U2-gossip, especially as data dimensionality `d` increases. Figure 3a shows scaling with network size `n`, but a more direct analysis of resource usage would be a valuable addition.
