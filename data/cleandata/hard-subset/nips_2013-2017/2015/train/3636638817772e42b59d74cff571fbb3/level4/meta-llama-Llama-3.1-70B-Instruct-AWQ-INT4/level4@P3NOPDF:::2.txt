This paper proposes a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm to compute higher-order statistics, specifically U-statistics, measured over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. These statistics include various quantities of interest, such as sample variance, correlation and scatter coefficients, and other aggregate measures. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, but the authors should strengthen their connection to existing approaches and ideas.

The main algorithmic innovation lies in the way nodes exchange estimate values and average them while swapping a secondary state variable representing their initial observation. This process enables the initial observations to perform a random walk, albeit coupled, which is not explicitly mentioned in the main manuscript. Clarifying this point and motivating the analysis method via multiple copies of the graph would improve the paper's clarity.

The authors should also situate their work in the context of existing gossip approaches for computing statistics, such as those mentioned in Dimakis et al.'s Proc. IEEE paper. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the current state of the art and the contribution of the proposed algorithm.

On page 4, it would be beneficial to clarify the induced distribution on edges resulting from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process. Specifically, the phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" does not specify the distribution, and a more detailed explanation would be helpful.

An alternative approach to this problem is to allow more memory at each node and use the same contact process to flood each node's value across the network. Nodes can then store the received observations and calculate the U-statistic on the subset of data they possess. Comparing this approach to the proposed method would help demonstrate the benefits of averaging and the potential performance loss when memory at each node is limited. Without this comparison, it is unclear whether the rate 1/t is optimal.

The authors should also examine the work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, as it appears to be related to their construction. Specifically, references such as Jung et al.'s "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains" and Li and Dai's "Location-aided fast distributed consensus" may provide valuable insights.

In general, the analysis of gossip algorithms tends to make worst-case assumptions on the initial data distribution. However, the problem statement and theorem do not clearly specify the assumption on the data distribution and the guarantees on the error as a function of the initial data. Clarifying these points would strengthen the paper's contributions.

Minor corrections include: p1, the bipartite definition using (S \times T) \cup (T \times S) implies directed graphs; p4, "let us stress out that" should be "we stress that"; p4, "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts"; p6, "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating"; and p7, "allows to create" should be "allows us to create".

After the author feedback, the suggested clarifications and modifications are satisfactory, and the initial score of 7 has been revised to an 8. However, the authors should still investigate lifting constructions, particularly LADA, and modify the related work section. Additionally, the experiments should include baseline comparisons to further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

This paper presents a nicely formulated approach to extending gossip methods for computing complex statistics in distributed settings, such as sensor networks. Assuming the authors address the comments raised in this review, the paper is recommended for acceptance.
