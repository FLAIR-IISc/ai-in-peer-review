Summary
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics (e.g., AUC, variance, Gini mean difference, within-cluster scatter) over a network using gossip protocols. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous algorithms that maintain a running local estimate while propagating auxiliary observations across the graph. They prove convergence in expectation at rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants that depend on both data dispersion and the graph’s spectral gap. The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” reformulation to decouple averaging and data propagation. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several network topologies illustrate the approach and compare primarily to U2-gossip.

Relation to prior work: Gossip algorithms are well-studied for separable sums and averaging (e.g., Boyd et al.; randomized pairwise averaging), but they do not directly apply to pairwise statistics. The closest prior work is U2-gossip, which propagates two observations without averaging. This paper builds on that line by combining propagation with averaging and by providing synchronous and asynchronous variants with explicit rate bounds.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation for decentralized U-statistics, a class of quantities relevant in ML and data mining.
- Algorithms are simple and practical (especially the asynchronous variant), with low memory and communication per iteration compared to U2-gossip.
- Rigorous analysis with explicit graph- and data-dependent constants; the “phantom nodes” technique is neat and helps separate effects.
- Empirical results align with the theory and show advantages over U2-gossip on several graphs and tasks.

Weaknesses
- Conceptual novelty appears modest: the core algorithmic ideas (running averages plus random-walk propagation) are intuitive extensions of known gossip and estimation schemes.
- The experimental evaluation is limited in baselines. While U2-gossip is the main prior method, additional comparisons would make the case stronger. Several straightforward baselines (randomized and centralized) could be included.
- Empirical metrics focus on error vs. iterations; more informative would be wall-clock time and communication/byte complexity to reach a target accuracy, especially for high-dimensional data.
- Theoretical guarantees are in expectation; high-probability bounds or finite-time concentration would strengthen the guarantees.
- Practical robustness aspects (message loss, delays, degree heterogeneity, partial knowledge of |E| for pk) are not evaluated.

Quality
The paper is technically sound overall. The proofs are comprehensive and clearly tie the rates to spectral properties of the network and dispersion of the underlying U-statistic components. Minor concerns remain about the absence of high-probability results and limited empirical stress-testing under realistic network imperfections.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithm descriptions are readable, and the analysis is carefully structured. The “phantom network” construction is clearly positioned as an analysis tool. Some sections (experiments) would benefit from more detail on implementation choices, communication budgets, and stopping criteria.

Originality
The problem is important, and the proposed combination of data propagation with averaging is natural. Relative to U2-gossip, the contributions feel incremental but meaningful: asynchronous operation, reduced memory/communication, and refined convergence analysis.

Significance
Estimating U-statistics arises in several distributed ML tasks; thus, a practical, analyzable gossip solution is valuable. The significance would be higher with stronger empirical validation across more baselines and with communication/time-to-accuracy metrics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baseline comparisons beyond U2-gossip, such as:
  - Centralized aggregation (gold standard time/communication) and spanning-tree or MST-based aggregation.
  - Naive pairwise-sampling approximations (e.g., Monte Carlo subsampling of pairs with gossip aggregation).
  - Push-sum or standard average gossip applied to precomputed local proxies (mini-batch hi estimates).
- Report communication and memory costs to reach a target error (messages and bytes), and wall-clock time where possible.
- Include sensitivity analyses: effect of graph size and spectral gap, degree heterogeneity, dimension d, and noisy/asynchronous environments (message drops, delays).
- If possible, provide high-probability convergence bounds or variance decay results across nodes.
- Clarify the assumptions needed for pk in the asynchronous algorithm and discuss how to proceed if |E| is unknown or time-varying.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored problem with practical algorithms and solid theoretical analysis.
- Asynchronous variant is useful in realistic decentralized settings.
- Demonstrated improvements over the main prior method.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty; the core ideas extend standard gossip mechanisms in a fairly direct way.
- Evaluation is comparatively narrow in baseline coverage and does not fully assess communication/time efficiency or robustness.

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem with a clean approach and strong analysis, but it would benefit from broader empirical comparisons and more practical performance metrics.
