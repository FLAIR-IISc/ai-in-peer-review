This paper addresses the problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics, which are functions computed over pairs of observations distributed across a network. Examples of such statistics include sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster point scatter. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, that compute these statistics by having nodes simultaneously average local estimates while propagating their initial data observations through the network via a swapping mechanism. Theoretical convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case are established. This work builds upon the gossip algorithm literature, which has primarily focused on simpler averages, and directly aims to improve upon the U2-gossip algorithm [17], the main prior work on this specific problem.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Many useful statistics in machine learning are U-statistics, and developing efficient, decentralized algorithms for their computation is a valuable contribution, especially for sensor networks and large-scale data systems.
*   **Originality:** The proposed GoSta algorithm is a novel extension of gossip methods. The core idea of combining estimate averaging with the swapping of auxiliary observations is elegant and seems effective. The analysis via a "phantom network" construction is also an interesting technical approach.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and provides convergence rate guarantees that explicitly depend on data and network properties (i.e., the spectral gap). The experimental results on real-world datasets convincingly demonstrate that the proposed algorithms outperform the existing U2-gossip method in both accuracy and variance, confirming the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of the Core Mechanism:** The intuition behind the algorithm could be better articulated. The key mechanism is that while nodes average their estimates, their initial observations perform coupled random walks on the graph via the swapping step. Stating this more explicitly in the introduction or algorithm description would help motivate the design and provide a clearer entry point to the "phantom network" analysis that follows.
*   **Insufficient Context and Related Work:**
    1.  The literature review on gossip algorithms is somewhat narrow. Situating the paper's contribution within the broader context of the field, for instance by referencing comprehensive surveys like Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, 2010), would be beneficial.
    2.  The "phantom network" construction used for the analysis appears strongly related to the concept of "lifted Markov chains," a technique used to analyze and accelerate gossip algorithms. The authors should discuss this connection and cite relevant work, such as Jung et al. (Allerton, 2007), Li & Dai (IEEE T-IT, 2010), and Chen et al. (STOC, 1999). This would strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding and clarify its novelty with respect to existing analytical tools.
*   **Lack of a Natural Baseline Comparison:** A straightforward alternative for this problem is a flooding-based approach: each node broadcasts its own observation, and every other node stores all observations it has received. At any time, a node can compute the U-statistic on the subset of data it possesses. Including a comparison to this baseline would be very informative. It would help contextualize the O(1/t) rate—is this fast or slow?—and would clearly illustrate the trade-off between the proposed method's low memory footprint and the potentially faster convergence of a flooding method on well-connected graphs (e.g., expanders).
*   **Technical Ambiguity:**
    1.  In the algorithm descriptions, the phrase "draw an edge uniformly at random from E" is used. It would be clearer to specify the underlying process, e.g., if this means that every edge has a 1/|E| probability of being chosen, or if it results from a "node wakes up and contacts a random neighbor" model, which induces a different distribution on edges.
    2.  The theoretical guarantees should be clarified with respect to the initial data. It is not explicitly stated whether the convergence bounds hold for any worst-case initial data vector X, which is typical for gossip algorithm analysis. Please clarify the assumptions on the data.

**Minor Points:**
*   p. 1: The definition of a bipartite graph using `(S × T) ∪ (T × S)` is more suitable for directed graphs. For an undirected graph, `E ⊆ (S × T) ∪ (T × S)` is sufficient.
*   p. 4: "we stress that" is preferable to "let us stress out that".
*   p. 6: "requires initiating" is preferable to "requires to initiate".
*   p. 7: "allows us to create" is preferable to "allows to create".

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a well-motivated and elegant approach to extending gossip algorithms for the distributed computation of U-statistics. The proposed methods are supported by both theoretical analysis and strong empirical results. While the core contribution is solid, the paper would be significantly improved by clarifying the connection to related theoretical concepts (lifting), providing a more thorough comparison against natural baselines to better establish significance, and refining some technical details in the problem statement. Assuming the authors can address these points in a revision, I recommend acceptance.
