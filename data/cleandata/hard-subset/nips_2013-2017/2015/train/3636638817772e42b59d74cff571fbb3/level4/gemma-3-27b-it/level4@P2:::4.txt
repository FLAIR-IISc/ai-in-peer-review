This paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics, a less-explored area compared to distributed mean estimation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and provide convergence rate bounds for both cases. The core idea of propagating data and maintaining local estimates is intuitive, and the theoretical analysis appears comprehensive.

The paper builds upon prior work in gossip algorithms [19, 18, 5], which have become a standard approach for decentralized computation. It correctly identifies the limitation of existing gossip algorithms when applied to U-statistics, which require averaging over pairs of observations [12]. The motivation for tackling this problem is well-articulated, as U-statistics are essential for describing global properties and appear in various applications like AUC calculation, variance estimation, and Gini mean difference.  The connection to work on spectral graph theory [3] to relate convergence to network connectivity is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The paper tackles a relevant and challenging problem in distributed estimation.  U-statistics are important, and efficient decentralized computation is crucial for many applications.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis appears solid, with explicit data and network-dependent terms. The proof techniques utilizing "phantom nodes" seem novel and potentially insightful.
*   **Algorithm Design:** The proposed algorithms are conceptually simple and well-defined, building on the established framework of gossip algorithms.
*   **Asynchronous Version:**  The development of an asynchronous algorithm is a significant contribution, as it addresses a more practical scenario than the synchronous setting.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The most significant weakness is the lack of a thorough empirical comparison against baseline methods. While the authors compare their results to a previous approach [17], it would greatly strengthen the paper to include comparisons against simpler, intuitive baselines. Several randomized algorithms could be readily implemented for comparison.  For example, a simple iterative averaging approach, where each node randomly exchanges U-statistic estimates with a neighbor, could serve as a useful benchmark.
*   **Novelty:**  While the approach is sound, the core idea of propagating information and averaging estimates feels incremental. A more detailed discussion of how this work significantly differs from existing gossip-based approaches beyond simply extending them to U-statistics is needed. 
*   **Clarity (Minor):** While generally well-written, the notation can be dense at times, particularly in Section 4.  More explanation of the "phantom node" concept would improve readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound solution to a relevant problem.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides useful insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
*   The asynchronous algorithm is a valuable contribution for practical applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is weak, lacking comparisons to baseline methods.
*   The novelty of the approach may be considered incremental.
*   The paper's impact could be limited without a more compelling demonstration of its advantages over existing methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of distributed estimation. However, the lack of a comprehensive empirical evaluation significantly weakens its impact. Addressing this weakness by including comparisons to appropriate baseline methods would substantially improve the paper. While the current work isn't groundbreaking, it represents a valuable extension to existing gossip algorithm frameworks. 

**Recommendation:**  **Weak Accept**. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors strengthening the empirical evaluation with comparisons to relevant baseline methods. If the authors cannot provide such comparisons, a rejection is warranted.
