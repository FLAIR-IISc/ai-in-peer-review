

Review:  
This paper addresses the computation of higher-order statistics (e.g., sample variance, correlation, scatter coefficients) over pairs of observations distributed across a network, proposing a gossip-based decentralized algorithm for aggregation. While the contribution extends gossip literature meaningfully, the manuscript requires stronger connections to prior work and clearer exposition of key mechanisms.  

The core innovation involves nodes exchanging estimates while swapping a secondary state variable tracking their initial observations, effectively enabling coupled random walks of the initial data. Explicitly framing this mechanism in the main text would clarify the analysis approach and motivate the use of multiple graph copies.  

Critically, the paper overlooks significant context in the related work. Dimakis et al.’s *Proc. IEEE* survey (2010) comprehensively covers gossip-based statistics computation, and situating this work within that framework would strengthen the contribution. The current treatment is insufficient compared to the breadth of existing approaches.  

Clarity gaps require attention:  
- On p. 4, the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process must specify the edge distribution (e.g., uniform over edges, or weighted by node degrees).  
- The analysis assumes worst-case initial data distributions (over all vectors $X$), but this is not explicitly stated in the theorem setup. Clarifying the data assumptions and error guarantees is essential.  
- A natural alternative—flood-based aggregation with node memory—deserves comparison. If nodes store tagged observations and compute U-statistics locally during communication, how does its performance (e.g., convergence rate for expander graphs) compare to the proposed averaging method? Without this, the significance of the $O(1/t)$ rate remains unclear.  

The authors should also address lifting constructions for gossip acceleration (e.g., Jung et al. *Allerton 2007*, Li & Dai *IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 2010*, Chen et al. *STOC 1999*), which directly relate to their state-swapping mechanism. The LADA (Location-Aided Fast Distributed Consensus) work, in particular, offers relevant insights.  

Minor issues:  
- p. 1: Bipartite definition $(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)$ implies directed graphs; clarify.  
- p. 4: "stress out" → "stress"; "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts."  
- p. 6: "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating."  
- p. 7: "allows to create" → "allows us to create."  

**After Author Feedback**:  
The authors’ revisions addressing the above points are substantial and improve clarity. I revise my initial score from 7 to 8. However, two items remain:  
1. A deeper engagement with lifting constructions (especially LADA) in the related work section.  
2. Inclusion of baseline comparisons (e.g., flood-based method) in experiments to contextualize the $O(1/t)$ rate.  

**Overall Recommendation**:  
This work presents a valuable extension of gossip algorithms to complex statistics in distributed settings. The mathematical formulation is sound, but the connection to prior literature and justification of the proposed method require refinement. With the suggested revisions—particularly clarifying the data assumptions, adding the flood-based baseline comparison, and integrating lifting constructions into the related work—the paper will make a strong contribution. I recommend **acceptance** upon addressing these points.
