This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics—higher-order statistics based on pairs of observations—in a decentralized network. The authors propose a novel gossip-style algorithm for this task, applicable to quantities such as sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients. While the work presents a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, its connection to existing methods and the clarity of its exposition could be substantially improved.

**Major Comments**

1.  The core algorithmic insight involves a dual-state update: when two nodes average their current estimates, they also swap a secondary state variable corresponding to their initial observation. This effectively causes the initial observations to perform a (coupled) random walk across the network. This is a clever mechanism, but it is not explained with sufficient prominence in the main text. Highlighting this aspect more clearly would not only improve the reader's understanding of the algorithm's mechanics but also provide a stronger motivation for the analysis, which relies on multiple graph copies.

2.  The discussion of related work is insufficient. The literature on gossip algorithms for distributed statistics is extensive. The authors should situate their contribution within this broader context, for instance, by referencing the state-of-the-art survey by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE). A more thorough comparison would better highlight the specific novelty and advantages of the proposed method.

3.  The communication model described on page 4 is ambiguous. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" does not specify the underlying probability distribution. The authors should clarify whether edges are drawn uniformly at random, or if the distribution is induced by the "node wakes up and contacts a random neighbor" process, and what the implications are.

4.  The paper lacks a comparison to a natural and important baseline algorithm. An alternative approach would be to allow each node to use more memory to store all observations it has received from others. Using the same communication process, nodes could effectively "flood" their initial values across the network. Each node could then compute the U-statistic locally on its growing subset of the data. A comparison against this flooding-based approach is essential for several reasons: it would help contextualize the convergence rate of 1/t, demonstrating whether it is competitive, and it would illuminate the trade-offs between memory constraints and performance. For certain graph structures, like expanders, a flooding approach might be very efficient.

5.  The analytical construction used in the paper appears closely related to the concept of "lifting" Markov chains, a technique used to accelerate mixing in gossip algorithms. The authors should investigate this connection and discuss it in the paper. Relevant works include:
    *   K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., 2007.
    *   W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, 2010.
    *   F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in STOC, 1999.

6.  The theoretical guarantees are not clearly specified with respect to the initial data. Typically, analyses of gossip algorithms provide worst-case guarantees over the initial distribution of values across the nodes. The problem statement and main theorem should explicitly state the assumptions made on the initial data and clarify how the error bounds depend on this initial data vector.

**Minor Comments**

*   p1: The definition of a bipartite graph using `(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)` seems to imply a directed graph. Please clarify.
*   p4: "let us stress out that" should be "we stress that".
*   p4: "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts".
*   p6: "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating".
*   p7: "allows to create" should be "allows us to create".

**Conclusion**

This paper presents an interesting and novel approach for extending gossip algorithms to compute complex, second-order statistics in a distributed manner. However, the current manuscript suffers from a lack of clarity in its exposition, an incomplete discussion of related work, and an absence of crucial baseline comparisons. For the paper to be acceptable, the authors must address the major points raised in this review. In particular, a revised version should: (1) provide a clearer explanation of the core algorithmic mechanism, (2) thoroughly situate the work within the existing literature, including a discussion of the connection to lifting constructions, and (3) include an experimental comparison against the flooding-based baseline described in comment 4. If the authors can satisfactorily address these points in a revision, the paper would represent a solid contribution to the field, and I would recommend its acceptance.
