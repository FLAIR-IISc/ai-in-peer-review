

This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a significant gap in the literature where existing gossip algorithms are limited to separable statistics like sample means. The authors extend the gossip framework to efficiently compute statistics that depend on pairs of observations (U-statistics), which are crucial for various applications including AUC, empirical variance, and within-cluster point scatter. The work builds upon foundational gossip algorithms [19, 18, 5] and specifically improves upon the U2-gossip approach [17] by addressing its limitations in memory requirements, communication costs, and asynchronous operation.

Quality: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous convergence rate analysis (O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings), featuring explicit data and network-dependent terms. The theoretical analysis is well-supported by a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation that enables clean convergence proofs. The experimental results on two real datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) provide compelling empirical evidence that the proposed algorithms outperform U2-gossip in convergence rate, memory efficiency, and communication costs across multiple network topologies.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear definitions, precise mathematical formulations, and thorough explanations of the algorithmic design. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the importance of U-statistics in machine learning applications and clearly distinguishing the gap in the literature. The algorithms are described with sufficient detail for reproduction, including pseudocode and clear explanations of the data propagation and averaging steps.

Originality: This work makes a significant contribution to the gossip literature by extending the framework to handle U-statistics, which are fundamentally different from the separable statistics addressed in prior gossip work. The phantom nodes approach for convergence analysis is novel and provides deeper insights into the problem structure. The paper carefully situates its contribution relative to [17], demonstrating clear improvements in convergence rate, memory requirements, and the ability to operate in asynchronous settings.

Significance: The proposed algorithms address a meaningful gap in distributed computation, enabling efficient estimation of important statistical quantities in decentralized networks. The practical significance is demonstrated through extensive experiments showing clear advantages over the state-of-the-art (U2-gossip) across multiple network topologies and real-world datasets. The work has potential to impact various applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and distributed machine learning where U-statistics are relevant.

Strengths:
- Novel extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics with improved theoretical guarantees
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and network topologies
- Clear distinction from and improvement over prior work (U2-gossip)
- Practical implementation details for both synchronous and asynchronous settings
- Well-motivated problem with clear applications in machine learning

Weaknesses:
- The phantom nodes approach is primarily explained in the supplementary material, though the main text provides sufficient intuition
- The practical impact of the log t/t factor in asynchronous convergence could be discussed more explicitly

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field with novel theoretical insights, practical algorithms, and compelling empirical validation. It meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS paper and should be accepted.
