This paper presents novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for the decentralized estimation of U-statistics. U-statistics represent a broad class of measures that depend on pairs of observations, such as sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster point scatter, which cannot be efficiently computed by standard gossip algorithms designed for simple averaging. The core idea of the proposed "GoSta" algorithms is to combine two processes: a data propagation step where nodes swap auxiliary data observations with neighbors, and an averaging step where nodes mix their local estimates of the U-statistic.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of their algorithms. For the synchronous version, they establish a convergence rate of O(1/t), and for the asynchronous version, a rate of O(log t/t). A key contribution of the analysis is an original reformulation of the problem using a "phantom network," which elegantly decouples the analysis of data propagation from estimate averaging. The derived bounds explicitly depend on the network's spectral gap and data-dependent terms, providing clear insights into the algorithm's performance. The paper contrasts its approach with the previously proposed U2-gossip algorithm [17], showing theoretically and empirically that GoSta offers faster convergence, lower memory requirements, and reduced communication costs. The claims are substantiated with experiments on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation across various network topologies.

**Quality and Originality**

The paper is of high quality, presenting a technically sound and complete piece of work. The problem of decentralized U-statistic estimation is important but has received limited attention compared to distributed averaging. This work significantly advances the state-of-the-art.

*   **Originality:** The proposed algorithms are novel and the combination of data swapping with estimate averaging is an elegant solution to the problem. The development of a fully asynchronous algorithm is a particularly strong and practical contribution, as it removes the often-unrealistic assumption of a global clock required by prior work. Furthermore, the "phantom network" construction used in the convergence proof is an original and powerful analytical tool that may be applicable to other distributed estimation problems.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous. The convergence proofs are solid, and the derived rates are insightful. The authors also provide a new, refined analysis of the competing U2-gossip method, which not only highlights the superiority of their approach but also demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem domain. Claims are carefully stated and well-supported by the provided theory.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and explaining their methodology.

*   The introduction clearly defines the problem and its importance with concrete examples.
*   The algorithms are presented in a straightforward manner.
*   The theoretical results are stated precisely, and the high-level intuition behind the proofs is communicated effectively.
*   The experimental section is clear, the setups are well-described, and the figures effectively visualize the performance advantages of the proposed methods.

**Significance**

The contributions of this paper are significant and likely to be influential. U-statistics are fundamental tools in statistics and machine learning, and the demand for decentralized computation continues to grow.

*   This work provides a practical, efficient, and theoretically-grounded solution for a non-trivial and important class of distributed estimation problems.
*   Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale distributed systems, such as sensor networks or federated learning environments, are likely to use these ideas.
*   The paper opens up avenues for future work, such as developing accelerated versions of these algorithms or using them as a foundation for the decentralized optimization of objective functions based on U-statistics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Tackles the challenging and relevant problem of distributed U-statistic estimation.
*   **Novel and superior algorithms:** Proposes new algorithms that demonstrably outperform the primary existing method in convergence speed, communication, and memory efficiency.
*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** Provides a rigorous convergence analysis featuring an original proof technique and insightful, explicit bounds.
*   **Practical relevance:** The inclusion of a fully asynchronous algorithm makes the work highly relevant for real-world applications.
*   **Solid empirical validation:** The theoretical claims are thoroughly supported by well-designed experiments on real datasets.

**Suggestions for Improvement (Minor Weaknesses):**

*   The convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm (O(log t/t)) is theoretically weaker than for the synchronous one (O(1/t)). While experiments show comparable performance, a brief discussion on whether this gap is fundamental to the asynchronous setting or an artifact of the analysis would be insightful.
*   In Theorem 2, the bound for the asynchronous algorithm contains a constant c'(G) whose dependence on network properties is not explicitly characterized. Clarifying this dependency, as was done for the synchronous case, would further strengthen the result.
*   The asynchronous algorithm assumes that nodes know their degree and the total number of edges in the network. While this is a mild assumption in static networks, it would be helpful to briefly comment on its implications for more dynamic network settings.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on distributed machine learning. It is well-written, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
