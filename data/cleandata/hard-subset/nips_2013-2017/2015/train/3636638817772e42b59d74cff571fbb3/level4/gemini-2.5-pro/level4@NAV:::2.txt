This work addresses the computation of higher-order statistics, specifically U-statistics, derived from pairs of observations that are distributed across a network. Such U-statistics encompass important quantities like sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients, among other aggregate metrics. The paper's primary contribution is a gossip-based decentralized averaging algorithm designed for this purpose.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the gossip algorithm literature, but its connection to existing methods and concepts could be significantly strengthened.

Note: This is a detailed review. The correctness of the supplementary material has not been verified due to time constraints.

**Comments**

1) The core algorithmic mechanism involves not only averaging estimate values upon contact but also swapping a secondary state variable that holds their original observation. Consequently, the initial observations effectively execute a set of coupled random walks on the network. Clarifying this dynamic in the main text would improve readability and provide a stronger motivation for the analytical approach, which relies on multiple graph copies.

2) A significant body of work exists on gossip algorithms for statistical computation beyond what is cited. The Dimakis et al. survey in Proceedings of the IEEE, for example, offers a comprehensive overview of the field from a few years prior. Positioning this contribution within the broader context established by such works would be more insightful than the current, somewhat superficial, literature review.

3) On page 4, the paper should specify the induced probability distribution on the edges that results from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous as it does not define this distribution.

4) A natural alternative approach to this problem exists, which would involve using more memory at each node to implement a flooding mechanism. Through the same contact process, each node could broadcast its initial value, and other nodes would store all received observations (tagged by their origin). Each node could then compute the U-statistic locally based on its collected subset of data. A comparison between this flooding-based method and the proposed algorithm is needed. Such a comparison would highlight the benefits of averaging or quantify the performance trade-offs associated with the proposed method's limited memory requirements. Without this baseline, the significance of the reported 1/t convergence rate is difficult to assess, especially since flooding could be highly efficient on expander graphs.

5) The proposed construction appears closely related to the concept of lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms. The authors are encouraged to review this line of work. See for example:
*   K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," in Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., Urbana-Champaign, IL, Sep. 2007.
*   W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," in IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 56(12), 2010.
*   F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," in Proceedings of the thirty-first annual ACM symposium on Theory of computing. ACM, 1999, pp. 275-281.

6) Typically, the analysis of gossip algorithms provides guarantees that hold for a worst-case distribution of initial data; that is, the performance bounds are established over all possible initial data vectors $X$. This aspect is not clearly defined in the paper's problem formulation or the main theorem. The manuscript should explicitly state the assumptions regarding the data distribution and clarify how the error guarantees depend on the initial data.

**Minor Points**
*   p1: The use of `(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)` in the bipartite definition implies a directed graph, which may be unintentional.
*   p4: Consider changing "let us stress out that" to "we stress that".
*   p4: "awakes and contact" should be "awakes and contacts".
*   p6: "requires to initiate" should be "requires initiating".
*   p7: "allows to create" could be rephrased as "allows us to create".

**AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK:**
The authors' proposed clarifications and modifications are satisfactory, and I have raised my score from 7 to 8.

However, I maintain that a thorough examination of lifting constructions (particularly LADA) and a corresponding revision of the related work section are necessary. Additionally, the experimental evaluation should be updated to include the suggested baseline comparisons.

**Summary**
This paper presents a well-written approach for extending gossip algorithms to the computation of more complex statistics within distributed environments like sensor networks. However, the mathematical formulation and its relationship to prior work lack clarity. Provided the authors undertake a thorough revision that addresses the points raised in this review, I recommend this paper for acceptance.
