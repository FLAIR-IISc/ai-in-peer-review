This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics in a network. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations, encompass many useful metrics such as sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster scatter. The authors correctly point out that standard gossip algorithms, designed for separable sums, are not directly applicable. The primary prior work in this specific area is the U2-gossip algorithm by Pelckmans and Suykens [17].

The main contribution of this paper is the proposal of new synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) randomized gossip algorithms for this task. The core idea is to combine two processes at each iteration: 1) a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary data observations with neighbors to disseminate information across the network, and 2) an estimate averaging step, where neighboring nodes average their current local estimates of the U-statistic. For the analysis, the authors introduce a novel and elegant proof technique based on "phantom nodes" to model the data propagation and averaging steps separately. They establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, showing explicit dependence on network connectivity (via the spectral gap) and data-dependent variance terms.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the use of the "phantom network" reformulation is a clever and insightful tool for proving convergence. The claims of convergence are well-supported by the proofs provided in the supplementary material. The paper also provides a refined convergence rate analysis for the prior U2-gossip algorithm, which allows for a more principled theoretical comparison.

The experimental evaluation is also strong. The authors compare their proposed methods against the U2-gossip baseline on two practical U-statistics (AUC and within-cluster scatter) using real-world datasets. The experiments are conducted on various network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), demonstrating the robustness of the proposed algorithms. The results clearly show that GoSta-sync consistently outperforms U2-gossip in terms of both convergence speed (average relative error) and stability (lower variance across nodes).

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem is formally stated, and the proposed algorithms are presented in an easy-to-understand pseudocode format. The distinction between the synchronous and asynchronous settings is handled well. The authors do a good job of building intuition before diving into the more complex theoretical analysis.

**Originality:**
The work is original and makes a significant contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms and distributed computation. While the problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner has been previously addressed [17], the proposed GoSta algorithms are novel in their specific mechanism that combines data swapping with explicit estimate averaging. This combination proves to be highly effective. The analytical technique using the phantom network is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:**
The results are significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners in distributed machine learning, sensor networks, and large-scale data analysis. U-statistics are fundamental quantities, and an efficient, robust, and asynchronous algorithm for their distributed computation is a valuable tool. The proposed methods are not only theoretically sound but also demonstrate clear practical advantages over the existing state-of-the-art, with lower communication and memory requirements per iteration, and an asynchronous variant that is crucial for many real-world decentralized systems. This work could serve as a foundation for more complex distributed optimization tasks involving U-statistics.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is very strong, but a few minor additions could make it even better:

1.  **Explicit Per-Iteration Cost Comparison:** The paper states that GoSta has lower memory and communication costs than U2-gossip, which is a key advantage. This claim is based on GoSta requiring one auxiliary observation and one edge activation per iteration, versus two for U2-gossip. While this is clear from the algorithm descriptions, the paper would be strengthened by including a small table that explicitly compares GoSta-sync and U2-gossip on key metrics per iteration:
    *   Memory per node (e.g., # of auxiliary observations).
    *   Communication per iteration (e.g., # of observations transmitted).
    *   Number of node-pair interactions per iteration.
    This would make the practical efficiency gains of the proposed method immediately apparent to the reader and would directly address any questions about the wall-clock time per iteration.

2.  **Discussion on Asynchronous Rate:** The experiments show that the asynchronous algorithm performs comparably to the synchronous one. However, the theoretical rate of O(log t/t) is slightly worse than the O(1/t) for the synchronous version. A brief discussion on this gap would be interesting. Is this gap an artifact of the proof technique, or is it fundamental to the challenges of the asynchronous setting (e.g., nodes having outdated estimates of the iteration number)? This is not a major weakness but would add more depth to the analysis.

### **Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Addresses an important and practical problem:** Provides an efficient method for computing a broad class of statistics (U-statistics) in a decentralized setting.
*   **Novel and effective algorithms:** The proposed GoSta algorithms (both synchronous and asynchronous) are new and demonstrably superior to the existing state-of-the-art.
*   **Rigorous theoretical foundation:** The paper provides strong convergence guarantees with insightful rates that depend on network and data properties. The "phantom network" proof technique is an original contribution in itself.
*   **Strong empirical validation:** Comprehensive experiments on real data and varied network topologies confirm the theoretical advantages of the proposed method.
*   **High clarity and quality of writing:** The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance (Minor weaknesses):**
*   The practical advantages regarding per-iteration costs (memory, communication) are claimed but not explicitly summarized in a direct comparison table, which would improve clarity.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and well-supported contribution to the field. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be easily addressed. I strongly recommend acceptance.
