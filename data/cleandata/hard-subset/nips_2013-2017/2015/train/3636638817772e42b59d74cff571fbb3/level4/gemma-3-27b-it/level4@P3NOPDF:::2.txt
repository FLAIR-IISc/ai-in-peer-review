This paper presents a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for computing higher-order statistics – U-statistics – over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. These statistics encompass quantities like sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients, representing important aggregate measures. The core contribution lies in a novel approach to decentralized aggregation and averaging.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, strengthening the connection to existing work and clarifying the theoretical foundations would significantly improve the paper. I did not have sufficient time to thoroughly verify the correctness of all details presented in the supplementary material.

Here are specific comments and suggestions:

1) The algorithm’s key innovation – the exchange of both estimate values *and* initial observation data during averaging – is crucial. This creates a coupled random walk for each initial observation. Explicitly highlighting this mechanism in the main manuscript would improve clarity and motivate the analytical approach utilizing multiple copies of the graph.

2) The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive review of related work. While the cited references are relevant, a significant body of research exists on gossip algorithms for computing statistics beyond those mentioned. For instance, the work by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) provides a useful snapshot of the state-of-the-art several years ago. Positioning this work within that broader context would be more insightful than the current, somewhat brief, discussion.

3) On page 4, the description of the induced distribution on edges from the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process lacks specificity. Saying “drawing a random edge of the network” doesn’t fully define the distribution. Clarifying how edges are selected (e.g., uniformly at random, based on node degree) is necessary.

4) A natural alternative approach to this problem involves increasing memory at each node to allow flooding of individual values across the network. Nodes could store received observations, tagged with the originating node, and compute the U-statistic locally on their held data. Comparing the proposed method to this flooding approach would be insightful. Such a comparison could illuminate the benefits of averaging, or potentially reveal performance limitations with the current method. Determining whether the achieved rate of 1/t is competitive requires understanding its performance relative to alternatives, particularly on expander graphs where flooding can be highly efficient.

5) The authors should investigate relevant work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms. This area appears highly pertinent to their approach. Specifically, consider the following papers: Jung, Shah, and Shin (Allerton 2007), Li and Dai (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, 2010), and Chen, Lovasz, and Pak (STOC 1999). These constructions may offer valuable insights and potential improvements.

6) Analyses of gossip algorithms typically operate under a worst-case assumption regarding the initial data distribution, guaranteeing performance across *all* possible initial data vectors *X*. The paper’s problem statement and theorem statement do not clearly articulate this assumption. It is critical to explicitly state the assumption on the data distribution and to define the guarantees on the error as a function of that initial data.

Minor points for correction:
* p1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs. This should be clarified.
* p4: “let us stress out that” should be replaced with “we stress that.”
* p4: “awakes and contact” should be “awakes and contacts.”
* p6: “requires to initiate” should be “requires initiating.”
* p7: “allows to create” should be “allows us to create.”

**After Author Feedback:**

I have reviewed the authors’ clarifications and modifications and find them to be significant and positive. I am revising my initial score from a 7 to an 8.

However, I still recommend a closer examination of lifting constructions, particularly LADA, and a revision of the related work section to reflect this. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation should incorporate baseline comparisons (e.g., the flooding approach mentioned in point 4) to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the algorithm’s performance.

In conclusion, this is a well-presented approach to extending gossip methods for computing complex statistics in distributed settings. While the mathematical formulation and connections to prior work could be more clearly articulated, I recommend acceptance contingent upon a strong revision addressing the points raised in this review.
