

This paper presents a significant extension of gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics in distributed networks. The authors address an important gap in the literature: while gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for computing averages of separable functions (e.g., sample means), they have not been effectively applied to U-statistics, which require pairwise averaging of observations. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic. They establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively, with explicit data and network-dependent terms. The theoretical analysis employs a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation that simplifies the convergence proof.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the NIPS community by addressing a well-defined gap in distributed computation. Previous work on gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation (e.g., [2, 10, 15]) focuses on separable functions, while U-statistics represent a fundamentally different computational challenge. The authors' comparison to U2-gossip [17] is particularly valuable, as they identify and address two key limitations: (1) U2-gossip requires storing two auxiliary observations per node and exchanging two pairs of observations per iteration, leading to higher memory and communication costs, and (2) U2-gossip is not truly asynchronous. The authors demonstrate that GoSta algorithms achieve faster convergence with lower memory and communication requirements.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The convergence proofs are rigorous and well-motivated, with the phantom node construction being particularly clever. The paper establishes meaningful theoretical bounds that explicitly incorporate network connectivity (through the spectral gap) and data characteristics. The experimental evaluation on real datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC and Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter) provides convincing empirical evidence that GoSta algorithms outperform U2-gossip across various network topologies (complete graphs, 2D grids, and Watts-Strogatz networks). The results show not only lower average error but also reduced variance across nodes, which is an important practical advantage.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with proper notation and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary technical details for verification. The significance of the work is evident: U-statistics are critical for many important applications (AUC, variance, Gini mean difference), and the proposed algorithms enable efficient decentralized computation of these quantities.

The main weaknesses of the paper are:
1. The paper does not provide detailed runtime analysis per iteration for GoSta compared to U2-gossip. While the paper mentions lower communication costs, it would be valuable to quantify whether each iteration of GoSta is significantly slower than U2-gossip (e.g., due to the additional data propagation steps). If GoSta requires substantially more computation per iteration, this could limit its practical utility despite better convergence rates.
2. The paper does not discuss the impact of network size on communication overhead. As the network grows, the communication cost of maintaining auxiliary observations could become significant, particularly for high-dimensional data.

Strengths:
- Novel problem formulation addressing a well-defined gap in distributed computation
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates
- Clear experimental validation on real datasets
- Practical improvements over prior work (U2-gossip) in communication complexity and asynchronicity
- Comprehensive comparison across multiple network topologies

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity per iteration
- No analysis of communication overhead for large networks or high-dimensional data
- The phantom node construction, while clever, might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory

Overall, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field of distributed optimization and gossip algorithms. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel and well-executed, and the results are significant. The minor weaknesses regarding computational complexity and scalability do not undermine the main contributions, but should be addressed in revision.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Addresses a meaningful gap in distributed computation of non-separable statistics
- Theoretical results improve upon prior work in convergence rate and communication complexity
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets and network topologies
- Clear presentation of the problem and solution

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity per iteration
- No analysis of scalability for very large networks or high-dimensional data

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, advancing the state of the art in distributed algorithms for U-statistics. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the computational complexity concerns in the final version.
