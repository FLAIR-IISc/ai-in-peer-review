This paper addresses the problem of computing higher-order statistics – including sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients – over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. The authors propose a gossip-style decentralized aggregation algorithm to efficiently compute these U-statistics. 

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, but the connection to existing related work could be strengthened. 

Please note that, due to time constraints, I was unable to fully verify the correctness of the material presented in the supplement.

**Comments:**

1) A key aspect of the algorithm is the simultaneous exchange of estimate values *and* the initial observation associated with those values between nodes. This induces a coupled random walk for each initial observation. Explicitly highlighting this in the manuscript would improve clarity and motivate the analysis, which leverages multiple copies of the graph.

2) The authors should broaden the scope of related work to include relevant research beyond the cited references. For example, the work of Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) provides a useful snapshot of the state-of-the-art in gossip approaches for statistical computation a few years prior to this work, and situating the present contribution within that context would be beneficial. The current discussion of related work feels somewhat limited.

3) On page 4, the induced distribution on edges resulting from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process requires clarification. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” is insufficiently specific to define the distribution.

4) A natural alternative approach involves increasing the memory capacity at each node and employing the same contact process to flood each node’s value across the network, storing received observations tagged with their originating node. Nodes could then calculate the U-statistic locally on the subset of data they possess. A comparison to this method would illuminate the advantages of the proposed averaging scheme, or reveal potential performance limitations resulting from constrained node memory. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the achieved rate of 1/t is favorable. For example, on expander graphs, the flooding method may perform well.

5) The authors should investigate and discuss the relationship between their construction and existing work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms. Relevant references include: Jung, Shah, and Shin (Allerton 2007); Li and Dai (IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 2010); and Chen, Lovász, and Pak (STOC 1999).

6) Analyses of gossip algorithms typically assume a worst-case scenario for the initial data distribution, providing guarantees applicable to all possible initial data vectors *X*. This assumption is not explicitly stated in the problem statement or the theorem statement. Please clarify the assumptions made regarding the data distribution and specify the error guarantees as a function of the initial data.

**Minor Points:**

*   p1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs.
*   p4: "let us stress out that" --> "we stress that"
*   p4: "awakes and contact" --> "awakes and contacts"
*   p6: "requires to initiate" --> "requires initiating"
*   p7: "allows to create" --> "allows us to create"

**After Author Feedback:**

The clarifications and modifications suggested by the authors are positive. I am revising my initial score from a 7 to an 8. 

However, the authors should still carefully consider the lifting constructions (particularly LADA) and revise the related work section accordingly. 

Furthermore, the experiments should include baseline comparisons to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

In summary, this paper presents a well-structured approach to extending gossip methods for computing more complex statistics in distributed settings, such as sensor networks. While the mathematical formulation and connection to prior work are somewhat unclear, I recommend acceptance if the authors address the comments raised in this review and make substantial revisions.
