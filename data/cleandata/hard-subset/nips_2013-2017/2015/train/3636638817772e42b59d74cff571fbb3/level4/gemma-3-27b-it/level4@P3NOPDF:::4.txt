## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission [Paper ID]

This paper addresses the problem of computing average statistics in a distributed computational environment. The proposed algorithm is conceptually straightforward, relying on an intuitive approach to aggregating information across distributed nodes. While the technical presentation and accompanying proofs are comprehensive and rigorous, the paper's novelty appears somewhat limited. 

A primary weakness of this work lies in the complete absence of comparison to baseline methods. Several readily implementable baselines exist, drawing from the well-established literature on randomized algorithms for distributed computation. For example, simple averaging schemes, weighted averaging based on node degree, or even stochastic gradient descent-inspired approaches could serve as strong comparative points. The lack of such comparisons significantly weakens the evaluation, making it difficult to ascertain the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm. While the problem addressed is interesting and potentially valuable, the evaluation presented fails to convincingly demonstrate an advantage over existing, simpler strategies. 

Specifically, the paper would benefit from experiments demonstrating performance relative to:

* **Centralized Averaging (as a theoretical lower bound):** While impractical for truly large-scale distributed systems, this serves as a useful benchmark for understanding potential inefficiencies.
* **Simple Randomized Averaging:** Each node randomly selects other nodes and averages their values. This is a trivial baseline but provides a starting point.
* **Weighted Averaging:** Nodes weight their contributions based on a metric like node degree or estimated data quality.
* **Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) Inspired Averaging:**  Treating the average computation as an optimization problem and using SGD-like updates could provide a competitive baseline.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear discussion of the computational cost associated with the proposed algorithm. While the proofs demonstrate correctness, a more detailed analysis of communication complexity and per-node computational requirements would be valuable. How does the cost scale with the number of nodes and the size of the data? Understanding these trade-offs is crucial for assessing the algorithm’s practicality.

The theoretical analysis is a strong point of the paper, and the proofs appear sound. However, the reliance on theoretical guarantees without empirical validation diminishes their impact. A more compelling argument would be built upon a combination of rigorous analysis *and* empirical evidence demonstrating the algorithm’s effectiveness in practical scenarios. 

**Strengths:**

* Rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive proofs.
* Conceptually simple and intuitive algorithm.
* Addresses a relevant problem in distributed computation.

**Weaknesses:**

* Lack of comparison to baseline methods.
* Insufficient empirical evaluation.
* Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability.
* Incremental novelty.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound algorithm for distributed average computation. However, the lack of empirical validation and comparison to baseline approaches severely limits its impact.  As it stands, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate a practical advantage over existing techniques. I recommend rejection, but would be open to reconsidering with a significantly expanded experimental evaluation that includes appropriate baselines and a detailed analysis of computational cost. The authors should address the concerns regarding novelty by clearly articulating the advantages of their approach compared to readily available alternatives.
