This paper presents a decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm for computing higher-order statistics, specifically U-statistics, over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. These U-statistics encompass various quantities of interest, including sample variance, correlation and scatter coefficients, and other aggregate measures. The proposed gossip-style algorithm is a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms.

However, to further strengthen the paper, the authors should establish a clearer connection between their approach and existing methods. Additionally, I did not have the opportunity to verify the correctness of the supplementary material.

Comments:

1. A key aspect of the algorithm is the exchange of estimate values and averaging between nodes, accompanied by the swapping of a secondary state variable representing the initial observation. This results in the initial observations performing a random walk, albeit coupled. Highlighting this aspect in the main manuscript would enhance clarity and motivate the analysis method via multiple graph copies.

2. The authors should situate their work within the broader context of gossip approaches for computing statistics, building upon existing literature such as Dimakis et al.'s Proc. IEEE paper. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the current state of the art.

3. On page 4, the authors should clarify the induced distribution on edges resulting from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process. Specifically, the phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" lacks specificity regarding the distribution.

4. An alternative approach to this problem involves increasing memory at each node, allowing nodes to flood their values across the network using the same contact process. Nodes would store received observations, enabling the calculation of U-statistics on the subset of possessed data. Comparing this approach to the current method would help evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm and its rate of 1/t. This comparison is particularly relevant for expanders, where the flooding method may be highly effective.

5. The authors should examine the work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, as it appears closely related to their proposed construction. Relevant references include Jung et al.'s "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," Li and Dai's "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," and Chen et al.'s "Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing."

6. Typically, gossip algorithm analyses assume the worst-case scenario for the initial data distribution, providing guarantees over all initial data vectors X. The problem statement and theorem should clearly outline the assumption on the data distribution and the guarantees on error as a function of the initial data.

Minor corrections:

- Page 1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs.
- Page 4: "let us stress out that" should be revised to "we stress that."
- Page 4: "awakes and contact" should be corrected to "awakes and contacts."
- Page 6: "requires to initiate" should be revised to "requires initiating."
- Page 7: "allows to create" should be corrected to "allows us to create."

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK:
The authors' clarifications and modifications are satisfactory. I am revising my initial score from 7 to 8.

However, I recommend that the authors further examine lifting constructions, particularly LADA, and modify the related work section accordingly. Additionally, the experiments should include baseline comparisons for a more comprehensive evaluation.

This paper presents a well-structured approach to extending gossip methods for computing complex statistics in distributed settings, such as sensor networks. While the mathematical formulation and connection to prior work require clarification, the authors' revisions have the potential to strengthen the paper. Assuming the authors address the comments raised in this review, I recommend accepting this paper.
