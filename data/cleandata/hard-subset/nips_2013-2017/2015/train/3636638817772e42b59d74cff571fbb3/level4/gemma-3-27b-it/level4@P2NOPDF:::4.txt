This paper addresses the problem of computing average statistics in a distributed computational setting. The proposed algorithm is conceptually straightforward, and while the mathematical proofs are thorough, the novelty of the approach feels incremental. 

A significant weakness lies in the lack of comparison to baseline methods. Several intuitive baselines, easily constructed using randomized algorithms, would provide valuable context for evaluating the performance of the proposed technique. The problem addressed is inherently interesting, but the evaluation presented is insufficient due to the absence of these comparative analyses. Without baselines, it is difficult to assess the true contribution and effectiveness of the algorithm. 

Specifically, the paper would benefit from comparisons to simpler averaging schemes (e.g., averaging a randomly sampled subset of the data), or established distributed estimation techniques. Demonstrating improvement over such readily implementable methods is crucial for establishing the value of the proposed approach. The current presentation focuses solely on the algorithm itself and its theoretical properties, leaving a critical gap in understanding its practical advantages.

Further clarification on the specific computational model assumptions would also strengthen the paper. A more precise characterization of the network and computational constraints under which the algorithm excels would be helpful.
