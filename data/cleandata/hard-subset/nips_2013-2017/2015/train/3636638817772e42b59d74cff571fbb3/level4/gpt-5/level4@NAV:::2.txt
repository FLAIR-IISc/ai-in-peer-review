This paper tackles the computation of pairwise higher-order statistics when observations are distributed across networked nodes. These U-statistics encompass many quantities of interest, including sample variance, correlation, scatter coefficients, and related aggregates. The primary objective is to introduce a gossip-based decentralized aggregation/averaging method to compute these statistics.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the gossip algorithms literature, though the paper should more clearly situate the work within existing approaches and ideas.

This is a heavy review. I did not have time to verify the correctness of the supplemental material.

Comments
1) A central algorithmic device is that when two nodes exchange and average their estimates, they also swap a secondary state that holds their initial observation. Consequently, the initial observations undergo (coupled) random walks. Stating this more explicitly in the main text would clarify the method and motivate the analysis via multiple copies of the graph.
2) There is extensive prior work on gossip for computing statistics beyond what is cited. A good snapshot from a few years ago is the Dimakis et al. Proc. IEEE survey. Positioning the current work within that body of results would be more informative than the relatively brief treatment in the manuscript.
3) On page 4, it would help to spell out the edge distribution induced by the “node wakes up, contacts neighbor” process. The phrase “drawing a random edge of the network” does not specify the distribution.
4) A natural alternative is to allow more memory per node and use the same contact process to flood each node’s value through the network. Nodes keep received observations (tagged by the originating node). At any time, nodes can compute the U-statistic on the subset they have collected. How does this compare to the proposed approach? This could illustrate when averaging helps, or quantify the performance loss under memory limits. Without such a comparison, it is unclear whether the 1/t rate is favorable. On expander graphs, flooding may perform quite well.
5) The literature on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip appears closely related to the paper’s construction. See, e.g.:
K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, “Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains,” Proc. Allerton Conf. on Comm., Control, and Comp., 2007.
W. Li and H. Dai, “Location-aided fast distributed consensus,” IEEE Transactions on Information Theory 56(12), 2010.
F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, “Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing,” STOC, 1999, pp. 275–281.
6) Analyses of gossip algorithms typically assume worst-case initial data, i.e., guarantees hold for all initial vectors X. This is not clear in the problem setup or theorem statement. What are the assumptions on the data distribution, and what error guarantees are provided as a function of the initial data?

Small things
- p1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs.
- p4: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”
- p4: “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”
- p6: “requires to initiate” → “requires initiating”
- p7: “allows to create” → “allows us to create”

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK: The clarifications and changes proposed by the authors are good. I am raising my initial score from 7 to 8. They should still carefully consider lifting-based constructions (especially LADA) and revise the related work accordingly. In addition, the experiments should include baseline comparisons.

This is a clearly presented approach to extending gossip methods to compute more complex statistics in distributed settings such as sensor networks. The mathematical framing and connection to prior work remain somewhat unclear. Assuming a strong revision that addresses the points above, I recommend accepting the paper.
