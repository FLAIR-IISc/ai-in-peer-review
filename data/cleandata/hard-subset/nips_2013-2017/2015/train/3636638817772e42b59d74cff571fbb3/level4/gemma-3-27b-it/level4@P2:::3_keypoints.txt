- Addresses decentralized estimation of U-statistics in networks, a less-explored problem than distributed averaging.  
- Proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for U-statistic estimation.  
- Provides convergence rate bounds: O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t / t) for asynchronous, with explicit dependence on data and network properties.  
- Motivates work via importance of U-statistics for tasks like AUC, variance estimation, and clustering.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly presented.  
- Empirical validation on two real datasets (AUC and within-cluster scatter) demonstrating superior performance to U2-gossip.  
- Experiments cover multiple network topologies, increasing robustness of evaluation.  
- Inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms broadens applicability.  
- Convergence analysis is a significant theoretical contribution and links performance to data dependency and network spectral gap.  
- Builds on existing gossip algorithm literature and extends methods to handle U-statistics, addressing a limitation of prior work.  
- Strength: Novel application of gossip algorithms to U-statistic estimation.  
- Strength: Strong theoretical guarantees via convergence rate bounds.  
- Strength: Clear empirical superiority over baseline U2-gossip.  
- Strength: Clear presentation and accessible exposition.  
- Strength: Practical relevance due to wide applicability of U-statistics.  
- Weakness: Lacks detailed runtime-per-iteration comparison to U2-gossip, leaving practical efficiency unclear.  
- Weakness: Limited baselines—should compare to other distributed estimation techniques even if not U-statistics-specific.  
- Weakness: Assumes undirected graphs; performance on directed or more complex networks not explored.  
- Acceptance arguments: Significant problem, novel and theoretically sound approach, convincing experiments, and clear writing.  
- Rejection concerns: Missing per-iteration runtime analysis and limited baseline comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: Strong contribution recommended for acceptance with a minor revision to include runtime-per-iteration comparisons.
