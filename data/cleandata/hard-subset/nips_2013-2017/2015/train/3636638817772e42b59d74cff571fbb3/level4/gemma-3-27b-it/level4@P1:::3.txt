The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. The proposed approach is evaluated on two datasets, demonstrating improved performance—measured by average relative AUC error—compared to the baseline (U2-gossip) across various network topologies. The paper provides convergence bounds for the proposed algorithms. 

However, I would like to see runtime measurements per iteration for both the proposed method and U2-gossip. If each iteration of the proposed method is significantly slower than U2-gossip, the practical utility of the work would be limited despite the improved convergence rate. The paper introduces synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics.

**Summary:**

The paper addresses the important problem of decentralized U-statistic estimation in networks, a less-explored area than distributed average computation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms and provide convergence rate analysis. The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of their approach compared to the existing U2-gossip algorithm on both AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** Decentralized computation of U-statistics is a relevant and challenging problem with applications in diverse fields.
* **Novelty:** The proposed algorithms represent a novel approach to tackling this problem using gossip protocols.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence rate analysis provides valuable insights into the performance of the algorithms, relating it to both data characteristics and network topology. The use of a “phantom node” approach is an interesting technique.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real datasets convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms compared to the existing U2-gossip approach.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Runtime Analysis:** The lack of runtime comparison per iteration is a significant omission. While convergence rate is important, practical performance also depends on the computational cost of each iteration. Providing this information would strengthen the claim of superiority.
* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The convergence rate for the asynchronous setting (O(log t/t)) is weaker than the synchronous setting (O(1/t)). While the paper acknowledges this, a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between synchronization and convergence speed would be beneficial.
* **Network Size Scaling:** While experiments are performed on different network topologies, the range of network sizes tested is somewhat limited. It would be helpful to investigate the scalability of the algorithms to larger networks.
* **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While U2-gossip is a relevant comparison, including results against simpler baselines (e.g., a centralized computation followed by distribution of the result) would provide additional context.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in distributed computation.
* The proposed algorithms are novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on convergence.
* The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of runtime analysis weakens the practical impact of the results.
* The asynchronous convergence rate is relatively slow.
* The scalability to large networks remains unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written. While the lack of runtime analysis is a concern, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to include runtime measurements.
