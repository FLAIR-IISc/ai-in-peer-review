This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics – statistics relying on averaging over pairs of observations – in a decentralized setting where data is distributed across a network of nodes. Such statistics, including variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients, are essential for characterizing global properties of data. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for this task, aiming to simultaneously propagate data and maintain local estimates.

The core idea of the proposed algorithms is to combine data propagation with local estimate averaging. Crucially, when nodes exchange estimates, they also swap a secondary state variable representing their initial observation, effectively allowing the initial observations to perform a coupled random walk across the network. This approach is then analyzed via a reformulation using “phantom nodes,” enabling a clear understanding of the convergence behavior. The authors establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case, with terms dependent on both data characteristics and network topology. Empirical evaluation demonstrates superior performance compared to a previously introduced approach.

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. However, strengthening the connection to existing work and providing more context would enhance its impact. While the authors cite relevant papers, a more comprehensive overview of the field, particularly the work of Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE), would be beneficial. This would better position the current work within the broader landscape of gossip-based computation of statistics. 

Specifically, clarifying the distribution governing edge selection in the asynchronous setting (“drawing a random edge of the network”) is important. Furthermore, exploring alternative approaches—such as allowing nodes to store all received observations and compute the U-statistic locally—could provide a useful benchmark and highlight the advantages of the proposed averaging strategy. It’s unclear without such a comparison whether the achieved rate of 1/t is favorable, particularly in well-connected networks (e.g., expanders). 

The authors should investigate the connection to lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms (e.g., Jung et al., Li & Dai, Chen et al.). These techniques seem potentially relevant to their use of “phantom nodes” and could offer further insights or improvements. Additionally, the analysis currently assumes a worst-case initial data distribution. Explicitly stating the assumptions about the data distribution and the resulting guarantees on error bounds would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.

Minor points: on page 1, the definition of bipartite graphs using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed graphs; “let us stress out that” should be “we stress that”; “awakes and contact” should be “awakes and contacts”; “requires to initiate” should be “requires initiating”; “allows to create” should be “allows us to create”.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner.
*   Clear mathematical analysis with provable convergence rates.
*   Empirical results demonstrate improved performance compared to existing methods.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with applications in various fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The connection to existing work could be stronger and more comprehensive.
*   The performance gain over alternative approaches (e.g., flooding with more memory) is not clearly established.
*   The analysis assumes a worst-case data distribution which is not explicitly stated.



After reviewing the authors' response to previous feedback, I have revised my initial score. The clarifications and modifications are well-addressed and improve the paper's clarity and impact. However, I still recommend that the authors carefully consider the literature on lifting constructions and include more comprehensive baseline comparisons in their experiments. Despite these remaining points, the paper presents a solid contribution and I recommend acceptance.
