

The paper presents a well-motivated and clearly written contribution on randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, addressing a relevant problem in distributed statistical computation. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous variants, which they rigorously analyze with convergence bounds—a significant theoretical advancement. Empirical evaluation on two datasets demonstrates that their approach significantly outperforms the U2-gossip baseline across diverse graph topologies, as measured by average relative AUC error. This robust performance across varied scenarios underscores the practical utility of their method and effectively justifies the proposed framework.

The theoretical contributions are sound, with clear convergence guarantees that provide a solid foundation for the algorithmic design. The empirical results are well-presented and convincingly support the claim that the proposed algorithms achieve superior accuracy compared to the baseline. The choice of evaluation metric (average relative AUC error) is appropriate for the problem domain and aligns with standard practices in distributed learning.

However, a critical omission concerns computational efficiency. The authors report performance gains in accuracy but do not provide runtime data per iteration for either their method or U2-gossip. This is a substantial gap, as the practical viability of any distributed algorithm hinges on its scalability and per-iteration speed. If the proposed method requires significantly more computation per iteration than U2-gossip—despite better convergence—the overall runtime advantage may be negligible or even negative in real-world deployments. For instance, if each iteration of the new method takes 2–3× longer than U2-gossip, the marginal accuracy gains could be offset by slower convergence in wall-clock time, especially on large-scale graphs. The authors should explicitly address this trade-off.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend including a detailed runtime analysis. A table comparing median/mean iteration times (e.g., in milliseconds) for both algorithms across the two datasets would be highly valuable. This should be reported alongside the AUC error results to clarify whether the accuracy improvements translate to practical efficiency gains. If the iteration time is comparable or only marginally higher, the paper’s contribution remains compelling; if it is substantially slower, the authors should discuss this limitation and explore potential optimizations. Without this data, the reader cannot assess the method’s real-world applicability, which is a key criterion for NIPS acceptance.

The paper’s clarity and technical depth are commendable, and the convergence analysis adds significant theoretical weight. The only major gap is the lack of empirical runtime comparison. Addressing this would transform a strong paper into a compelling one, as it directly impacts the reader’s ability to evaluate the method’s practical relevance. The authors should also briefly clarify whether their asynchronous variant incurs additional communication overhead compared to U2-gossip, as this could further affect scalability.

In summary, this work makes a meaningful contribution to distributed U-statistic computation with rigorous theory and promising empirical results. The proposed algorithms are well-motivated, and the performance gains over U2-gossip are substantial. The missing runtime analysis, however, prevents a full assessment of the method’s practical value. I strongly encourage the authors to include iteration-level timing data in the revision. If provided, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the omission of this critical empirical dimension warrants a minor revision to address scalability concerns.
