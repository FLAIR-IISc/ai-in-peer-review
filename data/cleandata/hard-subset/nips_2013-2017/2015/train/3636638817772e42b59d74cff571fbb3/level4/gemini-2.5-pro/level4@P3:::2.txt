### Review Summary

This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics (statistics based on pairs of observations, such as sample variance or AUC) in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip methods typically focus on simple averages of values held by individual nodes. To compute these more complex, second-order statistics, the authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, named GoSta. The core idea involves a dual process at each step: nodes not only average their local estimates of the U-statistic with a neighbor but also swap their auxiliary data observations. This data-swapping mechanism effectively makes each node's initial observation perform a random walk on the network, allowing all pairwise interactions to be eventually accounted for across the system. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, using a "phantom network" construction, to establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. Numerical experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation tasks demonstrate that the proposed algorithms outperform the main prior work in this area ([17]).

### Quality and Originality

This paper presents a solid and valuable contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. The problem of computing U-statistics in a distributed manner is important and less explored than standard averaging. The proposed algorithmic approach is novel and elegant in its simplicity. The theoretical analysis appears to be sound, although I have not verified the proofs in the supplementary material in detail.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Problem Extension:** The paper extends the gossip framework to a more complex and practical class of problems (U-statistics), which has received limited attention.
*   **Simple and Effective Algorithm:** The proposed GoSta algorithm is intuitive, combining a standard averaging step with an observation-swapping mechanism. This is a clever way to disseminate the necessary pairwise information throughout the network.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides formal convergence rate analyses for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, which is a significant strength. The bounds correctly capture the dependence on network topology (via the spectral gap) and data properties.
*   **Clear Improvement over Prior Art:** The authors make a convincing case, both theoretically and empirically, that their approach improves upon the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17] in terms of convergence, memory, and communication costs.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper could be significantly strengthened by better situating its contributions within the broader context of gossip algorithms.
    *   The related work section is somewhat cursory. A reference to and discussion of a comprehensive survey like Dimakis et al. ("Gossip Algorithms for Distributed Signal Processing," Proc. IEEE, 2010) would provide better context for the program committee and readers.
    *   The analytical technique, which relies on constructing a larger graph of "phantom nodes," bears a strong conceptual resemblance to "lifting" techniques used to accelerate mixing in Markov chains and gossip algorithms. The authors should review and discuss this line of work, as it seems highly relevant to their construction. See, for example:
        *   K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains," Allerton 2007.
        *   W. Li and H. Dai, "Location-aided fast distributed consensus," IEEE T-IT 2010.
        *   F. Chen, L. Lovasz, and I. Pak, "Lifting markov chains to speed up mixing," STOC 1999.
    This discussion is crucial for properly assessing the originality of the analytical approach.

2.  **Lack of a Key Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares against U2-gossip but misses another natural, albeit more memory-intensive, approach. A simple alternative would be for each node to broadcast its observation and for every other node to store all observations it receives. Each node could then compute the U-statistic locally on the subset of data it has accumulated. This "flooding" approach would converge quickly on well-connected graphs (like expanders) as soon as all data has been disseminated. Comparing against this baseline would:
    *   Help contextualize the O(1/t) convergence rate. Is this fast or slow relative to a method with higher memory requirements?
    *   Clearly illustrate the trade-off between memory/communication and convergence speed, which is a core motivation for gossip algorithms. The paper's contribution would be better highlighted by showing how it performs under memory constraints where flooding is infeasible.

3.  **Assumptions on Initial Data:** The analysis of gossip algorithms typically provides guarantees that hold for any initial distribution of data across the nodes. The problem statement and theorems should be more explicit about this. What assumptions are made on the data `X`? How do the constants in the error bounds depend on the initial data vector? Clarifying this would improve the technical precision of the results.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, clarity could be improved in a few areas:

1.  **Algorithmic Intuition:** The core algorithmic idea—that the initial observations perform a random walk on the graph—is a powerful piece of intuition. Mentioning this explicitly in the introduction or at the beginning of Section 4 would help motivate the algorithm and the subsequent "phantom graph" analysis.

2.  **Ambiguity in Process Description:** On page 4, the phrase "Draw (i, j) uniformly at random from E" does not fully specify the random process. It is important to clarify if this means every edge is chosen with probability 1/|E|, or if the process is driven by nodes waking up and selecting a neighbor (which induces a different distribution on edges if degrees are not uniform).

### Significance

The results presented are significant. They provide an efficient, practical, and theoretically-grounded method for a class of distributed computation problems not well-handled by existing gossip algorithms. If these ideas are adopted, researchers and practitioners could deploy decentralized algorithms for a wider range of monitoring and analysis tasks in sensor networks, P2P systems, and other distributed environments. The significance would be further enhanced by addressing the points above, particularly the comparison to the flooding baseline.

### Minor Comments
*   p2, Sec 2.1: The definition of a bipartite graph, `E ⊆ (S × T) ∪ (T × S)`, is more suited for directed graphs. For an undirected graph, it would be `E ⊆ {{s, t} | s ∈ S, t ∈ T}`.
*   p4, Sec 4.1: "we stress that the nodes need not to be aware" -> "we stress that the nodes need not be aware".
*   p6, Comparison: "requires to initiate" -> "requires initiating".
*   p7, Experiments: "allows us to create" is fine, but "is used to create" or "generates" is more direct.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a well-executed paper that introduces a novel and useful extension to gossip algorithms. The main weaknesses are the positioning of the work with respect to related literature (especially on lifting) and the lack of a comparison to a memory-intensive flooding baseline. Assuming the authors can satisfactorily address these points in a revised version, the paper is a clear candidate for acceptance.

**Recommendation: Accept**
