

This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are quantities depending on pairs of observations (e.g., sample variance, AUC, Gini mean difference). The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while gossip algorithms efficiently compute mean statistics (separable across observations), they cannot efficiently compute U-statistics, which require pairwise averaging. The proposed GoSta algorithms maintain local estimates of U-statistics while simultaneously propagating data across the network through coupled random walks. The authors establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings, with explicit dependencies on data and network properties.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The problem is well-motivated with clear applications to important statistical quantities. The approach is technically sound, with rigorous convergence analysis supported by theoretical bounds that explicitly capture network and data dependencies. The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real datasets provide strong empirical validation, demonstrating superior performance compared to the prior U2-gossip method.

The main strengths include:
1. **Originality**: The paper successfully extends gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, addressing a non-trivial gap in the literature. The "phantom nodes" technique for analysis is novel and elegantly resolves the coupled data propagation and averaging steps.
2. **Technical Quality**: The convergence analysis is thorough and well-justified, with clear theoretical bounds that reveal meaningful dependencies on network structure and data properties. The comparison with U2-gossip provides a convincing demonstration of the method's advantages.
3. **Significance**: The problem addressed is important for distributed systems, and the proposed algorithms have practical value for real-world applications in sensor networks and large-scale data processing.

Several points warrant improvement:
1. **Related Work**: The connection to the broader literature on gossip algorithms is insufficient. The authors should situate their work more clearly within the context of Dimakis et al.'s comprehensive survey on gossip algorithms for distributed signal processing, which provides an excellent framework for understanding this domain. The current discussion lacks a proper comparative analysis with key works in the field.
2. **Algorithm Clarity**: The core insight—that nodes exchange both estimates and auxiliary observations to enable coupled random walks—should be emphasized more prominently in the main text. This fundamental mechanism is critical for understanding the analysis but is currently buried in the algorithm description.
3. **Baseline Comparison**: The paper would benefit from comparing with an alternative approach where nodes store multiple observations (e.g., flooding their data across the network). This would clarify why the authors' averaging approach is advantageous, especially when memory is limited. Without this comparison, the O(1/t) rate appears arbitrary rather than demonstrably superior.
4. **Data Assumptions**: The theoretical guarantees should explicitly state the assumptions about data distribution. The current presentation implies worst-case guarantees over all possible initial data vectors, which is standard in gossip analysis but should be clarified.

Minor issues to address:
- Page 4: "let us stress out that" → "we stress that"
- Page 4: "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts"
- Page 6: "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating"
- Page 7: "allows to create" → "allows us to create"
- The bipartite definition should clarify that it applies to undirected graphs.

The paper is well-written overall, with clear mathematical formulations and a logical structure. The experiments are appropriately designed and provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority. The technical content is sound, and the theoretical results are significant for the field.

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to distributed algorithms for statistical computation. The authors have addressed the core problem of extending gossip to U-statistics in a technically rigorous manner with clear theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The main issue is the insufficient contextualization within the broader gossip literature, which is a common but critical oversight in distributed systems papers.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:
1. Strengthen the related work section by explicitly comparing with Dimakis et al. and other key works in gossip algorithms
2. Clarify the core mechanism of coupled random walks in the main text
3. Add a comparison with the flooding baseline approach to better justify their method
4. Explicitly state the data distribution assumptions in the theoretical guarantees

These revisions would substantially improve the paper's impact and clarity, making it a strong contribution to the NIPS community. The current work is already technically sound and significant, and with these minor improvements, it would be an excellent addition to the conference proceedings.
