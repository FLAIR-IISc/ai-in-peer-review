This paper addresses the problem of computing average statistics over data distributed across multiple nodes. The authors propose an algorithm for this task and provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of its properties.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Relevance:** The problem of distributed averaging is of significant practical importance in large-scale machine learning, federated learning, and distributed data analysis. The paper correctly identifies and tackles a relevant and challenging area.
2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions. The proofs detailing the algorithm's convergence and performance characteristics appear to be thorough and rigorous. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The proposed algorithm is conceptually straightforward and intuitive, which is a positive attribute for potential implementation and adoption. The paper is generally well-written, and the core ideas are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Comparison:** The most significant weakness of the paper is the empirical evaluation. The experiments presented serve to validate the algorithm's behavior in isolation but fail to compare it against any baseline methods. Without this crucial context, it is impossible to assess the practical advantages or trade-offs of the proposed algorithm. The claims of efficiency and effectiveness remain unsubstantiated in a comparative sense.
2.  **Questions of Novelty:** While the algorithm's simplicity is a potential strength, it also raises questions about its novelty. The core mechanism seems intuitive, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of how the proposed algorithm distinguishes itself from existing methods in the distributed consensus and gossip algorithm literature. The contribution appears to be more in the analysis than in the algorithm itself, which should be made more explicit.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

To strengthen the paper and substantiate its claims, I strongly urge the authors to include a comparative experimental study. Several intuitive baselines could be implemented to provide a much-needed frame of reference:

*   **Centralized Baseline:** As a gold standard, a simple approach where all data is sent to a central node for averaging could provide a bound on performance (though impractical in many settings, it is a useful benchmark).
*   **Randomized Gossip Algorithms:** Standard gossip or random-walk-based algorithms are a natural point of comparison for distributed averaging and consensus problems. Comparing against one or two well-established variants would be highly informative.
*   **Subsampling Baseline:** A naive but practical baseline could involve a central coordinator repeatedly polling a random subset of nodes and averaging their local statistics.

**Summary:**

In its current form, the paper presents an interesting theoretical result for an important problem. The analysis is comprehensive and appears sound. However, the contribution is significantly undermined by a weak evaluation that lacks any comparison to alternative methods. While the theoretical work is commendable, a strong empirical demonstration of an algorithm's practical utility is a critical component for a publication at this venue. Without benchmarks against reasonable baselines, the significance and practical impact of the proposed algorithm are difficult to judge. Therefore, I am hesitant to recommend acceptance at this time. The paper has potential but requires a substantial revision of its experimental section to become a compelling contribution.
