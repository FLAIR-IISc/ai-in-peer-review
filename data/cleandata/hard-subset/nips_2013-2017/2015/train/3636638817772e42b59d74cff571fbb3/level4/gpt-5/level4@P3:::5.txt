Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces two randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network: a synchronous variant (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous variant (GoSta-async). Each node maintains a local estimate and a single auxiliary observation. At each iteration, the chosen edge’s endpoints average their current estimates and swap their auxiliary observations, while all nodes (sync) or only the active pair (async) update their running averages based on H(Xk, Yk). The analysis uses an elegant “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from averaging, leading to convergence guarantees in expectation: O(1/t) for GoSta-sync and O(log t / t) for GoSta-async, with constants depending on data dispersion terms and the spectral gap of the network graph.

The work is grounded in the literature on randomized gossip for decentralized averaging (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006; Kempe et al. 2003; Dimakis et al. 2010), and contrasts with the only prior approach targeting U-statistics, U2-gossip (Pelckmans and Suykens), which propagates two auxiliary observations per node without averaging. The authors provide a refined analysis showing a √n/t factor for U2-gossip and argue that for realistic graphs the benefit from its faster propagation is outweighed by the lack of averaging. Empirical results on AUC and within-cluster scatter corroborate the theoretical improvements.

Quality
- Theory: Theorems 1 and 2 establish convergence in expectation under standard assumptions (connected, non-bipartite graphs). The data-dependent constants illuminate how problem difficulty and graph connectivity influence rates. The phantom graph analysis is insightful and technically sound. The asynchronous scheme’s unbiasedness is justified via counters mk with E[mk(t)] = t. The asynchronous rate bound O(log t / t) is plausible but somewhat loose, expressed with a graph-dependent constant multiplying ||H||; high-probability or finite-time concentration bounds would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
- Assumptions: Edge selection is uniform, and nodes must know degrees and |E| to compute pk in the asynchronous algorithm. While reasonable in some systems, it would be useful to discuss alternatives (e.g., Metropolis–Hastings weights) that avoid global |E| knowledge or support directed/asymmetric communications.
- Empirics: Experiments on complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs and two representative U-statistics show consistent gains in accuracy and variance reduction over U2-gossip. However, results are reported per iteration; complementary plots versus message complexity or bytes communicated would better capture practical efficiency, especially given the claimed communication/memory advantages. Evaluation under realistic asynchrony (variable delays, message loss) is not explored.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with clear algorithms and a helpful explanatory narrative for the phantom network analysis. Suggestions to improve readability:
- Define the uniform edge-sampling assumption and its realizability earlier, and clarify when all nodes update (sync) versus only active pair (async).
- In Algorithm 2, define pk and the need for |E| explicitly before the algorithm or provide a pointer to the derivation.
- Streamline notation around Wα(G), L(G), and spectral quantities; consider collecting definitions in a short table.
- Minor editorial issues: remove stray spaces in “U -statistic,” fix typos (“perfomance,” “non bipartite”), and standardize capitalization of algorithm names.

Originality
Extending gossip beyond separable functions to U-statistics is a relevant and non-trivial contribution. The combination of single-observation propagation with averaging is a clean idea that contrasts with the prior U2-gossip approach, and the phantom node technique provides a novel analytical pathway. The asynchronous variant with unbiased counters is also a practical addition.

Significance
U-statistics underpin many widely used measures (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter), so enabling their decentralized estimation is important. The demonstrated improvements in both theory and practice over the prior art make this work a useful building block for distributed learning and monitoring systems. The dependence on the spectral gap is standard and informative for network design. The potential to extend toward decentralized optimization of U-statistics increases the impact.

Pros
- Clear algorithms with modest memory and communication per iteration (one auxiliary vector per node, single edge activation).
- Solid convergence analysis with insightful dependence on data dispersion and spectral gap; clean O(1/t) rate in the synchronous case.
- Asynchronous algorithm requiring only local clocks and degree information; unbiasedness addressed.
- Empirical validation on multiple graphs and tasks; consistent advantages over U2-gossip; reduced variance due to averaging.
- The phantom-node analytical device is elegant and may be reusable.

Cons
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation; no high-probability or tail bounds.
- Asynchronous bound is relatively loose and expressed with a graph-dependent constant; practical convergence is stronger than the bound suggests.
- Assumes knowledge of |E| for pk; no discussion of weight choices that avoid global information or support directed/time-varying graphs.
- Experiments report iteration counts, not communication or wall-clock efficiency; no evaluation under realistic network imperfections.
- Limited set of tasks and moderate graph sizes; no very high-dimensional or large-scale stress tests.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide high-probability bounds or variance decay analysis, at least in the synchronous case.
- Discuss or experiment with alternative weighting (e.g., Metropolis or push-sum) to remove the need for |E| and to handle directed graphs.
- Report performance versus number of messages and total bytes communicated; include wall-clock on a simulated asynchronous network with delays and drops.
- Evaluate sensitivity to graph size and spectral gap more systematically; include larger graphs and dynamic/topology-changing networks.
- Add a brief complexity summary per iteration (compute, storage, communication) for GoSta vs U2-gossip.
- Clarify applicability when H(X, X) ≠ 0 and when nodes hold multiple data points; the remark could be expanded with precise update rules.

Overall recommendation
This is a solid and timely contribution that extends gossip-based computation to an important class of statistics with improved algorithms and analysis. While some theoretical and experimental aspects could be strengthened, the paper meets NeurIPS standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I lean toward acceptance.
