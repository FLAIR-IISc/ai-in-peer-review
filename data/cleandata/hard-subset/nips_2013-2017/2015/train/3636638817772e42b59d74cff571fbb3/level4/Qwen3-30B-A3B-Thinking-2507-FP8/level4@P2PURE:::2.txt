

This paper addresses the computation of higher-order statistics over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes, where these statistics encompass quantities like sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients. The primary contribution is a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for aggregating these U-statistics. While this represents a valuable extension to gossip literature, the authors must strengthen connections to existing approaches and ideas.

This is a lengthy review; I did not verify the supplement's technical correctness.

Comments:
1) The algorithm's key innovation involves nodes exchanging estimates while swapping a secondary state variable representing their initial observations. This causes each initial observation to perform a coupled random walk. Explicitly clarifying this mechanism in the main manuscript would improve motivation for the analysis method, which relies on multiple graph copies.

2) The manuscript overlooks significant prior work on gossip-based statistics computation. The state-of-the-art snapshot provided by Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE) offers a more relevant context than the current cursory discussion. Situating the work within this framework would be beneficial.

3) On page 4, the induced distribution over edges from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process requires clarification. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous without specifying the distribution.

4) A natural alternative is for nodes to use local memory to flood their initial values across the network (tagging observations by origin), then compute U-statistics on local subsets. Comparing this approach to the proposed method would clarify the value of averaging—particularly whether the 1/t convergence rate is advantageous or suboptimal, especially for expanders where flooding may perform well. Without this comparison, the rate's significance is unclear.

5) The authors should examine lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, which are highly relevant to their approach. Key references include:
   - Jung, Shah, and Shin (Allerton 2007)
   - Li and Dai (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 56(12), 2010)
   - Chen, Lovász, and Pak (STOC 1999)

6) The analysis assumes a worst-case initial data distribution (guarantees hold for all initial data vectors $X$), but this is not explicitly stated in the theorem. The problem statement must clarify the data distribution assumption and the error guarantees as a function of initial data.

Minor corrections:
- p1: The bipartite definition $(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)$ implies directed graphs.
- p4: "let us stress out that" → "we stress that"
- p4: "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts"
- p6: "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating"
- p7: "allows to create" → "allows us to create"

AFTER AUTHOR FEEDBACK: The authors' clarifications and modifications are satisfactory. I revise my initial score from 7 to 8. They should still closely examine lifting constructions (especially LADA) and refine the related work section. Additionally, the experiments must include baseline comparisons.

This work presents a well-motivated extension of gossip methods for complex statistics in distributed settings like sensor networks. The mathematical formulation and prior work connections require clarification. Assuming the authors implement the suggested revisions, I recommend acceptance.
