This paper introduces novel gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a class of data functionals important in various machine learning applications. The authors address a gap in the literature, as existing gossip algorithms are inefficient for computing U-statistics, which require averaging over pairs of observations rather than individual data points. The proposed algorithms, designed for both synchronous and asynchronous network settings, demonstrate improved convergence rates compared to the current state-of-the-art, specifically the U2-Gossip algorithm by Pelckmans and Suykens (2009), and offer advantages in terms of memory and communication costs. Experimental results on real datasets confirm the superiority of the proposed methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, effectively positioning the contribution within the broader context of decentralized computation and gossip algorithms. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work, including references to foundational papers in gossip algorithms [Boyd et al., 2005; Kempe et al., 2003] and applications of U-statistics [Lee, 1990]. The proposed algorithms appear novel, offering both theoretical improvements in convergence rates and practical benefits regarding computational resources. The extension to asynchronous settings is a particularly valuable contribution, as it addresses a common challenge in real-world distributed systems. 

A key strength of the paper lies in the thoroughness of the evaluation. The authors provide extensive empirical validation on two datasets—AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter—and compare their algorithms to a relevant baseline, demonstrating consistent performance gains. The convergence analysis, utilizing a clever "phantom node" reformulation, provides strong theoretical support for the observed empirical results. The analysis clearly articulates the dependencies of the convergence rate on both data characteristics and network topology, leveraging concepts from spectral graph theory [Chung, 1997]. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed algorithms present a novel approach to efficiently computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis provides strong guarantees on the algorithm’s performance, with rates dependent on data and network properties.
*   **Practical Significance:** The improved convergence rates and reduced computational costs compared to existing methods are practically relevant.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results on real datasets demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Complexity of Analysis:** While the analysis is rigorous, it relies on a somewhat complex "phantom node" construction, which may make it challenging for some readers to fully grasp.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While U2-gossip is a relevant comparison, exploring other potential baselines or variations could further strengthen the evaluation. 
*   **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t/t) rate for the asynchronous case is slower than the synchronous case. A discussion on the trade-offs and practical implications of choosing between the two algorithms would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While the analysis is somewhat complex, the clarity of the presentation and the strong experimental results outweigh this minor concern. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2015.
