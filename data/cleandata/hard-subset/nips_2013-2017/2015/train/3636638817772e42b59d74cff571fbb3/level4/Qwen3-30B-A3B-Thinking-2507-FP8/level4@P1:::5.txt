

This paper introduces novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing an important gap in the literature on decentralized computation. The authors focus on problems where the target statistic depends on pairwise interactions of data points (e.g., Area Under the Curve, Gini mean difference, within-cluster point scatter), which cannot be efficiently computed using existing gossip algorithms designed for separable statistics like sample means.

The paper makes a clear contribution by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics. The authors establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous variant, with explicit dependence on both data characteristics and network topology (through the spectral gap). This work builds on the foundational gossip literature (e.g., [19, 18, 5, 2]) but significantly advances the state-of-the-art by addressing a previously unexplored problem class. The related work section effectively contrasts their approach with the recent U2-gossip algorithm [17], highlighting key limitations in memory usage, communication complexity, and asynchrony support.

The technical quality is strong, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by well-constructed proofs. The use of "phantom nodes" for reformulating the problem is particularly elegant and facilitates the convergence analysis. The paper properly addresses both strengths and weaknesses: while the proposed algorithms achieve superior convergence rates and lower communication complexity compared to U2-gossip, the authors acknowledge the increased implementation complexity required to maintain auxiliary observations.

The clarity of presentation is good, with well-organized sections and helpful illustrations (e.g., Figure 1). The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The experimental section demonstrates clear advantages of the proposed approach on real-world datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) across different network topologies (complete, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz). The results consistently show faster convergence and lower variance compared to U2-gossip, with the performance gap widening as network size increases, as predicted by the theoretical analysis.

The originality of the work is substantial. While gossip algorithms for separable statistics have been extensively studied at NIPS (e.g., [2, 10, 15]), this paper identifies and solves a fundamentally different problem class. The authors effectively demonstrate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly in addressing the computational challenges of pairwise interactions. The paper also properly cites relevant NIPS work, including the U2-gossip approach [17] which the authors significantly improve upon.

The significance of this work is clear: U-statistics are fundamental to many applications in machine learning and statistics, and the proposed algorithms provide an efficient distributed solution that could benefit practitioners working with large-scale decentralized data. The paper successfully demonstrates how their algorithms can be applied to important real-world problems (AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter), showing practical relevance beyond theoretical contributions.

The paper has one minor weakness: while the authors mention the potential for adaptive communication schemes (referencing [6, 13]), they don't explore how these might specifically integrate with their approach. However, this is a natural direction for future work rather than a flaw in the current contribution.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization and learning. The technical quality is excellent, the approach is original and well-motivated, the results are significant and well-supported by both theory and experiments, and the writing is clear and well-organized. The proposed algorithms represent a meaningful advancement over existing approaches for U-statistic estimation in decentralized networks.

The paper should be accepted for NIPS as it meets all the quality criteria: technically sound with strong theoretical support, clearly written with well-structured arguments, substantially original in addressing a previously unexplored problem class, and significant in both theoretical and practical contributions to the field.
