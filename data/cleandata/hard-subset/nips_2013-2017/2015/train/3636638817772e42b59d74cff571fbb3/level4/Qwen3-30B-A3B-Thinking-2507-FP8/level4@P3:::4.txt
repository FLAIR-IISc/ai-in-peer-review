

This paper addresses an important gap in distributed computation: the efficient estimation of U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations) in decentralized networks. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for computing separable statistics (e.g., sample means), they cannot directly handle U-statistics like AUC, empirical variance, or Gini mean difference. The authors propose "GoSta" (Gossip for U-statistics) algorithms that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings.

The paper's main contribution is a novel approach to distributed U-statistic estimation that significantly improves upon the prior work of U2-gossip [17]. The authors introduce a clever reformulation using "phantom nodes" to separate data propagation from averaging steps, enabling rigorous convergence analysis. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with careful treatment of both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The convergence bounds explicitly incorporate data-dependent terms (quantifying estimation difficulty) and network-dependent terms (related to the graph's spectral gap), providing meaningful insights into how network structure affects performance.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The problem statement is well-motivated with clear examples of U-statistics in machine learning applications. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and well-structured, with detailed pseudocode. The experimental section demonstrates the approach's practical value through two compelling applications: AUC estimation on the SVMguide3 dataset and within-cluster point scatter on the Wine Quality dataset. The results consistently show that GoSta outperforms U2-gossip in both convergence speed and variance reduction across different network topologies (complete, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz).

The paper demonstrates significant originality by addressing a problem that has been overlooked in distributed computation literature. While gossip algorithms for separable statistics have been extensively studied [2, 5, 10, 15, 19], the authors correctly identify that U-statistics present a fundamentally different challenge requiring new algorithmic approaches. The comparison with U2-gossip [17] is particularly valuable, as it highlights the specific limitations of prior work (higher memory/communication costs, synchronous-only operation) and demonstrates how GoSta overcomes these drawbacks.

The significance of this work is substantial. U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and the authors provide a practical solution for their decentralized computation. The algorithms could enable new applications in distributed learning, where data is inherently partitioned across networks (e.g., sensor networks, distributed databases, or social media platforms). The authors' theoretical results provide not only practical algorithms but also principled insights into how network structure affects estimation quality.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed. The experimental section, while solid, could be strengthened by including additional baselines. For instance, the authors could compare with a simple "naive" distributed approach where nodes exchange all their data (which would require O(n^2) communication) or with a centralized computation as a theoretical lower bound. The paper mentions "additional comparisons to some baseline methods" in the supplementary material, but these should be included in the main paper to fully evaluate the practical advantages of GoSta.

Another minor weakness is the lack of discussion about how the algorithms scale to very large networks (e.g., n > 10,000 nodes). While the experiments show performance improvements as n increases, the paper doesn't explicitly address the computational complexity of the algorithm as network size grows. This is particularly relevant since the phantom network construction used in the analysis is O(n^2), though the authors correctly note it's only for theoretical analysis.

The paper also doesn't fully explore the limitations of their approach. For example, how does the algorithm perform when observations are high-dimensional (d >> 1)? While the authors mention that communication costs are lower for high-dimensional data, they don't provide specific numerical evidence.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed computation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the algorithms are novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate practical advantages over prior work. The connection to important machine learning applications (AUC, variance, clustering) further enhances the paper's significance.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel problem addressing a significant gap in distributed computation; comprehensive theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates; clear and well-structured presentation; compelling experimental results showing clear advantages over prior work.

Con arguments against acceptance: Limited experimental comparisons (could include more baselines); insufficient discussion of scalability to very large networks; minor limitations in the analysis of high-dimensional data.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important contribution to distributed learning. The theoretical results are sound, the algorithms are practical, and the experimental validation is convincing. While the experimental section could be strengthened with additional baselines, this does not undermine the paper's main contributions. The paper addresses a problem of genuine significance in distributed machine learning with a novel and well-executed solution.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the experimental comparisons. The paper should be considered for presentation at NIPS.
