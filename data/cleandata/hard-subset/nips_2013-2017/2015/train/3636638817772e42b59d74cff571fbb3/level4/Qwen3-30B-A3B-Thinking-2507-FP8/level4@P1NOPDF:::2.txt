

This paper addresses the problem of computing higher-order statistics—such as sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients—across distributed observations in a networked setting, using U-statistics. The authors propose a decentralized gossip-style algorithm for aggregating these statistics. While the contribution is valuable for extending gossip methods to more complex distributed statistics, the manuscript would benefit significantly from stronger connections to existing literature and clearer technical exposition.

The core algorithm relies on nodes exchanging both their current estimates and an auxiliary state variable representing their initial observations. This auxiliary variable enables each initial observation to perform a coupled random walk across the network. The manuscript should explicitly clarify this mechanism in the main text, as it directly motivates the subsequent analysis using multiple graph copies. Without this explanation, the algorithm's design and analysis appear disconnected from the underlying dynamics.

The related work section requires substantial strengthening. The authors cite foundational gossip references but omit key works that contextualize their approach within the broader literature on distributed statistics computation. A critical reference is the Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, 2010) survey, which comprehensively covers gossip-based approaches for statistical aggregation. Situating this work within that framework would better highlight its novelty and technical positioning. Additionally, the authors should consider recent advances in accelerating gossip via lifting constructions (e.g., Jung et al., Allerton 2007; Li and Dai, IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 2010; Chen et al., STOC 1999), as these methods share conceptual similarities with the proposed auxiliary state mechanism.

Regarding technical details:
- On page 4, the phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous. The manuscript must specify the exact distribution induced by the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process (e.g., uniform over edges, or weighted by node degrees).
- The paper does not compare its averaging approach to an alternative strategy where nodes retain full local data histories (e.g., flooding their initial values across the network while tracking source nodes). This comparison is crucial for evaluating the trade-off between communication efficiency (the paper’s focus) and memory usage. For instance, on expander graphs, flooding might achieve faster convergence with sufficient memory, making the 1/t rate of the proposed method appear suboptimal without this context.
- The error analysis assumes worst-case initial data distributions (over all vectors *X*), but this is not explicitly stated in the problem setup or theorem. Clarifying the data distribution assumptions and their impact on error bounds is essential for the guarantees.

Minor corrections:
- Page 1: The bipartite definition (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed edges; this should be clarified or adjusted.
- Page 4: "let us stress out that" → "we stress that"; "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts".
- Page 6: "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating".
- Page 7: "allows to create" → "allows us to create".

After author feedback, the revisions addressing the core technical points (especially clarifying the auxiliary state mechanism and strengthening the related work) are satisfactory. I revise my initial score from 7 to 8. However, the authors should still:
1. Incorporate the lifting constructions literature (particularly LADA, Li and Dai 2010) into the related work.
2. Add baseline comparisons in experiments (e.g., against flooding-based approaches) to demonstrate the trade-offs of their method.

This is a well-motivated paper that extends gossip algorithms to compute complex U-statistics in distributed settings like sensor networks. The mathematical formulation is sound, but the connection to prior work and technical justification require refinement. With the suggested revisions—particularly addressing the lifting constructions and adding comparative experiments—I recommend acceptance.
