- Addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics via gossip; proposes synchronous and asynchronous algorithms that maintain running local estimates while propagating observations.
- Proves expected convergence rates: synchronous O(1/t) and asynchronous O(log t / t), with constants depending on data dispersion and graph spectral gap.
- Introduces a “phantom nodes” analysis to decouple averaging and data propagation effects.
- Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across several network topologies show improvements over U2-gossip.
- Builds on well-studied gossip literature; closest prior is U2-gossip; contribution is combining propagation with averaging and providing asynchronous variant with explicit rates.
- Clear problem formulation relevant to ML and data mining applications.
- Algorithms are simple, practical, and have low memory/communication per iteration, especially the asynchronous variant.
- Rigorous analysis with explicit graph- and data-dependent constants; phantom nodes technique is a neat analytic tool.
- Empirical results align with theory and demonstrate advantages over the main prior method.
- Conceptual novelty is modest—ideas are intuitive extensions of known gossip and estimation schemes.
- Experimental baseline set is limited; needs comparisons beyond U2-gossip.
- Empirical metrics focus on iterations rather than wall-clock time or communication/byte complexity to reach target accuracy.
- Theoretical guarantees are in expectation; lacks high-probability or finite-time concentration bounds.
- Practical robustness (message loss, delays, degree heterogeneity, unknown |E| for pk) is not evaluated.
- Paper is technically sound with comprehensive proofs linking rates to spectral properties and data dispersion.
- Writing and organization are good; algorithm descriptions and phantom network construction are clear.
- Experiments would benefit from more implementation details, communication budgets, and stopping-criteria.
- Originality: problem important; contributions are incremental but meaningful (asynchronous operation, reduced resources, refined analysis).
- Significance: valuable for distributed U-statistics estimation, but impact would increase with broader empirical validation and time/communication metrics.
- Suggested additions: more baselines (centralized, MST aggregation, Monte Carlo subsampling, push-sum), and reporting of communication/memory and wall-clock time to reach target error.
- Suggested analyses: sensitivity to graph size/spectral gap, degree heterogeneity, dimension, and noisy/asynchronous conditions (drops/delays).
- Suggested theory: provide high-probability convergence bounds or variance decay results across nodes.
- Acceptance arguments: addresses a relevant problem with practical algorithms, solid analysis, useful asynchronous variant, and demonstrated improvements over main prior.
- Rejection arguments: limited novelty and narrow evaluation that omits communication/time efficiency and robustness testing.
- Overall: clean approach with strong analysis but needs broader empirical comparisons and practical performance metrics to strengthen the contribution.
