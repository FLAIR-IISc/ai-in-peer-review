Summary and contributions:
The paper introduces two decentralized algorithms—one synchronous and one asynchronous—for estimating U-statistics via gossip. The authors provide convergence guarantees: the synchronous method achieves a 1/t rate, while the asynchronous variant converges at (log t)/t. Theoretical analysis is complemented by experiments that compare against relevant baselines and demonstrate the proposed methods’ accuracy and efficiency.

Strengths:
- Clear problem motivation: computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting is practically relevant (e.g., pairwise similarity, kernel methods) and less explored than averaging or first-order moment estimation.
- Solid theoretical guarantees: the stated rates match what is often achievable in decentralized settings and are plausible for synchronous versus asynchronous regimes.
- Practical algorithms: the gossip-based formulations are simple, implementable in peer-to-peer networks, and avoid centralized coordination.
- Empirical support: experiments indicate favorable performance relative to competing approaches and illustrate the accuracy–efficiency trade-offs of the two variants.
- Presentation: the paper is well organized, with a logical flow from algorithm design to analysis and experiments.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Assumptions and scope: It is unclear under which precise conditions the convergence rates hold. The paper should state assumptions on the kernel (e.g., boundedness, Lipschitzness, moments), data distribution (i.i.d. across nodes?), and network model (static vs. time-varying, connectedness, spectral gap). The impact of these assumptions on constants and finite-sample behavior is important for practice.
- Degeneracy of U-statistics: For degenerate kernels (first-order Hoeffding projection equal to zero), classical asymptotics and variance behavior differ. Do the proposed algorithms maintain the same rates, or do rates depend on the degree of degeneracy? Clarifying this would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
- Dependence on graph topology: The rates are presented in terms of t, but practical performance often hinges on the network’s spectral gap or mixing time. Are constants explicit in terms of graph parameters? Providing bounds that highlight this dependence would help practitioners choose appropriate topologies or understand scaling.
- Communication and memory costs: Computing U-statistics involves pairwise (or higher-order) interactions. The paper should more explicitly quantify per-iteration communication, total message complexity to reach a given accuracy, and any additional storage (e.g., surrogate samples, sketches). This is crucial for assessing scalability, especially for higher-order U-statistics.
- Asynchronous model details: The asynchronous rate includes a logarithmic factor, which is typical, but the model (e.g., Poisson clocks, bounded delays, no collisions) should be clearly specified. How robust are the guarantees to message loss, delays, or clock heterogeneity?
- Experimental depth: While results are positive, it would be useful to broaden the experimental study: include diverse graph topologies (e.g., grid, random geometric, expander), network sizes, and stress tests with communication delays or packet drops. Additionally, report both iteration counts and wall-clock time, as well as message complexity.
- Baselines and fairness: Please clarify baseline implementations (e.g., push-sum, decentralized averaging with preprocessing, ADMM-based aggregation) and ensure comparable communication budgets. Including an oracle centralized estimator as a gold standard would contextualize accuracy.

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper is generally clear and well structured. Including concise pseudocode for both algorithms and a short “assumptions at a glance” box would further improve readability.
- Highlight the key lemmas driving the rates and briefly sketch proof ideas (e.g., contraction via spectral gap, variance reduction through averaging, martingale arguments). Directing readers to where constants are derived would be helpful.

Significance and novelty:
- The application of gossip to U-statistics is a meaningful extension beyond mean estimation and aligns with practical needs in distributed learning and signal processing. The theoretical rates, while not surprising, are valuable when specialized to U-statistic structure. Overall, the contribution appears novel and of interest to the community.

Suggestions for improvement:
- State all assumptions explicitly and discuss their necessity. Where possible, relax boundedness to sub-Gaussian or finite-moment conditions.
- Make constants explicit in convergence bounds, with dependence on the kernel, data variance, and graph spectral properties.
- Address degenerate U-statistics and whether the analysis changes with the Hoeffding decomposition.
- Provide a detailed communication and memory complexity analysis, including the dependence on the order of the U-statistic and network size.
- Expand experiments: more topologies, scalability plots, robustness to delays/losses, and a breakdown of accuracy versus communication.
- Add a positioning section contrasting with decentralized optimization methods (e.g., dual averaging, consensus ADMM) and randomized gossip for non-linear aggregates, clarifying when your approach is preferable.
- Discuss whether variance reduction or mini-batching strategies could improve constants without changing rates, especially for the asynchronous case.

Questions for the authors:
- What precise network assumptions are required for the asynchronous guarantees (e.g., independence of activation times, bounded delays, no message drops)?
- How do the bounds change with the graph spectral gap and network size? Are there regimes where asynchronous can match synchronous in practice?
- Do your results cover degenerate U-statistics, and if not, can they be extended?
- What is the per-node memory footprint, and can sketches or streaming approximations be integrated without biasing the estimator?
- Can the methods be extended to time-varying graphs or to settings with adversarial delays?

Overall assessment:
A well-written and timely paper that proposes practical gossip algorithms for decentralized U-statistic estimation with sound theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results. With clearer assumptions, explicit constants, deeper experimental analysis, and discussion of degeneracy and topology dependence, the paper would be a strong contribution. I lean positive.
