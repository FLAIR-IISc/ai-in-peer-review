

This paper addresses an important gap in decentralized estimation algorithms by extending gossip methods to compute U-statistics, which are essential for various statistical measures including Area Under the Curve (AUC), empirical variance, and within-cluster point scatter. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for estimating sample means (e.g., [2, 10, 15]), this work represents the first comprehensive approach to efficiently compute U-statistics, which require averaging over pairs of observations rather than individual data points. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic, establishing convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively.

The paper's main strengths are clear and well-supported. The theoretical contributions are significant: the authors introduce a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation to analyze the convergence of their algorithms, which is a clever technical innovation that simplifies the analysis of the coupled data propagation and averaging steps. The convergence rate bounds feature explicit data-dependent terms (reflecting estimation difficulty) and network-dependent terms (related to the spectral gap of the network), providing valuable insights about how network structure impacts performance. The empirical evaluation on two real-world datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC and Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter) demonstrates clear superiority of the proposed GoSta algorithms over the only existing baseline (U2-gossip [17]) across multiple network topologies (complete graphs, 2D grids, and Watts-Strogatz networks). The results show both lower average relative error and reduced variance across nodes due to the averaging step in the proposed algorithms.

The paper is well-written with clear notation and organization. The problem statement is precise, the algorithms are carefully described, and the experimental design is appropriate for the claims being made. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior NIPS contributions in gossip algorithms [2, 5, 10, 18, 19] and clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon the only existing work on U-statistics in decentralized settings [17].

However, a few weaknesses should be noted. While the paper establishes convergence rates, the authors do not provide detailed computational complexity analysis per iteration. It would be valuable to know whether the proposed algorithms incur higher computational costs per iteration compared to U2-gossip, as this would significantly impact their practical utility in resource-constrained distributed systems. The draft review rightly identifies this as a critical gap that needs addressing.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through rigorous theoretical analysis and compelling empirical results. The clarity of presentation is excellent, making the technical contributions accessible to the NIPS audience. The originality is significant, as the authors extend gossip algorithms to a previously unaddressed class of statistical estimators (U-statistics), with the approach representing a meaningful advance over prior work. The significance is clear: U-statistics are fundamental to many machine learning applications, and the proposed algorithms enable their efficient decentralized computation with lower memory and communication costs compared to existing methods.

The paper's contribution aligns well with NIPS's focus on high-quality scientific contributions that advance the state of the art. The authors have made a valuable contribution to distributed machine learning by solving a non-trivial problem that has been overlooked in the gossip literature. The work could enable new applications in decentralized systems where communication is limited, such as sensor networks and peer-to-peer systems.

For the program committee, I recommend accepting this paper. The technical contribution is substantial, the analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms. The paper's focus on a meaningful problem with clear applications aligns well with NIPS's scope. The only major concern is the need for more detailed computational complexity analysis to fully assess the practical utility of the proposed algorithms, but this does not undermine the paper's core contributions.

To strengthen the paper further, the authors should address the computational cost per iteration in their revision. This would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between the proposed algorithms and existing methods, allowing practitioners to better evaluate their applicability to specific distributed systems.
