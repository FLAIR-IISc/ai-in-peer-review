

This paper addresses the problem of computing higher-order statistics over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes, where these statistics include quantities such as sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients. The primary contribution is a gossip-style decentralized algorithm for aggregating these U-statistics. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to gossip algorithms literature, though the authors should strengthen the connection to existing approaches.

This is a detailed review; I was unable to verify the correctness of the supplemental material.

**Comments:**

1.  The algorithm's core innovation involves nodes exchanging estimates while simultaneously swapping a secondary state variable representing their initial observations. This causes the initial observations to undergo coupled random walks. Explicitly clarifying this mechanism in the main manuscript would improve readability and better motivate the analysis approach using multiple graph copies.

2.  The work should be situated more firmly within the broader context of gossip algorithms for computing statistics. The Dimakis et al. (Proc. IEEE, 2010) survey provides a comprehensive snapshot of the state of the art and would be a more appropriate reference point than the current, brief explanation.

3.  On Page 4, the induced distribution on edges from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process requires clarification. The phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous without specifying the distribution.

4.  A natural alternative approach involves nodes storing more memory to flood their initial observations across the network (tagged by origin node). Each node could then compute the U-statistic on its local subset of data. Comparing this flooding method to the proposed averaging approach would clarify the benefits of the averaging strategy and the significance of the convergence rate $1/t$ (e.g., whether it is optimal for memory-constrained nodes). For expander graphs, flooding might achieve comparable performance, making the trade-off explicit.

5.  The authors should examine related work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, which appears highly relevant to their approach. Key references include:
    *   Jung, Shah, and Shin (Allerton 2007) on lifted Markov chains.
    *   Li and Dai (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 56(12), 2010) on Location-Aided Fast Distributed Consensus (LADA).
    *   Chen, Lovász, and Pak (STOC 1999) on lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing.

6.  The analysis assumes a worst-case initial data distribution (i.e., guarantees hold for all initial data vectors $\mathbf{X}$), but this is not clearly stated in the problem setup or theorem statement. The authors must explicitly specify the data distribution assumption and clarify the error guarantees as a function of the initial data.

**Minor Corrections:**
*   Page 1: The bipartite definition using $(S \times T) \cup (T \times S)$ implies directed graphs.
*   Page 4: "let us stress out that" → "we stress that"
*   Page 4: "awakes and contact" → "awakes and contacts"
*   Page 6: "requires to initiate" → "requires initiating"
*   Page 7: "allows to create" → "allows us to create"

**After Author Feedback:** The authors' clarifications and modifications are generally good. I am revising my initial score from 7 to 8. The authors should still carefully incorporate the lifting constructions (especially LADA) into the related work section. Furthermore, the experiments must include comparisons against the flooding baseline to address point 4.

This is a nicely presented approach for extending gossip methods to compute complex statistics in distributed settings like sensor networks. The mathematical formulation and connection to prior work require strengthening. Assuming the authors implement a thorough revision addressing the above points, I recommend acceptance.
