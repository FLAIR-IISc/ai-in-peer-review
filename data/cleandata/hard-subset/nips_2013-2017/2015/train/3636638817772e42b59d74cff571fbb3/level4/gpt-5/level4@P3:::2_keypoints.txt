- Paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics and proposes two gossip algorithms: GoSta-sync and GoSta-async.  
- Key idea: maintain one auxiliary observation per node that is swapped and averaged, yielding coupled random walks of observations and convergence in expectation.  
- Analysis uses a “phantom network” construction to decouple data propagation from averaging and derive convergence-rate bounds.  
- Derived rates: O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t / t) for asynchronous, with constants depending on data-dispersion terms and the network spectral gap.  
- Empirical results show better memory/communication efficiency and faster convergence than U2-gossip on AUC and within-cluster scatter across multiple topologies.  
- Connects to classic and modern gossip literature and to U-statistics; contrasts with U2-gossip which propagates two auxiliaries without averaging.  

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea: pairwise averaging combined with swapping a single auxiliary observation implementing coupled random walks.  
- Provides convergence guarantees with explicit dependence on network spectral gap and data terms.  
- Presents an asynchronous variant using local counters to maintain unbiased updates without a global clock.  
- Refined comparison to U2-gossip showing improved memory/communication complexity and rate analysis.  
- Empirical validation on real datasets and diverse network structures supports theoretical claims.  

Weaknesses / Suggestions
- Clarify the random-walk perspective up front: swaps imply each initial observation performs a random walk.  
- Broaden related work (e.g., Dimakis et al. Proc. IEEE survey) to better situate the contribution within decentralized averaging/function computation.  
- Explicitly state the edge-selection distribution in the asynchronous model (degree-proportional) and how it maps to the stochastic-matrix assumptions.  
- Compare to a flooding/memory-rich baseline (analytically or empirically) to contextualize O(1/t) and O(log t / t) rates under different resource constraints.  
- Discuss lifted Markov chains and acceleration literature and clarify whether the phantom-node construction relates to lifting/LADA approaches.  
- Make data assumptions explicit: whether guarantees are uniform over datasets or only in expectation, boundedness assumptions on H/X, and scaling of data-dependent norms with n.  

Quality
- Technical development appears sound with careful matrix formulations and spectral-gap-based rates, though supplementary proofs were not verified; refined U2-gossip comparison is convincing.  

Clarity
- Paper is generally well organized and readable; recommend introducing the phantom-network idea earlier and clarifying the asynchronous model, induced edge distribution, and interpretation of constants.  

Originality
- Novel extension of gossip methods to U-statistics using a single auxiliary observation and phantom-graph analysis; asynchronous unbiasedness via local counters is a practical contribution.  

Significance
- Useful for sensor, P2P, and federated settings; low per-node cost with provable rates and empirical gains; spectral-gap dependence aligns with standard consensus results.  

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap in decentralized U-statistics with low memory/communication, solid theory with explicit network/data dependence, practical async algorithm, and empirical validation.  

Cons for acceptance
- Related work coverage should be deeper (lifting, broader gossip literature), missing flooding/memory-rich baseline comparisons, and some modeling/assumption details require clarification.  

Minor comments / typos
- Clarify bipartite-graph phrasing for undirected graphs; several wording fixes: “let us stress out that” → “we stress that”, “awakes and contact” → “awakes and contacts”, “requires to initiate” → “requires initiating”, “allows to create” → “allows us to create”.  

After author feedback
- Authors’ proposed clarifications address key points; recommendation to accept contingent on strengthening related work (lifting/LADA) and adding baseline comparisons.
