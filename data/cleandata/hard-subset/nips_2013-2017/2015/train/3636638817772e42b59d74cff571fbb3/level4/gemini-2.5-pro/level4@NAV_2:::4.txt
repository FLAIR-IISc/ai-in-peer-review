This paper addresses the important and relatively under-explored problem of estimating degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network. While gossip algorithms are well-established for computing simple averages of separable functions (e.g., sample means), they are not directly applicable to U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of observations. The authors introduce new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, to tackle this challenge. The core idea is to combine two iterative processes: a data propagation step, where auxiliary data observations perform a random walk on the network via swapping, and an averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, respectively. Their analysis, which leverages a novel "phantom network" reformulation, clearly shows how the convergence depends on both the network's spectral properties and data-dependent terms. The proposed methods are shown to improve upon the only known prior work on this problem, U2-gossip [17], in terms of convergence rate, memory, and communication overhead. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on real datasets for estimating the Area Under the Curve (AUC) and within-cluster point scatter.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical importance. U-statistics encompass a wide range of useful metrics in machine learning, such as variance, AUC, and various ranking and clustering measures. Providing an efficient and robust decentralized algorithm for their computation is a significant contribution to the fields of distributed learning and large-scale data analysis.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound and complete. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing explicit convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The analysis offers valuable insights by separating the data-dependent difficulty from the network-dependent connectivity (via the spectral gap). The experimental results, though focused on one competitor, are clear and effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods.
*   **Originality:** The proposed GoSta algorithms represent a novel and elegant approach to a challenging problem. While they build upon existing gossip primitives (averaging and random walks), their specific combination to handle the pairwise nature of U-statistics is new. The "phantom network" construction used for the convergence proof is also an original analytical tool. The paper clearly improves upon the state-of-the-art established by [17].
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem is stated precisely, the algorithms are described clearly, and the main theoretical results are presented upfront. The structure logically flows from background and related work to the proposed methods, analysis, and experiments.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental comparison, while directly addressing the most relevant prior work [17], could be strengthened by including simpler, more intuitive baselines. For instance, a naive baseline could involve nodes broadcasting their data to neighbors for a fixed number of rounds and then computing a local estimate. While likely less efficient, comparing against such an approach would provide a clearer context for the performance gains offered by the sophisticated gossip mechanism. The paper mentions additional comparisons in the supplementary material, but the main paper would be more self-contained and convincing if it briefly summarized the results against the most compelling baseline.
*   **Novelty of the Algorithm Design:** The algorithmic contribution, while effective, can be seen as an intuitive combination of two well-known gossip mechanisms: pairwise averaging for consensus and pairwise swapping for random data propagation. The primary novelty is therefore more in the rigorous analysis and the successful application to the U-statistics problem, rather than in the invention of a fundamentally new distributed primitive.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   The performance gap between GoSta-sync and U2-gossip appears to widen as the network size `n` increases (Figure 3a). This is consistent with the `sqrt(n)` factor in the U2-gossip bound (10). Could the authors comment on how these algorithms are expected to scale on extremely large networks (e.g., n > 10^4)?
*   Remark 2 briefly mentions extending the method to nodes holding multiple observations. It would be helpful to elaborate slightly on the "judiciously" weighted new edges. Does this introduce significant complexity, or is the extension straightforward?
*   In the asynchronous setting, each node must know its degree `d_k` and the total number of edges `|E|` to compute its update probability `p_k`. While this is a standard assumption, could the authors discuss the robustness of the algorithm if these quantities are only known approximately?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a solid contribution to the literature on distributed computation. It addresses a relevant problem with a well-designed and thoroughly analyzed solution, demonstrating clear improvements over prior work. Despite the somewhat narrow experimental comparison, the paper's strengths in significance, technical quality, and originality make a strong case for acceptance.
