This paper addresses the important problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics in networks, a less explored area compared to sample mean estimation despite its relevance to many applications. The authors propose new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and establish convergence rate bounds, demonstrating improvements over existing work like U2-gossip [17]. The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, connecting the problem to various real-world scenarios such as AUC calculation and within-cluster point scatter estimation. The experimental results on two datasets demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed algorithms compared to U2-gossip across different network topologies. The theoretical analysis, including convergence rate bounds and the impact of network structure via the spectral gap, is a strong point of the paper. 

The strengths of this work lie in its technical soundness, the clear presentation of ideas, and the empirical validation of the proposed algorithms. The convergence analysis, leveraging a “phantom node” reformulation, is particularly clever and provides insights into the algorithm's behavior. The explicit data and network dependency in the convergence bounds are valuable, and the comparison to U2-gossip is well-justified given its position as the previous state-of-the-art. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous versions broadens the applicability of the work. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately situates the contribution within the broader literature on gossip algorithms. 

However, one potential weakness is the lack of detailed computational complexity analysis. While the paper demonstrates improved convergence rates, information regarding the per-iteration runtime of the proposed algorithms compared to U2-gossip would strengthen the practical significance of the results. Knowing if each iteration of the proposed method is significantly more computationally expensive than U2-gossip is crucial for assessing its overall utility. Furthermore, while the paper mentions extensions to scenarios with multiple observations per node, elaborating on the practical considerations and potential overhead of such extensions would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
*   The proposed algorithms achieve demonstrably faster convergence rates than existing methods.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior.
*   The experimental results validate the effectiveness of the approach on real-world datasets and various network topologies.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly references related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed runtime analysis per iteration hinders a complete assessment of the practical advantages.
*   Further elaboration on extensions to multiple observations per node would enhance the paper’s completeness.



Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in distributed estimation. Addressing the minor weaknesses regarding computational complexity would further solidify its impact. I recommend acceptance.
