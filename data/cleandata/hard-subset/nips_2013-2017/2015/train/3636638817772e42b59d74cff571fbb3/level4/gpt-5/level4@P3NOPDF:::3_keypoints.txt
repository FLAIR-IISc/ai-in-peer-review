- Paper addresses decentralized computation of U-statistics via randomized gossip, proposing synchronous and asynchronous algorithms with convergence guarantees and strong empirical AUC performance vs U2-gossip.  
- Clear motivation and writing; problem is important for decentralized learning where pairwise statistics matter (e.g., AUC).  
- Providing both synchronous and asynchronous variants increases practical applicability.  
- Theoretical convergence bounds are provided for a nontrivial combination of U-statistics and network effects.  
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over U2-gossip across multiple graph topologies.  
- Missing runtime and communication analysis: no per-iteration wall-clock time, computational cost, or message-volume comparisons.  
- Limited experimental scope: only two datasets and a single metric (relative AUC error); no error bars across seeds/graphs.  
- Missing baselines and ablations: need comparisons to other decentralized aggregation schemes and analyses of design choices.  
- Asynchrony under-characterized: unclear clock model, allowance for delays/drops, and their impact on bounds/empirics.  
- Assumptions and tightness of bounds are not fully stated: kernel assumptions, boundedness/independence, graph connectivity, scaling with network size/spectral gap/variance.  
- Unclear per-node memory and computation: naive U-statistics are quadratic—need per-update complexity, memory footprint, and unbiased pair-sampling details.  
- Requests: report per-iteration wall-clock and total time to reach target accuracy (with hardware/implementation notes).  
- Requests: report communication cost (messages/iteration, bytes/message, total to target error) and show convergence vs wall-clock time.  
- Requests: sensitivity analyses for network size, degree/spectral gap, and asynchrony (delay distributions, message drops).  
- Requests: compare to a centralized estimator and evaluate on at least one additional dataset with error bars over random graphs.  
- Presentation fixes requested: explicit pseudocode for both algorithms with per-step costs, define graph models and their spectral properties, and unify/fix terminology and minor grammar.  
- Overall: technically sound and promising; recommend acceptance conditional on adding comprehensive runtime/communication analyses, broader empirical validation, and clearer assumptions/costs.
