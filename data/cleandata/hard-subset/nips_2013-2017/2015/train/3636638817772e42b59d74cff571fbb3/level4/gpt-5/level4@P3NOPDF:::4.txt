Summary
The paper addresses the problem of computing average statistics in a distributed environment. It proposes an intuitive algorithm and provides comprehensive theoretical analysis. The topic is important for large-scale and resource-constrained systems where centralized aggregation is impractical.

Strengths
- Clear problem setting with practical relevance to distributed systems and sensor networks.
- The algorithm is conceptually simple and appears easy to implement.
- The theoretical section is thorough; the proofs seem comprehensive and carefully constructed.
- The paper’s focus on rigorous guarantees is commendable and could be of interest if paired with strong empirical validation.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is unclear. The algorithm appears closely related to existing distributed averaging and consensus methods (e.g., randomized gossip, push-sum, Metropolis-weighted consensus, and accelerated gossip). The paper does not convincingly differentiate the approach or articulate a distinct advantage.
- Empirical evaluation is weak. There are no comparisons to baseline methods, making it difficult to assess practical impact, convergence speed, or robustness.
- The experimental section does not explore a range of network topologies, communication models, or failure modes. As a result, generality and scalability are not demonstrated.
- Assumptions and practical constraints (e.g., asynchrony, packet loss, quantization, communication costs) are not sufficiently discussed relative to the claims.

Quality and Significance
While the theory is solid, the lack of comparative experiments substantially limits the paper’s significance. In a mature area with many well-studied baselines, empirical evidence is crucial to establish improvements in convergence rate, message complexity, robustness, or implementation simplicity. Without this, it is not possible to judge whether the method advances the state of the art or merely restates known ideas with new analysis.

Clarity
The writing is generally clear, and the proofs are well organized. However, the paper would benefit from a more explicit positioning within the literature, including a discussion of how the algorithm differs from or improves upon:
- Classical randomized gossip and pairwise averaging (e.g., Boyd et al., 2006)
- Metropolis-Hastings weights for undirected graphs
- Push-sum for directed or imbalanced networks
- Accelerated gossip (e.g., Chebyshev-accelerated methods)
- Simple tree-based aggregation or spanning-tree protocols as centralized-leaning baselines

Suggested Experiments and Baselines
To strengthen the paper, I recommend including a comprehensive empirical comparison on synthetic and/or real network scenarios:
- Baselines:
  - Randomized pairwise gossip and synchronous consensus with Metropolis weights
  - Push-sum (for directed graphs)
  - Accelerated gossip methods
  - A centralized aggregator (oracle) to benchmark accuracy vs. communication cost
  - Simple spanning-tree or flooding protocols as communication-efficient references
- Metrics:
  - Convergence to epsilon-accuracy vs. communication rounds
  - Total message complexity and per-node communication volume
  - Wall-clock time under realistic link latencies and processing times
  - Robustness to packet loss, delays, and asynchrony
  - Sensitivity to network size and topology (ring, grid, Erdos–Rényi, scale-free)
  - Impact of quantization or limited precision if applicable
- Ablations:
  - Effects of algorithm parameters (e.g., step sizes or weights)
  - Heterogeneous data across nodes vs. homogeneous settings
  - Straggler or partial participation scenarios

Positioning and Theoretical Context
If the main novelty lies in the analysis (e.g., tighter bounds under specific topology assumptions, improved constants, or relaxed assumptions on connectivity or synchrony), this should be stated explicitly and contrasted theoretically with known results. A succinct proof roadmap highlighting what is technically new would also help readers appreciate the contribution.

Questions for the Authors
- What is the precise novelty over standard consensus and gossip algorithms? Is it the algorithmic mechanism, the analysis, or the assumptions?
- Under what conditions (graph classes, synchrony models) do you obtain provable improvements over baselines, and by how much?
- How does the method behave with asynchrony, message loss, or time-varying graphs?
- What are the per-iteration computational and memory costs per node, and how do these compare to established methods?
- Can the approach be extended to averaging with constraints, privacy/noise, or quantization, and would the guarantees persist?

Overall Assessment
The paper tackles an important and well-studied problem with solid theoretical work. However, the lack of empirical comparisons and unclear positioning relative to established baselines significantly limit the contribution. I encourage the authors to add a thorough experimental section with standard baselines and to sharpen the claim of novelty—either algorithmic or analytical. With these additions, the work could be a meaningful contribution to distributed averaging; in its current form, it falls short due to weak evaluation and unclear distinctiveness.
