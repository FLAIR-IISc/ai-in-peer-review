This paper introduces new randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in distributed networks. While distributed estimation of sample means has been well-studied, the computation of U-statistics – essential for describing global population properties like variance, AUC, and Gini mean difference – has received less attention. The authors address this gap by proposing algorithms that simultaneously propagate data and maintain local estimates, achieving convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous cases, respectively.

The paper builds upon the well-established field of gossip algorithms [Boyd et al., 2005; Kempe et al., 2003], which are fundamental for decentralized computation in constrained network environments. The core contribution lies in extending these algorithms to handle the more complex averaging over pairs of observations inherent in U-statistics, a challenge not addressed by standard gossip approaches.  Previous work by Pelckmans & Suykens [2011] attempted this, but the proposed algorithms offer improved convergence rates and reduced communication/memory costs. This work is also related to distributed optimization techniques [Duchi et al., 2011; Nedić & Ozdaglar, 2009], though the focus here is specifically on U-statistic estimation via a gossip-based approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The paper addresses an important problem – decentralized U-statistic estimation – that hasn’t received significant attention in the gossip algorithm literature. Extending the standard gossip framework to handle U-statistics is a valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, supported by theoretical analysis. The inclusion of data and network-dependent terms in the bounds is a strength, reflecting a nuanced understanding of the factors affecting performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is generally well-defined, and the algorithm descriptions are straightforward. The use of a “phantom node” reformulation to aid analysis is clever.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms, particularly when compared to the existing U2-gossip algorithm [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2011].
* **Practical Relevance:**  U-statistics are used in a variety of applications, making this work potentially valuable to researchers and practitioners in machine learning, data mining, and distributed systems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Asynchronous Complexity:** While an asynchronous algorithm is presented, its convergence analysis appears less straightforward than the synchronous case, and the resulting rate (O(log t/t)) is slower. Further clarification on the practical implications of this difference, and potential strategies for mitigating it, would be helpful.
* **Network Assumptions:**  The analysis relies on the network being connected and, for faster convergence, having a good spectral gap. The paper doesn’t fully explore the performance implications for networks with poorer connectivity or specific topologies.
* **Limited Comparison:** While a comparison to U2-gossip is provided, comparing to other relevant distributed estimation techniques (even those not specifically designed for U-statistics) could further strengthen the evaluation.
* **Notation Burden:** The introduction of the "phantom node" concept, while insightful, adds to the notational complexity and could make the paper harder to follow for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to an under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
* The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach.
* The work has the potential to impact multiple research areas.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The asynchronous algorithm’s slower convergence rate and complex analysis might limit its practical applicability.
* The assumptions about network connectivity could restrict the applicability of the algorithms in certain scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The novelty of the approach, the rigor of the analysis, and the compelling experimental results all support acceptance. While the asynchronous algorithm’s performance and the network assumptions are areas for potential improvement, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend accepting this paper.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
