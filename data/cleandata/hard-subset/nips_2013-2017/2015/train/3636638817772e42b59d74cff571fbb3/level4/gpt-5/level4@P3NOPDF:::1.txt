Summary
The paper proposes new gossip algorithms for distributed computation of U-statistics. The methods improve upon the U2-Gossip algorithm of Pelckmans and Suykens (2009) in both convergence speed and resource usage (memory and communication), and they are designed to operate in realistic asynchronous environments. Theoretical guarantees are complemented by empirical evaluations on two datasets, where the proposed methods consistently outperform existing baselines.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and positioning within the literature. The paper is well written and easy to follow.
- Novel algorithmic contributions for computing U-statistics in distributed settings, with careful attention to both synchronous and asynchronous operation.
- Strong theoretical component: the paper provides convergence guarantees and positions the improvements relative to prior work.
- Practical relevance: support for asynchronous (real-world) settings is a meaningful advance over many synchronous-only approaches.
- Empirical validation: experiments on two datasets show faster convergence and lower resource consumption compared to U2-Gossip and other baselines.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
- Asynchrony model: The paper would benefit from a precise and consolidated statement of the asynchrony assumptions (e.g., bounded delay vs. unbounded but with finite mean, partial asynchrony, atomic updates, clock drift, and fairness/activation assumptions). Clarifying whether delays, drops, or out-of-order messages are allowed and how they are handled would strengthen the practical claims.
- Network model: Please make explicit whether results hold for general connected undirected graphs, directed graphs, and/or time-varying topologies. If the rates depend on the spectral gap or mixing time, highlight this dependence in theorem statements and discuss implications for sparse vs. dense graphs.
- Non-asymptotic rates and constants: While improved rates are claimed relative to U2-Gossip, it would help to give side-by-side rate expressions (including constant factors and dependence on the number of nodes, the order of the U-statistic, and properties of the kernel). Non-asymptotic bounds in terms of iterations, messages, or bytes communicated would make comparisons more actionable.
- Resource accounting: Provide a clear per-node memory complexity and per-iteration communication cost for each algorithm, ideally summarized in a small table. In the experiments, report not only error vs. iterations but also error vs. wall-clock time and vs. total communicated bytes/messages to substantiate “cost” advantages.
- Scope of U-statistics: It would help to state explicitly the class of U-statistics covered (order, kernel properties such as boundedness/Lipschitzness, degeneracy assumptions) and whether the approach extends to higher-order statistics without blowing up memory or communication.
- Robustness: If possible, add experiments examining robustness to heterogeneous delays, packet loss, or temporary node failures. At minimum, discuss how the algorithm behaves under such conditions and which assumptions are critical for theoretical guarantees.
- Experimental breadth: The current two datasets are a good start. Including results across multiple network topologies (e.g., ring, grid, Erdos–Renyi, small-world) and network sizes would illuminate how performance scales with topology and graph size. Reporting variance across random seeds/topologies with confidence intervals would further bolster the empirical claims.
- Baseline tuning and fairness: Clarify hyperparameter tuning protocols for all methods and ensure that comparisons are made under matched budgets (e.g., same number of messages or same wall-clock time). If early-stopping or termination criteria differ, standardize or justify the choices.
- Practical deployment details: Indicate whether the algorithms require global knowledge (e.g., number of nodes, degrees, spectral gap) or can operate with purely local information. This affects deployability in large, dynamic networks.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact asynchrony model assumed in analysis and in experiments? Are message delays bounded? Are dropped or out-of-order messages possible, and if so, how are they handled?
- Do the convergence rates explicitly depend on the spectral properties of the communication graph? If yes, please provide the dependence and discuss its practical impact.
- What are the per-node memory requirements and per-iteration communication costs, in big-O and in practical units?
- Do the algorithms require knowledge of the total number of nodes or other global parameters?
- Can the approach handle directed or time-varying graphs, and if so, with what changes to the analysis?
- Are the estimators unbiased at every iteration or only in the limit? If biased early on, can you characterize the bias-variance trade-off?
- How do the methods scale with the order of the U-statistic? Are there bottlenecks for order > 2?

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent terminology for the U2-Gossip baseline and provide a complete citation to Pelckmans and Suykens (2009).
- Consider adding brief high-level pseudocode to improve readability and reproducibility.
- A short primer or appendix recap on U-statistics and target applications would make the paper accessible to a broader audience.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution. The paper addresses an important problem, proposes novel and practically relevant algorithms with theoretical support, and demonstrates empirical gains over credible baselines. With clearer articulation of the asynchrony and network assumptions, more detailed resource accounting, and broader experimental coverage, the work would be even stronger. I am leaning toward acceptance.
