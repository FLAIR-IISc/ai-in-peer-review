This paper addresses decentralized computation of U-statistics—averages over all pairs of observations—when each node in a network holds a single observation. Examples include sample variance, AUC, Gini mean difference, Kendall’s tau, and within-cluster scatter. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) that simultaneously propagate observations and average local estimates. Each node maintains an auxiliary observation that is swapped when two nodes communicate; thus observations execute coupled random walks over the graph. The analysis uses a “phantom graph” construction to model data propagation and establishes expected convergence rates of O(1/t) (synchronous) and O((log t)/t) (asynchronous), with constants that separate data-dependent dispersion from network-dependent spectral gap terms. The paper compares against U2-gossip, arguing lower memory/communication and faster convergence, and presents experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several network topologies.

Relation to prior work: Gossip for distributed averaging and separable functions is well studied (e.g., Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE). The most directly related prior work on U-statistics is U2-gossip. The paper’s averaging-plus-propagation design is natural given this context. It would benefit from a tighter positioning with the broader gossip literature (Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE provides a useful survey) and a discussion of lifting-based accelerations (Jung–Shah–Shin; Li–Dai’s LADA; Chen–Lovász–Pak), which seem related to the phantom-node construction.

Strengths
- Clear, practical algorithms for a relevant class of non-separable statistics.
- Elegant analysis via the phantom graph that yields explicit data/network-dependent rates.
- Asynchronous variant that removes the global clock assumption.
- Reduced memory/communication burden relative to U2-gossip.
- Empirical results on realistic tasks and network models broadly support the claims.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Related work is treated somewhat narrowly; connections to broader gossip methods and lifting constructions should be strengthened.
- The edge selection mechanism needs to be specified precisely (see Comment 3).
- Assumptions on data and the scope of guarantees (worst-case over data vs. distributional assumptions) are unclear (see Comment 6).
- Baseline comparisons are limited; a natural flooding-style baseline is missing (see Comment 4).

Quality
- The main algorithmic ideas are sound and well motivated. The convergence claims match known flavors of rates for randomized incremental methods with averaging. However, I did not verify the supplementary proofs in detail. The comparisons to U2-gossip are informative, and the explicit dependence on the spectral gap is appropriate and expected.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well written and organized. The key idea—that auxiliary observations perform random walks—should be emphasized earlier and more directly, as it motivates the phantom-node analysis. Minor language edits are listed below.

Originality
- Extending gossip beyond separable functions to U-statistics with explicit rates and an asynchronous variant is a useful step. The combination of averaging with single-observation propagation is a clean alternative to U2-gossip’s dual propagation. The phantom-node analysis is novel in this context, though related conceptually to lifted Markov chains.

Significance
- U-statistics are widely used, and a practical, lightweight decentralized estimator is valuable. The improvement over U2-gossip in memory/communication and (empirically) in speed is meaningful. With stronger baselines and connections to acceleration techniques, the impact would increase further.

Specific comments and suggestions
1) Key mechanism: When two nodes average their estimates, they also swap their auxiliary observations; each original observation thus performs a random walk (coupled through the contact process). Please highlight this mechanism clearly in the main text, as it motivates both the algorithm and the phantom-node analysis.

2) Related work: Beyond the current citations, please situate the work more fully within the gossip literature (e.g., Dimakis et al., Proc. IEEE) and discuss how your approach relates to:
- K. Jung, D. Shah, and J. Shin, Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains, Allerton 2007.
- W. Li and H. Dai, Location-aided fast distributed consensus (LADA), IEEE TIT 2010.
- F. Chen, L. Lovász, and I. Pak, Lifting Markov chains to speed up mixing, STOC 1999.

3) Edge selection distribution: In the asynchronous model “a random edge is drawn” is ambiguous. Under the “node wakes up and contacts a random neighbor” process, the induced edge distribution is proportional to degrees. Please state the exact distribution (e.g., pij = 1/|E| for uniform-edge selection, or specify pk = 2dk/|E| as used later) and ensure consistency between the model and analysis.

4) Baseline: A natural alternative is flooding: allow more memory per node, have each node flood its observation, store received observations (tagged), and compute the U-statistic on the subset available at each time. On expanders, flooding can be rapid. A comparison—empirical and/or analytical—would clarify when averaging helps and how performance degrades with memory limits. This would also contextualize whether the O(1/t) rate is competitive.

5) Connections to lifting: Your phantom-node construction resembles a lifted chain over an expanded state space. Please discuss whether existing lifting analyses could yield sharper mixing bounds or guide design choices (e.g., structured swaps) to accelerate convergence.

6) Assumptions and guarantees: Please clarify whether guarantees hold uniformly over all data matrices X (worst-case error bounds conditional on X) or under distributional assumptions. The norms appearing in the bounds suggest worst-case over X, but the problem statement and theorems should state assumptions and the sense in which expectations are taken (over algorithmic randomness only, conditional on data).

Small edits
- p1: The bipartite definition using (S × T) ∪ (T × S) implies directed edges; refine to the undirected case.
- p4: “we stress that” (instead of “let us stress out that”).
- p4: “awakes and contacts” (instead of “awakes and contact”).
- p6: “requires initiating” (instead of “requires to initiate”).
- p7: “allows us to create” (instead of “allows to create”).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important non-separable class of statistics with practical algorithms.
- Clear improvement in resource usage and empirical performance over U2-gossip.
- Solid, interpretable convergence analysis with network/data dependence.
- Both synchronous and asynchronous variants.

Cons for acceptance
- Related work and connections to lifting/acceleration are underdeveloped.
- Missing comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., flooding with memory).
- Some modeling details (edge distribution, assumptions on data) need clarification.

After author feedback: The clarifications and planned revisions address several concerns. I raise my initial score from 7 to 8. I still recommend incorporating lifting constructions (especially LADA) into related work and adding baseline comparisons in the experiments. Assuming these revisions, I recommend acceptance.
