This paper proposes a gossip-style decentralized aggregation/averaging algorithm to compute higher-order statistics, such as the sample variance, correlation, and scatter coefficients, measured over pairs of observations distributed across a network of nodes. The algorithm's main innovation is that when two nodes exchange their estimate values and average them, they also swap a secondary state variable representing their initial observation, effectively performing a random walk.

The paper makes a nice contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms, but it would benefit from a clearer connection to existing approaches and ideas. The authors should situate their work within the broader context of gossip approaches for computing statistics, referencing works beyond those cited in the manuscript. For example, Dimakis et al.'s Proc. IEEE paper provides a snapshot of the state of the art from a few years ago.

Several minor clarifications would improve the manuscript's readability. On page 4, the authors should specify the induced distribution on edges resulting from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process. Additionally, the phrase "drawing a random edge of the network" is ambiguous and should be rephrased.

A natural alternative approach to the problem is to allow more memory at each node and use the same contact process to flood each node's value across the network. By comparing this approach to the proposed method, the authors could demonstrate the benefits of averaging and the performance loss when memory is limited. This comparison would also provide a baseline to evaluate the rate 1/t.

The authors should also examine the work on lifting constructions for accelerating gossip algorithms, which appears to be related to their construction. Relevant references include Jung et al.'s "Fast gossip through lifted Markov chains" and Li and Dai's "Location-aided fast distributed consensus."

The analysis of gossip algorithms typically makes worst-case assumptions about the initial data distribution. The authors should clarify their assumptions on the data distribution and the guarantees on the error as a function of the initial data.

Minor corrections include: p1, rephrase the bipartite definition to avoid implying directed graphs; p4, change "let us stress out that" to "we stress that" and "awakes and contact" to "awakes and contacts"; p6, change "requires to initiate" to "requires initiating"; and p7, change "allows to create" to "allows us to create."

After the authors' feedback, the suggested clarifications and modifications are satisfactory. I have revised my initial score from 7 to 8. However, I still recommend that the authors examine lifting constructions, particularly LADA, and modify the related work section. Additionally, the experiments should include baseline comparisons.

Overall, this is a nicely presented approach to extending gossip methods for computing complex statistics in distributed settings. Assuming the authors address the comments raised in this review, I recommend accepting this paper.
