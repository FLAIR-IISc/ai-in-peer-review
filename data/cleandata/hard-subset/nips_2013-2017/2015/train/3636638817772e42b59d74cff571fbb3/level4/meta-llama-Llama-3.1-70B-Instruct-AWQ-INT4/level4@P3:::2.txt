This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics, which are essential to describe global properties of a statistical population, in decentralized settings. The authors provide a clear problem statement, and their approach is well-motivated by the need for decentralized computation and estimation in various applications, including sensor and peer-to-peer networks.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors do a good job of explaining their algorithms and analysis. The use of "phantom nodes" to model data propagation and estimate updates is a clever idea, and the convergence analysis is thorough. The authors also provide numerical experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithms, including comparisons with existing approaches.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. First, the authors could do a better job of situating their work in the context of existing gossip algorithms and decentralized computation methods. While they provide some references, a more comprehensive review of the literature would be helpful. Second, the authors could clarify the induced distribution on edges from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process. Finally, the authors could consider alternative approaches to the problem, such as allowing more memory at each node and using the same contact process to flood values across the network.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, and originality. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated problem statement, and their approach is novel and well-executed. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the authors provide sufficient background information and references for readers who may not be familiar with gossip algorithms or decentralized computation.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective approach to computing U-statistics in decentralized settings.
* The authors provide a thorough convergence analysis and numerical experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithms.
* The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow for readers.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive review of the literature on gossip algorithms and decentralized computation methods.
* The authors could clarify the induced distribution on edges from the "node wakes up, contacts neighbor" process.
* The authors could consider alternative approaches to the problem, such as allowing more memory at each node and using the same contact process to flood values across the network.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with revisions to address the areas mentioned above.
