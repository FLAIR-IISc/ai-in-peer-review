

The paper presents a well-motivated and clearly written study on randomized gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous variants, demonstrating significant performance gains over the U2-Gossip baseline across diverse graph topologies. Results are rigorously evaluated on two datasets using average relative AUC error, with the proposed method consistently outperforming the baseline in varied network scenarios. The inclusion of convergence bounds further strengthens the theoretical contribution.

However, a critical omission is the absence of runtime analysis. The authors should provide detailed per-iteration computational times for both their method and U2-Gossip. Without this, it remains unclear whether the performance improvements justify the potential computational overhead. If each iteration of the proposed approach is substantially slower than U2-Gossip—particularly given the scalability claims inherent in distributed gossip algorithms—the practical utility of the method would be significantly limited. This is especially important as the paper positions its algorithms as scalable solutions for large-scale distributed settings.

The theoretical analysis, including convergence guarantees, is sound and well-presented. The experimental design effectively validates the approach's robustness across different graph structures. Yet, the lack of runtime comparison represents a substantial gap in the evaluation. For a distributed systems paper targeting real-world deployment, computational efficiency is as crucial as accuracy. The authors should address this by including runtime measurements in their experiments, ideally comparing wall-clock times per iteration under identical hardware conditions.

The paper's core contribution—extending gossip protocols to U-statistics computation via both synchronous and asynchronous schemes—is novel and technically valuable. The convergence analysis provides a solid foundation, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. But the missing runtime data prevents a full assessment of the method's practical viability. Including this information would transform a promising theoretical contribution into a more compelling practical solution.

In summary, this is a strong paper with clear theoretical and empirical merits. The primary concern is the omission of runtime analysis, which is essential for evaluating the method's real-world applicability. Addressing this would significantly strengthen the submission. The authors should prioritize adding per-iteration timing results to their experimental section.
