This paper addresses the distributed estimation of degree-two U-statistics (e.g., AUC, sample variance) over a network using gossip algorithms. While gossip protocols for computing simple averages of separable functions are well-studied (e.g., Boyd et al., NIPS 2004), their extension to non-separable U-statistics is less explored. The authors propose GoSta, a novel randomized gossip algorithm in both synchronous and asynchronous variants. The key idea is to combine a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary data observations in a random-walk fashion, with an averaging step, where nodes also average their local U-statistic estimates. This work builds upon and directly improves the U2-gossip algorithm of Pelckmans et al. (2010). The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, using a novel "phantom network" formulation, to establish convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous versions.

The paper’s primary strength is its elegant and effective solution to an important problem. The GoSta algorithm is technically sound, and the theoretical analysis establishing its convergence rate is a significant contribution. The analysis correctly identifies the dependency on the network’s spectral gap, linking performance to connectivity. The paper is very clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental evaluation is another major strength; it is thorough, using real-world datasets and relevant U-statistics (AUC, within-cluster scatter) to demonstrate that GoSta empirically outperforms the main prior work, U2-gossip, across various network topologies, corroborating the theoretical claims of improved memory, communication, and convergence performance.

A minor weakness lies in the practical assumptions of the asynchronous algorithm. To maintain an unbiased estimate of the iteration count, each node must know its degree and the total number of edges `|E|` in the network. This assumption of global network knowledge somewhat reduces the algorithm's applicability in fully decentralized settings and warrants more explicit discussion as a limitation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper tackles an important, practical problem with a novel algorithmic approach that significantly advances the state-of-the-art over the primary existing method.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, complete, and features rigorous theoretical analysis alongside strong empirical validation.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The asynchronous setting relies on a potentially strong assumption about knowledge of a global network property.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a substantial contribution to the field of decentralized computation. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and significance far outweigh the minor weakness. I strongly recommend acceptance.
