- Proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms (GoSta) for distributed estimation of U-statistics, filling a gap in handling pair-dependent statistics.  
- Introduces a "phantom nodes" reformulation to separate data propagation from averaging, enabling convergence analysis.  
- Proves O(1/t) convergence for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t / t) for the asynchronous algorithm with explicit data- and network-dependent terms (spectral gap).  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful convergence proofs supported by the phantom nodes technique.  
- Convergence bounds explicitly incorporate network spectral gap and data-dependent terms, offering meaningful complexity insights.  
- Experiments on real datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC, Wine Quality for within-cluster scatter) show GoSta outperforms U2-gossip in convergence speed and variance reduction.  
- Paper clearly acknowledges limitations and compares memory/communication costs to U2-gossip.  
- Writing and organization are strong; pseudocode and figures effectively communicate algorithms and results.  
- Phantom nodes construction and some proof details need better intuitive motivation for non-expert readers.  
- Supplementary material contains necessary technical proofs, but main text could be more self-contained.  
- Work is original: extends gossip algorithms from separable statistics to U-statistics and introduces a novel analytical technique.  
- Significance is high: enables efficient distributed computation of fundamental statistics (e.g., AUC), with practical benefits across network topologies.  
- Strength: addresses an important distributed-statistics gap with rigorous theory and practical validation.  
- Strength: provides explicit rates and network/data-dependent analysis.  
- Strength: demonstrates consistent empirical improvements on multiple graph types.  
- Strength: clear comparison and better memory profile than prior U2-gossip.  
- Strength: phantom nodes technique is an elegant analytical contribution.  
- Weakness: phantom nodes concept may be hard to grasp without stronger intuition.  
- Weakness: reliance on supplementary material for full theoretical understanding.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of dynamic/changing network topologies.  
- Weakness: some technical proof steps lack intuitive explanation in the main text.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solves an important problem with novel algorithms, strong theory, solid experiments, and high practical relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: phantom nodes may be inaccessible to some readers, more topology trade-off analysis is needed, and proofs require supplementary reading.  
- Recommendation: accept — the paper is a strong, significant contribution suitable for NIPS.
