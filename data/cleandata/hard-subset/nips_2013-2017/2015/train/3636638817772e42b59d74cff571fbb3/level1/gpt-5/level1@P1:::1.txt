Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. While classic gossip achieves fast consensus for separable sums ∑i f(Xi), U-statistics Ûn(H) = (1/n2)∑i,j H(Xi,Xj) require averaging over pairs and are not directly handled by standard protocols. The authors propose two algorithms:

- GoSta-sync (synchronous): each node k maintains an auxiliary observation Yk performing a random walk induced by swapping along a randomly selected edge, and a local estimate Zk updated as a running average of H(Xk, Yk). In addition, when an edge (i,j) is selected, Zi and Zj are averaged. 
- GoSta-async (asynchronous): similar, but only the selected nodes update their Z with a local running average that uses a node-specific counter mk scaled by inverse selection probability pk = 2dk/|E| to debias the step size.

Key analysis idea is a “phantom nodes” construction that augments the state space to decouple averaging and data propagation, yielding explicit expected convergence rates. For synchronous updates, E[Zk(t)] → Ûn(H) with an O(1/t) bound featuring data-dependent dispersion terms and network dependence through the spectral gap of G (via λ2 of W2(G)). For asynchronous updates, E[Zk(t)] → Ûn(H) with an O(log t/t) rate. Compared to U2-gossip [17],which swaps two auxiliaries and lacks averaging, the proposed methods reduce memory/communication and empirically (and analytically) converge faster. Experiments on estimating AUC and within-cluster scatter over complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs support the claims.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-positioned within the gossip literature (e.g., Boyd et al. [2], Kempe et al. [10], Dimakis et al. [5,6]) and distributed optimization (Duchi et al. [7], Nedic and Ozdaglar [15]). It builds on U-statistics (Lee [11]) and prior NIPS/ML uses (e.g., Clémençon [4]). The closest prior is U2-gossip [17], for which the authors give sharper analysis and provide a clear algorithmic contrast (single auxiliary plus estimate averaging vs two auxiliaries and no averaging).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The synchronous analysis via the block upper-triangular transition with Wα(G) and the phantom graph is elegant and convincing, leading to explicit rates. The dependency on spectral gap matches consensus literature intuition. The asynchronous result is weaker (log t/t), but reasonable. Proofs are deferred; statements are precise.
- Claims support: Empirical comparisons to U2-gossip on two tasks and three graph families are consistent with theory. Communication/memory advantages are clear.
- Limitations: Results are in expectation; no mean-square, almost sure, or high-probability bounds are provided. The async bound hides constants c′(G) and lacks the informative data-dependent terms. The algorithms assume uniform random edge selection; implementation details for achieving this in a decentralized manner are not fully discussed. Async requires pk = 2dk/|E| (hence knowledge of |E|), which can be impractical.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. Algorithms are explicit. The phantom-node construction is carefully explained. A minor concern is that some practical assumptions (global edge sampling; knowledge of |E|) are not highlighted in the main text. A brief discussion on how to implement pair selection locally (e.g., independent Poisson edge clocks or node-initiated Metropolis sampling) would improve clarity and practicality.

Originality
Extending gossip to non-separable pairwise functionals with rigorous rates and network/data dependence is novel relative to the literature and to [17]. The analytical device (phantom network) is original and insightful. The asynchronous debiasing via local counters is a neat trick, though variants of event-based step-size control are known in stochastic approximation.

Significance
U-statistics (AUC, scatter, Gini) are widely used; having a communication- and memory-light decentralized estimator is of practical value in sensor/peer-to-peer networks and federated settings. The explicit spectral-gap dependence informs topology-aware design. The approach could serve as a building block for decentralized optimization of U-statistic-based objectives.

Strengths
- Simple algorithms with low per-iteration cost (swap one auxiliary vector; average two scalars).
- Clear convergence analysis with explicit network and data terms; O(1/t) (sync) improves upon [17] practically and theoretically.
- Thorough and clean comparison to U2-gossip, including a refined rate for the latter.
- Experiments on real datasets and multiple topologies match theory; variance reduction due to averaging is evident.
- Analysis technique (phantom nodes) is elegant and potentially useful beyond this problem.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality of edge sampling and pk: Uniform random edge selection and knowledge of |E| may be unrealistic. Suggest discussing implementations using:
  - Node-initiated neighbor selection with doubly-stochastic P (e.g., Metropolis weights) that depend only on local degrees.
  - Edge-based Poisson clocks, or lazy Metropolis to avoid bipartite issues without global info.
  - Step sizes in async that avoid |E|, e.g., using 1/mk without 1/pk and adjusting the analysis, or online estimation of pk.
- Only expectation bounds: Consider adding mean-square/high-probability bounds via martingale techniques to quantify across-node dispersion and provide stopping criteria.
- Graph model: Results assume static, undirected, connected, non-bipartite graphs. Extensions to time-varying/directed graphs (push-sum) would broaden impact; even a brief discussion would help.
- Limited baselines: Beyond U2-gossip, comparisons to naive schemes (e.g., multiple random-walk tokens to approximate pairwise sums, or mini-batch pair exchanges) would contextualize the gains in wall-clock and communication to accuracy.
- Scope of H: Assumes symmetric H with H(x,x)=0; state how to handle general degree-2 U-statistics (typical normalization n/(n−1)) or non-zero diagonals.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and understudied primitive (distributed U-statistics) with clean, implementable algorithms.
- Solid theory with interpretable constants and improved rates/complexity over prior art.
- Empirical validation on meaningful tasks and topologies; clear gains over [17].
- Novel analysis technique with potential reuse.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical deployment assumptions (uniform edge sampling; knowledge of |E| in async) not fully addressed.
- Asynchronous bound is somewhat loose and lacks data-dependent terms; no high-probability guarantees.
- Experimental section, while positive, could be broader in baselines and include communication-to-accuracy trade-offs and robustness.

Minor comments
- Clarify how to implement uniform edge selection in a decentralized way; otherwise specify that selection can be non-uniform with appropriate weights P (replace Wα with P-symmetrized matrices).
- Discuss handling of bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy updates) explicitly.
- Include per-iteration message sizes and memory cost summary.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically relevant contribution that extends gossip to a class of non-separable statistics with provable guarantees and good empirical behavior. With minor clarifications on implementability and some discussion of bounds beyond expectation, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
