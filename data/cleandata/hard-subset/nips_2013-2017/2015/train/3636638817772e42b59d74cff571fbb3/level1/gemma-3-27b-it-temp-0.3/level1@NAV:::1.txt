## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the important problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics in networked systems, a task less explored than distributed mean estimation despite its relevance to various applications like variance calculation, AUC, and Gini mean difference. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for this purpose, providing convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively, dependent on both data and network characteristics. 

The paper builds upon the existing body of work on gossip algorithms (Boyd et al., Kempe et al., Tsitsiklis) which primarily focus on separable functions. It correctly identifies the limitation of these algorithms for U-statistics and attempts to bridge this gap. The proposed approach, leveraging a “phantom node” reformulation, is a clever way to analyze and design the algorithms.  The comparison to the previous work by Pelckmans & Suykens (U2-gossip) is well-motivated, and the authors highlight the improvements in convergence rate, memory usage, and the ability to operate asynchronously. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is a valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence rate analysis, with explicit dependence on data and network properties (spectral gap), is a significant strength.
* **Asynchronous Algorithm:**  Developing an asynchronous version addresses a practical limitation of many distributed algorithms.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms over U2-gossip.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Phantom Node Complexity:** While insightful for analysis, the “phantom node” concept adds complexity and might not be immediately intuitive to readers unfamiliar with the technique. A more detailed explanation or visual aid could be helpful.
* **Limited Baselines:** The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on comparison with U2-gossip.  Comparing against simpler, potentially less efficient, but more widely known distributed estimation methods (e.g., basic averaging with pairwise exchange) could provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Network Topology Sensitivity:** The performance is heavily tied to the network’s spectral gap.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more in-depth discussion of how to mitigate the impact of poor connectivity would be beneficial.
* **Constant Factors:** The convergence rate bounds include constant factors dependent on the data and network.  A discussion of the tightness of these bounds and their practical implications would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
* Provides strong theoretical guarantees with insightful dependence on problem and network parameters.
* Demonstrates practical improvements over existing approaches through empirical validation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The “phantom node” technique adds complexity that might hinder understanding.
* Limited comparison to alternative baseline methods.




**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The novelty of the approach, the rigorous theoretical analysis, and the positive empirical results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the clarity of the phantom node concept and the inclusion of more comprehensive baseline comparisons.
