Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics in undirected networks via gossip protocols. While classic gossip algorithms efficiently compute separable functions such as sums/means, they do not directly handle pairwise functionals like U-statistics (variance, AUC, Gini mean difference, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter). The paper proposes two algorithms:
- GoSta-sync: a synchronous protocol where at each global iteration all nodes do a running average update using H(Xk, Yk), and one randomly drawn edge averages the two endpoints’ estimates and swaps their auxiliary observations Y. The auxiliary observations thus perform coupled random walks.
- GoSta-async: an asynchronous protocol using local Poisson clocks (cast equivalently as uniform random edge draws). Only the selected pair updates; each node keeps a counter m_k scaled by pk = 2d_k/|E| to ensure unbiased running averages without a global iteration counter.

Theoretical results show E[Z_k(t)] → Û_n(H), with explicit rates: O(1/t) for synchronous and O((log t)/t) for asynchronous. The rates depend on (i) problem difficulty via data-dependent dispersion terms (‖h − Û1‖, ‖H − h1^T‖) and (ii) network connectivity via the spectral gap c(G) = β_{n−1}/|E| = 1 − λ_2(W_2). The analysis uses an original “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from estimate averaging.

The work is closely related to randomized gossip for averages (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006; Kempe et al. 2003) and to the only prior attempt at decentralized U-statistics, U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens, IFAC 2009). Compared to U2-gossip, which requires two observations per node and two simultaneous swaps, the proposed methods use a single auxiliary observation and one edge swap per iteration, yielding lower communication and memory costs and (per the provided bound) a better dependence on n. Empirical comparisons on AUC and within-cluster scatter corroborate this advantage across several graph topologies.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The martingale/linear-system style analysis with the phantom network is thoughtful and appears correct at a high level. The dependence on the spectral gap is standard and interpretable. The synchronous O(1/t) and asynchronous O((log t)/t) rates are plausible given the compounded averaging + random-walk propagation dynamics. The comparison bound for U2-gossip is useful.
- Assumptions and completeness: The analysis assumes undirected, connected, non-bipartite graphs and uniform random edge selection. The bounds use norms on H and H − h1^T; it would help to explicitly state boundedness assumptions on H (or moment assumptions) and clarify which norm on matrices is used (Frobenius vs operator). Asynchronous correctness relies on each node knowing pk and hence |E|; feasibility of disseminating |E| should be discussed.
- Experiments: Experiments on two real datasets and three graph types are clear and align with theory (better on better-connected graphs; GoSta outperforming U2-gossip; similar performance for sync/async). However, the experimental scope is somewhat limited: no stress tests for very sparse/large-diameter graphs, no ablations for multiple observations per node (though mentioned as an extension), and limited exploration of high-dimensional communication costs.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithmic descriptions are crisp; the phantom network idea is clearly explained. A few clarity suggestions:
- Explicitly define the matrix norm used for H-related terms and state boundedness/moment conditions on H(Xi, Xj).
- In asynchronous Algorithm 2, make explicit the required knowledge of |E| and discuss how pk can be estimated or learned.
- Clarify the mechanism by which “uniform random edge” draws are realized in a decentralized manner (e.g., via independent node clocks leading to edge activation distribution).
- Provide a concise complexity table comparing communication/memory/compute per iteration vs U2-gossip.

Originality
Extending gossip to U-statistics with a single auxiliary observation and combining averaging with observation random walks is novel relative to U2-gossip. The phantom nodes analysis technique is original and insightful. While spectral-gap-based rates are not new per se, their integration here with data-dependent dispersion terms for U-statistics is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
U-statistics underpin many widely used measures (AUC, variance, correlation, scatter). Enabling their decentralized estimation with low per-iteration overhead is valuable for sensor, peer-to-peer, and federated-like settings where pairwise interactions are needed. The theoretical rates and explicit graph/data dependence give practitioners guidance. The significance is moderate-to-high: the problem is important and underexplored; the proposed approach clearly improves over the known baseline.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant, underexplored problem (decentralized U-statistics).
- Simple, implementable algorithms with low memory/communication overhead.
- Clear theoretical guarantees with interpretable network/data dependence.
- Insightful phantom-node analysis that may be reusable elsewhere.
- Empirical results on real data across multiple network types; consistent advantage over prior work.
- Both synchronous and asynchronous variants treated.

Cons
- Requires knowledge of |E| (asynchronous) and uniform random edge activation; practicality may be limited in some networks.
- Bounds assume implicit boundedness/finite moments for H; conditions should be stated.
- Experimental evaluation is relatively narrow (two tasks, moderate scales) and lacks comparisons to alternative communication schemes (e.g., push-sum/lazy updates, accelerated mixing).
- Discussion of privacy constraints is absent despite swapping raw observations; some applications cannot transmit raw data.
- No robustness analysis to time-varying or directed graphs; bipartite graphs require laziness to avoid periodicity.

Questions for authors
- Can pk be estimated online (e.g., via local degree and neighbor sampling) to avoid prior knowledge of |E|?
- Do the results extend to “lazy” edge updates to handle bipartite graphs without assuming non-bipartiteness?
- What are the precise assumptions on H (boundedness, sub-Gaussian tails) needed for the stated bounds?
- Can you report communication cost vs error tradeoffs in high-dimensional settings and with multiple observations per node?
- Is it possible to avoid exchanging raw observations by exchanging sketches sufficient for H (e.g., for specific H like inner products or distances)?

Overall assessment
Quality: good; Clarity: good; Originality: good; Significance: moderate-to-high. The paper meets NeurIPS reviewing criteria well, with solid theory, a simple algorithmic contribution, and empirical support. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions on H, discussing practical edge selection/|E| knowledge, and slightly expanding the discussion of limitations and applicability.
