

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, extending the well-studied gossip framework for single-observation statistics. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as existing gossip algorithms cannot efficiently compute quantities depending on pairs of observations (U-statistics), which are essential for important metrics like AUC, Gini mean difference, and within-cluster point scatter. Building on prior work by [2, 10, 15, 18, 19] on gossip algorithms for decentralized averaging, the authors introduce "GoSta" algorithms that combine data propagation with local averaging, using a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation for convergence analysis.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with rigorous convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for asynchronous, featuring data- and network-dependent terms that reflect problem hardness and connectivity. The proof technique using phantom nodes is elegant and well-executed. The empirical results on real datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality) convincingly demonstrate superiority over U2-gossip [17] in both convergence speed and variance reduction, with the performance gap widening as network size increases.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-organized exposition, precise algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2), and appropriate mathematical notation. The significance is clear: U-statistics are widely used in machine learning and data mining, and the proposed algorithms significantly reduce memory and communication costs while improving convergence rates.

Strengths: Novel problem extension, strong theoretical foundation, compelling empirical validation, clear writing. Weaknesses: The phantom node concept could benefit from additional intuitive explanation; the paper doesn't explore potential limitations for extremely large-scale networks with sparse connectivity.

The work is highly original compared to recent NIPS papers on gossip algorithms, making a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper meets all four criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), represents a novel extension to U-statistics (Originality), and addresses an important practical problem with significant potential impact (Significance).

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, clear experimental validation, significant practical implications. Con: Some technical concepts could be more accessible to non-specialists.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to distributed computation and deserves acceptance.
