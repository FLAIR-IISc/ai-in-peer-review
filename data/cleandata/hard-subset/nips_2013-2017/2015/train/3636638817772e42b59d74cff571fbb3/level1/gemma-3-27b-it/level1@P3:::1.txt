This paper introduces novel gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a problem significantly less explored than distributed mean estimation despite its importance in various applications. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, providing convergence rate bounds and empirical validation demonstrating superior performance compared to a prior work (U2-gossip). The paper is well-motivated, tackles a relevant problem, and presents a solid contribution to the field of distributed algorithms.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis, while detailed and relying on a clever "phantom node" reformulation, appears rigorous. The proofs are referenced to a supplementary material, which is standard. The convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous algorithms are reasonable and competitive. The authors carefully acknowledge the limitations and dependencies of the rates on data and network characteristics. The comparison with U2-gossip is fair and convincing, highlighting the benefits of the proposed approach in terms of both convergence speed and communication cost. The introduction of the phantom nodes is a neat trick that enables a clear analysis, although it adds complexity to the conceptual understanding. The experimental section provides strong evidence supporting the theoretical results. The authors explicitly address the differences in network connectivity and their effect on performance.  The step from the phantom graph analysis back to the original network behavior seems well justified.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent and clearly defined, although the introduction of the phantom nodes adds a layer of complexity requiring careful reading. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are concise and understandable. The motivation for the work is clearly stated, and the related work is adequately discussed.  However, the level of mathematical detail in the main body is high, and readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory might find some sections challenging.  A more intuitive explanation of the role of the spectral gap would be beneficial. The link between the convergence rates and practical implications could be further emphasized.

**Originality:** The problem of distributed U-statistic estimation via gossip algorithms is relatively unexplored, making this work inherently original. While gossip algorithms are a well-established area, extending them to handle the pairwise averaging required by U-statistics is a novel contribution. The "phantom node" technique is a creative approach to tackle this problem, and the comparison to U2-gossip highlights the improvements achieved.  The paper builds on existing work in gossip algorithms and spectral graph theory, but it combines these concepts in a new and meaningful way. The asynchronous algorithm adds to the originality.  The authors do a good job of citing relevant literature, including recent NIPS publications.

**Significance:** The results are significant. Distributed U-statistic estimation has applications in diverse fields like machine learning (AUC, variance), data mining (Gini mean difference), and statistical inference.  The proposed algorithms offer a practical solution to a challenging problem, with demonstrated advantages over existing methods. The convergence rate analysis provides valuable insights into the performance of the algorithms and their dependence on network properties. The practical experiments using real datasets further validate the significance of the work. This paper addresses a challenging problem, advancing the state of the art with both theoretical results and empirical evidence.  The potential for application in large-scale data analysis is considerable. The work provides a solid foundation for future research in decentralized statistical computation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem.
*   Technically sound and rigorous analysis.
*   Strong empirical validation.
*   Clear improvement over existing methods (U2-gossip).
*   Well-written and organized presentation.
*   Potential for significant impact in various application areas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The "phantom node" technique adds complexity and might be difficult for some readers to grasp without careful study of the supplementary material.
*   The mathematical detail may deter readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory.
*   The theoretical convergence rates, while strong, are stated with somewhat abstract dependencies on data and network characteristics; a discussion of how these parameters manifest in practical scenarios would be useful.



**Overall Assessment:** I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed algorithms, tackling a relevant problem with a novel and well-justified approach. The technical quality is high, and the empirical results are convincing. While some sections are mathematically demanding, the paper is generally clear and well-organized. The potential impact of this work is substantial, making it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.  A minor revision request regarding expanded discussion of the network and data dependencies in the convergence rates would further strengthen the paper.
