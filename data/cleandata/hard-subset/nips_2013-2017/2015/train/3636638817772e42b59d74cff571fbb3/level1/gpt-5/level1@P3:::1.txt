Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. Unlike standard gossip, which computes separable sums/means, U-statistics aggregate over all pairs and are not directly amenable to classic averaging protocols. The authors propose two algorithms—GoSta-sync and GoSta-async—that interleave (i) propagation of “auxiliary” observations via pairwise swaps (random walks on the graph), and (ii) averaging of local estimates. They introduce an analysis tool based on “phantom nodes” (a lifted product graph) to decouple data propagation from averaging. They prove that in expectation, the local estimates converge to the desired U-statistic at rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants depending on data dispersion and the network spectral gap. They further derive a refined bound for the previous U2-gossip method and argue its convergence is slower (due to a √n factor). Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over synthetic network topologies corroborate improved performance.

This builds on randomized gossip for averaging (e.g., Boyd et al.; Dimakis et al.), and relates to prior work on U-statistics gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens). The phantom network viewpoint is connected to lifted Markov chains and Kronecker-product constructions familiar in spectral graph theory.

Quality (soundness, support)
- Analysis: The synchronous analysis is neat and technically sound. The decomposition via Wα(G) and the use of spectral gap c(G)=1−λ2(W2) are standard and appropriate; the dependence c(G)=βn−1/|E| is correctly identified. The appearance of data-dependent terms ∥h−Û1∥ and ∥H−h1⊤∥ is informative. The expectation-only guarantees are clearly stated.
- Asynchronous: The model reduces independent Poisson clocks to uniform random edge selection. The proposed correction mk with increments 1/pk ensures E[mk(t)]=t, leading to unbiased “effective” steps; the O(log t/t) rate is plausible but weaker. The bound hides constants c′(G) and omits data-dependent terms; it would be valuable to make c′ explicit and discuss whether the log factor is an artifact of proof techniques.
- Comparison to U2-gossip: The refined bound including √n and (1−λ2(W1)2)−1 terms adds insight. The qualitative conclusion (GoSta faster in regimes of interest) is reasonable.
- Missing aspects: No high-probability or mean-square error bounds; only expectation. Variance across nodes is shown empirically, but not analyzed. The assumption of non-bipartite graphs could be relaxed via laziness/Metropolis weights; discussing this would strengthen robustness. The asynchronous algorithm requires knowledge of |E| to set pk—often unrealistic.

Clarity
The paper is well organized. The phantom-node construction is explained clearly and kept as an analysis device. Notation is dense but coherent. Suggestions:
- Clarify the AUC implementation: the denominator depends on class counts; explain how this separable quantity is computed in a decentralized way (e.g., a preliminary averaging phase).
- Explicitly state assumptions on H (boundedness) needed for Theorem 2’s bound.
- Discuss how to handle bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy random walks) and directed/asymmetric communication.
- For asynchronous, make the edge-selection model and required local knowledge precise; discuss how to avoid knowing |E| (e.g., push-sum to estimate normalization or using local activation counters).

Originality
Extending gossip to U-statistics with a single auxiliary token per node and combining it with local averaging is a nice, simple design that improves on U2-gossip in both rate and communication/memory. The phantom-node analysis for expectation dynamics is elegant, if conceptually related to existing lifting ideas. To my knowledge, no prior work provides these specific algorithms and rates; originality is good.

Significance
Degree-2 U-statistics cover widely used measures (variance, AUC, Gini, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter). A practical, lightweight gossip procedure with provable rates is valuable. The dependence on the spectral gap aligns with standard insights in networked computation. The work could serve as a building block for decentralized optimization over pairwise losses. The current experiments are modest in scale but demonstrate clear improvements.

Empirical evaluation
The experiments across three topologies and two tasks are appropriate for illustration. Strengths: consistent improvement over U2-gossip; effect of network connectivity; scaling trends with n. Weaknesses: limited range of datasets and network sizes; lack of communication cost and wall-clock comparisons (esp. sync vs async); no robustness tests (noise, quantization, packet loss). Adding comparisons to baselines that separately estimate hi then average, or sampling-based approximations, would help.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, implementable algorithms with better memory/communication than prior art.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with clear dependence on data and topology.
- Insightful analysis technique; refined bound for U2-gossip.
- Empirical results support theory on relevant tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Expectation-only convergence; no concentration/high-probability guarantees.
- Asynchronous method requires global |E|; constants in the bound are opaque; rate has extra log factor.
- Non-bipartite assumption not addressed in practice; no discussion of directed or time-varying graphs.
- Experiments are limited in scale and breadth; lack of communication/time-normalized comparisons.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide mean-square or high-probability bounds? Is the log factor in async necessary?
- Can pk be implemented without knowing |E| (e.g., using local counters or push-sum normalization), and would the analysis extend?
- How would you adapt to bipartite graphs (lazy updates) or directed/time-varying networks?
- Clarify how AUC denominators are computed in a decentralized way.
- Any insight on extending to higher-order U-statistics (degree >2) with multiple auxiliary tokens?
- Provide explicit expression/estimate for c′(G) in Theorem 2; discuss its scaling with spectral gap.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution to decentralized estimation beyond separable objectives. The algorithms are simple and practically relevant; the theory is sound and informative; the empirical evidence is supportive. Addressing the noted limitations would further strengthen the work. I lean toward acceptance.
