

# Peer Review

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a significant gap in the literature. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for decentralized averaging of separable statistics (e.g., sample means), the authors recognize that U-statistics—quantities depending on pairs of observations (e.g., Area Under the Curve, empirical variance, Gini mean difference)—have not been efficiently handled in distributed settings. The authors introduce "GoSta" algorithms that simultaneously propagate data across the network and maintain local estimates of the U-statistic. Their key insight is a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation that separates data propagation from averaging steps, enabling convergence analysis. The paper establishes O(1/t) convergence for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, with explicit data and network-dependent terms. The work builds on previous gossip algorithms for decentralized averaging (e.g., Boyd et al., 2006; Dimakis et al., 2008) but significantly extends the framework to handle non-separable statistics, differing substantially from the only prior work on U-statistics (U2-gossip by Pelckmans and Suykens, 2010).

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful convergence proofs supported by the phantom nodes technique, which elegantly separates the data propagation and averaging components. The convergence bounds explicitly incorporate network-dependent terms (spectral gap) and data-dependent terms, providing meaningful insights into the problem's complexity. The experimental validation on real datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC and Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter) convincingly demonstrates the superiority of GoSta over U2-gossip in terms of convergence speed and variance reduction. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations of their approach, particularly the comparison to U2-gossip's memory and communication costs. The supplementary material provides necessary technical details for the proofs, though the main paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the phantom nodes concept.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to algorithms to analysis to experiments. The pseudocode for Algorithms 1 and 2 is precise and well-explained. The notation is consistent and well-defined. The figures (particularly Figures 2 and 3) effectively illustrate the experimental results, making the comparisons between GoSta and U2-gossip visually clear. However, the phantom nodes construction (Section 4.1) could be better motivated for readers without strong graph theory backgrounds. Some technical details in the convergence proofs might benefit from more intuitive explanations to aid comprehension.

## Originality

This work makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. The authors successfully extend gossip algorithms from separable statistics to U-statistics, a meaningful gap in distributed computation. The phantom nodes technique represents a novel analytical approach, and the GoSta algorithms themselves are original contributions. The paper thoroughly contextualizes its work against prior gossip algorithms (e.g., Boyd et al., 2006; Dimakis et al., 2008) and the only prior U-statistic approach (U2-gossip). The theoretical results are substantially different from previous NIPS papers on gossip algorithms, which have primarily focused on separable statistics. The combination of synchronous/asynchronous algorithms with explicit convergence rates for U-statistics represents a significant advance.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. U-statistics are fundamental in many statistical applications (AUC for classification, clustering quality measures), and efficient distributed computation of these quantities enables new applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale data analysis. The paper demonstrates clear practical advantages: faster convergence (O(1/t) vs. O(1/√n t) for U2-gossip), lower memory requirements, and better handling of network topologies. The experiments confirm these advantages on real-world datasets, showing consistent improvements across different network structures (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz). This work could enable new distributed machine learning applications and serves as a building block for more complex decentralized optimization tasks involving U-statistics.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Addresses a meaningful gap in distributed computation (U-statistics).
2. Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates.
3. Demonstrates practical advantages through well-designed experiments.
4. Clear comparison to the previous U2-gossip approach.
5. Novel phantom nodes technique for analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
1. Phantom nodes concept could be better motivated for general readers.
2. Supplementary material is required for full theoretical understanding.
3. Limited discussion of network dynamics (changing topologies).
4. Some technical details in convergence proofs could be more intuitively explained.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- This work solves an important problem in distributed statistics with clear theoretical and practical contributions.
- The results significantly advance the state of the art in gossip algorithms.
- The paper is technically sound, well-written, and thoroughly validated.
- The significance of the work is high for both theoretical and applied communities.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The phantom nodes technique may be challenging for some readers to grasp without more intuitive explanation.
- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between network topologies.
- The supplementary material is necessary for full understanding of the proofs.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed algorithms and statistics. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature with a novel technical approach, provides rigorous theoretical analysis, and demonstrates clear practical advantages through well-designed experiments. The work is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on gossip algorithms, which have focused on separable statistics. The paper meets all the criteria for high-quality NIPS papers and would be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning and distributed systems.

I recommend acceptance.
