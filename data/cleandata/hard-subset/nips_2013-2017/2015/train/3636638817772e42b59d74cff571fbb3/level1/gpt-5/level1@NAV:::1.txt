This paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over networks by introducing synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta). Each node maintains one auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via swaps, while nodes continuously average local estimates. A novel “phantom nodes” construction enables a clean analysis separating data propagation and averaging. The authors prove convergence in expectation with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants that expose data dispersion and the spectral gap of the graph. Compared to prior gossip work focused on separable averages (e.g., Kempe et al.; Boyd et al.; Dimakis et al.), this paper targets pairwise statistics (AUC, variance, Gini, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter), a setting previously considered only by U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens). Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across several network topologies show consistent improvements over U2-gossip with lower memory/communication.

Quality: The technical development is sound and carefully reasoned. The phantom-network reformulation is insightful, and the synchronous proof gives explicit spectral-gap dependence. The asynchronous scheme is practical and unbiased via local counters. However, the asynchronous bound hides constants (c′(G)) and lacks explicit spectral-gap dependence; only expectation bounds are provided (no high-probability or almost-sure guarantees). Assumptions such as knowledge of |E| and degrees could be relaxed or estimated. Robustness to delays, packet loss, quantization, and directed graphs is not studied.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. Algorithm pseudocode is clear, though the role and communication cost of auxiliary swaps could be quantified more explicitly. The phantom-node exposition could be shortened with an intuitive overview. Minor typos remain.

Originality: The combination of random-walk data propagation with averaging for U-statistics, the phantom-node analysis, and the asynchronous design are novel relative to U2-gossip and standard gossip/consensus literature.

Significance: U-statistics are widely used in ML (AUC, clustering scatter, rank statistics). Providing scalable decentralized estimators with graph-dependent rates should be useful to practitioners and as a building block for distributed learning and optimization.

Pros (for acceptance):
- Clear, principled algorithms with improved rates/complexity over U2-gossip
- Insightful analysis linking data hardness and spectral gap; practical asynchronous variant
- Empirical validation on real data and multiple graphs; lower variance across nodes

Cons (against acceptance):
- Only expectation bounds; no concentration/AS convergence
- Asynchronous rate and constants not explicit; reliance on |E| and degrees
- Limited experimental scale and stress tests (lossy links, quantization, directed/asymmetric graphs)
- Degree-2 only; discussion of extension to higher-order U-statistics is brief

Overall: A solid and timely contribution; I lean toward acceptance after addressing clarity and robustness points.
