This paper introduces new gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a gap in the literature where most gossip algorithms focus on simpler statistics like the mean. The core idea is to combine data propagation with local averaging to efficiently estimate these more complex functionals. The paper provides convergence rate bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, supported by numerical experiments demonstrating superior performance compared to a prior approach (U2-gossip).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis, though relying on a “phantom node” reformulation which adds complexity, appears rigorous (though a complete verification requires significant effort). The bounds are explicitly stated and depend on both data and network properties, which is a strength. The comparison to U2-gossip is well-motivated and the experimental results support the claimed improvements. The extension to asynchronous settings is a valuable addition. However, the use of the phantom graph feels somewhat contrived and makes the analysis less intuitive. It's not immediately clear *why* this reformulation is necessary, beyond enabling application of existing spectral graph theory tools. While the authors mention extending results to multiple observations per node, the description remains brief. The discussion of limitations is adequate, but could be more explicit about the scalability of the approach with respect to network size and dimensionality of the data. The authors appropriately acknowledge the convergence rate for the asynchronous setting (logarithmic) is slower than the synchronous case.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though dense. The notation is standard for the field, but a reader unfamiliar with gossip algorithms and spectral graph theory might find it challenging. Section 2 provides necessary background, but could benefit from more intuitive explanations alongside the formal definitions. Algorithm 1 and 2 are clearly presented. The motivation for the work is clearly stated in the introduction.  The connection between the theoretical convergence rates and the experimental results is well explained. The “phantom node” construction is explained, but remains the least clear aspect of the paper; a visual illustration beyond Figure 1 might be helpful.

**Originality:** The problem of distributed U-statistic estimation via gossip algorithms is relatively unexplored, making this work a novel contribution. While gossip algorithms themselves are a well-established area, extending them to this more general class of statistics represents a significant step. The use of the phantom node technique, while not entirely novel in distributed optimization, is applied in a new and insightful way to tackle this specific problem. The comparison to U2-gossip highlights the improvement and strengthens the claim of originality. The authors adequately reference related work, including foundational papers on gossip algorithms and U-statistics.  A search of NIPS proceedings from 2013-2017 doesn’t reveal directly competing work.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant. U-statistics arise in various machine learning applications (AUC, variance, etc.), and efficient distributed estimation is crucial for large-scale datasets.  The proposed algorithms offer faster convergence and lower communication/memory costs compared to existing methods, making them practically appealing. The dependence of the convergence rate on the network’s spectral gap offers insights into network design for faster estimation. The demonstrated performance on real-world datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) further strengthens the significance. The algorithms could be useful for practitioners dealing with decentralized data analysis and could serve as a foundation for future research in distributed optimization of more complex functions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and important problem: Extends gossip algorithms to a challenging class of statistics.
*   Sound technical analysis: Convergence rates are established with explicit bounds.
*   Empirical validation: Experiments demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   Well-written and organized: The paper presents a clear and logical argument.
*   Potential for practical impact: Algorithms could be used in various real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Phantom node technique: Adds complexity and reduces intuition.
*   Scalability concerns:  The scalability of the approach with increasing network size/data dimensionality is not fully addressed.
*   Limited discussion of multiple observations per node: The extension to this case is briefly mentioned but lacks detailed analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the phantom node technique adds some complexity, the core ideas are novel, the technical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are compelling. The paper addresses an important problem with a practical solution and represents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. Minor revisions addressing the scalability concerns and providing more detail on the multiple observation extension would further strengthen the paper.
