

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computation: extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations rather than individual observations. The authors propose "GoSta" (Gossip for U-statistics) algorithms for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, establishing convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. The key innovation is a novel analysis technique using "phantom nodes" to separate data propagation from averaging steps.

The paper builds on the well-established gossip algorithm framework for distributed averaging (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006, Dimakis et al. 2008), but extends it to handle U-statistics, which have not been addressed in prior gossip literature. The authors carefully distinguish their work from the only prior attempt (U2-gossip [17]), highlighting key improvements in communication complexity, memory requirements, and convergence rates.

**Quality** - The paper is technically sound with rigorous convergence proofs for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with explicit bounds that incorporate both network-dependent terms (via spectral gap) and data-dependent terms. The authors demonstrate theoretical advantages through careful comparison with U2-gossip, showing that GoSta achieves better convergence rates and lower communication costs. The experimental section provides strong empirical validation on two real-world applications (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) across different network topologies. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the asynchronous algorithm has a slightly worse theoretical rate but similar empirical performance), showing careful self-evaluation.

**Clarity** - The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good use of notation and well-defined algorithms. The phantom node concept, while initially counterintuitive, is carefully explained and justified as a theoretical tool. The experimental setup is well-described with appropriate datasets and network topologies. Some technical details in the convergence proofs (particularly the matrix analysis) might benefit from additional intuition, but the main results are accessible. The supplementary material appears to contain important technical details for the proofs.

**Originality** - This work makes a clear contribution to the gossip algorithm literature by addressing a previously unexplored problem (U-statistics). The phantom node analysis technique is novel and clever, allowing the authors to separate the complex dynamics of data propagation and averaging. The paper thoroughly reviews related work (gossip algorithms for averaging, U-statistics) and clearly articulates how this work differs from prior art, particularly U2-gossip [17]. A quick check of NIPS proceedings from 2013-2015 shows no direct overlap with this specific problem or approach.

**Significance** - U-statistics are fundamental to many machine learning and data analysis applications (AUC, variance, clustering metrics), making this work highly relevant to practitioners. The proposed algorithms provide a practical solution for decentralized computation of these statistics, with demonstrably better performance than the previous best approach. The work establishes a foundation for future research on decentralized U-statistic optimization, which could impact a wide range of applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and distributed machine learning.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a meaningful gap in distributed computation literature
- Novel theoretical approach (phantom nodes) for analysis
- Rigorous convergence analysis with explicit bounds
- Strong empirical validation on real-world applications
- Clear comparison with prior work (U2-gossip) showing improvements

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical analysis of the asynchronous case is more complex and might be challenging for some readers
- The phantom node concept, while elegant, requires some mental adjustment to understand as a theoretical tool
- The paper doesn't discuss potential extensions to higher-order U-statistics (beyond degree 2)

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses an important gap in distributed computation literature
- Novel theoretical approach with rigorous analysis
- Strong empirical validation on real-world applications
- Clear comparison with prior work showing significant improvements
- High relevance to practical distributed systems

*Con:*
- Theoretical analysis may be challenging for some readers
- Limited discussion of extensions to higher-order U-statistics

Overall, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. The work is technically sound, original, and of high significance to the field. It advances the state of the art in gossip algorithms by extending their applicability to a broader class of statistical quantities. The authors have clearly demonstrated the superiority of their approach through both theory and experiments.

The paper meets all four criteria at a high level. It's a clear and significant contribution that would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
