

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a significant gap in the literature. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for distributed averaging of separable statistics (e.g., sample means), the paper correctly identifies that U-statistics (which require pairwise averaging) have not been properly addressed in distributed settings. The authors introduce "GoSta" algorithms that maintain local estimates while propagating data across the network, with convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings. The work builds upon and improves upon the only previous approach for this problem (U2-gossip [17]), which suffers from higher memory and communication costs.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a theoretically rigorous analysis of convergence rates for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, with explicit dependencies on network properties (spectral gap) and data characteristics. The authors introduce the novel "phantom nodes" technique to reformulate the problem, enabling separate modeling of data propagation and averaging steps. This analytical approach is sound and well-executed, with detailed proofs provided in supplementary material. Second, the paper demonstrates clear empirical advantages over U2-gossip on two important applications: AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter, using real-world datasets (SMVguide3 and Wine Quality). The experimental results consistently show faster convergence and lower variance in the estimates.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with thorough background in Sections 2 and 3 that effectively contextualizes the work within existing gossip algorithm literature. The problem statement is precise, the notation is consistent, and the algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are clearly described. The authors carefully address the limitations of previous work, particularly U2-gossip's higher memory requirements (storing two auxiliary observations per node) and lack of asynchronicity.

The main technical strengths are the convergence rate analysis and the clear demonstration of practical advantages. The theoretical results provide meaningful insights about how network connectivity (via spectral gap) and data properties affect performance. The empirical results corroborate the theoretical claims, showing that the performance gap between GoSta and U2-gossip widens as network size increases.

The paper has few weaknesses. While the phantom nodes technique is novel and effective for analysis, the paper could provide a brief sketch of the key insights in the main text rather than deferring to supplementary material. The comparison to U2-gossip is thorough, but the paper could better quantify the communication and memory savings for high-dimensional data, which would strengthen the practical argument. The paper does not address handling disconnected networks, though this is standard for gossip algorithms and the authors appropriately assume connectivity.

In terms of novelty, the paper extends gossip algorithms to a new class of statistics (U-statistics) with a clean theoretical framework and practical algorithms. The work is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on gossip algorithms (e.g., [10, 2, 15, 11, 9]) which focus on separable statistics. The paper's contribution is a clear extension of the gossip algorithm framework to handle pairwise statistics, which is an important class of quantities in machine learning.

The significance of this work is strong. U-statistics are fundamental in many applications including AUC evaluation, variance estimation, and clustering quality assessment. The proposed algorithms provide a more efficient solution for distributed estimation of these quantities, with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over the only existing approach. The lower memory and communication requirements make the algorithms particularly suitable for resource-constrained distributed systems.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality: the technical analysis is sound, the results are well-supported by both theory and experiments, and the authors are careful to acknowledge limitations. The clarity is excellent, with a well-organized structure and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The novelty is substantial within the context of distributed algorithms for machine learning, and the significance is clear for practitioners working with distributed systems.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses an important gap in the literature with a technically sound and well-motivated approach. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are compelling, and the contribution is clearly differentiated from previous work. The algorithms have practical advantages that would benefit many real-world distributed machine learning applications.

Arguments against acceptance: The novelty is somewhat incremental (extending gossip to a new statistic class), but the paper provides substantial theoretical and practical improvements over the only existing work in this area. The paper's contribution is significant enough to be considered for NIPS.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in distributed algorithms for machine learning. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem with meaningful practical implications. The work is well-suited for NIPS.
