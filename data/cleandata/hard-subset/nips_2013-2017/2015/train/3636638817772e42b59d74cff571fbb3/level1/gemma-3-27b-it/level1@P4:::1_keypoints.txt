- Introduces new gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, extending beyond sample-mean-focused work.  
- Core idea: combine propagation of local observations with averaging of local estimates and analyze via a “phantom node” reformulation.  
- Presents synchronous and asynchronous variants with theoretical convergence rates O(1/t) and O(log t / t) respectively.  
- Empirically outperforms prior U2-gossip on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.  
- Technical treatment appears rigorous with supporting numerical experiments; detailed proofs in supplementary material.  
- Authors clarify phantom nodes are an analytical tool that do not affect implementation.  
- Comparison with U2-gossip highlights the benefit of the averaging step in the proposed methods.  
- Limited exploration of trade-offs between synchronous and asynchronous algorithms beyond stated theoretical rates.  
- Clear link between spectral gap and convergence speed, explained intuitively.  
- Experiments use reasonable datasets but are limited in number and parameter variations (e.g., Watts-Strogatz settings).  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized but dense in parts; notation is well-defined though a glossary would help.  
- Algorithms are clearly presented but lack a high-level intuitive explanation of why the steps work; phantom-node discussion is abstract and could use a concrete example.  
- Novelty: tackles a less-explored problem, introduces a novel phantom-node reformulation, and adapts gossip frameworks to U-statistics meaningfully.  
- Significance: U-statistics are broadly useful; algorithms offer improved convergence and link performance to network properties, though practical gains are modest in some cases.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important gap, offers novel theoretically-grounded algorithms, shows empirical improvements, and is well-written with strong analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: phantom-node abstraction may confuse readers, insufficient exploration of sync/async trade-offs, experiments could be broader, and empirical gains are not always large.  
- Overall recommendation: solid, valuable contribution with recommendation for acceptance.
