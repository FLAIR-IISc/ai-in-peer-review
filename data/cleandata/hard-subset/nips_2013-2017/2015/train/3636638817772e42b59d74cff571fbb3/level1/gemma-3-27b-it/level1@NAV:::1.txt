## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses the problem of decentralized estimation of U-statistics in networked systems, a less explored area compared to distributed mean estimation. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, providing convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively, explicitly accounting for data and network characteristics. The work builds upon existing gossip algorithms [19, 18, 5] which primarily focus on estimating separable functions, and identifies the challenge of efficiently averaging over pairs of observations, as required by U-statistics [12].  The authors correctly highlight the importance of U-statistics in various applications like AUC, empirical variance, and Gini mean difference.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical contributions and practical validation. The convergence rate analysis, incorporating data and network dependencies via spectral gap considerations [3], is a significant advancement. The “phantom nodes” approach is a clever technique to reformulate the problem and facilitate the analysis.  Empirical results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real datasets demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms over the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17], showing faster convergence and reduced memory/communication costs. The inclusion of comparisons on different network topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts-Strogatz) is valuable. 

However, the paper could benefit from further clarity and detail in certain areas. While the theoretical analysis appears solid, the connection between the phantom node model and the actual algorithm implementation could be made more explicit. The discussion of the asynchronous algorithm’s convergence rate (O(log t/t)) feels somewhat brief compared to the synchronous case and could benefit from more elaboration on the impact of the local clock assumptions.  Furthermore, while comparisons to U2-gossip are present, a more comprehensive comparison against other relevant distributed estimation techniques, even if they don't directly address U-statistics, could strengthen the contribution.  The notation, especially in Section 2, is dense and could be made more accessible.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Extends gossip algorithms to a challenging problem (U-statistic estimation).
*   Theoretical Soundness: Provides rigorous convergence analysis with informative bounds.
*   Empirical Validation: Demonstrates improved performance compared to existing work.
*   Significance: Addresses a practically relevant problem in distributed systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity:  Some sections (e.g., phantom node explanation, asynchronous analysis) lack sufficient detail.
*   Completeness: A more exhaustive comparison with other distributed estimation algorithms would be beneficial.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of distributed estimation. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and completeness.
