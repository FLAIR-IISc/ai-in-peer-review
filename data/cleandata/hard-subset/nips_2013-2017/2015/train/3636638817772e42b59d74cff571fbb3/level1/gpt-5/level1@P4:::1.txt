Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over networks, where each node holds a single observation. Unlike separable aggregates (e.g., means) that standard gossip efficiently computes, U-statistics involve pairwise terms and require propagating data across the network. The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (fully asynchronous). Each node maintains an auxiliary sample that performs a random walk via swaps along randomly selected edges while nodes update local estimates using running averages; in parallel, nodes average their estimates when they are selected. The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” augmented graph to decouple the averaging and data-propagation dynamics, yielding convergence in expectation with rates O(1/t) for GoSta-sync and O(log t/t) for GoSta-async. Bounds have explicit dependence on (i) data dispersion terms (norms of H and H−h1ᵀ, where H is the matrix of pairwise values and h are per-row averages) and (ii) the graph spectral gap via the second eigenvalue of a standard lazy random-walk-like operator Wα(G). The method reduces memory/communication per iteration relative to the prior U2-gossip approach [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2009], and the analysis suggests faster convergence in practice. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter across different graph topologies (complete, grid, Watts–Strogatz) support the claims.

This work connects to classical randomized gossip averaging [Boyd et al., Kempe et al.], geographic gossip [Dimakis et al.], and distributed optimization [Nedic & Ozdaglar; Duchi et al.], and is closely related to the only prior decentralized U-statistic algorithm (U2-gossip). Work on U-processes at NIPS (e.g., Clémençon) provides broader context for the importance of U-statistics.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation for degree-2 U-statistics with relevant ML examples (AUC, variance-like scatter).
- Simple algorithms with low per-iteration communication: swap one auxiliary observation and average a scalar; memory footprint is small (one auxiliary observation vs two for U2-gossip).
- Solid analysis framework using augmented “phantom” graphs to separate averaging and data propagation; yields interpretable rates with spectral-gap dependence consistent with gossip literature.
- Asynchronous variant supports realistic local-clock models and unbiased updates via simple per-node counters; requires only degrees and total edge count.
- Empirical results across multiple network types demonstrate consistent improvements over U2-gossip; variance across nodes is reduced due to averaging.
- Discussion compares theoretically and empirically with prior work, including an improved bound for U2-gossip.

Weaknesses:
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation; no high-probability or almost-sure bounds, and no finite-sample concentration results. Variance control and tail behavior are unaddressed.
- The asynchronous rate is O(log t/t) with an unspecified constant c′(G); the lack of an explicit expression obscures sensitivity to graph structure.
- Experimental evaluation reports error versus iterations; communication- and time-normalized comparisons (e.g., messages, bytes, latency) and energy cost are not systematically analyzed, which is important for distributed settings. U2-gossip uses two edge activations per iteration; fair comparisons should normalize for communication.
- Experiments are on moderate-sized datasets/networks; scalability to much larger n and higher-dimensional observations (where communication payload is large) is not demonstrated.
- The method assumes undirected, connected, non-bipartite graphs, and knowledge of |E| for asynchronous updates; robustness to directed, time-varying, or partially known topologies is not studied.
- The analysis relies on bounded norms of H; assumptions on H (e.g., boundedness, variance) and how they affect constants are not fully spelled out in the main text.

Quality:
- The technical development is sound and consistent with established tools in spectral graph theory and stochastic averaging. The block matrix formulation and decoupling via phantom nodes are convincing. However, proofs are deferred to supplementary material, and some constants (especially for the asynchronous case) are opaque. The comparison bound for U2-gossip is helpful and fair.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. Algorithms are clearly specified; notation is heavy but standard. The phantom network construction is explained with intuition. Minor improvements: explicitly define norms used for matrices/vectors and restate assumptions (bounded H, symmetry, zero diagonal) where invoked in bounds; quantify per-iteration communication and memory costs; clarify practical requirements (knowledge of |E|) and discuss alternatives when |E| is unknown.

Originality:
- The combination of gossip averaging with random-walk-based data propagation for U-statistics, together with the phantom-node analytical device, appears novel relative to U2-gossip. Providing both synchronous and asynchronous variants and comparative rates is a meaningful advance.

Significance:
- Many ML/statistics tasks rely on pairwise functionals; enabling decentralized estimation with provable rates is useful. While the rates are slower than exponential consensus for separable means (likely inherent to data propagation), the approach is simple, practical, and potentially extensible to higher-order U-statistics and decentralized optimization with U-statistic objectives.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a meaningful gap (U-statistics) in the gossip literature with simple, implementable algorithms.
- Provides clear theoretical rates with interpretable graph/data dependence; improves on prior U2-gossip both analytically and empirically.
- Includes an asynchronous version suited to realistic systems.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of concentration/high-probability guarantees and opaque constants limit theoretical sharpness.
- Evaluation does not normalize for communication cost or show large-scale scalability; directed/time-varying graphs not considered.
- Assumes knowledge of |E| in asynchronous updates; robustness to partial knowledge is not addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add communication/time-normalized comparisons (messages, bytes) and larger-scale experiments.
- Provide high-probability bounds or variance analyses; make constants explicit where possible.
- Discuss extensions to directed or time-varying graphs (e.g., push-sum), unknown |E|, and higher-order U-statistics.
