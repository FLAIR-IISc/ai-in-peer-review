- Proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms ("GoSta") for distributed estimation of U-statistics, filling a gap in the literature.  
- Identifies that existing gossip work focuses on separable statistics and that U-statistics (pairwise) were not properly handled.  
- Derives convergence rates: O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t / t) for asynchronous settings.  
- Introduces the "phantom nodes" technique to decouple data propagation and averaging for analysis.  
- Provides theoretically rigorous convergence analysis with explicit dependence on network spectral gap and data properties.  
- Offers detailed proofs in the supplementary material supporting the theoretical claims.  
- Demonstrates empirical advantages over U2-gossip on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter using real datasets (SMVguide3, Wine Quality).  
- Shows faster convergence and lower variance than U2-gossip; gap widens with increasing network size.  
- Highlights practical improvements: lower memory and communication requirements compared to U2-gossip (which stores two auxiliary observations per node).  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with precise problem statement and consistent notation; algorithms are clearly described.  
- Main technical strengths are the convergence analysis and linking performance to network connectivity and data characteristics.  
- Weakness: key insights of the phantom nodes technique are deferred to the supplement and should be sketched in the main text.  
- Weakness: communication and memory savings for high-dimensional data are not fully quantified.  
- Assumes network connectivity and does not address disconnected networks (standard but unhandled).  
- Novelty: meaningful extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics, distinct from prior work on separable statistics.  
- Significance: strong—U-statistics are widely used (AUC, variance, clustering metrics), and the methods suit resource-constrained distributed systems.  
- Recommendation: overall strong contribution with compelling theory and experiments; arguments for acceptance outweigh minor weaknesses, though novelty could be viewed as incremental.
