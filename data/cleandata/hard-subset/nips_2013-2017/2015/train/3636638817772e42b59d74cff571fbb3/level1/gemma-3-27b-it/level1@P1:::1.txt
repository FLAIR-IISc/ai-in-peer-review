This paper introduces novel randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in distributed networks. U-statistics, requiring pairwise averaging, are more complex to estimate in a decentralized manner than simple means. The authors establish convergence rate bounds of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, respectively, and demonstrate improved performance compared to a previous approach [17] through both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear gap in the literature regarding decentralized estimation of U-statistics. Gossip algorithms are a natural fit for distributed computation, and extending them to handle U-statistics is a significant contribution. The problem statement is clearly defined, and the connections to applications like AUC and within-cluster point scatter are helpful for illustrating the relevance of the work. The introduction provides a good overview of related work, particularly highlighting the limitations of existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed algorithms represent a significant extension of gossip algorithms to a more challenging class of functions (U-statistics).
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence rate bounds provide strong theoretical justification for the algorithms’ performance. The inclusion of data and network-dependent terms is a plus, offering insight into the factors influencing convergence speed.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on real datasets demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms over the existing approach [17]. The comparison across different network topologies is also valuable.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The use of “phantom nodes” to facilitate the analysis is a clever technique, and the explanation is relatively clear.
*   **Asynchronous Algorithm:** Providing both synchronous and asynchronous versions enhances the practical applicability of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Proof:** While the theoretical analysis is a strength, the complexity of the proof (particularly the use of phantom nodes) might make it difficult for readers to fully grasp the underlying mechanics.  More intuitive explanations or a simplified outline of the key proof steps would be beneficial.
*   **Network Assumptions:** The analysis assumes a connected and non-bipartite graph. While common, this assumption limits the applicability of the algorithms to certain network structures.  Discussing the potential performance degradation or modifications needed for bipartite graphs would be valuable.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to [17] is present, a broader comparison to other distributed estimation techniques, even those not specifically designed for U-statistics, could strengthen the paper. Specifically, comparing against distributed methods utilizing parameter servers, even if less decentralized, would establish better relative performance benchmarks.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experimental section doesn’t thoroughly explore the sensitivity of performance to parameters like network size and topology. A more detailed analysis of how the algorithms scale with network size would be useful.
*   **Implementation Details:** More details about the implementation (e.g., programming language, libraries used) would enhance reproducibility.



**Originality:**

The work is highly original. Extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is a novel contribution. The approach of using “phantom nodes” and the resulting analysis are also unique. The authors adequately reference related work, including the key paper [17] that their work builds upon.

**Significance:**

The results are significant. U-statistics are fundamental in various statistical applications, and the ability to estimate them efficiently in a decentralized manner has broad implications. The algorithms could be useful in areas such as distributed machine learning, sensor networks, and data mining. The theoretical analysis and empirical validation demonstrate the practical potential of the proposed techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in distributed computation.
*   The proposed algorithms are novel and offer significant improvements over existing approaches.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the algorithms’ convergence rate.
*   The empirical results validate the effectiveness of the algorithms on real datasets.
*   The clear writing and organization make the paper accessible to a wide audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the theoretical analysis might limit the paper’s accessibility.
*   The network assumptions may restrict the applicability of the algorithms.
*   The comparison to existing methods could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work—particularly the novelty, theoretical guarantees, and empirical validation—outweigh them. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation and has the potential to impact various applications.  Addressing the suggestions for improvement would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
