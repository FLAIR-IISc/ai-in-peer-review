Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network via gossip. Each node holds one observation and aims to estimate Ûn(H) = (1/n^2) ∑i,j H(Xi, Xj), where H is symmetric and H(X,X)=0. The main idea is to combine two iterative mechanisms: propagation of data (each node maintains an auxiliary observation that performs coupled random walks through swaps along randomly selected edges) and averaging of local estimates. Two algorithms are proposed: GoSta-sync (synchronous global clock) and GoSta-async (fully asynchronous pairwise updates). The authors introduce a “phantom nodes” analytical device that augments the graph with n copies to decouple data propagation and averaging, enabling a linear system analysis. They prove convergence in expectation to the true U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) in the synchronous setting (with explicit data- and network-dependent constants) and O(log t / t) in the asynchronous setting. They compare against U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), which propagates two auxiliary observations without averaging, and argue analytically and empirically that their methods converge faster with lower memory and communication. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter over complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs corroborate the analysis.

The work is closely related to randomized gossip for averaging (e.g., Boyd et al.), and to prior attempts at distributed computation of non-separable functions (U2-gossip). The paper leverages standard connections between convergence rates and spectral gap of the graph Laplacian, and follows common asynchronous modeling via Poisson clocks. The combination of averaging with observation propagation for U-statistics and the phantom-network analysis appear to be the main new contributions.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The synchronous analysis is clearly set up via block-structured transition matrices M(t) with Wα(G) operators. The bound captures both data dispersion terms (‖h−Û1‖ and ‖H−h1⊤‖) and the graph-dependent rate through c(G)=1−λ2(W2), which aligns with spectral-gap intuition. The comparison bound for U2-gossip is consistent with the idea that lack of averaging induces a √n penalty. The asynchronous algorithm uses a local counter mk and pk=2dk/|E| to calibrate step sizes; Theorem 2 shows convergence in expectation with a log t / t rate.
- Missing details/assumptions: Convergence is in expectation only; there are no high-probability or almost-sure guarantees, which are common in gossip analyses. For Theorem 2, the constant c′(G) is not explicit and the dependence on spectral properties and degree heterogeneity is not characterized. It would help to state conditions on H (boundedness or finite second moments) needed for the norms and bounds. The unbiasedness intuition for mk in the asynchronous setting is terse and could be clarified given the coupling between activation events and auxiliary observation dynamics.
- Correctness of modeling: The phantom graph is used only for analysis, not in the algorithm, which is appropriate. The non-bipartite assumption is standard for avoiding periodicity; it would be helpful to note that a lazy step or self-loop augmentation can handle bipartite graphs.

Clarity:
The presentation is generally clear, with precise notation and algorithm pseudocode. The phantom-network derivation is informative, though some steps are deferred to the supplement. A few points would benefit from clarification:
- Explicitly define the norms used in bounds (it appears to be Euclidean for vectors and Frobenius for matrices).
- Briefly justify why including i=j terms with H(X,X)=0 makes (1) equivalent to the classical U-statistic up to the usual factor n/(n−1).
- In the asynchronous algorithm, motivate the choice mi ← mi + 1/pi and the step 1/(pi mi); provide a brief derivation explaining why this emulates 1/t scaling despite random activations.
- Consolidate notation (e.g., consistent use of Wα) and fix minor typos and missing citation metadata.

Originality:
While gossip averaging and U2-gossip are established, the proposed combination of averaging with single-observation propagation for estimating U-statistics, the synchronous and asynchronous variants with reduced memory/communication, and the phantom-nodes analytical technique together form a novel contribution. The improvement over U2-gossip is conceptually simple yet non-trivial.

Significance:
Degree-2 U-statistics (e.g., variance proxies, AUC, rank correlations, within-cluster scatter) are widely used, and enabling their decentralized estimation with low memory and communication is valuable. The results advance understanding of how network topology affects non-separable estimation. The empirical gains appear meaningful. The work is likely to be used as a building block in decentralized learning/optimization where pairwise statistics are needed. The scope (degree-2 only) and expectation-only guarantees limit broader impact, but the contribution is solid.

Pros:
- Clear problem formulation with practical relevance.
- Simple algorithms with low per-iteration communication and memory (one auxiliary observation).
- Rigorous convergence analysis with data- and graph-dependent rates; improved rate over U2-gossip.
- Both synchronous and fully asynchronous variants.
- Empirical validation on real datasets and multiple graph topologies; consistent gains and reduced variance across nodes.
- Phantom-network analysis is elegant and potentially of independent interest.

Cons:
- Convergence only in expectation; no almost-sure or concentration bounds.
- Asynchronous rate has an unspecified constant; dependence on spectral gap not explicit.
- Fairness of experimental comparison: iterations vs. messages/bytes not normalized, while U2-gossip uses two edge activations per iteration.
- Assumption that nodes know |E| (to compute pk) may be unrealistic; alternatives not discussed.
- Non-bipartite requirement not mitigated (e.g., laziness).
- Limited exploration of scalability (e.g., larger n, higher d), higher-order U-statistics, and robustness to unbounded H.
- Some references are incomplete and could be expanded (e.g., related decentralized estimation of pairwise functions).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide high-probability/MSE bounds, or at least discuss extending the analysis beyond expectation.
- Make c′(G) explicit in Theorem 2, relating it to spectral gap and degrees.
- Report results normalized by total messages or transmitted bytes to ensure fair comparisons with U2-gossip; include communication/memory per iteration in a small table.
- Discuss how to handle bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy randomization) and how to relax the requirement of knowing |E| (e.g., using local activation counts only).
- Clarify the asynchronous weighting and the role of mk more rigorously in the main text.
- Comment on potential extensions to higher-order U-statistics and to non-uniform edge selection P.

Overall assessment:
This is a clear, technically sound, and moderately novel paper that improves decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics with provable rates and practical algorithms. With stronger concentration results and more careful empirical normalization, it would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS on distributed algorithms.
