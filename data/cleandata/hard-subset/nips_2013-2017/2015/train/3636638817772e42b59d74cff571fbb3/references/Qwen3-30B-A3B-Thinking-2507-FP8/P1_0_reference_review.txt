

# Peer Review: "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computation: existing gossip algorithms can efficiently compute separable functions (e.g., averages of individual observations) but cannot efficiently handle U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations). The authors propose two novel gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are important in machine learning applications like AUC estimation, sample variance, and within-cluster point scatter. Their key innovation is a "phantom nodes" reformulation that allows them to model data propagation and averaging separately, enabling convergence analysis. They prove O(1/t) convergence for the synchronous algorithm and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, with lower memory and communication costs compared to the prior work U2-gossip [17]. Experiments on real datasets confirm their theoretical advantages.

This work builds on the extensive literature on gossip algorithms for decentralized averaging (e.g., [2, 10, 15, 18, 19]), extending it to a more complex class of problems. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon prior work.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Extension**: The paper addresses a meaningful gap in distributed computation by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, which are common in machine learning but previously not efficiently computable via gossip.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The convergence proofs are thorough and use an elegant "phantom nodes" approach to reformulate the problem. The analysis clearly shows how network connectivity (via spectral gap) affects convergence rates.

3. **Practical Improvements**: The authors demonstrate clear advantages over U2-gossip [17] in terms of memory complexity (O(1) vs. O(2) auxiliary observations per node), communication cost (1 vs. 2 pairs of nodes per iteration), and convergence rates.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real datasets convincingly demonstrate the practical superiority of their approach across different network topologies.

## Weaknesses

1. **Clarity of Phantom Nodes Concept**: While the phantom nodes approach is clever, the explanation in the paper (particularly in Section 4.1) is somewhat dense and could be clearer for readers unfamiliar with this technique. A more intuitive explanation or additional examples would help.

2. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The paper only compares to U2-gossip [17], but doesn't discuss how their approach might compare to other distributed optimization methods for U-statistics (e.g., [7, 16]) or to non-gossip-based distributed approaches.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: While the paper shows improvements for networks up to n=1599, it doesn't address how the algorithms scale to extremely large networks (e.g., n > 10^5), which is a common scenario in distributed systems.

4. **Asynchronous Implementation Details**: The asynchronous algorithm (Algorithm 2) requires nodes to know their degrees and the total number of edges, which might not be practical in all decentralized settings.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous convergence proofs. The theoretical analysis is well-supported by experiments. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations of prior work (U2-gossip) and demonstrate how their approach improves upon them. The paper is complete, with both theoretical analysis and experimental validation. (Score: 9/10)

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with pseudocode for the algorithms. However, the phantom nodes concept could be explained more intuitively. Some sections (particularly the convergence proofs) are quite dense. The experimental results are well-presented. (Score: 8/10)

**Originality**: The extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics is novel, and the phantom nodes approach represents a creative solution to the problem. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work and demonstrates significant improvements. (Score: 9/10)

**Significance**: U-statistics are important in many machine learning applications, and distributed estimation of these quantities is a practical problem in sensor networks, social media analysis, and other large-scale decentralized systems. The authors demonstrate clear improvements in convergence rates, memory usage, and communication costs, making this work highly relevant to the distributed systems and machine learning communities. (Score: 9/10)

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed algorithms. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and theoretically sound, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages over prior work. The minor weaknesses in clarity and limited baseline comparisons do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Addresses a meaningful gap in distributed computation
- Novel approach using "phantom nodes" for convergence analysis
- Theoretical guarantees with practical improvements over prior work
- Strong experimental validation on real datasets
- Clear improvements in memory, communication, and convergence rates

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Some technical details (phantom nodes) could be clarified
- Limited comparison to other distributed optimization methods
- No discussion of extreme scalability (n > 10^5)

## Final Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of distributed algorithms and machine learning. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and theoretically sound, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of practical advantages. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in the camera-ready version with minor revisions.

This work aligns well with NIPS' focus on machine learning and distributed systems, and it advances the state of the art in distributed computation of non-separable statistics. The paper is of high quality and would be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS.
