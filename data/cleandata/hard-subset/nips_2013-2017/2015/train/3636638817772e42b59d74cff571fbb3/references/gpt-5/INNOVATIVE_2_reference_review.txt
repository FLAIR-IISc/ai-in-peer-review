Summary
The paper proposes two gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous), for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network where each node holds one observation. The key idea is to decouple (i) data propagation via swapping a single auxiliary observation that performs a random walk and (ii) consensus averaging of local U-statistic estimates. The authors introduce an analytical device (“phantom nodes”) to separately model propagation and averaging, leading to convergence in expectation at rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t / t) (async), with constants that cleanly decompose into data-dependent terms and a network-dependent spectral gap. The algorithms use less memory/communication than the prior U2-gossip method and achieve better rates; experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several graph topologies support the theory.

Relation to prior work
- Extends classical randomized gossip for separable functions to pairwise-dependent quantities (U-statistics), a setting not handled by standard averaging. Closest prior is U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010], which the paper improves upon theoretically and empirically.
- Builds on spectral analysis of gossip/consensus (Boyd et al., Chung) and random-walk-based propagation, but contributes a new analytical reformulation (phantom networks) and a combined propagate-and-average scheme.
- Asynchronous modeling follows standard Poisson-clocks reductions but incorporates a node-specific step-size correction.

Strengths
- Novel direction: first practical, low-overhead gossip algorithms for a broad class of non-separable statistics (U-statistics), a gap in the literature. The “phantom nodes” trick is conceptually elegant and potentially reusable.
- Theoretical guarantees with explicit, interpretable constants: rates depend on both problem difficulty (dispersion of h, H) and network connectivity (spectral gap). This is insightful for practitioners.
- Improves over U2-gossip in both rate and resource usage: one auxiliary sample and a single edge activation per iteration vs two; avoids the √n factor in U2’s bound.
- Asynchronous variant with unbiasedness via local counters; maintains simplicity and practicality.
- Empirical results on real datasets and multiple graph families corroborate theory; variance reduction due to averaging is apparent.

Weaknesses and questions
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation only. High-probability or almost-sure convergence (e.g., via martingale/Robbins–Siegmund or concentration for time-averaged random walks) would substantially strengthen the result and better explain the across-node variance behavior.
- Asynchronous model and implementability: Algorithm 2 still relies on drawing a uniformly random edge and requires pk = 2dk/|E| and knowledge of |E| at each node. In decentralized systems this can be unrealistic. Can the algorithm be instantiated with local waking + neighbor selection via Metropolis weights (bi-stochastic P) without knowledge of |E|, and do the rates and unbiasedness still hold? If uniform-edge sampling is only an analysis device, please clarify the implementable protocol.
- Non-bipartite assumption: standard fix is to add laziness (α > 2) or use Metropolis weights; please discuss how to remove/relax the assumption in both algorithms and reflect it in the theorems.
- Scalability and networks: experiments are up to ~1.6k nodes and simple topologies. Since the rates depend on the spectral gap, it would be helpful to include larger/sparser graphs (e.g., power-law, random geometric) and report wall-clock vs iteration trade-offs.
- Communication/computation costs: a more explicit accounting would help (message size = one d-dimensional vector per activated edge per iter; local compute H(Xi, Yi)). In high dimensions, swapping Yi could be expensive; any support for compression/quantization without bias?
- U-statistics scope: H(X,X)=0 is assumed; many U-statistics use the unbiased n(n−1) normalization and exclude diagonals. You note this, but a brief discussion on adapting the algorithms/bounds to the canonical U-statistic definition (and nonzero diagonals) would improve completeness.

Quality
- The theory appears sound and the analysis is clear; the phantom-node block formulation is neat and the bounds are plausible and interpretable. Missing: stronger convergence modes and a more detailed asynchronous implementability discussion.
- Experiments are consistent with claims; more breadth in networks and scales would help.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; algorithms are easy to follow. The phantom network construction is explained clearly with helpful notation.
- Minor issues: the asynchronous section mixes the Poisson-clock rationale with uniform edge sampling; please clarify the exact decentralized protocol. Consider adding a brief complexity table contrasting GoSta vs U2-gossip.

Originality
- Solid novelty in both problem setting (gossip for U-statistics) and the analytical technique. The combination of propagation + averaging with a single auxiliary sample is simple yet impactful.

Significance
- Useful building block for decentralized evaluation/optimization where pairwise interactions matter (AUC, rank correlation, clustering, pairwise losses). The spectral-gap-aware rates make it relevant for networked ML systems. I expect others to build on this (e.g., decentralized pairwise SGD).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap (non-separable statistics in gossip) with a simple, elegant, and provably convergent approach.
- Strong conceptual contribution (phantom nodes) and interpretable rates.
- Clear improvements over the only prior method; practical asynchronous variant.

Cons for acceptance
- Only expectation guarantees; limited experimental scale and network diversity.
- Some implementability assumptions (uniform edge sampling; knowledge of |E|) need clarification or relaxation.

Scores
- Quality: 7/10
- Clarity: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept. The paper introduces a fresh and useful direction with a clean idea and solid analysis; the minor limitations are addressable and do not detract from the main contributions.
