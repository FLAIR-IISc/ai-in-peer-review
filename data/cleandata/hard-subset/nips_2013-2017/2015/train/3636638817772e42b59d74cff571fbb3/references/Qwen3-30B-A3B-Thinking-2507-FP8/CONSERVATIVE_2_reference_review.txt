

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes novel gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) for distributed estimation of U-statistics in peer-to-peer networks. The authors address a gap in the literature by extending gossip algorithms—which efficiently compute separable functions like averages—to handle U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations), such as AUC, sample variance, and within-cluster point scatter. The theoretical contribution involves a "phantom node" reformulation to prove convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. The authors claim their approach outperforms the only prior work on this problem (U2-gossip) in terms of convergence rate, memory, and communication costs.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with rigorous convergence proofs using an interesting phantom node framework.
- The paper clearly articulates the problem and provides a well-structured presentation of the algorithms.
- The experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter show consistent improvements over U2-gossip across different network topologies.
- The connection between convergence rates and network properties (spectral gap) is well-established.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from several critical limitations that undermine its contribution as a significant advance for NIPS:

1. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The authors only compare against U2-gossip, which is the only prior work on distributed U-statistics. They fail to compare against established gossip algorithms for related problems (e.g., gossip for separable functions [2, 10, 15]) or standard distributed optimization approaches that could be adapted to this problem. This makes it impossible to assess whether the claimed "improvements" are meaningful relative to existing distributed learning techniques.

2. **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments use only two small datasets (n=1260 and n=1599) and three network topologies. Real-world distributed systems often operate on much larger datasets (millions of points) and more complex network conditions (e.g., heterogeneous node capabilities, network partitions, or dynamic topologies). The paper lacks scalability analysis beyond n=1599.

3. **Unsubstantiated Practical Claims:** The authors claim "lower memory and communication costs" but provide no concrete measurements of these metrics. For high-dimensional data (d=23 in experiments), the memory requirements of storing auxiliary observations would be significant, yet this isn't quantified. The paper also fails to demonstrate how the algorithms perform under realistic distributed constraints (e.g., node failures, bandwidth limitations).

4. **Theoretical-Practical Disconnect:** The asynchronous algorithm has a theoretically worse convergence rate (O(log t/t)) than the synchronous version (O(1/t)), yet Figure 3b shows comparable practical performance. This discrepancy isn't adequately explained, raising questions about the practical relevance of the theoretical analysis.

5. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The experimental results present average error and standard deviation but don't include statistical significance tests, making it difficult to determine whether the observed improvements are meaningful.

6. **Overstated Contribution:** The paper claims "largely improve upon those presented in [17]" but doesn't clearly articulate how their method fundamentally advances the state of the art beyond a minor refinement of U2-gossip. The phantom node technique is clever but appears to be a specialized tool for this specific problem rather than a generalizable technique.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically sound extension to gossip algorithms for a specific class of problems, the lack of comprehensive validation against established baselines and the limited scope of experimental evaluation make it difficult to assess the true significance of the contribution. The claimed improvements over U2-gossip appear marginal given the problem's complexity, and the paper fails to demonstrate why this particular problem warrants a dedicated algorithm when existing distributed optimization methods could potentially be adapted.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
- Comparing against established gossip algorithms for related problems
- Providing concrete memory/communication cost measurements
- Demonstrating scalability to larger networks and datasets
- Including statistical significance tests for experimental results
- Clarifying the practical advantages over existing approaches

As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes rigorous validation over theoretical novelty, I find the evidence supporting this paper's claims insufficient for acceptance at NIPS. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the limited validation fails to meet the high standards expected for a NIPS paper. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed algorithms offer meaningful practical advantages over existing approaches.

**Recommendation: Reject**
