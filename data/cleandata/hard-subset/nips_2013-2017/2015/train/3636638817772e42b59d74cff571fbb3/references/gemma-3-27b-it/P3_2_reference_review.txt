## NIPS Review: Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation, specifically addressing the challenging problem of computing U-statistics with gossip algorithms. The proposed algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) offer improvements over existing work (particularly [17]) in terms of convergence rate, memory usage, and applicability to asynchronous settings. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results provide compelling evidence of the algorithms' effectiveness. While the phantom node reformulation feels slightly contrived, it’s a clever technique to facilitate the analysis. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of efficiently estimating U-statistics in a distributed setting where nodes communicate via gossip protocols. U-statistics are prevalent in machine learning (AUC, variance, etc.) but are notoriously difficult to compute in a decentralized manner. Existing gossip algorithms excel at averaging, but fail when calculations require pairwise interactions between data points. This paper addresses this limitation by proposing novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async. The authors provide a thorough review of related work in gossip algorithms and U-statistics, correctly positioning their contribution as an advancement over previous efforts, particularly [17]. The connection to spectral graph theory for analyzing convergence rates is well-established and appropriately applied.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is a significant contribution. The problem is genuinely challenging, and the proposed solution is not trivial.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis is thorough and provides clear convergence rates for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The bounds are insightful, factoring in both data-dependent terms (hardness of the estimation problem) and network-dependent terms (spectral gap).
* **Practicality:** The algorithms have lower memory and communication costs compared to [17], making them more suitable for large-scale, resource-constrained networks.
* **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results on AUC and within-cluster scatter demonstrate the superiority of GoSta-sync over U2-gossip across different network topologies. The experiments are well-designed and clearly presented.
* **Asynchronous Algorithm:** The inclusion of an asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) broadens the applicability of the proposed approach to more realistic scenarios where global synchronization is impractical.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the algorithms and theoretical concepts.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Phantom Node Reformulation:** While effective for the analysis, the introduction of "phantom nodes" feels somewhat artificial and could potentially confuse readers. It adds a layer of abstraction that isn't strictly necessary for understanding the algorithms themselves. A more direct analysis, even if more complex, might have been preferable.
* **Convergence Rate of Asynchronous Algorithm:** The logarithmic convergence rate for GoSta-async (O(log t / t)) is slower than the O(1/t) rate for GoSta-sync. While this is inherent to asynchronous settings, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between synchronization and convergence speed would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results are compelling, the experiments are limited to two specific U-statistics and three network topologies. Exploring a wider range of applications and network configurations would further strengthen the conclusions.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors do not explicitly address the sensitivity of the performance to different network parameters (e.g., edge probabilities, network size). A brief discussion of this would be valuable.




**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant problem in distributed estimation.
* Presents novel and theoretically sound algorithms.
* Demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches.
* Backed by compelling experimental results.
* Well-written and organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The phantom node reformulation adds unnecessary complexity.
* The asynchronous algorithm has a slower convergence rate.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.


**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the phantom node reformulation, perhaps illustrating how it maps back to the original algorithm’s behavior?
* What is the practical impact of the slower convergence rate of GoSta-async? Under what network conditions would the benefits of asynchronicity outweigh the slower convergence?
*  Are there any theoretical limitations on the types of U-statistics that can be efficiently estimated using your algorithms?
* How sensitive is the performance of the algorithms to network parameters like edge probabilities and network size?



**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

* Provide a more intuitive explanation of the phantom node reformulation.
* Elaborate on the trade-offs between synchronization and convergence speed in the asynchronous setting.
* Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include more U-statistics and network configurations.
* Briefly discuss the sensitivity of the algorithms to network parameters.



This paper represents a significant step forward in the field of distributed estimation and is a strong candidate for acceptance to NIPS.
