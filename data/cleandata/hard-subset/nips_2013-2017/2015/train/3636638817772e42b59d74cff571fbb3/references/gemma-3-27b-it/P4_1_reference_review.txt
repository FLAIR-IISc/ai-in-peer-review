This paper introduces randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. U-statistics, involving averages over pairs of data points, are a challenge for standard gossip algorithms designed for separable functions. The authors propose algorithms based on propagating local observations and averaging local estimates, achieving convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for synchronous and asynchronous versions respectively. The theoretical analysis utilizes a novel “phantom node” reformulation, and experimental validation demonstrates superior performance compared to a recent related work [17] on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis, while complex, appears carefully constructed, and the use of phantom nodes to facilitate analysis is a clever approach. The theorems stating the convergence rates are clearly stated and supported by the analysis. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the reliance on a connected, non-bipartite graph. The comparison to [17] is valuable, highlighting improvements in convergence, memory, and communication costs. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous versions adds to the paper’s completeness. The algorithms themselves seem well-defined and practical. However, the implementation details in the experimental section are sparse; more details on the network topology generation and parameter tuning would strengthen the reproducibility. The proofs, while alluded to, are relegated to supplementary material, which is acceptable, but the main paper would benefit from a more high-level outline of the key proof steps.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and connects it to relevant applications. The notation is consistent and well-defined, though a glossary of the more specialized terms (e.g., bi-stochastic matrix) might be helpful for readers less familiar with the area. The algorithm descriptions are clear and concise (Algorithm 1 and 2). The connection between the phantom node reformulation and the actual algorithm execution could be made more explicit—it feels somewhat abstract. Section 2 provides necessary background, although the level of detail could be adjusted (e.g., is the entire discussion of matrix properties necessary?).

**Originality:** The problem of extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is novel. While gossip algorithms are well-established for separable functions, this work addresses a significant limitation. The use of phantom nodes and the reformulation of the problem are original contributions. The combination of synchronization/asynchronization with this novel approach is also a valuable contribution.  The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the paper within the existing literature, specifically contrasting it with [17]. The authors do a good job highlighting the differences and improvements over previous approaches.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant. U-statistics appear in a wide range of applications (machine learning, statistics, data mining), and the ability to compute them efficiently in a decentralized manner has practical value. The faster convergence rates and lower communication costs compared to existing methods are important improvements. The experimental results on real datasets further support the practical relevance of the work. The theoretical results, linking convergence rates to network properties (spectral gap), provide insights into the performance of the algorithms in different network topologies. This work could inspire further research on decentralized computation of more complex functions.  The extension to asynchronous settings is particularly important for real-world applications.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem (U-statistic estimation in distributed settings).
*   Sound theoretical analysis with provable convergence rates.
*   Demonstrated improvements over existing work (specifically [17]) in both theory and practice.
*   Clear presentation and well-defined algorithms.
*   Relevance to a broad range of applications.
*   Extension to both synchronous and asynchronous settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental section lacks sufficient detail for full reproducibility.
*   The connection between the phantom node reformulation and the practical algorithm could be more clearly explained.
*   Proofs relegated to supplementary material - while common, a higher-level outline in the main paper would be beneficial.
*   The theoretical analysis is complex and may be difficult for some readers to fully grasp without detailed knowledge of spectral graph theory.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed computation. The originality, technical soundness, and potential significance of the work support its acceptance. Addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above would further strengthen the paper.
