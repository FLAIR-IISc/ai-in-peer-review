

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computation: the extension of gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance, Gini mean difference). Existing gossip algorithms can compute separable statistics (like averages), but cannot efficiently handle U-statistics, which require pairwise interactions. The authors propose "GoSta" algorithms (both synchronous and asynchronous) that combine data propagation with averaging to estimate U-statistics in distributed networks.

The key novelty lies in the "phantom nodes" reformulation, which enables the convergence analysis by modeling data propagation separately from the averaging process. This approach allows the authors to prove convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings, with constants reflecting both data-dependent and network-dependent properties. The theoretical results significantly improve upon the prior work U2-gossip [17], which required more memory and communication, had slower convergence, and was not asynchronous.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature. Gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for distributed averaging (e.g., [10, 2, 15]), but this is the first to extend them to handle U-statistics, a class of statistics that appears frequently in machine learning and data mining. The phantom nodes technique is a clever mathematical construct that enables the convergence proof, and the paper demonstrates how this approach can be adapted to handle high-dimensional data and multiple observations per node.

The experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real datasets confirm the theoretical advantages. GoSta consistently outperforms U2-gossip across all network types (complete, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz), with the performance gap widening as network size increases. The asynchronous version (GoSta-async) shows comparable practical performance to the synchronous variant despite its slightly worse theoretical convergence rate.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization and estimation. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and elegant, and the results are both theoretically sound and practically validated. While the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations (e.g., how the approach scales to higher-order U-statistics), these are minor concerns given the paper's strong novelty.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance. The authors have identified and solved a meaningful problem that has been overlooked in the gossip algorithm literature. The phantom nodes approach is a creative technical contribution that opens up new directions for distributed computation of non-separable statistics.

The paper's significance is clear: it enables the distributed computation of a wide range of important statistics that were previously difficult to handle in decentralized settings. This work has direct applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale data processing where data is distributed across multiple nodes without a central coordinator.

Strengths:
- Solves a fundamental gap in distributed computation (U-statistics)
- Introduces a novel "phantom nodes" technique for convergence analysis
- Provides improved theoretical convergence rates and practical performance
- Demonstrates clear experimental advantages over prior work
- Addresses a problem with broad applications in machine learning

Weaknesses:
- Limited to degree-two U-statistics (though this covers many important cases)
- The phantom nodes construction, while mathematically elegant, might be challenging to intuit for some readers
- Experiments could have included more diverse datasets or additional baselines

The paper is well-written, logically structured, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in distributed algorithms by extending their applicability to a fundamentally new class of problems. As a reviewer who values bold, novel approaches over incremental improvements, I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper represents a meaningful step forward in distributed computation that will likely inspire future work in the field.
