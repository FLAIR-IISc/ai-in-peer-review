

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computation: the inability of existing gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations, such as AUC, sample variance, and within-cluster point scatter). The authors propose two novel algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, which combine data propagation with local averaging to estimate U-statistics in distributed networks. They provide convergence guarantees (O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous) and demonstrate superior performance over the previous U2-gossip algorithm in both theory and practice. The analysis uses an interesting "phantom node" technique to model data propagation, and experiments on real datasets confirm the advantages of the proposed approach.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured theoretical analysis with rigorous proofs for convergence rates. The use of phantom networks for analysis is clever and provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior. The comparison with U2-gossip is thorough, showing both theoretical advantages (faster convergence, lower communication/memory costs) and empirical validation.

2. **Significance**: U-statistics are widely used in machine learning (AUC, Gini mean difference, etc.), and the paper addresses a clear practical need in distributed systems. The proposed algorithms enable efficient computation of these statistics in decentralized networks, which is important for applications like sensor networks and social media analysis.

3. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithms are described precisely with pseudocode. The phantom node concept, while somewhat complex, is well-explained and visualized.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on real-world datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC, Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter) are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over U2-gossip across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz).

## Weaknesses
1. **Network Dynamics**: The paper assumes a static network topology but doesn't address how the algorithms handle dynamic networks (nodes joining/leaving) or failures, which are common in practical distributed systems. This limits the applicability of the approach to real-world scenarios.

2. **Algorithmic Complexity**: While the paper claims lower communication and memory costs, the analysis doesn't fully quantify how these costs scale with problem size (n, d, network topology). For high-dimensional data (large d), the communication cost of swapping observations could still be significant.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: The asynchronous convergence rate (O(log t/t)) is slower than the synchronous version (O(1/t)), but the paper doesn't provide a detailed explanation for this gap or suggest potential improvements. The theoretical bounds also contain data-dependent terms that aren't fully characterized in terms of how they affect practical performance.

4. **Comparison Scope**: The comparison is limited to U2-gossip, but it would be valuable to include comparisons with other potential approaches for distributed U-statistic estimation (e.g., using local approximations or alternative communication patterns).

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of distributed computation. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and well-supported by both theory and experiments, and the writing is clear and professional. The paper addresses a gap that has been overlooked in the literature, and the proposed algorithms represent a meaningful improvement over the state of the art.

The main weaknesses are the lack of discussion on dynamic network behavior and the theoretical gap between synchronous and asynchronous convergence rates. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is well-justified and significant. The paper meets all the criteria for a NeurIPS submission: it is technically sound, clearly written, original in its approach, and addresses an important problem with practical significance.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of distributed algorithms with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over existing approaches. The minor weaknesses identified are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's strengths, and the authors could address these in future work.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. In the final version, consider adding a brief discussion of how the algorithms might be adapted to handle dynamic network topologies.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of how the data-dependent terms in the convergence bounds affect practical performance across different U-statistic types.

3. Include a more comprehensive comparison with potential alternative approaches for distributed U-statistic estimation, even if just in the supplementary material.

4. Consider discussing the practical implications of the communication cost for high-dimensional data in the main text.
