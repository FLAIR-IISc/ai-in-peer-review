### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes novel randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, for the decentralized estimation of degree-two U-statistics over a network. U-statistics, such as sample variance, AUC, or within-cluster scatter, are averages over pairs of data points and cannot be computed by standard gossip algorithms designed for separable sums. The authors present both synchronous and asynchronous versions of their algorithm. The core idea is to have each node maintain its original data point and a single "auxiliary" data point. At each step, a pair of connected nodes swap their auxiliary observations, causing the data to propagate through the network via random walks. Simultaneously, nodes update their local estimate of the U-statistic by incorporating the kernel function evaluated on their fixed and auxiliary data points, and in the synchronous version, by also averaging their estimates with their interacting partner.

The authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis for both algorithms, introducing a "phantom network" construction as an analytical tool to decouple the data propagation and estimate averaging dynamics. They prove that the synchronous algorithm converges in expectation at a rate of O(1/t) and the asynchronous version at O(log t/t). These rates are shown to depend on the network's spectral gap and data-dependent variance terms. The paper contrasts this approach with the most relevant prior work, U2-gossip [17], arguing that GoSta offers improved convergence rates, lower memory requirements (one auxiliary variable vs. two), and reduced communication costs. These claims are supported by experiments on real datasets for estimating AUC and within-cluster point scatter across various network topologies.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem. U-statistics are a fundamental class of estimators used for many key metrics in machine learning. Developing efficient, fully decentralized algorithms for their computation is highly relevant for applications in sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale distributed learning. This work significantly advances the state-of-the-art for this problem.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The proposed GoSta algorithm is an elegant and novel combination of existing gossip primitives (data swapping and value averaging). While the components are familiar, their application to U-statistics is new and effective. The theoretical analysis is a major strength of the paper. The "phantom network" reformulation is a creative and powerful analytical device that enables a clear derivation of the convergence rates. The derived rates explicitly connect the algorithm's performance to graph connectivity (spectral gap) and the statistical properties of the data, which is insightful.

3.  **Clear Improvement Over Prior Art:** The paper makes a compelling case for its superiority over the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17]. The authors not only claim better performance but also provide a refined theoretical analysis of U2-gossip (Eq. 10) to demonstrate its slower convergence due to a √n factor, which they attribute to the lack of an estimate-averaging step. This is a strong contribution in itself. The claims of lower memory and communication overhead for GoSta are also well-founded and practically important.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The conceptual explanation of the analysis, especially the phantom network, is very helpful for the reader. The experiments are well-designed and the results are presented clearly, effectively supporting the paper's claims.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The derived convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm is O(log t / t), which is slightly worse than the O(1/t) rate for the synchronous version and for many standard asynchronous averaging algorithms. While this is still a solid theoretical result, the paper could benefit from a discussion on whether this logarithmic factor is an artifact of the proof technique or an inherent limitation of the proposed asynchronous scheme.

2.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are well-executed and convincing. However, they could be strengthened further. For instance, a direct comparison of communication cost (e.g., total bytes transmitted vs. estimation error) would provide more explicit evidence for the claimed communication efficiency over U2-gossip. Additionally, exploring a wider range of network sizes and parameters could provide more insight into the scalability of the algorithms.

### **Constructive Feedback for Improvement**

-   In the comparison with U2-gossip, you present a new, tighter bound (Eq. 10). It would strengthen the paper to add a sentence in the main text offering intuition for the origin of the √n factor in that bound. For instance, is it because without averaging, the estimation errors at each node do not cancel and thus accumulate in the L2 norm? This would make the superiority of your averaging-based approach even more apparent.

-   Regarding the asynchronous rate, a brief discussion on the potential sources of the `log t` factor would be valuable. Could an alternative update rule for the asynchronous setting, perhaps involving different step sizes, recover the O(1/t) rate? Acknowledging this and framing it as a direction for future work would be a good addition.

-   The claim in Remark 2 about extending the method to nodes with multiple observations is very interesting. The sketch of the solution (splitting nodes) is plausible, but a bit more detail on how the new edge weights would be defined to maintain correctness would make this point more concrete and useful for practitioners.

### **Decision**

This is an excellent paper that presents a clear, simple, and effective solution to an important problem in decentralized computation. The work is technically sound, with a novel theoretical analysis and strong empirical results that demonstrate a significant improvement over the state of the art. The paper is well-written and will be of interest to a broad audience at NeurIPS working on distributed learning, gossip algorithms, and large-scale optimization. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
