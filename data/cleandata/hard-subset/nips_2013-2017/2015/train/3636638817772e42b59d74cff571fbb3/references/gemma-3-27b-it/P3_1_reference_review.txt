## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension of gossip algorithms to a challenging problem – distributed estimation of U-statistics. The authors tackle a practical limitation of standard gossip algorithms and provide both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The paper is well-written and addresses a significant problem, though some aspects of the analysis and experimental setup could be strengthened. I lean towards acceptance, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of efficiently computing U-statistics (averages over pairs of observations) in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip algorithms are well-suited for averaging separable functions, but struggle with U-statistics. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) which combine data propagation and averaging steps. They provide convergence rate analysis (O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous) dependent on network topology (spectral gap) and data characteristics. The work builds upon the existing literature on gossip algorithms (Boyd et al., Kempe et al.) and acknowledges the previous attempt by Pelckmans & Suykens (U2-gossip), but significantly improves upon it in terms of convergence, memory, and communication costs. The "phantom nodes" reformulation is a clever technique for analyzing the distributed process.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:**  Computing U-statistics is essential in various machine learning tasks (AUC, variance, etc.), and decentralized computation is crucial for large-scale datasets.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of data propagation and averaging within a gossip framework is a creative solution. The phantom node reformulation is insightful and allows for a rigorous analysis.
* **Strong theoretical guarantees:** The convergence rate analysis is well-developed and provides valuable insights into the algorithm's performance based on network properties and data difficulty.  The data-dependent terms are particularly valuable.
* **Improved efficiency:**  GoSta demonstrably improves upon the previous work (U2-gossip) in terms of communication and memory requirements, which is a significant practical advantage.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms and confirm the theoretical advantages.
* **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally easy to follow and the notation is well-defined.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the analysis:** While the phantom node approach is clever, it adds complexity to the analysis. It would be helpful to have a more intuitive explanation of *why* this reformulation works and its connection to the original problem. 
* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t/t) convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm seems relatively slow.  While theoretically sound, it is weaker than some other asynchronous averaging algorithms. Is this inherent to the U-statistic problem or could further optimizations be explored?
* **Limited comparison to baselines:** The comparison with U2-gossip is strong, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to simpler distributed estimation methods (e.g., directly applying asynchronous averaging to the elements of H(Xi, Xj)).  This would help contextualize the gains achieved by GoSta.
* **Experimental scope:** The experiments are limited to two applications and a few network topologies. Expanding the experimental evaluation to more diverse datasets and network structures would increase confidence in the generalizability of the results.
* **Practical considerations:** The discussion of network selection (e.g., Watts-Strogatz) feels a bit cursory. A discussion of the trade-offs between different network types in terms of communication cost and convergence speed would be valuable.




**4. Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. However, the complexity of the phantom node formulation and the relatively slow asynchronous convergence rate leave some room for improvement.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The notation is well-defined.
* **Originality (8/10):** The application of gossip algorithms to U-statistic estimation is novel. While building upon existing work, the authors present a unique and significant contribution.
* **Significance (8/10):** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to be widely applicable in decentralized machine learning. The improved efficiency compared to existing methods is a significant advantage.

**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* Demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches.
*  Empirically validated on real datasets.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Against acceptance:**

* The phantom node reformulation is complex.
* Asynchronous convergence rate could be improved.
* Limited comparison to baseline methods.
* Experimental scope could be broadened.


**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, I suggest the following:

* **Clarify the intuition behind the phantom node reformulation.**
* **Discuss potential improvements to the asynchronous convergence rate.**
* **Include a comparison to simpler distributed estimation methods.**
* **Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include more datasets and network topologies.**
* **Provide a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between different network structures.**



I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed machine learning and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
