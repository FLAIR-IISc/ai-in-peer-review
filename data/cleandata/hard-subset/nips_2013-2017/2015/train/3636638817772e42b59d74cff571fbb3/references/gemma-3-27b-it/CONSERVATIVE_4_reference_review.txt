This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting using gossip algorithms. While the problem itself is well-motivated and relevant to several application areas, and the authors correctly identify the limitations of existing gossip algorithms in this context, the paper falls short of convincing me of the significance of the proposed solution. My primary concern stems from a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a substantial advantage over existing approaches, particularly given the added complexity of the proposed algorithms.

The introduction does a reasonable job of framing the problem and highlighting its importance. The background section provides a necessary overview of gossip algorithms and U-statistics. However, the related work section feels somewhat superficial. While [17] is appropriately discussed, there’s little discussion of existing distributed estimation techniques beyond gossip, which might offer alternative baselines. 

The core contribution – the GoSta algorithms – is presented clearly, and the theoretical analysis appears sound. The use of “phantom nodes” is an interesting technique to reformulate the problem and facilitate the analysis. However, the derived convergence rates, while mathematically correct, lack practical interpretability without more extensive empirical validation. The O(1/t) and O(log t/t) rates are not demonstrably better than existing methods *in practice* based on the presented experiments. The claim of faster convergence, paired with lower memory and communication costs, isn’t forcefully supported by the experimental results. 

The experiments themselves are a weak point. While the authors compare against U2-gossip, this comparison alone isn't sufficient. It's crucial to compare performance against simpler, well-established distributed averaging methods (even if they require more iterations) to demonstrate the practical benefit of the more complex GoSta algorithms. Furthermore, the choice of network topologies (complete graph, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz) feels somewhat limited. Exploring more realistic and challenging network structures would strengthen the evaluation. The experiments also lack a thorough analysis of the communication cost. The authors mention lower communication cost, but don't quantify it or demonstrate this advantage.

The asynchronous version (GoSta-async) introduces additional complexity, and the O(log t/t) convergence rate is hardly surprising. The experimental comparison between synchronous and asynchronous versions, while showing comparable performance, doesn’t justify the added algorithmic intricacy of the asynchronous approach. 

**Strengths:**

*   Well-defined problem with clear motivation.
*   Sound theoretical analysis using a clever “phantom node” technique.
*   Clear presentation of the algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited empirical validation and insufficient comparison to baselines.
*   Lack of practical interpretability of convergence rates.
*   Experiments don't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   Superficial related work discussion.
*   The advantages claimed regarding communication costs are not quantified or demonstrated.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is solid and provides a formal understanding of the algorithm’s convergence properties.
*   The problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting is an important one.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical results are not compelling enough to justify the added complexity of the proposed algorithms.
*   The lack of comparison to standard distributed averaging techniques weakens the contribution.
*   The practical significance of the theoretical results is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** this paper. While the theoretical work is sound, the lack of strong empirical evidence and the limited comparison to relevant baselines fail to convince me that this contribution is significant enough for NIPS. The authors need to provide a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including a thorough comparison to simpler distributed averaging methods and a detailed analysis of the communication cost, to demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach. Furthermore, a more in-depth discussion of related work would strengthen the paper’s position within the existing literature.
