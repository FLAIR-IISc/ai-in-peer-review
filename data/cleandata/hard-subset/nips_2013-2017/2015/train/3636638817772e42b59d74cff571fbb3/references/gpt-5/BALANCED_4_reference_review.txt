Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network, where each node holds one datum. It introduces two randomized gossip algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous), which combine (i) propagation of auxiliary observations via random edge swaps and (ii) averaging of node-local estimates. A key analytical device is a “phantom nodes” reformulation that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging and yields tractable linear dynamics. The main results show convergence in expectation of each node’s estimate to the global U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async). The rates depend on data dispersion terms and on the spectral gap of the network (through 1 − λ2 of certain gossip matrices), indicating faster convergence on well-connected graphs. Compared to U2-gossip [17], GoSta uses only one auxiliary observation per node, requires a single edge activation per iteration, supports asynchronous updates, and empirically converges faster on AUC and within-cluster scatter tasks over several graph topologies (complete, 2D grid, Watts–Strogatz).

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within the gossip literature [2,5,10,18] and the distributed computation of separable aggregates [14,15]. Prior work has focused on separable sums; computing pairwise statistics has been addressed primarily by U2-gossip [17], which propagates two auxiliary observations without averaging. This paper’s novelty is to fuse single-observation propagation with consensus averaging and to handle asynchronous operation with appropriate debiasing via local counters. The spectral-gap dependence and random edge activation modeling are in line with standard gossip analyses [2,3]. The “phantom nodes” construction resembles product-graph/Markov chain liftings but appears new in this U-statistics context.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: many practical U-statistics (AUC, Kendall’s tau, within-cluster scatter) are non-separable and common in ML.
- Algorithmic simplicity and practicality: one auxiliary datum per node; single edge activation/iteration; asynchronous version with local clocks.
- Theory: expectation convergence with explicit rates; dependence on spectral gap matches intuition; analysis technique is elegant and reusable.
- Empirics: consistent gains over U2-gossip across network types and tasks; lower across-node variance due to averaging; asynchronous variant competitive in practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Guarantees in expectation only. It would strengthen the paper to add mean-square error or high-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration or coupling to mixing times), and/or almost sure convergence.
- Scaling constants: The synchronous bound scales as 1/(c(G) t), where c(G) ~ Θ(1/n) for dense graphs (e.g., complete graphs). Thus constants can grow with n. The paper argues practical superiority over U2-gossip (which suffers √n/t), but a more explicit comparison of constants across graph families (complete, expanders, grids) would clarify regimes of advantage.
- Asynchronous constant c′(G) is not explicit and the bound depends on ||H||; assumptions on H (e.g., boundedness/sub-Gaussian tails) are not stated. Please clarify conditions ensuring finite moments and how ||H|| scales with n.
- Knowledge of |E|: The asynchronous algorithm requires pk = 2 dk / |E| and increments m_k by 1/p_k. In many decentralized systems, |E| may be unknown. Suggest discussing how to estimate |E| (e.g., via distributed degree/edge counting) or designing a variant that only needs dk (e.g., with local Poisson clocks and implicit time scaling).
- Non-bipartite requirement: Standard fixes include “lazy” updates or self-loops to handle bipartite graphs. It would be useful to discuss or include such a variant.
- Comparison breadth: Experiments compare mainly to U2-gossip. Including baselines such as centralized-oracle (for reference), randomized sampling approximations, or push-sum variants on directed graphs (if applicable) would strengthen the empirical section. If in supplement, summarize key takeaways in the main text.
- Communication and compute costs: While communication per iteration is minimal, in the synchronous scheme all nodes compute H(Xk, Yk) every iteration. A brief discussion of computational cost and possible amortization (e.g., subsampling updates, mini-batching Y) would be helpful, especially for expensive H.
- Practical considerations: Privacy/sensitivity of swapping raw observations (Yi) could be a concern. Consider commenting on compressed or privatized exchanges.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and is consistent with known gossip analyses. The phantom-node construction is insightful and seems correct. Proofs are deferred, but the main steps are sketched clearly. The experiments are sensible and corroborate the claims, though additional statistical summaries (confidence intervals) and broader baselines would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithms are clearly specified. Notation is dense but standard. Minor suggestions:
- Summarize the main assumptions on H (boundedness, symmetry, H(X,X)=0) up front when stating theorems.
- Make c(G) and c′(G) explicit where possible; tabulate values for canonical graphs.
- Briefly justify how uniform random edge selection is implemented in a decentralized way (or clarify it is a model surrogate for asynchronous Poisson activations).

Originality
Moderately original. The problem (distributed U-statistics) is niche but relevant; the key novelty is combining single-observation propagation with averaging and handling asynchrony, plus the phantom-node analysis. Relative to [17], this is a meaningful algorithmic and analytical advance.

Significance
The contribution is useful for decentralized analytics where pairwise statistics matter (AUC, rank correlation, clustering scatter, nonparametric tests). The improved practicality (memory/communication) and asynchrony support increase applicability. While not a breakthrough, it advances the state of the art in a focused area.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide mean-square error or high-probability bounds?
- How sensitive is GoSta-async to mis-specification of |E|? Any adaptive variant?
- Can you handle bipartite graphs via laziness and preserve rates?
- Any results for directed/asymmetric communication via push-sum?

Pros
- Simple, practical algorithms with synchronous and asynchronous variants
- Solid analysis; spectral gap dependence; insightful phantom-node technique
- Empirical superiority over U2-gossip across tasks and graphs
- Lower memory and communication than prior work

Cons
- Expectation-only guarantees; constants scale with n for dense graphs
- Asynchronous bound lacks explicit constants; requires knowledge of |E|
- Limited baselines and discussion of implementation details

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper presents a clear, technically solid, and practically relevant improvement over prior work on decentralized U-statistics. Addressing the noted theoretical and practical clarifications would strengthen it further.
