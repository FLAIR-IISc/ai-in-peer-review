This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized fashion using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip protocols are well-suited for separable sums (e.g., averages of f(Xi)), but U-statistics, which involve sums over pairs of observations (H(Xi, Xj)), are non-separable and pose a significant challenge. The authors propose a novel and elegant approach, called GoSta, that cleverly combines two mechanisms: a data propagation step, where auxiliary copies of the data points perform a random walk on the network via swaps, and a standard estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic with their neighbors.

The paper presents both synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) versions of the algorithm. A key theoretical contribution is the convergence analysis, which relies on an original and insightful "phantom network" construction. This allows the authors to decouple the data propagation and estimate averaging processes for analytical tractability. They prove that their synchronous algorithm converges in expectation at a rate of O(1/t), while the asynchronous version converges at O(log t/t). The bounds insightfully depend on the spectral gap of the network graph, confirming that the algorithms are faster on better-connected networks. The proposed method is benchmarked against the only existing work on this specific problem, U2-gossip [17], and the authors claim significant improvements in convergence rate, memory usage, and communication costs. These claims are well-supported by experiments on real-world datasets for computing the AUC and within-cluster point scatter.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of distributed U-statistic estimation is highly relevant to modern machine learning, with applications in federated learning, sensor networks, and large-scale data analysis. The proposed solution is novel and non-trivial. The dual mechanism of propagating data while averaging estimates is an elegant way to handle the non-separability of U-statistics. This work significantly advances the state-of-the-art from the prior work of [17].

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. The introduction of the "phantom network" is a clever analytical tool that provides a clear path to deriving the convergence rates. The analysis resulting in Equation (10) for the competing U2-gossip algorithm is particularly strong, as it provides a precise, quantitative reason (the √n factor) for the superiority of GoSta, moving beyond simple empirical observation. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying dynamics.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem is motivated clearly, the algorithms are presented concisely, and the main theoretical results are stated upfront. The intuition behind the approach is easy to follow.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. They are conducted on real datasets for meaningful U-statistics (AUC, point scatter) and across a variety of standard network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz). The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over U2-gossip, and the experiment showing the performance gap widening with network size `n` provides excellent empirical support for the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Global Information in Asynchronous Algorithm:** The asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) requires each node `k` to know its activation probability `pk = 2dk/|E|`. While knowing its own degree `dk` is a reasonable local assumption, knowing the total number of edges `|E|` requires global information. This slightly undermines the claim of a fully decentralized protocol. The authors should address this assumption. Is `|E|` pre-computed? Or could it be estimated using another gossip protocol, and if so, how would that affect the convergence analysis?

2.  **Clarity on the Phantom Network:** While the phantom network is a powerful analytical tool, its full exposition is relegated to the supplementary material. The main paper would benefit from a slightly more detailed and intuitive explanation of how the dynamics of the simple Algorithm 1 map onto the complex block matrix M(t). A sentence or two connecting the swap operation to the `W1(G)` matrix and the estimate averaging to `W2(G)` would strengthen the reader's understanding without requiring the full proof.

3.  **Communication Cost:** The paper correctly criticizes U2-gossip for requiring more memory and communication (two auxiliary observations). However, the proposed GoSta algorithm still relies on swapping an entire data observation `Yk` at each communication step. For high-dimensional data (large `d`), this can still be a significant communication bottleneck compared to standard gossip averaging, which only exchanges a single scalar value. A brief discussion of the communication complexity per step (O(d) for GoSta vs. O(1) for standard averaging) and the trade-offs involved would make the paper more complete.

4.  **Assumption H(X,X)=0:** The framework assumes H(Xi, Xi) = 0. While this holds for the presented applications (distances and AUC on distinct points), it is not a general property of all U-statistics (e.g., based on some kernels). The authors should briefly comment on how the algorithm or analysis would change if this assumption were relaxed. It seems likely it would introduce a diagonal bias term that could be estimated and corrected separately, but this is worth clarifying.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and high-quality contribution to the field of decentralized computation and machine learning. The authors tackle a difficult problem with a novel and effective solution, support their claims with rigorous and insightful theory, and validate their method with strong empirical results. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to points of clarification rather than fundamental flaws. The work is original, technically sound, clearly presented, and likely to be influential for researchers working on distributed algorithms and large-scale statistical estimation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
