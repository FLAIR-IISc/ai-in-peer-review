This paper addresses the problem of distributed computation of U-statistics using gossip algorithms, a relevant and challenging problem in decentralized settings. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, provide convergence analysis, and demonstrate empirical performance on real datasets. The work builds upon the existing literature on gossip algorithms, specifically addressing a limitation of prior work regarding non-separable functions like U-statistics.

**Summary of Contributions:** The core contribution is the development of gossip-based algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed manner. The paper introduces “phantom nodes” as a clever technique for analyzing the convergence properties of these algorithms, resulting in convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. The authors also demonstrate the superiority of their approach over a recent related work [17] through both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation.  The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms broadens the applicability of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Relevance:**  Computing U-statistics is important in various machine learning applications (variance, AUC, correlation), and doing so in a decentralized manner is crucial for large-scale datasets and privacy-preserving computation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, utilizing a novel “phantom node” reformulation. The connection between the spectral gap of the network graph and the convergence rate is a valuable insight.  The separation of data propagation and averaging is a clean and intuitive approach.
* **Theoretical Results:** Providing convergence guarantees, even with data-dependent terms, is a significant achievement. The bounds provide insights into the algorithm’s performance based on network topology and data characteristics.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. The comparison with U2-gossip [17] provides a clear performance advantage. The exploration of different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) adds to the robustness of the evaluation.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined (though potentially dense for a non-expert). Algorithm 1 and 2 are well-presented and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Analysis:** The "phantom node" approach, while clever, significantly increases the complexity of the analysis. It might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory to fully grasp the details. A more intuitive explanation or simplification could be beneficial.  The transition matrix `M(t)` and its derivation are dense and could be streamlined.
* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t / t) convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm is slower than the synchronous rate. While typical for asynchronous algorithms, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between synchronization and convergence speed would be valuable. Is the practical performance difference significant enough to warrant choosing the synchronous version when feasible?
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is largely limited to U2-gossip [17].  Comparing against other relevant decentralized algorithms (even if not specifically designed for U-statistics, such as standard distributed averaging applied in a workaround) could further strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The experiments don’t explicitly discuss the sensitivity of the algorithms to network parameters (e.g., the degree distribution). How does the performance degrade as the network becomes less connected?
* **Computational Cost:** The paper focuses on communication complexity, but doesn't delve into the computational cost per node, which could be a limiting factor in some applications.  Given the averaging involved, is it substantial?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant problem with practical relevance.
* Presents a technically sound solution with provable convergence guarantees.
* Demonstrates a clear improvement over existing approaches through both theory and experiments.
* The introduction of the “phantom node” technique is a novel and potentially useful contribution to the analysis of gossip algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The analysis is complex and may be inaccessible to some readers.
* The asynchronous convergence rate is slower than the synchronous rate.
* Limited comparison to alternative algorithms.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed computation. The proposed algorithms are well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While the analysis is complex, the clarity of the presentation and the significant improvements over existing methods outweigh this concern.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the “phantom node” technique. Consider adding a simplified illustration or analogy.
* Discuss the trade-offs between the synchronous and asynchronous algorithms in more detail.
*  Include a comparison to other relevant decentralized algorithms.
*  Investigate the sensitivity of the algorithms to network parameters.
* Briefly address the computational cost per node.
* Consider adding a discussion on the applicability of the algorithms to different data distributions (e.g., skewed data).
