## NIPS Review: Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics

**Paper Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of efficiently computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting using gossip algorithms. U-statistics, commonly used in machine learning (e.g., AUC, variance), require pairwise interactions and are thus not directly amenable to standard gossip-based averaging techniques. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, which combine data propagation and averaging steps. They provide theoretical convergence guarantees (O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous) dependent on both data characteristics and network topology.  A clever reformulation using “phantom nodes” aids the analysis.  The paper also includes experimental validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation with real datasets, demonstrating improved performance compared to a recent related work (U2-gossip).

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established literature on gossip algorithms for decentralized averaging (e.g., Boyd et al., Kempe et al.). It explicitly acknowledges the limitations of existing gossip algorithms for U-statistics and positions itself as an extension to address this gap. The primary point of comparison is U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2017], and the authors demonstrate both theoretical and empirical improvements over this work. The discussion of spectral graph theory and its connection to convergence rates is also standard in this field (Chung, 1997).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:**  Efficiently computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting is a relevant and challenging problem with applications in various areas.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of data propagation and averaging steps within the gossip framework is a novel and insightful approach.  The "phantom node" reformulation is a clever technique for analysis.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical convergence analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, with bounds incorporating both data and network characteristics.  The explicit dependence on the spectral gap is valuable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms and confirm improvements over U2-gossip.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithms are clearly described, and the notation is consistent.
* **Comparison to Baseline:** The comparison to U2-gossip is well-executed and highlights the advantages of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Analysis:** While the theoretical analysis is thorough, the introduction of "phantom nodes" and the subsequent matrix manipulations make the proof somewhat difficult to follow. More intuitive explanations would be beneficial.
* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t/t) convergence rate for the asynchronous setting is relatively slow compared to some other asynchronous averaging algorithms. While the analysis is correct, exploring potential improvements in this setting would be valuable.
* **Network Model Assumptions:** The analysis relies on assumptions about the graph connectivity (connected and non-bipartite).  Discussing the performance and potential modifications for graphs that do not meet these criteria would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While U2-gossip is a good baseline, comparing to other relevant decentralized estimation techniques (even if not specifically designed for U-statistics) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two specific U-statistics and a few network topologies. Expanding the evaluation to other U-statistics and more diverse network structures would increase the robustness of the findings.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the theoretical analysis might hinder broader adoption.
* The asynchronous convergence rate is relatively slow.
* The limited scope of the experimental evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of decentralized estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some aspects of the analysis are complex and the experimental scope could be broadened, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Simplify Analysis:** Provide more intuitive explanations for the "phantom node" reformulation and the matrix manipulations involved in the convergence proof.
* **Investigate Asynchronous Rate:** Explore potential techniques to improve the convergence rate of the asynchronous algorithm.
* **Address Network Assumptions:** Discuss the performance and potential modifications for graphs that are not connected or bipartite.
* **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Include comparisons to other relevant decentralized estimation techniques and evaluate the algorithms on a wider range of U-statistics and network topologies.



**References:**

*   Chung, F. R. K. (1997). *Spectral graph theory*. American Mathematical Society.
*   Pelckmans, K., & Suykens, J. (2017). Gossip Algorithms for Computing U-Statistics. *IFAC Workshop on Estimation and Control of Networked Systems*.
