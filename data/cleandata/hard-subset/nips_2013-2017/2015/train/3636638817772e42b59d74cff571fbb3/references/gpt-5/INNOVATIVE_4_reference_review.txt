Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. The key idea is to decompose Ûn(H) = (1/n)∑i hi with hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi, Xj), and design algorithms that (i) propagate data via random walks so each node can estimate its own hi, and (ii) average local estimates via standard gossip to drive consensus on Ûn(H). Two algorithms are proposed: GoSta-sync (global clock) and GoSta-async (local Poisson clocks), each maintaining a single auxiliary observation per node and swapping it along randomly selected edges. A novel “phantom nodes” reformulation enables analysis by lifting the dynamics to an O(n^2)-size graph purely for proofs. The authors prove convergence in expectation with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async), with constants that separate data difficulty and network spectral gap. They provide a refined bound for prior U2-gossip and show both theoretically and empirically that GoSta converges faster with lower memory/communication. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter on several graph topologies support the claims.

Relation to prior work
This work sits at the intersection of randomized gossip/consensus and decentralized computation of non-separable functions. Classic gossip handles separable sums; U2-gossip is, to my knowledge, the main prior tackling pairwise averages. The proposed combination of coupled random-walk data propagation with averaging, and the phantom-node analysis, clearly differentiates this paper from prior work. The comparisons to [2] for averaging and to [17] for U-statistics are appropriate; the refined analysis of U2-gossip is a nice addition.

Strengths
- Novelty: Extends gossip to a broad class of non-separable functionals (U-statistics) with a conceptually simple yet effective mechanism. The phantom-node proof device is elegant and potentially reusable.
- Theory: Clean convergence-in-expectation results with rates and network/data-dependent constants; connects to spectral gap. Asynchronous variant analyzed (rare in this area).
- Practicality: Requires only one auxiliary sample per node, one edge activation per iteration, and simple local updates. Asynchronous algorithm needs only degrees and |E|; no global clock.
- Empirics: Real datasets, multiple network topologies, consistent improvements over U2-gossip; sync vs async comparison suggests robustness.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees are in expectation only; no high-probability or almost-sure convergence, nor finite-time concentration across nodes. This limits robustness guarantees in stochastic/asynchronous environments.
- Constants in the async bound (c′(G)) are opaque; guidance on scaling with degree distribution and spectral gap would help practitioners.
- Assumption of known |E| at each node for pk can be unrealistic; the paper does not discuss estimating pk or robustness to misspecification.
- Bipartite graphs are excluded; while standard “lazy” modifications would fix periodicity, this is not discussed or evaluated.
- Experiments focus primarily on comparison to U2-gossip; additional baselines (e.g., random sampling approximations, push-sum variants, or centralized budget-aware baselines) are relegated to the supplement.
- Minor clarity issues in notation (e.g., boldface H as a matrix vs scalar kernel H) and some typographical glitches in the provided text.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide high-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration) on node errors or on the disagreement term, at least for the synchronous case?
- How sensitive is GoSta-async to errors in pk estimates? Can pk be learned online (e.g., via degree/edge count gossip) without biasing the updates?
- For bipartite graphs, would adding self-loops (lazy random walk) or occasional self-averaging remove the restriction without changing rates qualitatively?
- Could the approach extend to higher-order U-statistics (order m > 2) by maintaining m−1 auxiliary observations and still keep communication manageable? Any preliminary bounds?
- Can you quantify wall-clock and communication cost to reach a given error vs n and spectral gap, to complement iteration-based plots?
- It would be useful to report not just mean error but also disagreement (variance across nodes) over time, highlighting the benefit of the averaging step.
- Please clarify the definition of the matrix H in the bounds and ensure consistent notation; explicitly define norms and the construction of H = [H(Xi, Xj)].

Quality
The analysis appears sound and leverages standard spectral tools with a clever lifting trick. The async analysis is less explicit but plausible. Empirical evidence is consistent with theory. The work feels complete for a conference contribution.

Clarity
Generally well organized; algorithms are easy to follow. Some notation and minor typos could be cleaned up, and a short intuition for the O(log t/t) term in async would help.

Originality
High. Computing non-separable U-statistics via gossip with provable rates and an async variant is a clear step beyond prior art. The phantom-node framework is a neat analytical contribution.

Significance
Moderate to high. U-statistics underpin many metrics (AUC, rank correlations, clustering quality); enabling their decentralized estimation with low memory/communication is valuable. The ideas may catalyze further work on non-separable decentralized computation/optimization.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a simple, novel, and broadly useful direction with solid theory and convincing experiments. While some bounds could be strengthened (concentration, explicit constants), the conceptual advance outweighs these omissions.

Confidence
High for the algorithmic novelty and empirical interpretation; moderate for the depth of the async proof details (relied on the provided summary).
