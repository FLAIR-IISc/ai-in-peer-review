### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of computing degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network setting. Standard gossip algorithms are efficient for computing separable functions like averages but cannot be directly applied to U-statistics, which depend on pairs of data points distributed across the network. The authors propose two novel gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous), to fill this gap. The core idea is to have each node maintain a local estimate and an "auxiliary" data observation. At each iteration, a randomly chosen pair of connected nodes performs two actions: they average their local estimates, and they swap their auxiliary observations. This combined process allows for both the dissemination of data information (via the random walk of auxiliary observations) and the consensus of estimates.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of their algorithms. They introduce an innovative "phantom network" construction as a proof technique to decouple the data propagation and estimate averaging steps. They prove that the synchronous algorithm converges in expectation to the true U-statistic at a rate of O(1/t), and the asynchronous version at a rate of O(log t/t). The convergence bounds clearly show a dependency on the network's spectral gap (faster on well-connected graphs) and data-dependent terms that characterize the problem's difficulty. The proposed method is compared favorably to the main prior work in this area, U2-gossip [17], arguing for faster convergence and lower memory and communication overhead. These claims are substantiated through experiments on real-world datasets for estimating the AUC of a classifier and the within-cluster point scatter.

### Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem. Extending the scope of gossip algorithms beyond simple averages to a broad class of statistics like U-statistics is a significant contribution to the fields of decentralized computation and distributed machine learning. The proposed approach of combining estimate averaging with a random walk of data observations is elegant and novel. It represents a clear and substantial improvement over the only known prior work [17].

2.  **Excellent Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical analysis is a highlight, particularly the introduction of the "phantom network" as an analytical tool. This is a creative and effective technique that may be of independent interest. The derived convergence rates are explicit and provide valuable insights, correctly identifying the roles of network connectivity and data variance in the algorithm's performance. The analysis also provides a principled explanation for why GoSta should outperform U2-gossip by avoiding a detrimental `sqrt(n)` factor in the convergence bound.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the problem is formally defined, and the proposed algorithms are presented with clarity. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and the high-level proof strategy is well-explained in the main text. The distinction and comparison with related work are sharp and convincing.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and effectively support the paper's theoretical claims. The use of two practical U-statistics (AUC and within-cluster scatter) on real datasets demonstrates the applicability of the method. The choice of different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) convincingly illustrates the impact of network connectivity on convergence speed, corroborating the theory. The direct comparison with U2-gossip clearly shows the superiority of the proposed GoSta algorithm in practice.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a very strong paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Asynchronous Algorithm's Practicality:** Algorithm 2 requires each node `k` to know `pk = 2dk/|E|`. While the node's own degree `dk` is local information, the total number of edges `|E|` is global network information. The paper does not discuss how nodes would acquire this value in a fully decentralized setting. This could be accomplished with a preliminary consensus/averaging algorithm, but this adds a non-trivial overhead and complexity that should be acknowledged. A brief discussion of this practical requirement in the main text would make the presentation more complete.

2.  **Gap between Theory and Practice for Asynchronous Rate:** The theoretical rate for GoSta-async (O(log t/t)) is slightly worse than for GoSta-sync (O(1/t)). However, Figure 3b shows their practical performance to be nearly identical. This suggests the asynchronous analysis might not be perfectly tight. It would be valuable for the authors to comment on this gap. Is it an artifact of the proof technique, or are there specific conditions under which the logarithmic factor vanishes?

3.  **Clarification on U2-gossip Rate Analysis:** The paper presents a convergence rate for U2-gossip in Equation (10) and refers to it as a "refined rate" obtained by "applying our convergence analysis." This suggests it is a novel contribution of this paper. If this is the case, it should be stated more explicitly as a secondary contribution. This analysis strengthens the paper's argument but its origin should be unambiguous to the reader.

4.  **Potential for Broader Impact:** The work focuses on degree-two U-statistics. A natural question is whether the core idea of propagating auxiliary observations can be extended to higher-order U-statistics (which depend on triplets, quadruplets, etc., of observations). While this is likely beyond the scope of this paper to solve completely, a short discussion in the conclusion on the potential challenges (e.g., managing the communication of `k-1` auxiliary variables) and future directions would enhance the paper's forward-looking perspective.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the literature on distributed algorithms. It addresses a relevant problem, proposes an elegant and effective solution, supports it with rigorous theory, and validates it with convincing experiments. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are mostly points for clarification. The work is of high quality and is very likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
