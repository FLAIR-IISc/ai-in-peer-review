Summary
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. It proposes two randomized algorithms: (i) a synchronous method (GoSta-sync) where each node maintains one auxiliary observation that performs a random walk and nodes average their estimates; and (ii) an asynchronous variant (GoSta-async) that uses local Poisson clocks and an importance-weighted running average to address unequal activation frequencies. The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from estimate averaging and proves convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic at rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous), with constants depending on data dispersion and the graph spectral gap. The methods are positioned as improvements over U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens ’10], with lower memory/communication per iteration and improved finite-time bounds. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter on several graph topologies show lower error and variance than U2-gossip.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Computing pairwise quantities arises in AUC, correlation, and clustering quality; tackling them in decentralized settings is meaningful.
- Conceptual simplicity: Combining data propagation via swaps with averaging is natural and easy to implement; requiring only one auxiliary observation per node is appealing.
- Theoretical guarantees: Clear expected convergence rates with interpretable dependence on the spectral gap and data-dependent terms. The phantom-node analysis is neat and may be of independent interest.
- Asynchronous algorithm: Addresses the more realistic case where there is no global clock.
- Empirical results: On two real datasets across multiple network topologies, the proposed method consistently outperforms U2-gossip.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modest novelty vs. [17]: The main novelty is averaging plus a refined analysis. The setting, random-walk data propagation, and U-statistic target are not new. The improvement seems incremental and largely about constants, while rates remain O(1/t) (sync) and sublinear (async).
- Convergence only in expectation: No almost-sure or high-probability bounds are provided, even though such guarantees are standard in gossip/consensus literature. This limits robustness claims, especially given the variance introduced by importance weighting in the asynchronous version.
- Asynchronous assumptions: GoSta-async requires each node to know pk = 2dk/|E| and to increment mk by 1/pk when activated. Knowing |E| and exact dk at each node is a strong assumption in decentralized settings; the paper does not discuss how to estimate these reliably, nor the impact of errors. The constant c′(G) is left unspecified.
- Communication and memory costs per accuracy not quantified: Although per-iteration costs are discussed (one swap and one averaging vs. two in U2), the paper does not provide message/byte complexity to reach ε-accuracy, which is essential for practical assessment. Swapping an entire d-dimensional observation can be expensive for large d; there is no analysis of the accuracy-communication trade-off or compression.
- Coordination and activation model: Both algorithms assume uniform-at-random edge activations. Implementing uniform edge selection without centralized coordination is nontrivial. The analysis does not cover more realistic nonuniform activation matrices P (besides a brief mention) or time-varying graphs.
- Limited empirical validation: Experiments focus on relative error vs. iterations, not wall-clock or message counts; they do not test robustness to message loss/asynchrony skew; the comparison set is limited (primarily U2-gossip). Practical baselines such as sub-sampling/sketching-based decentralized estimators of U-statistics, or push-sum style protocols with lazy updates, are missing.
- Bipartite graphs and laziness: The analysis excludes bipartite graphs; adding laziness is a standard fix but is not discussed empirically or analyzed in detail.
- Bound tightness and constants: For the synchronous case, the rate scales with 1/(1−λ2(2)) which is 1/n on complete graphs, implying n/t behavior; this can be pessimistic. For the asynchronous case, the O(log t / t) rate with a hidden c′(G) and only ‖H‖ is likely loose. Practical convergence appears better than the bound but theory does not capture this.

Detailed questions/suggestions
- Provide high-probability and/or almost sure convergence guarantees, at least under standard independence assumptions on edge selections.
- Make c′(G) explicit and extend the analysis to nonuniform activation (general stochastic P) and time-varying graphs; discuss how to handle bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy random walks) and the impact on rates.
- Discuss how nodes can obtain or estimate |E| and degrees for GoSta-async; analyze sensitivity to misspecification of pk and the variance introduced by 1/pk weighting.
- Report communication complexity to reach a target ε (number of edge activations and total bytes transmitted), especially as a function of d and spectral gap. Consider compression or quantization to mitigate cost of swapping high-dimensional observations.
- Compare against additional baselines: decentralized sub-sampling/mini-batch estimators of U-statistics, push-sum variants, and simple random-walk sampling schemes that estimate h locally followed by averaging.
- Clarify whether consensus (agreement across nodes) holds almost surely, not only in expectation. The plots show reduced variance but a theoretical statement would strengthen the claim.
- Expand empirical evaluation: include time-varying graphs, message drops, degree/edge-count estimation errors, and wall-clock measurements.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The phantom-node construction is explained carefully. Some steps (proofs, constants, asynchronous details) are deferred to the supplement—bringing key lemmas and explicit constants into the main text would help.

Reproducibility
Algorithms are described precisely; however, lacking details on how edge activations are implemented and how pk, |E| are obtained may hinder reproduction in realistic settings. Code/data availability is not stated.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant decentralized estimation problem with clean algorithms.
- Provides theoretical rates with interpretable dependence on graph structure.
- Asynchronous version and improved constants vs. prior U2-gossip.
- Empirical gains on real datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental contribution over [17] with limited novelty.
- Only expectation guarantees; missing high-probability/as convergence and robustness.
- Unrealistic assumptions for asynchronous implementation (knowledge of |E|, pk).
- Lacks communication/byte complexity vs. accuracy; limited experimental breadth.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper is solid and promising but, given NeurIPS standards and my preference for thoroughly validated advances, the novelty is incremental and the evidence (theory and experiments) falls short on robustness and practicality. Strengthening theoretical guarantees (beyond expectation), relaxing implementation assumptions, and providing communication-accuracy trade-offs and broader baselines would significantly improve the case.

Confidence
High.
