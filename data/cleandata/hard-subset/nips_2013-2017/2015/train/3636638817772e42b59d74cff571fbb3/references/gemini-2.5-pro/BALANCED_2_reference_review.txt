This paper presents novel randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, for the distributed estimation of degree-two U-statistics over a network. U-statistics are a broad class of quantities, such as sample variance and Area Under the Curve (AUC), that are defined by an average over pairs of data points. This pairwise dependency makes them non-separable and thus challenging to compute with standard gossip algorithms, which are typically designed for separable sums (e.g., sample means).

The proposed approach cleverly combines two iterative processes: 1) a data propagation step, where each node's original data point is passed around the network via random swaps of an "auxiliary observation" with a neighbor, and 2) an estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic with their neighbors. The authors present both synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) versions of their algorithm. This work builds upon and aims to improve the only known prior work on this problem by Pelckmans & Suykens [17] (termed U2-gossip).

The theoretical contribution consists of convergence proofs for both algorithms. Using an elegant "phantom network" construction for the analysis, the authors show that the local estimates converge in expectation to the true U-statistic at a rate of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t / t) for the asynchronous one. The convergence bounds insightfully depend on both data-dependent terms and the spectral gap of the network graph. The paper empirically validates the superiority of its approach over U2-gossip on AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation tasks using real-world datasets and various network topologies.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem in decentralized machine learning. U-statistics are fundamental, and providing an efficient, fully decentralized estimation algorithm is a valuable contribution. The proposed method of combining data swapping with explicit estimate averaging is a novel and effective improvement over the prior state-of-the-art [17], which relied solely on data swapping. The paper not only proposes a better algorithm but also provides a refined theoretical analysis of the prior work to clearly contextualize its improvements.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical analysis is a highlight, particularly the introduction of the "phantom network" concept as a tool to decouple the data propagation and averaging dynamics. This is a creative and effective proof technique that may be of independent interest. The derived convergence rates are compelling and the dependence on the graph's spectral gap aligns well with intuition and the broader literature on gossip algorithms.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the algorithms are presented concisely, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented in a clear manner that strongly supports the paper's claims. The authors do an excellent job of explaining a complex system in an accessible way.
*   **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete piece of research. It identifies a problem, proposes novel synchronous and asynchronous solutions, provides strong theoretical guarantees, and validates them with thorough experiments. The comparison to the most relevant baseline is direct and convincing.

### Weaknesses

*   **Practicality of Assumptions in the Asynchronous Setting:** The asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) requires each node `k` to know its activation probability `pk = 2dk/|E|`. While a node can easily know its own degree `dk`, the total number of edges `|E|` is a global property of the network. In a truly decentralized setting where nodes lack global topology information, obtaining `|E|` would require a preliminary consensus/averaging protocol, adding complexity and overhead. This assumption, while not uncommon in the literature, is a practical limitation that is not discussed in the paper.
*   **Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence analysis is for the expectation of the estimates. While this is a standard and valuable first step, it does not characterize the variance or provide concentration bounds around the mean. A high-probability bound would offer stronger guarantees on the behavior of a single run of the algorithm.
*   **Minor Lack of Intuition:** The update rule for the local estimate `Zk` in the asynchronous algorithm (lines 6-7 of Algorithm 2) is presented without much intuition. While formally derived from the need for an unbiased running average, a brief explanation of why the `(1 - 1/(pk*mk))` weighting is necessary would improve the paper's pedagogical quality.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  The paper provides a significant advance on an important problem in decentralized learning.
2.  The proposed algorithms are novel, efficient, and demonstrably superior to the previous state-of-the-art in terms of performance, memory, and communication costs.
3.  The theoretical analysis is strong and introduces an elegant and potentially reusable proof technique.
4.  The paper is very clearly written and the empirical validation is thorough and convincing.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  The practicality of the asynchronous algorithm is limited by the assumption that nodes have access to global network information (`|E|`).
2.  The theoretical analysis is confined to convergence in expectation, which is a weaker guarantee than a high-probability bound.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a high-quality contribution that advances the field of distributed machine learning.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

Even though I recommend acceptance, the following suggestions could further strengthen the manuscript:

*   **Discuss the `|E|` assumption:** Please add a brief discussion in Section 4.2 acknowledging that knowledge of `|E|` is a non-trivial requirement in a decentralized setting. You could mention that it can be estimated using a standard averaging gossip algorithm, but that this would constitute an initialization phase.
*   **Acknowledge analysis limitations:** In the conclusion or alongside the theorems, it would be good to briefly mention that the analysis is in expectation and that deriving high-probability bounds is a valuable direction for future work.
*   **Clarify Remark 2:** The remark about extending the method to nodes with multiple observations is intriguing but very brief. If space permits, elaborating slightly on how edge weights would be chosen in the "split node" model would make the claim more concrete.
*   **Provide intuition for Algorithm 2:** A short sentence explaining the update in lines 6-7 of Algorithm 2 would be helpful. For example: "Here, `mk` serves as an unbiased estimate of the number of times node `k` *should have* updated its estimate by time `t`, and the `1/pk` factor corrects for the non-uniformity of node selection, ensuring the running average remains unbiased."
