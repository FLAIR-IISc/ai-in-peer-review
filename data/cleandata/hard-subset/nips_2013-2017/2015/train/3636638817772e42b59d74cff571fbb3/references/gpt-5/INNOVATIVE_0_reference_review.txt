Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of pairwise quantities (degree-2 U-statistics) over networks via gossip, a setting where classic gossip excels only for separable sums. The authors propose two algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node i holds one datum Xi, maintains an auxiliary observation Yi that performs a random walk via edge swaps, and a running local estimate Zi. GoSta-sync alternates between (i) per-node running averaging with H(Xi, Yi) and (ii) edge-based averaging of Zi and swapping Yi. GoSta-async adapts this to an edge-activation model with local counters to correct for nonuniform activation, requiring pk=2dk/|E|. Theoretical results show convergence in expectation to the U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t / t) (async), and with constants depending on data dispersion and the spectral gap. A key analytical device is a “phantom node” construction that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several network topologies show faster convergence and lower variance than prior U2-gossip, with lower memory/communication per step.

Relation to prior work
The study builds on randomized gossip for averaging (Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al.; related spectral gap analyses) and addresses a known limitation: pairwise statistics. The closest prior work is U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), which propagates two auxiliary samples and updates all nodes each iteration. The paper improves on U2-gossip by introducing averaging among estimates, using a single auxiliary observation per node, supporting asynchrony, and providing refined rate comparisons (U2-gossip bound includes a √n factor). The phantom-node analysis technique appears original in this context.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual advance: Turning gossip into an efficient estimator for U-statistics is a meaningful step; the phantom-node reformulation is an elegant analytical tool that could be reused for other coupled gossip processes.
- Asynchronous algorithm: Fully asynchronous version with provable convergence and reasonable rate (O(log t/t)) is important for practical deployments.
- Theory with topology- and data-dependent constants: The bounds explicitly separate spectral gap (network effect) from data dispersion, offering insight into when/where the method is fast.
- Practical efficiency: One auxiliary observation and one edge activation per iteration reduces memory/communication vs U2-gossip. Averaging estimates lowers across-node variance.
- Empirical validation: Demonstrates consistent gains over U2-gossip for AUC and within-cluster scatter, and shows dependence on topology.

Weaknesses and questions
- Expectation-only guarantees: Convergence is in expectation; there are no high-probability or almost-sure guarantees, nor finite-sample concentration bounds. This limits reliability in time-limited settings.
- Asynchrony assumptions and required knowledge: GoSta-async assumes edge activations are uniform and each node knows pk=2dk/|E| (hence |E|). In many decentralized settings, nodes do not know |E| or cannot guarantee uniform edge sampling. Can the method be adapted to node-initiated activations (à la Boyd et al.) with only local degree knowledge, or to estimate pk online?
- Communication model realism: Uniform edge sampling effectively requires a global scheduler or extra coordination. Clarify how to implement this with local Poisson clocks choosing neighbors.
- Constants in rates: c(G) and c′(G) are not fully explicit in the async case; tighter or explicit constants would help gauge practicality. Also, the O(1/t) sync bound contains both 1/ct and exp(−ct) terms—clarify crossover and typical magnitude for common graphs.
- Scope of experiments: Experiments are limited to two tasks and modest network sizes. Include larger graphs, other U-statistics (e.g., Kendall tau), and more varied topologies, and provide wall-clock or message-complexity vs error to complement iteration plots.
- Objective definition: The target statistic is 1/n^2 sum including i=j terms with H(X,X)=0, which matches the algorithmic convenience but differs from the classical unbiased U-statistic by a factor n/(n−1). Discuss implications when H(X,X)≠0 or when strictly unbiasedness is required.
- Robustness and practicalities: High-dimensional Yi swaps cost O(d) per activation; discuss compression/sketching. Also, the proofs are deferred; some key lemmas (e.g., bounding the mixed term with Wα) deserve more intuition in the main text.

Clarity and presentation
Generally clear and well organized. The phantom-node construction is a nice explanatory device. Suggestions:
- Clearly define the matrix H (pairwise values) early, to avoid overloading the symbol H for both kernel and its matrix.
- Summarize the main steps of the proof in the main paper (beyond referring to the supplement), especially how block-triangular dynamics yield the rate.
- Explicitly state communication/memory per iteration and contrast with U2-gossip in a small table.
- Expand on how pk can be known or estimated in practice for GoSta-async.

Significance and impact
The work addresses a relevant and underexplored gap: decentralized estimation of pairwise statistics. Many learning objectives (AUC, rank correlations, cluster dispersion, two-sample tests) are U-statistics; providing efficient gossip-based estimators is valuable for sensor networks, federated settings, and graph analytics. The phantom-node technique and the asynchronous scheme could inspire extensions to higher-order U-statistics and to decentralized optimization over pairwise objectives.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear conceptual advance on a fundamental decentralized computation problem.
- Asynchronous algorithm with theory.
- Better memory/communication and empirical performance than prior art.
- Analysis with insightful dependence on spectral gap and data.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing stronger probabilistic guarantees and explicit constants for async.
- Practical assumptions (uniform edge sampling, knowledge of |E|) may be restrictive.
- Experimental scope is somewhat limited.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). The paper opens a promising direction with solid ideas and evidence; the novelty and potential impact outweigh the moderate limitations, which can be addressed in revision.

Reviewer confidence
High on the algorithmic and gossip literature; medium on all technical proof details since core proofs are in the supplement.
