This paper tackles the challenging problem of extending gossip algorithms to estimate U-statistics, a class of functions crucial in various machine learning applications but intractable with standard gossip-based approaches. The core idea of using “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem and enable propagation of information is genuinely novel and represents a significant conceptual leap. The authors demonstrate convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, with bounds that appropriately reflect the problem’s difficulty and network connectivity. This is a bold and ambitious undertaking, and the success in achieving even partial results is commendable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics is a novel contribution. The phantom node approach is particularly clever and allows leveraging existing gossip algorithm analyses.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis is well-developed and provides informative bounds incorporating data and network characteristics.
*   **Practical Relevance:** U-statistics appear in a wide range of applications, making this work potentially impactful.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrate the practical viability of the proposed algorithms and show improvement over existing methods (specifically U2-gossip).
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Phantom Node Complexity:** While conceptually elegant, the phantom node construction introduces a level of abstraction that may hinder practical implementation and understanding. The computational cost of maintaining and updating these phantom nodes isn’t fully explored in terms of overhead.
*   **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t/t) convergence rate in the asynchronous setting is slower than the synchronous rate. While understandable, a deeper investigation into strategies for improving asynchronous convergence could be valuable.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While a comparison to U2-gossip is provided, a broader comparison to other relevant distributed estimation techniques (even if not directly applicable to U-statistics) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Network Model:** The analysis assumes undirected graphs. Exploring the extension to directed graphs, common in many real-world scenarios, would increase the applicability of the work.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two applications and a few network topologies. A more extensive empirical evaluation across diverse datasets and network structures would bolster confidence in the robustness of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding distributed U-statistic estimation.
*   The novel phantom node approach is a clever and potentially impactful contribution.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical feasibility and performance benefits of the approach.
*   The paper’s boldness and originality outweigh the minor methodological shortcomings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The phantom node construction adds complexity and may hinder practical implementation.
*   The asynchronous convergence rate is relatively slow.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The novelty of the approach, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis and promising experimental results, outweighs the minor weaknesses. The authors have tackled a challenging problem with a creative and well-justified solution. While further refinement and exploration are warranted, this work has the potential to open up new avenues for research in decentralized machine learning.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I recommend accepting this paper, recognizing its originality and potential impact. I encourage the authors to address the reviewer comments regarding the complexity of the phantom node construction and the asynchronous convergence rate in future work. Further expansion of the experimental evaluation would also be beneficial.
