Summary
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. The authors propose two simple randomized algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node holds a single observation Xi, maintains an auxiliary observation Yk that is swapped along edges to propagate data, and a local estimate Zk that is averaged both temporally (running average) and spatially (pairwise gossip averaging). The key technical contribution is an analysis via a “phantom nodes” construction that decouples averaging and data propagation. The authors prove convergence in expectation to the true U-statistic: O(1/t) for the synchronous algorithm with explicit data- and graph-dependent constants (involving the spectral gap of G), and O((log t)/t) for the asynchronous algorithm. They compare against the prior U2-gossip method, deriving a refined bound for it that includes an unfavorable √n factor, and demonstrate lower memory/communication and faster convergence. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over three network types corroborate the theory.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on randomized gossip for averaging (e.g., Boyd et al., 2006) and the use of spectral gap to quantify rates.
- Extends beyond separable aggregates to pairwise U-statistics, improving on the earlier U2-gossip approach (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010) in both rate and resource use.
- Connects to decentralized optimization literature (Nedić & Ozdaglar; Duchi et al.) as a potential building block when objectives involve pairwise terms.
- The “phantom network” analysis resembles product/lifted Markov chain techniques but is applied here in a novel way to track coupled data propagation within a gossip process.

Quality
- The synchronous analysis is technically sound and insightful. The decomposition via Wα(G) and the spectral-gap dependence are standard but well adapted to this setting. The bound features intuitive data-dispersion terms and a network term c(G)=1−λ2(W2).
- The asynchronous algorithm is reasonable and easy to implement; the proof is deferred but the resulting O((log t)/t) rate is plausible. However, the constant c′(G) is not made explicit and the bound is looser and lacks the useful data-dependent decomposition of the synchronous case.
- Convergence is established in expectation only. There are no high-probability or almost sure results, and no finite-time MSE bounds; variance across runs is assessed empirically only.
- Minor technical concern: the paper states 1−λ2(W2) = 1/n for the complete graph; using W2 = I − (1/|E|)L(Kn) with βn−1 = n and |E| = n(n−1)/2 gives 1−λ2(W2) = n/|E| = 2/(n−1), not 1/n. Please clarify.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with a clean problem setup and algorithms. The phantom-node construction is explained well and helps intuition.
- Some proofs are deferred; that’s fine for NIPS but key steps (e.g., the origin of the exponential term in Thm. 1) could be sketched.
- A few typos/formatting issues and incomplete references. Please proofread and complete citations.
- The assumptions (symmetric H, H(X,X)=0) and their necessity should be discussed more explicitly.

Originality
- The problem (distributed estimation of U-statistics) is important and underexplored; improving both complexity and convergence over U2-gossip is a clear contribution.
- The combination of pairwise averaging with single-auxiliary data propagation and the phantom-node analysis appear novel for this task.

Significance
- U-statistics underpin many useful metrics (AUC, Kendall tau, scatter). A decentralized estimator with simple local memory/communication and provable rates is valuable for sensor and P2P networks.
- The spectral-gap dependence gives actionable insights for network design.
- The method is a plausible building block for decentralized optimization with pairwise losses.

Strengths
- Simple, practical algorithms; lower memory and communication than U2-gossip.
- Clear theoretical guarantees with graph- and data-dependent constants (sync), and an asynchronous variant.
- Novel analysis technique (phantom nodes).
- Empirical validation on two tasks and multiple graph families; results match theory and show variance reduction due to averaging.

Weaknesses and questions
- Expectation-only bounds; no high-probability/MSE guarantees. Can you provide concentration or almost-sure convergence, at least under bounded H?
- Asynchronous bound: c′(G) unspecified and no data-dependent terms; can you express c′(G) via spectral quantities and separate data/network effects as in Thm. 1?
- Bipartite graphs: Theorems assume non-bipartite graphs, yet the 2D grid (used in experiments) is bipartite. Please reconcile this (e.g., by adding laziness/self-loops or showing theorems extend).
- Global knowledge: GoSta-async requires pk=2dk/|E|; knowing |E| may be unrealistic. Can pk be estimated online, or can Metropolis weights/push-sum remove this requirement?
- Communication metric: U2-gossip swaps two d-dimensional vectors per iteration; GoSta swaps one. Consider reporting error versus total transmitted words rather than iterations.
- Scope of experiments: Only two datasets; more diversity (e.g., varying d, sparser/path graphs, scale-free networks, varying Watts–Strogatz p) would strengthen claims.
- Minor: clarify the complete-graph spectral constant; correct typos; complete references.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant, underexplored problem with practical algorithms and improved guarantees.
- Solid analysis with insightful spectral-gap dependence; asynchronous variant included.
- Empirical results substantiate theory and resource advantages.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of high-probability/variance bounds and incomplete constants in async rate.
- Assumption-versus-experiment inconsistency on bipartiteness.
- Limited experimental breadth and communication-normalized comparisons.
- Minor technical inconsistency on spectral constant.

Recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper makes a clear, novel, and useful contribution with sound theory and convincing experiments. Addressing the bipartite-graph assumption, clarifying constants, and adding either a concentration result or a communication-normalized comparison would strengthen the final version.
