This paper introduces randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. The core idea is to combine propagation of local observations with averaging of local estimates, using a “phantom node” reformulation for analysis. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous versions, and validate their approach with experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs appear rigorous, although detailed verification would require significant effort. The convergence analysis, particularly the use of phantom nodes, is a clever technique to tackle the problem. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential extensions. The paper is a complete piece of work, presenting a well-defined problem, proposed algorithms, theoretical analysis, and experimental validation. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms is a strength. The careful consideration of data-dependent and network-dependent terms in the convergence bounds is commendable.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and notations. However, some sections, particularly the convergence analysis, are dense and require careful reading.  Algorithm 1 and 2 are clearly presented. The motivation for the phantom nodes could be explained more intuitively, and the connection between the phantom nodes and the actual data propagation in the network could be visually clarified. While the paper references related work, a more detailed explanation of *why* previous gossip algorithms are unsuitable for U-statistics early in the paper would strengthen the motivation.  The use of mathematical notation is consistent, but could benefit from more descriptive variable names in some places (e.g., 'mk' in the asynchronous algorithm).

**Originality:** The problem of extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is novel. While gossip algorithms are well-studied for simpler averaging tasks, adapting them to handle the pairwise dependencies in U-statistics requires a non-trivial approach. The “phantom node” technique and the resulting convergence analysis appear to be original contributions. The paper correctly positions its work with respect to [17], and claims improvements in convergence, memory, and communication costs.  The exploration of both synchronous and asynchronous variants adds to the originality.

**Significance:** The ability to efficiently compute U-statistics in a distributed setting has potential applications in various domains, including machine learning (AUC, Gini coefficient), statistics (variance), and data mining. The convergence rates, which depend on both data and network characteristics, provide valuable insights into the algorithm’s performance.  The experimental results, while limited to two specific applications, demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. The improved convergence and reduced communication costs compared to [17] represent a tangible advancement. The practical relevance of the algorithms, coupled with the theoretical guarantees, suggests that this work could be impactful for researchers and practitioners working with decentralized data.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel problem and solution: extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics.
*   Solid theoretical foundation: rigorous convergence analysis with informative bounds.
*   Practical relevance: potential applications in distributed machine learning and statistics.
*   Clear presentation of algorithms and experimental results.
*   Improvements over existing work ([17]) in terms of convergence, memory, and communication.
*   Consideration of both synchronous and asynchronous settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The convergence analysis is dense and potentially difficult for some readers to fully grasp.
*   The experimental validation is limited to two applications; broader evaluation would strengthen the claims.
*   The connection between the phantom nodes and the actual data propagation could be made more intuitive.
*   A more detailed justification for the limitations of existing gossip algorithms for U-statistics early in the paper would improve the motivation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed computation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and practically relevant. While there are some minor areas for improvement in terms of clarity and experimental validation, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
