Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses decentralized computation of degree-2 U-statistics in networks via randomized gossip. Standard gossip efficiently averages separable functions, but not pairwise functions like U-statistics. The authors propose GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous) algorithms that combine: (i) propagation of raw observations via random edge swaps (random walks of auxiliary tokens), and (ii) averaging of local estimates on the communicating pair. A key analytic device is a “phantom network” of size O(n^2) that decouples averaging and data propagation, yielding expected convergence rates O(1/t) (sync) and O((log t)/t) (async). Bounds depend on network connectivity through the spectral gap and on problem difficulty through data-dependent norms. Compared to U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens, IFAC 2010), GoSta requires one auxiliary observation and one edge update per iteration (vs. two), and the authors derive a refined U2-gossip bound showing an extra √n factor, predicting slower convergence for U2. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across three graph families support the claims.

This work is well-positioned within the randomized gossip literature (Boyd et al.; Kempe et al.; Dimakis et al.) and identifies a gap: efficient decentralized computation of pairwise statistics. The phantom-node analysis is reminiscent of product/lifted Markov chains and block operator analyses in consensus, but (to my knowledge) is novel in this context.

Strengths
- Clear, simple algorithms with low per-iteration communication and memory: one edge activation and a single auxiliary token per node.
- Solid theory: convergence in expectation with explicit dependence on the spectral gap (1 − λ2), and informative data-dependent terms. The synchronous bound shows a clean O(1/t) rate; the asynchronous bound O((log t)/t) is standard given random asynchronous activations.
- Conceptual contribution: the phantom-network reformulation to separate averaging and data mixing is elegant and may be reusable.
- Fair and constructive comparison to U2-gossip, including a new (tighter) rate bound and a discussion of why averaging helps (eliminating the √n penalty).
- Empirical validation on two representative U-statistics and multiple graphs, showing consistent improvements and the predicted sensitivity to connectivity.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity of norms and constants: The paper defines ‖·‖ as the ℓ2 vector norm but then applies it to matrices H and H − h1ᵀ without specifying whether this is the spectral or Frobenius norm. Since the bounds mix vector and matrix terms, the norm choice and its implications for constants should be made explicit.
- Assumptions and remedies: All results assume connected, non-bipartite graphs. In practice, bipartite graphs are common (e.g., grids). A standard fix is to add laziness/self-loops; the paper should state whether laziness in the edge selection (or α ≠ 1,2) suffices to handle bipartite graphs and how this changes constants.
- Asynchronous update rule and unbiasedness: Algorithm 2 increments mk by 1/pk when picked and uses step-size 1/(pk mk). Since pk mk equals the count of activations, this is an online average over the number of updates S. The text’s statement “E[mk(t)] = t, hence unbiased” is a bit hand-wavy; the proof likely shows that the expectation over the coupled random walks and activation process yields the correct target, but the intuition in the main text could be clarified (it is not averaging over global iterations but over local activations).
- Communication/computation trade-offs: Synchronous GoSta recomputes H(Xp, Yp) at all nodes every iteration, even when Yp has not changed. This is fine theoretically (it implements a time average) but may be wasteful in high-dimensional or expensive H. An incremental variant that updates only at endpoints of the chosen edge (with adjusted weighting) could reduce computation and deserves discussion.
- Knowledge of global quantities: GoSta-async uses pk = 2dk/|E|, requiring each node to know |E|. This can be unrealistic; the paper should discuss robustness if nodes only know dk (e.g., can push-sum or degree-normalized weights be used to eliminate |E|?), or reference distributed estimation of |E|.
- Experimental scope: Only U2-gossip is compared. Baselines such as (i) naive two-phase approaches (random-walk sampling of pairs + local averaging), (ii) push-sum/ratio consensus variants (for directed/unbalanced selection), or (iii) sketch-based approximations for certain H (e.g., AUC via sampling) would strengthen the empirical case. Also, include performance on graphs with very small spectral gap (e.g., paths), and report communication/computation per unit accuracy.
- Minor: Privacy/security implications of swapping raw observations are not discussed; in some applications, exchanging H(Xi, Yj) evaluations or sketches might be preferable.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The operator derivation using Wα(G) is convincing; the rate statements match known mixing/ergodic arguments. The asynchronous analysis is only summarized; given the nontrivial time-varying transition matrix, it would help to surface a bit more proof intuition in the main text. Empirics are consistent but could be broader and include wall-clock or message complexity.

Clarity
Well written and organized; the phantom network section is clear. Improve precision around norms, the bipartite assumption, and the intuition behind Algorithm 2’s weighting. Fix minor typos (e.g., “lo- cal”, “perfomance”, “rate 1 Poisson process” → “rate-1 Poisson process”).

Originality
Extends gossip to U-statistics with a new analysis technique and improved rates/efficiency vs prior U2-gossip. To my knowledge, this is a novel and meaningful contribution.

Significance
Decentralized estimation of pairwise statistics is important (AUC, clustering, rank correlations). The approach is simple enough to be adopted and the analysis gives actionable guidance (spectral gap matters). The phantom-node perspective may inspire further decentralized algorithms for higher-order statistics and learning.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you handle bipartite graphs by introducing laziness (self-loops)? How do bounds change?
- Can GoSta be adapted to directed or time-varying graphs via push-sum?
- Can you reduce redundant H evaluations in synchronous GoSta (e.g., endpoint-only updates with corrected weights)?
- Please clarify the norm used for matrix terms in bounds, and whether constants depend on Frobenius vs spectral norms.
- How sensitive is GoSta-async to misspecification of |E|?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful and under-explored problem with a simple, practical algorithm.
- Strong, interpretable theoretical guarantees with novel analysis.
- Clear improvements over best prior (U2-gossip) in both theory and practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical baselines and scope.
- Some practical assumptions (knowledge of |E|, exchanging raw observations) not fully addressed.
- Minor clarity issues around norms and asynchronous intuition.
