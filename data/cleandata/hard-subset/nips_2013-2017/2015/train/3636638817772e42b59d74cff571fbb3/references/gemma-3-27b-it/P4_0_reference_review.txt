This paper introduces randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are notoriously difficult to compute efficiently with standard gossip algorithms designed for separable functions. The authors address this limitation by proposing algorithms leveraging “phantom nodes” and iterative information exchange, achieving convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous versions, respectively.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, related work, proposed algorithms, convergence analysis, and experimental results. The motivation for extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is well-articulated, highlighting the prevalence of these statistics in various machine learning and data mining applications (variance, AUC, Gini mean difference, etc.). The connection to existing work on gossip algorithms is thoroughly established, and the limitations of previous approaches (specifically [17]) are clearly outlined.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is a significant contribution. The use of "phantom nodes" as a reformulation technique is clever and facilitates the analysis.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, with clear proofs provided (although detailed proofs are in the supplementary material). The convergence rates are explicitly stated and depend on both data-dependent and network-dependent terms, offering valuable insights into algorithm performance. The careful analysis of the synchronous and asynchronous settings adds to the technical depth.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The algorithms are described in a clear and concise manner, and the notation is consistent. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the proposed solution.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms compared to the baseline U2-gossip algorithm [17]. The comparison across different network topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts-Strogatz) strengthens the findings.
*   **Thorough Comparison:** The detailed comparison against U2-gossip, including an analysis of the convergence rates and an explanation of the performance differences, is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Phantom Node Complexity:** While the "phantom node" approach is innovative, the reliance on a separate graph G̃ for analysis, despite the algorithm operating on the original graph G, introduces some conceptual overhead.  The connection between the phantom graph and the actual computation could be made more intuitive.
*   **Asynchronous Rate:** The O(log t/t) convergence rate in the asynchronous setting is slower than the O(1/t) rate in the synchronous setting. While this is typical for asynchronous algorithms, the practical implications of this difference in performance are not thoroughly discussed.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on comparing against U2-gossip [17]. Including comparisons with other relevant distributed estimation algorithms (even if they don't directly address U-statistics) could further strengthen the results.
*   **Network Topology Impact:** The paper demonstrates the influence of network topology on convergence rates, but the experiments primarily focus on three specific topologies. A more systematic exploration of different network structures and their impact on performance would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in distributed computation with a novel and technically sound solution.
*   The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The "phantom node" approach introduces some conceptual complexity.
*   The asynchronous convergence rate is slower than the synchronous rate.
*   The experimental evaluation could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other relevant algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed computation. The proposed algorithms are novel, technically sound, and experimentally validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section (particularly clarifying the role of phantom nodes and exploring more baseline methods).
