

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a gap in the literature where standard gossip algorithms cannot efficiently compute quantities that depend on pairs of observations. The authors introduce a "phantom network" technique for convergence analysis and claim superior performance over existing U2-gossip algorithm [17]. The paper is well-organized and presents a theoretically sound approach.

**Strengths:** The paper identifies a meaningful problem in distributed estimation and provides a novel solution using gossip algorithms. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with convergence rates O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings. The experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation demonstrate practical advantages of the proposed algorithms over U2-gossip. The paper is well-written with clear notation and logical flow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The paper contains multiple inconsistent formatting issues that undermine professionalism. "U -statistics" appears with a space before the hyphen throughout (e.g., abstract, introduction), while it should be "U-statistics" without space. In equation (1), the expression "1 n2" should be "1/n²". In equation (3), the denominator has inconsistent spacing and uses "−" instead of "-" for negative signs. Algorithm 1, step 4 contains "t−1t" which should be "(t-1)/t".

2. **Technical Ambiguities:** The phantom network construction (Section 4.1) is confusingly defined. The notation "Vk = {vki; 1 ≤ i ≤ n}" and "vkk" creates ambiguity about how phantom nodes are indexed. The description of M2(t) as "a block diagonal matrix" contradicts its explicit definition, which is not block diagonal. The spectral gap definition is incorrect: "βn−1" should be "β₂" (the second smallest eigenvalue of the Laplacian).

3. **Insufficient Explanation:** The paper claims "the two norm terms are data-dependent and quantify the difficulty of the estimation problem" but fails to explain what these dispersion measures are or how they relate to problem difficulty. The "√n factor" in the U2-gossip comparison is asserted without justification, which is a significant claim that requires explanation.

4. **Citation Issues:** The paper cites [17] as "the only prior work" but [17] is actually the U2-gossip paper by Pelckmans and Suykens (referenced as [17] in the bibliography), creating confusion. The authors should clarify whether U2-gossip is their own work or prior work.

5. **Experimental Details:** In the AUC experiment, they "set θ to the difference between the class means" without specifying which class means (positive or negative) or addressing class imbalance. For within-cluster point scatter, they mention "partition P associated to class centroids" but don't explain how these centroids were determined.

6. **Proof Omission:** The paper references "supplementary material" for proofs of Theorems 1 and 2, which is acceptable, but the main text should at least outline key steps to demonstrate the proof's validity.

**Recommendation:** While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and presents theoretically sound algorithms, the cumulative effect of these minor but numerous technical and formatting issues significantly undermines the paper's quality. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these inconsistencies particularly troubling in a theoretical paper where precision is paramount. The paper would be suitable for a conference with less stringent standards, but for NIPS, where technical precision is expected, these issues are unacceptable. The minor errors suggest the authors may not have thoroughly proofread the paper, which is concerning for a theoretical contribution.

**Summary of Pro/Con Acceptance:**
- *Pro:* Novel problem, sound theoretical approach, comprehensive experiments.
- *Con:* Multiple formatting errors, technical ambiguities, incorrect spectral gap definition, insufficient explanations, citation confusion.

Given the paper's theoretical nature, these seemingly minor issues collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that would make the paper difficult to verify and reproduce. For a venue like NIPS, where precision and rigor are paramount, these flaws are sufficient to warrant rejection.
