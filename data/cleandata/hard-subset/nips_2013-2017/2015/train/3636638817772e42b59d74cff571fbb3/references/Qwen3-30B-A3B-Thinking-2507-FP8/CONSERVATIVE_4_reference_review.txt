

**Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"**

This paper proposes new synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) for computing U-statistics in distributed networks. U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance), cannot be directly computed with standard gossip algorithms designed for single-observation functions. The authors introduce a "phantom network" approach to analyze their algorithms, claiming O(1/t) convergence for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous versions, with improvements over U2-gossip [17] in convergence rate, memory, and communication costs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper clearly articulates a specific problem that existing gossip algorithms cannot solve directly.
- The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful convergence rate proofs using the phantom network reformulation.
- The experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter consistently show improvements over U2-gossip.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Lack of meaningful baselines:** The paper only compares to U2-gossip [17], which is a recent paper addressing this specific problem. No comparison is made to more established distributed optimization methods (e.g., decentralized gradient descent, standard gossip for pairwise functions) that could potentially solve the same problem with simpler modifications. This makes it difficult to assess the significance of the claimed improvements.
- **Overstated limitations of existing methods:** The paper claims "existing gossip algorithms cannot be used to efficiently compute U-statistics," which is an overstatement. Many distributed algorithms can handle pairwise functions with reasonable modifications (e.g., by maintaining additional state), and the paper fails to demonstrate why a specialized algorithm is necessary rather than a simple adaptation of existing gossip.
- **Marginal theoretical improvements:** Both GoSta and U2-gossip achieve O(1/t) convergence. The paper claims better constants in the convergence bound, but the experimental results don't clearly demonstrate this advantage in practical terms. The paper doesn't explain why constant factors matter in practice, especially for large-scale networks where the O(1/t) term dominates.
- **Limited scope and significance:** The paper focuses solely on degree-2 U-statistics, while many important U-statistics are of higher order. The authors don't discuss how their approach would scale to more complex statistics or larger networks. The problem of distributed U-statistics hasn't been established as a major challenge in distributed computing, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of their contribution.
- **Lack of discussion on limitations:** The paper doesn't address potential failure cases (e.g., highly unbalanced networks, non-uniform data distribution) or situations where their algorithm might underperform.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper is technically sound and the analysis is carefully executed, it presents a narrow technical contribution that doesn't significantly advance the field of distributed algorithms. The problem is narrowly defined, the improvements over the only baseline (U2-gossip) are marginal, and the paper fails to demonstrate why this specialized approach is necessary rather than simpler adaptations of established methods.

The paper would be substantially stronger if it:
1. Compared to more standard distributed optimization approaches beyond U2-gossip
2. Provided a clearer justification for why specialized algorithms are required
3. Discussed limitations and failure cases
4. Demonstrated broader applicability beyond degree-2 U-statistics

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of new approaches without extensive validation, I find this paper's contribution to be incremental rather than transformative. The problem of distributed U-statistics isn't yet established as a major challenge in distributed computing, and the proposed solution doesn't clearly advance the state of the art beyond a minor improvement over a recently proposed method.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject

**Arguments for acceptance:** Technically sound analysis, well-written, consistent experimental improvements over U2-gossip.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Narrow problem scope, lack of meaningful baselines, marginal theoretical improvements, overstatement of existing method limitations, limited practical significance.
