This paper proposes randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are crucial in many machine learning applications but are challenging to compute in a decentralized manner using traditional gossip algorithms designed for separable functions. The authors address this gap by introducing algorithms that combine data propagation and averaging steps, providing convergence guarantees and demonstrating improvements over existing approaches like U2-gossip [17].

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing gossip algorithms for U-statistics and highlighting the importance of the problem. The proposed approach, based on “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem, is a clever idea that allows for a rigorous analysis. The convergence analysis, presenting bounds on the convergence rate for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, is a significant contribution. The experimental validation using real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) and different network topologies further strengthens the paper's claims.  The comparison with U2-gossip is well-executed, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed algorithms in terms of convergence speed and resource usage. The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms broadens the applicability of the work.  The writing is generally clear and well-organized, and the mathematical notation is standard.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The phantom node reformulation, while enabling the analysis, feels somewhat convoluted and might hinder intuition.  The connection between the phantom nodes and the actual distributed computation could be made more explicit in the explanation.  While the theoretical convergence bounds are provided, the practical implications of the data-dependent terms within those bounds (e.g., how they relate to the specific characteristics of the data) are not thoroughly discussed.  The experimental section, while demonstrating good performance, lacks a deeper exploration of parameter sensitivity. For example, how do the performance differences vary with different network sizes or different values of the rewiring probability in the Watts-Strogatz network?  The experiments primarily focus on two applications. Including an additional application, perhaps related to rank correlation coefficients as mentioned in the introduction, could further showcase the versatility of the algorithms.  The discussion on extending the approach to scenarios where nodes hold multiple observations feels somewhat brief; a more detailed elaboration would be beneficial.  Finally, the citation of [3] (Spectral Graph Theory by Chung) is a little odd, as only the volume and author are given, without the publisher or year.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing efficient gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, a problem not adequately addressed by existing techniques.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis, supported by the phantom node reformulation, provides rigorous guarantees on the performance of the algorithms.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithms over existing approaches on real datasets and different network topologies.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible to readers familiar with gossip algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity of Phantom Node Reformulation:** The phantom node approach, while effective for analysis, adds complexity and may hinder intuition.
*   **Limited Discussion of Data-Dependent Terms:** The practical implications of the data-dependent terms in the convergence bounds are not thoroughly explored.
*   **Scope of Experiments:**  The experimental section could be more comprehensive, with a deeper exploration of parameter sensitivity and a broader range of applications.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While the phantom node reformulation adds some complexity, and the experimental section could be more thorough, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically regarding a clearer explanation of the phantom nodes, a discussion of the data-dependent terms, and a more detailed exploration of experimental results.  Fixing the citation of [3] is also essential.
