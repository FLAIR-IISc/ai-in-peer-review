This paper introduces randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are a challenging class of functions for traditional gossip algorithms which excel at separable functions. The authors address this limitation by proposing algorithms based on propagating local observations and averaging local estimates, achieving convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for synchronous and asynchronous versions, respectively. The theoretical analysis relies on a clever reformulation using "phantom nodes" and demonstrates dependency on both data characteristics and network topology. Empirical validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation confirms the superiority of the proposed approach compared to a prior work [17].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis, while complex, appears rigorous and is supported by the theoretical framework built around phantom nodes. The inclusion of data-dependent terms in the convergence bounds is a notable strength. The algorithms themselves are clearly defined (Algorithm 1 and 2). However, the presentation could be improved by providing a more intuitive explanation of the phantom node approach. The experimental section demonstrates the algorithms’ effectiveness, but a more thorough ablation study, varying parameters like network topology and data distribution, would strengthen the evaluation. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential future work, which demonstrates intellectual honesty. The comparison to U2-gossip is strong, highlighting advantages in both convergence and resource usage.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the contribution within the existing literature. The notation is standard, although the use of several different matrices (A, L, D, P) requires careful attention. Section 2 provides necessary background, though a brief explanation of why existing gossip algorithms fail for U-statistics within this section could improve understanding. While the mathematical derivations are correct, they could benefit from more descriptive text explaining the reasoning behind each step. For instance, the link between the transition matrix and the algorithm's update rules isn’t immediately obvious. The use of Algorithm 1 and 2 is helpful.

**Originality:** The problem of extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is a novel and relevant contribution.  While the core concept of gossip algorithms is well-established, applying it to non-separable functions like U-statistics requires significant innovation.  The phantom node reformulation appears to be a key original contribution. The paper builds upon existing work in gossip algorithms [19, 5, 2] and U-statistics [12, 14], but proposes a fundamentally different approach to address the limitations of previous methods. The authors adequately reference related work and clearly distinguish their approach from [17].

**Significance:**  The results are significant. U-statistics are fundamental in various machine learning applications (AUC, variance, correlation), and efficient distributed computation of these quantities is crucial for large-scale datasets.  The proposed algorithms offer a practical solution for decentralized estimation in resource-constrained environments. The convergence rate analysis, linking performance to both data characteristics and network topology, provides valuable insights. The empirical validation on real-world datasets further demonstrates the potential impact of the work. The ability to handle asynchronous updates makes the algorithms applicable to a wider range of practical scenarios.  Researchers and practitioners dealing with distributed machine learning and statistical estimation are likely to benefit from these findings.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a challenging and important problem in distributed computation.
*   Proposes novel algorithms with provable convergence guarantees.
*   The phantom node technique is a clever and original contribution.
*   Empirical results demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   The theoretical analysis provides insights into the influence of network topology and data characteristics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The phantom node approach could be explained more intuitively.
*   The experimental evaluation could be expanded with a more comprehensive ablation study.
*   The mathematical derivations could benefit from more descriptive text.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed computation. The technical soundness, originality, and potential impact of the work clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
