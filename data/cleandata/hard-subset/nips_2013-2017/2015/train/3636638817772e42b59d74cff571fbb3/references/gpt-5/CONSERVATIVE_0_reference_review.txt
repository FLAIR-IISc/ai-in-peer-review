Summary
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. The key idea is to decompose Ûn(H) = (1/n)∑i hi with hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi,Xj), and to combine two mechanisms per iteration: (i) propagation of observations via random swaps so each node can estimate its own hi, and (ii) averaging of local estimates across randomly chosen edges. The authors propose a synchronous algorithm (GoSta-sync) and an asynchronous variant (GoSta-async). They analyze convergence in expectation, obtaining O(1/t) for synchronous and O((log t)/t) for asynchronous, with constants depending on data dispersion terms and the spectral gap of the graph. A “phantom nodes” construction (size O(n^2)) is used for analysis. The authors compare to U2-gossip [17], arguing better communication/memory and faster rates. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several graph topologies show improved error-vs-iteration curves.

Relation to prior work
The problem of decentralized averaging is classical; pairwise functions are harder and prior work (U2-gossip) addressed U-statistics with O(1/t) rate. The contribution here is to integrate averaging with single-token propagation, and to provide an asynchronous scheme. The spectral-gap dependence and use of lazy-like mixing matrices align with standard gossip analyses. The “phantom network” trick is a technical device to separate propagation and averaging.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and nontrivial decentralized estimation task (pairwise U-statistics) with clear applications (AUC, clustering).
- Simple algorithms: only one auxiliary observation per node; synchronous and asynchronous variants; explicit pseudocode.
- Theoretical guarantees: convergence in expectation with explicit dependence on spectral gap; bound highlights data-dependent difficulty.
- Comparison to U2-gossip with a refined bound that clarifies when averaging helps.
- Empirical results on multiple network topologies and two real datasets show consistently lower error and variance across nodes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Convergence only in expectation. No high-probability or mean-square error bounds, and no almost-sure convergence guarantees. For practitioners, concentration around the mean (as a function of t and spectral gap) is important; current results may be too weak to endorse broad use.
- Asynchronous algorithm requires each node to know pk = 2dk/|E| and thus |E|. Knowledge of the total number of edges is a strong global requirement that contradicts the motivation of limited global knowledge. It is not discussed how to estimate |E| in a decentralized way or the impact of errors in pk on bias/variance.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and favors the proposed method:
  - Baselines: only U2-gossip is compared. There are other plausible baselines, e.g., (i) Monte Carlo pair sampling via random walks with push-sum averaging, (ii) multiple-token random walks with periodic averaging, (iii) variants using mini-batch local pair sampling plus consensus. Without these, it is hard to assess practical dominance.
  - Metrics: Plots are error vs iteration. Communication- and memory-efficiency are central claims, but there is no report of messages/bytes transmitted per unit error, or wall-clock/activation complexity. Since U2-gossip uses two edge activations per iteration, fairness requires normalizing by total communications or data words exchanged.
  - Scale and robustness: Experiments are on small-to-moderate n with static, noise-free links. No tests on larger networks (n ≫ 10^3), higher-dimensional data, degree heterogeneity, packet drops, or dynamic graphs. The async advantage is asserted but only lightly tested.
- Modeling assumptions:
  - H(X,X)=0 and use of n^2 normalizer simplifies the algebra. The paper claims straightforward extension to standard U-statistics with n(n−1), but this is not spelled out (especially for async) and the impact on constants is unclear.
  - Non-bipartite connected graphs only. In practice, many graphs are bipartite; adding standard laziness/self-loops could remove periodicity, but this is not discussed as an algorithmic fix.
- Theoretical tightness: The O((log t)/t) bound for async seems loose relative to standard exponential mixing in gossip for separable sums; an explanation of whether the log term is an artifact of analysis versus inherent to pairwise propagation would help. Also, the analysis yields bounds in vector/matrix operator norms; guidance on how these translate to RMSE across nodes would be useful.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide high-probability (or MSE) bounds for both algorithms? Is almost sure convergence attainable via standard martingale techniques?
2. For GoSta-async, is knowledge of |E| strictly necessary for unbiasedness, or can nodes use a purely local counter j of activations (yielding 1/j updates) without bias? If not, can you quantify bias when pk is misspecified?
3. How do your conclusions change if you introduce laziness/self-loops to handle bipartite graphs?
4. Can you report communication efficiency (e.g., number of words transmitted) to a target error, and include additional baselines as noted above?
5. Do your rates extend cleanly to the standard U-statistic normalization n(n−1) and to H(X,X)≠0?

Minor comments
- Some typos/formatting: “lo- cal”, “non bipartite” vs “non-bipartite”, footnotes and references formatting.
- Please define clearly the norm used on matrices in the bounds (spectral norm?).
- Clarify whether the exponential term in Theorem 1 is from consensus averaging only; an intuition section would improve readability.
- The “phantom nodes” construction is helpful analytically; a short comparison to a direct Markov chain or stochastic approximation view would enhance clarity.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a meaningful problem and proposes simple algorithms with reasonable theoretical support and improved empirical performance over the most directly related prior work. However, from a methodological rigor and validation standpoint, I find the current evidence insufficient: only expectation guarantees, strong global-information assumptions in the async setting, limited baselines, and missing communication-efficiency evaluations. The contribution feels incremental relative to [17], albeit with a cleaner algorithm and analysis.

I lean toward a weak reject at this stage. Strengthening the theory (concentration, robustness to pk misspecification), broadening and fairing the empirical evaluation (communication-normalized metrics and additional baselines), and clarifying practical assumptions would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
