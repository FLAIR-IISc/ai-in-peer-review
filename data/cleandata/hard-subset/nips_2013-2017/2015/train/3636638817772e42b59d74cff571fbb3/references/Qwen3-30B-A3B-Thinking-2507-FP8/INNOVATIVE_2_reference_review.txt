

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper introduces a novel approach to extending gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which represent a fundamental class of statistics that depend on pairwise interactions between observations (e.g., sample variance, AUC, Gini mean difference). The authors propose two algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, which combine data propagation and averaging steps to efficiently compute U-statistics in distributed networks.

The core novelty lies in the "phantom node" reformulation technique, which allows the authors to model the complex data propagation and averaging steps separately. This approach is both elegant and powerful, enabling the authors to prove convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, with data-dependent and network-dependent terms that reflect the problem's inherent difficulty. The convergence analysis reveals how network connectivity (via the spectral gap) affects performance, a crucial insight for practical applications.

The paper makes a strong case for the significance of this work by highlighting the limitations of existing gossip algorithms (which can only compute separable functions) and the shortcomings of the previous U2-gossip approach [17]. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves faster convergence with lower memory and communication costs, particularly in high-dimensional settings where U2-gossip's requirements become prohibitive.

The experimental validation on real datasets (AUC estimation using SVMguide3 and within-cluster point scatter using Wine Quality) is convincing. The results consistently show GoSta outperforming U2-gossip across different network topologies (complete graphs, 2D grids, Watts-Strogatz networks), with the performance gap widening as network size increases. The comparison between synchronous and asynchronous versions in Figure 3b also provides valuable practical insights.

The paper's main strength is its clear identification of a significant gap in the literature and the bold, novel approach to addressing it. The phantom node concept is particularly impressive—it's not just a technical trick but a fundamental rethinking of how to model the distributed estimation of non-separable statistics. This work opens new avenues for distributed computation beyond what was previously possible with gossip algorithms.

The paper's organization is excellent, with clear definitions, well-structured algorithms, and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The related work section properly situates the contribution within the gossip algorithm literature, and the comparison to U2-gossip is thorough and well-supported by both theory and experiments.

Minor weaknesses include some proofs being relegated to supplementary material (though the main paper provides sufficient detail for understanding), and the paper could more explicitly discuss potential limitations for certain types of U-statistics or network topologies. However, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's significant contribution.

This paper is a strong example of the kind of novel, bold approach that NIPS values. It doesn't just incrementally improve existing methods—it fundamentally extends the capabilities of gossip algorithms to a new class of problems with important applications in distributed machine learning and data mining. The convergence analysis is theoretically sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the practical implications are clear.

While the paper is not perfect methodologically (as is true of any cutting-edge work), the novelty and significance of the contribution far outweigh any minor imperfections. The phantom node reformulation alone represents a significant conceptual advance that could inspire further work in distributed optimization and statistical learning.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and should be accepted. It's exactly the kind of paper that advances the state of the art by addressing a fundamental limitation in a new way, rather than making incremental improvements to existing methods. The authors have successfully demonstrated both the theoretical soundness and practical value of their approach, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
