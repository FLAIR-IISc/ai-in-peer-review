Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics Ûn(H) over a network using gossip. While classic gossip computes averages of node-separable quantities, U-statistics depend on pairs of observations and thus are not directly amenable to standard averaging. The authors propose two randomized algorithms:

- GoSta-sync (synchronous): Each node keeps a single auxiliary observation Yk that is swapped along uniformly sampled edges and maintains a running average of H(Xk, Yk). At each iteration, one randomly chosen edge averages the two endpoint estimates and swaps their Y’s. They show E[Zk(t)] → Ûn(H) with rate O(1/t), and constants reflecting both data dispersion and graph connectivity via the spectral gap.

- GoSta-async (asynchronous): In a local-clock setting, only the activated pair updates. To debias the running averages, each node maintains a surrogate iteration counter mk that increments by 1/pk upon activation (pk ∝ degree), yielding E[mk]=t. They prove E[Zk(t)] → Ûn(H) with rate O(log t / t).

The analysis uses an original “phantom nodes” construction that decouples averaging and data propagation, leading to block-matrix dynamics and bounds depending on the second eigenvalue of Wα(G) (hence the Laplacian spectral gap). Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs show faster and lower-variance convergence than U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010], with smaller memory and communication per iteration.

This work builds on and extends randomized gossip [Boyd et al., 2006], consensus/spectral gap analyses [Chung, 1997], geographic gossip [Dimakis et al., 2008], and prior U-statistics applications in ML (e.g., Clémençon at NIPS). It directly improves on the only prior method for gossiping U-statistics [U2-gossip].

Strengths
- Clear, simple algorithms with minimal local state (one auxiliary observation vs. two in U2-gossip) and a natural swap+average primitive.
- Solid theoretical analysis yielding explicit rates with data- and graph-dependent constants; the phantom graph technique is neat and broadly reusable.
- Asynchronous variant provided, a practical necessity.
- Empirical results on two representative U-statistics (AUC, within-cluster scatter) across diverse topologies show consistent gains over U2-gossip, with lower cross-node variance due to averaging.
- Good discussion relating rates to spectral gap (connectivity), matching intuition and practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality/technical soundness
- Convergence is in expectation; no almost-sure or high-probability guarantees, and no bounds on variance across runs or nodes (beyond qualitative comments). Suggest adding concentration or a.s. convergence via martingale arguments, or at least bounding Var(Zk(t)).
- The asynchronous algorithm assumes knowledge of pk=2dk/|E| and effectively uniform-at-random edge activations. In a fully decentralized setting, |E| is typically unknown and exact uniform edge sampling is hard. Please (i) provide an implementable node-activation model (e.g., active node picks a neighbor by local stochastic matrix P) and analyze the corresponding pk and rate; (ii) remove the dependence on |E| by using local counters mi (increment by 1 upon activation) and a normalization trick (or push-sum/ratio consensus) to correct for heterogeneous activation probabilities, with analysis of the bias/variance.
- The spectral gap mapping includes a likely indexing slip: for Wα(G)=I−(2/(α|E|))L, the second largest eigenvalue λ2 corresponds to the second smallest Laplacian eigenvalue β2, not βn−1. Please correct and make the dependence explicit.
- The asynchronous bound ∥E[Z(t)]−Û1n∥ ≤ c′(G) (log t)/t · ∥H∥ depends on ∥H∥ over all n2 pairwise values, which can scale with n and be loose. It would be more informative to provide data-dependent dispersion terms akin to Theorem 1 and to make c′(G) explicit.
- Assumption H(X,X)=0 is stated; please clarify how to handle general degree-2 U-statistics with diagonal terms (or show the reduction).

Clarity
- The phantom-network analysis is interesting but dense; a diagram and intuitive derivation of the block matrix M(t) would help. Define Wα and its properties once, and clearly relate λ2(Wα) to the Laplacian spectrum.
- Algorithm 2 currently “draws (i,j) uniformly from E,” which reads centralized. Restate it with local clocks and neighbor selection for implementability, and connect to the analysis via the equivalent global-clock model.
- Minor: ensure consistent notation for eigenvalues (β2 vs βn−1) and give complete citations.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first gossip method that computes U-statistics with one token per node and integrates averaging, with a new analytical device (phantom graph). The comparison to U2-gossip is appropriate and the rate/memory/communication improvements are substantive.

Significance
- U-statistics include widely used measures (AUC, Kendall tau, Gini, within-cluster scatter). Making them computable in decentralized settings is useful. The spectral-gap-sensitive rates and asynchronous variant increase practical relevance.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines: Beyond U2-gossip, consider sampling-based decentralized approximations to U-statistics (e.g., token-based Monte Carlo, block gossip of mini-batches), or a two-phase approach that estimates hi via random walks then gossips their average.
- Report communication- and memory-normalized performance (messages, words transferred, and per-node storage) vs error, not only iterations. Include larger n and higher d to highlight the reduction from two tokens to one.
- Provide more details on the construction and parameters of the Watts–Strogatz graphs, and show the measured spectral gaps to corroborate the theory.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: decentralized computation of pairwise statistics.
- Simple algorithms with lower memory/communication than prior art.
- Nontrivial analysis with interpretable rates tied to spectral gap.
- Consistent empirical gains on real datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Expectation-only guarantees; asynchronous model requires global quantities and uniform edge sampling, which is unrealistic without further mechanisms.
- Some clarity/notation issues and loose constants in the async bound.
- Empirical scope and baselines could be broader; no communication-cost analysis.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The core idea and theory are solid and improve the state of the art on an important problem. Addressing the implementability of the asynchronous setting (removing |E| and uniform-edge assumptions), tightening/clarifying the analysis (eigenvalue indexing, constants, concentration), and expanding empirical comparisons would further strengthen the paper.
