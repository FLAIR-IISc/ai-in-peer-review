### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of computing U-statistics of degree two in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip algorithms are efficient for separable sums (e.g., averages), but fail for U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of data points distributed across the network. The authors propose two novel algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for the synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively. The core idea is to combine two iterative processes: 1) a data propagation step, where each node's local data point performs a random walk on the network via swapping with neighbors, and 2) an estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic with their neighbors.

The main theoretical contribution is a convergence analysis that shows the local estimates converge in expectation to the true U-statistic. The convergence rates are O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. A key and original element of the proof is the introduction of a "phantom network" construction, which allows the authors to model the coupled dynamics of data propagation and estimate averaging as a single linear system. The paper provides a detailed comparison to the main prior work in this area, U2-gossip [17], arguing for superior convergence, memory, and communication efficiency. These claims are supported by experiments on real data for estimating AUC and within-cluster point scatter on various network topologies.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem. U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and their efficient computation in decentralized settings is a challenging but increasingly relevant task. This work significantly extends the capability of the popular gossip algorithm framework to a non-trivial class of non-separable functions. The proposed dual mechanism of propagating data via random walks while simultaneously averaging estimates is an elegant and powerful concept.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The convergence analysis is the paper's main strength. The rates are explicitly derived and correctly show dependence on both data-dependent terms (quantifying the problem's intrinsic difficulty) and network-dependent terms (the spectral gap, which links performance to network connectivity). The "phantom network" formalism is a clever and non-obvious analytical tool that successfully decouples the analysis. Furthermore, the authors provide a new, refined analysis of the competing U2-gossip algorithm to theoretically justify their method's superiority, identifying a detrimental $\sqrt{n}$ factor in the prior work's convergence bound that is absent in theirs.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the theoretical claims. The choice of two practical U-statistics (AUC and within-cluster scatter) on real-world datasets demonstrates the applicability of the method. Testing on three different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) effectively illustrates the impact of network connectivity on performance, consistent with the theory. The direct comparison against U2-gossip clearly shows the proposed GoSta algorithm achieves lower error and variance, with the performance gap widening for larger networks as predicted.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of the Analysis Section:** While the "phantom network" proof technique is powerful, its presentation in Section 4.1 is very dense. The transition from the intuitive algorithm (Algorithm 1) to the abstract matrix formulation (Eq. 5-8) could be explained more gradually. Providing more intuition on how the blocks of the transition matrix `M(t)` (specifically `M1`, `M2`, `M3`) map to the distinct steps of the algorithm (averaging `Zk`, updating `Zk` with `H(Xk, Yk)`, and swapping `Yk`) would greatly improve readability for a non-specialist audience.

2.  **Unstated Assumptions in the Asynchronous Algorithm:** The asynchronous algorithm (Algorithm 2) requires each node `k` to know its update probability `pk = 2dk/|E|`. While obtaining the local degree `dk` is trivial, knowing the total number of edges `|E|` requires global information about the network. In a truly decentralized setting, this value is not immediately available to each node. The authors should explicitly state this requirement and discuss how `|E|` could be obtained (e.g., through a preliminary consensus/gossip phase). Without this clarification, the "fully asynchronous" claim is slightly weakened.

3.  **Discussion on Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The paper shows a theoretical rate of O(log t / t) for GoSta-async, which is slightly worse than the O(1/t) for GoSta-sync. However, the experiments in Figure 3b suggest their practical performance is very similar. This discrepancy is interesting and warrants discussion. Is the log factor an artifact of the proof technique, or is there a fundamental reason for the slower theoretical rate that doesn't manifest in these specific experiments? A brief comment on this would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Limited Baselines:** The primary comparison is against U2-gossip [17], which is appropriate as it is the most direct prior work. However, including a simple, more "brute-force" baseline could provide additional context. For instance, a method where each node uses random walks to collect *all* data points from the network before locally computing the U-statistic would serve as a useful, albeit communication-heavy, benchmark. This would help contextualize the trade-offs made by GoSta.

### **Final Assessment**

Overall, this is a very strong paper that makes a significant and well-supported contribution to the literature on decentralized computation. The problem is important, the proposed solution is novel and elegant, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and minor unstated assumptions, which can be addressed in a final version of the paper. This work advances the state-of-the-art in distributed estimation and is likely to be influential, providing a foundational building block for more complex decentralized optimization tasks involving U-statistics. I strongly recommend acceptance.
