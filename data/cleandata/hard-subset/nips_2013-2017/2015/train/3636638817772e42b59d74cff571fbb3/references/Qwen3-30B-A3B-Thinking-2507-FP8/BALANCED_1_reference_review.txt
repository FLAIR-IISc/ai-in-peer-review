

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses an important problem in distributed computing: the estimation of U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations) using gossip algorithms. The authors propose novel synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) that combine data propagation (via auxiliary observations) and local averaging to estimate degree-2 U-statistics. The key insight is the use of "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem for convergence analysis, enabling the derivation of convergence rates of O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous). The paper demonstrates theoretical and empirical superiority over the prior U2-gossip algorithm [17] in terms of convergence rate, memory requirements, and communication complexity.

**Strengths:**

1. **Problem relevance and novelty:** The paper identifies a significant gap in distributed computation literature: existing gossip algorithms cannot efficiently compute U-statistics, which are crucial in many applications (AUC, Gini mean difference, etc.). The proposed GoSta approach is genuinely novel and fills this gap.

2. **Strong theoretical contribution:** The convergence analysis is rigorous, with clear rates that depend on meaningful network properties (spectral gap) and data properties. The phantom node construction is elegant and enables a clean separation of data propagation and averaging dynamics.

3. **Clear empirical validation:** The experiments on real datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC, Wine Quality for within-cluster point scatter) convincingly demonstrate GoSta's superiority over U2-gossip. The performance gap widens with network size, as predicted by the theory.

4. **Practical considerations:** The authors thoughtfully address both synchronous and asynchronous settings, which is important for real-world applications. The comparison to U2-gossip is thorough, highlighting specific advantages in communication complexity and memory requirements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited discussion of broader context:** While the paper references U2-gossip, it could better articulate why U-statistics specifically pose challenges for gossip algorithms compared to other non-separable functions. A more detailed discussion of the fundamental limitations of existing gossip approaches for U-statistics would strengthen the motivation.

2. **Phantom node explanation:** The phantom node construction, while mathematically sound, is somewhat abstract and might be difficult for readers to grasp without additional intuition or examples. A simple illustrative example (e.g., for a small network) would improve accessibility.

3. **Scalability to higher-order U-statistics:** The paper focuses on degree-2 U-statistics. It would be valuable to discuss whether the approach generalizes to higher-order U-statistics (e.g., degree 3), which would significantly broaden the applicability of the work.

4. **Experimental depth:** While the experiments are convincing, they could be strengthened by:
   - More detailed communication complexity analysis (e.g., number of messages per iteration)
   - Comparisons to additional baselines beyond U2-gossip
   - Analysis of how performance varies with network topology parameters

**Constructive suggestions:**

1. In Section 4.1, consider adding a small example (e.g., with n=3 nodes) to illustrate the phantom node construction and how data propagates through the network.

2. Expand the discussion in Section 3 to more clearly articulate why U-statistics are challenging for gossip algorithms, perhaps by contrasting with separable functions that gossip algorithms can already handle.

3. Consider adding a brief discussion of potential extensions to higher-order U-statistics in the conclusion or future work section.

4. In the experimental section, include a table quantifying the communication and memory costs of GoSta versus U2-gossip for the tested applications.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on distributed algorithms by addressing a previously unsolved problem (distributed U-statistic estimation) with a novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated approach. The theoretical results are strong, the experimental validation is convincing, and the practical advantages over prior work are clear. While the phantom node construction could be made more accessible, and the scope could be slightly broadened, these are minor issues that could be addressed in a revised version.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel approach (Originality), and addresses an important problem with meaningful practical implications (Significance). The theoretical and empirical results advance the state of the art in distributed computation.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, making a clear contribution to the field of distributed algorithms with strong theoretical foundations and practical validation. The minor weaknesses noted can be addressed with relatively straightforward revisions.
