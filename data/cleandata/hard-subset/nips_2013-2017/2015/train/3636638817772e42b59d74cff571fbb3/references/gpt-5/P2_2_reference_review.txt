Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network where each node holds one data point. It proposes two randomized gossip algorithms: GoSta-sync (with a global clock) and GoSta-async (fully asynchronous). The key idea is to couple two mechanisms per iteration: (i) data propagation via swapping an auxiliary observation along a randomly chosen edge (so each Xi performs a random walk), and (ii) averaging local estimates across the communicating pair. The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction that decouples averaging from data propagation, enabling an expectation convergence analysis. The authors show E[Zk(t)] → Ûn(H) with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async), with network dependence through the spectral gap and data-dependent dispersion terms. They derive an improved bound for prior work (U2-gossip) showing a √n/t dependence, and demonstrate empirically (AUC and within-cluster scatter) that GoSta converges faster and with lower variance on several graph families.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithms are simple and implementable. The sync analysis is clear at a high level: the block upper-triangular evolution with Wα(G) captures averaging and random-walk propagation; the limit and rate fall out through spectral properties. The asynchronous construction uses a per-node counter mk with scaling by 1/pk to ensure unbiasedness of the effective step-size; the expectation argument is reasonable.
- Correctness and support: The main claims are supported with formal theorems (proofs in supplement). The synchronous rate bound is interpretable: an O(1/t) term scaled by data dispersion and 1 − λ2(2), plus an exponential consensus term. The async bound is weaker (O(log t/t)) with an unspecified c′(G), which is believable but less informative.
- Limitations: Convergence is in expectation; there are no high-probability or almost-sure guarantees, no variance bounds, and no explicit consensus error bounds beyond an e−ct term in the synchronous bound. The async constant c′(G) is opaque, and the dependence on spectral gap is not spelled out. The async method requires pk = 2dk/|E|, i.e., knowledge of |E|, which may be impractical in decentralized settings without an additional estimation phase.
- Experiments: The comparison to U2-gossip is appropriate, with multiple graph topologies, 50 runs, and two meaningful U-statistic tasks. Results are consistent with theory. However, experiments are limited to two datasets and do not explore sensitivity to dimension d, compression/quantization, or larger/dynamic networks.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The phantom graph construction is clearly communicated and explicitly noted as an analysis device (no runtime overhead).
- Notation: H is used both as a kernel and as an n×n matrix [H(Xi,Xj)]; this could be clarified earlier. The meaning of h and its role in the bounds is clear but could benefit from a short intuition paragraph. The order of averaging vs. estimate updates in Algorithm 2 deserves a brief justification.
- Practicality: The async algorithm’s need for pk (hence |E|) should be emphasized; offering a variant with stepsize 1/mk and a bias/variance discussion would improve clarity and practicality.

Originality
- Extending gossip to pairwise statistics with a provable rate and modest memory/communication is novel relative to U2-gossip [17]. The coupling of random-walk data propagation with averaging, and the phantom-node analytical device, are interesting and useful. The derivation of a refined rate for U2-gossip that reveals a √n penalty is also a nice contribution.
- Related work is mostly adequate (gossip averaging, geographic gossip, distributed optimization). It would help to discuss connections to push-sum, randomized token/walk methods for distributed counting, and any existing work on pairwise function computation beyond [17].

Significance
- U-statistics cover many important quantities (AUC, Kendall tau, cluster scatter), making the problem practically relevant. The algorithms reduce memory/communication compared to [17], and the rates are better in theory and practice. The spectral-gap dependence aligns with the distributed averaging literature and gives actionable guidance about topology.
- The scope is currently limited to degree-2 U-statistics and static connected non-bipartite graphs; extensions to higher-order U-statistics, dynamic graphs, communication constraints, and compressed messages would expand impact.

Strengths
- Simple, implementable algorithms with clear intuition.
- Solid convergence analysis for the synchronous case with interpretable constants.
- Asynchronous variant without global clock.
- Clear improvement over U2-gossip in both theory and experiments.
- Phantom-node analysis technique is elegant and may be reusable.

Weaknesses
- Only expectation guarantees; no high-probability/AS convergence or variance bounds.
- Async algorithm relies on knowing |E| (and pk), which may be unrealistic.
- c′(G) in the async bound is unspecified; lack of explicit spectral-gap dependence weakens interpretability.
- Experimental scope is modest; no study of communication/computation trade-offs or compression.
- Limited discussion of extensions (multiple observations per node gets only a remark).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide high-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration) or at least a variance bound for Zk(t)? Even for sync, a finite-time bound on consensus error would strengthen the paper.
- Can c′(G) be characterized in terms of spectral gap and degree statistics? Even a loose expression would improve interpretability.
- Is it possible to avoid requiring |E| in Algorithm 2 by using 1/mk stepsizes or by estimating pk online (e.g., via local counters and consensus)?
- How sensitive are the methods to message quantization/compression? Could you adapt known quantized gossip techniques?
- Any insight on extending to higher-order U-statistics (order m > 2) and the resulting memory/communication scaling?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful, underexplored problem with practical relevance.
- Methodologically clean with clear improvements over prior work.
- Theoretical results are nontrivial and insightful (spectral-gap dependence; phantom-node technique).
- Empirical results match theory and show significant gains.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of stronger probabilistic guarantees and limited characterization of async constants.
- Practicality concern in async setting (need for |E|).
- Limited experimental breadth and systems-level evaluation.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-executed contribution that advances decentralized computation to an important class of statistics with both theoretical and empirical benefits. While there are gaps (probabilistic guarantees, async practicality, breadth of experiments), the core ideas and results are strong and likely to be useful. I lean towards acceptance, recommending the authors address the async practicality and clarify constants, and, if space permits, add a brief variance/concentration discussion.
