

# Peer Review: "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computing: extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance, Gini mean difference). While existing gossip algorithms can compute separable statistics (like averages), they cannot efficiently handle U-statistics. The authors propose two novel algorithms—GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous)—that combine data propagation with local averaging. Their key innovation is a "phantom nodes" reformulation for convergence analysis, proving O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence rates respectively. The paper demonstrates superior performance over the only prior work (U2-gossip) in both theory and experiments on real datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Soundness**: The phantom nodes approach provides a clean framework for analyzing the coupled data propagation and averaging steps. The convergence proofs are rigorous, with clear dependence on network properties (spectral gap) and data properties (dispersion measures), addressing a significant gap in the literature. The comparison to U2-gossip is particularly strong, showing how their approach avoids the √n factor in convergence rates.

2. **Practical Relevance**: U-statistics are ubiquitous in machine learning (AUC, variance, clustering metrics), making this work highly applicable. The paper's focus on reducing communication and memory costs (critical in distributed systems) is well-motivated and demonstrated through experiments.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section is thorough, comparing against U2-gossip across multiple network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) and two real-world applications (AUC estimation, within-cluster point scatter). The results consistently show GoSta's superiority, with the performance gap widening as network size increases.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear notation, precise problem definition, and logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experiments. The algorithm descriptions are concise and implementable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of U-statistics**: The paper focuses exclusively on degree-2 U-statistics. The extension to higher-order U-statistics (e.g., triplets) is non-trivial and would significantly broaden the paper's impact. The authors briefly mention this in Remark 2 but don't explore it, which is a notable limitation.

2. **Assumptions on Network Knowledge**: The asynchronous algorithm requires nodes to know their degree and total number of edges (for pk = 2dk/|E|). In many practical distributed systems, this information might be unavailable, especially in dynamic networks. The paper should discuss how to relax this assumption or quantify the impact of its absence.

3. **Superficial Comparison to U2-gossip**: While the paper claims "largely improve upon" U2-gossip, the theoretical comparison (Equation 10) is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of why the √n factor dominates in practice would strengthen the argument.

4. **Lack of Scalability Analysis**: The experiments show performance improvements but don't explicitly analyze how the algorithms scale with network size beyond the n=50 to n=1599 range in Figure 3a. A more comprehensive scalability study would be valuable.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of distributed algorithms by solving a well-motivated problem that has been overlooked in the gossip literature. The theoretical framework is novel and well-executed, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate practical advantages over prior work. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in distributed machine learning.

The primary limitations relate to the focus on degree-2 U-statistics and the network knowledge assumption for the asynchronous algorithm. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is already significant. The paper is clearly within NeurIPS scope as it addresses a fundamental problem in distributed optimization with broad applications in machine learning.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a strong contribution that advances both the theory and practice of distributed algorithms for machine learning. The technical quality is high, the writing is clear, and the results are significant. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's overall merit, and the work would be valuable to the NeurIPS community.

**Pro**: Novel theoretical framework (phantom nodes), strong experimental validation, clear practical improvements over prior work, high relevance to distributed machine learning.

**Con**: Limited to degree-2 U-statistics, requires network knowledge for asynchronous version.

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS and will be of interest to researchers working on distributed optimization, statistical learning, and network algorithms. The results provide a valuable foundation for future work on decentralized estimation of more complex statistical quantities.
