**Summary**

This paper proposes two randomized gossip algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for the distributed estimation of degree-two U-statistics. U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of data points, cannot be computed by standard gossip averaging algorithms. The proposed approach cleverly combines a data propagation mechanism, where nodes swap auxiliary data observations in a random-walk fashion, with a standard estimate averaging step. The authors provide a convergence analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, yielding rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. The analysis, which relies on a "phantom network" construction, shows that convergence speed depends on the spectral gap of the network graph. The paper positions this work as a significant improvement over the only known prior work, U2-gossip [17], by offering lower memory and communication costs and a faster convergence rate. Experimental results on AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation tasks appear to support these claims.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized network, a natural and non-trivial extension of the well-studied distributed averaging problem.
2.  **Novelty and Methodological Contribution:** The core idea of combining data propagation (via swapping auxiliary observations) with estimate averaging is elegant and effective. This directly addresses a key weakness of the prior art [17], which lacked the averaging component. This leads to a method that is more efficient in terms of memory, communication, and, as the analysis suggests, convergence speed.
3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a formal convergence analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The use of the phantom network is a creative analytical tool, and deriving explicit rates that connect to graph properties (the spectral gap) provides valuable insight.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on real datasets demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed GoSta algorithms over the U2-gossip baseline, corroborating the theoretical claims about improved performance.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel contributions, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, technical inaccuracies, and presentation issues that collectively undermine its quality and polish.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:**
    *   **AUC Formula:** The definition of the AUC in Equation (3) appears incorrect. For a negative sample `i` (`l_i = -1`) and a positive sample `j` (`l_j = 1`), the indicator function becomes `I{-1*(θ>Xi) > -1*(θ>Xj)}`, which simplifies to `I{θ>Xi < θ>Xj}`. This is the opposite of what is desired; a good classifier should rank the positive sample `j` higher. This is a significant error in the formulation of a key motivating example.
    *   **V-statistics vs. U-statistics:** The paper defines its target quantity in Equation (1) with a `1/n^2` normalization. As acknowledged in Footnote 1, this is technically a V-statistic. The standard U-statistic includes only distinct pairs `i != j` and has a `1/(n(n-1))` normalization. While related, the distinction is important for statistical precision, and the manuscript should have either used the standard definition or been more explicit about this choice.
    *   **Unsupported Claims:** Footnote 2 and Remark 2 claim the results can be generalized to nodes holding multiple observations by "splitting each node... and new edges weighted judiciously." This is a non-trivial extension, and the paper provides no details on the weighting scheme or how the analysis would be affected. This claim is unsubstantiated.
    *   **Obscure Notation:** The update rule in Algorithm 2 is written in a needlessly complex way. The term `p_k * m_k(t)` is simply the number of times node `k` has been activated. The update is a standard running average, but the notation `(1 - 1/(p_i m_i))` obscures this and introduces potential confusion (e.g., division by zero at initialization, although it is handled correctly).
    *   **Formatting:** The references are poorly formatted, with missing information (publisher, journal, volume, etc.) for several key entries (e.g., [1], [2], [3], [8]). This suggests a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.

2.  **Discrepancy Between Theory and Experiments:**
    *   A major concern is the conflict between the theoretical rates and the experimental results in Figure 3b. The theory proves a faster `O(1/t)` rate for GoSta-sync and a slower `O(log t/t)` rate for GoSta-async. However, the plot shows the asynchronous algorithm converging slightly *faster* than the synchronous one. The authors dismiss this as "comparable performance," but this discrepancy requires a thorough explanation. It may be that the definition of an "iteration" is inconsistent between the two experiments, or that the constants in the theoretical bounds dominate the asymptotic terms. Without a discussion, this contradiction casts doubt on the practical relevance of the theoretical analysis.

3.  **Methodological Details:**
    *   The synchronous model in Algorithm 1 is somewhat unusual. An "iteration" consists of all `n` nodes updating their local estimates based on `H(X_p, Y_p)`, followed by a *single* random edge swap and averaging step. This seems communication-light but computationally heavy per iteration compared to more standard gossip models. The implications of this modeling choice are not discussed.

**Recommendation**

While the paper presents a novel and promising approach to an important problem, the numerous inaccuracies, presentation flaws, and the critical unexplained discrepancy between theory and experiments prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The error in the AUC formula is particularly concerning. The work has clear potential, but it requires a very thorough revision to address the issues of clarity, correctness, and consistency.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a relevant problem with a novel and intuitive algorithm.
*   Provides a clear improvement over the primary prior work [17].
*   The core theoretical analysis appears sound.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   A significant number of minor but cumulative flaws indicate a lack of care and polish.
*   A key motivating example (AUC) is incorrectly formulated.
*   A critical contradiction between the theoretical rates and the empirical performance of the asynchronous algorithm is left unexplained.
*   Claims regarding extensions (e.g., multiple observations per node) are not substantiated.

**Decision:** Reject.
