Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network, where each node holds one data point and nodes can communicate only with neighbors. While standard gossip algorithms handle separable sums (e.g., averages of f(Xi)), U-statistics require averaging over all pairs, which is not directly separable. The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms:

- GoSta-sync (synchronous): each node maintains an auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via edge swaps; at every global iteration t, each node updates its estimate by a running average with weight 1/t, and one randomly drawn edge performs pairwise estimate averaging and swaps auxiliaries. Using an analysis based on a “phantom network” construction, they prove convergence in expectation to the desired U-statistic with rate O(1/t), with constants depending on data dispersion terms and the spectral gap of the graph via W2(G) = I − (1/|E|)L(G).

- GoSta-async (asynchronous): removes global synchronization; only the pair on the randomly sampled edge updates. Each node maintains a local counter mk that accumulates 1/pk whenever it is selected (pk = 2dk/|E|), so that E[mk(t)] = t, yielding unbiased running-average weights 1/(pk mk). They prove convergence in expectation with rate O((log t)/t), with a graph-dependent constant.

The work contrasts with U2-gossip [17], which swaps two auxiliaries per iteration without averaging. The authors reanalyze U2-gossip and derive a refined bound with an overall √n/t factor, arguing their methods are faster and lighter in memory/communication. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter over several network topologies support the claims.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of the challenge in extending gossip to pairwise statistics; principled combination of auxiliary observation propagation and estimate averaging.
- Synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, the latter compatible with standard Poisson clock models for decentralized systems.
- Convergence proofs with explicit dependence on spectral gap, aligning with consensus literature; insightful “phantom nodes” analysis device to decouple propagation and averaging.
- Practical resource advantages over U2-gossip: one auxiliary per node, one edge activation per iteration, asynchronous feasibility.
- Empirical evaluation on real datasets and multiple topologies, consistently favoring GoSta over U2-gossip; discussion of how connectivity impacts speed.

Weaknesses
- Guarantees are in expectation only; no high-probability or almost-sure bounds, nor finite-time MSE bounds that combine bias and variance due to stochastic edge sampling.
- The asynchronous bound has a log t/t rate with an unspecified c′(G); no explicit relationship to spectral quantities is given, limiting interpretability and design guidance.
- The assumption that each node knows |E| (to compute pk) may be unrealistic in some decentralized settings; alternatives or robustness to misspecification are not discussed.
- Some notation/clarity issues: matrix norms in Theorem 1 (e.g., ‖H − h1>‖) are ambiguous because the norm is defined as ℓ2; specifying Frobenius/operator norms would help.
- Experimental scope is modest: only two tasks, limited range of graph sizes, and limited analysis of communication/sample complexity to reach a target error.

Quality
- The algorithms are well-specified and appear technically sound. The use of a single auxiliary undergoing a random walk to estimate hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi,Xj), combined with consensus averaging, is coherent. The asynchronous use of mk with increments 1/pk ensures that, conditionally on selections, the local running averages are unbiased in expectation.
- The spectral-gap-dependent bounds for the synchronous case are standard and credible given the Wα(G) formulation; the dependence on data dispersion terms is a useful refinement.
- The asynchronous analysis is less explicit; providing a more detailed characterization of c′(G) and conditions under which the log factor is tight would strengthen the technical contribution.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with algorithms and main theorems clearly stated. The phantom network construction is explained clearly despite being used only for analysis.
- Minor clarity issues: specify the matrix norm used in bounds; ensure consistency in notation for W1(G) and W2(G); some formatting artifacts in Algorithm 1 and the block matrices could be cleaned up.
- The assumptions (connected and non-bipartite graph; H symmetric with H(x,x)=0) are stated, though discussion of their necessity (e.g., bipartite issues) could be expanded.

Originality
- The problem setting (U-statistics via gossip) has prior work [17], but the proposed approach differs materially by combining single-auxiliary propagation with averaging and by providing an asynchronous variant. The phantom node analysis is a novel and neat technique in this context.
- The refined analysis of U2-gossip is also a useful contribution for the community.

Significance
- U-statistics cover widely used metrics (variance, AUC, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter), making the problem practically relevant in decentralized learning and sensing. The reduction in memory/communication and support for asynchrony are important for realistic deployments.
- Theoretical rates are competitive, and empirical results suggest practical benefits. Adding variance/high-probability bounds and explicit communication complexity to ε-accuracy would further increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and previously under-explored decentralized computation problem with practical importance.
- Provides both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms with provable convergence and improved resource usage over prior work.
- Analysis connects rates to spectral gap; experiments on real data corroborate theory and show gains over U2-gossip.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of high-probability/MSE bounds and incomplete characterization of c′(G) limit theoretical depth for the asynchronous case.
- Assumption that nodes know |E| may be restrictive; robustness or workarounds are not discussed.
- Experimental evaluation could be broader (larger graphs, more metrics, communication rounds to ε-error).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide high-probability or mean-square error bounds, at least for the synchronous case, and clarify the dependence of c′(G) on spectral quantities in the asynchronous case.
- Clarify norm choices in Theorem 1 and unify notation for matrix norms; improve algorithm and matrix block formatting.
- Discuss how to handle unknown |E| (e.g., local estimation, push-sum variants) and potential extensions to time-varying or directed graphs.
- Report communication complexity (edge activations, message sizes) to reach a target relative error; include larger-scale experiments and sensitivity to H’s computational cost.
- Consider extensions to higher-order U-statistics and non-symmetric kernels.
