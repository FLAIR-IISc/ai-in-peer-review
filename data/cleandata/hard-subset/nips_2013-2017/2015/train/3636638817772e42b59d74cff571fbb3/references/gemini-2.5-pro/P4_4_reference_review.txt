### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. U-statistics, such as sample variance or the Area Under the Curve (AUC), are averages over pairs of data points, making them non-separable and thus incompatible with standard gossip averaging algorithms which compute sums of the form $\sum_i f(X_i)$. The authors propose novel synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) algorithms to solve this problem. The core idea is to combine two processes: 1) data propagation, where each node's data point performs a random walk on the network via swapping with neighbors, and 2) estimate averaging, where nodes exchange and average their local estimates of the U-statistic.

For the analysis, the authors introduce a novel and elegant "phantom network" construction. This analytical tool reformulates the problem on a larger graph of size $O(n^2)$, allowing the coupled dynamics of data propagation and estimate averaging to be modeled and analyzed using linear algebra on a state transition matrix. The authors prove that their synchronous algorithm converges in expectation at a rate of $O(1/t)$, while the asynchronous version converges at $O(\log t/t)$. These rates depend on the spectral gap of the network graph, indicating faster convergence on better-connected networks.

The proposed methods are compared to the only known prior work on this problem, U2-gossip by Pelckmans et al. [17]. The authors demonstrate that their GoSta algorithms offer significant advantages, including lower memory and communication costs, and a theoretically faster convergence rate. These claims are substantiated by experiments on real datasets for estimating AUC and within-cluster point scatter across various network topologies.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality (Technical Soundness)**

The paper is technically very strong.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is the main highlight. The introduction of the "phantom network" to decouple the data propagation and estimate averaging processes is a creative and powerful analytical technique. The resulting convergence proofs for both synchronous and asynchronous settings appear sound and provide clear convergence rates. The derived bounds insightfully connect the convergence speed to both data-dependent properties (via norms of the kernel matrix) and network topology (via the spectral gap), which is consistent with the broader literature on gossip algorithms. The claims of superiority over the previous state-of-the-art [17] are well-supported by both a refined theoretical analysis of the prior method and compelling empirical evidence. The experiments are well-designed, using real-world datasets and standard network models to validate the theoretical findings.

*   **Weaknesses:** The theoretical analysis is limited to convergence in expectation. While common in this field, results on almost sure convergence or high-probability bounds would have further strengthened the claims. The proofs are deferred to the supplementary material, which is standard practice but makes a full audit of the technical details within the main paper's scope impossible.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation for the problem is clear, and the limitations of existing gossip algorithms are well-explained. The proposed algorithms are described precisely in pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2). The novel analytical approach using the "phantom network" is introduced with a helpful illustration (Figure 1) and a clear, step-by-step motivation, making a complex proof strategy accessible to the reader. The experimental section is also very clear, with figures that effectively communicate the performance comparison between the proposed methods and the baseline.

*   **Weaknesses:** The description of the competing U2-gossip algorithm in the main text is brief. While a detailed description is provided in the supplement, including a slightly more explicit summary in Section 4.1 would have made the comparison more self-contained for readers unfamiliar with [17].

#### **Originality**

The paper presents significant original contributions.

*   **Strengths:** While the problem of distributed U-statistic estimation has been previously addressed [17], it remains a niche and challenging area. The proposed solution, which synergistically combines data swapping for propagation with standard estimate averaging, is a novel approach for this problem. The most original contribution is arguably the "phantom network" proof technique. This method for analyzing coupled distributed processes is non-trivial and could potentially be adapted to analyze other complex multi-process algorithms in decentralized systems. The paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior work, providing a head-to-head comparison on multiple fronts (memory, communication, convergence).

*   **Weaknesses:** The core algorithmic components (random walks via swapping, estimate averaging) are themselves standard primitives in distributed algorithms. The novelty lies in their specific combination and the analysis thereof.

#### **Significance**

The work is significant and likely to have an impact on the field.

*   **Strengths:** U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and the need for decentralized computation is growing with the rise of large-scale distributed data (e.g., in sensor networks or federated learning). This paper provides a practical, efficient, and well-analyzed solution to a relevant problem. The proposed algorithms are simple to implement and demonstrably outperform the previous state-of-the-art, making them likely to be adopted by practitioners and researchers. The novel analytical framework is also a valuable contribution that could inspire future theoretical work on distributed algorithms.

*   **Weaknesses:** The direct applicability is for a specific class of statistics (degree-two U-statistics). While this class is broad and important, it does not cover all distributed estimation tasks.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles an important, practical problem and proposes a novel and elegant solution that significantly improves upon the state-of-the-art.
2.  **Technical Depth:** The theoretical analysis is a standout feature, introducing a creative and powerful "phantom network" technique that is a contribution in its own right.
3.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The work is presented with exceptional clarity. It is a complete contribution, offering new algorithms, rigorous theory, and thorough empirical validation.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The algorithms are simple, efficient (in terms of communication and memory), and applicable to a wide range of important metrics like AUC and sample variance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The convergence analysis is in expectation, which is a standard but weaker mode of convergence compared to almost sure or high-probability results.
2.  **Incremental Problem Formulation:** The problem is an extension of the canonical gossip averaging problem, which could be viewed as an incremental step rather than a completely new direction. However, the non-separability of U-statistics makes this a non-trivial extension.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are substantially stronger. This is a high-quality paper that makes clear and significant contributions to the field of distributed machine learning.
