

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computation: the inability of existing gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations), which are important in machine learning applications like AUC estimation and clustering metrics. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) that extend traditional gossip methods by incorporating data propagation through auxiliary observations and local averaging. They introduce a novel "phantom nodes" technique for convergence analysis, showing O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence rates for synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively, with constants that depend on both data characteristics and network topology.

**Strengths:**
1. **Significant Problem Addressed:** The paper tackles a well-motivated problem that has been overlooked in the gossip literature, with clear applications in distributed machine learning (AUC, within-cluster scatter). The gap between existing gossip algorithms (which handle separable functions) and U-statistics is well-articulated.

2. **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence proofs are technically sound, with the "phantom nodes" reformulation providing an elegant approach to separate data propagation and averaging steps. The connection between convergence rates and network spectral properties (via the spectral gap) is insightful and well-motivated.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments on real datasets (SMVguide3 for AUC, Wine Quality for within-cluster scatter) convincingly demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over U2-gossip [17] in terms of convergence speed and variance reduction across different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz).

4. **Clear Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper effectively highlights the limitations of U2-gossip (higher memory/communication costs, lack of asynchronous operation) and provides refined convergence analysis that explains why GoSta performs better.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity of Novel Technique:** While the "phantom nodes" approach is mathematically elegant, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation or visualization to help readers grasp this key conceptual contribution. The supplementary material could be leveraged to provide additional intuition.

2. **Scalability Discussion:** The paper doesn't address how the algorithms scale to extremely large networks (e.g., millions of nodes), which is critical for real-world applications. The phantom nodes analysis suggests the method might face scalability challenges, but this isn't discussed.

3. **Comparative Analysis of Constants:** The paper claims "faster convergence" but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the constants in the convergence rates (e.g., the relationship between the O(1/t) constants of GoSta and U2-gossip). This is important for understanding the practical implications.

4. **Asynchronous Rate Justification:** The theoretical convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm (O(log t/t)) is slower than the synchronous version (O(1/t)), but the paper doesn't fully explain why this difference exists or how significant it is in practice. The experimental results show comparable performance, suggesting the theoretical gap may not be as pronounced as the analysis suggests.

5. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** Beyond U2-gossip, the paper doesn't compare to other relevant distributed algorithms for specific applications (e.g., distributed AUC computation methods), which would strengthen the significance claim.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to distributed computation by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, a class of problems previously outside their scope. The theoretical analysis is strong, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical implications are significant for distributed machine learning applications. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and appropriately positioned relative to prior work.

The main weaknesses relate to the clarity of the novel technique and the need for more detailed discussion of scalability and constant factors in the convergence rates. These are important but not fatal issues for a NIPS submission.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper addresses a significant problem with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art in distributed computation. The contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful for NIPS, with the theoretical insights and practical benefits clearly demonstrated. The weaknesses noted are primarily matters of presentation and additional analysis rather than fundamental flaws in the work.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a more intuitive explanation or figure illustrating the "phantom nodes" concept.
2. Discuss scalability to large networks (e.g., computational complexity as n grows).
3. Provide a more detailed comparison of the constants in the convergence rates.
4. Include a brief discussion of why the asynchronous rate is theoretically slower but practically comparable.
5. Consider adding a comparison to a relevant baseline method for one of the applications (e.g., a distributed AUC algorithm).
