Summary
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network, where each node holds one observation and nodes can communicate only with neighbors. The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms: a synchronous version (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous version (GoSta-async). The key idea is to combine (i) propagation of auxiliary observations via random neighbor swaps (so each node gradually encounters other nodes’ data) and (ii) averaging of local estimates. A notable analysis technique is a “phantom nodes” construction that decouples the averaging and propagation dynamics.

Main theoretical results: in connected, non-bipartite graphs, both algorithms produce local estimates whose expectations converge to the global U-statistic. The synchronous algorithm achieves O(1/t) convergence in expectation with explicit constants involving data dispersion terms and a network dependence through the spectral gap. The asynchronous algorithm achieves O(log t/t) convergence in expectation. Compared to prior work (U2-gossip), GoSta requires only one auxiliary observation per node and one edge activation per iteration (vs two), and the authors derive a refined bound for U2-gossip that reveals an extra √n factor, explaining its slower convergence.

Experiments on real data (AUC and within-cluster scatter) over several topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts–Strogatz) show GoSta’s faster convergence and smaller across-node variance.

Relation to prior work
The paper is situated within the literature on gossip/consensus algorithms for decentralized averaging (Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al. 2010; Kempe et al. 2003) and extends it to pairwise functionals (U-statistics), a setting previously explored only by U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens 2009). The spectral analysis and dependence on the graph Laplacian spectral gap aligns with classical consensus results. The phantom-graph analysis trick appears novel in this context.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem: computing U-statistics is central to many ML tasks (AUC, Kendall τ, within-cluster scatter). Addressing it in a decentralized/gossip setting is timely and important.
- Algorithmic contributions: simple, implementable protocols with low per-iteration communication/memory, especially compared to U2-gossip (one auxiliary sample per node; one edge per iteration).
- Theory: convergence in expectation with explicit rates; dependence on spectral gap is interpretable and standard in gossip; the synchronous O(1/t) rate is an improvement over U2-gossip’s effective rate; the refined comparison bound for U2-gossip is informative.
- Analysis technique: phantom graph construction is clean and may be useful beyond this paper.
- Empirics: consistent improvements over U2-gossip on two tasks and multiple network types; variance reduction across nodes due to averaging is well demonstrated.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation only. High-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration or mixing-time arguments) or almost-sure convergence would strengthen the claims, especially for practitioners.
- The asynchronous bound is looser (O(log t/t)) and hides constants c′(G); more explicit constants and discussion of tightness would help. Is O(1/t) achievable asynchronously with a different stepsize or “lazy” updates?
- Assumptions: non-bipartite graphs and uniform random edge selection. It would be useful to discuss standard fixes (e.g., laziness/self-loops) to handle bipartite graphs, and the impact of non-uniform or time-varying edge selection.
- Knowledge requirements: GoSta-async needs pk = 2dk/|E| and thereby |E|. In many networks, |E| may be unknown locally. Can pk be estimated online (e.g., via degree gossip) without bias? What is the performance impact?
- Complexity/fairness of comparisons: In GoSta-sync, every node computes H(Xk, Yk) at each iteration (global clock), while U2-gossip requires two swaps per iteration. A communication- or computation-normalized comparison (bytes or H evaluations to reach a target error) would better isolate algorithmic efficiency. Similarly, wall-clock or event-based comparisons would be valuable for asynchronous settings.
- Scope of experiments: Only two U-statistics/tasks and moderate-scale datasets. Testing on larger n and d, and additional U-statistics (e.g., Kendall τ, Gini mean difference), would increase confidence. Reporting sensitivity to spectral gap (e.g., varying Watts–Strogatz rewiring) and to skewed class balance for AUC would also be informative.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Some notational points could be clarified: define the H matrix explicitly as [H(Xi, Xj)], specify norms used in the bounds (Frobenius vs operator), and ensure block matrices B and C have clearly stated shapes. Fix minor typos (e.g., “rate 1 Poisson process” should be “rate-1 Poisson process”; “perfomance”; “svmguide3” spelling).
- The intuition behind the data-dependent terms in the rate (‖h − Û1n‖ and ‖H − h1⊤‖) is helpful; adding a brief explanation or bound relating these to variance of H would aid interpretation.

Originality
- The problem setting (gossip for U-statistics) is relatively unexplored; the averaging+swap design and phantom node analysis are novel contributions. The differentiation from U2-gossip is clearly articulated.

Significance
- U-statistics underpin many pairwise ML measures; enabling their decentralized computation with proven rates is impactful. The approach should be applicable as a building block in decentralized optimization of pairwise losses. Broader uptake would be helped by stronger empirical validation and practical considerations (unknown |E|, message loss/delay robustness).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored decentralized estimation problem.
- Simple algorithms with improved memory/communication profile over prior work.
- Solid theory with interpretable network dependence; clear improvement over U2-gossip.
- Empirical results consistent with the theory across multiple graph topologies.

Cons against acceptance
- Expectation-only guarantees; asynchronous bound is relatively weak and with unspecified constants.
- Experimental scope is limited and comparisons could better normalize for computation/communication.
- Some practicality gaps (knowledge of |E|; bipartite/time-varying graphs; robustness) are not addressed.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide high-probability or almost-sure convergence guarantees?
- Can pk be estimated online without bias, removing the need for |E|?
- How do results extend to bipartite or time-varying graphs (e.g., with self-loops/lazy random walks)?
- Can you report error vs total H evaluations and bytes communicated to fairly compare to U2-gossip?
- Any empirical results for additional U-statistics (Kendall τ) or larger-scale settings?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. This is a well-motivated and technically sound contribution with clear improvements over prior work. Strengthening empirical evaluation and addressing practical assumptions would further increase its impact.
