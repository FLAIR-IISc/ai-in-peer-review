Summary
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. Classic gossip algorithms efficiently compute sums of separable functions but do not handle pairwise quantities such as U-statistics. The authors propose two algorithms, GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous), that combine two iterative mechanisms: (i) data propagation via swapping of auxiliary observations (each observation performs a random walk on the graph), and (ii) averaging of local estimates. A key analytic device is a “phantom nodes” construction that lifts the dynamics to an O(n^2)-sized graph to separate the averaging and propagation components. The authors prove that the expected local estimates converge to the true U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous). The bounds include data-dependent terms capturing dispersion of H(Xi, Xj) and network-dependent factors tied to the spectral gap. Compared to the prior U2-gossip algorithm, GoSta reduces memory/communication (one auxiliary sample instead of two; one edge activation per iteration instead of two) and yields faster convergence in expectation. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter over several graph families corroborate the theoretical claims.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on randomized gossip averaging (e.g., Boyd et al.) and geographic/accelerated gossip, extending this literature beyond separable objectives to U-statistics. The most closely related prior is U2-gossip, which propagates two auxiliary observations without averaging; the authors provide an improved analysis of U2-gossip (refined rate with √n factor) and a head-to-head empirical comparison showing GoSta’s advantage. The proofs leverage spectral graph theory and standard Poisson clock models for asynchrony, both widely used in the gossip literature.

Quality
- Theory: The analysis is technically sound and clearly outlines the block-triangular transition operator and its spectral properties. The O(1/t) synchronous rate with explicit dependence on λ2(W2(G)) is plausible and aligns with averaging dynamics plus mixing of random walks. The asynchronous bound O(log t / t) is weaker but expected under partial updates. The data-dependent norm terms are informative; however, more intuition on how they behave for common H (e.g., bounded/Lipschitz kernels) would help interpret practical rates. The results are in expectation; high-probability or MSE bounds would strengthen the contribution.
- Assumptions: Uniform random edge selection and non-bipartite connectivity are standard. The asynchronous variant requires nodes to know pk = 2dk/|E| and maintain counters mk; this is realistic if degrees and |E| are known or can be estimated, but the paper could discuss robustness to misspecification or how to estimate |E| in-network.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main ideas are explained sufficiently for review. The comparison to U2-gossip includes both theoretical and empirical components.
- Experiments: The evaluation includes two meaningful U-statistics, three graph types with different spectral gaps, multiple runs, and comparisons to U2-gossip. The results support claims on speed and variance reduction. It would be valuable to include message/byte complexity per epsilon-accuracy to quantify communication savings, and to visualize how spectral gap correlates with convergence across more graphs.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The phantom graph construction is explained clearly with the block matrix decomposition; minor additions could improve readability:
- Clarify early that H(X, X) = 0 is required to avoid diagonal terms; note how to handle general symmetric kernels (subtract diagonal if needed).
- Explicitly state communication/memory per node/iteration and contrast to U2-gossip in a small summary.
- In the async algorithm, explain how nodes obtain |E| (or provide a distributed estimator) and discuss what happens under non-uniform edge sampling.
There are a few typographical issues (e.g., hyphenation/line breaks) and some references lack details; polishing would help.

Originality
The combination of random-walk-based data propagation with averaging for U-statistics, the phantom-node analytical framework, and the asynchronous variant with unbiased local counters appear novel relative to U2-gossip and standard gossip literature. The refined analysis of U2-gossip is also a useful contribution.

Significance
Estimating U-statistics decentrally is relevant for many tasks (AUC, rank correlation, within-cluster scatter). The algorithms are simple, resource-efficient, and improve convergence compared to prior work, with explicit dependence on graph connectivity. This is likely to be useful for practitioners in distributed learning and for researchers building decentralized optimization methods for pairwise objectives.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; addresses a gap beyond separable functions.
- Elegant, simple algorithms with low memory/communication.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with interpretable graph/data dependencies.
- Empirical results consistent with theory; refined analysis of U2-gossip.

Cons
- Convergence guarantees are in expectation; no high-probability or finite-time MSE bounds.
- Async version assumes knowledge of |E|; robustness and practical estimation are not discussed.
- Experiments could include more baselines (e.g., push-sum variants, geographic gossip adaptations) and communication-accuracy tradeoffs.
- Focused on degree-2 U-statistics; discussion of higher-order extensions is brief.

Questions/Suggestions for authors
- Can you provide high-probability bounds (via Azuma/ Freedman) using mixing times of the random walks?
- How sensitive is GoSta-async to errors in pk (e.g., approximate |E|)? Could adaptive step sizes remove the need for |E|?
- Can the analysis extend to non-uniform edge sampling or time-varying graphs?
- Any privacy implications of swapping raw observations? Could sketches suffice (e.g., random features of Xi)?
- Briefly discuss extension to higher-order U-statistics and the computational/communication implications.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a clear, novel, and practical advance on decentralized estimation of U-statistics, with solid theory and supporting experiments. My confidence is moderate.
