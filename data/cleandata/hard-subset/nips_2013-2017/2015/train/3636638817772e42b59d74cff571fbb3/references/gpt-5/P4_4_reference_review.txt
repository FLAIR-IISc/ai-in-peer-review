Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics Ûn(H) = (1/n2)∑i,j H(Xi,Xj) over a network where each node holds one observation. Existing gossip algorithms handle separable sums but not pairwise statistics efficiently. The main contribution is two randomized gossip algorithms that interleave (i) propagation of observations via swaps along randomly selected edges and (ii) averaging of local scalar estimates: a synchronous version (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous version (GoSta-async). The analysis uses an original “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from estimate averaging. For GoSta-sync, the authors show convergence in expectation to Ûn(H) at rate O(1/t), with explicit data-dependent terms and a network-dependent term controlled by the spectral gap 1−λ2(W2(G)). For GoSta-async, they show O((log t)/t) convergence in expectation with a graph-dependent constant. The paper contrasts with U2-gossip [17], which propagates two auxiliary observations without averaging; the authors derive a refined O(√n/t) bound for U2-gossip, arguing GoSta should be faster in practice, especially as n grows. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter on several network topologies (complete, grid, Watts–Strogatz) support the theoretical claims and show lower variance across nodes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and convergence analyses are plausible and align with standard tools in distributed averaging (e.g., contraction via spectral gap, Poisson clock model, block-triangular transition matrices). The phantom network reformulation is a neat device to track observation propagation. The synchronous bound cleanly separates data dispersion terms (∥h−Û1∥ and ∥H−h1⊤∥) from network mixing via c(G)=1−λ2(W2(G)). The asynchronous scheme uses node-specific counters scaled by pk=2dk/|E|; the step-size 1/(pk mk) effectively averages over the number of times a node is selected, maintaining unbiasedness in expectation. Proofs are in the supplement; the main steps presented are consistent with prior analysis frameworks (e.g., [2], [3]).
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are well-chosen to illustrate both applications and network effects, with multiple runs and error bars. The comparison to U2-gossip demonstrates lower error and variance and widening gaps with n, coherent with the theory. A sync-vs-async comparison is included.

Areas where rigor could be improved or clarified:
- The asynchronous bound hides constants as c′(G) and loses the data-dependent structure present in the synchronous case; making c′ explicit and separating data and graph terms would strengthen the result.
- The norm notation is ambiguous when applied to matrices: the paper defines ‖·‖ as the ℓ2 norm, but uses ‖H‖ and ‖H−h1⊤‖; clarify whether these are spectral or Frobenius norms.
- Convergence is in expectation; high-probability or almost-sure guarantees (standard in gossip/Markov mixing analyses) would be valuable.
- The uniform random edge selection assumption is standard in analysis, but the implementability in a fully decentralized setting typically uses node-initiated neighbor selection with a stochastic matrix P. It would help to state how the assumptions map to implementable local rules.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well-organized, with clear algorithm boxes and a helpful overview of the phantom graph approach. Minor presentation issues:
- Pseudocode has formatting glitches (e.g., stray colons, line breaks).
- Some language issues (“need not to be aware”) and equation references could be tightened.
- Define all symbols upon first use (e.g., B and C in the block matrices; Wα(G) is defined but the relationship to Laplacian eigenvalues could be summarized inline).
- Explicitly state the computational and communication costs per iteration (scalars vs d-dimensional swaps) and memory per node for both GoSta and U2-gossip.

Originality
The combination of observation propagation with averaging for U-statistics and the phantom nodes analysis appear novel relative to [17], which lacked averaging. The asynchronous variant and the refined bound for U2-gossip add value. The approach extends the classical randomized gossip literature [2,10,18] to a non-separable statistic with improved rates and lower resource usage than prior work.

Significance
U-statistics underpin many metrics (AUC, rank correlations, cluster scatter) that arise in decentralized analytics. A simple, analyzable, and resource-efficient gossip algorithm for such statistics is practically relevant, and the dependence on spectral gap connects well to network design. The work can also serve as a building block for decentralized optimization involving pairwise losses. The empirical section is convincing but could further strengthen claims by reporting communication to ε-accuracy (in bits or d-vectors exchanged) and scaling on larger/different topologies.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumption of knowledge of |E| in GoSta-async (to compute pk): this can be unrealistic. Consider schemes that only require local degrees (e.g., activation with node clocks selecting a neighbor uniformly, leading to known selection probabilities up to a normalizing constant) or adaptive estimation of pk.
- Limited experimental baselines: Include sampling-based approximations (e.g., running pairwise reservoir sampling with gossip averaging) or one-pass sketch-based baselines, and report communication/compute per ε-accuracy.
- Only expectation bounds are provided; high-probability bounds (e.g., via Azuma or mixing-time arguments) would improve robustness claims.
- The bipartite graph exclusion is standard for averaging, but a brief note on how to handle it (lazy updates or self-loops) would make the method more complete.
- Clarify the per-iteration communication: GoSta-sync swaps one d-dimensional observation along one edge per iteration; U2-gossip swaps two observations on two edges. Provide a table comparing memory and communication precisely.
- Minor typos and formatting; ensure all references are complete.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear and relevant problem with practical importance.
- Simple algorithms with provable convergence; improved rates and resource use over [17].
- Novel analytical technique (phantom nodes) and insightful bounds linking data dispersion and spectral gap.
- Empirical results across multiple network types and tasks corroborate theory.

Cons:
- Asynchronous analysis is looser; constants and data dependence are not explicit.
- Requires knowledge of |E| for unbiased updates in async setting.
- Only expectation guarantees; no concentration results.
- Experimental baselines and communication-to-accuracy reporting could be expanded.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and useful advance on a timely decentralized estimation problem, with solid theoretical and empirical support. The above clarifications and additional experiments would further strengthen it.
