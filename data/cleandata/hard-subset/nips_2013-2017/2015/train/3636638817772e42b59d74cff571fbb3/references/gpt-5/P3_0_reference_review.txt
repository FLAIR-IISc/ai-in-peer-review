Summary
The paper addresses distributed estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. The key challenge is that U-statistics couple pairs of observations across nodes, unlike separable sums handled by standard gossip. The authors propose two algorithms:
- GoSta-sync (synchronous): each node maintains an auxiliary observation Yk (initially Xk). At each global tick, every node updates its running average Zk with H(Xk, Yk); then a random edge is selected, the two incident nodes average their Z’s and swap Y’s. Hence auxiliary data perform coupled random walks while estimates are averaged.
- GoSta-async (asynchronous): a random edge is selected at each iteration; the two endpoints average Z’s, update their running averages with step size 1/(p_k m_k) where m_k is an adjusted counter to make E[m_k]=t (p_k ∝ degree), and swap Y’s.

Main results: in both regimes, E[Zk(t)] → Ûn(H) under connected, non-bipartite graphs. Rates are O(1/t) for synchronous with problem- and graph-dependent constants (via spectral gap 1−λ2(W2)), and O(log t/t) for asynchronous. The analysis introduces a “phantom network” of size O(n^2) to decouple data propagation (random walks of Y) and averaging (gossip on Z), yielding a block-triangular expected transition operator. The paper compares to U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010], deriving a refined bound showing √n/t degradation due to lack of averaging, and demonstrates empirical improvements on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several graphs.

Strengths
- Problem novelty and relevance: U-statistics include many key metrics (variance, AUC, Kendall’s tau, within-cluster scatter). Extending gossip beyond separable sums is impactful for distributed sensing and ML on networks.
- Algorithmic simplicity and practicality: only one auxiliary per node, one edge interaction per iteration; communication/memory lower than U2-gossip.
- Clean analysis via phantom nodes: the block structure neatly separates averaging and propagation; spectral gap appears naturally, aligning with intuition and prior gossip theory.
- Synchronous rate with interpretable constants: data-dependent dispersion terms and dependence on 1−λ2(W2)=βn−1/|E|.
- Empirical evaluation on two representative U-statistics and three graph families corroborates the theory and the advantage over U2-gossip; asynchrony performs comparably in practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- Expectation-only convergence: Results are in expectation; no high-probability, almost-sure convergence, or MSE bounds. Given the stochasticity of both random walks and gossip, concentration bounds (e.g., via martingale techniques or mixing-time arguments) would significantly strengthen guarantees.
- Asynchronous bound is coarse: The O(log t/t) bound with constant c′(G)·||H|| drops the informative data-dependent dispersion terms and gives no explicit spectral-gap dependence. It likely can be tightened to O(1/t) with explicit graph constants, using standard stochastic approximation arguments and the fact that 1/(p_k m_k)=1/s_k after s_k activations.
- Knowledge of |E| in async: Algorithm 2 requires each node to know the global |E| to set p_k and to update m_k. This is a strong assumption for decentralized settings. Can one avoid this by:
  - Using local counters s_k (number of activations) with 1/s_k step sizes and proving unbiasedness/convergence?
  - Employing Metropolis-Hastings or push-sum-style normalization that eliminates dependence on |E|?
  - Using clock rates proportional to degrees to induce uniform edge selection without global knowledge?
- Activation model ambiguity: The asynchronous model posits an “equivalent” global clock drawing a uniform random edge each iteration. This is not the same as i.i.d. local Poisson clocks with uniform neighbor selection (which does not yield uniform edge sampling). The paper should clarify the activation mechanism implementable in a decentralized way and the conditions under which uniform edge sampling is realizable.
- Non-bipartite assumption: Standard for pairwise gossip mixing, but can be relaxed with lazy or weighted (Metropolis) gossip. It would be useful to comment on how to handle bipartite graphs (e.g., by adding self-loops/laziness) and how that affects constants.
- Comparison breadth: The comparison focuses on U2-gossip. It would be valuable to include:
  - Variants of U2 augmented with averaging to test whether the √n penalty is intrinsic to the “no averaging” design.
  - Push-sum or broadcast-style algorithms adapted to pairwise functions where appropriate communication primitives exist.
  - Larger n and sparser graphs to more clearly manifest spectral-gap dependencies.
- Generality: The restriction H(X,X)=0 simplifies inclusion of diagonal terms; while many kernels satisfy this in the examples, the paper should clarify how to handle general degree-2 U-statistics with the standard n(n−1) normalization and diagonal exclusion.
- Clarity/organization: While overall clear, some derivations are terse. Suggestions:
  - Provide more intuition for the phantom network and the roles of B and C; add a small illustrative example.
  - Clean up minor notational issues (e.g., consistent equation numbering, typo fixes).
  - Explicitly state the computational and communication costs per iteration for both algorithms and U2-gossip.

Significance and originality
- The paper addresses a meaningful gap in decentralized computation: gossip for pairwise-dependent statistics. The phantom-node analysis and the synchronous algorithm with averaging are novel (to my knowledge) in this context. The results should be useful for practitioners needing AUC/Kendall/dispersion metrics in sensor and peer-to-peer systems. The approach could also serve as a building block for distributed optimization over U-statistics.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, relevant problem; simple and implementable algorithms.
- Solid theoretical contributions with spectral-gap dependence.
- Empirically compelling improvements over the main prior art.
- Analytical technique (phantom nodes) is interesting and potentially reusable.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of concentration/AS guarantees; async bound is weak and relies on global |E|.
- Activation model realism and implementability need clarification.
- Limited comparisons and ablations; some assumptions could be relaxed.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide MSE or high-probability bounds (even if conservative), at least in the synchronous case?
- Can the async analysis be strengthened to O(1/t) with explicit spectral-gap constants?
- How to implement uniform edge sampling (or an equivalent scheme) without global knowledge, and how would the bounds change with Metropolis weights?
- Could you empirically study the impact of spectral gap by sweeping Watts-Strogatz rewiring p and reporting rates vs 1−λ2?
- Any thoughts on extending to U-statistics of order m>2?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a useful and original contribution with good theory and experiments. Addressing the async assumptions and tightening the bounds would further strengthen the work. Confidence: high.
