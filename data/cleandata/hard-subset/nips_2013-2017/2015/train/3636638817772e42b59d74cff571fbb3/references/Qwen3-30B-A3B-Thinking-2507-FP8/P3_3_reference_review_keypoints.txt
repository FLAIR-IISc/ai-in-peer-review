- Novel problem & approach: Extends gossip algorithms to estimate U-statistics in decentralized networks via new "GoSta" synchronous and asynchronous algorithms.  
- Phantom node technique: Introduces an elegant phantom node construction to transform coupled data propagation and averaging into a tractable linear system for analysis.  
- Strong theoretical results: Provides rigorous convergence rates (O(1/t) synchronous, O(log t/t) asynchronous) that depend on data norms and network spectral gap.  
- Comparison with prior work: Shows GoSta outperforms U2-gossip in convergence and has lower memory and communication costs.  
- Practical validation: Experiments on real datasets (AUC, within-cluster scatter) and multiple topologies (complete, grid, Watts–Strogatz) confirm theoretical predictions and superiority over U2-gossip.  
- Synchronous vs asynchronous: Empirical comparison between variants is provided, helping practitioners choose based on network conditions.  
- Clear algorithm description: Algorithms are precisely described (Algorithms 1 and 2) and implementable, with a clear separation of data propagation and averaging steps.  
- Motivation gap: The necessity of the phantom node construction is not sufficiently motivated for application-focused readers; alternatives are not discussed.  
- Computational complexity missing: Lacks quantified per-iteration computational cost, especially for high-dimensional data and cost of computing H(X_i,X_j).  
- Network topology limits: Does not examine performance on highly sparse or adversarial graphs with small spectral gaps; behavior on poorly connected networks is unclear.  
- Limited baselines: Empirical comparisons are restricted to U2-gossip; more recent gossip variants or centralized baselines should be included.  
- Implementation assumptions: Asynchronous algorithm assumes nodes know their degree and total edges, which may not hold in dynamic or unknown-degree networks.  
- Overall assessment & recommendation: Technically strong, original, and impactful work with rigorous theory and convincing experiments; recommended for acceptance.  
- Pro/Con summary: Pro — novel theory (phantom nodes), faster convergence, lower resources; Con — incomplete complexity analysis, limited topology/baseline evaluation.
