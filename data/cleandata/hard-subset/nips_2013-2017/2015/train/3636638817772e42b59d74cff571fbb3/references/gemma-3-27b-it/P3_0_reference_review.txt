## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension of gossip algorithms to a challenging problem – the distributed estimation of U-statistics. The proposed GoSta algorithms (synchronous and asynchronous) offer improvements over existing approaches (specifically, [17]) in terms of convergence rate, memory footprint, and applicability to asynchronous settings. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. While the theoretical analysis is somewhat involved, it is clearly presented, and the connection to network properties (spectral gap) is insightful. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of estimating U-statistics in a decentralized setting where data is distributed across a network of nodes and communication is limited to pairwise gossip interactions. Existing gossip algorithms excel at computing averages but struggle with U-statistics due to their dependence on pairwise data combinations. The authors propose GoSta algorithms, which combine data propagation and averaging steps, and provide theoretical convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous scenarios. 

The related work section is comprehensive, correctly positioning the problem within the context of gossip algorithms and highlighting the limitations of existing approaches.  The discussion of [17] is particularly good, clearly outlining the shortcomings that GoSta addresses. The references to spectral graph theory are relevant and appropriately used in analyzing the convergence rates.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is a significant contribution. While the idea of combining propagation and averaging is intuitive, the detailed algorithmic design and analysis are novel.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The convergence rates, O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively, are respectable. The inclusion of data-dependent and network-dependent terms (spectral gap) in the bounds is a strength, demonstrating a nuanced understanding of the problem.
* **Improved Efficiency:** GoSta algorithms offer clear advantages over [17] in terms of memory consumption and communication costs. 
* **Asynchronous Algorithm:** The asynchronous version (GoSta-async) is particularly valuable, as it addresses a common challenge in distributed systems.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) are convincing and support the theoretical findings. The comparison with [17] clearly demonstrates the superiority of GoSta.
* **"Phantom Node" Technique:** The clever use of "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem and facilitate the analysis is a noteworthy methodological contribution.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, while correct, is quite dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory to fully grasp. Simplifying the presentation or providing more intuitive explanations would be beneficial.
* **Practical Implications of Spectral Gap:** While the theoretical analysis links convergence to the spectral gap, the paper doesn’t offer much guidance on how to *effectively* utilize this information in practice (e.g., network design to optimize the spectral gap). 
* **Limited Comparison to Other Baselines:** While a comparison to [17] is present, comparing with more standard distributed estimation methods (e.g., distributed gradient descent) could further highlight the benefits of the gossip-based approach.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of symmetric H(X,X) = 0 might be limiting in some applications. A discussion of how the algorithms could be adapted to handle non-symmetric kernels would be useful.




**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 4.1 (Synchronous):** The explanation of the "phantom network" G̃ is helpful, but could be visually enhanced with a clearer diagram (the current Figure 1 is a bit cluttered).
* **Theorem 1:**  Could the authors provide a more intuitive interpretation of the data-dependent terms (∥∥∥h− Ûn(H)1n∥∥∥ and ∥∥H− h1>n ∥∥) in the convergence bound?  What types of U-statistics will lead to faster or slower convergence based on these terms?
* **Section 4.2 (Asynchronous):** The connection between the Poisson process clock modeling and the practical implementation on asynchronous networks should be explained more clearly.  
* **Section 5 (Experiments):** The choice of network topologies is good. However, exploring how the network topology impacts performance (especially concerning the spectral gap) would be valuable. The authors mention that the gap is larger for complete graphs, but don’t show how this translates to a faster convergence speed in their experiment.
* **Minor:** Throughout the paper, consider standardizing the notation (e.g., using consistent symbols for matrices and vectors).

**5. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem in distributed estimation.
* Presents novel algorithms with proven convergence guarantees.
* Offers improvements over existing approaches in terms of efficiency and applicability.
* Supported by convincing experimental results.
* Technically sound and well-motivated.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is complex and might be inaccessible to some readers.
* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to other baselines.
* Limited discussion of practical implications of the spectral gap.

**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

* Simplify the explanation of the theoretical analysis, potentially adding more intuitive explanations.
* Expand the experimental evaluation with a comparison to more standard distributed estimation methods and explore the impact of network topology on performance.
* Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the spectral gap.
