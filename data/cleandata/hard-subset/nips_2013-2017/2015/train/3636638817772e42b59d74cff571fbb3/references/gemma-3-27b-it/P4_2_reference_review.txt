This paper introduces randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting. The core idea is to combine propagation of local observations with averaging of local estimates, leveraging a “phantom node” reformulation to analyze convergence. The authors demonstrate convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, and validate their approach empirically on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work on gossip algorithms, specifically citing [19, 18, 5, 10, 2, 15, 11, 9] for averaging and related tasks. It correctly identifies the limitation of standard gossip algorithms for U-statistics and contrasts its approach with the prior work of [17], claiming improvements in convergence, memory usage, and the ability to handle asynchronous settings. The connection to U-statistics is well-motivated, with clear examples given in the introduction (sample variance, AUC, Gini mean difference, etc.).  The paper also appropriately references relevant work on spectral graph theory [3] for bounding convergence rates based on network connectivity.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics represents a genuine contribution. While [17] addressed this problem previously, the authors claim and demonstrate improvements in convergence, resource usage, and asynchronous capability, which is valuable.
* **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, employing a "phantom node" reformulation to tackle the problem.  The convergence proofs, though not fully detailed in the main paper (reference to supplementary material), seem well-motivated, and the bounds are clearly stated.  The connection to the spectral gap of the network graph is a strong point.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation and overview. The notation is mostly standard and consistently used. Algorithm 1 and 2 are clearly presented.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) support the claims of the paper, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed algorithms over [17].  The investigation of different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) is also a strength.
* **Asynchronous Algorithm:** The extension to an asynchronous setting is a significant addition and addresses a practical limitation of the synchronous approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Detail in Proofs:** While the paper claims rigorous proofs, the main text only provides high-level summaries. Full details are relegated to the supplementary material, making it difficult for program committee members to fully assess the validity of the analysis.  A more substantial discussion of the key proof steps within the main paper would strengthen the submission.
* **Complexity of Phantom Node Reformulation:** The introduction of “phantom nodes” is a clever trick for analysis, but it could be made more intuitive. A clearer explanation of *why* this reformulation works and what benefits it provides beyond the mathematical convenience would be helpful.  The connection between the phantom nodes and the original problem needs to be more clearly articulated.
* **Discussion of Constant Factors:** The convergence rates are stated with leading constants. A more detailed discussion of these constants and their dependence on problem parameters (e.g., the magnitude of H, network properties) would enhance the practical relevance of the results.  How sensitive are the convergence rates to these constants in practice?
* **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While the comparison to [17] is appropriate, a comparison to simpler baselines (e.g., decentralized estimation without the specialized algorithms) would provide a more complete picture of the performance gains.
* **Notation could be improved**: Some notation is a little dense and could benefit from simplification. For example, the definition of Wα(G) could be slightly more explicit.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem – decentralized estimation of U-statistics – that is not well-solved by existing gossip algorithms.
* The proposed algorithms are novel and offer improvements in convergence, memory usage, and asynchronous capability compared to existing approaches.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous (though details are in the supplementary material) and provides meaningful convergence guarantees.
* The empirical results validate the approach on real datasets and demonstrate its practical effectiveness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed proofs in the main paper hinders a complete assessment of the technical soundness.
* The phantom node reformulation, while clever, could be explained more clearly.
* A more thorough comparison to simpler baselines and a discussion of the constant factors in the convergence rates would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and solid technical foundation. The main weakness is the lack of detailed proofs in the main body, but the supplementary material mitigates this to some extent. I believe the paper is suitable for NIPS, provided the authors can address the concerns regarding proof clarity and provide further insights into the practical implications of their results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and discussion.
