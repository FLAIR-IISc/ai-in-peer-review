This paper proposes randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a problem not well addressed by existing gossip algorithms. The core idea of using "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem and enable propagation and averaging steps is intriguing. While the theoretical convergence analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate improvements over a prior work (U2-gossip), I find several aspects of the presentation and methodology concerning, potentially warranting rejection despite the novelty.

**Summary:**

The paper tackles the challenge of efficiently computing U-statistics in a decentralized setting using gossip algorithms. It introduces GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, leveraging a novel "phantom node" technique for data propagation and averaging. Convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) are established for the synchronous and asynchronous versions, respectively. Empirical validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation confirms the superiority of the proposed algorithms compared to U2-gossip.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics is a valuable contribution, addressing a gap in the existing literature. The “phantom node” approach is a clever reformulation that facilitates the analysis.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence analysis appears mathematically sound, with bounds dependent on both data characteristics and network topology. The connection to the spectral gap of the network graph is insightful.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on real-world datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) demonstrate practical improvements over U2-gossip, supporting the theoretical claims.
*   **Clear Algorithm Descriptions:** Algorithm 1 and 2 are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Excessive Detail in Background:** Section 2 is overly detailed for a NIPS paper. While providing definitions is necessary, the level of detail regarding graph theory (e.g., bipartite graphs, stochastic matrices) feels unnecessarily exhaustive and detracts from the core contribution. This excessive background material makes the paper harder to read.
*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The notation `Ûn(H)` in Section 2.2 is slightly different from the usual U-statistic definition (missing a factor of n/(n-1) as the authors acknowledge in the footnote), but this difference isn't adequately justified.  This seemingly minor detail introduces ambiguity.
*   **Phantom Node Explanation Lacks Intuition:** While the "phantom node" technique is presented, the intuitive motivation behind it remains unclear. The explanation in Section 4.1 is mathematically formal but lacks a clear explanation of *why* this reformulation simplifies the problem or allows for the derived convergence rates.
*   **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison is largely limited to U2-gossip. While showing superiority over that algorithm is good, a comparison to more standard distributed estimation techniques (e.g., distributed stochastic gradient descent, or other averaging methods) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Experimental Setup Details Missing:** The experimental section lacks crucial details. For example, the network topology used in the simulations is described in general terms, but the specific parameters (e.g., number of nodes, edge probabilities) are not provided, hindering reproducibility. The implementation details are also missing.
*   **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The asynchronous convergence rate (O(log t/t)) is weaker than the synchronous rate (O(1/t)). While this is expected, the paper does not adequately discuss the trade-offs between the two algorithms or provide guidance on when to choose one over the other.
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** There are minor typographical errors throughout the paper (e.g., "lo-" instead of "local" in the introduction).
*   **Figure 1 is very difficult to interpret:** The diagram does not clearly show the connections between the original nodes and their "phantom" copies.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*   Novelty of extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with insightful convergence rates.
*   Empirical validation demonstrating improvements over U2-gossip.

**Against:**

*   Excessive detail in the background section.
*   Lack of clarity in the motivation behind the "phantom node" technique.
*   Limited comparison to broader baselines.
*   Insufficient experimental details.
*   Numerous minor inconsistencies and errors.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and theoretical soundness, the paper suffers from several shortcomings in presentation, methodology, and experimental evaluation. While the core idea is promising, the lack of clarity, insufficient experimental detail, and minor inconsistencies detract from the overall quality. The paper feels incomplete and, while correct, doesn't present its ideas in a particularly accessible or convincing manner.  I believe the paper, in its current form, falls short of the standards expected for NIPS acceptance. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above is required.

**Recommendation:** **Reject** (with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revision).
