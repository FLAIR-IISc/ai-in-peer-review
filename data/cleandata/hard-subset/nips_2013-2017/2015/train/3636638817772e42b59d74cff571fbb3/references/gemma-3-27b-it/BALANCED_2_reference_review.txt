This paper proposes randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are important in various machine learning tasks, and existing gossip algorithms struggle to compute them efficiently. The authors address this limitation by introducing algorithms that combine data propagation and averaging steps, analyzing their convergence rates, and validating them through experiments on real datasets.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of distributed U-statistic computation is genuinely important and not well addressed by existing gossip protocols. The introduction clearly articulates the challenges and positions the paper’s contribution. The related work section provides a good overview of the gossip algorithm landscape, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches for U-statistics and appropriately citing relevant works like [19, 5, 17]. The definitions and notations are comprehensive and clearly presented, aiding readability. The connection to phantom nodes for analysis is a clever idea and a key technical contribution.  The experimental validation using AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation on real data demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms and provides a meaningful comparison with [17].

A major strength of the paper is the rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proofs, while likely complex, are claimed and appear sound (though verifying all details is beyond the scope of this review).  The convergence rates (O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous) are reasonably good, and the data-dependent and network-dependent terms provide valuable insights into the algorithm’s performance.  The comparison to [17] and the demonstration of improvements in convergence, memory usage, and communication costs are compelling.  The inclusion of both synchronous and asynchronous variants broadens the applicability of the work. The thorough experimentation, including different network topologies and datasets, strengthens the empirical validation. The analysis distinguishing performance based on network spectral gap is particularly insightful.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the phantom node trick is clever, the explanation of *why* it works could be more intuitive. It feels a bit hand-wavy currently, and a more detailed explanation of the correspondence between the phantom network dynamics and the original algorithm’s behavior would be beneficial. The notation, while comprehensive, can be dense and overwhelming, particularly in Section 4. The equations could benefit from further simplification and explanation. The discussion of the asynchronous algorithm feels less detailed than the synchronous version. While Theorem 2 is stated, the reasoning behind the slightly slower convergence rate (compared to the synchronous case) isn’t fully articulated. The experimental section, while strong, could be enhanced by including comparisons to more baseline distributed estimation methods beyond just U2-gossip.  For instance, a simple distributed average followed by a centralized U-statistic computation would establish a stronger baseline. Furthermore, the discussion of the results could be expanded to include a more detailed analysis of the impact of network topology on performance. Finally, while the authors mention extensions to multi-observation nodes, this is merely stated rather than explored. Providing even a brief sketch of the complexity or potential benefits would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in distributed computation.
*   The proposed algorithms are novel and provide significant improvements over existing approaches.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the algorithms’ behavior.
*   The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the algorithms.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented (despite some density in notation).

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The explanation of the phantom node trick could be more intuitive.
*   The notation is dense and potentially overwhelming for some readers.
*   The discussion of the asynchronous algorithm is less detailed than the synchronous version.
*   The experimental section could benefit from comparisons to more baseline methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation.  While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation are strong. I lean towards **acceptance**, but would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above to further improve the clarity and impact of their work. Specifically, expanding the explanation of the phantom node method and simplifying the notation would be particularly valuable.
