## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed setting using gossip algorithms. Existing gossip algorithms struggle with U-statistics due to their dependency on pairwise observations. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) and demonstrate convergence guarantees of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. A clever reformulation using “phantom nodes” is key to their analysis, and they show performance improvements over prior work (U2-gossip [17]) through both theoretical bounds and empirical evaluation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Distributed estimation of U-statistics is a crucial problem with applications in various domains like machine learning, data mining, and sensor networks.  The limitation of standard gossip algorithms in handling these statistics is well-motivated.
* **Novel Approach:** The introduction of “phantom nodes” is a smart and insightful technique to reformulate the problem and allow for a convergence analysis leveraging existing tools from gossip algorithm theory. This is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide clear convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, with bounds that depend on both network topology (spectral gap) and data characteristics. This provides valuable insights into the algorithm’s performance.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real-world datasets convincingly demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over U2-gossip, validating the theoretical findings.  The exploration of different network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz) is particularly commendable.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithms are clearly presented, and the explanations are understandable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Phantom Node Explanation:** While the phantom node concept is clever, the initial explanation (and accompanying figure) could be more intuitive. A step-by-step example illustrating how the phantom nodes map to the actual computation might improve understanding.
* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t/t) convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm, while an improvement over naive approaches, is somewhat less appealing than the O(1/t) rate of the synchronous version.  The practical trade-offs between the two algorithms could be discussed in more detail. Specifically, under what network conditions does the added complexity of synchronization outweigh the faster convergence?
* **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to U2-gossip is strong, it would be beneficial to include comparisons against other relevant distributed estimation methods, even if they aren't directly applicable to U-statistics. This could better contextualize the performance gains of GoSta.
* **Scalability Discussion:**  The paper focuses on demonstrating correctness and comparing to one other method. A deeper discussion of the scalability of GoSta (both in terms of communication and memory) to very large networks (millions of nodes) would be valuable. While the authors note lower memory costs than U2-gossip, a more quantitative analysis would be helpful.




**Originality:**

The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a difficult problem. The use of phantom nodes and the subsequent analysis are original contributions. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous efforts, particularly U2-gossip.  The extension to asynchronous settings further adds to the originality.

**Significance:**

The work is significant because it opens up new possibilities for distributed computation of important statistical quantities. The theoretical results and empirical validations suggest that GoSta is a practical and efficient solution for many real-world applications.  The insights into the interplay between network topology and convergence rate are valuable for designing and deploying distributed estimation systems.  

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors are careful to address both the strengths and limitations of their work. The overall quality is high.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Solves an important and challenging problem in distributed computation.
* Presents a novel and insightful solution based on phantom nodes.
* Provides strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.
* Clearly written and well-organized.
* Offers practical improvements over existing methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The phantom node explanation could be improved for clarity.
* The asynchronous convergence rate is slightly less attractive than the synchronous rate.
* The comparison to baselines could be expanded.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation and presents a well-reasoned and technically sound solution. The strengths of the paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The work is likely to be influential and stimulate further research in this area.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**
