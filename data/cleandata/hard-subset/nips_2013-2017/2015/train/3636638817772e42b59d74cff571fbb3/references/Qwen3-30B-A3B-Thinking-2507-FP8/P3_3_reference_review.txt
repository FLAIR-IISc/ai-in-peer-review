

# Peer Review: "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses an important gap in distributed computing: the efficient estimation of U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations) in decentralized networks. The authors propose "GoSta" algorithms—both synchronous and asynchronous—that extend gossip protocols to compute these statistics, with convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. The key insight is to combine data propagation (via auxiliary observations) with local averaging, using a novel "phantom node" construction for theoretical analysis.

## Strengths

**Novel Problem and Approach:** The paper identifies a significant limitation of existing gossip algorithms (which handle separable functions but not U-statistics) and proposes a clean solution. The phantom node technique for analysis is particularly elegant—it transforms the coupled data propagation and averaging processes into a tractable linear system. This is a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed algorithms beyond simple averages.

**Strong Theoretical Results:** The convergence analysis is rigorous, with rates that depend meaningfully on both data properties (through the norm terms) and network structure (via the spectral gap). The comparison with U2-gossip [17] is insightful: the authors show GoSta's O(1/t) rate is superior to U2-gossip's O(1/t) but with significantly lower memory and communication costs. The asynchronous version's O(log t/t) rate is also well-justified.

**Practical Validation:** The experiments on real-world datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) convincingly demonstrate GoSta's superiority over U2-gossip across multiple network topologies (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz). The results align with the theoretical predictions—performance gaps widen as network size increases, and the variance of estimates is lower due to the averaging step. The comparison between synchronous and asynchronous variants in Figure 3b is particularly valuable for practitioners.

**Clear Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a clean distinction between the data propagation and averaging steps, which is crucial for understanding how U-statistics can be distributedly computed. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and implementable.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

**Motivation for Phantom Nodes:** While the phantom node construction is mathematically sound, its necessity isn't sufficiently motivated for readers focused on applications. The paper could better explain why this approach is preferable to alternative analyses (e.g., directly tracking the joint evolution of Z_k and Y_k). This could be addressed with a brief discussion in the main text.

**Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper emphasizes lower communication and memory costs but doesn't quantify the per-iteration computational complexity. For high-dimensional data (d large), how does the cost of H(X_i, X_j) scale? This is critical for real-world deployment.

**Network Topology Limitations:** While the experiments cover three network types, they don't explore highly sparse or adversarial topologies where convergence might be slower. The spectral gap analysis assumes well-connected networks, but the paper doesn't discuss how GoSta behaves on poorly connected graphs (e.g., with small spectral gaps).

**Comparison with Baselines:** The comparison is limited to U2-gossip. Including more recent gossip variants (e.g., those from [6, 13] for geographic networks) or even simple centralized baselines would strengthen the empirical claims.

**Practical Implementation Details:** The asynchronous algorithm requires each node to know its degree and total edges. While reasonable for many networks, this assumption may not hold in all settings (e.g., dynamic networks). The paper could briefly discuss how to handle such cases.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to distributed learning. It successfully bridges a gap in gossip algorithms by addressing U-statistic estimation—a problem with wide applications in machine learning (AUC, clustering quality, etc.). The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the algorithms are well-designed, and the experiments convincingly validate the claims.

The paper advances the state of the art by providing faster convergence rates with lower resource requirements than existing methods. The phantom node technique is a clever analytical tool that could inspire similar approaches for other distributed estimation problems. While the experimental section could be expanded, the core contribution is significant and well-supported.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem in distributed learning. It meets all NeurIPS criteria: high quality, clear presentation, genuine originality, and significant impact for distributed machine learning practitioners.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of distributed algorithms, with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over prior work. The minor weaknesses noted above are not fundamental to the contribution and could be addressed in the final version. This work would be valuable to the NeurIPS community, particularly for those working on decentralized learning and large-scale optimization.

**Pro:** Solves a meaningful problem with novel theoretical insights (phantom nodes), strong convergence guarantees, and practical validation across multiple datasets/networks.  
**Con:** Limited discussion of computational complexity for high-dimensional data and sparse networks; could include more baselines.
