Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network. Each node holds one observation Xi and maintains (i) an auxiliary observation Yk that performs a random walk via swaps along randomly selected edges and (ii) a local running estimate Zk. The synchronous algorithm updates all Zk each iteration using a running average of H(Xk, Yk), and then averages Z and swaps Y along one randomly sampled edge. The asynchronous algorithm updates only the two endpoints of the sampled edge and uses a local counter mk with importance weighting (1/(pk mk)) to maintain an unbiased running average despite irregular activations, where pk=2dk/|E| is the activation probability of node k.

Theoretical results show that E[Zk(t)] converges to the desired U-statistic Ûn(H) for all nodes, with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O((log t)/t) (asynchronous). The bounds separate data-dependent terms (measuring the dispersion of H across pairs) and network-dependent terms via the spectral gap of a weighted averaging matrix Wα(G). The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple averaging and data propagation, leading to a block upper-triangular transition matrix and enabling explicit bounds.

The work is positioned against classical gossip for separable functions (e.g., distributed averaging) and the prior decentralized U-statistic method U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens], which uses two independent random walks of auxiliary observations without averaging. The authors prove a refined bound for U2-gossip showing a √n penalty and argue GoSta’s averaging dominates in practice. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter on real datasets over grid, Watts–Strogatz, and complete graphs corroborate faster convergence and lower across-node variance for GoSta.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant gap: decentralized computation of pairwise (non-separable) statistics, with applications such as AUC and clustering scatter.
- Conceptually simple algorithms with low per-iteration communication (one swap and one averaging along a single edge).
- Clear separation of data and network effects in the convergence bounds; dependence on spectral gap is aligned with consensus literature.
- Asynchronous variant that compensates for uneven activations via local counters, removing the need for a global clock.
- Novel “phantom nodes” reformulation that makes the analysis modular and transparent.
- Empirical results support theoretical claims across different network topologies.

Weaknesses
- Convergence is shown in expectation only; no high-probability or mean-square error bounds, which limits robustness guarantees.
- The asynchronous bound is loose (O((log t)/t) with an unspecified c′(G)) and lacks the informative data-dependent terms present in the synchronous analysis.
- Implementation assumptions: both algorithms sample edges uniformly at random and require nodes to know |E| (to compute pk in the async case). These assumptions may be nontrivial in decentralized settings and deserve discussion or mitigation strategies.
- Minor inconsistency in spectral-gap constants for complete graphs: with W2(G)=I−(1/|E|)L, 1−λ2(2)=β2/|E|=2/(n−1), whereas the paper states 1/n in the experiments section.
- Proofs and several algorithmic details are deferred to the supplementary material; the main text could better summarize key steps and constants (especially for the async analysis).
- Scope limited to degree-2 U-statistics; extensions to higher-order U-statistics are not addressed.

Quality
- Technical approach is sound for the synchronous case; the block-matrix analysis and use of spectral properties are standard and appropriately applied. The asynchronous correction via mk (with increments 1/pk so that pk mk equals the number of activations) is reasonable, but the argument relies on expectation and the independence structure; more detailed discussion of potential bias/variance and coupling with averaging would strengthen the result.
- The comparison bound for U2-gossip is useful and clarifies where the √n factor arises.
- Experimental evaluation is consistent with the theory and uses meaningful tasks, but the scale is modest and confidence intervals or concentration analyses are absent.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-structured, with helpful algorithm listings. The phantom network construction and block matrices are explained sufficiently for the main idea.
- Notational density is high; a brief recap of spectral quantities and their values for standard graphs would help (and would likely have prevented the noted constant mismatch).
- Assumptions (connected, non-bipartite graph; H symmetric with H(X,X)=0; uniform edge sampling; knowledge of |E|) should be consolidated early and discussed in the context of practical implementations.

Originality
- The problem formulation (decentralized U-statistics) is not new per se, but the proposed combination of single-walk data propagation with explicit averaging, and the phantom-nodes analytical device, constitute a novel approach compared to U2-gossip.
- Theoretical rates with data- and network-dependent terms for this setting appear new and useful.

Significance
- Estimating U-statistics is a core primitive for distributed ML tasks (AUC, rank correlation, clustering metrics, nonparametric tests). Providing a general, simple, and analyzable method is impactful.
- The dependence on spectral gap provides actionable insight for network design or choice of overlays.
- Without concentration results, practitioners may be cautious in unreliable networks; nonetheless, the contributions are likely to be used and extended.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear algorithmic contribution addressing an important gap.
- Theoretical guarantees with interpretable constants; improved rates over prior work in this setting.
- Practical asynchronous variant and empirical validation on real data and multiple topologies.
- Analysis technique (phantom nodes) may be of independent interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Only expectation convergence; lack of high-probability/variance bounds.
- Asynchronous bound is relatively weak and underspecified.
- Practical assumptions (uniform edge sampling, knowledge of |E|) not fully justified or addressed.
- Minor inconsistency in spectral-gap constants suggests care is needed in the presentation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide mean-square and/or high-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration) and quantify variance across nodes.
- Clarify and correct spectral-gap constants for standard graphs; summarize key constants in the main text.
- Discuss implementation of uniform edge sampling in a decentralized manner (e.g., edge-based Poisson clocks) and alternatives when |E| is unknown. Consider remedies for bipartite graphs (lazy updates/self-loops).
- Include communication and memory cost per iteration and per ε-accuracy complexity comparisons.
- Explore robustness to message delays/loss and heterogeneity in activation probabilities.
- Outline extensions to higher-order U-statistics.

Overall, this is a solid and useful contribution to decentralized learning and gossip algorithms, with room for strengthening theoretical guarantees and clarifying practical assumptions. The paper fits NeurIPS scope.
