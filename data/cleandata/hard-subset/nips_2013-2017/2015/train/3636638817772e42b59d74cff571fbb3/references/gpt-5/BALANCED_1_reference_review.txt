Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles decentralized computation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network, where each node holds one observation. The authors propose two gossip-style algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node maintains a single “auxiliary” observation that performs a random walk via edge swaps and a local estimate subject to pairwise averaging when an edge is activated. The key insight is to decompose the U-statistic as an average of node-wise means hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi, Xj), and to combine (i) data propagation to let nodes estimate hi, and (ii) consensus-style averaging to aggregate these into Ûn(H).

The analysis introduces a “phantom nodes” construction: an augmented graph with n copies of the original graph that track propagation of the values H(Xk, ·), and a coupling between the original and phantom nodes to model both propagation and averaging. Using this, the authors show convergence in expectation to the U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O((log t)/t) (async). The rates depend on graph connectivity via the spectral gap of the Laplacian and on data dispersion terms. Compared to the prior U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, IFAC’09], GoSta achieves faster rates in practice, requires only one auxiliary observation per node, and reduces communication (one edge per round vs two).

The paper builds on a large literature on randomized gossip for averaging [e.g., Boyd et al. 2006; Dimakis et al. 2010], geographic/accelerated gossip, and applies to ML-relevant U-statistics (AUC, within-cluster scatter). The work appears novel in addressing U-statistics with gossip via averaging plus token propagation and in the “phantom network” analysis.

Strengths
- Problem importance: U-statistics cover key ML metrics (AUC, rank correlation, clustering scatter) and are not separable, making standard gossip inapplicable. Bridging this gap is valuable.
- Algorithmic simplicity and practicality: Each node stores one auxiliary token and an estimate; communication per activation is modest (swap one token and average estimates).
- Theoretical guarantees: Convergence in expectation with explicit rates; dependence on spectral gap aligns with established consensus theory; asynchronous variant with rate O((log t)/t).
- Analysis technique: The phantom graph reformulation is elegant and may be reusable for other non-separable distributed objectives.
- Empirical validation: Real datasets, multiple graph topologies, consistent gains over U2-gossip, and a reasonable scaling study.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Expectation-only guarantees: The paper lacks variance or high-probability/concentration bounds. Practitioners need node-wise accuracy guarantees. Suggest adding martingale concentration or mixing-time-based variance bounds, or at least empirical variance scaling with t.
- Asynchronous assumptions: Algorithm 2 requires each node to know pk = 2dk/|E| to maintain unbiasedness. Knowing |E| globally is often unrealistic. Please discuss: (i) how to estimate |E| (e.g., via a preliminary gossip to compute sum of degrees), (ii) robustness if pk is misspecified, or (iii) alternatives such as push-sum or Metropolis weights that avoid global knowledge.
- Non-bipartite requirement: The analysis assumes connected, non-bipartite graphs. In practice one can remove periodicity with laziness or self-loops. Please clarify whether adding a self-loop (lazy edge activation) suffices and how it affects constants.
- Norm ambiguity: The bounds use ‖H − h1>‖ and ‖h − Û1‖ but the paper defines ‖·‖ as the Euclidean norm; for matrices, clarify whether this is Frobenius norm (via vectorization). Make the choice explicit and justify dependence on data scale.
- Communication/compute complexity to accuracy: While per-iteration costs are clear, there is no analytic communication complexity to reach ε-accuracy. Providing a bound of the form messages = Õ((gap)−1 · data-term · 1/ε) would strengthen significance and enable comparison to U2-gossip beyond rates.
- Baselines and scope: Consider comparing against subsampling-based U-statistic estimators adapted to gossip (e.g., randomly sample pairs via token meeting times) or sketching-based approximations, to contextualize accuracy vs communication trade-offs.
- Generality: The work focuses on degree-2 U-statistics. A discussion or preliminary extension to order-m U-statistics (multiple tokens, trade-offs) would broaden impact.
- Edge activation model: The async model abstracts to uniform random edge selection. With independent node Poisson clocks selecting random neighbors, edge selection typically is degree-biased. Please clarify modeling assumptions and how Algorithm 2 adapts to more realistic activation (or reference standard equivalence results precisely).

Quality
Technically sound overall. The phantom network derivation is convincing, and the rates and spectral gap dependence are plausible. However, proofs are said to be in the supplement; the main text would benefit from a clearer sketch, especially for the asynchronous log t/t rate and the role of pk. The comparison bound for U2-gossip is useful.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The motivation and algorithms are clear. Notation needs tightening around norms and the matrix H. Minor typos (line breaks in “lo-cal”). Clarify assumptions (knowledge of |E|; non-bipartite graphs) and the computation models for synchronous vs asynchronous settings.

Originality
Good. Extends gossip beyond separable sums to pairwise U-statistics with a neat analysis tool. The combination of averaging with token propagation and the asynchronous correction is, to my knowledge, novel in this context.

Significance
Moderate to high. U-statistics are central in ML evaluation and some learning objectives; enabling decentralized computation with provable rates is valuable. The practical reduction in communication/memory vs U2-gossip and the empirical gains support impact. Stronger complexity-to-accuracy analysis and broader baselines would further elevate significance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and previously underexplored decentralized computation problem.
- Simple algorithms with provable rates; asynchronous version included.
- Clear empirical improvements over the only prior method (U2-gossip).
- Analysis technique (phantom nodes) is potentially reusable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Only expectation guarantees; lack of concentration/finite-sample error bounds.
- Async algorithm relies on global |E|; modeling assumptions about edge activations need clarification.
- Communication-to-accuracy complexity not quantified; limited baselines.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The paper offers a clear, novel, and practically relevant contribution with solid theory and experiments. Addressing the noted clarity and robustness points would strengthen it further.
