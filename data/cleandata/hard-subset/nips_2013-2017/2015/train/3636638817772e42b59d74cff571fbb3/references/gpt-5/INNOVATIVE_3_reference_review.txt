Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over networks, a problem not handled efficiently by standard gossip. The authors propose two simple randomized gossip algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node maintains a local estimate and a single auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via edge swaps; in parallel, nodes average their estimates when an edge is activated. A novel “phantom nodes” construction decouples averaging from data propagation for analysis. The authors prove convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async), with constants that depend on both the data (dispersion terms) and network connectivity (spectral gap). They also give a refined comparison to the prior U2-gossip algorithm, arguing lower per-iteration memory/communication and better rates/constants. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over three graph families support the advantages of GoSta.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: Extends gossip beyond separable statistics to pairwise U-statistics with a clean, implementable protocol; includes an asynchronous variant. This opens a new direction for decentralized estimation of important ML quantities (AUC, Kendall tau, scatter).
- Conceptual clarity: The idea of mixing random-walk-based data propagation with consensus averaging is elegant and broadly applicable.
- Analysis: The phantom graph technique is an original and compelling way to analyze the coupled process; rates expose both data- and graph-dependent terms. Comparisons with U2-gossip are theoretically grounded.
- Practicality: The algorithms use a single auxiliary observation per node and one edge activation per step, lowering memory/communication versus U2-gossip. The async design aligns better with realistic settings without global clocks.
- Empirical support: Across datasets and topologies, GoSta outperforms U2-gossip in error and variance, with widening gains as n grows, qualitatively matching theory.

Weaknesses and questions
- Guarantees in expectation only: No high-probability or almost-sure convergence, no finite-time variance/MSE bounds. Given the stochastic nature and pairwise dependencies, concentration would be valuable for practice.
- Edge activation model: The analysis relies on uniform random edge sampling. In decentralized implementations with independent Poisson clocks and neighbor selection, the induced edge distribution is typically not uniform. Please clarify the exact protocol that achieves uniform edge activation without central coordination or discuss robustness of the analysis to non-uniform activations. If non-uniform, can push-sum-style corrections be used?
- Knowledge of degrees and |E|: GoSta-async requires pk = 2dk/|E|. In large dynamic networks, |E| is not always known; discuss how pk could be estimated online and the impact on guarantees.
- Graph assumptions: Results assume undirected, connected, non-bipartite graphs. How do the methods extend to directed, time-varying, or intermittently connected networks? Can push-sum or weighted variants be integrated?
- Communication cost: While lower than U2-gossip, each step still swaps a full d-dimensional vector. Quantization/compression or sparsification could be important in high dimensions; no analysis of communication vs error is given.
- Experiments: Datasets and graphs are modest in size; runtime/message complexity and robustness to delays/packet loss are not reported. Comparisons are only to U2-gossip; consider baselines such as (i) random-walk Monte Carlo estimators of U-statistics, (ii) decentralized SGD on pairwise losses with negative sampling, (iii) centralized benchmarks for reference.
- Generality of H: Assumes symmetric H with H(X,X)=0; many U-statistics fit this, but clarify how to handle cases where H(X,X) ≠ 0 or asymmetric kernels (via symmetrization?). Also, AUC requires normalization by counts of positives/negatives; detail how those counts are computed decentrally (e.g., separate gossip of counts).
- Constants and tightness: The async O(log t/t) bound lacks explicit constants; could you provide them or at least clarify dependence on the spectral gap and degree distribution?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide high-probability (or MSE) convergence bounds and/or concentration inequalities under mild moment conditions; even loose bounds would strengthen the claims.
- Discuss implementation details for achieving uniform edge sampling, or extend the theory to the standard pairwise-averaging activation model with non-uniform edges.
- Add experiments with larger n, report wall-clock time and total messages/words communicated, and include baselines beyond U2-gossip.
- Explore quantized/compressed variants and their effect on convergence.
- Outline extensions to directed/time-varying graphs (e.g., push-sum) and to k>2 U-statistics.
- Minor: clean up reference formatting and ensure all claims relegated to the supplement (proofs, constants) are traceable.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a new, broadly useful capability for gossip: computing pairwise U-statistics.
- Simple algorithms with solid, insightful analysis and a genuinely novel proof device.
- Asynchronous protocol increases practical relevance.
- Clear empirical gains over prior art.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees only in expectation; limited experimental scope; reliance on a potentially idealized activation model.
- Restricted to undirected non-bipartite graphs and assumes knowledge of |E|.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a fresh and useful direction for decentralized statistical computation with a neat algorithmic idea and nontrivial analysis. Despite some gaps (concentration, activation model realism), the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to spur follow-up work.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
