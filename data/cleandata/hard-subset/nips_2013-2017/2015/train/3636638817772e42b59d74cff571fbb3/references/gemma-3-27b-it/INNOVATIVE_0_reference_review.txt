This paper tackles the challenging problem of extending gossip algorithms to compute U-statistics, a class of functions frequently used in machine learning but not amenable to straightforward decentralized computation via standard gossip protocols. The core idea – combining data propagation with local estimate averaging, and cleverly reformulating the problem with “phantom nodes” – is genuinely novel and represents a significant step forward. While the presentation could be tightened, and the experimental validation, while present, isn’t exhaustive, the boldness and potential impact of the approach strongly outweigh these minor shortcomings.

**Summary:** The paper introduces GoSta, a novel gossip algorithm for distributed U-statistic estimation. It presents both synchronous and asynchronous variants, proving convergence rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. The key technical contribution is the phantom node reformulation, which enables a clean analysis and a practical algorithm. Empirical evaluation on AUC and within-cluster scatter demonstrates the algorithm’s effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the primary strength. Efficiently computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner is a non-trivial problem, and the proposed approach is a significant departure from existing gossip algorithms. The phantom node trick is particularly clever and elegant.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence analysis, although complex, provides concrete bounds on the algorithm’s performance. The inclusion of data-dependent and network-dependent terms is a plus, demonstrating a nuanced understanding of the problem.
*   **Practical Relevance:** U-statistics appear in many machine learning applications (as highlighted in the introduction), making this work potentially impactful.
*   **Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper explicitly acknowledges and improves upon existing approaches, specifically [17], in terms of convergence, memory usage, and communication cost.
*   **Asynchronous Version:** Offering both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms broadens the applicability of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity. The notation is heavy, and the connection between the phantom node formulation and the actual algorithm isn't immediately obvious. A more intuitive explanation would be helpful. The transition matrix derivation, while technically sound, feels unnecessarily complicated.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is somewhat limited. While the results demonstrate the algorithm works, more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and network topologies would strengthen the claims. A more thorough comparison to relevant baselines beyond [17] would also be beneficial. The discussion of the experimental results is relatively brief.
*   **Scalability Discussion:** While the authors point out reduced memory and communication costs compared to [17], a more detailed discussion of the algorithm’s scalability with network size and data dimensionality would be valuable.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of symmetric H(X,X) = 0, while common in U-statistic definitions, is not explicitly justified or discussed regarding its impact on broader applications.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides meaningful convergence guarantees.
*   The experimental results, though limited, support the claims of the paper.
*   The potential impact on decentralized machine learning is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and could be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   The experimental validation is not exhaustive.
*   The discussion of scalability is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a strong contribution. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the theoretical guarantees and promising experimental results, makes it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. While the presentation and experimental validation could be improved, these are minor issues that do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I lean towards acceptance, prioritizing the innovative nature of the solution over methodological perfection. The introduction of the phantom node technique is particularly impressive and may inspire further research in this area.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I encourage the authors to address the clarity issues and consider expanding the experimental evaluation in a future version of the paper.
