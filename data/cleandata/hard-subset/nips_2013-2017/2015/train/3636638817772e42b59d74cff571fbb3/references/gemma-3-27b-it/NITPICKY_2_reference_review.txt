The paper proposes randomized gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a problem not efficiently solvable by standard gossip algorithms. The core idea of propagating local observations and averaging local estimates, coupled with a “phantom node” reformulation for analysis, is conceptually sound. The authors present convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, and demonstrate empirical performance on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

However, despite the novelty and potential significance of the problem tackled, and the relatively clear presentation, I find numerous shortcomings that, cumulatively, lead me to recommend rejection. My assessment is highly critical, focusing on a perceived lack of rigor in the analysis, superficial experimental validation, and a frustrating level of detail-oriented issues that betray a lack of polish.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics is a genuinely novel contribution. Existing gossip algorithms are not directly applicable, and addressing this limitation is valuable.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The introduction clearly motivates the problem and its relevance to various applications.
*   **Conceptual Approach:** The combination of data propagation and averaging is a reasonable strategy.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Analytical Rigor:** The “phantom node” reformulation, while intuitively appealing, feels like a mathematical sleight of hand. The justification for its validity is not sufficiently detailed. While the authors claim a formal proof in the supplementary material, the presented theorems rely on assumptions about network connectivity and stochastic matrix properties that aren’t sufficiently discussed in the main text, particularly concerning the stability of the iterative process. The derivation of the convergence rates, particularly the data-dependent terms, feels hand-wavy and lacks sufficient justification.
*   **Network Model Assumptions:** The analysis heavily relies on properties of the graph Laplacian and spectral gap. The discussion of these properties feels cursory. Are the results sensitive to the choice of graph? What happens when the graph is not strongly connected or is bipartite (despite the claim about bipartite graphs)? These questions are not addressed.
*   **Asynchronous Setting:** The analysis of the asynchronous setting feels significantly less developed than the synchronous one. The convergence rate (O(log t/t)) is weaker, but the justification for this rate is even less clear. The reliance on Poisson process modeling and the resulting complexities are not adequately explained. The claim of unbiased estimates seems dubious without deeper analysis of the mk(t) dynamics.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are rather limited. Only two applications (AUC and within-cluster scatter) are considered, and only on a single dataset for each. The comparison with U2-gossip is a good start, but the choice of network topologies is not well-motivated. Furthermore, the plots presented are of relatively low resolution and lack clear error bars (standard deviations are mentioned but not always visible). The comparison to other baselines is missing, hindering the ability to assess the true performance gain. The claim of "superiority" feels premature based on this evidence.
*   **Formatting and Notation:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting inconsistencies. For example, inconsistent use of subscripts and superscripts, unclear notation (e.g., the meaning of ‘t’ in the convergence rates), and a generally cluttered presentation. The definition of `[p]` as {1, . . . , p} is unnecessary and distracting.
*   **Redundancy & Verbosity:** The text contains several redundant statements and phrases. For instance, the repeated emphasis on the memory and communication costs of U2-gossip feels excessive. The introduction is overly lengthy and could be more concise.
*   **Minor Errors:** Several minor errors and typos throughout the manuscript detract from its credibility. For example, the reference to "[17]" regarding convergence rate is confusing, as that paper is claimed to be surpassed.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The problem is novel and important.
*   The proposed algorithms offer a potential solution to a challenging problem in distributed estimation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The analytical rigor is insufficient, and the convergence analysis lacks sufficient detail and justification.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and does not provide strong evidence of the algorithm’s superiority.
*   The paper suffers from numerous formatting inconsistencies, minor errors, and redundancy.
*   The “phantom node” reformulation lacks a thorough justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an important and novel problem, the numerous shortcomings in its analysis, experimental evaluation, and presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper feels incomplete and rushed. Significant revisions, including a more rigorous analysis, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and careful attention to detail, are needed before this work would be suitable for NIPS. I recommend rejection.
