Summary
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. The authors propose two randomized algorithms: a synchronous variant (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous variant (GoSta-async). Each node maintains a local estimate and one auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via edge swaps. The update combines averaging of local estimates with incremental incorporation of pairwise evaluations H(Xi, Yk). The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from averaging, yielding convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous). Bounds feature data-dependent norms and dependence on the spectral gap via 1 − λ2(Wα(G)). The paper contrasts GoSta with U2-gossip [17], arguing improved rates and lower memory/communication. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over three graph families suggest empirical advantages for GoSta.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant gap: standard gossip handles separable sums, not pairwise U-statistics. The formulation and “phantom network” analysis are conceptually clear and could be reused elsewhere.
- Algorithms are simple, with lower per-node memory/communication than U2-gossip (one auxiliary sample vs two, one edge activation per step vs two).
- Provides both synchronous and asynchronous variants, with explicit dependence on graph spectral properties.
- Theoretical results are stated with some data-dependent terms, not just worst-case constants.
- Empirical results on two tasks support the claimed speedups and reduced variance across nodes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Convergence guarantees are only in expectation. No high-probability or almost-sure results, and no bounds on the variance across nodes or MSE. For distributed estimation, concentration and robustness to randomness are important.
- Asynchronous algorithm requires each node to know pk = 2 dk / |E| and to maintain mk with increments 1/pk. This presumes global knowledge of |E|, which is not standard in decentralized settings; the paper should either remove this requirement or show a distributed way to estimate |E| and analyze the induced bias/variance.
- The spectral gap discussion appears to contain an error: for W2(G) = I − (1/|E|)L(G), on the complete graph Kn (|E| = n(n−1)/2, Laplacian eigenvalues 0, n), one has 1 − λ2(W2) = β2/|E| = n / (n(n−1)/2) = 2/(n−1), not 1/n as stated. Also, the text uses βn−1 to denote the second smallest Laplacian eigenvalue, which is unconventional and confusing (β2 is standard).
- Experimental evaluation is limited:
  - Only two moderate-size datasets (n up to 1599, small d), despite claims that memory/communication benefits grow with d. No scalability study in high dimensions or larger n.
  - Comparison is only to U2-gossip. Missing baselines include: tree-based aggregation (e.g., spanning tree reduction of pairwise contributions), push-sum variants, randomized pair sampling approximations, or simple multi-token random-walk estimators. No comparison on communication per unit accuracy (messages and bytes), which matters when d is large.
  - The asynchronous algorithm is evaluated only superficially; no systematic study across graph classes, no stress tests with heterogeneous degrees or delays, and no assessment under practical activation models (non-uniform edge activations, clock drift).
  - Claims about dependence on spectral gap are not empirically validated beyond three graph types; a controlled study varying the gap (e.g., on expanders vs grids vs paths) would strengthen the story.
- The analysis places most proofs in the supplement. The main text lacks intuition for the O(log t / t) rate in the asynchronous case and does not give explicit constants c′(G), making it hard to assess practical implications.
- Model assumptions are idealized: uniform random edge selection in synchronous setting, exact edge-swaps without contention, and no message loss or delays. It is not clear how robust the algorithms are to realistic asynchronous communication patterns or to directed/weighted graphs.
- The H(X, X) = 0 assumption is restrictive for some U-statistics; although easy to adjust, the paper should be clearer about the generality (e.g., unbiasedness with n/(n−1) correction).

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but several gaps reduce confidence: expectation-only guarantees, reliance on global graph knowledge in the asynchronous variant, and an apparent mistake in the spectral gap example. The comparison to prior art [17] is thoughtfully discussed, but empirical validation is narrow.

Clarity
Generally readable and organized, but notation issues (βn−1 vs β2) and the incorrect complete-graph constant are concerning. Many key derivations are deferred; adding more intuition and correcting the spectral facts would help. Please define all matrices and eigenvalue orderings consistently.

Originality
Extends gossip to a non-separable class (U-statistics) with a neat analytical device and a simpler per-node state than [17]. The contribution is incremental but nontrivial relative to U2-gossip.

Significance
Potentially useful for decentralized evaluation of common metrics (AUC, clustering scatter), but current scope and assumptions limit immediate impact. Stronger probabilistic guarantees and realistic system evaluations would increase significance.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide high-probability (or almost sure) convergence and variance bounds for both algorithms?
2. How to remove the requirement that nodes know |E| in the asynchronous variant? Would using local counters mk ← mk + 1 and stepsizes 1/mk suffice without bias?
3. Please correct and clarify the spectral gap relationships and eigenvalue indexing. For Kn, what is 1 − λ2(W2) in your definition?
4. How do your methods compare in communication cost (messages and bytes) to simple tree-based reductions for U-statistics?
5. Can you evaluate on higher-dimensional data and larger n, and report time/communication to reach a target accuracy?
6. How sensitive are the algorithms to non-uniform edge activation probabilities, message loss, and delays?

Minor comments
- Be explicit about whether H must be bounded; your asynchronous bound depends on ‖H‖.
- Clarify whether the algorithms extend to directed/weighted graphs and to non-symmetric H.
- Some typos/formatting: misplaced footnotes, inconsistent equation numbering.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation; simple algorithms.
- Theoretical convergence with graph-dependent rates.
- Lower memory/communication per node than [17].
- Both synchronous and asynchronous variants.

Cons
- Expectation-only guarantees; no concentration/MSE analysis.
- Requires global |E| in async; idealized activation assumptions.
- Apparent error in spectral gap example; notation inconsistencies.
- Limited empirical scope; missing strong baselines and comm-cost analysis.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (weak reject). While the idea is interesting and the algorithms are simple, the paper lacks rigorous probabilistic guarantees, relies on unrealistic global knowledge in the asynchronous setting, and presents limited empirical evidence. With corrected spectral analysis, stronger guarantees, and broader, communication-aware experiments, this could be a solid contribution.

Confidence: 4/5.
