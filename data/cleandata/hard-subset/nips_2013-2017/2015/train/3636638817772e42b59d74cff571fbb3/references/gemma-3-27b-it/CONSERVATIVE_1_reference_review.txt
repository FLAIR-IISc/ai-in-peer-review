This paper tackles the problem of extending gossip algorithms to compute U-statistics, a challenging task given the inherent limitations of gossip-based approaches for non-separable functions. The motivation is sound, as U-statistics appear in numerous machine learning applications. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous algorithms (GoSta) and provide convergence analysis, claiming improvements over prior work [17].

The core idea of using “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem and facilitate analysis is clever, although not entirely novel in the context of distributed estimation. However, the presentation suffers from a lack of clarity and sufficient justification for several design choices. The connection between the phantom node formulation and the actual algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not immediately obvious, and requires careful parsing. While the mathematical analysis appears rigorous, the numerous definitions and notations introduced in Section 2, while standard, contribute to the paper’s density and hinder readability. The reliance on existing results regarding spectral graph theory is appropriate, but a more detailed explanation of how the spectral gap influences the convergence rate would be beneficial.

A key weakness lies in the empirical evaluation. While the experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter are a good starting point, they are not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over U2-gossip [17]. The experimental setup lacks detail – crucial parameters beyond network topology are not specified. The claim of superiority relies solely on these two datasets and network types. The comparison with U2-gossip is valuable, but a comparison to simpler, albeit less efficient, centralized or parameter server-based approaches would provide essential context. Furthermore, the runtime experiments (Figure 3) are somewhat underwhelming; the gains shown are not substantial, and the practical relevance is unclear without knowing the communication/computational costs. The authors state the algorithms can handle multiple observations per node, but this is not demonstrated in the experiments.

The asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) is presented with less detail than the synchronous version. The justification for the asynchronous update rule and the analysis supporting the logarithmic convergence rate are less convincing, relying heavily on references to prior work on asynchronous gossip algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in distributed estimation.
*   Novel use of “phantom nodes” for analysis.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrates improvements over a recent baseline [17] on two datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of clarity in presentation and justification of design choices.
*   Insufficiently detailed experimental evaluation.
*   Limited comparison to alternative approaches.
*   Asynchronous algorithm lacks the same level of detailed analysis and justification as the synchronous version.
*   Dependence on several definitions/notations which increase density.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem is relevant and challenging.
*   The theoretical results are solid and provide valuable insights.
*   The improvement over [17] is a positive contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of clarity and insufficient experimental validation raise concerns about the practical impact of the work.
*   The paper feels incomplete; the connection between theory and practice is not fully established.
*   The empirical evidence isn’t robust enough to warrant strong endorsement.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject**. While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach to a relevant problem, the lack of clarity, limited experimental validation, and insufficient comparison to alternative methods prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The paper requires substantial revision, particularly regarding the experimental setup and presentation of results, to convincingly demonstrate its significance and practical value. A stronger emphasis on clarifying the connections between the theory and the algorithm is also crucial. I find the theoretical contributions valuable, but are not sufficient for acceptance to NIPS without more substantial empirical support.
