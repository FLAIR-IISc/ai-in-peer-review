Summary and contributions
The paper addresses decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a connected network, where each node holds a single data point. The authors propose two gossip-style algorithms:
- GoSta-sync: a synchronous scheme where each node keeps an auxiliary observation that performs a random walk (via swaps along randomly chosen edges) and a local running average of H(Xk, Yk). At each iteration, all nodes update their local running average, and a single randomly chosen edge averages local estimates and swaps auxiliary observations.
- GoSta-async: a fully asynchronous variant where only the active pair updates; each node keeps a corrected local clock mk to form an unbiased running average based on the number of times it has been activated.

The analysis introduces a “phantom node” construction to decouple averaging and data propagation. The authors prove convergence in expectation to the true U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous). The rates depend on (i) data-dependent terms measuring dispersion of H and h, and (ii) graph-dependent terms via the spectral gap (1 − λ2) of a mixing matrix Wα(G), yielding faster convergence on well-connected graphs. They also analyze and refine the convergence of the prior U2-gossip algorithm [17], arguing that GoSta improves both rate and communication/memory costs. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several network topologies corroborate the theory.

Relation to prior work
This paper builds on randomized gossip for averaging [Boyd et al. 2006; Kempe et al. 2003], geographic gossip [Dimakis et al.], and distributed optimization [Nedic & Ozdaglar; Duchi et al.]. It tackles an understudied problem: estimating non-separable, pairwise quantities via gossip. The closest prior is U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens], which propagates two observations per node and lacks averaging. The presented approach is novel in combining observation random walks with consensus steps and in using the phantom graph to obtain explicit rates with spectral-gap dependence.

Quality and technical correctness
- The modeling and analysis are sound. The block upper-triangular transition matrix separating (i) estimate averaging (M1 = W2(G) with time-varying weights) and (ii) data propagation (M3 = W1(G) ⊗ In) is elegant and leads to clean bounds.
- The rate O(1/t) in expectation for the synchronous case is plausible, with an additional exponential term that decays with the graph spectral gap, and the constants correctly reflect both data and graph properties.
- The asynchronous correction mk is handled carefully. The update uses 1/(pik mk), which simplifies to 1/Nk(t) where Nk is the activation count; this yields an unbiased running average conditional on activations.
- The improvement over U2-gossip is well argued: although U2 propagates two observations (helpful for mixing), the absence of averaging yields an unnecessary √n factor in the bound; in realistic graphs λ2 ≈ 1, so GoSta should dominate.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Expectation-only guarantees: Theorems 1 and 2 bound E[Z(t)] but do not provide mean-square error or high-probability bounds. Concentration (e.g., via martingale or mixing-time arguments) would strengthen the results and better match empirical variance reductions.
- Asynchronous rate and constants: The O(log t/t) bound uses an unspecified c′(G). It would be useful to make this constant explicit and to clarify whether the logarithmic factor is an artifact of the proof technique or fundamental.
- Practical assumptions: Both algorithms assume uniform random edge activations and (for async) knowledge of degrees and |E| to compute pk. In many decentralized settings, nodes may not know |E| or may have non-uniform or time-varying activation patterns. Discussing robustness (e.g., push-sum for directed/non-doubly-stochastic weights, lazy updates to handle bipartite graphs, or estimators of pk) would improve practicality.
- Communication model: Although GoSta reduces memory/communication versus U2 (one auxiliary observation vs two), communication still involves swapping full data vectors of dimension d. The paper would benefit from a quantitative comparison of per-iteration message size and total communicated bits to reach a target error (including dependence on d), and from a discussion of compression/quantization.
- Scope of experiments: Evaluations are on n ≤ 1599 with relatively small d and a handful of graph types. There is limited assessment of the asynchronous variant and no wall-clock communication-time measurements. Larger graphs (e.g., n ≥ 10^4), sparser/heterogeneous topologies, and sensitivity to degree heterogeneity would better validate graph-dependent rates. Baselines such as sampling-based approximations (e.g., each node maintains a small reservoir of foreign samples) would also be informative.
- Clarity/minor issues: Some matrix dimensions (M2) are terse and could be clarified. The non-bipartite assumption could be removed by adding laziness to Wα. The note H(X, X) = 0 is restrictive; discussing how to handle nonzero diagonals (or using the classic n(n − 1) normalization) would broaden applicability.

Significance and originality
Decentralized estimation of pairwise statistics is important for metrics like AUC, Gini, Kendall’s tau, and cluster scatter, which arise in monitoring, ML evaluation, and hypothesis testing across distributed data. The paper provides a simple, implementable algorithm with provable rates tied to the spectral gap and improves upon the only prior decentralized U-statistic method. The phantom-node analysis is a nice conceptual contribution that may be reusable.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Proofs relegated to the supplement are acceptable, but adding brief proof sketches (especially for the key decoupling and the origin of the log factor in async) would help. A clearer description of per-iteration costs and a figure/table summarizing complexity and bounds would improve readability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide MSE or high-probability convergence guarantees; characterize variance across nodes over time.
- Make c′(G) explicit and discuss whether O(log t/t) is tight for async.
- Remove the non-bipartite requirement via lazy updates or discuss how to handle bipartite graphs.
- Discuss robustness to unknown |E|/pk, directed or time-varying graphs (e.g., push-sum).
- Quantify communication/memory complexity and compare total communication to achieve a target error; consider compressed communication.
- Expand experiments: larger n, more async runs, ablation on spectral gap, and additional baselines.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant, underexplored problem; practical algorithms with clean theory.
- Novel analysis via phantom nodes; rates with spectral-gap dependence.
- Improves over U2-gossip in both theory and practice.
- Simple, implementable methods; both synchronous and asynchronous variants.

Cons
- Only expectation bounds; async constant unspecified; limited exploration of robustness.
- Practical assumptions (uniform edge selection, knowledge of |E|) may be strong.
- Communication cost and scalability not fully quantified; experiments modest in scale.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is solid, original, and useful, with clear improvements over prior work. Addressing the noted theoretical and experimental gaps would strengthen the paper further. Confidence: high.
