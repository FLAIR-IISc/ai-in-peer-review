### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes two randomized gossip algorithms, one synchronous (GoSta-sync) and one asynchronous (GoSta-async), for the distributed estimation of degree-two U-statistics. Standard gossip algorithms are typically limited to computing separable sums or averages, whereas U-statistics involve averages over pairs of data points distributed across a network. The proposed approach cleverly combines a data propagation mechanism, where nodes swap auxiliary data observations, with a standard estimate averaging step. The authors provide a convergence analysis for both algorithms, establishing rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. The analysis relies on a novel reformulation using a "phantom graph" to model the coupled dynamics of data propagation and estimate averaging. The paper compares its methods against the prior work of Pelckmans and Suykens (2011) [17], arguing for improved convergence rates, lower memory overhead, and reduced communication costs. Experimental results on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation support these claims across various network topologies.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner, which has numerous applications in machine learning (e.g., AUC, variance, ranking statistics) but is not solvable by standard gossip averaging techniques.

2.  **Novel Approach:** The core idea of combining data propagation via swapping with consensus-based estimate averaging is elegant and well-motivated. It directly addresses the challenge that each node's component of the final sum depends on data from all other nodes.

3.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The convergence analysis, particularly the "phantom node" construction, is a novel and interesting analytical tool for this class of problems. The derived rates explicitly connect convergence speed to both data-dependent properties and network topology (via the spectral gap), which is insightful.

4.  **Strong Comparison to Prior Art:** The paper provides a direct and thorough comparison to the most relevant prior work, U2-gossip [17]. It not only claims superiority but also provides a refined theoretical analysis of the competing method to better highlight the specific advantages of its own approach (i.e., the negative impact of the `sqrt(n)` factor in the U2-gossip bound).

5.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate the superiority of GoSta-sync over U2-gossip on real datasets and across three different network types (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), corroborating the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core contribution, the paper is beset by a substantial number of minor-to-moderate flaws in its presentation, notation, and methodological description, which collectively undermine the confidence in its technical soundness and polish.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:**
    *   **Notation:** There is a persistent and fundamental notational error: the transpose operator is written as `>` instead of the standard `T`. This appears throughout the paper (e.g., `M>`, `1n>`, `e_i>`). Furthermore, standard mathematical notation is used inconsistently (e.g., `Xn` instead of `X^n`, `Rd` instead of `R^d`).
    *   **Figures:** The figures are missing captions entirely. Figure 2 and 3 are presented without any description, and their axes are not explicitly labeled, forcing the reader to deduce their meaning from the main text. This is a major oversight for a scientific publication.
    *   **References:** The bibliography is incomplete. Several entries are missing journal or conference information (e.g., [8] Hanley & McNeil, [4] Clémençon), which is unacceptable for a submission.
    *   **Typos and Grammar:** The manuscript contains several grammatical errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "The nodes need not to be aware"; "among classical U-statistics ... one can mention, among others").

2.  **Methodological and Technical Ambiguities:**
    *   **Algorithm 1 (Synchronous):** The description of GoSta-sync is highly problematic. Lines 3-5 state `for p = 1, . . . , n do`, implying that *every* node `p` updates its estimate `Zp` at *every* time step `t`. This constitutes a fully synchronous update step that is separate from the pairwise gossip communication. This strong synchrony requirement is unrealistic for most decentralized settings and contradicts the high-level motivation of gossip algorithms where typically only a small number of nodes are active at once. This needs significant clarification.
    *   **Algorithm 2 (Asynchronous):** There appears to be an error in the definition of `pk` as `2dk/|E|`. For an undirected edge `(i,j)` drawn uniformly from `E`, the probability of node `k` being an endpoint of that edge is `dk/|E|`. The factor of 2 is unjustified and potentially incorrect. This could have implications for the unbiasedness of the time estimator `mk(t)` and the subsequent analysis.
    *   **Non-Standard U-statistic Definition:** The paper defines the U-statistic with a `1/n^2` denominator (Eq. 1), which differs from the standard definition that uses `1/(n(n-1))` and sums over distinct pairs. While the authors note this in a footnote and assume `H(X,X)=0`, they do not discuss the implication of this choice. This deviation from standard statistical definitions should be more prominently justified.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** Remark 2 claims the method extends to nodes with multiple observations by "splitting each node... and new edges weighted judiciously." This is a non-trivial extension presented in a hand-wavy manner without any detail, and such unsupported claims should be either substantiated or removed.

**Decision**

While the paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of distributed computation, the high density of errors—ranging from distracting notational typos to serious ambiguities in the algorithm descriptions—prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The lack of care in the manuscript's preparation raises concerns about the rigor of the work that is not directly visible (i.e., the proofs in the supplementary material). The ambiguity in the synchronous algorithm's description (Algorithm 1) is a particularly critical flaw that must be addressed. I therefore recommend **rejection**, but I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper by thoroughly correcting the numerous presentation issues and clarifying the methodological details for a future submission. The core ideas are strong and deserve a more polished and precise treatment.
