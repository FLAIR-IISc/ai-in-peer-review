Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network via gossip. It proposes two randomized algorithms:
- GoSta-sync (synchronous): each node k keeps a single auxiliary observation Yk (initialized to Xk) and a running estimate Zk. At each global iteration t: every node performs a running average update Zk ← (t−1)/t Zk + (1/t) H(Xk, Yk); then a uniformly random edge (i, j) is activated, the endpoints average Zi, Zj, and swap Yi, Yj. The analysis introduces a “phantom network” with O(n^2) nodes to decouple averaging and data propagation; it proves convergence in expectation to the desired U-statistic with an O(1/t) rate featuring the spectral gap of the graph.
- GoSta-async (asynchronous): at each iteration, a random edge is selected; the two endpoints first average their Zi’s, then each updates its running average using a local counter mk that increments by 1/pk when selected (with pk = 2dk/|E| assumed known), ensuring E[mk(t)] = t. Expected convergence is shown with rate O(log t / t).

The paper compares with U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2009], arguing lower memory/communication (one auxiliary vs two; one edge activation vs two) and faster convergence. Experiments on AUC estimation and within-cluster scatter on complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs support the claims.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored problem: decentralized computation of pairwise statistics via gossip.
- Simple algorithms with low per-iteration communication/memory cost (one auxiliary variable; one edge message).
- Analysis using the phantom-network construction is insightful and may be reusable.
- Clear dependence of rates on graph connectivity (spectral gap) and data dispersion; intuitive.
- Empirical results on real datasets and various graph topologies consistently favor the proposed method.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness/derivations:
- Inconsistency in the averaging operator: The expected pairwise-averaging operator for uniform edge selection is E[I − 1/2 (ei − ej)(ei − ej)T] = I − (1/(2|E|)) L(G). You define Wα(G) = I − 2/(α|E|) L(G). Matching the operator yields α = 4, not α = 2. However, you repeatedly use W2(G) in M1(t) and define c(G) = 1 − λ2(2), yet later state “for a complete graph 1 − λ2(2) = 1/n,” which matches α = 4 (since |E| = n(n−1)/2 and βn−1 = n gives 1 − λ2(4) = 1/(n−1) ≈ 1/n). This factor-of-two inconsistency pervades Theorem 1, the definition of c(G), and the narrative examples.
- AUC as a U-statistic: Eq. (3) normalizes by (#pos × #neg), which is a global quantity. It is unclear how nodes obtain this denominator in the decentralized setting (especially in the asynchronous case). If it is folded into H, you need to state how nodes learn it; otherwise, the proposed algorithms as written cannot compute AUC without additional global aggregation.
- Expectation-only guarantees: Both the synchronous and asynchronous results are in expectation. No high-probability or almost-sure convergence, nor variance bounds, are provided. This is weaker than standard randomized gossip analyses that often give concentration around the mean.
- Asynchronous algorithm relies on global knowledge: Algorithm 2 requires pk = 2dk/|E| at each node; knowing |E| is a strong global requirement that contradicts your earlier “nodes need not be aware of the global topology” claim. Using the unscaled local count (number of times selected) would avoid this, but then unbiasedness and analysis must be revisited.
- Communication/compute normalization in experiments: Plots are vs. “iterations,” but GoSta-sync uses one edge communication per iteration while U2-gossip uses two. Also, GoSta-sync computes H(Xk, Yk) at every node at every iteration (O(n) H-evaluations per iteration), whereas U2-gossip computes one H per node per iteration as well, but communication differs. A fair comparison should normalize by edge activations or total messages/bytes, and ideally also consider H-evaluation cost if H is expensive/high-dimensional.
- Bound for U2-gossip: The presented refined rate (Eq. 10) includes a √n/t factor in Euclidean norm. This mixes algorithmic behavior with the choice of norm and makes cross-paper comparison tricky; at minimum, report per-coordinate error or sup-norm bounds to disentangle trivial √n factors.
- Clarity of the phantom model matrices: The definitions of M2(t) (“B”) and M3(t) (“C”) are hard to parse; dimensions and exact block structure are not fully explicit. This affects reproducibility of the analysis.
- Graph assumptions: You require connected and non-bipartite graphs. It would be helpful to clarify whether non-bipartiteness is essential (e.g., to avoid periodicity) under your random edge model, or whether lazy variants remove the need for this assumption.

Presentation/clarity:
- Numerous typographical and formatting issues: “U -statistics” spacing, hyphenation breaks (“lo- cal”), missing spaces (“datadependent”), “1 Poisson process,” footnote markers misplaced in the main text, inconsistent notation (W2 vs W1/4), and some awkward grammar (“need not to be aware”). These hinder readability and suggest limited proofreading.
- Missing details: How are degree and |E| disseminated to all nodes? How are labels/partitions made available to nodes for H in the applications? How is random edge sampling implemented in practice without a centralized oracle?
- Figures referenced but not shown in the submission; statistical reporting (variance across nodes vs. runs) is ambiguous.

Originality and related work:
- The idea to mix data propagation (random-walk swapping) with averaging is natural and relates to token-based or randomized rumor-spreading schemes. Citing additional closely related work on random-walk-based aggregation or coalescing random walks would strengthen positioning.
- Prior art on concentration for randomized gossip (e.g., Boyd et al., 2006; or more recent high-probability bounds) is not leveraged.

Significance:
- The problem is important, and the algorithms are simple and potentially impactful. However, the current theoretical guarantees (expectation-only, unspecified constants in async) and experimental normalization limit the strength of the claims.

Pros
- Addresses a practical gap (pairwise/statistics over networks) with simple algorithms.
- Insightful phantom-network analysis; clear spectral-gap dependence.
- Memory/communication-light per iteration; single auxiliary variable.
- Empirical results favorable across multiple graph families and tasks.

Cons
- Core inconsistency in averaging operator (W2 vs actual operator) affecting constants and statements.
- Expectation-only results; no concentration or almost-sure convergence.
- Asynchronous algorithm assumes global knowledge of |E|.
- AUC denominator/global quantities not specified in decentralized setting.
- Experimental comparisons not normalized for communication; figures/methodology details limited.
- Numerous clarity/typographical issues.

Questions for authors
1) Please resolve the Wα(G) inconsistency and restate c(G) and examples accordingly.
2) How do nodes obtain #pos, #neg, |E|, and other global counts? Can you remove these assumptions?
3) Can you provide high-probability bounds or at least variance bounds on Zk(t)?
4) Can you replot results normalized by total edge activations/messages and report computational costs (H evaluations)?
5) For bipartite graphs, would adding laziness (self-loop probability) remove the non-bipartite assumption?

Recommendation
Leaning toward rejection in its current form. The conceptual contribution is promising, but the technical inconsistencies, reliance on global knowledge in the asynchronous setting, and unnormalized experimental comparisons are significant. With corrections to the analysis (operator constants), clarified assumptions, stronger probabilistic guarantees, and properly normalized experiments, this work could become a solid contribution.
