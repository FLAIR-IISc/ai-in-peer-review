

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

## Summary
This paper proposes synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, addressing a gap in existing gossip algorithms that cannot efficiently compute pairwise statistics. The authors introduce "GoSta" algorithms that combine data propagation with averaging, proving convergence rates of O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings. The paper compares their approach to U2-gossip [17], claiming improvements in convergence rate, memory, and communication costs. The experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation support their claims.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a well-motivated problem in decentralized computation with clear relevance to practical applications. The phantom node reformulation is an interesting technical contribution that enables the convergence analysis. The experimental validation on real datasets is appropriate and supports the theoretical claims. The paper properly contextualizes its work within the gossip algorithm literature and identifies a genuine gap in existing approaches.

## Weaknesses
Despite the promising contribution, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation issues that undermine its credibility as a scientific contribution. As a perfectionist reviewer, I must highlight these flaws:

1. **Mathematical Errors**: The paper claims "λ2(2) = 1 - 1/n for the complete graph" (Section 4.1). This is incorrect. For a complete graph with n nodes, the second largest eigenvalue of W2(G) is actually 1 - 2/n, not 1 - 1/n (see standard spectral graph theory references). This fundamental error calls into question the accuracy of the convergence rate analysis.

2. **U-statistic Definition Inconsistency**: The paper acknowledges that "the usual definition of U-statistic differs slightly from (1) by a factor of n/(n-1)" but fails to address this consistently throughout the paper. The algorithm and convergence proofs assume the biased estimator (1/n²), while the problem statement and applications (AUC, within-cluster scatter) typically use the unbiased version. This inconsistency creates ambiguity in the theoretical claims.

3. **Formatting and Typographical Errors**: 
   - Equation (1): "1 n2" should be "1/n²"
   - Equation (3): Inequality direction appears incorrect ("`i(θ>Xi)>−`j(θ>Xj)" should likely be "≥" or ">" with proper sign handling)
   - Algorithm 1: "t−1t" should be "(t-1)/t" in line 4
   - Multiple inconsistent uses of "U -statistic" vs "U-statistic"

4. **Broken Dataset References**: The SMVguide3 dataset reference "http://mldata.org/repository/data/viewslug/svmguide3/" is broken (mldata.org is no longer active), and the Wine Quality dataset link "https://archive.ics.uci.edu/ml/datasets/Wine" is not a direct dataset link. This undermines reproducibility.

5. **Incomplete Technical Justification**: The paper claims "faster convergence together with lower memory and communication costs" compared to [17] but fails to provide a clear theoretical comparison of communication complexity (e.g., O(1) vs O(2) messages per iteration). The memory comparison is also vague.

6. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses both "λ2(2)" and "λ_2(2)" without consistency. The phantom network construction (G̃) is described with inconsistent notation (e.g., "Gk" vs "G_k").

7. **Reference Issues**: The reference for [17] is incomplete in the main text, and the reference list contains multiple entries with missing information (e.g., "Modern Graph Theory, volume 184, Béla Bollobás, , , ").

8. **Theoretical Claim Overstatement**: The paper states "these convergence bounds feature data-dependent terms that reflect the hardness of the estimation problem" without providing any empirical evidence to support this claim (e.g., how different datasets affect the convergence rate).

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the problem addressed is interesting and the overall approach has merit, the paper contains multiple fundamental mathematical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and unaddressed technical gaps that significantly undermine its scientific contribution. The error in the complete graph eigenvalue calculation is particularly concerning as it directly impacts the convergence rate analysis. The inconsistent treatment of the U-statistic definition creates ambiguity about the actual quantity being estimated.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to correct these issues, particularly the mathematical errors and inconsistent notation. As it stands, the technical inaccuracies prevent me from recommending acceptance, despite the potentially valuable contribution to the field. The authors should revisit their convergence analysis and ensure all mathematical claims are rigorously verified before resubmission.

The paper's significance is potentially high, but the quality of presentation and technical accuracy is insufficient for publication at NIPS. The authors would be well advised to address these concerns in a revised version before resubmitting to a venue that prioritizes mathematical precision.
