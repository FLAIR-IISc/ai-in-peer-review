Summary
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network via gossip. It introduces two randomized algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node holds one observation Xi and maintains a local estimate Zk and a single auxiliary observation Yk that performs a random walk over the graph. Each iteration combines (i) propagation of auxiliary observations (by swapping Y’s along a randomly chosen edge) to let node k sample terms H(Xk, Yk), and (ii) averaging of local estimates between the two incident nodes. The authors analyze convergence in expectation: GoSta-sync achieves O(1/t) with data-dependent constants and a network-dependent factor tied to the spectral gap 1−λ2(W2(G)), while GoSta-async achieves O(log t/t). The analysis uses a “phantom network” construction to decouple propagation and averaging, leading to a block-matrix recursion. The paper also refines the convergence analysis of the prior U2-gossip algorithm [Pelckmans & Suykens 2009], showing a √n/t dependence that explains its poorer empirical performance. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several network topologies show GoSta’s advantages in both accuracy and variance across nodes, with practical parity between synchronous and asynchronous variants.

Relation to prior work
Classical randomized gossip computes separable aggregates (e.g., sums/means) with fast mixing dependent on the spectral gap (Boyd et al., Dimakis et al., Kempe et al.). However, these approaches do not directly handle pairwise interactions required for U-statistics. The closest prior work is U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens], which propagates two auxiliary observations but lacks averaging, incurs higher memory/communication, and is synchronous. This paper’s main novelty is to marry averaging with single-sample propagation and provide explicit rates that expose both data- and graph-dependent terms. The “phantom nodes” analytical device resembles lifted/product graph arguments, but its application to U-statistics gossip and the resulting block-structured bounds appear new. The asynchronous variant follows the standard Poisson clock model (Boyd et al.), but adapts stepsizes via local counters to retain unbiasedness.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic ideas with small per-iteration footprint: one edge activation, one auxiliary sample per node, swap + averaging.
- Rigorous expectation convergence analysis with explicit spectral-gap dependence; O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous.
- Refined comparison to U2-gossip with a bound explaining empirical inferiority (√n factor).
- Practical relevance: U-statistics cover AUC, Kendall tau, scatter, etc. Demonstrations on real datasets and varied topologies.
- The analysis cleanly separates data propagation and averaging via the phantom network, yielding interpretable constants.

Weaknesses and questions
- Only convergence in expectation is proved. No high-probability or mean-square error bounds are provided, which are standard in gossip analyses and important to understand variability and stopping criteria.
- The asynchronous algorithm assumes nodes know pk = 2dk/|E| and |E|. Global knowledge of |E| is often unrealistic; the paper should discuss how to estimate pk locally or replace with adaptive stepsizes (e.g., Robbins–Monro) and the impact on rates.
- The O(log t/t) bound for asynchronous is looser and the constant c′(G) is not explicit. Can tighter bounds (e.g., O(1/t)) be obtained under mild additional assumptions (lazy updates, doubly-stochastic edge selection) or by analyzing expected steps per node rather than wall-clock ticks?
- Robustness and generality: undirected, static, connected, non-bipartite graphs are assumed. Practical systems often face time-varying graphs, directed/asymmetric links, delays, and packet loss. Some discussion or mild extensions (e.g., push-sum for directed graphs, laziness for bipartite graphs) would strengthen the impact.
- Experiments focus on relative error vs iterations. It would be valuable to report communication and memory complexity to reach a target accuracy ε, and wall-clock time under realistic asynchrony to corroborate the claimed efficiency.
- Data-dependent constants appear in the bounds (norms of h−Û1 and H−h1ᵀ). The paper does not illustrate how these impact convergence across datasets or kernels H; some synthetic experiments varying dispersion would improve insight.
- Scope: only degree-2 U-statistics are treated. A brief discussion on extending the propagation/averaging scheme and analysis to higher-order U-statistics would be welcome.

Quality
The technical development is sound and well-motivated. The block-matrix formulation and spectral-gap dependence are consistent with established gossip analyses. Proofs are deferred; assuming they are correct, the claims are well-supported. The empirical comparisons are appropriate but could be more comprehensive on communication/time complexity.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The phantom network construction is explained carefully. Minor suggestions: explicitly state the per-iteration communication and memory costs for GoSta vs U2; clarify the non-bipartite assumption and how to handle bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy edge selection); and discuss the practicality of knowing |E| for pk.

Originality
The problem (distributed estimation of U-statistics) is timely and underexplored; the combination of averaging with single-sample propagation and the accompanying analysis appear novel relative to U2-gossip. The asynchronous variant is also new in this context.

Significance
Estimating U-statistics is important in distributed evaluation and learning. The proposed algorithms reduce memory/communication and improve rates compared to prior work, and the spectral-gap dependence suggests practical efficiency on well-connected networks. The work is likely to be used as a building block for decentralized evaluation/optimization.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and elegant algorithms with clear advantages over prior work
- Solid theory with interpretable constants and graph dependence
- Practical asynchronous variant
- Empirical validation on real data and multiple network models

Cons for acceptance
- No concentration/high-probability guarantees
- Asynchrony requires global knowledge of |E|
- Limited exploration of robustness (time-varying/directed graphs) and communication/time-to-accuracy

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide high-probability or mean-square error bounds.
- Discuss or implement pk estimation or stepsizes not requiring |E|.
- Report communication complexity and time-to-ε accuracy.
- Add notes on handling bipartite/directed/time-varying graphs.
- Explore sensitivity to data-dependent constants and consider higher-order U-statistics.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a clear, technically sound, and practically relevant contribution to decentralized estimation, improving the state of the art for U-statistics gossip and opening avenues for further work in distributed learning and optimization.
