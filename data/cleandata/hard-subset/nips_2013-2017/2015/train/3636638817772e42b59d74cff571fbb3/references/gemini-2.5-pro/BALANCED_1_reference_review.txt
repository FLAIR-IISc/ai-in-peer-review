### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces novel randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, for the decentralized estimation of degree-two U-statistics over a network. U-statistics, such as sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster scatter, are averages over pairs of data points, making them unsuitable for standard gossip averaging algorithms which compute sums of separable functions. The proposed approach cleverly combines two mechanisms: (1) a data propagation step, where auxiliary data observations perform a random walk on the network via swaps between neighboring nodes, and (2) an estimate averaging step, where nodes exchange and average their local estimates of the U-statistic. The authors present both synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) versions of their algorithm.

The main theoretical contribution is a convergence analysis for both algorithms, showing that the local estimates converge in expectation to the true U-statistic at rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version. A key element of the proof is an original and elegant reformulation of the system dynamics using a "phantom network" construction, which decouples the analysis of data propagation and estimate averaging. The paper positions this work as a significant improvement over the only known prior work in this area, U2-gossip [17], arguing for better convergence rates, lower memory requirements (one auxiliary variable vs. two), and lower communication costs (one swap vs. two per iteration). These theoretical claims are empirically validated on real-world datasets for AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation tasks, demonstrating superior performance across various network topologies.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithms are well-motivated and the theoretical analysis appears sound. The use of the "phantom network" is a creative and powerful analytical tool that makes the convergence proof tractable. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical bounds and comprehensive experimental results. The experiments are thoughtfully designed, comparing against the relevant state-of-the-art on different network types (complete, grid, Watts-Strogatz), which effectively demonstrates the influence of the network's spectral gap on performance as predicted by the theory.

A potential weakness in the analysis is that it focuses on convergence in expectation. While this is a standard and valuable first step, a high-probability bound or an analysis of the variance of the estimates would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's behavior and would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution. Additionally, the asynchronous algorithm (Algorithm 2) requires each node `k` to know its degree `dk` and the total number of edges `|E|` to compute `pk`. This might be a strong assumption in a fully decentralized setting where nodes have only local knowledge. A discussion of this assumption and potential ways to relax it would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, the motivation is compelling, and the related work is appropriately contextualized. The algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode, and the overall logic is easy to follow. The inclusion of Figure 1 to illustrate the phantom network construction is particularly helpful for understanding the proof technique. The experimental section is also clear, with figures that effectively convey the main results.

A minor point for improvement is the density of Section 4.1. The construction of the transition matrix `M(t)` could benefit from a slightly more detailed, step-by-step explanation. For instance, explicitly stating how the running average update in Algorithm 1 leads to the `(t-1)/t` and `1/t` factors in `M1(t)` and `M2(t)` would enhance readability for those less familiar with this style of analysis.

**Originality:** While the problem of gossip-based U-statistic estimation has been addressed before ([17]), this work offers a novel and substantially different approach. The core idea of combining a random walk of auxiliary data points with a parallel gossip averaging of estimates is, to my knowledge, original in this context. It elegantly resolves the core challenge of bringing pairs of data points together in the network. The "phantom network" proof technique is also a novel contribution to the analysis of such distributed algorithms. Therefore, the paper makes a clear and original contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms and decentralized computation.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant. U-statistics represent a broad and important class of functions in machine learning and statistics. This work provides a simple, efficient, and theoretically-grounded set of algorithms for their computation in decentralized environments. Given the demonstrated improvements over the previous state-of-the-art, the GoSta algorithms are likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners working on large-scale, distributed machine learning, particularly in sensor networks or peer-to-peer systems where central coordination is infeasible. Furthermore, this work could serve as an important building block for more complex decentralized tasks, such as the distributed optimization of objective functions based on U-statistics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses an important problem:** Provides an efficient solution for computing a wide class of important statistics (U-statistics) in a decentralized setting.
2.  **Novel and elegant approach:** The combination of data propagation via random walks and estimate averaging is a simple yet powerful idea. The analysis technique is also original.
3.  **Strong theoretical results:** Provides clear O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence rates that depend explicitly on network connectivity.
4.  **Significant improvements:** Demonstrates substantial gains over the existing state-of-the-art in terms of computational, memory, and communication efficiency, backed by strong empirical evidence.
5.  **High clarity and quality:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and technically solid.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Assumptions for async algorithm:** The requirement for nodes to know their degree and the total edge count in the asynchronous version might limit its applicability in some practical scenarios.
2.  **Analysis in expectation only:** The lack of high-probability bounds or variance analysis is a minor limitation of the theoretical results.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   It would strengthen the paper to discuss the assumptions made for the asynchronous algorithm. Could `|E|` be estimated using a separate gossip protocol? How sensitive is the algorithm to inaccuracies in `pk`?
*   While likely beyond the scope of a conference paper revision, mentioning the possibility of deriving high-probability bounds as a direction for future work would be valuable.
*   In Remark 2, the suggestion for handling multiple observations per node is a bit vague ("weighted judiciously"). It might be better to either provide a more concrete sketch in an appendix or simply frame it as an interesting avenue for future research.
*   The comparison to U2-gossip is excellent. For completeness, it might be interesting to briefly discuss why a naive approach (e.g., each node gossiping its own `Xi` to all other nodes first, then computing) would be inefficient in terms of communication/time complexity.

Overall, this is a very strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths. I recommend acceptance.
