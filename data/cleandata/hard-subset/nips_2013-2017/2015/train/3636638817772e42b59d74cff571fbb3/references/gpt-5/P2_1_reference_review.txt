Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of pairwise functionals—degree-2 U-statistics—over a network using gossip. Standard gossip efficiently computes separable sums but not pairwise quantities. The authors propose two randomized algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous), where each node maintains a single auxiliary sample that performs a random walk via swaps along randomly selected edges, while nodes also run a gossip averaging step on their current local estimates. A key analytical device is a “phantom nodes” construction that decouples data propagation from estimate averaging and yields linear recursion bounds. The main results show convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async), with constants that expose data difficulty and graph connectivity via the spectral gap. Compared to U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), the proposed methods require less memory/communication and achieve stronger rates in theory; experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several graphs confirm faster convergence and smaller variance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling is careful. The phantom-network reformulation leads to a clear block-matrix recursion, and the bounds separate data-dependent terms (dispersion of h and H) from graph-dependent terms (λ2 of Wα and the Laplacian spectral gap). The asynchronous scheme uses per-node counters scaled by 1/pk so that the effective step-size per node approximates 1/t, yielding unbiasedness and a rate with a log factor.
- Claims vs. support: The paper proves convergence in expectation; empirical results support superiority over U2-gossip across graph families. The comparison bound for U2-gossip, including its √n penalty, is informative and matches empirical behavior.
- Caveats: (i) Only convergence in expectation is proven; no high-probability or mean-squared error bounds are provided. Variance control (and inter-node disagreement) is observed empirically but not analyzed. (ii) The asynchronous rate has an extra log factor and the constant c′(G) is not explicit; a more interpretable dependence on spectral gap would strengthen the result. (iii) The asynchronous update requires nodes to know pk = 2dk/|E| (i.e., their degree and |E|). This may be unrealistic; robustness to misspecification or ways to estimate pk online are not discussed. (iv) U-statistics usually exclude diagonal terms; while the paper notes the normalization difference, a brief discussion of the bias induced when H(X,X)≠0 would help.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, with intuitive descriptions of the algorithms and their roles (propagation + averaging). The phantom node construction is well-motivated and the block structure helps understanding.
- Some presentation issues: a few typos and missing details in the references; “1 Poisson process” phrasing; Table 1 referenced but not included; notation is dense and some steps (e.g., the exact derivation of the exp term in the sync bound) are deferred to the supplement. It would help to explicitly summarize communication and memory per iteration and to clarify how denominators in AUC are handled in a decentralized manner.

Originality
- The problem—gossip for U-statistics—has prior work (U2-gossip). This paper’s combination of a single auxiliary random-walked sample with averaging, the phantom-node analysis, and a fully asynchronous scheme appear novel and nontrivial. The refined comparison bound for U2-gossip is also a useful contribution.

Significance
- U-statistics include widely used metrics (AUC, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter); enabling their decentralized estimation with low memory and communication is valuable. The explicit dependence on spectral gap is practically relevant for network designers. While rates are sublinear (unlike standard averaging), the approach improves on the previous state of the art and is likely to be adopted or extended (e.g., to optimization over U-statistics).

Pros
- Addresses a meaningful gap; practical algorithms with low per-iteration communication and memory.
- Solid theory with interpretable constants; new analysis technique (phantom nodes).
- Asynchronous variant with unbiased updates.
- Clear empirical improvement over U2-gossip across graphs and tasks; lower cross-node variance.

Cons
- Only expectation convergence; no variance/high-probability guarantees.
- Asynchronous rate has log factor and unclear constant; reliance on knowing pk and |E|.
- Experimental scope is modest (two datasets, moderate n); no wall-clock or message complexity comparisons; no tests on time-varying/directed graphs.
- Some clarity/presentation gaps and missing details.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide mean-squared error or high-probability bounds (possibly via martingale techniques) to complement expectation results? A disagreement bound across nodes would also be useful.
- Can pk be learned online (e.g., via push-sum or local degree estimation) to avoid requiring |E|? What is the impact of pk misspecification?
- Is the O(log t/t) rate intrinsic to this asynchronous scheme, or can it match O(1/t) with different step-sizes or bias-correction?
- Please add a concise communication/memory/runtime comparison table versus U2-gossip and baselines; include message counts and wall-clock on simulated platforms.
- Discuss extensions to time-varying or directed graphs and to nodes holding multiple samples beyond the brief remark (with a formal statement if possible).
- Minor: ensure referenced tables/values are present; clean up typos; clarify handling of AUC denominators.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated, technically competent paper with a clear advance over prior work on decentralized U-statistic estimation. Despite some limitations (expectation-only guarantees, pk knowledge, modest experiments), the contributions are original and significant for the NeurIPS community working on distributed learning and networks. I lean to accept (weak accept) conditional on clarifications and minor additions.
