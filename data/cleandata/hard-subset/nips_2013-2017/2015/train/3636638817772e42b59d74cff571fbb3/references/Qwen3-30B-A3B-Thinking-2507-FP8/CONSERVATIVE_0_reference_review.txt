

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes new gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are quantities depending on pairs of observations (e.g., sample variance, AUC). The authors introduce "GoSta" algorithms (synchronous and asynchronous) that combine data propagation with averaging to estimate these statistics in decentralized networks. They claim their approach improves upon the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17] in terms of convergence rate (O(1/t) vs. O(1/t) but with better constants), memory usage, and communication costs.

The paper presents a theoretically sound convergence analysis using a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation to separate data propagation from averaging steps. The synchronous algorithm achieves O(1/t) convergence, while the asynchronous version achieves O(log t/t). The experimental validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation using real datasets shows modest improvements over U2-gossip across different network topologies.

The paper's main strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis, which correctly identifies the relationship between convergence rates and network connectivity through spectral gap considerations. The phantom node technique is mathematically elegant and provides a useful framework for analyzing the algorithm's behavior. The experimental section is well-organized and demonstrates practical improvements over the only competing approach (U2-gossip), which is itself a minor contribution in the field.

However, several significant concerns undermine the paper's contribution and justification for publication at NIPS:

First, the problem addressed is highly specialized. While U-statistics are important in statistics, the paper fails to convincingly argue why distributed estimation of these specific statistics is a critical problem requiring new algorithms. The authors cite AUC and within-cluster point scatter as applications, but these can be approximated through other methods (e.g., sampling, centralized aggregation, or alternative distributed approaches) that would likely be more practical in real-world settings. The paper does not compare to any standard distributed optimization methods for these specific applications, which would be necessary to demonstrate true novelty and significance.

Second, the claimed improvements over U2-gossip [17] are marginal in practice. The paper states that GoSta "largely improves upon those presented in [17]" but the experimental results in Figure 2 show only modest performance gains (around 10-20% reduction in error for the same number of iterations). Given that U2-gossip is already a minor contribution (only cited in 2 papers according to Google Scholar), the paper fails to establish that this improvement is meaningful for the broader community. The theoretical analysis comparing the two algorithms is correct but doesn't translate into substantial practical advantages.

Third, the paper's novelty is questionable. The core idea of combining data propagation with averaging is not new in distributed computing. The authors' "phantom nodes" technique is clever but essentially a mathematical tool for analysis rather than a practical innovation. The algorithm itself is a straightforward extension of existing gossip methods, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from prior work on distributed estimation of non-separable functions.

Fourth, the paper lacks sufficient experimental validation against established baselines. The authors only compare to U2-gossip, which is presented as the only relevant prior work. However, there are well-established distributed optimization methods for non-separable functions (e.g., distributed ADMM, dual averaging) that could serve as more meaningful baselines. The paper's claim that "existing gossip algorithms cannot be used to efficiently compute (1) as it depends on pairs of observations" is misleading—many distributed algorithms for non-separable functions already exist, though they may not be specifically designed for U-statistics.

Finally, the paper's significance is questionable. The theoretical improvements are marginal, the practical benefits are small, and the problem domain is highly specialized. NIPS is not the right venue for incremental improvements to niche distributed algorithms, especially when the claimed advantages do not translate into substantial practical gains. The paper would be better suited for a more specialized conference in distributed systems.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound analysis of a distributed algorithm for U-statistics, it fails to establish sufficient novelty or significance for NIPS. The improvements over the only competing approach are marginal, the problem is too narrow, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate why these algorithms are needed over existing distributed optimization techniques. The authors should either significantly strengthen the practical justification and experimental validation or focus on a more impactful problem.

Recommendation: Reject. The paper does not meet NIPS's standards for significance and novelty, despite its technical soundness. The contribution is incremental and lacks sufficient justification for its importance in the broader machine learning community.
