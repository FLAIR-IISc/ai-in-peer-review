

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes "GoSta" algorithms (synchronous and asynchronous) for distributed estimation of U-statistics using gossip algorithms. The authors claim to extend gossip methods to handle quantities that depend on pairs of observations (U-statistics), which standard gossip algorithms cannot efficiently compute. They present theoretical convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version, and validate their approach on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

The paper builds upon prior work in gossip algorithms for decentralized averaging (e.g., [2, 10, 15, 18, 19]) and the limited work on U-statistics in distributed settings ([17]). The main theoretical innovation appears to be the "phantom nodes" reformulation used to analyze the algorithm's convergence, which the authors claim enables a more refined analysis than previous approaches.

**Strengths:**
1. The problem addressed is significant - U-statistics (AUC, variance, etc.) are widely used in machine learning, and distributed estimation is crucial for large-scale networks.
2. The theoretical analysis is mathematically sound, with careful attention to the convergence rates and their dependencies on network structure.
3. The paper provides a reasonable comparison to U2-gossip [17], which is the only prior work on distributed U-statistic estimation.
4. The experiments on real datasets demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Inadequate experimental validation:** The authors only compare to U2-gossip [17], but fail to include other relevant baselines. For instance, they don't compare to a simple centralized approach (gathering all data at a single node and computing the U-statistic) or to other distributed algorithms that might handle pairwise statistics differently. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming "superiority" of their approach.

2. **Lack of quantitative performance metrics:** The paper states "GoSta-sync outperforms U2-gossip on every network" but provides no specific quantitative measures (e.g., "GoSta-sync achieves 30% lower error" or "2.5x faster convergence"). The figures show relative error but don't provide enough detail to assess the magnitude of the improvement.

3. **Insufficient network configuration details:** The paper describes three network types (complete, 2D grid, Watts-Strogatz) but doesn't specify the actual network sizes, degrees, or parameters used in experiments. Without these details, it's impossible to assess how general the results are or whether the claimed improvements hold across different network topologies.

4. **Theoretical claims not fully substantiated:** The paper claims "faster convergence" but doesn't provide enough detail about the constants in the convergence rates. The theoretical analysis shows a rate improvement, but the paper doesn't demonstrate whether this translates to meaningful practical improvements in the experiments. The claim that "the performance gap widens as the size of the graph increases" (Figure 3a) is promising, but the paper doesn't show how the gap grows with network size in a way that demonstrates the significance of the theoretical improvement.

5. **Limited experimental scope:** The authors only test on two applications (AUC and within-cluster point scatter), which are both specific to supervised learning. U-statistics are used in many other contexts (e.g., nonparametric statistics, hypothesis testing), and the paper would be stronger with validation across a broader range of U-statistics.

6. **Asynchronous algorithm claims:** The paper claims "comparable performance" for GoSta-async despite its worse theoretical convergence rate (O(log t/t) vs O(1/t)), but provides insufficient evidence to support this claim. The empirical comparison in Figure 3b is limited and doesn't clearly demonstrate the practical equivalence.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in distributed learning, and the theoretical contributions are sound. However, as a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the experimental validation insufficiently thorough. The paper claims to "largely improve upon" prior work but fails to provide adequate empirical evidence to support this claim. The lack of comparison to additional baselines and the absence of specific quantitative performance metrics undermine the paper's significance claim.

While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the practical significance of the proposed algorithms remains unconvincing without more comprehensive experimental validation. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding comparisons to more established baselines, providing specific quantitative performance metrics, and testing on a broader range of U-statistics.

**Recommendation: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical extension to gossip algorithms, but the lack of thorough experimental validation against established baselines prevents me from endorsing it as a significant contribution to the field. The claims of superiority over prior work are not sufficiently substantiated with empirical evidence.
