## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics using gossip algorithms, a task previously challenging due to the pair-wise dependency inherent in U-statistics. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) that propagate local observations and average local estimates. They provide convergence guarantees (O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous) that depend on both data characteristics and network topology (spectral gap).  A clever reformulation using "phantom nodes" is key to their analysis.  Empirical results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrate the superiority of their approach compared to a previous work (U2-gossip).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Distributed estimation of U-statistics is crucial for many real-world applications involving decentralized data.  Existing methods struggle with this task, making this a valuable contribution.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of data propagation and local averaging within a gossip framework, coupled with the phantom node reformulation, is a novel and insightful approach.
* **Theoretical guarantees:** The convergence analysis provides valuable insights into the performance of the algorithms and identifies factors influencing convergence speed. The dependence on the spectral gap is particularly nice, connecting the algorithm's performance to network properties.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms and show improvements over a baseline (U2-gossip).
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured and relatively easy to follow, with a clear introduction of the problem and related work. The algorithm is presented concisely and clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to other baselines:** The primary comparison is to U2-gossip. While important, it would be helpful to see comparisons with more standard distributed estimation techniques (e.g., distributed stochastic gradient descent, or simpler averaging approaches) even if those are known to be less effective for U-statistics. This would further solidify the value of the proposed approach.
* **Complexity of analysis:** While the phantom node reformulation is clever, it introduces significant complexity to the analysis.  While the analysis appears rigorous, it makes the paper harder to digest and verify.
* **Asynchronous analysis less detailed:** The asynchronous analysis is less detailed and the convergence rate (O(log t/t)) is slower than the synchronous rate (O(1/t)). The justification for the slightly weaker bound could be expanded.
* **Network setup:** The experiments used relatively small network sizes. Exploring performance with larger networks and different network topologies would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the empirical results support the claims. The authors are careful to discuss limitations and potential extensions.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm descriptions are clear. However, the phantom node reformulation and its associated analysis could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Originality:** The approach of extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is original. The specific techniques used (data propagation, phantom nodes) are novel in this context. The related work section is adequate, though expanding it with comparisons to general distributed estimation methods would be beneficial.
* **Significance:** The results are significant.  Efficiently computing U-statistics in a distributed manner is a valuable contribution with the potential to impact various applications in machine learning, data mining, and statistics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging and important problem.
* Presents a novel and theoretically grounded solution.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance.
* Offers valuable insights into the interplay between network topology and convergence speed.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to broader baselines in distributed estimation.
* Analysis complexity makes the paper harder to follow.
* Asynchronous analysis could be more detailed and its convergence rate improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  It presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem with significant practical implications. While the analysis is complex, the authors provide convincing evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness. Addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above (expanding baseline comparisons, clarifying the analysis) could further strengthen the paper, but I don’t believe they are critical for acceptance. The contribution is valuable and fits well within the scope of NIPS.



**Confidence:** 8/10
