This paper tackles the problem of extending gossip algorithms to compute U-statistics, a class of functions not efficiently solvable by standard gossip approaches. The motivation – decentralized computation in resource-constrained environments – is well-established and relevant to the NIPS audience. The authors present both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms, providing convergence analysis and empirical validation.

However, despite the relevance of the problem, I find the paper lacking in several key areas, and ultimately, I am leaning towards rejection. My primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution. While extending gossip algorithms is valuable, the presented work feels more like an application of existing techniques (gossip averaging, phantom node reformulation) to a specific problem, rather than a fundamentally new methodological advancement. The “phantom nodes” trick feels contrived and serves primarily to facilitate the application of existing analysis tools.

The theoretical analysis, while present, relies heavily on established spectral graph theory. The convergence rates (O(1/t) and O(log t/t)) are not particularly surprising given the connection to averaging, and the data-dependent terms, while acknowledged, don’t seem to provide significant practical insight beyond existing bounds for similar distributed estimation problems. The authors claim improvements over [17], but the presented comparison primarily focuses on communication and memory costs, which are secondary considerations to the fundamental convergence properties. A more rigorous comparison of the theoretical convergence rates, and a detailed analysis of the constants involved, is needed to substantiate this claim.

The experimental evaluation, while helpful, feels limited. The datasets are relatively small, and the network topologies explored are somewhat standard. More extensive experimentation, including larger graphs, different network structures (e.g., scale-free networks), and comparison with alternative distributed estimation methods (beyond just [17]), would significantly strengthen the paper. The claim of superiority in the AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation is not particularly convincing without a more thorough benchmark against established algorithms on these tasks. The observed performance difference could be due to factors other than the algorithm itself (e.g., hyperparameter tuning).

The clarity is adequate, but the presentation could be improved. The notation is dense, and the connection between the phantom node reformulation and the actual algorithm is not immediately obvious. A more intuitive explanation of the algorithm's operation, and a clearer derivation of the convergence bounds, would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in decentralized computation.
*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithms.
*   Includes empirical validation on real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution – largely applies existing techniques to a new problem.
*   Theoretical analysis relies heavily on established results and doesn’t offer substantial novelty.
*   Experimental evaluation is limited and doesn’t provide strong evidence of significant performance improvements.
*   The phantom node reformulation feels artificial and hinders clarity.
*   Comparison to prior work [17] is not sufficiently detailed and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate superiority.



**Overall:** This paper presents a technically sound, but ultimately incremental contribution. The lack of significant novelty, combined with the limited experimental evaluation, makes it a weak submission for NIPS. While the problem is relevant and the authors demonstrate competence in applying existing techniques, the paper doesn’t rise to the level of significance expected for publication at this venue. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to consider submitting to a more applied conference where the practical aspects of the algorithm might be more highly valued.
