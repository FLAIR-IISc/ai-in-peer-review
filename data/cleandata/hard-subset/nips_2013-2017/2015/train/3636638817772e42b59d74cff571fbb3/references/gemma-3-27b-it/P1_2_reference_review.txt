## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics – quantities that require averaging over pairs of data points – using gossip algorithms. Existing gossip algorithms excel at distributed averaging of single observations but struggle with U-statistics. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, dubbed GoSta, leveraging propagation of local observations and averaging of local estimates. They provide theoretical convergence guarantees (O(1/t) for synchronous, O(log t/t) for asynchronous) and demonstrate improvements over prior work [17] in terms of convergence rate, memory usage, and communication cost. Experimental validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation using real datasets confirms the superiority of their approach.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of gossip algorithms, acknowledging prior work on distributed averaging ([19, 2, 10, 15]) and highlighting the limitations of existing approaches for U-statistics. The reference to [17] as the primary related work is accurate, and the authors clearly articulate the improvements their methods offer. The discussion of spectral graph theory ([3]) and its relation to convergence rates is also well-placed.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to efficiently estimate U-statistics is a valuable contribution. This tackles a limitation of existing distributed computation techniques.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides formal convergence analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms. The convergence rates are clearly stated and linked to network properties (spectral gap) and data-dependent terms.  The use of “phantom nodes” for the analysis is a clever and insightful technique.
* **Practical Improvements:** The authors demonstrate improvements over the prior art [17] in terms of memory and communication costs, making their approach more scalable and practical.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.  The comparison to U2-gossip is particularly convincing.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic descriptions (Algorithm 1 & 2) are clear and concise. The notation is mostly consistent.
* **Asynchronous Algorithm:** Providing an asynchronous algorithm in addition to the synchronous version is a significant strength, increasing the applicability of the work to real-world scenarios where global synchronization is impractical.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Analysis:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, it is quite involved and relies on the somewhat abstract “phantom node” construction. This could make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory to fully grasp the details. More intuitive explanations of this key element would be beneficial.
* **Network Assumptions:** The analysis assumes a connected, undirected graph. The robustness of the algorithms to disconnections or directed graphs is not discussed.  Exploring these scenarios would strengthen the work.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments focus on specific network topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts-Strogatz).  A more thorough investigation of the algorithms’ performance under varying network parameters (e.g., degree distribution, rewiring probability) would provide a more comprehensive understanding of their sensitivity.
* **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** While U2-gossip is compared, a more extensive comparison to simpler distributed averaging strategies (even if they are less efficient for U-statistics) would provide a stronger baseline for evaluating the benefits of the proposed algorithms.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The paper doesn’t delve into practical considerations like node failures or heterogeneous computation/communication capabilities. Addressing these aspects would further enhance the real-world relevance of the work.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important problem in distributed computation.
* The proposed algorithms are novel and theoretically sound.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.
* The authors address a significant limitation of existing gossip algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is complex and potentially inaccessible to some readers.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, exploring a wider range of network topologies and parameters.
* The paper does not address practical challenges like node failures or heterogeneity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed computation. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While the complexity of the analysis and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend the authors consider:

* Providing more intuitive explanations of the “phantom node” technique.
* Discussing the robustness of the algorithms to network disconnections and directed graphs.
* Expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of network parameters and baseline algorithms.
* Adding a discussion of practical considerations (e.g., node failures, heterogeneity).
