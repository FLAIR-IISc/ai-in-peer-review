

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes a novel approach to extending gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a significant class of statistics that depend on pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance, Gini mean difference). The authors introduce two algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, which combine data propagation with local averaging to estimate U-statistics in decentralized networks. The key innovation is their "phantom nodes" reformulation of the problem, which enables convergence analysis and proves O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence rates for the synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively.

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature on gossip algorithms. While previous work (notably U2-gossip) has attempted to solve this problem, it suffers from higher memory/communication costs and lacks asynchronous support. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach outperforms U2-gossip in both theory and practice, with lower communication costs, better convergence rates, and the ability to operate without a global clock.

The theoretical contribution is particularly strong. The phantom node construction is novel and elegantly enables the separation of the data propagation and averaging steps, which is crucial for the convergence analysis. The authors provide careful proofs of convergence rates that incorporate both data-dependent terms (reflecting problem hardness) and network-dependent terms (related to the spectral gap), showing how performance improves on well-connected networks. This level of theoretical insight is rare in distributed algorithms papers and significantly advances our understanding of the problem.

The experimental section thoroughly validates the theoretical claims with real-world applications (AUC estimation and within-cluster point scatter) across multiple network topologies (complete graphs, 2D grids, and Watts-Strogatz networks). The results consistently show GoSta outperforming U2-gossip, with the performance gap widening as network size increases - precisely as predicted by the theoretical analysis. The comparison between synchronous and asynchronous versions is also well-handled, showing that despite the slightly worse theoretical convergence rate, the practical performance is comparable.

The paper stands out as highly novel in a field where gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for simple averages but not for more complex statistics. It opens up an entirely new direction for gossip algorithms, moving beyond the well-trodden path of average computation to tackle a broader class of statistical problems. This is precisely the kind of bold, novel contribution that NeurIPS should prioritize.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, the problem statement is precise, and the related work section effectively situates the contribution within the broader literature on gossip algorithms. The only minor weakness is the somewhat abstract nature of the phantom node concept, but the authors provide sufficient explanation to make it accessible to the reader.

This work significantly advances the state of the art in distributed algorithms. It solves a problem that has been largely overlooked in the gossip literature (U-statistics), introduces a novel theoretical framework (phantom nodes), and demonstrates clear practical improvements over existing methods. The authors have made a substantial contribution that will likely inspire future work on extending gossip algorithms to more complex statistical problems.

Strengths:
- High novelty: Solves a previously overlooked problem (U-statistics estimation via gossip)
- Bold theoretical approach: Phantom node reformulation is innovative and enables rigorous analysis
- Strong theoretical results: Convergence rates with meaningful data- and network-dependent terms
- Clear empirical validation: Experiments confirm theoretical advantages across multiple datasets and network topologies
- Practical significance: Addresses a real need in decentralized machine learning

Weaknesses:
- The phantom node concept may require some mental effort to grasp initially
- The asynchronous convergence rate (O(log t/t)) is slightly worse than the synchronous rate (O(1/t)), though the authors justify this as necessary for asynchronicity

This paper represents a significant advance in the field. It's not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamentally new direction for gossip algorithms. The novelty and technical contribution far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work has the potential to influence future research in distributed statistics and machine learning, making it highly significant for the NeurIPS community.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel research that NeurIPS should celebrate. It addresses a meaningful problem with a creative solution that advances the field in a demonstrable way. The authors have made a substantial contribution that will likely be cited and built upon for years to come.
