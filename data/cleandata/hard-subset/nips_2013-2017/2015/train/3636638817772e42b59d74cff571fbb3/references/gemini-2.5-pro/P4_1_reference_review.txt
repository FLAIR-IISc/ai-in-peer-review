### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the problem of estimating degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip algorithms are efficient for separable functions like averages but are not directly applicable to U-statistics, which involve averages over pairs of data points distributed across the network. The authors propose a novel approach, GoSta, that combines two mechanisms: a data propagation step, where auxiliary copies of the data perform random walks on the network via neighbor-swapping, and an estimate averaging step, where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic with their neighbors.

The paper introduces both synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) versions of the algorithm. For the synchronous algorithm, the authors prove that the local estimates converge in expectation to the true U-statistic at a rate of O(1/t). For the asynchronous version, a convergence rate of O(log t / t) is established. A key element of the theoretical analysis is a novel reformulation of the system using "phantom nodes," which effectively decouples the data propagation and estimate averaging processes for the proof.

The proposed methods are compared to the main prior work in this area, U2-gossip [17]. The authors argue that their approach offers significant advantages, including lower memory and communication costs, faster theoretical convergence rates (lacking a detrimental `sqrt(n)` factor present in the analysis of U2-gossip), and the introduction of a fully asynchronous algorithm. These claims are supported by experiments on real datasets for estimating the Area Under the Curve (AUC) and within-cluster point scatter, demonstrating superior performance across various network topologies.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithms are simple and well-defined. The theoretical analysis appears sound and provides formal convergence guarantees. A major strength is the introduction of the "phantom network" construction, which is a clever and effective tool for analyzing the complex dynamics of the coupled processes. The convergence bounds are insightful, as they explicitly depend on both data-dependent terms (quantifying the estimation difficulty) and a network-dependent term (the spectral gap of the graph), which is consistent with established results in the gossip literature. The claims of superiority over previous work are well-supported by both a refined theoretical analysis of the competing method and comprehensive experimental results. The work is presented as a complete contribution, covering both synchronous and asynchronous settings.

A minor weakness is that the intuition behind the O(log t / t) rate for the asynchronous algorithm is not fully developed in the main text, with the details relegated to the supplementary material. While standard, a brief outline of why the logarithmic factor appears would improve the self-containedness of the paper.

#### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the contribution within the existing literature. The problem statement and notation are precise. The algorithms are presented clearly in pseudocode, and the analysis strategy is explained logically. The use of a diagram (Figure 1) to illustrate the "phantom network" construction is particularly helpful for conveying the core idea of the proof technique. The experimental section is clear, with well-chosen metrics and visualizations that directly support the paper's claims.

#### Originality

The work is original and makes a significant contribution to the field of decentralized computation. While gossip averaging and random walks are established concepts, their combination to solve the problem of U-statistic estimation is novel. This approach is fundamentally different from that of the primary existing work [17], which relies exclusively on data propagation. The development of a fully asynchronous algorithm for this problem is a key novel contribution, as it addresses a major practical limitation of prior work. Furthermore, the "phantom node" proof technique appears to be an original methodological contribution for the analysis of such algorithms.

#### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant. U-statistics encompass a wide range of important metrics in statistics and machine learning, including sample variance, AUC, Kendall's tau, and various clustering and hypothesis testing metrics. Providing an efficient, scalable, and robust (asynchronous) algorithm for computing these quantities in a decentralized manner has broad applicability in areas such as sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale distributed learning. The proposed algorithms demonstrably advance the state of the art by offering lower resource requirements and faster convergence than the best-known previous method. The work also opens up promising avenues for future research, such as extending the framework to decentralized optimization of objectives based on U-statistics.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of computing U-statistics in a distributed setting, a task for which standard gossip algorithms are insufficient.
2.  **Novel and Effective Solution:** It proposes a novel algorithmic approach that cleverly combines data propagation with estimate averaging, leading to significant performance gains.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis with an original proof technique, yielding insightful convergence bounds for both synchronous and asynchronous settings.
4.  **Significant Improvement over State-of-the-Art:** The proposed methods are shown, both theoretically and empirically, to be superior to the main existing competitor in terms of computational cost, communication overhead, and convergence speed.
5.  **High Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a complex topic accessible and the contributions easy to appreciate.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  **Incremental Nature:** The work could be viewed as a clever combination of existing primitives (gossip averaging, random walks) rather than a completely new paradigm. However, this combination and its rigorous analysis constitute a substantial and non-trivial contribution.
2.  **Scope Limitation:** The analysis is focused on degree-two U-statistics. While this is the most common case, the paper does not discuss potential extensions to higher-order U-statistics.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are compelling. The paper presents a strong, well-executed piece of research that advances the field of decentralized algorithms.
