Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics Ûn(H) = (1/n^2) ∑_{i,j} H(X_i, X_j) over a network where each node holds a single observation. Standard gossip algorithms efficiently compute separable sums but do not directly handle pairwise quantities. The authors propose randomized synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) gossip algorithms that combine two operations per iteration: (i) propagation of auxiliary observations via random edge swaps, and (ii) averaging of local U-statistic estimates. A key analytical device is a “phantom nodes” construction that decouples averaging and data propagation into an augmented graph, enabling convergence analysis.

Main results: in expectation, GoSta-sync achieves O(1/t) convergence with constants that separate data-dependent dispersion terms (in norms of h − Û1 and H − h1^T) from network-dependent terms determined by the spectral gap (1 − λ2) of a weighted averaging matrix W_α(G). GoSta-async achieves O((log t)/t) in expectation. Compared to U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2009], the proposed methods reduce memory/communication (one auxiliary sample per node; one edge activation per step) and improve rates (removing a √n factor in the bound). Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz networks corroborate faster convergence and lower across-node variance than U2-gossip. The paper situates the work relative to randomized gossip for averaging [Boyd et al., 2006], geographic gossip, and spectral graph theory.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The synchronous analysis appears careful. The block upper-triangular transition structure on the augmented graph and the relation to W_α(G) are standard and appropriate for average consensus-type processes. The O(1/t) and O((log t)/t) expectation bounds are plausible, with explicit dependence on spectral gap for the synchronous case. The comparison bound for U2-gossip clarifies where its √n penalty comes from.
- Assumptions and implementability: The theorems require the graph to be connected and non-bipartite and assume uniformly random edge selection per iteration. In practice, uniform edge sampling is nontrivial in a fully decentralized setting (standard asynchronous gossip induces edge selection proportional to node activation and neighbor-selection probabilities). The paper should clarify how to implement uniform-over-edges activation, or relax the analysis to Metropolis weights/edge rates arising from node-based activations.
- Bipartiteness vs experiments: The 2D grid is bipartite, yet it is used in experiments while the theorems assume non-bipartite graphs. Either justify why GoSta converges on bipartite graphs (e.g., via laziness/self-loops or running-averages that eliminate oscillations), or report that experiments used a lazy variant; otherwise, there is a theory–experiment mismatch.
- Asynchronous update weights: Algorithm 2 maintains m_k by adding 1/p_k upon activation and uses weight 1/(p_k m_k). Since p_k m_k equals the local activation count in expectation (not exactly), the justification that this yields an unbiased update at each t needs more intuition in the main text (proof is deferred). It would help to explain why a simpler 1/count weighting does or does not suffice, and to state clearly what knowledge is required at nodes (knowing d_k and |E|).
- Norms and constants: The manuscript defines ||·|| as the Euclidean norm but then applies it to matrices like H and H − h1^T. Please specify whether this is Frobenius or spectral norm and reconcile scaling with n. Quantifying constants (e.g., c′(G) in Theorem 2) in terms of spectral quantities, degrees, etc., would improve interpretability.
- Complexity: The communication/memory advantages over U2-gossip are qualitatively argued and visible in code, but a concise per-iteration cost table (messages, words moved, memory per node) would strengthen the case, especially for high-dimensional data.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The phantom-graph explanation is clear and helpful. Suggested improvements:
- Explicitly define all norms for vectors and matrices.
- State the exact conditions on edge activation processes needed for both algorithms (uniform over edges vs node-based activations) and how to realize them in practice.
- Address the bipartite issue or adopt a lazy averaging operator to cover bipartite graphs in theory.
- Add a brief complexity summary and discuss robustness to missing knowledge of |E| or degrees.

Originality
Estimating U-statistics via gossip is underexplored, with [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2009] being the main prior. The combination of averaging with single-sample propagation, the phantom-nodes analytical technique, and an asynchronous algorithm with rates are novel contributions relative to that prior and to standard gossip literature on separable functions.

Significance
U-statistics (variance, AUC, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter) are widely used; enabling their decentralized estimation with provable rates and practical resource use is valuable. The improvements over U2-gossip and the dependence on spectral gap make the results broadly relevant. Further significance would be demonstrated by larger-scale experiments, communication cost measurements, and application to additional U-statistics.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: gossip for pairwise statistics.
- Clear algorithms with lower resource usage than prior work.
- Solid convergence analysis with data- and network-dependent constants.
- Empirical results on real datasets and multiple network topologies showing consistent gains.

Cons against acceptance
- Theory–practice gap regarding non-bipartite assumption vs grid experiments.
- Implementability of uniform edge sampling not fully discussed.
- Ambiguity in norms and constants; asynchronous update rationale could be clearer.
- Limited empirical evaluation of communication/memory costs and large-scale performance.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- How to implement uniform edge sampling in a decentralized manner? Would Metropolis–Hastings neighbor selection and corresponding analysis suffice?
- Can you incorporate laziness (self-loops) to cover bipartite graphs in theory, matching your grid experiments?
- Please clarify the matrix norm used in bounds and provide scaling guidance.
- Could using the simple local activation count (without 1/p_k) in Algorithm 2 suffice, and how would that affect bias and rates?
- Add a concise analytical and empirical comparison of per-iteration and total communication/memory costs vs U2-gossip.
- Discuss robustness when nodes do not know |E| or degrees exactly.

Overall, this is a technically solid and novel contribution that advances decentralized estimation of U-statistics, with some clarifications and practical considerations that, if addressed, would strengthen the paper.
