

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper proposes new gossip algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) for distributed estimation of U-statistics, which are quantities depending on pairs of observations (e.g., AUC, sample variance). The authors claim their methods achieve O(1/t) convergence for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings, with lower memory and communication costs compared to the prior U2-gossip algorithm [17]. The theoretical analysis employs a novel "phantom nodes" reformulation to prove convergence.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-structured convergence proofs for both synchronous and asynchronous settings.
2. The paper provides a refined convergence rate analysis for U2-gossip [17], demonstrating that the √n factor in its error bound makes it less efficient than the proposed methods.
3. The phantom node reformulation is an interesting technical contribution that simplifies the convergence analysis.
4. The experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter show consistent improvement over U2-gossip across different network topologies.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The paper only compares to U2-gossip [17], which is a single prior method. Crucially, it fails to compare with a straightforward baseline: using standard gossip algorithms to compute the average of pairwise terms (i.e., having each node maintain all pairwise values and gossip those). This would be a natural baseline for distributed U-statistic estimation and would better demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.

2. **Lack of Practical Validation:** The experimental results use relatively small networks (n=50 to 1599), which don't represent the large-scale distributed systems where such algorithms would be most valuable. The paper doesn't provide evidence of how the algorithms scale to much larger networks or high-dimensional data.

3. **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper claims "smaller memory and communication cost per iteration" but provides no concrete metrics to support this. It also claims "faster convergence on well-connected networks" but the experiments don't sufficiently demonstrate this across the full range of network topologies tested.

4. **Limited Practical Significance:** The paper doesn't convincingly show that the theoretical improvements translate to meaningful practical advantages. The performance gap between GoSta-sync and U2-gossip appears modest in the experiments (e.g., Figure 2), and the paper doesn't quantify how much faster the proposed method is in terms of actual wall-clock time or communication rounds.

5. **Theoretical-Practical Disconnect:** The O(1/t) vs O(log t/t) convergence rates are asymptotic, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how these translate to practical performance differences. For example, the constant terms in the convergence bounds (which depend on network properties) aren't sufficiently explored in the experiments.

**Critical Concerns:**
The most significant weakness is that the paper doesn't adequately justify why the proposed approach is necessary. The problem of distributed U-statistic estimation could potentially be solved by simply using existing gossip algorithms to compute the average of pairwise terms (i.e., having each node maintain all pairwise values and gossip those). The paper doesn't explain why this straightforward approach isn't sufficient or why the proposed method is better.

The phantom node analysis, while theoretically interesting, seems designed to make the convergence proof tractable rather than reflecting a practical implementation. The paper acknowledges that "the phantom network is of size O(n²), but we stress the fact that it is used solely as a tool for the convergence analysis," which suggests the theoretical contribution may not directly translate to practical implementation advantages.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution, the lack of rigorous validation against more natural baselines and the limited demonstration of practical significance undermine its contribution. The problem is important, but the proposed solution doesn't sufficiently demonstrate superiority over simpler, more straightforward approaches that would be more likely to be adopted in practice. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with standard gossip approaches applied to this problem, as well as experiments on larger-scale networks that better reflect real-world distributed systems. Without these, the paper doesn't provide enough evidence to justify the new algorithmic approach over established methods.
