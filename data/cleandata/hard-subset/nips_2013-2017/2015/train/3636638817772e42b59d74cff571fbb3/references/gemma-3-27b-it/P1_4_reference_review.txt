## Peer Review for "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics, a class of functions common in machine learning (variance, AUC, etc.) that are challenging for traditional gossip algorithms. The authors propose randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms (GoSta) that combine data propagation and averaging to efficiently estimate U-statistics. They provide theoretical convergence guarantees, demonstrating rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) for the synchronous and asynchronous versions, respectively, and show dependence on both network topology and data characteristics.  The paper also introduces a clever reformulation using “phantom nodes” to aid in the analysis and demonstrates empirical improvements over a recent related work (U2-gossip) on real datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established literature of gossip algorithms for distributed averaging (Boyd et al., Kempe et al.) which struggle with non-separable functions like U-statistics. Existing attempts to address this, notably U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens), are highlighted as having limitations in memory usage, communication cost, and requiring synchronous updates.  The authors correctly position their work as an improvement on this existing approach, addressing its drawbacks. They also acknowledge and reference relevant work on spectral graph theory (Chung, Tsitsiklis) to contextualize network connectivity’s impact on convergence.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Distributed estimation of U-statistics is crucial for large-scale decentralized datasets, and the limitations of existing approaches motivate this research.
* **Novel approach:** Combining data propagation and averaging within a gossip framework is a clever and effective strategy for tackling the problem. The phantom node reformulation is insightful and facilitates the analysis.
* **Strong theoretical guarantees:** The convergence analysis provides valuable insights into the factors affecting performance – both network characteristics (spectral gap) and data-dependent terms.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrate the superiority of GoSta over U2-gossip, supporting the theoretical findings. The comparison across different network topologies is also insightful.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.  The notation is consistent, and the algorithms are presented clearly.
* **Asynchronous variant:** The inclusion of an asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) is a significant contribution, making the method more practical for real-world deployments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Phantom node complexity:** While theoretically helpful, the phantom node explanation feels somewhat convoluted. A more intuitive explanation linking this reformulation directly to the algorithmic steps might benefit readers.
* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t/t) rate for the asynchronous version is slightly weaker than the synchronous rate. While expected in many asynchronous settings, it might be worthwhile to discuss whether further optimizations could improve this bound.  The practical performance comparison is good, but a deeper theoretical discussion would be helpful.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While U2-gossip is a relevant comparison, a more extensive comparison with other potential baselines (e.g., a centralized approach with communication constraints) could further strengthen the results.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't delve into the practical challenges of implementing and deploying these algorithms in real-world scenarios, such as handling node failures or dealing with dynamic network topologies.
* **Dataset Size:** The datasets used for evaluation (SMVguide3, Wine Quality) are relatively small.  Testing on larger datasets could provide further validation of the scalability of the proposed algorithms.



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in decentralized computation.
* The proposed algorithms are novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong convergence guarantees.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is generally well-written and clear.

**Con:**

* The phantom node explanation could be improved for clarity.
* The asynchronous convergence rate is slightly weaker than the synchronous rate.
* The baseline comparison is limited.
* Limited discussion of practical considerations.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The proposed algorithms are innovative, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The paper meets the NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  **Clarify the phantom node explanation:** Provide a more intuitive connection between the phantom nodes and the algorithmic steps.
2.  **Discuss asynchronous convergence:**  Provide a more in-depth discussion of the asynchronous convergence rate and potential avenues for improvement.
3.  **Consider additional baselines:** Include a comparison with a centralized approach with communication constraints for a more comprehensive evaluation.
