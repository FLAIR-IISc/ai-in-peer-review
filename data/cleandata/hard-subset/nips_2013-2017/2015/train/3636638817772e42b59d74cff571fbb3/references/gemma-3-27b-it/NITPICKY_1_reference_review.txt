This paper proposes randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, a class of quantities not efficiently computable by standard gossip algorithms. The core idea is to combine data propagation with averaging of local estimates, utilizing a "phantom node" reformulation for analysis. The authors present convergence bounds and demonstrate the algorithms' effectiveness on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

While the problem addressed is relevant – extending the applicability of gossip algorithms – and the proposed solution appears theoretically sound, the paper suffers from a remarkable number of minor, yet distracting, flaws that collectively detract from its overall quality. My assessment is heavily influenced by these details, leading me to recommend rejection despite acknowledging the novelty of the approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is a worthwhile contribution.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence analysis, using the phantom node technique, appears rigorous (although I haven’t verified every step) and provides useful insights into the impact of network topology.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on real datasets demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The introduction effectively motivates the problem and its relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Formatting Inconsistencies:** Throughout the paper, there are minor formatting issues. For example, the spacing around equations is inconsistent, the use of boldface is sometimes arbitrary, and the citation style appears to fluctuate.
*   **Notational Ambiguity:** The use of both *n* for network size and sample size is confusing, especially in Section 2.2 where the dependence on *n* is crucial. A different symbol (e.g., *N* for network size) would improve clarity.
*   **Redundancy:** The introduction reiterates the advantages of gossip algorithms extensively, which feels unnecessary for a NIPS audience.
*   **Overly Detailed Introduction of Related Work:** While a review of related work is important, the level of detail in Section 3 feels excessive and could be streamlined. The specific references cited feel somewhat cherry-picked; important work on distributed optimization and consensus algorithms is missing.
*   **Inconsistent Use of Terminology:** In Section 2.1, the definition of a bi-stochastic matrix is correct but unnecessary. The algorithms described do not explicitly rely on this property.
*   **Minor Mathematical Errors/Imprecisions:** The equation in line 1 of Section 4.1, “HP(X,X ′) = ‖X −X ′‖ · ∑ C∈P I{(X,X′)∈C2}”, does not align with typical definitions of within-cluster point scatter where the sum is often over points *within* the same cluster.
*   **Algorithm 1 Description:** Line 5 of Algorithm 1 contains a typo ("Set Zp ← t−1t Zp + 1 t H(Xp, Yp)")—it should be “Set Zp ← (t-1)/t Zp + (1/t) H(Xp, Yp)". This is a small detail but indicative of a lack of careful proofreading.
*   **Missing Details in Experiments:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. For example, the specific network topologies used (beyond "complete graph," etc.) are not described in sufficient detail (e.g., degree distribution in the Watts-Strogatz network). The performance metrics are not fully explained; the relative error w.r.t. what ground truth?
* **Figure 1 is referenced but not present in the provided text.**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and previously unexplored problem.
*   The theoretical results are promising.
*   The experiments provide preliminary validation of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The sheer number of minor flaws (formatting, notation, typos, redundancy) reflects a lack of attention to detail.
*   The paper feels incomplete – lacking sufficient experimental details.
*   The contributions, while novel, might not be *significant* enough to overcome the quality issues. The performance gains demonstrated in the experiments are not overwhelmingly substantial.
*   The level of mathematical rigor, while present, is not flawless, and I lack the time to thoroughly verify all claims.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the ideas presented, the numerous minor flaws and the lack of polish significantly detract from the paper’s quality. While the theoretical analysis and experimental validation are promising, the paper requires substantial revision and improvement before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should prioritize careful proofreading, clarification of notation, and a more thorough experimental evaluation. I believe the core idea has potential, but it is currently obscured by these avoidable issues.
