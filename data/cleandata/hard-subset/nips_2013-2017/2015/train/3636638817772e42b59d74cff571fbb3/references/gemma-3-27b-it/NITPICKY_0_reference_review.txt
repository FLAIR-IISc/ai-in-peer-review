The paper proposes randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics, a class of functions challenging for decentralized computation. The core idea of introducing “phantom nodes” to reformulate the problem and enable the application of existing gossip-based averaging techniques is clever. The authors present convergence analysis and empirical validation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation.

However, despite the novelty of extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics, and the demonstrated empirical success, I find a substantial number of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to questionable claims and a lack of sufficient detail, leading me to recommend rejection. My primary concern stems from the meticulousness with which the analysis *appears* to be conducted, but then falls short in several crucial aspects.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics is a genuinely novel contribution. Existing gossip algorithms are not directly applicable, and the proposed approach addresses this limitation.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence analysis, while complex, provides theoretical guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The inclusion of data-dependent and network-dependent terms in the convergence rates is a plus.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on real datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of computing U-statistics in a decentralized manner and motivates the need for new algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Sloppiness:** There are numerous minor notational issues throughout the paper. For example, the definition of `Ûn(H)` uses `n∑ i,j=1`, which implies `i` and `j` cannot be equal. However, the definition of `H(X,X)` is 0, meaning the summation *should* allow `i=j`. This is a seemingly small detail, but suggests a lack of careful review. The use of both `[p]` and `[n]` for set notation is confusing. The notation `e1, . . . , en` for the standard basis is unusual and potentially ambiguous.
*   **Phantom Node Justification:** While the phantom node approach is conceptually interesting, the justification for its correctness feels hand-wavy. The connection between the phantom network and the original data propagation isn’t fully rigorous and relies heavily on the reader accepting the equivalence. The diagram (Figure 1) lacks sufficient detail to fully grasp the construction.
*   **Convergence Rate Details:** The reported convergence rates, while theoretically appealing, lack sufficient justification for practical utility. The constants hidden within the O(1/t) and O(log t/t) terms are crucial, but their dependence on problem-specific parameters (e.g., the norm of `H`) is not adequately explored. The claim of "faster convergence" compared to [17] relies heavily on these constants, which aren’t fully compared.
*   **Asynchronous Algorithm Weaknesses:** The asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) introduces `mk(t)` to account for clock drifts, but the analysis assumes perfect knowledge of `pk` (the selection probability). In a realistic asynchronous setting, estimating `pk` would introduce additional error and complexity, which isn't addressed.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** The related work section is surprisingly limited. The authors cite [17], but fail to comprehensively compare GoSta to U2-gossip beyond the stated memory and communication costs. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between the two algorithms is needed. The mention of [6, 13] regarding geographic gossip is superficial and lacks context.
*   **Experimental Details:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail. The parameters used for the Watts-Strogatz network (k=5, p=0.3) are chosen arbitrarily without justification. The sensitivity of the results to these parameters is not investigated. More rigorous statistical analysis of the experimental results (e.g., confidence intervals, p-values) is needed. The statement that “GoSta outperforms U2-gossip” is too strong; it should be qualified by mentioning the specific datasets and network topologies where this is observed.
*   **Equation (5) and Block Matrix Analysis:** The representation of M(t) in Equation (5) is poorly explained. The role of M2(t) and M3(t) are stated, but the derivation of their specific forms is missing. This makes it difficult to follow the convergence proof.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel extension of gossip algorithms.
*   Theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated empirical performance.

**Con:**

*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and sloppiness.
*   Lack of rigor in the phantom node justification.
*   Insufficient detail on convergence rate constants.
*   Questionable assumptions in the asynchronous algorithm.
*   Limited related work discussion and comparison.
*   Insufficient experimental detail and analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a novel approach, the numerous flaws in technical detail, analysis, and presentation significantly detract from its overall quality. The paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. The concerns regarding the phantom node justification, convergence rate constants, and experimental details are particularly serious. I believe that addressing these issues would require substantial revisions, and therefore recommend rejection.
