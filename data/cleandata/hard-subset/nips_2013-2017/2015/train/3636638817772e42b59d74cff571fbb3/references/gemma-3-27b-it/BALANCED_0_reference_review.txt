This paper addresses the problem of extending gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics in a distributed setting. U-statistics, representing averages over pairs of observations, are prevalent in machine learning (variance, AUC, etc.) and are challenging for standard gossip algorithms designed for separable functions. The authors propose synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms and provide convergence rate analysis, improving upon previous work [17]. The paper is well-motivated, tackles an important problem, and presents a solid technical contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Relevance:** The extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics fills a gap in the existing literature. Many real-world problems require computations of this form, and a decentralized approach is crucial for large-scale datasets.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms appear theoretically well-founded. The use of “phantom nodes” is a clever technique to reformulate the problem and facilitate the analysis. The convergence rate analysis, demonstrating O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous settings, is a significant contribution.
* **Improvements over Prior Work:** The paper clearly demonstrates improvements over the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17], achieving faster convergence, lower memory requirements, and asynchronous capability.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter using real datasets provide strong empirical evidence supporting the theoretical results and demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.  The comparison against U2-gossip is valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is relatively clear, and the algorithms are presented in a concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theoretical Analysis:** While the theoretical analysis is a strength, it’s also a potential barrier to understanding. The introduction of phantom nodes and the subsequent matrix manipulations (M1, M2, M3) might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory.  A more intuitive explanation alongside the mathematical details could be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** While U2-gossip is a relevant comparison point, the experiments could benefit from a comparison to more standard distributed averaging algorithms (e.g., those in [2]) to demonstrate the specific advantage of the U-statistic tailored approach.
* **Network Topology Sensitivity:**  The performance heavily relies on the spectral gap of the network graph. The paper mentions this, but a more detailed discussion of the implications for different real-world network topologies would be useful.  How robust are the algorithms to poorly connected graphs?
* **Asynchronous Algorithm Analysis:** The O(log t/t) convergence rate for the asynchronous algorithm feels slightly weaker than the synchronous case. While understandable given the increased complexity, exploring potential techniques to improve this rate could be valuable.
* **Lack of Scalability Analysis:** While experiments demonstrate performance on moderately sized datasets, a more in-depth analysis of scalability (e.g., how performance degrades with increasing network size and data dimensionality) would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in distributed computation with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The improvements over existing work (U2-gossip) are substantial.
* The experimental results are compelling and validate the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the proposed algorithms and analysis.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical complexity might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The limited comparison to other baselines leaves some questions about the absolute performance gains.
* The sensitivity to network topology needs further discussion, particularly regarding robustness.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed computation and gossip algorithms. The theoretical results are strong, the improvements over existing work are significant, and the experimental validation is convincing. While the theoretical complexity and limited baseline comparison are minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the paper.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

1. **Clarify the theoretical analysis:** Provide more intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical derivations, particularly concerning the phantom node concept and the matrix transformations.
2. **Expand the experimental comparison:** Include comparisons to more standard distributed averaging algorithms to better highlight the advantages of the proposed U-statistic algorithms.
3. **Discuss network topology robustness:** Elaborate on the impact of different network topologies on algorithm performance and explore potential mitigation strategies for poorly connected graphs.
4. **Consider scalability analysis:** Include a discussion about the scalability of the algorithms with respect to network size and data dimensionality.
