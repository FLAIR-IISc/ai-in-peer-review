

**Review**

**Summary of Main Ideas**  
This paper addresses decentralized estimation of U-statistics (quantities depending on pairs of observations) using gossip algorithms. While existing gossip algorithms compute separable functions (e.g., averages), they cannot efficiently handle U-statistics, which include important machine learning metrics like AUC, variance, and within-cluster point scatter. The authors propose "GoSta" algorithms that combine data propagation across the network with local averaging of estimates. They introduce a novel "phantom node" reformulation to analyze convergence, showing O(1/t) convergence for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous versions. The algorithms require lower memory and communication costs compared to U2-gossip [17], with experiments on AUC and within-cluster point scatter confirming practical superiority.

**Quality**  
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. Convergence proofs are rigorous, with careful analysis of dependencies on both data characteristics (through dispersion terms) and network properties (via spectral gap). The "phantom node" reformulation is a clever technical contribution that enables separation of data propagation and averaging steps in the analysis. The authors provide clear comparisons with U2-gossip [17], showing superior convergence rates (O(1/t) vs. O(√n/t)), lower memory requirements (one auxiliary observation vs. two), and reduced communication costs. The supplementary material contains sufficient detail for verification of all claims.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and well-structured sections. Algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and easy to follow. The "phantom node" concept is well-explained, though it may require some effort to grasp initially. The experimental section clearly presents results on real datasets, with appropriate metrics (average relative error, variance across nodes) that directly support the theoretical claims. The writing effectively communicates the relationship between the proposed approach and prior work.

**Originality**  
The paper identifies a clear gap in distributed computation literature: U-statistics estimation. Previous gossip work (e.g., [10, 2, 15, 11, 9]) focuses on separable functions, while U2-gossip [17] is the only prior work on U-statistics but has significant limitations (O(√n/t) convergence, higher memory/communication costs). The authors' "phantom node" reformulation represents a novel contribution that enables their superior convergence analysis. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, with the supplementary material confirming the novelty through comparison with recent NIPS proceedings.

**Significance**  
U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning and statistics, and decentralized estimation has practical value for sensor networks, peer-to-peer systems, and large-scale data processing. The paper demonstrates both theoretical and practical advantages over U2-gossip, with experiments confirming faster convergence and lower variance across networks. The results could enable new applications in decentralized machine learning, and the authors identify promising future directions (adaptive communication schemes, decentralized optimization of U-statistics). The work advances the state of the art in distributed computation by solving a previously unaddressed problem.

**Strengths**  
- Clear identification of a significant gap in distributed computation
- Novel theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs
- Practical algorithms with demonstrable advantages over prior work
- Well-executed experiments on two relevant applications (AUC, within-cluster point scatter)
- Strong organization and clear writing

**Weaknesses**  
- The "phantom node" concept may require deeper study of supplementary material for full comprehension
- Limited to two experimental applications (though these are well-chosen and representative)

**Overall Assessment**  
This paper makes a significant contribution to distributed computation by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics. The theoretical results are sound, the algorithms are well-designed, and the experimental validation is convincing. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work and demonstrates both theoretical and practical advantages. The minor weaknesses (phantom node complexity, limited experimental scope) do not detract from the paper's strong contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**
