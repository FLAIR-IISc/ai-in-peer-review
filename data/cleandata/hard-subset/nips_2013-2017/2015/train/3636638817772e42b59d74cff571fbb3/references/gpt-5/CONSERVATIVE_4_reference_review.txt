Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. The authors propose two randomized algorithms: a synchronous version (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous version (GoSta-async). The key idea is to combine (i) propagation of data observations via swaps along randomly selected edges and (ii) averaging of local estimates. The analysis uses an auxiliary “phantom network” to separate the averaging and data-propagation effects. The authors prove convergence in expectation to the true U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous), with constants depending on a dispersion of the kernel values (data-dependent) and the spectral gap of the graph (network-dependent). They compare against the U2-gossip algorithm [17], arguing improved rates/constant factors and lower memory/communication overhead. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over several graph topologies show empirical improvements over U2-gossip.

Relation to prior work
This work sits at the intersection of gossip/consensus (e.g., Boyd et al., Kempe et al.) and U-statistics in decentralized settings. Prior work [17] proposed U2-gossip for U-statistics. The main difference here is incorporating an explicit averaging step and providing an asynchronous variant. The use of spectral gap in rates is standard in gossip analysis; the “phantom network” is mainly an analytical device.

Quality
- Theory: The expectation convergence rates are plausible and consistent with intuition: averaging leads to network-dependent exponential contraction for the averaging component, and the need to propagate pairs slows overall rates to O(1/t) or O(log t / t). However, the guarantees are only in expectation; no high-probability or almost-sure convergence bounds are given. The bounds use norms on matrices (e.g., ||H − h1^T||) without specifying the matrix norm (Frobenius vs operator), which impedes reproducibility.
- Assumptions: Convergence requires connected, non-bipartite graphs. The asynchronous algorithm requires each node to know pk = 2dk/|E|, thus global knowledge of |E| (and degrees), which is often unrealistic in decentralized networks. The authors assert unbiasedness via E[mk(t)]=t; while correct in expectation, the variance impact of this correction is not characterized.
- Practicality: Communication cost is reduced relative to U2-gossip (one edge per iteration, one auxiliary observation per node), but the paper does not report error vs communication bytes or messages, only error vs iterations.
- Experiments: Empirical validation is rather limited. The main comparison is to U2-gossip; other plausible baselines (e.g., Monte Carlo pair sampling with random-walk token propagation, push-sum/broadcast variants, centralized sampling bounds) are deferred or absent. There is no study of concentration across runs beyond standard deviations, nor analysis of scaling w.r.t. spectral gap or network size except for a single figure on complete graphs. No wall-clock or bandwidth accounting is provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but notation-heavy. The phantom-node construction is clearly described conceptually. However:
- The norm used for matrix quantities is not specified.
- Algorithm 2’s presentation is terse; the dependence on knowing |E| should be emphasized as a requirement/limitation.
- Several details are deferred to the supplement (all proofs, additional baselines). Given the importance of guarantees, summarizing at least one key proof idea and one high-level lemma in the main text would help.

Originality
The problem is not entirely new (cf. [17]). The main novelty lies in a simpler algorithmic design (single auxiliary observation and averaging), an asynchronous variant, and a cleaner analysis with spectral-gap-dependent constants. The phantom network trick is a useful analytical reformulation but not conceptually groundbreaking. Overall, this feels like a solid incremental contribution over [17] rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.

Significance
Estimating U-statistics in decentralized settings is relevant (AUC, scatter, rank correlations). However, practical impact is tempered by:
- Guarantees only in expectation (no finite-sample concentration).
- Dependence on global knowledge (|E|) in the asynchronous algorithm.
- Potentially slow constants on sparse graphs: since 1 − λ2(2) = β2(L)/|E|, on many sparse topologies this scales poorly with n, and the paper does not systematically quantify this empirically.
That said, the algorithms are simple and could be used as building blocks in broader decentralized tasks.

Specific strengths
- Clear identification of a gap in gossip literature for pairwise U-statistics.
- Simple algorithms with reduced per-iteration memory/communication compared to U2-gossip.
- Asynchronous variant with a convergence-rate guarantee.
- Rates with interpretable data- and graph-dependent constants.
- Empirical improvements over [17] across several networks/datasets.

Specific weaknesses and questions
- Only expectation convergence; no MSE/high-probability/AS bounds. Can the authors provide variance bounds or martingale concentration?
- What norm is used for matrix terms in Theorems 1–2? Are elements of H assumed bounded? If not, how do constants depend on tails?
- Asynchronous algorithm requires |E| (global). Can pk be estimated locally or adaptively without bias/variance blow-up?
- No experiments reporting error vs communication (bytes/messages) or wall-clock time; only iterations are shown.
- Limited baselines. How does GoSta compare to simple Monte Carlo estimators where nodes sample a small number of pairs via random walks/tokens?
- Scalability on sparse graphs: empirical study of how the error scales with spectral gap (e.g., varying WS rewiring, k) is missing.
- The synchronous algorithm updates all nodes every iteration using possibly unchanged Yk, which may induce high correlation; any discussion on variance or alternatives (e.g., mini-batch swaps)?

Pros
- Addresses a relevant decentralized estimation problem.
- Conceptually simple algorithms; asynchronous variant.
- Theoretical rates with spectral-gap dependence.
- Improved memory/communication vs U2-gossip.
- Empirical gains over a known baseline.

Cons
- Incremental over [17].
- Guarantees only in expectation; no concentration.
- Requires global knowledge (|E|) in async.
- Limited experimental scope and metrics; no communication/time analysis.
- Some clarity gaps (norms, assumptions).

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the paper is technically competent and addresses a meaningful problem with simple algorithms and improved analysis over [17], the contribution feels incremental and the empirical validation is not sufficiently thorough for NeurIPS standards. Strengthening with high-probability/MSE bounds, removing global knowledge assumptions (or proposing practical estimators), and adding comprehensive experiments (including communication budgets, more baselines, and scaling vs spectral gap) would significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: 0.7
