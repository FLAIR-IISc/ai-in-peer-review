Summary
This paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network via gossip. The authors propose two algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node i maintains a local estimate Zi and a single auxiliary observation Yi that performs a random walk via edge swaps. At each iteration, nodes update Zi using a running average of H(Xi, Yi); in addition, the selected edge’s endpoints average their Zi’s (classic gossip step). The authors introduce a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple analysis of estimate averaging and data propagation. They prove convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic, with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t / t) (async). Rates depend on data dispersion terms and the graph spectral gap (via 1 − λ2 of a weighted Laplacian). Compared to the only prior decentralized U-statistic method (U2-gossip), GoSta uses half the memory, half the communication per iteration, and enjoys better rates. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across several graph topologies support the theory.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on randomized gossip for averaging (e.g., Boyd et al. 2006) and explicitly targets non-separable pairwise quantities (U-statistics), a gap in the gossip literature.
- Improves on U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens 2010) by adding averaging of local estimates and reducing auxiliary state to one sample per node. The paper also derives a refined bound for U2-gossip.
- Connects rates to spectral gap as in standard gossip, highlighting network effects.

Quality
- Theory: The expected convergence guarantees are clearly stated. The O(1/t) synchronous and O(log t / t) asynchronous rates are plausible given the need to propagate data and average estimates. The dependence on the spectral gap is well-motivated. The “phantom network” analysis is technically neat and seems sound at a high level. Proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main text provides enough structure (block matrices, Kronecker product form) to assess plausibility.
- Assumptions: Connected, non-bipartite graphs; symmetric H with H(x,x)=0. The bounds rely on norms of H and h; boundedness assumptions for H are implicit—this should be stated for the norm-based rates to be meaningful.
- Completeness: Guarantees are in expectation only; no almost-sure or high-probability bounds are given. Asynchronous algorithm requires knowledge of pk=2dk/|E|; the paper notes this but does not analyze robustness to estimation error or unknown |E|.
- Experiments: Compare fairly against U2-gossip on two U-statistics (AUC, within-cluster scatter) across multiple graphs. Plots show lower error and variance for GoSta; scaling with n is explored. However, results are shown versus iterations; a comparison normalized by total messages or bytes communicated would better reflect communication-efficiency claims. Datasets are moderate-sized; still sufficient to illustrate trends.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-structured. Algorithms are easy to follow; the phantom-node construction is explained with sufficient intuition. Some minor notational issues (e.g., the block “B” matrix description and a few formatting glitches) could be polished. The relationship between Wα(G), L(G), and spectral gap is explained. The comparison to U2-gossip is explicit and helpful.

Originality
- Addresses an underexplored problem: gossip-style computation of U-statistics. The core algorithmic idea (combining data propagation via a single auxiliary sample with averaging of estimates) and the phantom-node analysis technique are, to my knowledge, novel in this context. The refined bound for U2-gossip is also a useful contribution.

Significance
- U-statistics underpin many important measures (AUC, Kendall’s tau, scatter, rank-based tests). Enabling their decentralized estimation with provable rates and low per-iteration overhead is practically valuable in sensor and P2P networks. The spectral-gap dependence aligns with established theory and gives actionable insight on topology effects. I expect others working on decentralized learning/monitoring will adopt or extend these methods.

Pros
- Clear, simple algorithms with low memory/communication per iteration.
- Tight, interpretable expected-rate guarantees with data- and graph-dependent constants.
- Novel analysis via phantom nodes that isolates data propagation from averaging.
- Empirical validation across tasks and topologies; improved accuracy and variance versus prior art.
- Asynchronous variant without global clock.

Cons
- Only expectation guarantees; no high-probability or almost-sure convergence, and no variance decay bounds.
- Asynchronous method assumes pk known (requires dk and |E|); robustness to mis-specification is not analyzed.
- Non-bipartite assumption; a lazy-gossip tweak to remove this limitation is not discussed.
- Experimental comparisons are iteration-based; communication- or time-normalized comparisons would strengthen the case.
- Extensions to multiple observations per node are mentioned but not analyzed in detail.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide high-probability bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration) or at least variance bounds for E[∥Z(t)−Û1∥^2]?
- Discuss a lazy version to handle bipartite graphs, and weighted/Metropolis-Hastings edge selection.
- Comment on boundedness assumptions for H and the norms in the bounds; if H is unbounded, what moment conditions are needed?
- For GoSta-async, can pk be estimated online (e.g., via local degree only) and how sensitive is convergence to pk errors?
- Include communication-cost-normalized plots (messages or bytes) to substantiate the claimed efficiency gains.
- Clarify the block matrix B in M2(t) and fix minor formatting issues.

Recommendation
- Overall: Accept (7/10). The paper is technically sound, addresses a relevant gap with novel ideas, offers clean theory with meaningful constants, and shows convincing empirical improvements. With minor clarifications and an added communication-normalized comparison, it would be even stronger.

Confidence
- High.
