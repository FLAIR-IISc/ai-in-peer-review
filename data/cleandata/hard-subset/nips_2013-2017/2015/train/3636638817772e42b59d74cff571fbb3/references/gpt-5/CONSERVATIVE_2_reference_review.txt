Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. The key idea is to combine two mechanisms at each iteration: (i) data propagation by swapping an auxiliary observation along a randomly selected edge, and (ii) averaging of local estimates across the same edge. Two algorithms are proposed: a synchronous version (GoSta-sync) and an asynchronous version (GoSta-async). The analysis introduces a “phantom graph” construction to decouple propagation and averaging, leading to expected convergence rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants depending on data dispersion and the spectral gap of the network. The authors compare primarily against U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, 2009], arguing improved convergence, memory, and communication, and provide experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter across several graphs.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on the rich literature on randomized gossip for averaging and aggregation (e.g., Boyd et al., Kempe et al., Dimakis et al.). Prior work achieves exponential convergence for separable functions. For non-separable pairwise quantities, the closest prior is U2-gossip, which propagates two observations without averaging. This paper’s novelty is to interleave averaging with single-token propagation and to analyze it using a phantom-node model. The asynchronous model follows the standard Poisson-clock framework.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Estimating pairwise statistics in decentralized settings is important (variance, AUC, Kendall tau, within-cluster scatter).
- Algorithmic simplicity: Single auxiliary observation per node and one edge activation per iteration lowers communication/memory relative to U2-gossip.
- Theoretical guarantees: Convergence in expectation with explicit network dependence via the spectral gap; the matrix/Laplacian-based analysis is clean. The asynchronous variant is appreciated in practice.
- Empirical results: On two datasets and several network topologies, GoSta-sync outperforms U2-gossip in error and variance across nodes; GoSta-async appears competitive.

Weaknesses
- Guarantees only in expectation: Unlike classical gossip for averaging (which often provides mean-square or a.s. convergence and high-probability finite-time bounds), here the results are in expectation. No concentration/variance bounds over runs are provided, and there is no characterization of node-to-node disagreement beyond qualitative statements. This weakens claims of robustness.
- Rate suboptimality vs averaging: For separable averaging, gossip converges exponentially. Here, the dominant term is O(1/t). While pairwise coupling likely limits achievable rates, the paper does not discuss lower bounds or whether faster rates (e.g., through variance reduction, multi-token schemes with averaging, or better step-size policies) are possible.
- Data dependence and scaling: Bounds use norms of H and h but do not carefully normalize by n, making constants potentially scale poorly with network size. For example, c(G)=1−λ2(2)≈1/n on complete graphs, so the 1/(ct) term yields a factor n/t; combined with ||H|| scaling, finite-time guarantees may become loose for large n.
- Asynchronous practicality: GoSta-async requires pk=2dk/|E| and uses m_k increments by 1/pk; nodes therefore need |E|. This global information is nontrivial to obtain in realistic distributed settings. The paper does not discuss how to estimate |E| online or robustness when pk is misspecified.
- Modeling assumptions: Uniform random edge activation and lossless, instantaneous communication are strong assumptions. No discussion of delays, message loss, or time-varying graphs.
- Empirical validation is limited: Only two tasks, modest datasets, and limited exploration of dimension d (despite claims of improved memory/communication in high d). No wall-clock message complexity, communication volume, or energy metrics; only iteration-based error. Comparisons beyond U2-gossip (e.g., subsampling-based decentralized approximations to U-statistics, sketching, or push-sum-style variants) are relegated to the supplement. The asynchronous algorithm gets minimal empirical coverage.
- AUC formulation: The standard AUC denominator depends on global class counts. In a decentralized setting, it is unclear how nodes obtain or track these counts; this is not addressed explicitly. Similarly, the mapping of AUC to the U-statistic form should be clarified in decentralized normalization.
- Scope: Extension to higher-order U-statistics (order >2) is not discussed, and the multi-observation-per-node extension is only sketched without formal results.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of expectation-based convergence with reasonable spectral-gap dependence. However, the lack of high-probability or mean-square rates, sensitivity to global information (|E|), and limited experimental rigor reduce confidence in practical utility.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The phantom-node analysis is clear. Some notational overload (H as kernel and matrix) could be clarified. Several important details (proofs, exact U2-gossip statement, additional baselines) are deferred to the supplement. Minor typos present.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of interleaving averaging with single-token propagation and the phantom graph analysis are novel relative to U2-gossip. However, the general framework follows established randomized gossip paradigms.

Significance
Potentially useful, but current evidence is limited. Without stronger guarantees and broader empirical validation (including practical constraints), it is uncertain how widely this will be adopted.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide high-probability or mean-square convergence bounds and bounds on the disagreement across nodes?
2) How sensitive is GoSta-async to misspecification of pk and unknown |E|? Can pk be estimated online?
3) Can you report communication/message complexity to reach ε-accuracy and compare against U2-gossip and simple subsampling baselines?
4) How does performance scale with d and n in practice (beyond iteration counts)?
5) Can you clarify precisely how AUC normalization is handled in the decentralized setting?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant non-separable aggregation problem.
- Simple algorithms with clean spectral-gap-based analysis.
- Asynchronous variant and better empirical performance than U2-gossip.

Cons for acceptance
- Only expectation-based convergence; no concentration or robustness results.
- Dependence on global graph quantities in the asynchronous method.
- Limited and narrow empirical evaluation; no communication/time measurements.
- Unclear handling of AUC normalization and broader practical issues.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is worthwhile and the approach is interesting, but the theoretical guarantees are relatively weak compared to established gossip results, and the empirical validation is too limited to justify publication without stronger evidence and clearer practical considerations.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
