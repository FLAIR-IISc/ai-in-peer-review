### Summary and Relation to Prior Work
This paper addresses the problem of computing degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip algorithms excel at computing separable functions, such as averages, but cannot handle U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of data points distributed across the network. This work proposes novel synchronous (GoSta-sync) and asynchronous (GoSta-async) algorithms to tackle this important problem.

The core idea is a clever combination of two concurrent processes: 1) a data propagation mechanism, where each node maintains an "auxiliary" data observation that performs a random walk on the network via swaps with neighbors, and 2) a standard estimate averaging mechanism, where nodes average their local U-statistic estimates with neighbors. This dual process allows each node to gradually compute its local contribution to the U-statistic (by pairing its own static data point with the traveling auxiliary points) while simultaneously reaching a global consensus on the final value.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing convergence in expectation for both algorithms, with rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous one. A key feature of their analysis is an original reformulation of the problem using a "phantom network," which is a novel and insightful tool for decoupling the data propagation and averaging steps. This work builds upon and significantly improves the only known prior work in this specific area, U2-gossip [17], by offering faster convergence, lower memory and communication costs, and an asynchronous variant.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and largely unexplored problem in the gossip literature. Extending gossip methods beyond simple averages to the broad and important class of U-statistics (which includes AUC, sample variance, and various rank statistics) is a significant conceptual leap. This work opens up a new and promising direction for decentralized computation and machine learning, with potential applications in large-scale sensor networks and federated learning.

2.  **Elegant and Bold Approach:** The central idea of propagating data points via random walks (swaps) while simultaneously averaging local estimates is elegant and powerful. It cleanly addresses the core challenge of U-statistics—the need for pairwise interactions—within the minimal communication constraints of the gossip framework. This is a non-trivial and creative solution to a difficult problem.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The convergence analysis is a major strength. The introduction of the "phantom network" construction as an analytical tool is particularly impressive and novel. It provides a principled way to analyze the complex interplay between the data propagation and consensus dynamics. The resulting convergence bounds, which explicitly depend on the network's spectral gap and data-dependent terms, provide valuable insight.

4.  **Substantial Improvement Over State-of-the-Art:** This is not an incremental paper. It identifies the significant drawbacks of the only existing competitor, U2-gossip [17], and provides a new approach that is superior on multiple fronts: it requires less memory and communication per iteration, achieves a faster theoretical convergence rate (as shown by their refined analysis of U2-gossip), and, crucially, offers a practical asynchronous version, which is often a necessity in real-world decentralized systems. The experimental results convincingly support these claims.

### Weaknesses
The weaknesses of this paper are minor, particularly when weighed against its significant contributions.

1.  **Sub-linear Convergence Rate:** The O(1/t) and O(log t/t) rates are slower than the exponential rates achievable for standard gossip averaging. While this is almost certainly an inherent feature of the much harder problem of estimating U-statistics, a brief discussion contextualizing this rate and exploring whether it is fundamental to the problem class would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Discrepancy between Asynchronous Theory and Practice:** The experiments in Figure 3b show the asynchronous algorithm performing on par with the synchronous one, which is an encouraging practical result. However, this seems to contradict the slightly weaker theoretical bound of O(log t/t). A short comment on this observation—perhaps noting that the bound may not be tight or that the log factor is negligible in practice—would be helpful.

3.  **Extension to Multiple Observations:** The paper notes in Remark 2 that the method can be extended to the setting where nodes hold multiple observations. This is an important practical scenario, but the description of the extension is very brief. While a full analysis is likely beyond the scope of this paper, a slightly more detailed explanation would have been beneficial.

### Recommendation
This paper presents a novel, elegant, and significant contribution to the field of decentralized computation. It extends the powerful gossip algorithm framework to the important class of U-statistics, a problem that had been barely explored. The proposed method is a clear and substantial improvement over prior work, supported by a solid theoretical analysis that introduces its own novel techniques.

The work's strengths—its novelty, ambition, and the quality of its core idea—are precisely what the NIPS community should value. It introduces a new direction that others are likely to build upon. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall impact of the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a new and important problem for decentralized learning.
*   The core idea is simple, novel, and highly effective.
*   The theoretical analysis is strong and introduces a creative proof technique.
*   Represents a significant, non-incremental advance over the state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The convergence rates are sub-linear (though this is likely inherent to the problem).
*   Some practical extensions (e.g., multiple observations per node) are not fully detailed.

The arguments for acceptance are far more compelling. This is an excellent paper.

**Final Decision: Accept**
