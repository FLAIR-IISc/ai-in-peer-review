Summary
The paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip. While classical gossip efficiently averages separable functions, many useful statistics (variance, AUC, Kendall’s tau, within-cluster scatter, Wilcoxon–Mann–Whitney) are pairwise and thus non-separable. The authors propose two randomized algorithms: a synchronous variant (GoSta-sync) and an asynchronous variant (GoSta-async). Each node maintains a local estimate and a single auxiliary observation that performs a random walk via pairwise swaps. At each step, nodes update estimates by incorporating H(Xi, Yi) and use local averaging on the activated edge. The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” construction to decouple data propagation from estimate averaging, leading to convergence in expectation at rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t / t) (asynchronous). Bounds depend on data dispersion terms and on the network via the spectral gap (through λ2 of a weighted averaging operator). The method improves on U2-gossip [17] by requiring fewer auxiliary observations/communications and by achieving tighter rates. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over complete, Watts–Strogatz, and grid graphs support the theory and show lower error and variance than U2-gossip.

Relation to prior work
This work sits at the intersection of randomized gossip for distributed averaging [2, 10, 15, 18] and pairwise/U-statistics in ML [12, 4, 14]. It directly extends U2-gossip [17] by combining propagation of a single auxiliary observation with consensus averaging, and it connects rates to spectral properties as in classical gossip [3]. The “phantom network” analysis resembles lifted-state analyses and product-graph/Kronecker constructions common in consensus analysis, though the specific block-structured decomposition is tailored to the pairwise-propagation + averaging setting.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: U-statistics are pervasive in ML (ranking, clustering, nonparametric testing), and decentralized estimation is timely for sensor/edge networks.
- Algorithmic simplicity and practicality: only one auxiliary observation per node; a single edge activation per step; asynchronous variant compatible with local clocks.
- Theoretical guarantees: clear convergence-in-expectation rates with explicit dependence on spectral gap and data dispersion; insightful decomposition showing the impact of averaging (exponential transient) versus pairwise propagation (1/t tail).
- Comparison to prior art: a refined bound for U2-gossip clarifies where it loses (√n factor, no averaging), matching empirical observations; reduced memory and communication per step.
- Empirical validation: across networks with varying connectivity; consistent improvements in both mean error and across-node variance; useful scaling plots.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Only expectation guarantees are provided. High-probability or mean-square error bounds (or bounds on across-run variance) would strengthen the theory and better match practice.
- Assumptions in the theorems require connected and non-bipartite graphs, yet the experiments include 2D grids, which are bipartite. Please clarify how theory applies in that case (e.g., add laziness/self-loops or show that your Wα operator remains contractive on bipartite graphs).
- Implementation details for asynchronous selection: the analysis assumes uniform edge sampling and requires each node to know pk = 2dk/|E| and to maintain mk increments by 1/pk. In practical decentralized settings, |E| may be unknown and edge sampling is induced by local wake-ups. Please discuss how to implement or estimate pk locally, and robustness to inexact pk.
- The data-dependent terms in the bounds (||h − Û1||, ||H − h1^T||) are not bounded or interpreted. It would help to provide intuition/ranges for common H (e.g., AUC, within-cluster scatter) or normalize to make constants comparable across problems.

Clarity
- The phantom-node construction is elegant but dense. A small running example and a more intuitive explanation of M1/M2/M3 and why the bottom-left block vanishes would improve readability.
- Minor presentation issues: some footnotes and references appear inline; define all norms precisely; ensure all symbols in Algorithm 2 are defined where used.
- Please reconcile the “non-bipartite” requirement with the grid experiments in the main text.

Originality
- Extending gossip to pairwise U-statistics with a single auxiliary observation and consensus averaging appears novel relative to [17]. The analysis via a coupled lifted system is also original to this context. You might cite related “lifted” or product-graph analyses in consensus if applicable.

Significance
- The contribution is practically useful for decentralized evaluation of widely-used metrics and may serve as a building block for decentralized learning with pairwise losses. That said, the empirical section is moderate in scope; adding:
  - results on larger graphs and richer topologies (e.g., scale-free, random geometric),
  - more thorough asynchronous experiments,
  - communication-to-accuracy plots (total bytes or edge activations to reach ε),
  - robustness to message loss and delays,
would strengthen the case for impact.

Additional suggestions
- Consider presenting mean-square or high-probability bounds using martingale or mixing arguments on the coupled random walks.
- Address bipartiteness by using a lazy update (e.g., averaging with probability < 1 or adding self-loops) and reflect this in the analysis.
- Discuss possible accelerations (Chebyshev-accelerated gossip, multi-consensus between propagation steps) and whether they can improve constants or the tail rate.
- Clarify how global quantities in certain U-statistics (e.g., counts in AUC’s denominator) are handled; if estimated via gossip, quantify the induced bias/variance.
- Provide code or detailed reproducibility settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful gap: gossip for non-separable pairwise statistics.
- Simple, memory/communication-light algorithms.
- Theoretical rates with interpretable dependence on spectral gap and data.
- Clear improvement over prior U2-gossip in both theory and experiments.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees only in expectation; lack of high-probability/MSE analysis.
- Bipartite-graph assumption vs. experiments mismatch needs resolution.
- Limited empirical breadth (asynchronous evaluation, larger networks, communication-efficiency).
- Practical aspects (unknown |E|, pk estimation) not fully addressed.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a clean and useful extension of gossip to U-statistics with solid analysis and convincing improvements over the closest prior work. Addressing the bipartite-graph issue, adding at least partial stronger concentration results, and clarifying practical implementation details would make the contribution stronger.
