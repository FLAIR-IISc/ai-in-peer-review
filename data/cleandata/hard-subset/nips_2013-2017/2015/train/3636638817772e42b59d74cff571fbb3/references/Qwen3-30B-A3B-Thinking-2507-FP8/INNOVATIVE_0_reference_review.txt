

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper addresses a significant gap in distributed computation literature: the inability of existing gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics, which depend on pairwise interactions between data points. The authors propose a novel approach called GoSta (Gossip for U-statistics) that extends gossip algorithms to handle these more complex statistics. This is a bold and important contribution, as U-statistics are fundamental in machine learning (e.g., AUC, sample variance, Gini mean difference) but have been largely overlooked in distributed settings.

The paper's core innovation is the introduction of "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem, enabling a clean convergence analysis. This technique allows the authors to decouple data propagation from estimation, providing elegant theoretical guarantees. The synchronous algorithm achieves O(1/t) convergence while the asynchronous version reaches O(log t/t), both with data-dependent and network-dependent terms that reflect problem hardness and connectivity. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of convergence rates that build upon existing gossip algorithm literature.

The paper's significance is substantial. Prior work (U2-gossip) struggled with high memory and communication costs for high-dimensional data and lacked asynchronous support. The authors convincingly demonstrate that GoSta improves upon these limitations by requiring only a single auxiliary observation per node (vs. two in U2-gossip) and enabling true asynchronous operation. The experiments on real-world datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) confirm the theoretical advantages, showing superior performance across different network topologies.

What makes this paper particularly compelling is its clear identification of a meaningful gap in the literature. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for separable statistics (e.g., averages), U-statistics represent a fundamental class of non-separable statistics that have been neglected. The authors' solution is not merely incremental—it fundamentally extends the applicability of gossip algorithms to a broader class of problems.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a clear problem statement, thorough theoretical development, and strong experimental validation. The "phantom nodes" approach is a clever technical contribution that could inspire similar reformulations in other distributed optimization settings. The authors properly contextualize their work against existing gossip algorithms and U2-gossip, making it clear how their approach advances the field.

I note a few minor weaknesses: the experimental section could include additional comparisons with alternative baselines, and the phantom node concept is somewhat abstract for readers. However, these are relatively minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's core contribution.

In terms of novelty, this paper makes a clear and significant contribution. It identifies a previously overlooked problem (distributed U-statistics estimation), proposes a novel solution with a clever analytical framework, and demonstrates its superiority through both theory and experiments. This represents a meaningful extension of gossip algorithm literature rather than an incremental improvement.

Compared to recent NIPS papers on gossip algorithms (e.g., [10, 2, 15]), this work moves beyond the standard average computation problem to address a more complex and practically important class of statistics. The authors have successfully bridged a gap that has been overlooked in the distributed computing literature.

The paper advances the state of the art in distributed estimation by expanding the scope of problems solvable with gossip algorithms. The authors' theoretical insights and practical algorithms open up new possibilities for distributed machine learning applications, particularly those involving pairwise statistics like AUC, which is crucial in many classification tasks.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The identification of a meaningful gap, the novel solution using phantom nodes, and the clear demonstration of advantages over prior work make this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

**Strengths:**
- Clear identification of a meaningful gap in distributed computation
- Novel "phantom nodes" approach for theoretical analysis
- Rigorous convergence guarantees with meaningful rates
- Practical improvements in memory/communication costs over prior work
- Strong experimental validation on real datasets

**Weaknesses:**
- Minor limitations in experimental comparisons with additional baselines
- Phantom node concept could be more intuitively explained

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant and novel contribution to distributed computation by extending gossip algorithms to handle U-statistics, a class of statistics previously considered intractable with gossip approaches. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the work opens up new directions for distributed machine learning.
