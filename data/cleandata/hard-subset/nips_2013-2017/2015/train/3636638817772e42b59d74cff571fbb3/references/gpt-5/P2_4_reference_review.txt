Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network using gossip-style communication. The key idea is to decompose the target U-statistic Ûn(H) into an average over per-node quantities hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi, Xj), and to interleave two mechanisms: (i) propagation of auxiliary data via random walks so each node can form an online average of H(Xi, Yk), and (ii) standard pairwise averaging of local estimates to reach consensus. The authors propose (a) a synchronous algorithm (GoSta-sync) that swaps a single auxiliary observation and averages a randomly selected pair each iteration, and (b) a fully asynchronous variant (GoSta-async) that uses local counters scaled by node-dependent activation probabilities to maintain an unbiased running average without a global clock.

For analysis, they introduce a “phantom nodes” construction that embeds data propagation and averaging into a larger block-triangular linear system, enabling clean expectation dynamics and rates. They prove convergence in expectation with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O((log t)/t) (async), with constants that separate data difficulty terms (norms of H and its row averages) and network connectivity via the spectral gap. Compared to the prior U2-gossip method (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2010), the proposed algorithms reduce memory/communication (only one auxiliary observation, one edge per iteration), provide faster convergence (no √n factor), and include an asynchronous version. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over three graph families support these claims.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The synchronous analysis is carefully derived using the block-triangular transition and spectral properties of Wα(G). The expectation bounds and dependence on the spectral gap are standard and credible. The asynchronous design that increments a local counter by 1/pk upon activation, yielding expected step size ≈1/t, is clever and plausible. Proofs are in the supplement; from the main text, the arguments appear consistent.
- Scope and assumptions: Convergence is in expectation; there are no almost-sure or high-probability guarantees, nor mean-square error bounds. The analysis requires connected, non-bipartite undirected graphs. It would be straightforward to handle bipartite graphs via a lazy step, but this is not discussed. The asynchronous analysis assumes knowledge of pk = 2dk/|E| (i.e., degrees and |E|), which may not always be available; practical schemes to estimate pk online are not treated. Communication model toggles between “random edge draws” and “Poisson clocks with random neighbor selection”; while equivalent in distribution, the mapping could be explained more explicitly.
- Claims vs. evidence: The empirical results match the theory: GoSta outperforms U2-gossip across graph types and tasks, with the gap widening on larger networks. However, experiments are modest in scale (n up to ~1600) and focus on two applications; no ablation on data difficulty terms or spectral gap is provided, and no wall-clock/communication complexity to ε-accuracy is reported. Still, the experimental section is sufficient to validate main claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The phantom-node construction is well-motivated and useful. Algorithms are explicit. Minor typos exist, and several key technical details and proofs are deferred to the supplement (acceptable for NeurIPS but some brief intuition for the async log t/t rate would help). The relation between λ2(1) and λ2(2) and the interpretation via the Laplacian spectral gap are appropriately explained.

Originality
- The problem—gossip computation of pairwise U-statistics—has been explored only in U2-gossip. This work introduces a different algorithmic design (single auxiliary random walk plus consensus averaging) and a novel analysis tool (phantom nodes) that yields improved rates and costs. The asynchronous construction with unbiased local counters is also a meaningful contribution. Overall, this is a clear advance over the closest prior art.

Significance
- U-statistics include many practically relevant quantities (variance-like measures, AUC, rank correlations), so being able to estimate them efficiently in decentralized settings is useful. The improved convergence and reduced resource footprint over U2-gossip are meaningful. While the current results are in expectation and for degree-2 U-statistics, the techniques could inspire extensions (higher-order U, optimization over U, robustness). The paper is likely to be of interest to the distributed learning/optimization and networks communities.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, implementable algorithms with lower memory/communication than U2-gossip.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with interpretable data/network constants; improved rates over prior work.
- Asynchronous algorithm without global clock and with unbiased estimates.
- Experiments on real datasets and multiple graphs corroborating theory.

Cons
- Convergence only in expectation; no high-probability or MSE bounds and no ε-accuracy communication complexity.
- Assumes non-bipartite, undirected graphs and knowledge of |E|, dk for async; no discussion of practical remedies (e.g., lazy updates, online pk estimates).
- Experimental scale and breadth are limited; no analysis of robustness to skewed label distributions (AUC) or heavy-tailed H.
- Focuses on degree-2 U-statistics; path to higher-order U-statistics is not developed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide mean-square or high-probability bounds and corresponding sample/communication complexity to reach ε error.
- Discuss and/or implement standard tricks for bipartite/directed graphs (lazy gossip, push-sum) and the impact on rates.
- Show how to estimate pk online and analyze the resulting bias/variance.
- Add experiments that vary spectral gap systematically and report communication per node to ε-accuracy.
- Comment on extensions to higher-order U-statistics and to privacy-preserving settings.

Overall evaluation: Weak accept. The paper addresses a relevant problem with a simple and effective algorithmic idea, improved theory over prior work, and supportive experiments. The main weaknesses (expectation-only guarantees and limited empirical breadth) are addressable and do not undermine the contribution. Confidence: high.
