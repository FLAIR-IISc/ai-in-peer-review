### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of computing degree-two U-statistics in a decentralized network using gossip algorithms. Standard gossip methods are designed for separable sums (like averages) and cannot efficiently handle U-statistics, which are averages over pairs of data points distributed across the network. The authors propose two novel algorithms, GoSta-sync and GoSta-async, for the synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively. The core idea is to combine two processes: 1) a data propagation mechanism where each node's local data point performs a random walk on the network via swaps with neighbors, and 2) a standard gossip averaging step where nodes average their local estimates of the U-statistic.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of their algorithms, introducing a novel "phantom network" construction as an analytical tool. They prove that the local estimates converge in expectation to the true U-statistic at a rate of O(1/t) for the synchronous version and O(log t / t) for the asynchronous one. These convergence bounds explicitly depend on the network's connectivity (via the spectral gap) and data-dependent variance terms. The proposed methods are shown to improve significantly upon the only known prior work in this area ([17]), offering lower memory and communication costs, faster convergence, and a practical asynchronous variant. These claims are substantiated through experiments on real-world datasets for estimating the AUC and within-cluster point scatter.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical and theoretical importance. U-statistics are fundamental quantities in machine learning and statistics (e.g., AUC, variance, ranking metrics), and developing efficient, scalable methods for their computation in decentralized settings is a valuable contribution. The problem is well-motivated and highly relevant to the NeurIPS community.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The proposed approach is elegant and novel. The combination of propagating data via random walks (swaps) and averaging local estimates is a clever way to handle the pairwise nature of U-statistics within the gossip framework. The theoretical analysis technique, based on a "phantom network" reformulation, is a key technical innovation that could potentially be applied to analyze other complex distributed algorithms. This goes well beyond a simple combination of existing techniques.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The authors provide rigorous convergence proofs for both synchronous and asynchronous algorithms. The theoretical bounds are insightful, clearly connecting the convergence rate to both the network topology (spectral gap) and the statistical properties of the data. The refined analysis of the competing method from [17] is also a valuable contribution, providing a clear theoretical justification for why the proposed GoSta algorithm should be superior.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem is precisely defined, the algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode, and the main theoretical results are stated upfront. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate choices of network topologies and tasks, and the results are presented effectively to support the paper's claims.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is very strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved.

1.  **Clarity of the Analysis:** The "phantom network" construction is a powerful tool but is introduced very densely in Section 4.1. While the full proof is rightly deferred to the supplementary material, the main text could benefit from a bit more intuition. For instance, a sentence explaining that the upper-triangular structure of the transition matrix `M(t)` arises because the estimate vector `Z(t)` is influenced by the data propagation, but not vice-versa, would be helpful. Similarly, providing a more intuitive description of what the block matrices `B` and `C` represent would aid the reader's understanding.

2.  **Practicality of Assumptions in Asynchronous Setting:** The asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) requires each node `k` to know its degree `d_k` and the total number of edges `|E|` to compute its selection probability `p_k`. While this is a common assumption in the literature, it may not hold in all practical decentralized settings. A brief discussion on the robustness of the algorithm to inaccuracies in these values or potential strategies for estimating them (e.g., via local averaging) would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

3.  **Theoretical Gap between Synchronous and Asynchronous Rates:** The theoretical rate for GoSta-async (O(log t / t)) is slightly worse than for GoSta-sync (O(1/t)). However, the experiments in Figure 3b show their performance to be nearly identical. It would be beneficial for the authors to comment on this discrepancy. Is the `log t` factor a loose artifact of the proof technique, or is there a deeper reason? Could the asynchronous update rule be modified to close this theoretical gap?

4.  **Baselines in Experiments:** The primary comparison is against U2-gossip [17], which is the most relevant prior work. However, to provide a more complete picture, a comparison against a simple, albeit potentially inefficient, baseline could be useful. For example, a "flooding-based" approach where each node broadcasts its observation `X_i` to the entire network, after which each node computes the U-statistic locally. While this would have high communication overhead, it would provide a useful reference in terms of convergence time (i.e., time-to-accuracy). The paper mentions supplementary comparisons, but a brief mention in the main text would be valuable.

### **Decision**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on decentralized algorithms. It provides a novel, efficient, and well-analyzed solution to the important problem of computing U-statistics in a distributed manner. The work is technically strong, clearly presented, and convincingly validated. The identified weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for improving clarity and expanding the discussion, which can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
