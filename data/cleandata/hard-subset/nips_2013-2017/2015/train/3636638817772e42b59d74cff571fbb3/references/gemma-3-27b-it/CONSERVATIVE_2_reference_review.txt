This paper proposes randomized gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a problem not well-addressed by existing gossip protocols. The core idea of propagating observations and averaging local estimates using a “phantom node” reformulation is interesting. The authors provide convergence analysis and experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter.

However, while the problem statement is well-motivated and the proposed approach appears reasonable, I find the paper lacking in several key areas, leading to a negative assessment. My primary concern is the limited novelty and insufficient justification for the complexity introduced. Existing gossip algorithms effectively handle separable functions, and the extension to U-statistics, while non-trivial, doesn’t represent a paradigm shift. The “phantom node” reformulation feels like a complex workaround rather than an elegant solution, and its necessity isn’t convincingly demonstrated.

The convergence analysis, while present, lacks crucial details in the supplementary material (referred to repeatedly but not provided). Without access to proofs, assessing the tightness of the bounds and the validity of assumptions is impossible. The claim of “faster convergence” compared to [17] is stated without a thorough comparison that isn’t simply visual inspection of plots – a quantitative comparison is needed, and the authors should provide a theoretical justification. Furthermore, the reported convergence rate of O(log t/t) for the asynchronous version is surprisingly slow given the existing literature on asynchronous gossip, and I’m skeptical without more detailed analysis.

The experiments, while demonstrating some improvement over U2-gossip, are limited in scope. The choice of datasets is reasonable, but the network topologies are simplified. Real-world networks are rarely complete graphs or simple grids, and the performance on more realistic topologies (e.g., scale-free networks) is not shown. The comparison to U2-gossip is appreciated, but a comparison to more standard distributed estimation techniques (even if not gossip-based) would provide better context. The claim of comparable performance between synchronous and asynchronous variants requires further scrutiny, as asynchronous algorithms generally have a price to pay in convergence speed. Finally, the discussion of practical implications is weak.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important problem: distributed estimation of U-statistics.
*   Proposes a novel approach based on propagating observations and averaging estimates.
*   Provides convergence analysis (though inaccessible without the supplementary material).
*   Demonstrates some improvement over a specific baseline (U2-gossip) in limited experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty: the extension of gossip algorithms to U-statistics is incremental.
*   Complex "phantom node" reformulation without sufficient justification.
*   Incomplete convergence analysis (supplementary material missing).
*   Weak experimental evaluation: limited network topologies, lack of comparison to standard techniques.
*   Slow convergence rate for the asynchronous version.
*   Insufficient discussion of practical implications.
*   The improvement over U2-gossip appears marginal in the provided figures.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is relevant to decentralized machine learning and data analysis.
*   The approach is technically sound, *if* the analysis in the supplementary material is correct.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty is limited, and the complexity of the approach isn’t justified.
*   The convergence analysis is inaccessible for review.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the claims of superiority.
*   The slow convergence rate of the asynchronous version is concerning.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. The paper presents a reasonable but ultimately incremental contribution. The lack of a complete and accessible convergence analysis and the limited experimental evaluation prevent a positive recommendation. The authors need to substantially strengthen the justification for the complexity of their approach, provide a more thorough evaluation, and make the full details of their analysis available for review. I am unconvinced that this work represents a significant advance in the field of distributed estimation.
