Summary
The paper tackles decentralized computation of degree-2 U-statistics over a network where each node holds one sample. The authors propose two randomized gossip algorithms: a synchronous variant (GoSta-sync) and a fully asynchronous one (GoSta-async). The key idea is to blend (i) propagation of data via swapping auxiliary observations along randomly selected edges (making observations execute random walks), with (ii) averaging of local estimates. A novel “phantom nodes” construction decouples averaging and data propagation in the analysis, yielding convergence in expectation to the target U-statistic. For GoSta-sync the bound is O(1/t) with explicit data-dependent terms and dependence on the spectral gap 1−λ2(W2(G)); for GoSta-async the bound is O(log t/t). The paper refines the analysis of the prior U2-gossip algorithm (Pelckmans & Suykens, 2009), showing a bound with an extra √n factor and arguing inferior practical performance. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs support the claims.

Relation to prior work
Gossip/consensus for separable functions is well-established (Boyd et al., Dimakis et al., Nedic & Ozdaglar, Kempe et al.). Prior work on pairwise U-statistics in a decentralized setting appears limited to U2-gossip, which propagates two auxiliary observations and forgoes averaging. This paper’s main novelty is showing that a single auxiliary observation per node suffices when combined with averaging, delivering better rates and lower memory/communication. The phantom-node analysis is original in this context and connects cleanly to spectral graph theory.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: one auxiliary sample per node; standard pairwise averaging and swapping; asynchronous variant with local counters.
- Theory: clear dependence on spectral gap; O(1/t) for synchronous and O(log t/t) for asynchronous; informative data-dependent constants in the synchronous bound; refined comparison bound for U2-gossip that illuminates why averaging helps.
- Analysis technique: the phantom graph reformulation is elegant, separates dynamics, and should be reusable.
- Experiments: use real datasets and multiple graph topologies; show consistent advantages in mean error and across-node variance; investigate scaling with n; compare synchronous vs asynchronous.

Weaknesses and questions
- Convergence only in expectation: No almost-sure or high-probability bounds, no MSE bounds separating bias/variance. This limits robustness claims in stochastic or lossy settings. Can the authors provide concentration (e.g., Azuma/McDiarmid or mixing-based) or a.s. convergence via supermartingale arguments?
- Synchronous practicality and compute cost: In GoSta-sync, all nodes update Zk with a 1/t step and recompute H(Xk, Yk) every iteration, even when not participating in communication. This is heavy computationally and requires a global clock. The asynchronous version is more realistic; please emphasize when one should prefer it and report compute/communication per iteration for both.
- Edge selection model: “Draw (i,j) uniformly at random from E” presupposes global coordination. Standard randomized gossip assumes nodes wake and pick a random neighbor. For synchronous, can you restate results under node-driven activation (e.g., Metropolis weights), or add laziness/self-loops to cover bipartite/periodic issues?
- Assumptions and norms: Theorems use norms of H and H − h1^T but the matrix norm is not specified; please clarify (Frobenius vs operator). Are bounds assuming H is bounded? If so, state this. For Theorem 2, the bound loses the informative data-dependent terms; is a tighter (data-dependent) asynchronous bound possible?
- AUC as U-statistic: Equation (3) includes normalization by counts of positive/negative samples, which are not pairwise. Please spell out how this is handled in the decentralized setting (e.g., precomputing counts via one round of averaging, or incorporating normalization into H and a scalar correction). Also clarify symmetry and zero-diagonal requirements for the H used in AUC.
- Spectral gap scaling and fairness of metrics: c(G)=1−λ2(W2) can be as small as 1/n for complete graphs (hence 1/(ct)~n/t). The experiments plot relative error vs iterations; given different per-iteration work/communication in the algorithms, a second axis reporting messages/bytes or total H-evaluations would strengthen the comparison.
- Knowledge of |E| and degrees: GoSta-async needs pk=2dk/|E|. In networks where |E| is unknown or time-varying, can pk be estimated online, or can step sizes be designed to avoid this requirement (e.g., use local counters only)?
- Extensions and limitations: The paper claims extensions to multiple observations per node via node splitting and edge weighting, but details are scant. Similarly, directed/time-varying graphs, packet loss, or quantization are not discussed; push-sum or robust gossip could be relevant.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide high-probability and/or a.s. convergence results, at least for GoSta-sync; report MSE and variance decay across nodes.
- Clarify norms, boundedness assumptions on H, and the precise mapping for AUC to a symmetric, zero-diagonal H with the needed normalization.
- Report communication and computation costs per iteration and to reach a target accuracy, including message sizes, to complement iteration-based plots.
- Discuss handling bipartite graphs (e.g., lazy gossip) and nonuniform/node-driven activation; consider Metropolis-Hastings or Chebyshev acceleration to improve spectral dependence.
- If possible, tighten the asynchronous bound to reintroduce data-dependent terms akin to Theorem 1.
- Add experiments on time-varying or directed graphs (with push-sum), and explore robustness to delays/packet loss.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Technically solid; proofs deferred but the linear dynamical system view is sound. Some assumptions and norms need clarification; concentration results would bolster rigor.
- Clarity: Generally well written; the phantom-node construction is clear. Please fix norm ambiguities and better explain AUC instantiation and multiple-observation extension.
- Originality: A nontrivial extension of gossip to U-statistics with a new analysis device and a practical asynchronous algorithm; refined analysis of U2-gossip is also valuable.
- Significance: Useful building block for decentralized methods with pairwise losses (AUC, ranking, metric learning). Likely to be adopted/extended.

Pros
- Simple, implementable algorithms (sync/async) with theory and practice aligned.
- Novel analysis via phantom networks; clear spectral-gap insight.
- Stronger guarantees and empirical gains over the only prior method.

Cons
- Expectation-only guarantees; synchronous practicality; norm/assumption ambiguities; limited evaluation of communication/compute and robustness.

Recommendation: Weak accept. This is a meaningful and well-supported contribution to decentralized learning over networks, with room for strengthening the theory and experimental evaluation.
