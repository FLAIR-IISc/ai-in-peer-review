

# Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

This paper introduces a novel extension of gossip algorithms to efficiently compute U-statistics in distributed networks, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors propose two algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async) that combine data propagation with averaging steps to compute U-statistics like the sample variance, AUC, and within-cluster point scatter. The key insight is using "phantom nodes" to reformulate the problem, enabling convergence analysis and achieving O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence rates for synchronous and asynchronous settings, respectively. The paper demonstrates clear advantages over the previous U2-gossip method in terms of memory, communication costs, and empirical performance.

**Originality and Significance:** This work represents a bold extension of gossip algorithms to a fundamentally new problem domain. While gossip algorithms have been extensively studied for computing separable functions (like averages), U-statistics require pairwise interactions that existing methods cannot handle efficiently. The authors correctly identify this gap and propose a novel approach that fundamentally changes how gossip algorithms operate for non-separable statistics. This is not just an incremental improvement but a new direction for distributed computation. The phantom node reformulation is particularly clever and could inspire similar approaches for other non-separable distributed computation problems. The significance is reinforced by the wide applicability of U-statistics in machine learning (AUC, variance, clustering metrics) and the practical value of efficient distributed computation in sensor networks and large-scale data systems.

**Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful convergence proofs for both synchronous and asynchronous settings. The use of spectral graph theory to connect network connectivity to convergence rates is well-executed. The comparison to U2-gossip is compelling, showing both theoretical advantages (faster convergence rates, lower memory/communication costs) and empirical superiority. The experimental section demonstrates clear performance gains across multiple network topologies (complete graph, grid, Watts-Strogatz) for two important applications (AUC and within-cluster point scatter).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is precise, the algorithms are carefully described, and the technical contributions are well-motivated. The phantom node reformulation is initially surprising but becomes clear with the illustrative Figure 1 (though the figure reference is missing in the provided text). The notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:** The paper has a few minor weaknesses that don't detract from its novelty. The convergence rate for the asynchronous version is O(log t/t), which is standard for such algorithms but could be more explicitly compared to the synchronous rate in terms of practical implications. The experiments, while convincing, are limited to two applications; more diverse examples of U-statistics could further strengthen the case. The paper doesn't discuss how the algorithms handle non-uniform data distribution across nodes, though this is a common challenge in distributed systems that might be beyond the paper's scope.

**Comparison to Related Work:** The authors correctly position their work against U2-gossip [17], highlighting its limitations (higher memory/communication costs, lack of asynchronicity). They also provide a thorough review of gossip algorithms for separable functions, establishing a clear context for their contribution. The paper properly cites relevant work on gossip algorithms and U-statistics, though a deeper comparison to recent work on distributed optimization for non-separable functions would be valuable (e.g., recent NIPS papers on distributed learning of non-separable objectives).

**Overall Assessment:** This paper makes a significant contribution by extending gossip algorithms to a new class of problems (U-statistics) that has been largely unaddressed in distributed computation. The novelty is substantial, the theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the practical implications are clear. The minor weaknesses noted don't undermine the core contribution, which is a bold new direction for gossip algorithms.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper introduces a novel concept with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over existing work. It addresses an important gap in distributed computation and demonstrates how gossip algorithms can be extended to handle non-separable statistics. The phantom node reformulation is particularly elegant and could inspire further work. While the convergence rates themselves are standard for gossip algorithms, the paper shows how to apply these techniques to a new problem domain in a way that achieves superior performance. This represents a meaningful advancement in the field, not just an incremental improvement.

**Pro Accept:**
- Addresses a fundamental gap in gossip algorithms (U-statistics)
- Novel "phantom nodes" reformulation for analysis
- Clear theoretical and empirical advantages over previous work
- Broad applicability of U-statistics in machine learning
- Practical implementation with lower memory/communication costs

**Con Accept:**
- Convergence rates are standard for gossip algorithms (though applied to a new problem)
- Limited to two applications in experiments
- No discussion of non-uniform data distribution

This work represents a valuable new direction for distributed computation and should be accepted for NeurIPS.
