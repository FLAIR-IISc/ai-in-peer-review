### Summary

This paper presents randomized gossip algorithms, named GoSta, for the distributed estimation of degree-two U-statistics. Standard gossip algorithms are well-suited for separable functions like averages but fail for U-statistics, which depend on pairs of observations. The proposed method combines two mechanisms: a data propagation step, where nodes swap auxiliary data points, and a standard estimate averaging step. The authors provide both synchronous and asynchronous versions of their algorithm. They derive convergence rates of O(1/t) for the synchronous case and O(log t/t) for the asynchronous case. The analysis, which relies on a novel "phantom nodes" construction, shows that convergence speed depends on the spectral gap of the network graph. The paper positions GoSta as a significant improvement over the existing U2-gossip algorithm [17], claiming faster convergence and lower communication and memory costs. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter estimation tasks support these claims.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem. U-statistics represent a broad and important class of estimators, and developing efficient, fully decentralized algorithms for their computation is a valuable contribution to the fields of distributed machine learning and network science.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The core idea of the synchronous algorithm (GoSta-sync) is simple, elegant, and methodologically sound. Combining the data propagation mechanism (inspired by [17]) with the well-established consensus/averaging step from the gossip literature is an intuitive and effective way to ensure all nodes converge to the same global value.

3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence analysis for the synchronous algorithm is a primary strength. The O(1/t) rate is rigorously derived, and the use of the "phantom network" is a clever analytical tool for decoupling the system's dynamics. The explicit characterization of the convergence rate in terms of data-dependent dispersion and network-dependent spectral gap provides valuable insight. The theoretical argument for GoSta's superiority over U2-gossip, by identifying a detrimental `sqrt(n)` factor in the latter's error bound, is particularly convincing.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem is well-defined, the algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode, and the main theoretical results are stated precisely.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Novelty:** The contribution, while solid, can be viewed as an incremental improvement over existing work. The core mechanism of propagating observations via random walks was introduced in U2-gossip [17]. The main innovation here is the addition of a standard averaging step, a canonical technique in the gossip literature. While the combination is effective and its analysis is non-trivial, the conceptual leap is modest. The paper builds directly on established components rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach.

2.  **Assumptions in the Asynchronous Algorithm:** The proposed asynchronous algorithm (GoSta-async) relies on a problematic assumption. For the update rule, each node `k` needs to know its probability `pk = 2dk/|E|` of being selected. While the node's own degree `dk` is local information, the total number of edges `|E|` is a global property of the network. The paper does not specify how a node would obtain this global information in a truly decentralized setting without a central coordinator or significant communication overhead (e.g., via another consensus algorithm). This assumption undermines the claim of a fully asynchronous and decentralized algorithm and requires significant justification or an alternative approach.

3.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is centered almost exclusively on comparing GoSta to U2-gossip [17]. While this is the most direct competitor, the validation would be more compelling if it included other, even simpler, baselines. For example, a naive flooding-based algorithm where nodes broadcast their data point to all neighbors until the whole dataset is collected at each node would provide a useful, albeit communication-intensive, reference point for convergence time. Without a broader set of comparisons, it is difficult to assess the algorithm's performance in the wider landscape of distributed computation methods.

4.  **Discrepancy in Asynchronous Analysis:** The theoretical bound for GoSta-async is O(log t/t), which is worse than the O(1/t) for the synchronous version. However, the experiments in Figure 3b show their performance to be nearly identical. This discrepancy is not adequately discussed. While it is common for theoretical bounds to be loose, the authors should offer some intuition or analysis as to why the logarithmic factor does not manifest in practice in their experiments. This leaves a gap between the theoretical claims and the empirical evidence.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a methodologically sound and well-analyzed algorithm for a significant problem in decentralized computation. The synchronous version, GoSta-sync, is a clear and well-justified improvement over the prior state-of-the-art. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong support for the algorithm's design.

However, the work is weakened by the strong, seemingly unrealistic assumption of global network knowledge in the asynchronous setting, which limits its practical applicability. Furthermore, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the experimental validation could be strengthened with a wider range of baselines.

Given the strength of the synchronous algorithm and its supporting theory, the paper makes a valuable contribution. However, the concerns regarding the asynchronous version are significant. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the assumption of global knowledge in GoSta-async in the final version, either by proposing a method to estimate `|E|` decentrally or by clearly acknowledging this limitation and its practical implications.
