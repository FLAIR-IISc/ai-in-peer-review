This paper proposes randomized gossip algorithms for distributed estimation of U-statistics, a problem not well-addressed by existing gossip-based methods. The core idea of combining data propagation with averaging, and the use of a “phantom node” reformulation for analysis, are interesting. However, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to a lack of compelling evidence of significant improvement over existing approaches, particularly given the complexity introduced.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem setup and the proposed algorithms (GoSta-sync and GoSta-async). The notation is standard and the connection to related work is adequately established, though the discussion of existing gossip algorithms feels somewhat cursory given their importance as baselines. The theoretical analysis, while detailed, relies heavily on spectral gap properties which, while relevant, might not be easily accessible or controllable in many practical network settings.

My primary concern lies with the significance of the claimed improvements. The authors assert faster convergence and lower communication/memory costs compared to [17], but the experimental results, while demonstrating *some* improvement, don’t fully justify the added algorithmic complexity. The performance gains appear modest, and the improvement on the asynchronous version is not particularly strong, given the theoretical convergence rate of O(log t/t). Moreover, the comparison to [17] is the only direct comparison, and the lack of comparisons to simpler distributed averaging strategies (even if they don’t theoretically apply directly to U-statistics, their practical performance as a proxy could be informative) is a substantial weakness. The claim of superiority is largely based on asymptotic convergence rates and theoretical analysis, without a correspondingly strong empirical demonstration that these rates translate to significant advantages in realistic scenarios.

The phantom node reformulation, while clever, feels unnecessarily abstract and obfuscates the core mechanics of the algorithm. While it aids the analysis, it doesn't contribute to a more intuitive understanding of *why* the algorithm works and its limitations. The discussion of the practical implications of the data-dependent terms in the convergence bounds is also limited. How sensitive are the results to the specific characteristics of the U-statistic being estimated? This isn’t sufficiently addressed.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important and previously under-explored problem in distributed estimation.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the algorithms and theoretical analysis.
*   The phantom node reformulation is a novel analytical technique.
*   Demonstrates convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited empirical evidence of substantial performance gains compared to existing methods.
*   Lack of comparison to simpler distributed averaging baselines.
*   The phantom node reformulation adds complexity without clear practical benefits.
*   Insufficient discussion of the sensitivity of performance to data characteristics.
*   Convergence rates are not substantially better than existing methods and do not strongly translate to empirical improvement.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is relevant and challenging.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the proposed algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods.
*   The complexity of the algorithms may outweigh the modest performance gains.
*   The lack of comparison to a broader range of baselines weakens the claims of novelty and significance.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound and well-written contribution, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the limited practical significance of the claimed improvements outweigh the strengths of the work. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a practical solution to a real-world problem. I encourage the authors to conduct more extensive experimental evaluations, including comparisons to a wider range of baselines, and to provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach.
