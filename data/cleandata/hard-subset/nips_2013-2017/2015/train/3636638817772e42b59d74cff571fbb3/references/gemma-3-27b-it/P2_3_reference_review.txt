## Review of "Extending Gossip Algorithms to Distributed Estimation of U-statistics"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of distributed estimation of U-statistics using gossip algorithms. Existing gossip algorithms are limited to separable functions, hindering their applicability to quantities like variance, AUC, and correlation coefficients which are expressed as U-statistics. The authors propose both synchronous and asynchronous randomized gossip algorithms, dubbed GoSta, to overcome this limitation. They provide convergence rate analysis, showing O(1/t) and O(log t/t) convergence for synchronous and asynchronous versions respectively, with data- and network-dependent terms.  A key contribution is a clever reformulation of the problem using "phantom nodes" to facilitate the analysis.  Empirical evaluation on AUC and within-cluster point scatter demonstrates the superiority of their approach compared to a previous work (U2-gossip).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Extending gossip algorithms to handle non-separable functions like U-statistics significantly broadens their applicability in decentralized learning and estimation.
* **Novel approach:** The use of "phantom nodes" provides an elegant and insightful way to analyze the data propagation and averaging steps, enabling a rigorous convergence analysis.
* **Strong theoretical analysis:** The paper provides convergence rate analysis for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, with clear dependence on both data characteristics and network topology (spectral gap). This allows for a nuanced understanding of performance.
* **Empirical validation:** Experiments on real-world datasets (AUC and within-cluster point scatter) demonstrate the effectiveness of GoSta compared to U2-gossip, supporting the theoretical findings.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear definitions and notations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison:** While the paper demonstrates superiority over U2-gossip, comparisons to other relevant distributed estimation methods (e.g., those based on ADMM or other consensus algorithms) are lacking.  It would strengthen the paper to position GoSta more broadly within the existing literature.
* **Complexity of Phantom Node Argument:** While conceptually elegant, the phantom node construction can be initially challenging to grasp. Further clarification and/or a more intuitive explanation could improve accessibility.
* **Asynchronous Convergence Rate:** The O(log t/t) convergence rate for the asynchronous setting is weaker than the synchronous rate. The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between synchrony and asynchrony, and potential techniques to improve the asynchronous rate, would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two U-statistics on two datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to other U-statistics and datasets would increase confidence in the generalizability of the results.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation and well-supported claims. The analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, though the phantom node argument could benefit from further clarification. The notation is standard and clearly defined.
* **Originality:** The approach of extending gossip algorithms to U-statistics is novel, and the use of phantom nodes is a clever and original technique for analysis. The paper adequately references related work.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant as they open up new avenues for applying gossip algorithms to a wider range of decentralized estimation problems. The ability to analyze the impact of network topology on convergence is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* Provides a strong theoretical analysis with insightful convergence guarantees.
* Demonstrates empirical effectiveness compared to a relevant baseline.
* Well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to other distributed estimation methods.
* Phantom node argument could be more accessible.
* Asynchronous convergence rate is weaker.
* Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The approach is novel, the theoretical analysis is strong, and the experimental results are promising. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths significantly outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors consider:

1.  Expanding the related work section to include a more thorough comparison to other distributed estimation methods.
2.  Providing a more intuitive explanation of the phantom node construction.
3.  Discussing potential strategies for improving the asynchronous convergence rate.
4.  Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more U-statistics and datasets.
