This paper introduces randomized synchronous and asynchronous gossip algorithms for computing U-statistics in a distributed setting. The core idea is to decompose the U-statistic computation into data propagation and averaging steps, leveraging a "phantom node" reformulation to facilitate analysis. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings, demonstrating rates of O(1/t) and O(log t/t) respectively. Experimental results on AUC and within-cluster point scatter estimation validate the superiority of their approach compared to existing work, particularly U2-gossip [17].

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear limitation of traditional gossip algorithms – their inability to efficiently compute U-statistics. The problem setting is relevant to various decentralized learning applications. The approach of combining data propagation with averaging is intuitive and effectively tackles the challenge. The theoretical analysis, while dense, appears sound, and the convergence rates are clearly stated. The experimental validation on real datasets further strengthens the claims. The inclusion of comparisons to U2-gossip provides a valuable benchmark.  The phantom node reformulation is a clever technique used to derive the convergence guarantees.  The consideration of both synchronous and asynchronous scenarios enhances the practical applicability of the work.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. First, the notation is quite heavy and requires careful attention from the reader. While rigorous, the mathematical presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations alongside the formal derivations.  Second, while the authors mention extending the results to scenarios where nodes hold multiple observations, the discussion feels cursory and lacks detailed analysis or experimental validation.  Third, the connection between the convergence rates and the properties of the network graph (spectral gap) is stated, but the practical implications for network design or parameter tuning are not thoroughly explored.  Finally, the comparison to other baseline methods beyond U2-gossip is limited. Including comparisons with more standard decentralized estimation techniques (e.g., distributed gradient descent, or simpler averaging schemes) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a gap in the capabilities of gossip algorithms by extending them to efficiently compute U-statistics.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** Provides convergence guarantees for both synchronous and asynchronous settings.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Applicable to a wide range of decentralized learning problems.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates superior performance compared to U2-gossip on real datasets.
*   **Clever Approach:** The phantom node reformulation is a creative solution for analyzing the distributed computation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notationally Dense:** The mathematical presentation can be challenging to follow.
*   **Limited Discussion of Multiple Observations:** The extension to nodes holding multiple observations is not fully developed.
*   **Insufficient Network Parameter Exploration:** The practical implications of the network graph’s properties are not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to other decentralized estimation methods is limited.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing gossip algorithms, extending their applicability to a broader class of problems.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the proposed algorithms.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach and validate the theoretical findings.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The heavy notation and dense mathematical presentation may limit the accessibility of the paper to a wider audience.
*   The limited exploration of practical implications and comparison to a wider range of baselines weakens the overall impact of the work.
*   The cursory treatment of the extension to multiple observations diminishes the generalizability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of distributed estimation. The authors have identified an important problem, proposed a novel solution, and provided rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation. While the paper has some weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them. I lean towards **acceptance**, but strongly encourage the authors to address the aforementioned weaknesses in a revised version, particularly improving the clarity of the presentation and expanding the baseline comparisons. Specifically, simplifying the notation and including discussion or experiments related to multiple observations per node would be very valuable.
