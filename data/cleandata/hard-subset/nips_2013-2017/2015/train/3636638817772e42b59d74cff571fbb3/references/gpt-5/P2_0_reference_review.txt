Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over networks via gossip. The key idea is to (i) propagate data so each node can locally approximate hi = (1/n)∑j H(Xi,Xj) while (ii) simultaneously performing consensus averaging over the local estimates to obtain Ûn(H) = (1/n)∑i hi. The authors propose two algorithms: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (fully asynchronous). A main analytical device is a “phantom nodes/network” construction that decouples data propagation from averaging and enables a clean linear dynamical analysis. They prove convergence in expectation to Ûn(H) with rates O(1/t) (sync) and O(log t/t) (async). The bounds include data-dependent dispersion terms and a network-dependent spectral gap term (via the Laplacian), showing faster convergence on well-connected graphs. Compared to the prior U2-gossip (Pelckmans & Suykens 2009), GoSta uses only one auxiliary observation and one edge activation per iteration, supports asynchronous operation, and enjoys better rate constants (and no √n factor). Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter over complete, grid, and Watts–Strogatz graphs support the theoretical claims.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and algorithms are standard for gossip; the phantom network reformulation is well-motivated and leads to an upper triangular block system capturing averaging and propagation separately. The convergence claims are plausible and consistent with known consensus rates tied to spectral gaps. The synchronous rate O(1/t) with explicit dependence on λ2(W2) aligns with averaging-with-diminishing-stepsize behavior; the asynchronous O(log t/t) is also standard.
- Rigor and support: Key proofs are deferred to the supplement, but the sketch provided (definition of Wα, relation to Laplacian eigenvalues, recursion for E[S(t)]) is convincing. The comparison bound for U2-gossip (exhibiting a √n factor) is informative. One limitation is that guarantees are in expectation; there are no high-probability or almost-sure convergence bounds, nor mean-square error rates.
- Assumptions and practicality: The algorithms assume connected, non-bipartite, undirected graphs. The asynchronous method requires each node to know pk = 2dk/|E| (i.e., degree and total edges). Knowing |E| may be unrealistic in some settings; a discussion of how to estimate pk online (and the impact on bias/variance) would strengthen practicality. The synchronous algorithm samples a uniform random edge globally; a distributed implementation (e.g., Metropolis weights with local neighbor sampling) could be discussed to avoid a global edge oracle.
- Experiments: The empirical section demonstrates consistent improvements over U2-gossip on two tasks and multiple graph families, including variance reduction due to averaging. The experiments report relative error vs iterations; it would be helpful to add communication/wall-clock metrics and larger graphs to illustrate spectral-gap scaling, but the current results are adequate to support claims.

Clarity
- Organization and exposition are generally clear. The algorithms are stated precisely. The phantom network construction and block-matrix evolution are explained well.
- Notational issues: “‖·‖” is used for vectors as ℓ2; for matrices it is ambiguous (operator vs Frobenius). Clarify which norm is used in the bounds. Explicitly define H as the n×n matrix [H(Xi,Xj)] to aid readability.
- Minor: The non-bipartite assumption should be motivated briefly (to avoid oscillations). Mention that “lazy” variants can handle bipartite graphs. Also, note explicitly that H(X,X)=0 ensures (1) equals the usual U-statistic up to n/(n−1).

Originality
- The problem—distributed estimation of U-statistics via gossip—has limited prior treatment (notably U2-gossip). The proposed combination of local running averages with single-observation propagation and the phantom nodes analysis constitute a novel and useful advance. The asynchronous variant with unbiased local time-counters is also a thoughtful contribution.

Significance
- U-statistics underpin many widely used measures (AUC, Kendall’s tau, scatter), so a scalable decentralized estimator is valuable. The results improve communication/memory and rates over the main prior work and provide insight via spectral-gap dependence. The analysis technique could be reused in related decentralized estimation/optimization. While not a breakthrough in consensus theory, it meaningfully advances a specific, practically relevant problem.

Pros
- Clear, principled algorithms; asynchronous version included.
- Solid theory with data- and network-dependent rates; improved constants vs prior work.
- Lower memory and communication per-iteration than U2-gossip.
- Empirical validation on real datasets and multiple graph topologies.

Cons
- Guarantees only in expectation; no high-probability or a.s. convergence or MSE rates.
- Asynchronous method assumes knowledge of |E|; practicality of obtaining pk not fully addressed.
- Synchronous edge sampling assumes a global uniform edge oracle; distributed implementation details are not discussed.
- Experiments lack communication/wall-clock metrics and larger-scale tests; asynchronous bound is looser and constants are opaque.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you provide high-probability or MSE bounds (e.g., via martingale concentration) and a.s. convergence?
- How sensitive is GoSta-async to errors in pk? Can pk be estimated on the fly without bias?
- Can you handle bipartite graphs via laziness or self-loops, and would the bounds change?
- Clarify the matrix norm in bounds and define H as a matrix explicitly.
- Report communication complexity and wall-clock in addition to iterations, and include larger graphs to illustrate spectral-gap scaling.
- Discuss extension to higher-order U-statistics (m>2) and directed/weighted graphs.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, incremental-yet-meaningful contribution that is technically sound, clearly written, and relevant to the NeurIPS community. It improves the state of the art for decentralized estimation of U-statistics in both algorithm design and analysis, with supportive experiments. With minor clarifications and an added discussion on pk and implementation details, it meets the NeurIPS reviewing criteria well. I recommend acceptance.
