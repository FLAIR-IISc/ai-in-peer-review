Summary
The paper proposes two randomized gossip algorithms for decentralized estimation of degree-2 U-statistics over a connected undirected network: GoSta-sync (synchronous) and GoSta-async (asynchronous). Each node holds one local observation Xi, keeps one auxiliary sample Yk that performs a random walk via pairwise swaps, and maintains a running estimate Zk. In the synchronous variant, all nodes update Zk each iteration, then one random edge averages its endpoints’ estimates and swaps auxiliary observations. In the asynchronous variant, only the sampled edge’s endpoints update; to debias the running average, nodes maintain a scaled counter mk with weights 1/(pk mk), where pk is the probability a node is sampled.

The analysis uses a “phantom nodes” construction: for each k, a copy of the network carries the vector (H(Xk, X1),…,H(Xk, Xn)) that evolves via swap operations; the original nodes average their estimates and read from their own “phantom” counterpart. The authors show convergence in expectation to the global U-statistic with rates O(1/t) (synchronous) and O(log t/t) (asynchronous), with constants depending on a spectral gap term 1 − λ2(W2(G)) and data-dependent norms. Experiments on AUC and within-cluster scatter on several graph families indicate improved empirical performance vs. U2-gossip [Pelckmans & Suykens, IFAC 2009] with lower memory and communication per round.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Extends gossip beyond separable aggregates to pairwise U-statistics, with clear applications (AUC, clustering scatter).
- Algorithmic simplicity: Single auxiliary variable per node; single edge communication per iteration; asynchronous variant is natural.
- Analysis idea: The phantom-network reformulation is elegant and may be of independent interest.
- Rates: Clean expectation bounds with explicit spectral-gap dependency; intuitive explanation for faster mixing on well-connected graphs.
- Empirics: Consistent improvements vs. U2-gossip across graphs and two tasks; lower variance due to averaging step.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Technical soundness and completeness
- Only convergence in expectation is proven. No almost-sure, mean-square, or high-probability bounds; no variance analysis. This is weaker than typical gossip results and weakens the practical relevance of the O(1/t) and O(log t/t) rates (which bound bias only).
- Asynchronous debiasing: The use of mk increments by 1/pk and weights 1/(pk mk) is justified by E[mk(t)] = t, but unbiasedness of the iterative estimator requires a more careful conditional expectation argument (mk is adapted to the filtration; dependence between mk and Yk/edge sequence is nontrivial). This needs a rigorous proof sketch in the main text.
- Assumptions on H: Bounds involve norms like ||H − h 1ᵀ|| and ||H||. No boundedness or moment assumptions are stated; it is unclear which matrix norm is used (spectral? Frobenius?), while earlier “‖·‖ is ℓ2 norm” applies to vectors. This ambiguity matters for constants and interpretability.
- Non-bipartite requirement: Standard fix via laziness (self-loops) would remove this restriction; authors should discuss or incorporate it.

Notation and analytical precision
- Wα(G) is defined “for any α > 1,” but W1(G) is used later (data propagation block). Either allow α ≥ 1 in the definition or justify α = 1 separately.
- Equation (8): notation W2(G) t−s and Cs−1 suggests matrix powers but is ambiguous; use W2(G)^(t−s), C^(s−1).
- Blocks B and C: Dimensions and explicit forms are hard to parse. M2(t) “block diagonal with eᵢᵀ” is unclear; please provide precise Kronecker forms for both B and C consistently.
- Theorem 2: c′(G) is unspecified and the norm on the LHS is not explicitly stated.

Modeling/algorithmic assumptions
- Synchronous algorithm assumes drawing a uniform random edge from E at each iteration; this typically requires a centralized scheduler or precomputed global randomness. In decentralized gossip, nodes waking and choosing neighbors induce degree-biased edge selection. The gap between the model and implementable protocols needs discussion or an adaptation (e.g., use random neighbor selection and adjust analysis with P).
- Asynchronous algorithm requires each node to know |E| to compute pk = 2 d_k/|E|. This is a strong global-information requirement. Without |E|, how to implement pk or estimate it in a decentralized way?
- Only one edge activates per iteration; comparison across graphs should normalize for total communications. Reporting wall-clock or message complexity to reach a target error would be more meaningful.

Applications and correctness
- AUC kernel: As written, H((x,ℓ),(x′,ℓ′)) = (1 − ℓℓ′) I{ℓ θᵀx > −ℓ′ θᵀx′} is neither clearly symmetric nor properly normalized to recover AUC in [0,1]. The standard symmetric U-kernel uses unordered positive/negative pairs, e.g., I{ℓ=+1,ℓ′=−1} I{θᵀx > θᵀx′} + I{ℓ=−1,ℓ′=+1} I{θᵀx′ > θᵀx}. Please fix the definition and show how it maps into (1).
- Experimental details: “Time” axis appears to be iterations; please clarify and report message/byte counts. For asynchronous, provide more extensive results. Clarify initialization, tie-handling in AUC, and label/graph assignment strategies.

Writing/formatting
- Numerous minor issues: hyphenation breaks (“lo- cal”), “perfomance,” inconsistent use of bi-stochastic/bi-stochastic, stray colons in algorithms, inconsistent equation numbering and footnotes, missing figure/table references in text (e.g., Table 1 not shown).
- References are incomplete and inconsistent (missing years, venues, page numbers).

Relation to prior work
- Good coverage of standard averaging gossip [Boyd et al.], geographic gossip, and U2-gossip. However, please discuss related push-sum/ratio consensus methods in asynchronous settings and whether they can be adapted here; also comment on random-walk-based U-statistic estimators in statistics literature.

Pros
- Tackles an important and underexplored problem (decentralized U-statistics).
- Simple, memory- and communication-efficient algorithms.
- Clear spectral-gap dependence; intuitive analysis via phantom nodes.
- Empirical improvements over the main prior method.

Cons
- Only expectation convergence; no almost-sure/MSE/high-probability guarantees.
- AUC kernel appears incorrect/non-symmetric as written.
- Several modeling assumptions are unrealistic for decentralized systems (uniform edge sampling; knowledge of |E|).
- Notational ambiguities (norms, Wα domain), missing constants, and reliance on supplement for key proofs.
- Numerous minor writing/formatting/reference issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The contribution is promising, but the accumulation of technical, modeling, and presentation issues—especially the AUC kernel definition, weak convergence guarantees, and decentralized implementability—undercuts confidence. With fixes (correct U-kernels, stronger convergence statements, clarified/realistic protocols, improved notation and experiments), this could be a strong submission.
