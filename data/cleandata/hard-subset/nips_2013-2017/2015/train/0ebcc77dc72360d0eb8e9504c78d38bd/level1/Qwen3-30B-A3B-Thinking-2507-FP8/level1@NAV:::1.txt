

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization method that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling—two practical limitations in prior approaches. The authors build upon the theoretical framework of de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], addressing the open problem of creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence that avoids impractical sampling procedures. The key innovation lies in considering "infinitely many possible bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior, formalized through an unknown semi-metric assumption. The paper proves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, demonstrating theoretical superiority over BaMSOO [2] which achieves only polynomial regret.

Strengths include a rigorous theoretical analysis with well-motivated assumptions, clear algorithmic description with pseudocode, and comprehensive experiments showing consistent performance improvements across multiple benchmark functions (including high-dimensional cases). The authors effectively address the practical concern of δ-cover sampling while maintaining theoretical guarantees, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications. The paper also provides insightful theoretical analysis of how GP's bound quality affects the regret, offering a principled framework for understanding when and why the method works well.

Weaknesses include somewhat dense theoretical exposition that could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the infinite-metric exploration loss concept. The experimental section would be strengthened by comparing against additional recent baselines like BOHB or TuRBO. While the paper claims exponential convergence, the constant λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, suggesting potential scalability challenges that warrant deeper discussion.

The paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization theory and practice. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work (notably BaMSOO and GP-UCB) and provides both theoretical rigor and practical implementation insights. The theoretical framework for understanding when to rely on GP bounds versus considering multiple bounds is particularly valuable for the broader optimization community. The method's ability to achieve exponential convergence without impractical sampling procedures represents a meaningful advance in the field.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong theoretical contribution while addressing a practical limitation in Bayesian optimization. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and should be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers. I recommend acceptance.
