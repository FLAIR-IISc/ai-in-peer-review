This paper presents a Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence that avoids auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, addressing limitations of prior work (de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]). The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric inherent in the function’s continuity, combined with the information from the GP prior, to guide search and achieve exponential convergence. The authors introduce “Infinite-Metric GP Optimization” (IMGPO) and provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating its convergence rate. Experiments on several benchmark functions show IMGPO outperforms other methods.

The paper is well-structured and addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian optimization – achieving exponential convergence without computationally expensive components like δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization. The motivation is clearly stated, and the related work is appropriately cited, acknowledging both the theoretical advancements (de Freitas et al. [1]) and the practical limitations (Wang et al. [2]). The introduction of the unknown semi-metric ` is a clever idea, and the theoretical analysis appears sound, culminating in Theorem 1 which states the exponential convergence rate. The inclusion of experimental results strengthens the claim, showcasing IMGPO’s performance across a variety of benchmark functions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis seems rigorous, and the convergence result is a significant contribution. The connection to bound-based search methods provides a useful context.
*   **Novelty:** The introduction of the infinite-metric concept and its integration with GP optimization is novel.  Eliminating the need for δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization is a substantial improvement.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm description is clear, and Figure 1 effectively illustrates the process.
*   **Significance:** Exponential convergence is a desirable property for optimization algorithms. The removal of practical barriers (δ-cover sampling, auxiliary optimization) makes the approach more applicable to real-world problems.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm performs well compared to existing methods, lending credibility to the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the discussion of scalability (particularly with respect to input dimension *D*) acknowledges limitations. The remark about needing additional assumptions for higher dimensionality hints at a potential weakness.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of IMGPO appears sensitive to the parameter Ξmax, as evidenced by the discussion in the experimental section.  More detailed analysis of Ξmax's impact would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Setup:** The experiments initialize hyperparameters blindly. While acknowledging this is a common practice, it could be more robust to include a brief sensitivity analysis on hyperparameter choices.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to SOO is somewhat limited as GP-UCB/BaMSOO were evaluated with handpicked kernels/hyperparameters. A fairer comparison would involve a similar effort for all methods.
*   **Complexity of Algorithm:** The algorithm description, while clear, appears somewhat complex, particularly the step-by-step process outlined in Section 3.2. The number of steps and conditional checks could make implementation challenging.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to Bayesian optimization, addressing a significant gap in the field.
*   The theoretical analysis guarantees exponential convergence, a highly desirable property.
*   Experimental results support the claims and demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness.
*   The elimination of δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization makes the approach more practical.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Scalability to very high dimensions remains a concern.
*   The parameter Ξmax appears sensitive, and a more in-depth analysis of its impact is needed.
*   The complexity of the algorithm could pose implementation challenges.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The novelty of the approach, the strength of the theoretical analysis, and the promising experimental results outweigh the weaknesses. The potential scalability issues and parameter sensitivity warrant further investigation, but do not negate the value of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions – specifically, a more detailed discussion of scalability and the influence of Ξmax, and a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and a fairer experimental setup.
