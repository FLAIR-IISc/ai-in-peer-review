

# Peer Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations in existing methods: GP-UCB requires auxiliary optimization (solving an additional non-convex problem), and the exponential convergence method by de Freitas et al. (2012) requires impractical δ-cover sampling. IMGPO eliminates both requirements while maintaining exponential convergence, representing a significant theoretical and practical advance in Bayesian optimization.

The paper builds on the NIPS tradition of theoretical analysis of Bayesian optimization methods. It directly extends the GP-UCB framework (Srinivas et al., NIPS 2010) and relates to Munos' SOO (NIPS 2011) and Wang et al.'s BaMSOO (AISTATS 2014). The authors correctly position their work against these prior contributions, noting that BaMSOO only achieves polynomial regret bounds while their method achieves exponential convergence without the impractical δ-cover sampling.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis establishes an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 under reasonable assumptions, which is a significant improvement over the polynomial regret bounds of existing methods. The proofs are sound, and the supplementary material provides sufficient detail. The experimental section compares IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on eight benchmark functions, showing consistent improvements. The authors acknowledge limitations, noting that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, suggesting scalability challenges for high-dimensional problems. They also appropriately discuss the trade-off between using GP to reduce exploration loss (ρ̄t) versus increasing Ξn.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for the approach is effectively explained, and Figure 1 provides an intuitive illustration of the algorithm's operation. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The theoretical analysis, while technically demanding, is presented logically. Some readers might struggle with the formal definition of infinite-metric exploration loss (Definition 1), but this is a necessary technical detail for the analysis.

**Originality:** The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution. The core idea of considering "infinitely many possible candidates of the bounds" rather than relying on a single upper bound encoded by the GP prior is a creative solution to a long-standing problem in Bayesian optimization. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work, particularly from BaMSOO and GP-UCB. This represents a meaningful advancement beyond the state of the art, as confirmed by the authors' thorough comparison with relevant prior work.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. Achieving exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling is a major practical contribution, as both requirements have been recognized as barriers to real-world adoption of theoretically strong methods. The paper provides a new theoretical framework for understanding when not relying on a specific bound is advantageous, which could influence other bound-based methods beyond Bayesian optimization. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple benchmark functions, supporting the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**
- First method to achieve exponential convergence in Bayesian optimization without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling
- Strong theoretical analysis with a clear regret bound
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing consistent performance improvements
- Clear exposition of the theoretical framework explaining why not relying on a single bound is beneficial
- Practical implementation with publicly available code

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of scalability to high-dimensional problems beyond the theoretical note about λ approaching 1
- The parameter Ξmax is fixed to 22 without thorough analysis of its optimal value across different problem classes
- Statistical significance testing between IMGPO and baselines would strengthen the experimental claims
- The connection to the REMBO approach for high-dimensional problems (in the 1000-dimensional Sin2 test) could be more thoroughly explained

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Solves a well-known practical limitation in Bayesian optimization (no auxiliary optimization)
- First method to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides meaningful insights
- Experimental results consistently outperform existing methods
- The approach has potential to influence other bound-based optimization methods

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited scalability analysis for high-dimensional problems
- Some technical details in the analysis may be challenging for non-specialists
- Parameter sensitivity (Ξmax) is not thoroughly explored

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It addresses a long-standing practical limitation in the field while providing a novel theoretical framework that explains why their approach is advantageous. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the state of the art. The weaknesses noted are minor compared to the substantial contribution the paper makes.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS as this work represents a meaningful advance in Bayesian optimization with both theoretical and practical significance. The authors have successfully demonstrated that exponential convergence can be achieved without the impractical requirements that have previously limited the applicability of such methods.
