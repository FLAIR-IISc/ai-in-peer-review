- Presents IMGPO, a Bayesian optimization algorithm achieving exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ‑cover sampling.
- Core idea: use an unknown semi‑metric to encode continuity and perform simultaneous global and local search.
- Theoretical analysis proving exponential convergence under the stated assumptions.
- Builds on GP‑based Bayesian optimization literature and addresses limitations of de Freitas et al. and Wang et al.
- Clear exposition and helpful illustrations (e.g., Figure 1) aid understanding of the algorithm.
- Empirical results show competitive performance on standard benchmark functions against SOO, BaMSOO, GP‑PI, and GP‑EI.
- Detailed discussion on the interaction between GP‑derived bounds and the unknown semi‑metric, with useful remarks on bound tightness.
- Significant theoretical contribution by removing impractical δ‑cover sampling and auxiliary optimization requirements.
- Novel approach leveraging an unknown semi‑metric and combined global/local search strategy.
- Scalability concerns: potential issues as dimensionality increases and computational bottlenecks (e.g., dependence on Ξmax).
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: fixed kernel hyperparameters and limited exploration of sensitivity or robustness to initialization and empirical Bayes updates.
- Algorithm complexity: hierarchical partitioning and multi‑step procedure may be computationally intensive and hard to implement.
- Limited depth in empirical comparisons; need more analysis of cases where IMGPO fails or underperforms (e.g., Rosenbrock2).
- Strong assumptions (existence of global optimizer and unknown semi‑metric) may limit applicability when violated.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a major limitation, provides strong theory, novel method, and promising empirical evidence.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability, computational complexity, hyperparameter issues, and relatively superficial experimental comparisons.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, deeper empirical comparisons, and discussion of Assumption 1 implications.
