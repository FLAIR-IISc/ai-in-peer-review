This paper presents a Bayesian optimization method achieving exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing limitations of prior work by de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The core idea is an “Infinite-Metric GP Optimization” (IMGPO) algorithm that leverages an unknown semi-metric encoding function continuity, conducting both global and local searches simultaneously. This circumvents the need for δ-cover sampling, previously identified as impractical, and eliminates auxiliary optimization steps. The approach is grounded in a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in Bayesian optimization, particularly Gaussian Process (GP) based methods [13, 14, 15]. The paper correctly identifies the challenges associated with Lipschitz continuity assumptions in earlier optimization approaches [7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12] and positions Bayesian optimization as a means to relax these constraints. It directly addresses the limitations of de Freitas et al.’s [1] exponential convergence result by removing the reliance on δ-cover sampling, a major practical obstacle, as noted by Wang et al. [2].  The connection to bound-based search methods like A* and PAC-MDP algorithms [18, 23, 24] is also well-articulated.  The authors appropriately reference relevant literature including work on DIRECT [19] and SOO [18].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength is the theoretical result guaranteeing exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization. This is a significant advancement in the field, addressing a clear limitation in existing methods.
* **Novel Approach:** The concept of leveraging an unknown semi-metric to guide the search is innovative and offers a potentially powerful strategy for optimization.  The simultaneous global and local search is a compelling idea.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and theoretical results. The use of Figure 1 to illustrate the algorithm's operation is helpful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO on a variety of benchmark functions, showcasing its competitive performance compared to existing algorithms (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI).
* **Detailed Analysis:** The discussion following the experimental results and in Section 4 provides a valuable, nuanced analysis of the algorithm's behavior and the interplay between GP-based bounds and the unknown semi-metric. The remarks clarifying the impact of tightness of the GP-derived bounds are particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** While the theoretical analysis is promising, the discussion acknowledges potential scalability issues with increasing dimensionality (mentioned towards the end of Section 4). This is a crucial limitation, and more detailed discussion regarding strategies to mitigate this issue would be beneficial. The reliance on parameters like Ξmax also hints at potential computational bottlenecks.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The experiments use fixed hyperparameters (σ and l) for the kernel, initialized to arbitrary values.  While updated via an empirical Bayesian method, the sensitivity of the results to these initial values and the efficiency of the empirical Bayesian update are not thoroughly explored.
* **Complexity of Algorithm:** The algorithm appears complex to implement, despite the clear explanation.  The hierarchical partitioning and the interplay between different steps (i)-(v) seem computationally intensive, potentially offsetting some of the gains from avoiding auxiliary optimization.
* **Limited Comparison Depth:** The comparison to other algorithms (BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) seems somewhat superficial. A deeper analysis of the scenarios where IMGPO excels and where it falters would strengthen the empirical validation. For instance, the authors mention that SOO performs better for Rosenbrock2 because of a poor GP prior, but this isn’t investigated further.
* **Assumptions:** Assumption 1 (existence of a global optimizer and unknown semi-metric) is relatively strong. While common in optimization, it's worth acknowledging the potential impact of violations of this assumption.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation in Bayesian optimization, providing a theoretical and practical solution to the δ-cover sampling problem.
* The proposed IMGPO algorithm demonstrates competitive performance on standard benchmark functions.
* The detailed theoretical analysis and insightful discussion contribute to a deeper understanding of Bayesian optimization.
* The work is novel and advances the state-of-the-art in global optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability concerns and potential computational complexity of the algorithm raise questions about its practical applicability to high-dimensional problems.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and the relatively superficial comparison to other algorithms could be addressed with further experimentation.
* The strong assumptions underlying the theoretical results warrant careful consideration.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The proposed IMGPO algorithm represents a notable advancement in Bayesian optimization by achieving exponential convergence without the limitations of previous methods.  While scalability concerns and hyperparameter sensitivity warrant further investigation, the theoretical soundness, clear presentation, and promising empirical results make this paper worthy of acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, and a more in-depth comparison to other algorithms.  The authors should also briefly discuss the implications of violating Assumption 1.
