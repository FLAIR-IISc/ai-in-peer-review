This paper introduces IMGPO, a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate. This work addresses a significant open problem in the field, previously highlighted by de Freitas et al. (2012) and Wang et al. (2014), by developing a method that guarantees exponential regret without requiring either an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure or a computationally expensive auxiliary optimization step. The core idea is a novel synthesis of GP-UCB with the infinite-metric optimization paradigm of algorithms like SOO (Munos, 2011). IMGPO uses hierarchical partitioning to implicitly consider an infinite set of potential function smoothnesses, while leveraging the GP-UCB bound to prune unpromising regions of the search space efficiently.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper’s primary strength is its significant theoretical contribution. By successfully combining two distinct lines of global optimization research, it provides the first practical GP-based algorithm with an exponential regret bound. This convincingly advances the state of the art beyond polynomial bounds, such as those for the related BaMSOO algorithm. The analysis in Remarks 1-3, which provides intuition on the interplay between the GP-bound and the infinite-metric approach, is particularly insightful.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, with its central claim of exponential convergence supported by a formal proof in the supplementary material. The algorithm is evaluated on a diverse set of benchmark functions, demonstrating superior or competitive performance against relevant baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-EI/PI methods. The authors are forthright about the algorithm's limitations, such as its scalability challenges in high dimensions.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** While the paper is well-structured, the description of the algorithm itself is quite dense. The connection between the high-level textual description of the steps and the detailed pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is challenging to trace, which may hinder ease of implementation and reproducibility for other researchers. A more detailed walkthrough of the pseudocode with a simple example could improve clarity.
*   **Practical Complexity:** The algorithm, while avoiding auxiliary optimization, is fairly complex, with multiple nested loops and rules for selecting and partitioning hyperrectangles. This complexity might pose a barrier to adoption for practitioners when compared to simpler, though less theoretically grounded, methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a strong theoretical result that solves a recognized open problem in Bayesian optimization.
2.  The proposed method demonstrates excellent empirical performance.
3.  The work is original and elegantly combines ideas from two different optimization subfields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The algorithm’s description is dense, potentially impacting reproducibility.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a significant and novel contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. The strengths far outweigh the minor weakness in clarity, which can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
