

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of existing Bayesian optimization methods: the need for an additional non-convex global optimization problem (auxiliary optimization) and the impractical requirement of δ-cover sampling. The paper builds on the work of de Freitas et al. (2012) who presented a method with exponential convergence but requiring δ-cover sampling, and Wang et al. (2013) who combined GP-UCB with SOO to achieve polynomial regret bounds. The authors' key insight is to simultaneously consider all possible candidates for an unknown semi-metric bound (rather than relying on the GP's bound) while using the GP bound to prune the search space.

**Quality (Strengths):** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1. The proof is well-structured and builds on the theoretical framework of Munos (2011) for SOO. The authors carefully analyze the effect of the GP prior and the infinite-metric exploration loss, providing valuable insights into when their approach outperforms previous methods (Remarks 1-3). The experimental results across eight benchmark functions demonstrate consistent improvements over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, with IMGPO achieving lower regret values in most cases. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the potential dimensionality scalability issue noted in Remark 1.

**Quality (Weaknesses):** While the main proof is provided in the supplementary material, the core theoretical result (Theorem 1) is stated without full proof in the main text, which is somewhat unusual for NIPS. The paper could better clarify how the constants in the regret bound (C, D, etc.) scale with dimensionality, as the practical implications of the dimensionality dependence are not fully explored. The experimental section could also include more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to Ξmax parameter choice.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The algorithm description (Section 3.2) is supported by a clear figure (Figure 1) and the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is detailed and executable. The relationship to previous work (Section 3.4) is well-explained, particularly the distinction from BaMSOO and DIRECT. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The paper could be slightly improved by providing more intuition for the infinite-metric exploration loss concept earlier in the paper.

**Originality:** The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by proposing the "infinite-metric" approach that simultaneously considers all possible candidates for the bound rather than relying on a single estimated bound. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous work that either relied on a single bound (GP-UCB) or used a fixed bound (SOO). The authors clearly articulate how their approach combines elements from both paths in the literature (the bound-based methods and the multi-bound methods), which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a practical limitation in Bayesian optimization that has been recognized in the literature (Wang et al. 2013). The exponential convergence rate is theoretically significant, and the experimental results demonstrate practical advantages. The paper's insights about the benefits of not committing to a single bound could influence future work beyond Bayesian optimization, as noted in the conclusion. The algorithm's ability to avoid auxiliary optimization makes it more practical for real-world applications where computational resources are limited.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- First method achieving exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear insights into when the approach outperforms previous methods
- Strong experimental results across multiple benchmark functions
- Practical insights with potential to influence broader optimization community

*Con:*
- Supplementary material required for full theoretical proof
- Limited exploration of dimensionality scaling behavior
- Relatively small number of test functions (8) for experimental validation
- Some aspects of the theoretical analysis could be more accessible to general NIPS audience

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. The paper makes a novel theoretical contribution with clear practical implications, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. While there are minor weaknesses in the presentation of the theoretical results and the experimental scope, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper meets all four criteria with high scores: quality (strong), clarity (good), originality (high), and significance (high). The authors have addressed a meaningful limitation in Bayesian optimization with a theoretically sound and practically useful method, and their insights about the value of not committing to a single bound could have broader implications for optimization research. The paper represents a substantial advance over previous work in this area.
