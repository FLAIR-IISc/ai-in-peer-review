### Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to achieve an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret of a black-box function. The work positions itself as a solution to a key open problem in the field. Previous work by de Freitas et al. [1] demonstrated exponential convergence for GP-based optimization but relied on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. More recent work, like BaMSOO [2], made the algorithm practical by removing this requirement but at the cost of weakening the theoretical guarantees to a polynomial convergence rate.

The core contribution of this paper is an algorithm that achieves the best of both worlds: an exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling. Furthermore, IMGPO cleverly avoids the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization step to maximize an acquisition function (like UCB or EI), which is a significant computational bottleneck in standard GP-based Bayesian optimization. The method achieves this by integrating ideas from hierarchical partitioning methods (akin to SOO [18] and DIRECT [19]) with the probabilistic bounds from a Gaussian Process. The central idea is to not rely solely on the GP-derived bound, which might be loose, but to simultaneously consider an infinite family of potential local smoothness assumptions (encapsulated in an "unknown semi-metric" `) via the partitioning scheme. The GP-UCB is then used as a mechanism to efficiently prune unpromising branches of the search tree that are identified by the partitioning strategy.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important open problem in Bayesian optimization. Achieving an exponential convergence rate in a practical algorithm is a significant theoretical advance. The proposed approach of unifying two major streams of global optimization research—Lipschitz-style hierarchical partitioning and probabilistic GP-based models—is highly novel and insightful. The conceptual framework of leveraging a known bound (from the GP) to accelerate a search that implicitly accounts for an unknown but tighter bound is a powerful and potentially generalizable idea.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, formally establishes the exponential convergence rate O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1. This is a substantial improvement over the polynomial rates of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The analysis provides valuable insights through concepts like the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt), which helps formalize the trade-off between exploring different smoothness assumptions and the overall convergence speed. The remarks following the theorem clearly articulate the benefits of the proposed approach over methods that rely on a single, fixed bound.

3.  **Improved Practicality:** By design, IMGPO eliminates the auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function, which is often a major implementation challenge and computational cost in methods like GP-UCB/EI/PI. The algorithm is deterministic (given a kernel and domain), which aids reproducibility. The empirical results in Table 1 confirm that it is computationally faster than standard GP optimization methods.

4.  **Solid Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a compelling empirical comparison against relevant and strong baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI) across a range of benchmark functions with varying characteristics and dimensionalities (from 1 to 1000). IMGPO demonstrates consistently superior or competitive performance, lending strong support to the theoretical claims. The inclusion of the high-dimensional experiment using REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine effectively demonstrates its potential as a core component in more complex optimization frameworks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the clarity of the algorithm's presentation. Section 3.2 and Algorithm 1 are very dense and difficult to parse for a reader not already intimately familiar with the intricacies of SOO-style algorithms.
    *   The multi-step filtering process (steps i-iii) lacks a clear, high-level intuitive explanation. A more detailed walk-through with a simpler example *before* presenting the full, complex pseudocode would greatly improve readability.
    *   The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is convoluted. The `while true` loop (line 9) for resolving dummy values, the intertwined updates of `υmax`, and the logic for selecting `i*h` are hard to follow.
    *   The computational cost of step (iii) (line 22) seems potentially very high. It requires computing the maximum UCB over all nodes in a fully expanded subtree up to depth Ξ. While `Ξmax` bounds this, this step appears to reintroduce a significant search cost, which somewhat tempers the "no auxiliary optimization" claim. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of this specific step and its impact on the overall runtime.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** While the convergence is exponential, the rate λ approaches 1 as the dimension D increases (via the constant C in the theorem). The paper briefly acknowledges this, but the curse of dimensionality inherent in hierarchical partitioning methods is a major limitation that deserves a more explicit discussion. The algorithm is likely to be most effective in low-to-moderate dimensions, which should be stated more clearly.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces new parameters, particularly `Ξmax` and the adaptive schedule for `Ξ`. The paper provides little justification for the chosen values and schedule, and the sensitivity analysis is limited to a brief comment at the end of the experiments section. A more thorough study of how these parameters affect performance and runtime would strengthen the practical claims of the paper.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical result is strong, and the proposed algorithm, IMGPO, is both practical and empirically effective. The unification of hierarchical partitioning with GP-based search is elegant and powerful. While the presentation of the algorithm could be substantially improved for clarity, and a deeper discussion of scalability and parameter sensitivity would be welcome, these issues are addressable and do not detract from the core importance of the contribution. The paper's strengths in significance, originality, and technical quality are outstanding. I strongly recommend acceptance.
