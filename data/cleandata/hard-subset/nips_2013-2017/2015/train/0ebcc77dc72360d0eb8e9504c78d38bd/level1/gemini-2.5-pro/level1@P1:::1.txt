This paper presents IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel algorithm for global optimization of black-box functions. The main contribution is a practical Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization method that achieves an exponential convergence rate for simple regret. This is significant because it overcomes two major hurdles of previous work: it does not require solving an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next point (a common step in methods like GP-UCB), and it avoids the need for an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure, which was a key requirement for the exponential regret bound in the work of de Freitas et al. [1].

The core idea of IMGPO is a clever synthesis of two distinct lines of research in global optimization. It builds on the hierarchical partitioning framework of optimistic optimization algorithms like SOO [18], which implicitly consider an infinite family of possible function smoothnesses (or semi-metrics) without requiring knowledge of a Lipschitz constant. However, unlike SOO, which treats all partitions equally at a given level, IMGPO uses a GP prior to provide additional information. The GP posterior is used not to define the primary optimistic objective (as in GP-UCB), but rather as a pruning mechanism. The algorithm proceeds by optimistically selecting promising regions based on observed function values (as in SOO), and then uses the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB) to prune away regions that are demonstrably worse than the best value found in a more refined partition. This hybrid approach allows the algorithm to benefit from the strong theoretical guarantees of optimistic optimization while leveraging the data-efficiency of a GP model. The paper directly addresses and solves an open problem stated by Wang et al. [2], who developed a similar hybrid (BaMSOO) but only achieved a polynomial regret bound.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, establishes an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The analysis provides valuable insights through the introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" `ρ_t`, which formalizes the cost of considering multiple bounds and clarifies how a useful GP model can improve performance by reducing this loss (Remark 1). The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against a strong set of baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on a diverse suite of benchmark functions. The results are convincing and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of IMGPO, which generally outperforms its competitors. The authors are also honest about the limitations, noting the degradation of the bound with increasing dimensionality.

*   **Originality:** The proposed method is highly original. While it leverages components from SOO and GP-UCB, the way they are combined is novel and insightful. The key conceptual leap is to use the GP not as the sole source of optimism, but as a tool to prune the search space of an "infinite-metric" optimistic algorithm. This avoids over-reliance on a potentially misspecified or loose GP bound while still benefiting from its ability to model the function globally. This work provides a clear and novel contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature, directly advancing the state of the art by solving a recognized open problem.

*   **Significance:** The results are very significant for both researchers and practitioners.
    1.  **Theoretical Advance:** It provides the first practical GP-based optimization algorithm with an exponential convergence guarantee, bridging a gap between theory and practice in Bayesian optimization.
    2.  **Practical Utility:** By eliminating the need for an auxiliary optimizer, IMGPO is a self-contained algorithm that is easier to implement and potentially more robust than standard GP-BO methods that depend on external solvers like DIRECT or CMA-ES for their acquisition function optimization. The experiments show it is not only theoretically sound but also performs very well in practice.
    3.  **Conceptual Framework:** The analysis provides a new way to think about combining different sources of knowledge (e.g., a formal model like a GP versus a general assumption of smoothness). The idea of using a model for pruning rather than direct optimistic estimation could inspire similar approaches in other bound-based search problems, as the authors suggest in their conclusion.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and the high-level overview and illustrative Figure 1 are excellent. However, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is quite dense and can be difficult to parse. Specifically, the logic for candidate selection and pruning across different depths (lines 8-24), involving nested loops and checks against `υmax`, is complex. A more detailed walk-through of these lines in the text, perhaps connecting them more explicitly to the example in Figure 1, could improve reproducibility and understanding for readers trying to implement the algorithm. The introduction and usage of `Ξ` and `Ξmax` could also be explained more intuitively.

*   **Algorithm Complexity:** The algorithm itself is fairly complex, with several new hyperparameters (`Ξmax`, `η`). While the authors provide settings used in the experiments, a sensitivity analysis or more guidance on how to set these parameters would be valuable for practitioners. For instance, how does the performance degrade if `Ξmax` is set too low or too high?

**Arguments Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   Presents a significant theoretical breakthrough by achieving an exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm.
*   Solves a clearly stated open problem from prior work in NIPS/AISTATS.
*   The core idea is novel, elegant, and provides a new perspective on combining model-based and optimistic optimization methods.
*   Strong empirical results on a variety of benchmarks validate the theoretical claims and practical utility of the algorithm.
*   The paper is well-structured and clearly written, despite the complexity of the algorithm itself.

**Con:**
*   The algorithm's implementation details are complex and the pseudocode is challenging to fully grasp without significant effort.
*   Limited discussion on setting the new hyperparameters introduced by the algorithm.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The strengths—a significant theoretical advance, a novel and practical algorithm, and strong empirical validation—far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to the presentation of the algorithm's complexity. This work is highly likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. It is a clear accept.
