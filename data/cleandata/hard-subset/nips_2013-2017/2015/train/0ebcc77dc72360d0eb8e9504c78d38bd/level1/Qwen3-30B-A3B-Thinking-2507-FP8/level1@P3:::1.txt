

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two limitations of previous approaches: (1) GP-UCB [1] requires auxiliary optimization (solving an additional non-convex problem), and (2) the exponential convergence result by de Freitas et al. [1] requires impractical δ-cover sampling. IMGPO's key insight is to consider an infinite set of possible bounds (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior, which they formalize as "infinite-metric exploration."

The paper builds upon several important lines of work: de Freitas et al.'s exponential convergence result [1], Wang et al.'s BaMSOO [2] (which combines SOO with GP-UCB but achieves only polynomial regret), and Munos' SOO algorithm [18] that uses hierarchical partitioning for global optimization. It also relates to the DIRECT algorithm [19], which considers multiple bounds but lacks theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 (Theorem 1), which is an exponential improvement over BaMSOO's polynomial regret. The analysis is sound and includes insightful remarks (Remarks 1-3) that explain when the infinite-metric approach is beneficial, even when the GP bound is not tight.

2. **Practical Impact:** The authors successfully eliminate two practical limitations (auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling) that have hindered previous methods. The experimental results (Figure 2) consistently show IMGPO outperforming BaMSOO, SOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across multiple test functions.

3. **Novel Perspective:** The paper provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding when it's beneficial to consider multiple bounds rather than committing to a single one. This insight could extend beyond Bayesian optimization to other bound-based methods like A* search and PAC-MDP algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Algorithmic Complexity:** The algorithm description (Section 3.2-3.3) is quite complex. The hierarchical partitioning procedure (Algorithm 1) has multiple nested loops and conditional checks that make it difficult to understand the core innovation. The paper could benefit from a simpler explanation of why the specific partitioning scheme is necessary.

2. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't adequately explain how to choose the key parameter Ξmax. While they mention it's fixed to 22 in experiments, they don't provide guidance on selecting it for different problems. The effect of Ξmax on performance is only briefly discussed in the experiments (Section 5).

3. **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality D increases, suggesting scalability limitations. However, they don't provide concrete strategies for addressing this challenge in higher dimensions (beyond mentioning REMBO [14] for a 1000-dimensional test case).

4. **Comparison with GP-UCB:** The paper claims GP-UCB requires auxiliary optimization, but this is not entirely accurate. While GP-UCB does require optimizing the UCB acquisition function, this is a standard practice in Bayesian optimization. The paper could better clarify the practical differences between the two approaches.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are convincing, and the insights about the trade-offs between using a single bound versus considering multiple bounds are valuable. 

The main concerns are the algorithm's complexity and the lack of clear guidance on parameter selection. However, these are common challenges in theoretical contributions that provide new algorithmic frameworks. The paper's contribution is clearly novel compared to previous NIPS work (e.g., [1, 2, 18, 19]) and represents a meaningful advance in Bayesian optimization theory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- First to achieve exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear regret bound
- Consistent empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Novel theoretical insights about the value of considering multiple bounds

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Algorithm complexity may hinder practical implementation
- Limited guidance on parameter selection (Ξmax)
- Scalability to high dimensions remains a challenge

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It addresses a well-known limitation of previous methods while providing a theoretically sound solution. The theoretical framework for understanding when multiple bounds are beneficial is particularly valuable and could influence future work in optimization. While the algorithm's complexity is a drawback, the theoretical significance and empirical performance make this a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper represents a significant theoretical advance in Bayesian optimization with clear practical implications. The novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation meet all NIPS quality standards.
