Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm that attains an exponential simple regret rate without requiring (i) auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function or (ii) δ-cover sampling. The method combines hierarchical partitioning (in the spirit of DOO/SOO [18]) with GP-derived upper confidence bounds (UCBs) as filters. A key conceptual device is the “infinite-metric” view: assume existence of an unknown semi-metric ‘ bound around a global optimizer (Assumption 1), with regularity `(x′,x) ≤ L||x′−x||p^α (Assumption 2), but do not commit to a specific metric; instead, explore all candidate bounds implicitly via a depth-wise selection and pruning strategy. GP UCBs are used to (a) skip function evaluations when the local UCB is already suboptimal and (b) screen candidate rectangles at multiple depths. The algorithm adaptively divides hyperrectangles and may temporarily assign GP-based proxy values to avoid unnecessary queries (with a parameter Ξmax controlling lookahead breadth). The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) gives a high-probability exponential regret bound rN = O(λ^{N+Ngp}) with λ < 1, where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp counts GP-based proxy uses, with explicit dependence on dimension D, ρ̄t (the “infinite-metric exploration loss”), and constants L, α, p. The analysis contrasts with BaMSOO [2], which yields only polynomial rates, and with exponential bounds for GP bandits with δ-cover sampling [1]. Empirically, IMGPO is compared against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on standard benchmarks, typically outperforming alternatives and avoiding auxiliary acquisition optimization.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Formalization of “infinite-metric” exploration and the exploration-loss ρ̄t provides a clear lens to analyze trade-offs between not committing to a specific Lipschitz/semi-metric and leveraging GP information. This bridges two lines of work (DIRECT/SOO vs GP-UCB).
- Theoretical contribution: An explicit exponential simple regret bound without δ-cover sampling or acquisition optimization is a notable advance relative to BaMSOO [2] and the δ-cover-based result of [1]. The bound is informative in how L, α, p, D, ρ̄t, and Ξn affect convergence, and the remarks clarify when GP-derived UCBs help.
- Algorithmic design: Avoiding auxiliary global optimization simplifies implementation and reduces overhead commonly seen in GP-based BO. The hierarchical partitioning and GP-based skipping are practical and well-motivated.
- Empirical results: Across several deterministic benchmarks, IMGPO generally shows faster regret reduction than baselines, with reasonable default kernel/hyperparameter choices and evidence of robustness.

Weaknesses:
- Confidence bounds under hyperparameter learning: The guarantee f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with high probability typically requires fixed kernels and assumptions on the RKHS norm of f (e.g., [15]). Here, hyperparameters are updated via empirical Bayes at every iteration, but the analysis does not clearly address how this affects the validity of the union bound and confidence parameter choice ςM. The robustness of the “with probability ≥ 1−η” statement under data-dependent hyperparameter updates is not justified in the main text.
- Dependence on ρ̄t and dimension: The exponential rate depends inversely on ρ̄t and a dimension-dependent constant; λ approaches 1 as D grows, and the paper acknowledges scalability limitations. While this is common in global optimization, practical guidance or data-dependent control of ρ̄t is limited.
- Counting Ngp in the rate: The headline rate is in terms of N+Ngp, where Ngp counts GP proxy uses, not function evaluations. While the remarks relate to N and ρ̄t, the main statement may overstate convergence with respect to the practically constrained resource (queries to f).
- Clarity and notational load: Algorithm 1 is dense. The definition and computation of T′h+ξ (full expansion to depth ξ) and the cost of step (iii) are only partially specified; tighter complexity bounds and clearer pseudocode for building T′ would improve readability. Some constants (e.g., C) are referenced to prior work without restatement, making it harder for readers to follow the proof structure.
- Experimental scope: While baselines include SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, the acquisition optimization for PI/EI is handled by SOO plus a local method, and kernel choices/hyperparameter settings are uniform but not tuned. A more thorough comparison (e.g., GP-UCB with strong multi-start optimizers, sensitivity to kernel misspecification, and ablations isolating the effect of Ξmax and GP screening) would strengthen claims. No noisy-function results are reported.

Quality:
- The technical development is generally sound within the stated assumptions. The main gap is the unaddressed impact of hyperparameter learning on UCB validity. The proof sketch (in the main text) is limited; full details are relegated to the supplement. Empirics support the method, but broader sensitivity analyses would be beneficial.

Clarity:
- The paper is mostly well-organized, with a helpful 1D walk-through. Some sections would benefit from clearer definitions, explicit constants, and computational complexity discussion. Minor notational issues and cross-references to prior constants slow comprehension.

Originality:
- The combination of infinite-metric reasoning with GP-based screening, and the resulting exponential-rate bound without δ-cover or acquisition optimization, appears novel relative to [1], [2], [18], [19].

Significance:
- If the theoretical guarantees hold under practical hyperparameter learning, the contribution is significant both theoretically (first such exponential-rate result under these conditions) and practically (eliminates auxiliary optimization). The impact is moderated by dimensional dependence and the determinism assumption.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical result (exponential convergence without δ-cover/auxiliary optimization).
- Clear conceptual framework (infinite-metric exploration loss) connecting GP and partition-based methods.
- Practical algorithm with promising empirical performance and open-source code.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient justification of high-probability UCBs under adaptive hyperparameter estimation.
- Exponential rate stated in terms of N+Ngp and with sensitivity to ρ̄t; practical guarantees in terms of function evaluations alone are weaker.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for step (iii), and moderate clarity issues.
- Empirical evaluation lacks stronger baselines/tuning and does not cover noisy settings.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a formal treatment (or explicit assumptions) ensuring valid confidence bounds when hyperparameters are updated from data; alternatively, report ablations with fixed hyperparameters to align with theory.
- Include time complexity analysis of step (iii) and the overall per-iteration cost; report wall-clock time alongside regret.
- Add ablations for Ξmax, kernel/hyperparameter choices, and the contribution of GP screening vs pure SOO to quantify the role of Ngp and ρ̄t.
- Clarify constants (C, β, etc.) within the paper and streamline Algorithm 1 pseudocode for readability.
- Extend experiments to noisy observations and higher-dimensional settings with structured priors, or clarify the scope to deterministic problems.
