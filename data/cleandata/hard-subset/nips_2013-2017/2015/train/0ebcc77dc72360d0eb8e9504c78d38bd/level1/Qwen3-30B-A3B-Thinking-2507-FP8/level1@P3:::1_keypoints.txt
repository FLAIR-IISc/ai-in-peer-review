- Introduces IMGPO, a new Bayesian optimization algorithm claiming exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ‑cover sampling.  
- Key idea: "infinite‑metric exploration" — consider an infinite set of possible bounds (semi‑metrics) instead of a single GP prior bound.  
- Builds on prior work: de Freitas et al.'s exponential result, Wang et al.'s BaMSOO, Munos' SOO, and relates to DIRECT.  
- Theoretical contribution: proves regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 (Theorem 1), giving exponential improvement over BaMSOO.  
- Analysis includes insightful remarks explaining when infinite‑metric approach helps even with loose GP bounds.  
- Practical impact: removes need for auxiliary optimization and δ‑cover sampling, addressing two major limitations of prior methods.  
- Empirical results: IMGPO consistently outperforms BaMSOO, SOO, GP‑PI, and GP‑EI across test functions.  
- Novel perspective: provides a framework for when multiple bounds outperform committing to a single bound, with potential broader applicability.  
- Weakness — algorithmic complexity: hierarchical partitioning and nested loops make the algorithm hard to understand and implement.  
- Weakness — parameter sensitivity: lack of clear guidance for choosing Ξmax (fixed to 22 in experiments) and limited discussion of its effect.  
- Weakness — scalability: λ approaches 1 as dimension D grows, raising concerns for high‑dimensional problems and lacking concrete remedies.  
- Weakness — comparison with GP‑UCB: claim that GP‑UCB requires auxiliary optimization is overstated; the paper could better clarify practical differences.  
- Overall assessment: significant theoretical advance with rigorous analysis and convincing empirical support despite complexity and parameter/scalability concerns.  
- Arguments for acceptance: first GP‑based method with exponential convergence sans auxiliary optimization/δ‑cover, rigorous theory, strong empirical gains, and novel insights.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical implementation difficulty due to complexity, insufficient parameter guidance, and unclear high‑dimensional scaling.  
- Recommendation: Accept — paper is a meaningful and novel contribution to Bayesian optimization with strong theory and experiments.
