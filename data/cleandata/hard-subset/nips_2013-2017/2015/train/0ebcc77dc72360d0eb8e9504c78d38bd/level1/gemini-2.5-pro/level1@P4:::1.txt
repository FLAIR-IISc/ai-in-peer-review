### Summary

This paper introduces the Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) algorithm, a novel method for the global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The primary contribution is a Gaussian Process (GP)-based optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate for simple regret. Critically, it does so without two major encumbrances of previous methods: it does not require an auxiliary non-convex optimization to select the next point, and it avoids the impractical `δ-cover` sampling procedure required by prior work [1] that also claimed exponential convergence.

The proposed method integrates ideas from optimistic optimization algorithms like SOO [18], which use hierarchical partitioning of the search space, with the probabilistic models of Bayesian optimization. Instead of using the GP's Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) to directly select the next evaluation point (as in GP-UCB), IMGPO uses it as a mechanism to prune sub-regions of the search space that are unlikely to contain the global optimum. The core search strategy is driven by the optimistic principle of expanding regions with the current best observed values, implicitly accounting for an infinite family of potential function smoothness profiles. This hybrid approach leads to a theoretically-grounded algorithm with strong empirical performance.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, provides a proof of an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` where `λ < 1`. This is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of the most closely related practical algorithm, BaMSOO [2]. The analysis is built on the established framework of optimistic optimization, and the assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2, concerning the existence of a semi-metric) are standard in that literature.

The authors provide a nuanced discussion of the theoretical bound in Remarks 1-3, clarifying how the algorithm's performance depends on the quality of the GP model (via the "infinite-metric exploration loss," `ρ̄t`) and why considering an infinite set of bounds can be advantageous over committing to a single, potentially ill-fitting one. The experimental section is well-executed, comparing IMGPO against relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on a diverse set of benchmark functions. The empirical results robustly support the paper's claims, showing that IMGPO is generally superior in performance.

### Clarity

The paper is well-organized and the motivation is clearly articulated. However, the clarity of the algorithm's description is a point of weakness. While the high-level, six-step textual description in Section 3.2 is helpful, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to follow. The logic involving multiple loops over the tree depth `h`, the nested `while true` loop (Line 9), and the conditions for screening candidate intervals (Lines 11, 23) is complex. A more detailed explanation or a step-by-step trace on a simple 1D example would greatly improve the reader's ability to understand and reproduce the algorithm.

Specifically, the operation in Line 21, which requires finding `max U(...)` over the centers of a "fully expanded tree" within a given hyperrectangle, appears computationally demanding. The text mentions this can be computed by recursive division, but the computational cost and its management via the `Ξ_max` parameter are not sufficiently detailed. Additionally, the update rule for the parameter `Ξ` (Line 38) seems heuristic, and its rationale could be better explained.

### Originality

The work is highly original. It addresses what was explicitly noted as an open problem in prior work [2]: creating a practical GP-based algorithm with an exponential convergence rate. The core idea—using a GP-UCB not for exploitation but for pruning within an optimistic, partition-based search—is a novel and insightful synthesis of two powerful approaches to global optimization. This work successfully bridges the gap between the DIRECT/SOO line of research, which avoids strong smoothness assumptions, and the Bayesian optimization line, which leverages learned statistical models. The paper correctly positions its contribution relative to prior art, especially its direct predecessors SOO and BaMSOO.

### Significance

The paper's contribution is significant for both the theory and practice of global optimization. Theoretically, it closes an important gap by providing a constructive proof of exponential convergence for a practical GP-based algorithm. Practically, IMGPO offers a compelling alternative to standard Bayesian optimization methods. By eliminating the auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function, it removes a major implementation hurdle and a potential computational bottleneck. The strong empirical results suggest that the algorithm is not just a theoretical curiosity but a high-performing method that researchers and practitioners would be likely to use. Furthermore, the conceptual framework analyzing the trade-off between model-based and model-free bounds (Remarks 1-3) is an important contribution that could inspire future work in hybrid optimization algorithms.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper solves a known open problem, delivering the first practical GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by a formal analysis, and the experimental results are strong and convincing.
*   **Practical Impact:** The proposed algorithm avoids the auxiliary optimization step common to many Bayesian optimization methods, making it simpler to implement and potentially faster per iteration.
*   **Conceptual Contribution:** The work provides a novel and effective method for combining optimistic search strategies with probabilistic models.

### Arguments against Acceptance
*   **Clarity of Algorithm:** The pseudocode and technical description of the algorithm are complex and could be made more accessible to facilitate understanding and implementation.
*   **Computational Details:** The cost of key algorithmic steps, particularly the UCB maximization over sub-trees, is not fully analyzed.
*   **Parameterization:** The algorithm introduces new parameters (e.g., `Ξ_max`) whose tuning and sensitivity are not deeply explored.
