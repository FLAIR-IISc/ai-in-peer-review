This paper proposes a Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence that avoids both the auxiliary optimization step and the impractical δ-cover sampling required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to leverage the existence of an unknown semi-metric bound on the objective function, and to simultaneously explore multiple candidate bounds based on information from the GP prior. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and empirical results showcasing performance on several benchmark functions.

This work addresses a significant limitation of the previous theoretical result [1] by removing the δ-cover sampling requirement, which was a major barrier to practical application. The motivation is clear and well-articulated, and the problem setting is standard in the Bayesian optimization literature. The connection to bound-based search methods and the exploration-exploitation trade-off is well-explained, placing the contribution within a broader context. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the provided regret bound is a valuable contribution. The experiments, while not exhaustive, demonstrate promising results and compare favorably to existing methods.

However, several aspects of the paper require further clarification and strengthening.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of simultaneously considering multiple bounds, coupled with the GP prior to prune unlikely candidates, is a novel and interesting idea. This elegantly circumvents the need for δ-cover sampling.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a clear and detailed theoretical analysis, demonstrating an exponential convergence rate under reasonable assumptions.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Removing the δ-cover sampling constraint significantly enhances the practical applicability of Bayesian optimization with theoretical guarantees.
*   **Clear Motivation and Explanation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem, motivation, and core ideas. The connection to related work is well-established.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results provide preliminary evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the theory suggests exponential convergence, the analysis reveals a dependence on the input dimension *D*. The parameter λ = 3 - α<sup>2</sup>CDρ̄<sub>t</sub> < 1 highlights that increasing *D* could push λ towards 1, diminishing the exponential convergence rate. This is acknowledged by the authors, but further investigation into techniques to mitigate this issue is needed (e.g., dimensionality reduction, exploiting structure in the problem).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of Ξ<sub>max</sub>, and the justification for its selection isn't entirely clear. A more detailed analysis of the impact of Ξ<sub>max</sub> on performance and convergence rate would be beneficial. The default value chosen (22) seems somewhat arbitrary.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while well-executed, are limited to a relatively small set of benchmark functions.  A more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of problem types and dimensions would strengthen the empirical validation. In particular, how does it perform when the GP prior is significantly mis-specified?
*   **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** While Figure 1 is helpful, the description of the algorithm, particularly steps (i)-(v), is dense and can be difficult to follow without careful reading. A more step-by-step explanation with illustrative examples would improve clarity. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is complex and could benefit from clearer comments and structure.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper acknowledges the higher computational cost compared to SOO. A more detailed analysis comparing the computational cost of IMGPO with other algorithms (e.g., GP-UCB, BaMSOO) would be valuable.  A complexity analysis would be helpful here.
*   **Remark 3 Discussion:** The discussion around Remarks 1-3, while insightful, feels somewhat disconnected from the core presentation of the algorithm.  Integrating these points more directly into the main body of the paper would enhance the flow and impact. The justification in Remark 3, particularly regarding ρ̃<sub>t</sub>, requires further unpacking for broader understanding.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to Bayesian optimization with exponential convergence, addressing a critical limitation of previous work.
*   The removal of the δ-cover sampling requirement significantly enhances the practical applicability of the method.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the algorithm.
*   The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate promising performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Scalability concerns related to the input dimension *D* raise questions about the applicability of the method to high-dimensional problems.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity and a lack of detailed analysis on hyperparameter selection could limit the robustness of the algorithm.
*   The limited experimental scope and computational cost concerns require further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The novelty of the approach, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis and promising experimental results, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. However, the scalability concerns and limited experimental scope warrant some caution. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically:

1.  **Address Scalability:**  Discuss potential strategies for mitigating the impact of dimensionality on convergence rate.
2.  **Hyperparameter Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of Ξ<sub>max</sub> on performance and convergence rate, and justify the chosen value.
3.  **Expanded Experiments:**  Include experiments on a wider range of problems and dimensions.
4.  **Algorithm Clarity:** Revise the algorithm description and pseudocode for improved clarity and readability.
5.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of IMGPO compared to other algorithms.
