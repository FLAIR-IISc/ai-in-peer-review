### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first to provably achieve an exponential convergence rate without relying on either an auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function or an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The problem of designing such an algorithm was explicitly stated as an open problem in prior work.

The algorithm cleverly hybridizes two major lines of research in deterministic global optimization. On one hand, it uses a Gaussian Process (GP) prior to model the objective function and construct an upper confidence bound (UCB), in the tradition of GP-UCB and related Bayesian optimization methods. On the other hand, it employs a hierarchical partitioning scheme reminiscent of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO, which do not rely on a known smoothness constant (e.g., a Lipschitz constant) but instead implicitly search over a range of possible smoothness assumptions. The key innovation of IMGPO is to use the SOO-like partitioning as the main search driver, while leveraging the GP-UCB to prune unpromising branches of the search tree, thereby accelerating convergence. This synthesis directly addresses the shortcomings of previous attempts: de Freitas et al. [1] achieved exponential convergence but required impractical δ-cover sampling, while Wang et al. [2] (BaMSOO) removed this requirement but only proved a polynomial convergence rate.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical result. Theorem 1 provides a proof of exponential regret for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm, a significant advancement that resolves an open question in the field. The analysis is insightful, particularly the characterization of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt), which elegantly quantifies the trade-off between exploring different smoothness assumptions and provides a clear theoretical justification for how the GP component helps (by reducing ρt).

2.  **Novel and Elegant Algorithm Design:** The core idea of integrating a probabilistic GP model into a deterministic hierarchical partitioning framework to prune the search is novel and powerful. It combines the data-efficiency of GPs with the robustness of partitioning methods. This avoids two major practical issues: the need to know or estimate a single smoothness constant (a weakness of Lipschitz methods) and the computational burden of maximizing a non-convex acquisition function at each step (a weakness of standard GP-UCB/EI).

3.  **Strong Connection to and Advancement of Existing Literature:** The paper is well-situated within the literature and clearly articulates its relationship to DIRECT, SOO, and BaMSOO. The analysis in Remarks 1-3 does an excellent job of dissecting the benefits of the "infinite-metric" approach versus relying on a single (potentially misspecified) bound, and how the GP component improves upon this. This provides valuable intuition that extends beyond the specifics of the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Supportive Empirical Results:** The experiments compare IMGPO against a strong set of baselines on a range of benchmark problems. IMGPO is shown to be highly competitive and often superior, lending practical weight to the theoretical claims. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO demonstrates a pathway for applying the algorithm in more challenging settings. The experiments are conducted under realistic assumptions (i.e., without hand-tuning kernels for each function).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The description of the algorithm in Section 3 and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 are dense and challenging to parse. The logic for selecting, keeping, and rejecting intervals (steps i-iii) involves multiple interacting conditions and variables (`υmax`, `i*h`, `ξ`, `z(h, i*h)`) that are difficult to track. The update rule for `Ξ` seems heuristic. A more detailed, step-by-step explanation of the logic, perhaps with a more complex worked example, would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Hidden Computational Cost:** The paper claims to avoid auxiliary optimization, which is a key advantage. However, step (iii) (line 22 in the pseudocode) requires finding the maximum UCB over a potentially large set of points `T'_{h+ξ}`, which are the centers of a fully expanded subtree of depth `ξ`. As the authors note, this can require up to `3^Ξmax` UCB evaluations in the worst case. While this is a discrete optimization, it can still be computationally expensive and this cost should be more explicitly discussed and analyzed. The claim of "no auxiliary optimization" should be qualified to reflect this.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The hierarchical partitioning at the core of IMGPO is inherently susceptible to the curse of dimensionality. The regret bound in Theorem 1 shows the base of the exponent `λ` depends on dimension `D`, meaning the convergence rate degrades as `D` increases. The authors acknowledge this, but the paper would be stronger if it included a more direct discussion of the practical dimensional limits of the core IMGPO algorithm, separate from its use within a framework like REMBO.

### Overall Evaluation and Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.

*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides the main result of an exponential convergence rate, which is well-supported.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the description of the algorithm itself is a major weak point in terms of clarity and could be substantially improved. The motivation and analysis, however, are very clear.
*   **Originality:** The proposed method is highly original. The synthesis of SOO-style partitioning with GP-based pruning is a novel approach that effectively addresses known limitations of prior work.
*   **Significance:** The results are important. It provides a new, practical algorithm with state-of-the-art theoretical guarantees. The conceptual framework of combining robust search strategies with probabilistic models is likely to inspire further research in optimization and other areas of AI involving sequential decision-making under uncertainty.

The strengths, particularly the novel theoretical breakthrough, far outweigh the weaknesses. This paper represents a clear advance in the state of the art. I strongly recommend acceptance. I urge the authors to use the camera-ready period to significantly revise Section 3 to improve the clarity of the algorithm's description.
