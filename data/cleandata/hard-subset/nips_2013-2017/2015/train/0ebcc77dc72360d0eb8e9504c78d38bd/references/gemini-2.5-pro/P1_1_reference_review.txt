This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first to achieve an exponential convergence rate without relying on either an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure or an auxiliary optimization of an acquisition function. The central problem is the global optimization of a deterministic black-box function, where the performance is measured by simple regret. The authors situate their work in the context of prior art, noting that de Freitas et al. [1] achieved exponential regret for GP optimization but required δ-cover sampling, and Wang et al. [2] removed this requirement in their BaMSOO algorithm but only achieved a polynomial regret bound.

The key idea of IMGPO is to combine two distinct approaches to global optimization. It uses the standard GP-UCB framework but does not rely exclusively on the UCB as the sole upper bound on the function. Instead, it simultaneously considers an infinite family of possible semi-metrics that could define a local upper bound around the global optimum (Assumption 1). This is inspired by algorithms like SOO and DIRECT. The algorithm uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. At each iteration, it performs a multi-stage selection process to determine which hyper-rectangle to divide. This process first uses function values to optimistically select candidates (implicitly searching over the space of unknown semi-metrics) and then uses the GP's UCB to prune candidates that are unlikely to contain the optimum. A clever mechanism is also introduced where the UCB value can be used as a temporary, "dummy" function value to avoid expensive function evaluations.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important open problem in the theoretical analysis of Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential convergence without impractical assumptions. The proposed solution is highly original. The synthesis of partitioning-based methods (which are agnostic to a specific function class) with model-based Bayesian optimization is a powerful and elegant concept. The idea of using the GP not as a strict bound but as a tool to prune the search over an infinite set of potential bounds is the paper's core conceptual contribution and is a significant departure from standard GP-UCB approaches.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a strong theoretical result (Theorem 1) that formally proves the exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, for the proposed algorithm. The analysis is insightful, particularly through the introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" `ρt`, which elegantly captures the cost of being agnostic to the true function smoothness. The remarks following the theorem are especially valuable, providing clear intuition on:
    *   How the quality of the GP's bound influences performance (Remark 1). Even with a useless GP, the bound is still better than prior work.
    *   The explicit benefit of using a GP (Remark 2) and of the infinite-metric approach itself (Remark 3). This analysis helps unify two previously divergent lines of research in global optimization.

3.  **Experimental Quality:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The algorithm is compared against strong and relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on a diverse set of benchmark functions. The experimental setup is realistic and fair, notably by using a standard kernel and a fixed initialization for hyperparameters rather than hand-picking them for each function. The results in Figure 2 clearly demonstrate that IMGPO generally outperforms its competitors. The authors are also honest about the algorithm's limitations, noting its performance on Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Complexity:** The main weakness of the paper is its clarity, particularly concerning the algorithm's description. The procedure described in Section 3.2 is complex, and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to parse. The nested loops and multiple filtering conditions (lines 8-24) make it challenging to build a clear mental model of the selection process. For example, the definition of `T'h+ξ(ch,i*h)` as a "fully expanded tree" within a sub-region is convoluted and could be explained more simply, perhaps with a small diagram. This high level of complexity could be a barrier to both understanding and reproduction.

2.  **Scalability with Dimension:** The authors correctly note that the constant `λ` in the regret bound can approach 1 as the input dimension `D` increases, which implies a degradation of performance in high-dimensional spaces. While they demonstrate a successful application by using IMGPO as a subroutine within REMBO for a 1000D problem, the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm remains a concern. A more explicit discussion of this limitation and the rate at which performance degrades with `D` would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new parameter `Ξmax`, which limits the lookahead depth for the UCB check. The experiments were run with a fixed value of `Ξmax = 22`. While the authors note that for some functions this limit wasn't reached, a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between performance and computational cost as a function of `Ξmax` would be beneficial. It is not clear how a user should set this parameter in practice.

### **Summary of Review**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It solves an important open problem with a highly novel and insightful approach. The theoretical analysis is strong and the experimental results are compelling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents the first GP-based algorithm with an exponential regret bound that does not require impractical sampling schemes or auxiliary optimization.
*   The core "infinite-metric" idea, which synthesizes model-based and partitioning-based optimization, is a novel and powerful conceptual contribution.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides deep insights into the behavior of the algorithm and the interplay between its components.
*   The experimental validation is strong, using fair comparisons against relevant baselines and demonstrating superior performance.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The algorithm's description and pseudocode are complex and difficult to follow, potentially hindering reproducibility.
*   The scalability of the core algorithm with input dimensionality is a potential weakness that is not fully addressed.

The strengths of this paper, particularly its significant theoretical and conceptual novelty, far outweigh its weaknesses. The clarity issues in the algorithm description can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
