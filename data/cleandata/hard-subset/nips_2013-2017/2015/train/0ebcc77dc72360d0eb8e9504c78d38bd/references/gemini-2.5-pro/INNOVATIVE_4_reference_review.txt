### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP) based Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first to achieve an exponential convergence rate without relying on impractical assumptions like δ-cover sampling or requiring a separate, costly optimization of an acquisition function. The central problem addressed is a well-known open question in the field: how to design a practical GP-based algorithm with theoretical guarantees of exponential regret. The authors' key insight is to combine two distinct lines of thought in global optimization. Rather than relying solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP, which may be loose, the algorithm simultaneously considers an infinite family of potential semi-metrics, an idea inherited from partitioning-based algorithms like DIRECT and SOO. IMGPO uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, but prunes branches using a clever two-stage process: one stage filters based on optimistic assumptions about an unknown "true" metric (à la SOO), while a second stage uses the GP-UCB to prune regions that are provably suboptimal. This hybrid approach allows the algorithm to fall back on a robust search strategy when the GP model is poor, but accelerate convergence dramatically when the GP provides useful information.

### **Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The most compelling aspect of this paper is its novelty. It directly and successfully tackles an open problem explicitly mentioned by prior work (Wang et al., 2012). The synthesis of the "infinite-metric" optimization paradigm from DIRECT/SOO with modern GP-based Bayesian optimization is a genuinely new and powerful concept. It isn't a trivial combination; the way the algorithm interleaves checks based on observed values and GP-UCB bounds is elegant and well-motivated.
2.  **Major Theoretical Contribution:** Providing the first exponential regret bound (O(λ^N)) for a practical GP-based algorithm is a significant theoretical breakthrough. This advances the state of the art from the polynomial bounds of prior work like BaMSOO. The analysis, particularly Remarks 1-3, provides a valuable conceptual framework for understanding the trade-offs between relying on an explicit (but possibly loose) model-based bound versus a more agnostic search strategy. This perspective is a contribution in its own right and could influence future algorithm design beyond Bayesian optimization.
3.  **Bold and Creative Approach:** The core idea—to "admit that the bound encoded in GP prior may not be tight enough" and to instead hedge by simultaneously considering all possible bounds—is a bold and refreshing departure from methods that wholly commit to the GP-UCB as the sole driver of exploration. This willingness to design a system that is robust to the failures of its own model is a hallmark of creative and impactful research.
4.  **Practical Advantages:** By embedding the exploration strategy directly into the partitioning scheme, the algorithm cleverly avoids the need to solve an auxiliary optimization problem to maximize an acquisition function (e.g., UCB or EI). This is a major practical bottleneck for many standard BO implementations, and its removal makes IMGPO computationally more appealing, as evidenced by the runtime comparison in Table 1.

### **Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity:** The proposed algorithm is quite intricate. The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 involves multiple nested loops, conditional checks, stateful labels ("GP-based"), and a dynamic, heuristically-updated parameter (Ξ). While the components are justified, the overall complexity may present a barrier to implementation, analysis, and adoption. A more intuitive, high-level discussion of the role and update rule for Ξ would be particularly helpful.
2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The authors are transparent about the algorithm's potential limitations in high dimensions, noting that the convergence rate `λ` approaches 1 as dimensionality `D` increases. The theoretical bound `λ = 3^(-α / 2CDρ̄t)` makes this dependency explicit. The 1000-dimensional experiment relies on embedding IMGPO within the REMBO framework, which confirms that the core algorithm is not intended as a standalone solution for high-dimensional problems. While this is a common challenge for BO methods, it remains a limitation.
3.  **Clarity of Computational Cost:** The description of the UCB check in step (iii) (line 21-23 of the pseudocode) is a bit opaque. The algorithm computes a maximum UCB over a "fully expanded tree" within a sub-region. While the search depth is limited by `Ξmax`, the practical computational cost of this step is not fully characterized. This seems like it could be a potential bottleneck, and a clearer analysis of its cost would strengthen the paper.

### **Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents a significant and exciting advance in the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. It solves a well-defined open problem with a highly novel approach that elegantly fuses two powerful optimization paradigms. The result is an algorithm with the strongest theoretical guarantees to date for a practical GP-based method.

While the algorithm's complexity is high and its direct scalability is limited, these are acceptable trade-offs for the conceptual leap and theoretical breakthrough it achieves. The paper's primary contribution is the introduction of a new algorithmic idea and the rigorous analysis that supports it. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-impact work that should be showcased at NIPS. It is very likely to inspire a new line of research into hybrid optimization methods.

I strongly recommend acceptance.
