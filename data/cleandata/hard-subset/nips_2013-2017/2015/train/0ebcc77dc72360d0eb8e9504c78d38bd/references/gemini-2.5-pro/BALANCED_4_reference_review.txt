**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for Bayesian optimization, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to solve global optimization problems for deterministic black-box functions. The primary contribution is a practical, GP-based algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret. This work addresses a significant open problem highlighted by previous research [2], which was to achieve such a rate without resorting to computationally impractical procedures like δ-cover sampling [1]. The proposed IMGPO algorithm cleverly integrates a hierarchical partitioning scheme, reminiscent of the SOO algorithm [18], with a Gaussian Process model. The key novelty lies in its cell selection strategy. Instead of relying solely on the GP's Upper Confidence Bound (UCB), IMGPO implicitly considers an infinite family of possible function smoothnesses (formalized as an unknown semi-metric in Assumption 1). It uses a multi-stage filtering process that first identifies promising regions based on observed function values at different scales (akin to SOO or DIRECT [19]) and then uses the GP-UCB to prune branches that are provably suboptimal. This hybrid approach avoids the need for an auxiliary optimization of an acquisition function at each step. The authors provide a theoretical analysis culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes the exponential regret bound `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` with `λ < 1`. Empirically, IMGPO is shown to outperform several strong baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, on a suite of benchmark optimization problems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Advance:** The paper successfully addresses a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization theory. Providing a practical algorithm with a guaranteed exponential convergence rate, improving upon the polynomial rates of comparable methods like BaMSOO [2], is a major theoretical contribution. The analysis is thorough and provides valuable insights through the introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (`ρ_t`), which helps characterize the trade-off between relying on a specific prior (the GP) and exploring more broadly.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Algorithm Design:** The core idea of IMGPO is both novel and compelling. It provides a principled way to fuse two distinct lines of work in global optimization: the Lipschitz-style partitioning methods that are agnostic to a specific smoothness constant (like SOO) and the model-based Bayesian optimization methods (like GP-UCB). The multi-step selection procedure (steps i-iii) is a clever mechanism for balancing exploration based on observed values (optimism about the unknown semi-metric) and exploitation guided by the GP model.

3.  **Practical Advantages:** Unlike many theoretically-focused BO algorithms, IMGPO is designed with practicality in mind. It circumvents two common practical hurdles: (a) it does not require the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure of [1], and (b) it avoids the potentially expensive sub-problem of optimizing a continuous acquisition function at each iteration, a common step in standard GP-BO. The latter is a significant practical benefit, as optimizing the acquisition function can be a major computational bottleneck.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is comprehensive, comparing IMGPO against a well-chosen set of state-of-the-art and relevant baseline algorithms (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI). The algorithm demonstrates superior or competitive performance across a range of functions with diverse characteristics and dimensionalities, lending strong empirical support to the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Algorithmic Clarity and Complexity:** While the high-level description is clear, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is quite dense and challenging to parse. The nested logic involving `υ_max`, the multiple loops over depths `h`, and the `while true` loop (line 9) could be clarified with a more detailed walkthrough or a simplified flowchart. Furthermore, the step for computing the UCB bound `z(h, i*_h)` (line 22) seems computationally intensive, as it requires maximizing the UCB over a "fully expanded tree" up to depth `ξ`. This appears to be a non-trivial discrete optimization problem. A discussion on the computational complexity of this step, and how it scales with `ξ` and `Ξ_max`, would be valuable. Clarifying how this cost compares to optimizing a standard acquisition function would strengthen the paper's claim of avoiding expensive auxiliary optimization.

2.  **Analysis of the Exploration Loss `ρ_t`:** The theoretical regret bound depends critically on the average infinite-metric exploration loss, `ρ̄_t`. Remark 1 provides some intuition, suggesting the bound degrades if the GP is "useless" and `ρ̄_t` grows large. This is a crucial point that deserves more exploration. It would be beneficial to provide more formal conditions or discussion on when a GP model can be considered "useful" in this context, i.e., under what conditions on the kernel and the objective function is `ρ̄_t` expected to remain small (e.g., constant or logarithmic in `N`)? This would make the theoretical result more concrete.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity and Justification:** The algorithm introduces a key new parameter, `Ξ_max`, and an adaptive scheme for a related variable `Ξ` (line 38). The paper fixes `Ξ_max=22` and notes that performance could be improved by increasing it, suggesting a trade-off. An ablation study showing the effect of `Ξ_max` on both performance (regret) and runtime would significantly strengthen the paper's practical guidance. Additionally, the specific update rule for `Ξ` seems somewhat arbitrary. Providing some justification or intuition for choosing `Ξ ← Ξ + 4` on success and `Ξ ← max(Ξ - 0.5, 1)` on failure would be helpful.

4.  **Experimental Setup:** The use of a single, fixed initialization for the GP hyperparameters across all functions might unfairly penalize the GP-based baselines, especially if the initial lengthscale (`l=0.25`) is poorly suited for some of the test functions. While hyperparameters are updated, BO can be sensitive to initialization. A brief comment on this choice and its potential impact would add nuance to the experimental conclusion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves a significant and clearly stated open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   Presents a novel algorithm that elegantly combines ideas from two different optimization paradigms.
*   Provides a strong theoretical result (exponential convergence rate) for a practical algorithm.
*   Demonstrates superior empirical performance against strong, relevant baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The description of the algorithm's implementation details and computational complexity could be clearer.
*   The analysis of the key term `ρ_t` in the regret bound could be more detailed.
*   The choice and sensitivity of new hyperparameters like `Ξ_max` are not fully explored.

**Decision:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The strengths, particularly the novel algorithm and the strong theoretical result that resolves an open problem, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily related to clarity and the need for additional analysis, which can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
