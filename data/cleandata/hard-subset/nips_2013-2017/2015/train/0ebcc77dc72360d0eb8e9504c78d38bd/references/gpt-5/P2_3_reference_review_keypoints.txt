- Paper proposes IMGPO: hybrid of SOO-style hierarchical partitioning and GP-UCB that hedges over infinitely many smoothness/semi-metric bounds.  
- Key idea: assume only a local Hölder-type bound at x* and use GP-UCB to prune clearly suboptimal regions without committing to a single bound.  
- Introduces "infinite-metric exploration loss" to quantify extra cost from not committing to a single metric.  
- Main theoretical claim: exponential simple-regret rate O(λ^{N+N_gp}) (λ<1) without δ-cover sampling or inner UCB optimization.  
- The framework clarifies when GP information helps via Ngp, average exploration loss ρ̄t, and depth Ξn, but pushes difficulty into these hard-to-control terms.  
- Major theoretical concern: empirical Bayes hyperparameter re-fitting undermines standard GP-UCB concentration guarantees used to safely skip evaluations.  
- Algorithm is clearly described with pseudocode, but step (iii) (max-UCB over expanded subtree up to ξ) has nontrivial computational cost and heuristic Ξ updates.  
- Proofs are relegated to the supplement; a short proof sketch or key lemma in the main text would strengthen the presentation.  
- Paper is readable and well organized, though some notation/constants and parameters (C, T′, Ξ, ςM) need earlier clarification and more intuition.  
- The "infinite-metric" hedging idea and explicit exploration-loss notion are novel relative to SOO/DOO, DIRECT, and BaMSOO.  
- Theoretical improvement (exponential rate) for deterministic BO is a nontrivial contribution, but applicability is narrower than noisy BO and scaling in D is a concern.  
- Empirical results show consistent gains over baselines on standard benchmarks and REMBO high-dimensional setting, with one case of GP mis-specification hurting performance.  
- Experimental fairness concerns: acquisition optimization details for GP-EI/PI, stochasticity of GP baselines vs deterministic partitioning, and lack of ablation isolating GP filter vs infinite-metric selection.  
- Computational overhead from step (iii) and U-evaluations is discussed qualitatively but lacks detailed wall-clock breakdown.  
- Pros: novel algorithmic combination, exponential regret bound under Hölder assumptions, insightful analysis of GP impact, solid empirical gains and code availability.  
- Cons: reliance on UCB validity with EB hyperparameter learning, uncontrolled key bound terms (ρ̄t, Ξn), heuristic/expensive step (iii) and Ξ updates, limited experiments (no noise robustness or ablations).  
- Questions for authors: formalize conditions under which EB preserves UCB guarantees; sensitivity analysis and guarantees for Ξ/Ξmax and adaptive stopping for step (iii); wall-clock breakdown of f-evals vs U-evals vs GP updates; behavior under small observation noise.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept (leaning accept) — novel and promising but needs clarifications on UCB/hyperparameter gap and more robust/empirical analysis.
