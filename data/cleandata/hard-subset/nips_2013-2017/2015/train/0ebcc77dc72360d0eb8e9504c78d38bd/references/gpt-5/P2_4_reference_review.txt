Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process–based global optimization algorithm that aims to achieve an exponential simple-regret rate without relying on the δ-cover sampling procedure required by prior work on deterministic GP bandits (de Freitas et al., ICML’12). The core idea is to blend two “optimistic” mechanisms: (i) a hierarchical partitioning search over all possible unknown Hölder-type bounds around a global optimum (“infinite-metric” idea, in the spirit of SOO/DOO and DIRECT), and (ii) GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCB) that are used to screen which cells to expand or to defer true evaluations by placing temporary UCB-based surrogate values. A new concept, the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt, quantifies the extra cost of not committing to a specific smoothness bound. The main theoretical claim (Theorem 1) is an exponential regret bound O(λN+Ngp) (λ < 1), where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp is the number of deferred GP-based “pseudo-evaluations”; the bound adapts to the best Hölder parameters (L, α, p) a posteriori. The method is compared experimentally to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on several low-dimensional benchmarks and via REMBO on a 1000D embedded problem; IMGPO generally performs best or near-best and is faster than conventional GP BO methods.

Quality:
- Positive: The algorithm is carefully motivated and described, with concrete step-by-step rules and pseudocode. The analysis introduces informative quantities (ρt, Ξ) to reason about the cost-benefit of using GP UCB to prune search. The overall structure (hierarchical partitioning; using GP UCB to avoid unnecessary evaluations) connects well to BaMSOO and SOO, and the proof strategy promises an interesting synthesis.
- Concerns on correctness/assumptions:
  - The UCB validity: The analysis relies on the event f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holding uniformly with high probability, with ςM = sqrt(log(π^2 M^2/(12η))). However, no explicit assumption is stated under which this holds (e.g., f is a sample from the GP prior, or f belongs to the RKHS of κ with bounded norm, as in Srinivas et al. 2010). With empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates, standard coverage guarantees typically do not hold without additional control. The paper even notes that the UCB property sometimes fails in practice. Since step (iii) uses U to reject intervals, the regret guarantee seems to depend materially on a condition that is not clearly justified.
  - Theorem clarity: Some notational issues obscure the result (e.g., λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t appears malformed; presumably λ = 3^{−α/(2 C D ρ̄t)} or similar). Several constants (C, ρ̄t, Ξn) and their interaction with dimension are only informally discussed, making it hard to assess tightness or typical regimes. The proof is deferred to the supplement; in the main text, the logical pathway from assumptions to the claimed exponential rate is not fully transparent.
  - Interaction with hyperparameter learning: Using empirical Bayes after each iteration likely breaks UCB concentration unless further assumptions/controls are imposed. This gap should be addressed in the theory or at least discussed carefully.
- Experiments: The comparisons are sensible, kernels/hyperparameters are fixed across methods, and runtime is discussed. However, important diagnostics are missing: how often and how much the GP screening reduces true evaluations (Ngp), empirical behavior of ρ̄t, and sensitivity to η and Ξmax. The REMBO result is promising but anecdotal; reporting success rates over random embeddings would strengthen the claim.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and well-organized, but several mathematical expressions have typos/ambiguities (e.g., λ expression; the role of ςM; the definition and computation of T′h+ξ). The description of step (iii) involves building a “fully expanded tree” in the region, which can be expensive; clarification on complexity and practical implementation would help.
- Assumptions 1–2: It would help to explicitly interpret Assumption 1 as Hölder continuity at x*, and to relate p, α to standard smoothness classes; currently the direction of the inequality and the semi-metric notion may confuse readers.
- The connection between the guarantee and practical choices (empirical Bayes, η, Ξ update rule) should be made more explicit.

Originality:
- The combination of SOO/DOO-style infinite-metric search with GP UCB screening is novel, beyond BaMSOO’s polynomial-rate result. The introduction of the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt and the way GP reduces it and increases Ngp is a nice conceptual lens.
- However, several components (hierarchical partitioning, use of GP UCB to skip evaluations) are close to BaMSOO; the claimed exponential rate is the key distinguishing contribution, which hinges on the aforementioned assumptions.

Significance:
- If the exponential regret guarantee under realistic and clearly stated assumptions holds, this would be a notable advance: a GP-based BO method attaining exponential rates without δ-covering and without known smoothness. The empirical results suggest practical gains over BaMSOO/SOO on standard tests.
- At present, the theoretical significance is tempered by unclear/missing assumptions for UCB validity and by notation/typo issues; the practical significance is promising but would benefit from deeper ablations and diagnostics.

Pros:
- Interesting and timely problem; addresses a known gap (δ-cover impracticality).
- Conceptually clean synthesis of GP UCB with infinite-metric search; new analytical lens (ρt, Ngp).
- Empirical results are competitive or superior across several benchmarks; code is promised.

Cons:
- Theoretical dependence on a high-probability UCB event is not supported by clear assumptions, especially under empirical Bayes.
- Notational and clarity issues in the main theorem; some constants and scaling with dimension are opaque.
- Limited experimental diagnostics; missing discussion of failure modes when UCB is misleading.

Questions/requests for authors:
1. Under what precise conditions on f and κ (GP-sample assumption? RKHS norm bound?) does your choice of ςM ensure f(x) ≤ U(x|D) uniformly with probability ≥ 1−η, especially with hyperparameters learned online? Please state this assumption clearly and adjust the constants accordingly.
2. Please correct and clarify the expression for λ in Theorem 1 and define all constants precisely in the main text. Provide intuition for typical magnitudes of ρ̄t and Ξn.
3. Can you report Ngp/N, empirical ρ̄t over iterations, and sensitivity to η and Ξmax? Also, report success rates in the REMBO experiment over multiple random embeddings.
4. What is the computational cost of step (iii) in practice? How large does Ξn get, and how do you implement T′h+ξ efficiently?

Minor comments:
- Several typos and notation glitches throughout (e.g., “the forth diagram”, “resolve the the”, malformed equations). A careful proofreading is needed.
- Cite HOO/DOO explicitly when discussing bounds depending on near-optimality dimension.
- Clarify the choice and optimization of acquisition functions for GP-EI/PI, and ensure baselines are competitive.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has an appealing idea and promising empirical results, and could be significant. However, the current version has gaps in the theoretical assumptions underlying the main claim and clarity issues in the statement of the result. With clearer, correct statements of assumptions/results and stronger diagnostics, I would be inclined to accept.
