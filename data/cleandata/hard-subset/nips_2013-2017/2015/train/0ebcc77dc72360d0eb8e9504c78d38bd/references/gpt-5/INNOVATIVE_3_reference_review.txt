Summary
This paper tackles deterministic global optimization of black-box functions via Bayesian optimization and proposes IMGPO, a GP-based algorithm that achieves exponential simple-regret decay without the impractical δ-cover sampling used by de Freitas et al. The key idea is “infinite-metric” optimization: instead of trusting a single (possibly loose) bound induced by the GP, the method assumes there exists an unknown semi-metric that upper-bounds suboptimality at the global optimum and then systematically explores as if all such bounds could be correct. Practically, IMGPO couples a hierarchical partitioning scheme (DIRECT/SOO style) with a GP-UCB filter that prunes intervals and defers expensive function evaluations by temporarily substituting UCB values. Theoretical analysis derives an exponential regret rate O(λN+Ngp) (λ < 1), with a decomposition that illuminates how GP information (Ngp) and the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρ̄t influence the rate. Experiments on eight benchmarks (including a 1000-D REMBO setting) show consistent gains over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI and GP-EI under a fixed Matérn-5/2 kernel with empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
- GP-UCB (Srinivas et al.) provides sublinear regret without δ-cover; de Freitas et al. showed exponential convergence under δ-cover. Wang et al. (BaMSOO) married SOO with GP-UCB for polynomial rates. DIRECT and SOO explore without a known Lipschitz constant. REMBO tackles high-D BO under embedding assumptions. This paper explicitly bridges the “bound-based” and “bound-free” lines: it keeps the optimistic tree search but uses GP UCBs as a selective, computationally inexpensive screening device, aiming to recover exponential rates without δ-cover.

Strengths
- Novelty: The “infinite-metric” viewpoint—using an unknown semi-metric upper bound while leveraging GP-UCB only to prune—is conceptually fresh and bridges two traditions in global optimization. To my knowledge, this is the first GP-based method claiming exponential convergence in the deterministic setting without δ-cover.
- Theory: Derives an exponential regret bound and cleanly dissects how GP affects both the effective sample count (Ngp) and exploration loss (ρ̄t). The remarks are insightful, explaining when relying on an estimated bound can be worse than simultaneously hedging over many possibilities.
- Algorithmic practicality: Avoids auxiliary global maximization of acquisition functions, often a practical bottleneck in GP-BO. The “dummy UCB” trick is simple and effective for reducing function calls.
- Empirics: Broad comparison (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI/EI) with consistent improvements on multiple multimodal functions; includes a REMBO scenario to hint at high-dimensional potential. Public code.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and generality: Assumption 1 plus Assumption 2 essentially impose Hölder continuity around the optimum. This is standard, but the paper should better discuss when Assumption 1 is nontrivial beyond Hölder/Lipschitz continuity, and whether the results reduce to known smoothness assumptions.
- Bound clarity: Theorem 1 uses λ = 3−α/(2CDρ̄t) (as written: “3 − α2CDρ̄t”), which is dimensionally and typographically unclear and could be a misprint. The connection from the exp(−α[·] ln 3) bound to O(λN+Ngp) with a constant λ < 1 depending on ρ̄t needs to be stated precisely (what is fixed vs. data-dependent?).
- Validity of UCB under hyperparameter learning: The correctness event f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with probability ≥ 1−η relies on a union bound over M adaptive calls, but GP hyperparameters are re-fit at each iteration by empirical Bayes. This typically violates standard GP-UCB concentration arguments unless extra care (e.g., priors over hyperparameters or sample-splitting) is taken. The paper should clarify the probabilistic guarantee under hyperparameter re-estimation.
- Computational complexity: Step (iii) may require up to 3^Ξ UCB evaluations; Ξmax = 22 is large. Theoretical and empirical runtime dependence on Ξ, D, and the tree depth should be characterized more explicitly. Table 1 suggests IMGPO is slower than SOO but faster than GP-EI/PI; a more detailed runtime breakdown (fraction of time in UCB queries vs. function evaluations) would help.
- Empirics: 
  - Missing GP-UCB baseline; GP-PI/EI optimization via SOO/local search might handicap them relative to strong global optimizers used in practice.
  - No DIRECT baseline, despite close methodological lineage.
  - Only noise-free functions; applicability to noisy BO is not discussed.
  - Limited sensitivity analysis (e.g., Ξmax, η, kernel choice), and little ablation on the impact of substituting UCB values vs. always evaluating.
- Notational clarity: Constants C, D, p, and β appear in the bound; their roles and typical magnitudes could be made crisper. Some notation and indices in Algorithm 1 (e.g., i∗h+ξ) are hard to parse on first read.

Quality
- The algorithm is well-specified and accompanied by code. The main theoretical claim is important, but the probability guarantee under hyperparameter learning and the exact constants in the exponential rate need clearer presentation to ensure soundness. Experiments are positive but could be strengthened with additional baselines and sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
- Generally readable, with a thorough motivation and useful remarks. However, some notation is opaque, and Theorem 1 has likely typos. Step (iii) and the construction of T′ could be explained more concretely. A short complexity section would improve clarity.

Originality
- High. The infinite-metric concept and the way GP is used as a pruning device rather than a single trusted bound feel genuinely new and potentially influential.

Significance
- High for the BO community. If the exponential rate holds under practical model learning, this could reposition how BO uses GPs in deterministic settings and inspire similar hybrids in planning/RL.

Pros
- Conceptually bold and novel; bridges two major lines of work.
- First GP-based exponential rate without δ-cover (deterministic case).
- Practical algorithm that avoids inner global optimization.
- Insightful theoretical remarks; public code; strong empirical trends.

Cons
- Some theoretical details are underspecified/unclear (λ expression, hyperparameter learning under UCB).
- Computational complexity of step (iii) and dependence on Ξ not fully characterized.
- Empirical baselines could be stronger/more complete; no noisy setting.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify Theorem 1: exact expression for λ, the roles of C and ρ̄t, and what is fixed vs. data-dependent in the O(λN+Ngp) statement.
2. How do you justify the UCB high-probability event when GP hyperparameters are re-estimated? Any empirical calibration or theory (e.g., marginalizing hyperparameters or using a prior) to preserve validity?
3. Can you report wall-clock breakdown and the number of UCB-only (dummy) uses vs. true evaluations, and how performance scales with Ξmax?
4. Why is GP-UCB not included as a baseline? Can you add DIRECT?
5. Any path to a noisy extension (e.g., sub-Gaussian noise)?

Recommendation
Accept (borderline-to-strong on novelty). The paper introduces a compelling, high-impact idea with promising theory and practice. Addressing the clarity issues in the theorem and adding a couple of baselines would make it even stronger.
