## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, dubbed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical guarantees (de Freitas et al. [1]). The core idea is to consider a broader range of possible Lipschitz-like bounds on the objective function, leveraging the GP as a way to filter out overly restrictive bounds, rather than relying solely on the GP's own uncertainty estimates. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and validate the approach empirically against existing methods.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly on the theoretical framework established by de Freitas et al. [1], addressing the practical limitation of δ-cover sampling.  It positions itself against Wang et al. [2], which offers a polynomial convergence rate through a combination of GP-UCB and the SOO algorithm.  IMGPO aims to surpass both by achieving exponential convergence without the restrictive sampling requirement or reliance on specific optimization algorithms like SOO.  The authors appropriately situate their approach within the broader landscape of bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*, PAC-MDP), highlighting the importance of tight bounds and the difficulties in obtaining them. They also correctly acknowledge the lineage back to earlier work on global optimization like DIRECT [19] and its connection to the concept of considering multiple bounds.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis establishing an exponential convergence rate, a significant advancement in GP-based Bayesian optimization.
* **Novelty of Approach:**  The concept of considering a wider range of possible bounds, guided by the GP’s estimates, is a genuinely novel idea that addresses a fundamental challenge in bound-based optimization.  It effectively shifts the focus from *finding* the optimal bound to *leveraging* the information encoded in potentially imperfect bounds.
* **Practical Relevance:**  Removing the reliance on δ-cover sampling makes the algorithm more applicable to real-world problems where this procedure is computationally prohibitive.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that IMGPO generally outperforms baseline algorithms on a variety of benchmark functions, reinforcing the theoretical findings. The authors acknowledge the dependency on well-tuned kernels/hyperparameters in the baselines and appropriately control for this.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.  Algorithm 1 is helpful in understanding the procedure.
* **Detailed Analysis & Discussion:** The paper includes insightful remarks regarding the impact of GP tightness, the benefits of infinite-metric optimization, and potential future research directions (scaling to higher dimensions, alternative division procedures).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The algorithm itself appears relatively complex, with multiple parameters (Ξmax, η) and a hierarchical partitioning scheme that might be challenging to implement and tune in practice.  While the pseudocode helps, a clearer explanation of the interplay between the different components would be beneficial.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  While the paper addresses the lack of δ-cover sampling, the theoretical analysis highlights the potential scalability issues in high dimensions (as λ approaches 1). The experiments only reach 1000 dimensions using REMBO, and further investigation into methods to mitigate this limitation is needed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of parameters like Ξmax is not fully explored. The authors mention that increasing Ξmax can improve performance, but this comes at a computational cost, and a more thorough analysis of this trade-off would be valuable.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:**  The computational cost of IMGPO relative to other methods (especially the baselines) is not thoroughly discussed. While the authors mention it's faster than traditional GP optimization, a more detailed comparison of running times would be helpful.
* **Dependency on GP Quality:** While the approach *reduces* dependence on the GP providing a tight bound, performance still relies on the GP providing *some* useful information for filtering the possible bounds. A more detailed analysis of the robustness of the algorithm to poor GP models would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with a novel approach to Bayesian optimization.
* The demonstrated exponential convergence rate is a major achievement.
* The removal of the δ-cover sampling requirement makes the algorithm more practical.
* The empirical results are encouraging and support the theoretical findings.
* The detailed analysis and discussion provide valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior and potential future directions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm’s complexity might hinder its adoption in practice.
* Scalability concerns in high dimensions remain a potential issue.
* Further investigation into parameter sensitivity and computational cost is needed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant advance in the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical validation is encouraging. While the algorithm’s complexity and scalability concerns are valid points, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors address the following points:

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm and its relative cost compared to baselines.
* Elaborate on the interplay between the GP and the hierarchical partitioning scheme, potentially with a more illustrative example.
* Conduct a more thorough analysis of the parameter Ξmax and its impact on performance and computational cost.
* Further discuss potential strategies to address the scalability concerns in high dimensions.
