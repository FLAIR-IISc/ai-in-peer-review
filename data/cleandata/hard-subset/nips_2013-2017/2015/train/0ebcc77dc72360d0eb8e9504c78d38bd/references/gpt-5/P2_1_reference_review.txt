Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles deterministic global optimization of a black-box, non-convex function using Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes (GPs). It proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a hierarchical partitioning algorithm that mixes two sources of “optimism”: (i) an unknown Hölder-type upper bound around the global optimum (formalized via an unknown semi-metric ` satisfying f(x*) ≤ f(x) + `(x,x*), further bounded by L||x−x′||_p^α), and (ii) GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCB). Rather than committing to any single bound with fixed constants, the method keeps all candidates implicitly (as in SOO/DOO) and uses the GP UCB to screen and prioritize partitions. The algorithm avoids δ-cover sampling (required by de Freitas et al. 2012 for exponential rates) and avoids an auxiliary inner optimization of the acquisition function (as in GP-UCB). Theoretical contributions include: (a) an exponential simple regret bound of the form O(λ^{N+N_gp}) under the unknown Hölder regularity, where N is the number of true evaluations and N_gp counts skipped evaluations replaced by GP-UCB; (b) an analysis introducing “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρ_t to quantify how not committing to a specific bound affects data efficiency; (c) remarks that disentangle the roles of GP screening (via N_gp and reduced ρ̄_t) and the sensitivity to dimension D. Empirically, IMGPO is compared against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI and GP-EI on standard benchmarks, generally outperforming them, and a REMBO+IMGPO demonstration in 1000D is included. The work builds on and bridges DIRECT (Jones et al.), DOO/SOO (Munos), GP-UCB (Srinivas et al.), deterministic GP bandits with δ-cover (de Freitas et al.), and BaMSOO (Wang et al.).

Quality
- Theory: The central bound (Theorem 1) is technically interesting: it achieves an exponential rate without δ-cover by combining unknown Hölder continuity (Assumptions 1–2) with GP-based screening. The factors ρ̄_t and Ξ_n make explicit the algorithmic overhead. However, the guarantee relies on the UCB property f(x) ≤ μ(x|D) + ς_M σ(x|D) holding uniformly over all U calls with probability ≥ 1−η. This typically requires either (a) f drawn from the assumed GP with fixed, known hyperparameters, or (b) RKHS-type assumptions with a β_t schedule depending on information gain; neither is stated precisely. In contrast, the algorithm updates hyperparameters by empirical Bayes, which can invalidate the high-probability UCB coverage and hence the proof. The authors even note that in some experiments the UCB property failed (“misleading prior”), underscoring the gap between the stated guarantee and the implemented procedure. This needs a clear statement of assumptions for the theorem and a discussion of what is guaranteed when hyperparameters are learned online.
- Details/typos: The expression “λ = 3 − α/(2 C D ρ̄_t) < 1” appears dimensionally odd; likely λ = 3^{−α/(2 C D ρ̄_t)}. Please check and fix. The role of Ξ_n in the bound is explicit, but no upper bound on Ξ_n is provided; the update rule is heuristic and can increase computation. It would help to either bound Ξ_n (worst-case or typical) or show its empirical scaling.
- Algorithmic complexity: Step (iii) requires expanding a local full subtree to depth ξ per candidate and computing many U calls; you bound by Ξ_max but do not analyze wall-clock scaling vs N beyond a small table. A more explicit complexity discussion (in terms of D, Ξ_max, depth(T), number of U calls M) would strengthen the work.
- Experiments: The benchmarks are standard, code is available, and IMGPO is usually strongest. However, (i) GP-PI/EI acquisition optimization setup is nonstandard (SOO + gradient local search); clarify to ensure fairness. (ii) There is no ablation on the effect of GP screening vs infinite-metric exploration (e.g., IMGPO without GP, or with perfect vs misspecified kernels). (iii) No robustness tests to kernel/hyperparameter misspecification, despite the theory’s reliance on UCB coverage. (iv) No noisy setting—an important regime for BO in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable, with a helpful walkthrough of the 1D example and pseudocode. Some notation is overloaded (n, N, t) and the sets T′ and the construction at line 21 could be explained more compactly. Please correct the suspected typo in λ, define all constants when first introduced (C), and state the exact conditions under which f ≤ U holds in your analysis (fixed kernel? GP sample? RKHS norm bound?).

Originality
The core idea—marrying SOO-style infinite-metric partitioning with GP-UCB screening to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover—appears novel. The “infinite-metric exploration loss” is a useful conceptual tool to reason about the trade-off of not committing to a bound. The work is clearly differentiated from BaMSOO (polynomial rates) and from δ-cover-based deterministic GP bandits. While the smoothness model is a standard Hölder-type assumption localized at x*, leveraging it in a GP-based method this way is a new and valuable contribution.

Significance
If the exponential-rate guarantee can be solidified under realistic GP assumptions (and/or if the practical algorithm can be shown robust to hyperparameter learning), this would be a notable advance for deterministic BO and branch-and-bound-style methods, eliminating the δ-cover requirement and inner acquisition optimization. The empirical improvements are promising, and the framework could influence the design of hybrid optimistic methods.

Pros
- Conceptually clean hybrid of GP-based optimism and infinite-metric exploration.
- Exponential regret bound under unknown Hölder regularity, without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization.
- Useful analytical constructs (ρ̄_t, role of N_gp, impact of D).
- Competitive to strong empirical results; public code.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantee appears to require fixed, correct GP assumptions; hyperparameter learning undermines UCB coverage. Needs explicit assumptions and discussion.
- Dependence on ρ̄_t and Ξ_n makes the bound data-structure/heuristic-dependent; no bounds or diagnostics for these quantities are provided.
- Computational overhead of step (iii) and many U calls not fully analyzed.
- Experimental fairness/details for PI/EI optimization and lack of ablations/robustness tests.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- State precisely the conditions under which f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holds in your analysis. Does Theorem 1 assume known, fixed kernel hyperparameters and f drawn from the GP (or an RKHS norm bound)? What remains valid under empirical Bayes?
- Fix the expression for λ and list all constants (C) and their values under your division scheme.
- Provide empirical traces of ρ̄_t, N_gp, and Ξ_n versus N on a few problems to illustrate their behavior and support the remarks.
- Clarify and justify the acquisition optimization setup for GP-PI/EI; consider including a standard multi-start L-BFGS baseline.
- Add an ablation: IMGPO without GP screening; and/or with a well-specified vs misspecified kernel to quantify robustness.
- Discuss or extend to noisy observations, or clearly scope the contribution to deterministic settings.
- Expand the complexity analysis (number of U calls M, wall-clock scaling with Ξ_max and D).

Overall recommendation
This is a promising and innovative paper with a strong idea and potentially impactful theory. I lean toward acceptance if the theoretical assumptions are clarified and the empirical evaluation is strengthened as above; otherwise, the gap between the stated guarantees and the implemented, hyperparameter-learning algorithm should be addressed.
