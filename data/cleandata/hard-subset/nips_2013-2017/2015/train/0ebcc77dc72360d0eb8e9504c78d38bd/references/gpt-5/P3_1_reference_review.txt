Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian-process-based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions. The key idea is to decouple “optimism” from reliance on a single, potentially loose bound (e.g., GP-UCB), by simultaneously considering an infinite family of unknown upper bounds induced by continuity at the global optimizer. Practically, IMGPO uses a hierarchical partitioning (DIRECT-/SOO-style) over hyperrectangles, selecting intervals to expand via (i) center-value comparisons across depths (to reflect unknown Hölder-type bounds) and (ii) a GP-UCB test to prune intervals whose maximal UCB within a limited-depth subtree cannot beat the best known value. A central technical device is the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt, quantifying the cost of not committing to a single known bound. Theoretical analysis claims an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) (λ<1), where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp the number of times IMGPO substitutes UCB values for true evaluations. Empirical results on standard benchmarks suggest that IMGPO often outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-EI/PI without auxiliary acquisition optimization.

This work builds on: (1) δ-cover exponential regret for deterministic GP bandits (de Freitas et al.), (2) BaMSOO (Wang et al.), which merges SOO and GP-UCB to obtain polynomial rates without δ-cover, (3) optimistic partition methods (DIRECT, DOO/SOO). The claimed novelty is achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover and without auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The “infinite-metric” perspective is an original and interesting way to blend partition-based optimism (SOO/DOO/DIRECT) with GP posterior information, yielding a principled way to benefit from GP UCB even if the GP bound is loose.
- Theoretical framing: Introducing ρt to quantify the price of not committing to a particular bound is insightful, and the analysis relates regret to unknown Hölder constants (L, α, p) rather than presuming they are known.
- Practical algorithmic design: Avoids δ-cover and auxiliary acquisition optimization, which are both known practical hurdles. The skip-evaluation mechanism using UCB is appealing for expensive objectives.
- Empirical results: On multiple deterministic benchmarks, IMGPO is competitive or superior to strong baselines; code availability is appreciated.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Validity of the high-probability UCB guarantee under hyperparameter learning: The claimed property f(x) ≤ μ(x|D)+ςσ(x|D) “with probability ≥ 1−η” is critical for pruning and for the regret bound. However, the algorithm updates GP hyperparameters via empirical Bayes at every iteration. Standard concentration results for GP-UCB (deterministic or noisy) assume either known kernel hyperparameters or RKHS-norm/true-kernel assumptions; adapting hyperparameters from data typically breaks the uniform high-probability coverage unless additional assumptions are imposed. The experiments themselves note violations (Rosenbrock2), undermining the theorem’s practical applicability. The paper should either (a) provide a rigorous guarantee under adaptive hyperparameter learning or (b) clearly state that the exponential bound holds only under fixed, correctly specified GP priors (and limit experiments accordingly).
- The rate counts Ngp in the exponent: The main bound is in terms of N+Ngp, where Ngp is the number of GP-imputed (not evaluated) nodes. This is unusual: convergence rates in BO typically measure progress versus the number of expensive function evaluations. If Ngp can be large (and it can grow with the number of hypothetical subtree queries in step (iii)), the exponential rate may overstate practical progress. A bound solely in terms of N (or explicit control/upper bounds relating Ngp to N) would make the claim substantially stronger.
- Complexity of step (iii): The paper states that in the worst case step (iii) computes UCBs “3Ξmax times although it would rarely happen.” In a ternary partition tree, the number of nodes within depth ξ typically grows like O(3^ξ), not O(3·ξ). If the intended cost is actually exponential in ξ, setting Ξmax=22 would be computationally infeasible; if it is linear, the paper should carefully justify that only O(ξ) UCB evaluations are needed. This point is critical to both practicality and the interpretation of Ngp.
- Assumptions and constants: Assumption 1/2 reduce to Hölder continuity near the optimum, which is standard; however, the exponential rate’s base λ=3^{−α/(2CDρ̄t)} depends strongly on dimension and on ρ̄t, which itself can grow unfavorably when GP UCB is not tight. In high dimensions, λ→1 quickly, and the practical exponential contraction may be negligible. This is acknowledged but not empirically explored beyond a REMBO toy case.
- Probability statements and notation: The derivation of ςM=√log(π^2 M^2/(12η)) is not justified relative to known GP concentration results (which usually depend on information gain, kernel smoothness, RKHS norm, etc.). The use of M as “number of U calls” with union bounds is heuristic without addressing adaptive design and hyperparameter learning. Some notation and line references are inconsistent, and the role of Ξ, Ξn, and Ξmax in the proof is not clearly tied to computable quantities.
- Experimental evaluation: While the benchmarks are reasonable, the study omits DIRECT and GP-UCB with well-tuned acquisition optimizers and kernels, and lacks ablations that isolate the contributions of (i) infinite-metric selection vs (ii) GP pruning vs (iii) the Ξ schedule. Since the theory depends heavily on the usefulness of UCB (and thus on kernel adequacy), it would help to report how often the UCB test prunes, how Ngp scales, and sensitivity to kernel mis-specification.

Quality: The core idea is promising and the analysis is ambitious, but the theoretical guarantees do not currently match the implemented algorithm due to adaptive hyperparameter learning, and the inclusion of Ngp in the rate complicates the claim of exponential convergence in the usual evaluation budget. Step (iii) complexity needs clarification.

Clarity: Generally well written, with a good high-level narrative. However, several definitions/assumptions and the complexity of step (iii) require clearer statements; the probability guarantee needs conditions spelled out. Some typos and minor inconsistencies in notation/line numbers.

Originality: High. The infinite-metric perspective and its formalization via ρt, and combining it with GP-based pruning, provide a novel angle beyond BaMSOO/SOO/DIRECT.

Significance: Potentially significant if the theory can be made robust to practical GP modeling (or the claims are appropriately scoped) and if the computational aspects of step (iii) are resolved. The empirical improvements and removal of auxiliary optimization are attractive.

Suggestions for improvement
- State precisely the assumptions under which Theorem 1 holds (fixed kernel/hyperparameters; f a GP sample or in an RKHS ball), and either align the implementation with those assumptions or weaken the claims for the adaptive-hyperparameter setting.
- Provide bounds that depend only on N, or upper bound Ngp in terms of N and Ξ, and give a precise, correct complexity analysis of step (iii) (is it O(ξ) or O(3^ξ)?).
- Add ablations for Ξ/Ξmax, and report Ngp and pruning rates across problems. Include comparisons to DIRECT and GP-UCB with strong acquisition optimization.
- Clarify the derivation of ςM and its relationship to known GP-UCB confidence sequences, or cite an appropriate result.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clear practical motivation.
- Ambitious theory that, if tightened, could impact BO for deterministic functions.
- Empirically competitive and avoids auxiliary optimization; code provided.

Cons for acceptance
- The main theoretical claim does not currently apply to the implemented algorithm (adaptive hyperparameters), and the “exponential” rate measures N+Ngp rather than N.
- Ambiguity/inconsistency in computational complexity of a core step.
- Limited ablations and missing baselines; sensitivity to kernel mis-specification not thoroughly addressed.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is close; with clarified assumptions/complexity, and theory aligned to practice (or claims carefully scoped), it could be a strong contribution. Confidence: high.
