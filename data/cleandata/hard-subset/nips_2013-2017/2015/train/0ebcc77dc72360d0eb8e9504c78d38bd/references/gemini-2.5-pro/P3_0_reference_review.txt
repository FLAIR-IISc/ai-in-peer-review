**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which achieves an exponential convergence rate without requiring impractical assumptions like δ-cover sampling or an auxiliary optimization loop for the acquisition function. The key idea is to synergistically combine two major paradigms in global optimization: the optimistic, hierarchical partitioning approach (exemplified by algorithms like SOO), which implicitly handles unknown function smoothness, and the probabilistic bound-based approach of GP-UCB. IMGPO uses the partitioning strategy to drive the search, while leveraging the GP-UCB criterion not to select the next point directly, but to prune unpromising branches of the search tree. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis that proves an exponential regret bound, thereby solving an open problem stated in prior work. The empirical evaluation across several benchmark functions demonstrates that IMGPO generally outperforms relevant baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-BO with EI/PI acquisition functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses and solves a well-defined and important open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature: achieving an exponential regret rate for GP-based methods without the computationally prohibitive δ-cover sampling procedure. The proposed method is highly novel in its synthesis of optimistic partitioning and GP-based pruning. Instead of using the GP to define a surrogate to be optimized, it uses the GP to make the underlying partitioning algorithm more efficient. This conceptual shift is a significant contribution that could inspire new hybrid optimization algorithms.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis. Theorem 1 formally establishes the exponential convergence rate, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which is a substantial improvement over the polynomial rates of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The analysis cleverly introduces the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (`ρ_t`) as a tool to quantify the number of partitions explored at each step, and compellingly shows how the GP-based pruning helps reduce this loss, thus tightening the regret bound. The remarks in Section 4 provide excellent intuition on the trade-offs between relying on a known (GP) bound versus an unknown (implicit) one, which is of broader interest to the optimization community.

3.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-conducted, comparing IMGPO against a strong set of relevant baselines on a diverse suite of test functions with varying dimensionalities and characteristics. The results convincingly demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The choice to not hand-pick kernels and hyperparameters for each function makes the experimental setup more realistic and the results more credible. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using IMGPO within the REMBO framework is a valuable addition, showing a path to scalability under suitable structural assumptions.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity and Clarity:** While the high-level intuition is well-explained, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and certain steps are difficult to parse, potentially hindering reproducibility and understanding.
    *   The `while true` loop (lines 9-17) for resolving "GP-based" (dummy) values is particularly opaque. A clearer explanation of its mechanics and termination conditions would be beneficial. It seems it could lead to repeated `arg max` computations at the same depth, and the computational implications are not discussed.
    *   The cost of computing the UCB over a fully expanded subtree (line 22: `max_{k:c_{h+ξ},k ∈ T'_{h+ξ}(...) } U(...)`) could be substantial, as it requires generating and evaluating the GP posterior at many points. While the authors state this is limited by `Ξ_max` and rarely happens, it is a core part of the pruning mechanism, and a brief analysis of its computational complexity would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scalability with Dimension:** The theoretical regret bound contains a term `C^D` (hidden in `λ`), which means the convergence rate degrades exponentially with the input dimension `D`. This is a common curse of dimensionality for space-partitioning algorithms and is a critical limitation. The paper acknowledges this briefly, but this point deserves more emphasis in the main text as a primary weakness. The 1000D experiment is promising but relies entirely on the low-effective-dimensionality assumption of the REMBO framework, which may not hold for general high-dimensional problems.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new key parameter, `Ξ_max`, which controls the depth of the UCB-based lookahead for pruning. The update rule for the adaptive `Ξ` (line 38) appears heuristic and its connection to the theory is not explicitly drawn. While the experiments mention that performance could be improved by increasing `Ξ_max` at the cost of CPU time, a more formal ablation study analyzing this trade-off would be very valuable for practitioners seeking to apply this method.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a significant advancement in the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. By elegantly combining optimistic partitioning with GP-based pruning, the authors have developed the first practical algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. The work is technically sound, novel, and addresses a clearly articulated open problem. Despite some minor weaknesses in the clarity of the algorithm's description and the inherent scalability challenges, the strengths of this work are substantial. It provides both a powerful new algorithm and a valuable analytical framework for understanding hybrid optimization approaches. This paper is a clear accept and is likely to have a strong impact on future research in this area.
