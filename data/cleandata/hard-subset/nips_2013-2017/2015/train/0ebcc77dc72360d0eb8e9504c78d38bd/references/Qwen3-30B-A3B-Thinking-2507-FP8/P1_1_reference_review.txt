

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure that was necessary in previous theoretical results. The authors address a key limitation in Bayesian optimization literature: the requirement of a tight Lipschitz constant, which is often unknown in practice.

The core insight of the paper is that instead of relying on the single upper bound provided by GP (which may not be tight), the algorithm simultaneously considers all possible upper bounds encoded in an unknown semi-metric ` (Assumption 1). The authors formalize this as "infinite-metric exploration" and prove that this approach can yield an exponential regret bound. This differs from previous work in several key ways:

- De Freitas et al. [1] achieved exponential convergence but required δ-cover sampling, which is impractical for high-dimensional problems.
- Wang et al. [2] proposed BaMSOO, which combines GP-UCB with SOO, but only achieved polynomial regret bounds.
- Munos [18] introduced SOO with a finite regret bound for the case of an unknown semi-metric, but without the exponential convergence.
- The DIRECT algorithm [19] considered infinitely many bounds but only had consistency (convergence in the limit) rather than finite regret bounds.

The authors position their work as bridging the gap between methods that use a specific bound (like GP-UCB) and methods that consider multiple bounds (like SOO), demonstrating that combining these approaches leads to improved performance.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proving an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) (λ < 1) without the δ-cover sampling requirement. This is a substantial improvement over previous results like BaMSOO [2] which had polynomial regret bounds.

2. **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The "infinite-metric" approach is conceptually fresh and provides a new perspective on how to leverage GP priors in optimization. The formal analysis of why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial (Remarks 1-3) is particularly insightful.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments on multiple benchmark functions clearly demonstrate that IMGPO outperforms state-of-the-art methods (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) in most cases. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., GP prior being misleading for Rosenbrock2).

4. **Practical Considerations**: The authors thoughtfully address practical implementation concerns (e.g., the Ξmax parameter, the use of isotropic Matern kernel with default hyperparameters), making their approach more accessible to practitioners.

5. **Honest Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges the dimensionality challenge (Remark 1) and discusses trade-offs between using GP and not using it (Remark 2), demonstrating scientific rigor.

## Weaknesses

1. **Algorithm Clarity**: The algorithm description (Section 3.2) and pseudocode (Algorithm 1) are complex and could benefit from more intuitive explanation. For example, the "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept (Definition 1) is introduced without sufficient motivation for readers unfamiliar with hierarchical partitioning methods.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the regret bound is exponential, the paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of IMGPO compared to baselines. It's unclear how the computational overhead of considering multiple bounds affects practical performance beyond the regret measure.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions that increasing Ξmax can improve performance for some functions (Section 5), but doesn't provide sufficient guidance on how to choose this parameter in practice. The effect of Ξmax on the regret bound (Remark 1) is not fully explored.

4. **Connection to Related Work**: The paper could better position itself against other "infinite" approaches. For instance, the connection to the work by Kandasamy et al. [20] on high-dimensional Bayesian optimization via additive models is not discussed.

5. **Implementation Details**: The authors state "the source code is publicly available" but the URL provided (http://lis.csail.mit.edu/code/imgpo.html) appears to be a placeholder rather than a working link.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis supported by experiments. The claims about the exponential convergence rate are well-supported by the proof in the supplementary material. The experimental setup is appropriate, using standard benchmark functions and comparing against relevant baselines. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding scalability in higher dimensions.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear section headings. However, the algorithm description could be improved with more intuitive examples and potentially a simpler pseudocode. Some technical terms (like "infinite-metric exploration loss") are introduced without sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with hierarchical partitioning. The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation would benefit from further elaboration.

## Originality Assessment

The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by demonstrating how to combine the benefits of GP with the flexibility of considering multiple bounds. While hierarchical partitioning has been used before (e.g., in SOO [18]), the specific way the authors integrate it with GP to achieve exponential convergence is original.

## Significance Assessment

This work addresses a fundamental limitation in Bayesian optimization theory (the δ-cover sampling requirement) and provides a practically applicable solution with exponential convergence. The exponential regret bound is a major theoretical advance over previous polynomial bounds. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods, making this work significant for both the theoretical and practical communities in optimization.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. Provide a more intuitive explanation of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept, possibly with a simple example showing how it differs from standard approaches.

2. Clarify the relationship between Ξmax and the regret bound in the main text (not just in remarks), perhaps with a small table showing how different Ξmax values affect performance.

3. Include a more thorough computational complexity analysis, comparing the runtime of IMGPO to baselines.

4. Fix the source code URL to a working link (or remove the placeholder URL).

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the algorithm might extend to constrained optimization settings, which is an important application area for Bayesian optimization.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Achieves exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling, a major theoretical advance
- Provides a clear theoretical framework for understanding why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple benchmark functions
- Addresses a clear limitation in the current Bayesian optimization literature
- The paper is well-written and technically sound

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- The algorithm description could be clearer for readers unfamiliar with hierarchical partitioning
- The computational complexity analysis is not thorough enough
- The parameter Ξmax requires careful tuning, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient guidance
- The source code URL appears to be broken

## Overall Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with clear practical implications. The exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling requirement is a major advance that addresses a key limitation in previous work. The paper is well-written, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over existing methods.

The minor weaknesses in algorithm clarity and computational complexity analysis are outweighed by the paper's substantial theoretical contribution and empirical validation. With the suggested improvements (particularly clarifying the algorithm and fixing the source code URL), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
