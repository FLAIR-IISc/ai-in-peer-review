

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work. The authors address a significant open problem identified by Wang et al. (2014) regarding creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence rate without impractical sampling procedures.

The key insight is the "infinite-metric" approach: instead of relying on the single bound provided by the GP prior (which may not be tight), the algorithm simultaneously considers infinitely many possible bounds encoded by an unknown semi-metric. The authors argue that even when the GP's bound is not tight, considering all possible bounds can lead to better performance than relying on a single bound. This represents a fundamental shift from traditional bound-based search methods like GP-UCB, which rely on a single upper bound.

The paper's theoretical contribution is significant: Theorem 1 proves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+N_gp)) with λ < 1, which is superior to the polynomial regret bounds of previous approaches like BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014). The analysis includes insightful remarks (Remarks 1-3) that clarify why the infinite-metric approach is beneficial even when the GP's bound is not tight.

The experimental results demonstrate that IMGPO outperforms several baselines including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on multiple benchmark functions. The authors provide a reasonable explanation for cases where GP-based methods underperform (like on Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading).

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel Concept:** The infinite-metric approach is genuinely novel and represents a bold new direction in Bayesian optimization. It fundamentally challenges the assumption that a single tight bound is necessary for efficient optimization.
2. **Theoretical Advancement:** The exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling is a significant theoretical achievement that addresses a known open problem.
3. **Practical Impact:** The algorithm is implemented and tested on standard benchmarks, showing clear practical advantages over existing methods.
4. **Theoretical Insight:** The paper provides a valuable framework for understanding why not relying on a particular bound can be advantageous, with implications beyond Bayesian optimization (e.g., to A* search, PAC-MDP algorithms).

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't thoroughly explain why the GP's bound is typically not tight enough to be useful by itself, which is a critical assumption for the approach.
2. The scalability for high-dimensional problems (beyond the 1000-dimensional example using REMBO) isn't fully addressed, though the authors acknowledge this as a limitation.
3. The paper could benefit from more detailed comparison with recent Bayesian optimization methods that have addressed scalability (e.g., REMBO, BoTorch).

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a genuinely novel approach to Bayesian optimization that achieves exponential convergence without impractical sampling procedures. The "infinite-metric" concept is bold and well-justified theoretically, with supporting empirical evidence. While there are some minor weaknesses in the presentation and the paper doesn't fully address high-dimensional scalability, these are outweighed by the novelty and significance of the core idea.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I find this paper highly compelling. The infinite-metric approach represents a fundamental shift in how we think about bound-based optimization, and the theoretical and empirical results support this novel perspective. The paper successfully addresses a well-known open problem in the field and provides a new framework for thinking about optimization algorithms.

The paper is well-written, clearly differentiates itself from prior work, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims. The paper's limitations are acknowledged and are not fundamental to the core contribution.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper introduces a significant new direction in Bayesian optimization with a novel theoretical framework and practical algorithm. The infinite-metric approach is bold, well-justified, and represents a meaningful advance beyond incremental improvements to existing methods. This is exactly the kind of novel contribution that advances the field and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
