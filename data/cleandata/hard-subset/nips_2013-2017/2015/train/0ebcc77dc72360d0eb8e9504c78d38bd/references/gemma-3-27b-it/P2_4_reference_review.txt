## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work (de Freitas et al., 2017). The core idea is to leverage the assumption of an unknown Lipschitz-like bound on the objective function, conducting both global and local searches based on all possible bounds, while using the GP to prune improbable candidates. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and empirical results showcasing superior performance compared to several baselines.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the work of de Freitas et al. (2017) by addressing the practical limitation of δ-cover sampling. It also relates to the broader landscape of Lipschitz optimization and Bayesian optimization, referencing relevant work on relaxation of Lipschitz constant assumptions (e.g., Bubeck et al., 2011), and recent GP-based approaches like those by Wang et al. (2018) and Snoek et al. (2012). The authors position their work as bridging the gap between theoretical results requiring impractical sampling strategies and practical, efficient algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate, a significant contribution.  The regret bound is clearly stated and analyzed.
*   **Practical Contribution:** Addressing the δ-cover sampling issue is a significant step towards making theoretically strong Bayesian optimization algorithms practically viable.
*   **Novel Approach:** The idea of simultaneously considering all possible bounds, guided by the GP, is a clever and potentially powerful technique for improving optimization performance. The exploration-exploitation trade-off appears well considered.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate their algorithm on a diverse set of benchmark functions, including those used in previous work (Munos, 2018), providing a good comparison with established methods.
*   **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.
*   **Availability of Code:** Providing publicly available code (http://lis.csail.mit.edu/code/imgpo.html) promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** While achieving exponential convergence is impressive, the analysis highlights potential scalability issues with increasing input dimensionality (as λ approaches 1).  The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion on mitigation strategies (e.g., leveraging dimensionality reduction techniques or different kernel choices) would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm has several parameters (e.g., Ξmax, η) that may require careful tuning. The paper provides some guidance, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be helpful.  The impact of these parameters on performance is discussed, but a practical guide for selecting appropriate values is missing.
*   **GP Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors mention using an empirical Bayesian method for GP hyperparameter tuning, but details are sparse.  The choice of a fixed initial hyperparameter configuration is not ideal and might limit performance in some cases.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** Comparing against more modern Bayesian Optimization methods that incorporate techniques like multi-fidelity optimization or meta-learning would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to Bayesian optimization with a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   It addresses a significant practical limitation of previous work by eliminating the need for δ-cover sampling.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   The code release promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability concerns related to high dimensionality could limit the applicability of the algorithm.
*   The potential sensitivity to parameter tuning requires further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, the proposed algorithm is novel and practical, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. While the scalability concerns and parameter sensitivity are valid points, they do not outweigh the overall merits of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept (Strongly)**. The paper meets the NIPS reviewing criteria well, particularly regarding quality, originality, and significance.  The clarity is good, and the authors have addressed a significant open problem in the field.  Addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above could further strengthen the paper, but it is already of high quality and deserves to be presented at NIPS.
