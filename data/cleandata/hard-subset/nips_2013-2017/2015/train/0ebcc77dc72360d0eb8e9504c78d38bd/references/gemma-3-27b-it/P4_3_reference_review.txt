This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results (de Freitas et al. [1]). The core idea is to simultaneously consider a wide range of potential bounds on the objective function, leveraging the GP to prune implausible bounds and guide the search. This contrasts with traditional GP-UCB methods which rely on a single, often loose, bound. The paper establishes a theoretical convergence rate and demonstrates empirical performance on several benchmark functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a theoretical guarantee of exponential convergence, a significant result in Bayesian optimization. Overcoming the δ-cover sampling limitation of previous work [1] is a substantial step.
* **Novelty:** The concept of simultaneously considering multiple bounds (implicitly represented by the infinite-metric optimization framework) is a novel approach to GP-based optimization.  It addresses a known weakness of GP-UCB – the potential for overly conservative exploration due to imprecise bounds.
* **Clarity of Explanation:** The algorithm's description, particularly the interplay between hierarchical partitioning and the UCB, is reasonably clear and well-illustrated with Figure 1. The core idea of balancing GP-informed bounds with an unknown, potentially tighter bound is conveyed effectively.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate IMGPO's effectiveness across a range of benchmark functions, showcasing its competitive performance compared to established algorithms like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-EI/PI methods. The analysis highlighting the impact of Ξmax provides valuable insight.
* **Connections to Prior Work:** The paper carefully positions its contribution within the broader context of bound-based search methods and highlights its relationship to previous results, including those of de Freitas et al. [1], Wang et al. [2], and Munos [18].

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Scalability Concerns:** While theoretically sound, the algorithm’s implementation appears complex. The nested loops and parameter tuning (Ξmax) could present practical challenges, especially in high-dimensional spaces. The paper acknowledges scalability issues and suggests potential future work, but this remains a significant concern.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears somewhat sensitive to parameters like Ξmax, requiring careful tuning. The paper provides some guidance, but a more thorough investigation of parameter sensitivity and robustness would be beneficial.
* **Limited Novelty in Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup largely mirrors previous work [2], using the same kernels and hyperparameter initialization strategy. While this allows for a fair comparison, it doesn’t fully explore the potential of IMGPO with tailored kernels or more sophisticated hyperparameter optimization techniques.
* **Discussion of Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1 is somewhat difficult to follow due to the heavily commented lines. While the comments are helpful in explaining the intent, the structure makes it difficult to understand the logic at a glance. A clearer presentation would improve readability.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The reliance on Assumption 1 (existence of an unknown semi-metric) is crucial for the theoretical guarantees. While reasonable, the paper could benefit from a discussion of how the algorithm performs when this assumption is significantly violated.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The exponential convergence rate and the novel approach to handling bounds represent a significant advance in the field of Bayesian optimization.
* **Addresses a Key Limitation:** The elimination of the δ-cover sampling requirement makes the algorithm more practical for real-world applications.
* **Positive Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate IMGPO’s competitiveness and effectiveness on standard benchmarks.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* **Implementation Complexity:** The algorithm’s complexity may limit its practical adoption.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The scalability to high-dimensional problems remains a significant open question.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The dependence on parameters like Ξmax requires careful tuning and could impact robustness.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for Bayesian optimization. The elimination of the δ-cover sampling requirement and the demonstration of exponential convergence are significant contributions. While there are some concerns regarding implementation complexity and scalability, the potential benefits of this approach outweigh these drawbacks.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on clarifying Algorithm 1, conducting a more thorough sensitivity analysis of parameters, and further discussing the implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis.
