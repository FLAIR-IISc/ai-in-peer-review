**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for globally optimizing deterministic black-box functions. The work is motivated by a key open problem in the field: designing a Gaussian Process (GP)-based optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate (in terms of simple regret) without resorting to impractical assumptions like a `δ-cover` sampling procedure, a limitation of prior work [1].

The proposed IMGPO algorithm cleverly combines two distinct lines of research in global optimization: the GP-UCB framework [15] and the hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]. The core idea is to not rely solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP model, which can be loose. Instead, the algorithm assumes the existence of an unknown, potentially much tighter, local bound on the function, characterized by a semi-metric `l`. IMGPO performs a search over an implicit, infinite set of these possible semi-metrics via a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. The GP-UCB is then used as a mechanism to prune unpromising branches of this hierarchical search, thereby accelerating convergence without being the sole driver of exploration. A key practical advantage of this integrated approach is that it obviates the need for a separate, often costly, auxiliary optimization step to maximize an acquisition function.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis proving that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` where `λ < 1`, which improves upon the polynomial bounds of the most closely related work, BaMSOO [2]. Experimental results on a suite of benchmark functions, ranging from 1 to 1000 dimensions, demonstrate that IMGPO generally outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods like GP-PI and GP-EI.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important open problem in the theory of Bayesian optimization. Achieving an exponential convergence rate without impractical assumptions is a significant step forward. The proposed solution is highly novel, providing an elegant synthesis of bound-based hierarchical methods and probabilistic GP models. Fusing these two perspectives—one that considers all possible Lipschitz constants (the "infinite-metric" idea) and one that uses a probabilistic surrogate—is a clever and insightful contribution.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main theoretical result, Theorem 1, formally establishes the exponential convergence rate. The analysis is valuable not just for the final bound but also for the insights it provides. The remarks following the theorem clearly articulate the trade-offs: how a "useful" GP bound improves the convergence rate (Remark 1), the benefit of using the GP to prune the search (Remark 2), and a compelling argument for why considering an infinite set of bounds can be superior even to using a single, known (but potentially loose) bound (Remark 3). This level of analysis deepens the reader's understanding of the problem itself.

3.  **Improved Practicality over Standard GP-BO:** A notable practical advantage of IMGPO is the elimination of the auxiliary optimization subroutine required by most GP-BO methods to maximize the acquisition function. This step can be a significant computational bottleneck and introduce its own optimization challenges. By integrating the selection process directly into the partitioning scheme, IMGPO presents a more self-contained and potentially more efficient framework.

4.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing IMGPO against a strong set of relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI, GP-PI) on a diverse collection of benchmark problems. The results convincingly demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed method in most cases. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO showcases a path toward scaling the method.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm's Description:** While the high-level intuition provided in Section 3.1 and Figure 1 is excellent, the detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and its description in Section 3.3 are dense and challenging to follow. The interplay between the two main loops over depth `h`, the roles of variables like `υmax` and `ξ`, and the mechanism for resolving "GP-based" dummy values (lines 13-17) are particularly complex.
    *   **Suggestion:** To improve clarity, I suggest the authors expand the description by walking through one or two steps of the 1D example from Figure 1 and explicitly tracking the state of key variables in Algorithm 1 (e.g., `i*h` for each `h`, `υmax`, `D`). Further justification for the update rule of `Ξ` in line 38, which currently seems somewhat heuristic, would also be beneficial.

2.  **Scalability and the Curse of Dimensionality:** The authors rightly acknowledge that the convergence rate degrades with dimensionality `D` (as `λ` approaches 1). The experiments are primarily on low-dimensional problems (D ≤ 6). The 1000D experiment is promising but relies on REMBO, which effectively reduces the problem to a low-dimensional one. The performance of the core IMGPO algorithm in moderately high dimensions (e.g., D = 15-30) remains an open question.
    *   **Suggestion:** A more direct empirical evaluation of the algorithm's scaling would strengthen the paper. Including results for a benchmark function like Hartmann or Ackley in 10 or 20 dimensions would provide a clearer picture of the practical limits of the standalone IMGPO algorithm.

3.  **Sensitivity to New Hyperparameters:** IMGPO introduces a new hyperparameter, `Ξmax`, which controls the lookahead depth for the GP-UCB pruning step. The paper mentions it was fixed to 22 and that performance could be improved by tuning it.
    *   **Suggestion:** A sensitivity analysis or ablation study for `Ξmax` would be a valuable addition. Showing how performance (regret) and runtime trade off as `Ξmax` varies on one or two of the test functions would give practitioners a better sense of how to set this parameter.

**Decision**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It successfully resolves a known open problem with a novel and well-motivated algorithm, supported by strong theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical results. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the clarity of presentation and the scope of the experimental analysis, but they do not undermine the core contribution. I am confident that this work will be of high interest to the NeurIPS community and will inspire further research at the intersection of deterministic and probabilistic global optimization. I recommend acceptance.
