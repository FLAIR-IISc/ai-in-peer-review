- Proposes IMGPO, a GP-based global optimization algorithm claiming exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.  
- Core idea: consider a set of possible bounds (via an unknown semi-metric), use the GP to prune candidates, and employ hierarchical partitioning for exploration.  
- Builds on de Freitas et al. and relates to Wang et al.; situates itself within bound-based search (DIRECT, SOO) and acknowledges hyperparameter tuning issues.  

- Theoretical guarantee: provides a rigorous proof of exponential convergence (details mostly in supplement).  
- Novelty: exploring a space of bounds rather than a single GP estimate is a clever, impactful idea.  
- Practical considerations: authors address hyperparameter tuning pragmatically rather than assuming perfect knowledge.  
- Empirical validation: competitive results on standard benchmarks; comparisons to SOO, GP-PI, GP-EI are valuable.  
- Clear motivation and strong connection to existing frameworks strengthen the paper.  

- Experimental scope is limited: simple isotropic Matern kernel and fixed initial hyperparameters may mask differences; need more kernels and high-dimensional tests.  
- Scalability concerns in high dimensions are noted but lack concrete mitigation strategies.  
- Algorithm complexity: multiple parameters (Ξmax, η) and multi-stage procedures may hinder implementation and tuning.  
- Reliance on strong assumptions (existence of unknown semi-metric and its Lipschitz continuity) needs more practical justification.  
- Figure 1 is dense and hard to follow; visualization should be simplified.  

- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical advance (exponential convergence), novel bound-estimation approach, clear relation to prior work, and competitive empirical performance.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited experiments, unresolved scalability, practical complexity, and dependence on strong assumptions.  

- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant theoretical contribution; recommend accept with minor revisions to address experiments, scalability, clarity, and assumptions.  

- Suggested revisions: expand experiments (harder benchmarks, different kernels, hyperparameter strategies, higher dimensions), discuss scalability remedies (dimensionality reduction, efficient partitioning), simplify Figure 1, explain practical implications of Assumptions 1–2 with examples, and provide a detailed computational cost comparison to baselines.
