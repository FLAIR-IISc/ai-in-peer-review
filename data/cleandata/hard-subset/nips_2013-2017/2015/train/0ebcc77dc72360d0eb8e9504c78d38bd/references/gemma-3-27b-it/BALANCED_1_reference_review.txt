This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric representing the function's continuity at the global optimum, alongside the GP’s uncertainty estimates, to guide the search. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and empirical results showcasing IMGPO’s performance on benchmark functions.

The paper addresses an important open problem highlighted by Wang et al. [2], making it a relevant contribution to the Bayesian optimization community. The proposed method is well-motivated, building upon existing work in bound-based search methods and recognizing the limitations of relying solely on GP-estimated bounds. The theoretical analysis, demonstrating exponential convergence, is a significant achievement. The inclusion of experimental results comparing IMGPO to several baseline algorithms adds further credibility. The connection to previous work like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18] is clearly explained. The code availability is a commendable practice.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The presentation, while generally clear, suffers from a dense writing style and could benefit from simplification. Several key concepts, like the “infinite-metric exploration loss” (ρt), are introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation. The reasoning behind certain design choices, such as the specific form of the UCB parameter (ςM) and the hierarchical partitioning scheme, could be more thoroughly discussed. While the theoretical analysis is strong, its reliance on assumptions (specifically Assumption 1) regarding the existence of an unknown semi-metric needs further discussion regarding its applicability in real-world scenarios. The experimental setup, while comprehensive, lacks detailed justification for the chosen hyperparameters and the specific comparison algorithms. The discussion of results is often cursory and doesn’t sufficiently delve into the reasons *why* IMGPO outperforms other algorithms in specific cases. The scalability limitations discussed in Section 4, despite acknowledging the issue, don’t offer concrete avenues for future research beyond leveraging additional assumptions. Furthermore, the empirical validation does not include a sensitivity analysis of the `Ξmax` parameter.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a significant open problem in GP-based optimization.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with an exponential convergence rate.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates competitive performance on benchmark functions.
*   **Clear Connection to Prior Work:** Effectively positions the contribution within the existing literature.
*   **Code Availability:** Facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dense Writing Style:** Can hinder understanding of key concepts.
*   **Lack of Intuition:** Insufficient explanation of core ideas (e.g., infinite-metric exploration loss).
*   **Assumption Dependence:** The reliance on the unknown semi-metric assumption requires more justification.
*   **Limited Experimental Analysis:** Insufficient detail on hyperparameter choices and result interpretation.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** Acknowledges limitations but offers limited solutions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   The theoretical contribution (exponential convergence) is significant.
*   The empirical results provide evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness.
*   The paper is well-written overall and makes a clear contribution to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The writing style and lack of intuition may make the paper difficult to understand for some readers.
*   The strong assumptions in the theoretical analysis limit the generality of the results.
*   The experimental analysis could be more thorough and insightful.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are particularly noteworthy, and the empirical evaluation provides further support for the proposed algorithm. However, the weaknesses related to clarity, assumption justification, and experimental analysis prevent me from giving a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Major Revisions.**

I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above through major revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  **Simplify the writing style:** Focus on clarity and provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts.
2.  **Justify the assumptions:** Discuss the applicability of Assumption 1 in real-world scenarios and explore potential alternatives.
3.  **Expand the experimental analysis:** Provide more detailed justification for hyperparameter choices, conduct a sensitivity analysis of `Ξmax`, and offer a more in-depth interpretation of the results.
4.  **Address scalability limitations:** Explore potential solutions for improving the algorithm’s scalability to higher dimensions.
5.  **Provide more examples and illustrations:** Use more visual aids to explain the algorithm’s workings and the impact of different parameters.
