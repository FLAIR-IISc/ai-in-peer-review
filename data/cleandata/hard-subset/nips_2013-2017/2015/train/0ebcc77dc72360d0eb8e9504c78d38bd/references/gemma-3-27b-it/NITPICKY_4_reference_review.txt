This paper presents a novel Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, IMGPO, aiming for exponential convergence without the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea revolves around simultaneously considering multiple potential Lipschitz-like bounds, leveraging the GP to prune implausible candidates, and utilizing a hierarchical partitioning scheme for exploration. The authors claim an exponential convergence rate and present experimental results demonstrating superior performance to several baselines.

The paper is, at first glance, promising, addressing a significant challenge in Bayesian Optimization – achieving theoretical guarantees without impractical sampling requirements. However, a meticulous examination reveals a concerning number of flaws, ranging from minor inconsistencies to potential conceptual issues that, in aggregate, raise serious doubts about the validity and practical impact of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The motivation—removing the δ-cover sampling bottleneck—is clearly articulated and relevant to the Bayesian Optimization community.
*   **Novel Idea (in principle):** The concept of simultaneously considering multiple potential bounds is intriguing and could potentially lead to more robust optimization.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform experiments on a range of benchmark functions, which is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Theoretical Justification:** While Theorem 1 presents an exponential convergence rate, the assumptions (specifically Assumption 1) feel weak and vaguely defined. The "semi-metric" ` is introduced without sufficient explanation of its practical implications or how it relates to the function's properties. The connection between ` and the Lipschitz constant, a standard concept in this field, is not made clear.  The dependence of the rate on parameters like `C` and `D` are not thoroughly explained, limiting the practical interpretability of the result.
*   **Algorithm Description is Convoluted:** The description of Algorithm 1 is excessively detailed and difficult to follow. The nested loops and the numerous variables (e.g., i∗h, ξ, ρ̄t) obscure the core logic.  The explanation of steps (i)-(v) in Section 3.2 is similarly convoluted and lacks clear justification for *why* these steps lead to efficient exploration. The explanation relies heavily on referring back to a figure, which isn’t ideal for a technical paper.
*   **Inconsistent Notation and Terminology:** The paper uses inconsistent notation. For example, the meaning of “Ξ” changes throughout the text, sometimes representing a parameter controlling exploration depth and other times representing its maximum value.  The introduction of `ρ̄t` as the *maximum* average exploration loss is odd and not standard.
*   **Empirical Validation is Insufficient:** The experimental setup, while covering a diverse set of functions, lacks crucial details.  The choice of hyperparameters (σ=1, l=0.25) appears arbitrary, particularly given the sensitivity of GP-based methods to kernel parameters. The claim of not knowing “good” hyperparameters, while realistic, doesn't justify using such simplistic initial values without a more thorough sensitivity analysis.  The fact that IMGPO outperformed the other algorithms does not necessarily mean that it is superior. It is possible that a better tuning or a different kernel could yield better performance on the baseline algorithms. The impact of the `Ξmax` parameter is only briefly discussed.
*   **Minor Technical Errors & Typos:** Several minor errors were noted throughout the manuscript, e.g. in lines 34-37 of Algorithm 1, “Ngp ← Ngp + 1” seems contradictory given Ngp represents the number of GP evaluations. This suggests a lack of careful review before submission.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The conclusion claims a "significant advance" and "combining two paths," which feels overstated given the number of issues.
*   **Remark 1 is confusing:** It's hard to follow the argument about the impact of "useful" UCBs. The notation is unclear, and the implications aren't adequately explained.
* **The argument regarding the theoretical effect of considering all possible bounds is difficult to follow, and feels somewhat contrived.** The comparisons to a "estimated bound" is not well defined, given the presented approach aims to avoid estimation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical foundation lacks sufficient rigor and clarity. The assumptions are weakly defined, and the connection to standard concepts like Lipschitz continuity is not made.
*   The algorithm description is excessively complex and difficult to understand, hindering reproducibility and making it difficult to assess its practical feasibility.
*   The empirical evaluation, while present, is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, given the arbitrary parameter settings and lack of sensitivity analysis.
*   The numerous minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper suggest a lack of careful attention to detail.
*   The overstatement of claims and convoluted argumentation weakens the overall credibility of the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance (Weak):**

*   The idea of exploring multiple bounds is potentially promising.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian Optimization.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in terms of theoretical rigor, algorithmic clarity, and empirical validation. The numerous minor flaws further detract from the overall quality. While the authors have identified an important problem, the proposed solution is not presented in a sufficiently clear, convincing, or well-supported manner.  I recommend **rejection**. The paper would benefit significantly from a substantial revision addressing the issues outlined above. A clearer theoretical justification, a more streamlined algorithm description, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, and careful attention to detail are all essential for this work to become a valuable contribution to the field.
