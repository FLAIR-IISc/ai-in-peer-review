

# NIPS Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work by de Freitas et al. The key innovation is considering "infinitely many possible candidates of the bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior. The authors claim their algorithm achieves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which they argue is superior to previous polynomial regret bounds.

The paper builds on the work of Munos (SOO) and Wang et al. (BaMSOO), which combined GP-UCB with hierarchical partitioning but only achieved polynomial regret bounds. The authors frame their approach as a solution to the open problem identified by Wang et al. regarding creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing an exponential regret bound, which is a significant improvement over previous work that only achieved polynomial regret bounds. This is an important theoretical contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature.

2. **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The idea of considering "infinitely many possible candidates of the bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior is conceptually interesting and represents a new perspective on bound-based optimization methods.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental results on standard test functions show that IMGPO outperforms several baselines in most cases, providing empirical support for the theoretical claims.

4. **Clear Algorithm Description**: The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and the 1D example in Figure 1 provide a reasonable explanation of how the algorithm works.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Clarity and Interpretation**:
   - The regret bound in Theorem 1 is overly complex and not well-explained. The expression "rN ≤ L(3βD 1/p)α exp(-α [ N + Ngp 2CDρ̄t - Ξn - 2 ] ln 3)" contains confusing notation (e.g., "2CDρ̄t" could mean 2C D ρ̄t or 2C D ρ̄_t). 
   - The authors don't clearly explain how λ = 3^(-α/2CDρ̄t) < 1 behaves in practice. If ρ̄t grows too fast (e.g., as O(N/t)), λ could approach 1, making the convergence rate effectively polynomial rather than exponential.
   - The comparison with BaMSOO's bound Õ(n^(-2α/D(4-α))) is unclear. The paper claims their bound is better, but the notation makes it difficult to see why.

2. **Algorithm Explanation**:
   - The explanation of the algorithm in Section 3.2 (steps (i)-(v)) is confusing and hard to follow without seeing the figure. The description of how the algorithm selects intervals for division is particularly opaque.
   - The role of Ξmax and its relationship to the algorithm's performance is not well-explained. The paper states that increasing Ξmax can improve performance but at the cost of extra CPU time, but doesn't provide guidance on how to choose this parameter.

3. **Experimental Limitations**:
   - The experimental section tests on only 8 functions, which is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method across a wide range of problems.
   - The experiments use a fixed kernel (isotropic Matern with ν=5/2) and fixed hyperparameters (σ=1, l=0.25), whereas previous work (e.g., Wang et al.) used hand-tuned kernels for each function. This makes the comparison less fair.
   - The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the results, making it difficult to assess the reliability of the performance differences.

4. **Practical Significance**:
   - The paper acknowledges in Remark 1 that "λ can get close to one as input dimension D increases," suggesting that the method may not scale well to higher dimensions. This is a significant limitation for a method that aims to be practical for real-world optimization problems.
   - The authors claim IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods" but "slower than SOO" (Table 1), which suggests that the improvement in convergence rate doesn't translate to significant practical speedups.

5. **Comparison with Related Work**:
   - The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with related work. For example, it doesn't discuss how their method relates to the work by Srinivas et al. (2010) on GP bandits, which also provides theoretical guarantees for Bayesian optimization.
   - The claim that "this is the first GP-based optimization method with an exponential convergence rate without the need of auxiliary optimization and the δ-cover sampling" is not adequately supported by a comprehensive literature review.

## Specific Comments

1. **Theorem 1 and Regret Bound**: The authors should provide a clearer interpretation of their regret bound. It would be helpful to see how the bound depends on the key parameters (α, L, p) in a more intuitive way. For example, they could provide a simplified version of the bound under specific assumptions about these parameters.

2. **Algorithm 1**: The pseudocode is quite complex and could be simplified. The authors should consider providing a more intuitive description of the algorithm's core ideas before presenting the detailed pseudocode.

3. **Experimental Section**: The authors should include more functions in their experiments, particularly higher-dimensional ones, to better demonstrate the scalability of their method. They should also compare to the best possible implementation of previous methods (e.g., using hand-tuned kernels).

4. **High-Dimensional Performance**: The paper mentions a 1000-dimensional test function but doesn't provide much detail on how IMGPO performs on it. Given the acknowledged scalability issue (Remark 1), this is a critical omission.

5. **Comparison with Srinivas et al. (2010)**: The authors should discuss how their approach relates to the work by Srinivas et al., which also provides theoretical guarantees for GP-based optimization.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel conceptual framework. However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that significantly limit its impact:

1. The theoretical analysis is not clearly explained or interpreted, making it difficult to understand the practical significance of the exponential convergence claim.
2. The experimental validation is limited and doesn't provide a fair comparison with previous methods.
3. The practical scalability of the method is not adequately addressed, despite being a major limitation for real-world applications.

While the core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds is conceptually interesting, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this approach leads to significant practical improvements over existing methods. The exponential convergence rate claim is theoretically appealing but may not translate to meaningful improvements in practice due to the dependence on parameters that could make λ close to 1.

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection with major revisions**. The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but it requires substantial improvements in the theoretical explanation, experimental validation, and discussion of practical implications. The current version does not sufficiently demonstrate that the proposed method offers significant advantages over existing approaches.

## List of Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The theoretical contribution of an exponential regret bound is significant and represents a potential advancement in the field.
- The idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds is conceptually novel and interesting.
- The experimental results provide some empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The theoretical analysis is poorly explained and the regret bound is confusingly written.
- The experimental validation is insufficient and does not fairly compare to previous methods.
- The paper fails to adequately address the scalability limitations of the proposed method.
- The practical significance of the exponential convergence rate is questionable given the dependence on parameters that could make λ close to 1.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Simplify and clarify the theoretical analysis, providing a more intuitive explanation of the regret bound.
2. Expand the experimental section with more test functions and a fairer comparison to previous methods.
3. Address the scalability issue more thoroughly, particularly for high-dimensional problems.
4. Provide a more comprehensive comparison with related work, especially Srinivas et al. (2010).
