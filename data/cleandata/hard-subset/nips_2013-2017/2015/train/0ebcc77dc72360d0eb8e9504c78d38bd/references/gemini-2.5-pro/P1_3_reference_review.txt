### Summary

This paper presents a new Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for globally optimizing deterministic black-box functions. The main contribution is a theoretical guarantee of an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret, which improves upon previous polynomial-rate results for practical GP-based algorithms. The key idea is to combine the hierarchical partitioning framework of optimistic optimization algorithms (like SOO) with a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. Unlike standard GP-UCB which relies solely on the GP-derived upper confidence bound, IMGPO operates on the principle of simultaneously considering an infinite set of possible local smoothness conditions (represented by an unknown semi-metric `l`). The GP-UCB is then used not as the sole driver of exploration, but as a tool to prune the search space and accelerate convergence within this "infinite-metric" framework. The authors provide a proof for the exponential regret bound and support their claims with experiments on several benchmark functions, showing superior performance against related algorithms like SOO and BaMSOO.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper successfully addresses a well-defined open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature: achieving an exponential convergence rate with a GP-based method without resorting to impractical sampling schemes like the δ-cover. Improving the regret bound from polynomial (as in BaMSOO) to exponential is a substantial theoretical advance.

2.  **Novel and Insightful Approach:** The core idea of combining the "infinite-metric" philosophy of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO with the probabilistic bounds from GPs is highly novel. This synthesis is not trivial; the paper provides a principled mechanism where the GP prunes branches of the search tree that would otherwise be explored under the purely optimistic strategy. The analysis, particularly through the lens of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt), offers a clear and valuable intuition for why this combination is powerful.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The algorithm is compared against strong and relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI/EI). The choice to use a standard Matern kernel without function-specific hyperparameter tuning represents a realistic and challenging scenario, making IMGPO's strong performance even more compelling. The inclusion of a high-dimensional problem (using REMBO) also hints at the algorithm's practical utility as a component in more complex systems.

4.  **Clarity of Motivation and High-Level Idea:** The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself within the existing literature and motivating its approach. The introduction clearly lays out the progression from Lipschitz optimization to GP-UCB, highlighting the specific gap left by the work of de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The overview in Section 3.1 effectively communicates the intuition of not fully trusting the GP bound and instead leveraging it to manage the uncertainty over an unknown, potentially tighter, bound.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Complexity of the Algorithm Description:** While the high-level idea is clear, the detailed pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is quite dense and difficult to parse. The interplay between the three main for-loops (lines 8, 19, 26), the updates to `υmax`, and the conditions for selecting the cell to divide (`i*h`) are complex. A clearer, step-by-step breakdown or a more detailed walk-through of the logic preceding the pseudocode could significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility for the reader. For instance, the exact mechanism and purpose of the `while true` loop in lines 9-17 for resolving "GP-based" labels are not immediately obvious.

2.  **Scalability with Dimensionality:** The paper acknowledges that the constant `λ` in the exponential bound approaches 1 as the dimensionality `D` increases, which is a known limitation of methods based on hierarchical space partitioning. While the authors demonstrate a use-case with REMBO, the discussion on the inherent scalability limitations of the core IMGPO algorithm could be more prominent. This is an important practical consideration for users.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new parameter `Ξmax`, which controls the depth of the UCB lookahead for pruning. The paper sets this to a fixed value (22) and briefly mentions that performance could be improved on some functions by increasing it, at the cost of CPU time. A more thorough discussion or sensitivity analysis regarding this parameter would strengthen the paper. It would be valuable to understand how to set this parameter in practice and how sensitive the algorithm's performance is to its value.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Analysis in Remark 1:** The remark states that if the GP is useless, the regret bound can still be better than known results by limiting the tree depth. The footnote suggests limiting depth to `O(sqrt(N))`. Could the authors clarify if this modification is implemented in the algorithm used for experiments? If not, how does the unbounded depth affect performance in cases where the GP prior is poorly specified (as hinted at in the Rosenbrock2 experiment)?

*   **Role of `Ξ` update (Line 38):** The update rule for `Ξ` (which seems to be the current lookahead depth, bounded by `Ξmax`) is an interesting heuristic. It increases `Ξ` when a better point is found and decreases it otherwise. This appears to be an adaptive mechanism to balance local vs. global search. Could the authors provide more intuition or justification for this specific update rule? It seems central to the algorithm's performance but is only briefly mentioned.

*   **Comparison to GP-UCB:** The paper compares IMGPO to BaMSOO (a hybrid) and standard GP-BO with EI/PI. A direct comparison to a pure GP-UCB algorithm would also be interesting, as GP-UCB is the most direct conceptual competitor from the "rely on a single bound" family.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It solves a recognized open problem by presenting the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. The core technical idea is novel, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results are compelling. While the algorithm's description could be clearer and a deeper discussion of its limitations (scalability, parameter sensitivity) would be beneficial, these are minor points that do not detract from the overall quality and importance of the work. The paper advances the state of the art and provides new insights that could inspire further research at the intersection of optimistic optimization and Bayesian methods.
