This paper proposes a novel Gaussian process-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) aiming for exponential convergence without relying on the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to simultaneously consider a multitude of potential Lipschitz-like bounds, informed by the GP’s uncertainty estimate, to guide the search process. The authors claim an exponential convergence rate and present experimental results demonstrating improved performance over several baselines.

While the motivation for addressing the δ-cover sampling bottleneck is valid and the goal of achieving exponential convergence with GPs is valuable, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough validation and a presentation that feels somewhat hand-wavy despite attempting a theoretical justification. My primary concern revolves around the practical significance of the proposed method and the clarity of the theoretical claims.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its attempt to bridge the gap between theoretical guarantees (exponential convergence) and practical implementation of Bayesian optimization. The idea of leveraging the GP’s uncertainty to implicitly explore a range of possible Lipschitz constants, rather than relying on a single, potentially inaccurate estimate, is intuitively appealing. The connection to bound-based search methods is also well-articulated.

However, several weaknesses significantly detract from the paper’s overall impact. First, the theoretical analysis, while present, lacks sufficient detail to be readily digestible. The meaning of parameters like ‘Ξ’ and the implications of the convergence rate formula are not immediately clear. The discussion of the conditions under which the algorithm’s performance relies on the tightness of the GP’s bound (Remarks 1 & 2) is insightful but feels tacked on rather than integrated into the core argument.  The claim of improving upon BaMSOO’s polynomial bound with an exponential bound needs more rigorous justification – the provided reasoning feels insufficient.

Second, the experimental setup, while including several benchmark functions, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The choice to *not* tune the GP hyperparameters is a significant limitation.  The authors acknowledge this, stating they assume lack of prior knowledge, but this is a strong assumption that severely impacts the fairness of the comparison. The claim that IMGPO outperformed other algorithms "in general" is not strongly supported by the presented results – differences are often marginal, and SOO performs well on Rosenbrock2, demonstrating the sensitivity to the GP's modeling capability.  The explanation for SOO’s performance is also weak; simply stating the GP “did not model the objective function well” isn’t a sufficient analysis.  Furthermore, using REMBO with IMGPO/BaMSOO only for the 1000-dimensional problem feels like an attempt to salvage a failing comparison.

Third, the algorithm itself, described in Algorithm 1, appears complex and relies on several parameters (e.g., Ξmax) whose impact isn’t fully explored. The explanation of steps (i)-(v) in Section 3.2 is difficult to follow and lacks sufficient justification for the chosen approach. The division procedure’s reliance on a hyperrectangle assumption is limiting, and the discussion of extending it to general domains feels superficial.

Finally, the related work section, while covering relevant literature, lacks critical engagement. The paper doesn’t adequately position itself within the broader context of Bayesian optimization research beyond the immediate goal of achieving exponential convergence.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in Bayesian optimization.
* Proposes a novel idea for improving the efficiency of GP-based optimization.
* Demonstrates some empirical improvement over existing methods (although this is not overwhelming).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Theoretical analysis is incomplete and difficult to follow.
* Experimental setup suffers from limitations (fixed hyperparameters, ad-hoc comparisons).
* Algorithm is complex and lacks a clear justification for several design choices.
* Lack of thorough comparison to well-tuned baselines.
* Limited discussion of the practical implications and scalability of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting idea, the lack of rigorous validation, the somewhat opaque theoretical analysis, and the incomplete experimental evaluation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The claim of exponential convergence needs significantly stronger support, and the practical benefits of IMGPO over simpler, well-tuned alternatives are not convincingly demonstrated. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version with a more thorough and compelling evaluation. The idea has potential, but the current presentation falls short of NIPS standards.
