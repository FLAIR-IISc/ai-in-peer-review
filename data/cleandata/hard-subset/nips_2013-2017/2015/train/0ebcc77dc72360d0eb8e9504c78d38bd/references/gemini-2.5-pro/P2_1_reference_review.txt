### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to achieve an exponential convergence rate for global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The work is motivated by a key open problem in the field: existing GP-based methods either rely on impractical sampling procedures (like δ-cover sampling) to achieve exponential regret bounds or, for practical algorithms, only provide weaker polynomial regret bounds.

The core idea of IMGPO is to synthesize two distinct lines of research: the bound-based GP-UCB approach and the hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO. Instead of relying solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP, which may not be tight, IMGPO operates under the assumption that an unknown, tighter bound defined by a semi-metric exists around a global optimum. The algorithm performs a search that is simultaneously optimistic with respect to an infinite family of such possible semi-metrics. The GP-UCB is cleverly employed not as the sole driver of exploration, but as a mechanism to prune the search space, eliminating regions that are provably suboptimal according to the GP model. This combination allows IMGPO to achieve an exponential regret bound, O(λ^(N+Ngp)), without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, a significant theoretical improvement over prior practical methods like BaMSOO. The authors support their theoretical claims with empirical results on several benchmark functions, showing that IMGPO generally outperforms existing methods.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The primary strength is the theoretical contribution. Theorem 1 provides the first exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm, directly addressing a well-known gap in the literature. The analysis, which introduces the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt), provides an insightful framework for understanding the trade-off between exploring based on the function values themselves (the "infinite-metric" part) and pruning based on the GP model. The experimental evaluation is solid, comparing against relevant and strong baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI) on a variety of standard test functions, and the results convincingly demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine is a nice addition that hints at broader applicability.

*   **Weaknesses:** The description of the algorithm in Section 3.3 and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 are quite dense and complex. The interplay between the nested loops, the various indices (i*h), and the conditions for pruning and division is difficult to parse. For instance, the update rule for Ξ feels somewhat heuristic and could benefit from more intuition or justification. While the authors provide code, the paper itself should strive for a clearer, more self-contained algorithmic description to ensure reproducibility. Furthermore, Assumption 1, while necessary for the proof, is quite abstract. A brief discussion on what class of functions (e.g., in terms of their RKHS norm) would satisfy this assumption could make the theory more grounded.

#### **Clarity**
The paper is mostly well-written, but clarity could be improved in key areas.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction and motivation are excellent. The authors clearly situate their work, explain the limitations of prior art (de Freitas et al. [1], Wang et al. [2]), and articulate the paper's contribution. The high-level overview in Section 3.1 is also very effective at conveying the core intuition behind the algorithm. The figures are helpful illustrations of the algorithm's behavior.

*   **Weaknesses:** As mentioned under Quality, the detailed algorithm description (Section 3.3) is a major clarity bottleneck. A reader would likely find it very challenging to implement the algorithm from the pseudocode and description alone. A simpler example or a more detailed walkthrough of the logic in lines 8-24 of Algorithm 1 would significantly improve the manuscript. For example, clarifying the precise mechanism and purpose of the `υ_max` variable across the different loops would be beneficial.

#### **Originality**
The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** The central idea of combining hierarchical partitioning with GP-UCB is not new (e.g., BaMSOO). However, the way it is done here is novel and powerful. Instead of using the GP-UCB to guide every decision, the authors propose a new philosophy: use the optimistic partitioning scheme of SOO/DIRECT as the primary search strategy and employ the GP as an efficient pruning tool. This concept of leveraging the GP to reduce the "infinite-metric exploration loss" is a significant conceptual contribution. It effectively unifies two previously divergent approaches to global optimization in a principled and effective manner.

#### **Significance**
The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   **Strengths:** This work provides a definitive answer to an important open question in Bayesian optimization theory. Achieving exponential convergence rates in a practical algorithm is a major step forward. Practitioners now have access to an algorithm that is not only empirically effective but also backed by much stronger theoretical guarantees than previous methods. The analysis itself provides a new lens through which to view the interplay between model-based and direct search methods, which could inspire further research. The fact that the algorithm avoids a costly auxiliary optimization step (unlike standard GP-EI/PI) also makes it computationally attractive.

*   **Weaknesses:** The primary limitation on the significance is scalability with dimension. The authors rightly note that the convergence rate degrades as dimension D increases, a known issue for all space-partitioning methods. While they demonstrate a potential solution via random embeddings (REMBO), the core IMGPO algorithm is likely best suited for low-to-moderate dimensional problems. This limitation should be stated more explicitly as a caveat.

### **Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Pros:**
*   Solves a significant open problem by providing the first practical GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   Presents a highly original and elegant synthesis of hierarchical partitioning methods and GP-UCB, where the GP is used for pruning.
*   The theoretical analysis is strong and provides novel insights into the behavior of such hybrid algorithms.
*   Empirical results are convincing and demonstrate superior performance over relevant baselines on a range of problems.
*   The algorithm avoids the expensive auxiliary optimization step common in other GP-BO methods.

**Cons:**
*   The detailed description of the algorithm and its pseudocode are complex and difficult to follow, hindering clarity and reproducibility from the text alone.
*   The core algorithm is, like other partitioning methods, susceptible to the curse of dimensionality, limiting its direct applicability to high-dimensional problems.
*   The theoretical assumptions, while standard for this type of analysis, are abstract, and their connection to concrete function properties could be elaborated upon.

### **Final Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. The originality of the core idea and the significance of the theoretical results are outstanding. The paper is well-motivated and the experiments strongly support the claims. While the clarity of the algorithm's detailed description could be improved, this is a correctable flaw that does not detract from the importance of the contribution. This work will be of great interest to both theorists and practitioners in the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
