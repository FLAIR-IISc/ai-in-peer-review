Summary
The paper proposes IMGPO, a GP-based global optimization algorithm that combines hierarchical partitioning (à la SOO/DIRECT) with GP-UCB-style bounds to prune exploration, aiming to achieve exponential simple regret without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary inner optimization. The key idea is to not commit to a single (possibly loose) bound; instead, the algorithm implicitly considers “infinitely many” potential upper bounds via a selection/pruning procedure driven by both the hierarchy and the GP UCB. The analysis introduces an “infinite-metric exploration loss” to quantify the cost of not fixing a bound and proves a high-probability exponential regret bound O(λN+Ngp) under (i) an “unknown semi-metric” assumption relating suboptimality to distance from the optimum, and (ii) a Hölder-type condition on that semi-metric. Experiments on several deterministic benchmarks compare IMGPO to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI and GP-EI with a fixed Matern-5/2 kernel; the authors report faster convergence and lower regret than baselines on most problems, with code available.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on DIRECT/SOO/DOO (Munos) and BaMSOO (Wang et al.). It targets the same setting as de Freitas et al. (deterministic observations) but removes δ-cover. Compared to GP-UCB, the novelty is the partition-based search that avoids solving inner optimization problems. Conceptually, the “infinite-metric” stance generalizes SOO’s independence from a fixed smoothness constant while using GP to prune.

Strengths
- Ambitious goal: exponential simple regret without δ-cover or inner optimization would be a notable advance if the assumptions and guarantees hold.
- Interesting hybridization: leverages GP-UCB to reduce the candidate set within a SOO-like tree, with a cleanly described algorithm and publicly available code.
- Analysis attempts to disentangle the contributions of GP (via Ngp and pruning) and of the infinite-metric idea (via ρ̄t), and acknowledges dimensionality issues.
- Empirical results show promising performance on several benchmarks; use of REMBO indicates an awareness of high-dimensional extensions.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Validity of the UCB guarantee under empirical Bayes. The high-probability regret bound relies critically on f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holding uniformly over all UCB calls (via a union bound with ςM). This concentration statement typically assumes a fixed, correctly specified GP prior/kernel/hyperparameters (cf. Srinivas et al.). In the algorithm, hyperparameters are re-estimated adaptively after each iteration via empirical Bayes; under such data-dependent model selection, standard UCB coverage is not guaranteed. The paper does not address this mismatch. As stated, the probability claim is not justified.

2) Assumptions and interpretation of “exponential rate.” Assumption 1–2 amount to a Hölder growth condition near the global optimizer (f(x*) − f(x) ≤ L||x − x*||αp). In the general Lipschitz/Hölder setting, known optimistic optimization results are polynomial in n (HOO/DOO/SOO). The theorem’s “exponential” bound hides heavy constants and depends on ρ̄t, Ξn, C, and D; indeed λ → 1 quickly with D, and if UCBs are loose, ρ̄t inflates and the improvement becomes unclear. There is no nontrivial bound on ρ̄t or Ξn provided by the algorithm; thus the guarantee may degenerate. The paper should reconcile its result with existing lower/upper bounds in optimistic optimization and clearly state the regimes where the exponent is meaningful.

3) Computational complexity and practicality of step (iii). The worst-case cost is said to involve up to 3^Ξmax UCB computations; with Ξmax=22, this is astronomically large. Even if “rare,” the paper needs a realistic complexity analysis and practical safeguards. Moreover, repeated UCB queries plus O(N^3) GP updates can dominate runtime; experiments do not quantify wall-clock cost vs function evaluations beyond a brief mention.

4) Experimental validation is not rigorous enough to support the strong claims.
- Baselines: The comparison omits GP-UCB; GP-PI/EI are used with a nonstandard inner solver; all GP baselines are constrained to a single isotropic Matern kernel, which likely handicaps them. Prior work that tuned kernels should be included as a stronger baseline and/or a sensitivity study should be provided.
- Ablations: There is no ablation to quantify the contribution of GP pruning (Ngp), the infinite-metric effect (ρ̄t), or Ξmax. The paper claims benefits in Remarks 1–3 but does not empirically validate them.
- Robustness: No analysis under kernel misspecification (inevitable in practice), nor under noise; the UCB coverage violation observed on Rosenbrock is acknowledged but not analyzed.
- Reporting: References to Table 1/Figure 2 are incomplete here; deterministic methods have no variability reported; wall-clock results are only briefly mentioned.

5) Clarity and presentation issues. The pseudocode contains several typos/unclear lines (e.g., ρt vs ρτ, “Ξ ← Ξ + 22,” the definition of C, the construction of T′h+ξ). The description of the UCB parameter ςM cites a union bound but does not align with adaptive hyperparameter updates. The “best unknown L, α, p” phrasing in the theorem is unusual and can be misleading—while acceptable as an analysis device, it complicates interpretation and comparison.

Suggestions for improvement
- Theoretically justify the UCB event under adaptive hyperparameter selection (e.g., confidence sets over kernel parameters, misspecification-robust bounds, or freeze hyperparameters to preserve coverage). Alternatively, state the theorem under fixed hyperparameters and separate the empirical Bayes variant as heuristic.
- Provide nontrivial, provable bounds or high-probability control on ρ̄t and Ξn under the algorithm, and a precise complexity analysis of step (iii) with practical settings of Ξmax.
- Strengthen experiments: include GP-UCB with both fixed and tuned kernels; add ablations (with/without GP pruning; varying Ξmax; measuring Ngp, ρ̄t); report wall-clock times; add more diverse tasks; and analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Clarify pseudocode and notation; ensure figures/tables fully support claims; discuss conditions under which exponential rate is practically meaningful.

Pros
- Interesting algorithmic idea bridging GP-UCB and optimistic partitioning.
- Ambitious theoretical target and an analysis that attempts to separate components.
- Public code; promising empirical trends.

Cons
- Core high-probability guarantee appears invalid under empirical Bayes as used.
- Key rate depends on unbounded internal quantities; practical exponent unclear.
- Computational cost of pruning step not convincingly addressed.
- Empirics do not rigorously validate claims or compare against strong baselines.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The direction is promising, but the current version does not meet the bar for methodological soundness and experimental rigor required to support the central claims. I encourage the authors to tighten the theory under realistic modeling choices and substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation.
