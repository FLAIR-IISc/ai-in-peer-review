## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, termed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work [1]. The core idea revolves around acknowledging the potential inadequacy of GP-derived bounds and leveraging an unknown semi-metric to guide exploration, effectively considering an infinite set of potential bounds.

The authors correctly identify a key limitation of existing Bayesian Optimization methods with theoretical guarantees, namely the impracticality of δ-cover sampling.  The proposed approach, combining GP modeling with a hierarchical partitioning scheme and an exploration strategy motivated by an unknown semi-metric, is a reasonable attempt to address this issue. The paper clearly outlines the algorithm (Algorithm 1) and attempts to provide a theoretical analysis, culminating in a claimed exponential convergence rate (Theorem 1).

However, despite the apparent novelty and ambition, I find several significant issues that ultimately lead me to recommend rejection. My concerns fall into the categories of clarity, technical soundness, and significance – though originality is less of a concern, as the combination of ideas appears novel.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** The reliance on δ-cover sampling is a genuine bottleneck for practical application of theoretically grounded Bayesian Optimization.
*   **Novel idea:** The concept of simultaneously exploring based on an infinite set of potential bounds, while utilizing the GP to prune improbable candidates, is intriguing.
*   **Clear presentation of Algorithm 1:** The algorithm is relatively easy to follow, and Figure 1 provides a helpful visual illustration.
*   **Attempts a theoretical analysis:** The authors provide a Theorem and supporting definitions, which demonstrates an effort to ground the method in rigorous analysis.

**Weaknesses (and substantial concerns):**

*   **Lack of Clarity in Key Definitions:** Assumption 1, introducing the unknown semi-metric ``, is vaguely defined. What properties *must* ` ` possess beyond being a semi-metric? The link between ` ` and the "unknown bound" is not sufficiently clear. This lack of precision undermines the theoretical arguments.
*   **Unjustified Assumptions in the Analysis:** Theorem 1 relies on several assumptions (L, α, p) about the unknown semi-metric ` `. While acknowledging the best values are used, the paper does not discuss the *impact* of these assumptions on the convergence rate in a practical context. How sensitive is the claimed exponential convergence to these parameters? This is critical.
*   **Overly Optimistic Experimental Setup:** The experiments, while showing promising results, are flawed by a lack of proper baseline comparison. The authors explicitly state they used “blindly initialized” hyperparameters for *all* algorithms. This is problematic. A fair comparison requires careful hyperparameter tuning for *each* algorithm on the given benchmarks, using established techniques. Without this, the results are not convincing.  Additionally, the claim that the GP prior was “misleading” for Rosenbrock2 is concerning – a good experimental design would have anticipated and addressed potential issues with kernel selection.
*   **Computational Cost Concerns:** The algorithm’s complexity is not adequately discussed. The repeated evaluation of UCBs, as described in the algorithm and alluded to in the text, could introduce significant computational overhead, potentially negating the benefits of avoiding δ-cover sampling.  The scaling of IMGPO with increasing dimensionality is not well-addressed, despite mentioning the potential for it to be a challenge.
*   **Remark 3’s Algebraic Manipulation is Hand-Wavy:** The discussion in Remark 3, intended to justify the method, relies on an informal algebraic manipulation without clear justification.  The conclusion that considering all bounds is always better lacks sufficient rigor.
*   **Minor Formatting/Writing Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are minor inconsistencies in notation and writing style (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface for variables, slight awkward phrasing). While not major, these detract from the paper’s overall polish.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** Novel approach to addressing a significant problem in Bayesian Optimization; clear algorithmic description.

**Con:** Lack of clarity in key definitions; questionable assumptions in the theoretical analysis; flawed experimental setup; concerns about computational cost and scalability; weak justification for the core idea.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea of IMGPO is intriguing, the significant technical issues and flaws in the experimental evaluation outweigh its strengths. The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions that are not thoroughly justified, and the experimental results are not convincing due to the inadequate baseline comparison.  The paper, as presented, falls short of NIPS standards for technical soundness and clarity. I recommend rejection. A substantial revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a rigorous experimental evaluation with properly tuned baselines and a more thorough discussion of the algorithm’s computational complexity, would be necessary for reconsideration.
