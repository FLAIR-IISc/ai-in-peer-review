Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions. The method combines hierarchical optimistic partitioning (à la DIRECT/SOO) with a GP upper confidence bound (UCB) used as a filter rather than as the sole optimistic bound. The key idea is to avoid reliance on a single, potentially loose, known bound and instead “hedge” over infinitely many possible unknown bounds characterized by a semi-metric ℓ near the global optimizer. The algorithm maintains a tree of hyperrectangles, repeatedly divides promising rectangles, and sometimes substitutes true function evaluations with GP-UCB values (tagged and later resolved if needed), thereby reducing evaluations while guiding search. The authors analyze a regret bound that they state is exponential in the number of function evaluations plus the number of GP-UCB calls, and they introduce a notion of “infinite-metric exploration loss” to account for the price of not committing to a single bound. Empirically they compare IMGPO to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on several standard benchmarks, reporting superior simple regret versus number of evaluations.

This work builds on and contrasts with: GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., 2010) and the deterministic-exponential analysis by de Freitas et al. (2012) which required δ-cover sampling; SOO/DOO (Munos, 2011) and DIRECT (Jones et al., 1993), which use optimistic partitioning with Lipschitz/Hölder assumptions; BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014), a hybrid of SOO and GP-UCB with polynomial regret. The paper aims to close the gap by removing δ-cover sampling and auxiliary acquisition optimization while claiming exponential convergence.

Quality
- Theory. The analysis is ambitious and potentially impactful, but parts are unclear or appear inconsistent with known rates. Standard lower bounds for optimizing Hölder/Lipschitz functions imply polynomial simple regret rN ≳ N−α/D̃ (with D̃ related to near-optimality dimension). Obtaining rN = O(exp(−cN)) uniformly under Assumptions 1–2 would contradict these. Your Theorem 1 states rN ≤ const · exp(−α[(N+Ngp)/(2CDρ̄t) − Ξn − 2] ln 3). If ρ̄t and Ξn are O(1), this is indeed exponential in N; however, in general ρ̄t can grow with N/t and your own Remark 1 suggests the bound then weakens substantially. It would help to (i) precisely relate t, N, and ρ̄t, (ii) give regimes under which ρ̄t is bounded (and whether those regimes are realistic), and (iii) reconcile the claimed exponential rate with known lower bounds (e.g., by explicitly assuming near-optimality dimension zero or other structural conditions).
- Possible typo/issue: λ is defined as λ = 3 − α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1, which seems dimensionally and numerically odd. Likely you meant λ = 3−α/(2CDρ̄t) (i.e., 3 to the power −α/(2CDρ̄t)), which lies in (0,1). Please check.
- UCB validity under hyperparameter learning. You set ςM via a union bound to ensure f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with probability ≥ 1−η. However, you re-estimate GP hyperparameters after each iteration. This adaptivity typically breaks fixed-parameter concentration guarantees unless carefully controlled. You note empirically that the UCB property “did not hold many times” on Rosenbrock, which undermines the conditions of the theorem. Please either provide a robustness argument (e.g., conditioning on a data-dependent prior, or using high-probability bounds that account for hyperparameter selection), or clearly scope the theorem to fixed hyperparameters.
- Definition/strength of assumptions. Assumption 1 (existence of an unknown semi-metric ℓ with f(x*) ≤ f(x)+ℓ(x,x*)) is vacuous without specifying semi-metric properties; Assumption 2 then constrains ℓ to be Hölder w.r.t. ℓp. In effect, this is a local Hölder smoothness assumption around x*. Please formalize “semi-metric” and explicitly relate to near-optimality dimension to position your results relative to SOO/DOO.
- Accounting for Ngp. The bound depends on N+Ngp, where Ngp counts UCB evaluations. While treating UCB calls as computationally cheap is reasonable in BO, the headline “exponential in evaluations” should be careful to specify whether it is exponential in N or in N+Ngp; if Ngp scales with N or worse, the practical rate in terms of N alone may revert to polynomial.
- Algorithmic complexity. Step (iii) may require evaluating U on a fully expanded subtree up to depth ξ (worst-case O(3^Ξmax) UCB calls). A clearer per-iteration complexity analysis and guidance on Ξmax are needed.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally readable, but notation and indexing are heavy and sometimes inconsistent. n, N, t, h, and M are easy to confuse. A table of symbols would help. Clearly define “iteration” t versus “function evaluations” N, and how ρt and ρ̄t scale with them.
- The pseudocode is helpful, but lines 21–24 are dense; a short worked example in the main text would aid understanding. Some typos: “Ξ ← Ξ + 22” (do you mean Ξ ← Ξ + 2^2?), reuse of l for both lengthscale and ℓ.
- The role of η and its propagation to global failure probability should be explicit.

Originality
- The “infinite-metric exploration loss” is a neat analytical device to quantify the cost of hedging over unknown bounds. The algorithmic idea of using GP-UCB as a filter within SOO-style partitioning is intuitive but, to my knowledge, not previously analyzed in this way. Relative to BaMSOO, the main novelty is the different integration of GP information and the claim of exponential convergence.

Significance
- If the claimed guarantees can be made precise (including conditions under which ρ̄t is bounded and UCB remains valid under hyperparameter estimation), this would be a notable advance for deterministic BO. Practically, avoiding auxiliary acquisition optimization is attractive. The empirical results are promising, though the study could be more thorough and fair.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines: GP-UCB is not included; GP-PI/EI optimization via SOO may disadvantage them relative to common multi-start L-BFGS/DIRECT. Please include GP-UCB and standard optimizers for PI/EI. Clarify budgets, restarts, and any tuning.
- Fairness: Plot x-axis as number of true function evaluations N (not including Ngp). If wall-clock is reported (Table 1), ensure identical implementation effort across methods.
- Ablations: Show IMGPO without GP (to isolate the “infinite-metric” effect), and with different Ξmax, η. Provide sensitivity to kernel mismatch.
- High-dimensional case: The 1000D REMBO experiment is encouraging; please include more details and comparisons (e.g., to REMBO+EI).

Questions and requests for clarification
- Precisely state conditions under which ρ̄t stays O(1). If not, translate Theorem 1 into a bound in terms of N only and compare to SOO/BaMSOO rates.
- Fix the definition of λ and clarify the dependence on D and ρ̄t.
- Provide a concentration argument that remains valid under adaptive hyperparameter learning, or scope the theorem to fixed hyperparameters.
- Define “semi-metric” and discuss the near-optimality dimension of f implied by Assumptions 1–2.

Pros
- Interesting and plausible hybrid of GP and optimistic partitioning without auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Novel analysis tool (infinite-metric exploration loss) and insightful remarks about when GP-UCB is helpful.
- Promising empirical performance on standard benchmarks.

Cons
- Theoretical claims (exponential convergence) appear in tension with known lower bounds unless additional structure is assumed; current presentation is unclear.
- UCB validity under hyperparameter updates not justified.
- Experimental comparisons could be fairer and more comprehensive; missing GP-UCB baseline and standard optimizers.

Overall
The paper targets an important problem and proposes a compelling algorithmic direction with thoughtful analysis ideas. However, I have significant concerns about the correctness/scope of the exponential rate claim and the clarity/rigor of the assumptions and concentration arguments. With clarifications and strengthened theory, and a more thorough empirical comparison, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, I lean toward a weak reject, encouraging revision to address the above points.
