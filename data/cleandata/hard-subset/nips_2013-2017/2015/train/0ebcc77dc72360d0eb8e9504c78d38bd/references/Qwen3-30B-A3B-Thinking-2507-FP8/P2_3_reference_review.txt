

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP) based global optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence rate without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work. The authors address an open problem identified by Wang et al. (2014) regarding creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without impractical sampling. Their key insight is to consider infinitely many possible bounds encoded by an unknown semi-metric ` (Assumption 1) rather than relying solely on the GP's UCB bound. The algorithm uses hierarchical partitioning to maintain hyperintervals and combines the GP's UCB with the unknown bound to guide search. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 and demonstrates superior empirical performance over baselines including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous theoretical analysis and thorough experimental evaluation. The regret bound in Theorem 1 is well-motivated and represents a significant improvement over previous work (BaMSOO has a polynomial regret bound). The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, noting that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, suggesting scalability challenges for higher dimensions.

The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, with Remark 1-3 providing insightful discussions about the role of GP, the tightness of UCB, and the benefits of considering infinitely many bounds. The analysis correctly identifies that relying on a single bound (even if tight) may not be optimal, which is a subtle but important contribution to the field.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on eight different functions across dimensions 1-1000. The authors properly acknowledge that the GP prior may be misleading (e.g., for Rosenbrock2), and they carefully compare against relevant baselines. The ablation study on Ξmax provides useful insights into parameter sensitivity.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to analysis. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is detailed but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the hierarchical partitioning approach. The notation is generally consistent, though "infinite-metric exploration loss" could be more intuitive.

The experimental results are presented clearly with informative figures, and the supplementary material appears to contain sufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper properly references related work, including the key paper by Wang et al. that identified the open problem this paper resolves.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by resolving the open problem of achieving exponential convergence in GP-based optimization without δ-cover sampling. The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds (rather than relying on a single bound from GP) is novel and well-justified. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly BaMSOO (which uses a single bound) and GP-UCB (which requires δ-cover).

The connection to broader bound-based search methods (A*, PAC-MDP) is insightful and demonstrates that the authors understand the broader context of their work. The paper builds upon but significantly advances the literature by providing a theoretically sound solution to a recognized open problem.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Exponential convergence is a major improvement over the polynomial convergence rates of previous GP-based methods. For practical applications where function evaluations are expensive (a common scenario in Bayesian optimization), this could lead to significant computational savings.

The paper successfully demonstrates that the theoretical advantage translates to empirical performance, with IMGPO outperforming baselines on most test functions. The authors provide a practical analysis framework that helps practitioners understand when and why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial.

The limitations are well-acknowledged, particularly regarding scalability in higher dimensions, and the authors suggest a path forward (using additional assumptions like in REMBO). The potential to apply this principle to other bound-based methods (A*, UCT) is a promising direction for future work.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Resolves a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization (exponential convergence without δ-cover)
- Theoretical analysis provides clear improvement over previous work (polynomial vs. exponential regret)
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse test functions
- Insightful discussion of when and why considering infinitely many bounds is beneficial
- Well-written and well-organized with clear connection to prior work

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of scalability to very high dimensions beyond the REMBO example
- The exponential rate constant λ approaches 1 as dimension increases, but the paper doesn't provide concrete examples of when this becomes problematic
- The parameter Ξmax requires tuning, though the paper shows its effect on performance

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with significant theoretical and practical implications. It addresses a clear open problem, provides rigorous analysis, and demonstrates empirical superiority over baselines. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful advancement over prior work.

The paper meets NIPS criteria for quality (rigorous analysis and evaluation), clarity (well-organized and readable), originality (novel approach to resolving an open problem), and significance (exponential convergence represents a major improvement with practical implications).

I recommend acceptance. The paper is an important contribution that will be valuable to both theoretical and applied Bayesian optimization researchers. The only minor improvement I'd suggest is to provide more concrete examples of when the dimensionality limitation becomes problematic, though this doesn't detract from the paper's substantial contribution.
