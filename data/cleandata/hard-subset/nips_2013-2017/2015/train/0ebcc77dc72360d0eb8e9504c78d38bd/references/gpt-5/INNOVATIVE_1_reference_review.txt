Summary
This paper proposes IMGPO, a new GP-based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions that claims exponential convergence in simple regret without relying on the impractical δ-cover sampling (de Freitas et al., 2012) and without solving auxiliary acquisition optimization. The key idea is to combine hierarchical partitioning with a GP-based UCB to prune, while not committing to any single smoothness/Lipschitz bound. Instead, IMGPO assumes the existence of an unknown semi-metric around the global optimizer (formalized via a one-sided Hölder continuity assumption) and explores “infinitely many” possible bounds through a selection-and-division scheme. The analysis introduces the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt to quantify the cost of maintaining multiple bound candidates. The main theorem gives a regret bound rN = O(exp(−c (N+Ngp))) with constants depending on dimension, partition constant, and ρ̄t, where Ngp counts GP-based “virtual” evaluations used for pruning. Experiments on standard test functions compare IMGPO to SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-PI/EI, showing faster convergence in most cases.

Relation to prior work
- DIRECT and SOO address deterministic global optimization with unknown smoothness; DIRECT is consistent, SOO achieves finite-time bounds under near-optimality dimension. BaMSOO combines GP-UCB with SOO and has polynomial rates.
- De Freitas et al. obtained exponential rates under δ-covers; Srinivas et al. provided frequentist GP-UCB bounds under RKHS assumptions but did not produce exponential rates in the deterministic setting without δ-cover.
- IMGPO is positioned as bridging these lines: it leverages GP UCB to prune while preserving uncertainty over the “true” smoothness bound, and claims exponential rates without δ-cover or solving an acquisition optimization.

Strengths
- Novelty: The “infinite-metric” viewpoint (explicitly reasoning over all candidate semi-metrics and quantifying the exploration loss ρt) is fresh and conceptually interesting. Using the GP only to reduce the candidate set (rather than fully trust its bound) is a bold and pragmatic twist that unifies the DIRECT/SOO and GP-UCB philosophies.
- Theoretical ambition: An exponential convergence result without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization, if correct under the stated assumptions, would be a significant advance.
- Practicality: The algorithm avoids expensive inner optimizations and includes a mechanism to skip evaluations via GP-UCB when safe, which is attractive in practice.
- Empirical results: On a range of benchmarks, IMGPO typically outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, and simple GP-PI/EI baselines, and the runtime is competitive with tree-based methods.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions/validity of UCB: The analysis relies on a high-probability property f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with a choice of ςM = sqrt(log(π^2 M^2/(12η))). This coverage is not guaranteed unless one assumes the function is a GP sample (Bayesian) or belongs to an RKHS with bounded norm plus appropriate βt scaling (frequentist; cf. Srinivas et al., 2010). The current paper does not state such assumptions, yet Theorem 1 claims “with probability at least 1−η.” Please clarify whether the probability is Bayesian (over the GP prior) or frequentist and what function class is assumed. As written, this is the biggest technical gap.
- Theorem clarity/typos: There are notational inconsistencies and what appears to be a typo defining λ (“λ = 3 − α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1” cannot be correct). The bound itself uses exp(−α[(N+Ngp)/(2CDρ̄t) − Ξn − 2] ln 3), which suggests λ = exp(−α ln 3/(2CDρ̄t)). Please fix these to ensure the statement is precise and check the ρ vs t/τ usage.
- Role of Ngp in the rate: The bound depends on N+Ngp, where Ngp counts GP-based placeholders. Since simple regret is defined using actually evaluated f values, including Ngp in the exponent is potentially misleading unless the analysis shows that virtual expansions provably reduce the number of real evaluations needed to reach a given resolution. Please make explicit how Ngp helps shrink near-optimal cells and translate that into a regret improvement measured in N, or present the result purely in terms of the number of node expansions if that is the true algorithmic budget.
- Dependence on dimension: The constants degrade quickly with D (λ approaches 1 as D grows). This is acknowledged; still, a discussion on any structural assumptions (e.g., additive structure, random embeddings) and how IMGPO plugs into those (beyond REMBO in one experiment) would improve the practical significance.
- Experimental setup: While the benchmarks are standard, the BO baselines are not very strong (e.g., no GP-UCB/TS with multi-start L-BFGS, no state-of-the-art acquisition optimizers). Using a single isotropic Matérn with fixed initialization may disadvantage GP methods; a tuning sweep or ARD would be informative. Report more seeds for stochastic baselines. Include ablations (e.g., IMGPO without GP pruning) to isolate the contribution of the GP-based screening and the parameter Ξ.
- Clarity/presentation: Pseudocode is hard to follow; several definitions (T’ sets, ξ selection, depth(T)) could be streamlined. Some references are incomplete; there are minor grammatical issues. The code link appears non-anonymized, which violates double-blind policy.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely state the probabilistic assumptions underlying f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with probability 1−η. Is the guarantee Bayesian or frequentist? If frequentist, what function class? How is ςM derived under that model?
- Can you restate Theorem 1 with a correct, self-contained definition of all constants and λ, and provide a brief proof sketch (key lemmas)?
- How should readers think about N vs Ngp budget? If the evaluation budget is N, what is the bound purely as a function of N?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration in terms of Ξmax and D? Any adaptive rule for Ξ that is theoretically justified?
- Can you provide stronger BO baselines and an ablation study?

Pros
- Bold, original idea of infinite-metric exploration combined with GP pruning.
- Claimed exponential rate without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization is potentially impactful.
- Good empirical performance and practical design choices.

Cons
- Key probabilistic assumption for UCB coverage is underspecified; theorem needs clarification.
- Some notational/typographical issues in the main result.
- Experimental baselines could be stronger; minor double-blind violation.

Clarity: Moderate; could be improved with careful editing and clearer pseudocode.
Quality: Promising but needs clarification on assumptions and a polished theorem statement.
Originality: High.
Significance: Potentially high if the theoretical claim is solid.

Recommendation: Weak accept (7). The paper advances a fresh direction with an ambitious and valuable claim; with clarifications and fixes, it would be a strong contribution. My enthusiasm stems from the novelty and the unification of GP and infinite-metric ideas, despite some rough edges. Confidence: medium.
