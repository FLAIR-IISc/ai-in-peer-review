### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization (BO): achieving an exponential convergence rate for GP-based optimization without relying on impractical assumptions like access to a δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which hybridizes the hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO with the probabilistic modeling of GP-UCB.

The key conceptual contribution is to move away from relying solely on the GP's upper confidence bound to guide the search. Instead, IMGPO operates on the principle of "infinite-metric optimization," first introduced in spirit by DIRECT and formalized by SOO. This approach implicitly considers an infinite set of possible smoothness assumptions (semi-metrics) around the global optimum. The algorithm maintains a hierarchical partitioning of the search space and, at each iteration, selects promising regions (hyperrectangles) for further division. The novelty lies in how the GP model is integrated: the GP-UCB is not used to select the next single point to evaluate, but rather as a powerful pruning mechanism to discard entire sub-trees of the search hierarchy that are unlikely to contain the optimum. This prevents wasteful exploration in regions deemed unpromising by the GP posterior.

This work sits at the intersection of two major lines of research in deterministic global optimization. It directly extends the work of Munos [18] on SOO and Wang et al. [2] on BaMSOO by providing a tighter, exponential regret bound, which BaMSOO's polynomial bound fell short of. It also provides a practical algorithm that realizes the theoretical promise of de Freitas et al. [1], who first showed exponential regret for GP-BO but required an impractical δ-cover sampler.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important open problem in the field. Achieving an exponential regret bound without unrealistic assumptions is a major theoretical advance for Bayesian optimization. The core idea of using the GP posterior as a pruning tool for a partitioning-based "infinite-metric" search is both novel and elegant. It successfully merges the robustness of partitioning methods with the sample efficiency of probabilistic models.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary result, Theorem 1, provides a formal proof of an exponential convergence rate, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which is a clear improvement over the state of the art (e.g., BaMSOO). The analysis introduces the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (`ρ_t`), a useful concept for quantifying the trade-off between global and local search in this framework. The remarks in Section 4 are particularly insightful, clearly delineating the benefits of the GP component (Remark 2) and the infinite-metric approach (Remark 3), providing a deeper understanding of why the hybrid method works.

3.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing IMGPO against a strong set of relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI) on a variety of benchmark functions with varying dimensionalities. The results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of IMGPO, validating the theoretical claims. The authors are also intellectually honest, noting a case (Rosenbrock2) where a misleading GP prior harms performance relative to the non-GP baseline (SOO), which strengthens the credibility of the evaluation. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO showcases a path towards scaling the method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Algorithm Complexity and Clarity:** The proposed algorithm is quite complex. While the high-level description in Section 3.2 is helpful, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to parse. The intricate interplay between the two main loops (lines 8-17 and 19-24), the management of dummy "GP-based" values, and the update rule for the parameter `Ξ` are not immediately intuitive. The paper would benefit greatly from a more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of the logic, particularly the filtering conditions in lines 11, 13, and 23.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** Like all methods based on hierarchical space partitioning, IMGPO is fundamentally susceptible to the curse of dimensionality. The regret bound in Theorem 1 has a dependency on the dimension `D` through the constant `C`, which is not made explicit but is known to be problematic for partitioning schemes. As `D` grows, the number of partitions required to isolate the optimum explodes, and `λ` will approach 1. While the authors acknowledge this and demonstrate a wrapper (REMBO), the scalability of the core algorithm remains a significant practical limitation.

3.  **New Hyperparameters and Ad-hoc Rules:** The algorithm introduces a new hyperparameter, `Ξ_max`, which limits the depth of the UCB lookahead search. Its sensitivity is not thoroughly analyzed. More critically, the update rule for `Ξ` (line 38) seems heuristic. While it intuitively balances exploration and exploitation (increasing `Ξ` on finding a better point), a more principled justification for this specific update form (`+2^2`, `-2^-1`) is missing. This adds a layer of "art" to what is otherwise a theoretically-grounded algorithm.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a very strong paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. The authors have successfully solved a standing open problem, delivering the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. The conceptual framework of unifying partitioning methods with GP-based pruning is powerful and could inspire further work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental results are compelling.

While the algorithm's complexity and scalability are valid concerns, they do not detract from the core contribution. The weaknesses are largely related to presentation and practical tuning, which can be addressed in future work (and potentially in a revision). The strengths—solving an open problem with a novel method backed by strong theory and experiments—are undeniable. This paper represents a clear advance for the field and is highly likely to be impactful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Solves a well-known open problem in BO theory.
*   Presents a novel and elegant algorithm combining two major optimization paradigms.
*   Provides the first exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based method.
*   Strong empirical results validate the theory against state-of-the-art baselines.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The algorithm is complex and its description could be clearer.
*   The method introduces new, somewhat ad-hoc hyperparameters.
*   Scalability to high dimensions remains a major challenge, inherent to the partitioning approach.

**Recommendation: Accept**
