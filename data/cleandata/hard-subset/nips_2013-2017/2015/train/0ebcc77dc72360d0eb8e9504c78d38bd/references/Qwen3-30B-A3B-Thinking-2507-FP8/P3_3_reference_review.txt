

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper proposes IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work. The authors address a significant gap identified by Wang et al. [2]: creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling. The core idea is to consider "infinitely many possible candidates of the bounds" rather than relying on a single upper bound from the GP prior, while using the GP's bound to prune less promising candidates. The paper provides a theoretical regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 and demonstrates empirical improvements over baselines like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

## Quality Assessment
The theoretical contribution is strong, with a clear formalization of the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt and its relationship to the convergence rate. The analysis (Theorem 1) properly establishes the exponential regret bound under reasonable assumptions. However, the paper has notable weaknesses in its practical evaluation. The authors note that λ approaches 1 as dimension D increases, which undermines the exponential convergence claim in higher dimensions. This limitation is acknowledged but not thoroughly explored - the paper doesn't provide guidance on when IMGPO would be preferable to other methods in high-dimensional settings. 

The experimental section is reasonably well-designed, comparing against relevant baselines on standard benchmarks. However, it lacks a detailed analysis of computational complexity, which is critical for an algorithm claiming to improve theoretical convergence rates. The claim that IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods" needs stronger justification, as the hierarchical partitioning approach could become computationally expensive.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm description to theoretical analysis. The algorithm description (Section 3.2) is somewhat complex due to the hierarchical partitioning scheme, but the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) provides sufficient detail. The relationship to prior work (Section 3.4) is well-articulated, particularly in distinguishing IMGPO from BaMSOO [2] and SOO [18].

The theoretical insights in Remarks 1-3 are particularly valuable and clearly presented, especially the demonstration that considering multiple bounds can be beneficial even when a "good" bound is known. However, the paper could improve clarity by explicitly stating the conditions under which the exponential convergence rate is meaningful in practice.

## Originality
The core idea of considering multiple bounds rather than a single one from GP is novel in the context of Bayesian optimization with exponential convergence guarantees. While the concept of multiple bounds appears in DIRECT [19] and SOO [18], the authors successfully integrate this with GP-based optimization in a way that achieves exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The paper properly situates itself against prior work and clearly articulates how it differs from BaMSOO [2] (which achieves only polynomial regret bounds).

The most significant original contribution is the theoretical framework that explains why considering multiple bounds can be beneficial, even when a "good" bound is known (Remark 3). This provides valuable theoretical insights beyond just the algorithmic contribution.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important theoretical gap in Bayesian optimization with significant practical implications. Exponential convergence rates are theoretically superior to polynomial ones, and the paper successfully demonstrates this in their experiments. The experimental results (Figure 2) generally support the theoretical claims, showing IMGPO outperforming baselines on most test functions.

However, the practical significance is somewhat tempered by two factors: (1) the computational cost of the hierarchical partitioning approach, which isn't thoroughly analyzed, and (2) the dimensionality limitation where λ approaches 1 as D increases. The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on when IMGPO would be preferable to other methods in real-world applications.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- First GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Clear theoretical analysis with well-defined assumptions
- Insightful discussion of the trade-offs between using a single bound vs. multiple bounds
- Good experimental validation against relevant baselines

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and practical scalability
- Limited exploration of the dimensionality limitation (λ→1 as D increases)
- No clear practical guidance on when to use IMGPO versus other methods
- Experimental section lacks depth in analyzing computational cost trade-offs

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization that addresses a clear gap in the literature. The exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling is a meaningful advance over prior work.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Expand the discussion of computational complexity and practical scalability, particularly regarding the dimensionality limitation
2. Provide more concrete guidance on when IMGPO would be preferable to other methods
3. Include a more detailed analysis of the computational cost trade-offs in the experiments

The paper's theoretical insights (particularly Remarks 1-3) are valuable contributions to the field that go beyond just the algorithmic proposal, making this a significant contribution to the literature on Bayesian optimization.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with clear practical implications, meeting NIPS's standards for quality, significance, and originality.
