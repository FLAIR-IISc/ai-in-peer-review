Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses deterministic global optimization of a black-box function f over a bounded domain. It introduces IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization method that aims to achieve exponential simple-regret convergence without relying on the δ-cover sampling procedure of de Freitas et al. (ICML’12) and without solving auxiliary continuous acquisition maximization problems (as in GP-UCB, EI/PI). IMGPO combines hierarchical partitioning (as in SOO/DOO and DIRECT) with GP-based upper confidence bounds to filter which rectangles to refine. A key conceptual ingredient is to not commit to a single (possibly loose) bound on f; instead, the method “hedges” over infinitely many admissible semi-metric bounds around a global optimizer, formalized via Assumption 1 and a Hölder-type continuity Assumption 2 (`(x, x*) ≤ L||x−x*||_p^α, with unknown L, α, p). The algorithm tracks an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρ_t that reflects the cost of not committing to a particular bound.

The main theoretical claim is an exponential simple-regret bound r_N = O(λ^{N+N_gp}) with λ < 1, with probability at least 1−η, where N is the number of function evaluations and N_gp counts GP-based “virtual” evaluations. The rate depends on constants including the dimension D, an expansion budget Ξ_n, and the average exploration loss ρ̄_t. The analysis positions IMGPO between the δ-cover result of de Freitas et al. (exponential but impractical sampling) and BaMSOO (AISTATS’14), which combines GP-UCB with SOO and yields polynomial rates. The empirical section compares IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI, and GP-PI on several multimodal benchmarks and a high-dimensional case via REMBO, showing improved or competitive performance.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Positive aspects:
  - The hierarchical partitioning scheme is clearly specified; the algorithm avoids continuous inner optimization by only evaluating UCBs at finitely many candidate centers in subtrees of bounded depth Ξ, which is computationally attractive.
  - Introducing the infinite-metric exploration loss ρ_t provides a transparent way to quantify the overhead of not fixing a bound and to connect it to the regret analysis.
  - The regret bound has an explicit exponential form in N+N_gp and clarifies the roles of D, α, p, and the algorithmic parameters, relating to the inherent difficulty of the function class.

- Concerns:
  - The probability statement underlying UCB requires assumptions beyond those stated. In standard GP-UCB analyses (e.g., Srinivas et al., ICML’10), the inequality f(x) ≤ μ(x) + β_t^{1/2} σ(x) with high probability is proven under assumptions such as f lying in the RKHS of the kernel with bounded norm and fixed kernel hyperparameters. Here, the paper asserts f(x) ≤ U(x|D) “with some probability,” chooses ς_M via a union bound, and updates GP hyperparameters online, but does not specify conditions ensuring that the high-probability UCB event holds for a fixed, unknown deterministic f. Without a frequentist-type assumption (e.g., RKHS norm bound) or assuming f is a GP sample from the prior, the claimed high-probability regret may not be justified.
  - The theorem statement appears to have a typographical or notational error: “Let λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t < 1” is inconsistent with the displayed exponential bound involving exp(−α[(N+N_gp)/(2CD ρ̄_t) − Ξ_n − 2] ln 3). Presumably λ should be 3^{−α/(2CD ρ̄_t)} (or similar). This should be corrected to avoid ambiguity.
  - The regret bound depends on algorithm-internal, data-dependent quantities (ρ̄_t, Ξ_n, N_gp) that are not bounded a priori in a way accessible to the practitioner. While the remarks discuss best- and worst-case regimes for ρ̄_t, a clean high-probability bound that depends only on N and problem parameters would strengthen the result.
  - Updating GP hyperparameters during optimization is standard in practice but can undermine the theoretical UCB property unless the analysis explicitly accommodates data-driven hyperparameters.
  - Complexity: Step (iii) requires evaluating U at all nodes in a fully expanded subtree up to depth Ξ, potentially up to 3^Ξ points per candidate rectangle. The paper caps this via Ξ_max but does not provide clear time-complexity bounds per iteration or guidance on setting Ξ_max beyond empirical choices.

Clarity
- The main ideas and algorithmic flow are explained, and the connection to DIRECT/SOO/BaMSOO is clear. However:
  - Several notational and typographical issues (e.g., the λ definition above; line “Ξ ← Ξ + 22” in Algorithm 1; inconsistent references to t vs. n) impede readability.
  - The probabilistic assumptions required for the UCB property should be explicitly stated and distinguished (Bayesian vs. frequentist guarantees).
  - Provide a succinct complexity discussion (in terms of N, D, and Ξ_max) and a short paragraph on how to set η and Ξ_max in practice.

Originality
- The combination of infinite-metric partition-based search with GP-UCB-style screening and the attempt to obtain exponential regret without δ-cover sampling appears novel. The analysis framework that quantifies the effect of considering infinitely many bounds via ρ_t is also original relative to SOO/BaMSOO. To my knowledge, there is no prior GP-based method with a provable exponential regret rate under similar assumptions and without δ-cover sampling.

Significance
- If the high-probability guarantees can be made precise (clarifying the assumptions under which UCB holds while hyperparameters may be adapted), this would be a meaningful theoretical advance and a practically relevant algorithm (no auxiliary acquisition optimization). The empirical results are encouraging, including in conjunction with REMBO for higher dimensions. Dependence on dimension remains exponential in constants; the authors acknowledge this and suggest known additivity/embedding remedies.

Pros
- Conceptually appealing integration of GP information and partition-based optimistic search.
- Exponential regret bound claimed without δ-cover sampling and without continuous inner optimization.
- Clear articulation of the benefits of not committing to a single bound; informative remarks.
- Solid empirical comparisons on standard benchmarks; code availability.

Cons
- High-probability UCB event is used without clearly stated sufficient conditions; online hyperparameter adaptation further complicates guarantees.
- The main theorem’s λ notation/typo and some algorithm pseudocode notational issues reduce clarity.
- The regret bound depends on data-dependent quantities (ρ̄_t, Ξ_n, N_gp) without a priori bounds or tuning guidance.
- No results for noisy observations; scope limited to deterministic settings.
- Limited ablations to isolate the contribution of GP screening versus pure SOO-like search under mis-specified kernels.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Specify the exact assumptions under which f(x) ≤ U(x|D) for all U calls holds with probability ≥ 1−η. Does the analysis assume f is a sample from the GP prior, or that f lies in an RKHS with bounded norm (as in Srinivas et al., 2010)? How do hyperparameter updates affect this property?
- Correct the definition of λ in Theorem 1 and clean up pseudocode lines (e.g., the update “Ξ ← Ξ + 22”).
- Provide computational complexity per iteration in terms of Ξ_max, D, and the number of active depths; offer practical guidance for choosing Ξ_max and η.
- Include an ablation that (i) disables GP screening (pure infinite-metric search) and (ii) fixes versus adapts hyperparameters to quantify their effects on ρ̄_t, N_gp, and performance.
- Discuss extension to noisy settings, or clarify that the scope is deterministic only.

Overall assessment
The paper proposes a promising and apparently novel approach with strong empirical performance and an ambitious theoretical claim. Clarifying and tightening the probabilistic assumptions behind the UCB usage and fixing presentation issues would substantially strengthen the submission.
