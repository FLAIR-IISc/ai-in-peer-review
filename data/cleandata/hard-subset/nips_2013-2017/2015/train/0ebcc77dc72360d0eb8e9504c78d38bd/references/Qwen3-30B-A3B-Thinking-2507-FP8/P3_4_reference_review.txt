

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary

This paper proposes IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm claiming to achieve exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous methods. The authors address an open problem identified by Wang et al. regarding GP-based optimization with exponential convergence. The key innovation is the "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept, where the algorithm simultaneously considers multiple candidate bounds (via hierarchical partitioning) rather than relying on a single GP upper confidence bound (UCB). The paper provides theoretical regret bounds and experimental results showing superiority over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement**: The paper effectively identifies the open problem (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling) and positions its contribution within the Bayesian optimization literature.

2. **Theoretical Contribution**: The regret bound in Theorem 1 is a significant theoretical advance, providing a concrete exponential convergence rate. The authors' analysis of how GP's UCB affects the regret bound (Remarks 1-3) is particularly valuable.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on eight benchmark functions demonstrate consistent performance improvements over baselines, with IMGPO outperforming other methods in most cases.

4. **Practical Considerations**: The authors address the practical concern about GP's UCB not being tight, providing a nuanced analysis of when it's beneficial to use GP information versus considering multiple bounds.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Claim Misleading**: The paper claims "exponential convergence rate" but the bound λ = 3 - α/2CDρ̄t < 1 depends on dimension D. As noted in Remark 1, λ approaches 1 as D increases, making the bound practically meaningless for high-dimensional problems. This severely limits the paper's significance for real-world applications where high dimensionality is common.

2. **Lack of Clarity on δ-Cover Avoidance**: The paper claims to avoid δ-cover sampling, but it's unclear how this differs from BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS 2014), which also claims to avoid it. The paper states "BaMSOO only achieves polynomial regret" while "IMGPO achieves exponential regret," but the difference in the algorithmic structure (hierarchical partitioning vs. SOO integration) needs clearer explanation.

3. **Algorithmic Ambiguity**: The explanation of how the "unknown bound" (Assumption 1) interacts with the GP's UCB is confusing. The paper states "we do not expect the known upper bound due to GP to be tight," but then uses GP's UCB as a filter for candidate intervals (steps (i)-(iii)). The relationship between these two bounds needs more precise mathematical formulation.

4. **Experimental Limitations**: The experiments are limited to low-dimensional problems (max dimension 1000, but only 1-100 dimensions tested). The paper claims to address high-dimensional optimization via REMBO, but this is a minor component of the main contribution and not properly evaluated. The computational cost of IMGPO (particularly the Ξmax parameter) isn't thoroughly analyzed.

5. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper cites BaMSOO as having polynomial regret, but the authors' own bound (Theorem 1) appears to be better than BaMSOO's. However, the paper doesn't clearly articulate *why* their bound is better or how their approach differs fundamentally from BaMSOO.

## Critical Analysis

The core theoretical contribution—exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp))—is compelling, but the dependence on dimension D (λ = 3 - α/2CDρ̄t < 1) is a critical flaw. For D > 3, λ approaches 1, making the exponential decay negligible. This is a fundamental limitation that the authors should address more honestly. In contrast, BaMSOO's regret bound (O(N^(-2α/D(4-α)))) doesn't have this dimensionality issue, though it's polynomial rather than exponential.

The paper's most interesting contribution is the "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept, which provides a principled way to balance between using GP's UCB and considering multiple candidate bounds. However, the practical implementation (Algorithm 1) doesn't clearly demonstrate how this leads to exponential convergence in practice, especially given the dimensionality issue.

The experimental results are promising but limited. The claim that IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods" is questionable without runtime comparisons. The paper shows that IMGPO outperforms baselines in terms of function evaluations, but doesn't provide the actual CPU time required, which is crucial for real-world applications.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to Bayesian optimization, but the exponential convergence claim is misleading due to the dimensionality dependence. The main limitation is that the proposed method would be impractical for high-dimensional problems, which are common in machine learning applications.

The paper would be significantly stronger if:
1. The authors more honestly addressed the dimensionality issue in the theoretical claim
2. They provided clearer differentiation between their approach and BaMSOO
3. They included more comprehensive scalability experiments
4. They clarified the relationship between the "unknown bound" and GP's UCB

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: 6/10 - The theoretical contribution is non-trivial but the key claim is undermined by dimensionality dependence. The experiments are well-executed but limited in scope.

**Clarity**: 7/10 - The paper is well-organized, but some technical explanations (especially regarding the "unknown bound") are confusing.

**Originality**: 7/10 - The "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept is novel, though the algorithmic approach builds on SOO and GP-UCB.

**Significance**: 5/10 - The exponential convergence claim is overstated for high-dimensional problems, which limits practical significance. The method would be more impactful if it could handle high dimensions.

**Final Recommendation**: Weak Reject. While the paper contains interesting theoretical insights, the key claim of exponential convergence is misleading for practical applications due to dimensionality constraints. The authors should substantially revise the theoretical claims and provide more comprehensive analysis of the method's scalability before submission to NIPS. The paper would be a better fit for a more specialized optimization conference where high-dimensional problems aren't the primary focus.
