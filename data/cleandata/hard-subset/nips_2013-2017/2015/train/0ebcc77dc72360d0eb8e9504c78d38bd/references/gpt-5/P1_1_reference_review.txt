Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP) based global optimizer for deterministic black-box functions that aims to achieve exponential convergence (simple regret O(λN+Ngp) with λ<1) without resorting to the δ-cover sampling procedure of de Freitas et al. (ICML’12) and without solving an auxiliary nonconvex acquisition optimization at each step. The algorithm combines hierarchical partitioning (in the spirit of DIRECT/SOO/DOO) with GP-derived upper confidence bounds (UCB) used only as a filter to prune unpromising subregions. A key conceptual device is the “infinite-metric” idea: instead of committing to a single (possibly loose) smoothness bound, the algorithm hedges over infinitely many potential semi-metrics and uses GP UCB to reduce the number of candidates. The analysis introduces the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt (how many cells divided per iteration), and shows how the regret depends on ρ̄t, the average of ρt, the number of true function evaluations N, the number of GP-only surrogate evaluations Ngp, and constants tied to a Hölder-type assumption near the global optimizer.

This connects two lines of work: optimistic partitioning methods without known Lipschitz constants (HOO/DOO/SOO; Munos NIPS’11) and GP-based BO (Srinivas et al. ICML’10; Snoek et al. NIPS’12). It explicitly improves upon BaMSOO (Wang et al. AISTATS’14), which had polynomial regret under similar assumptions, and on de Freitas et al. who required δ-covers to get exponential regret. The empirical section compares to SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-PI/EI (with a simple solver and empirical Bayes) on eight benchmark functions and reports faster convergence for IMGPO in most cases.

Quality:
- Theory: The main theorem claims exponential simple regret under Assumptions 1–2 (existence of an unknown semi-metric and local Hölder continuity). The structure of the bound is insightful: it shows how tighter GP UCB (smaller ρ̄t, larger Ngp) helps and how computational limits (Ξn) may hurt. However, key aspects need clarification/strengthening:
  - The expression for λ is unclear: the text states “λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t < 1”, which is dimensionally inconsistent and likely a typesetting error; presumably λ = 3^{-α/(2 C D ρ̄t)} or similar. Please state λ precisely.
  - The UCB validity (f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with probability 1−η across all calls) is asserted with a schedule ςM = sqrt(log(π^2 M^2/(12η))). This relies on a fixed GP prior. But the algorithm updates GP hyperparameters after each iteration via empirical Bayes. This generally invalidates standard GP-UCB confidence guarantees. Also, experiments note that UCB can be violated (Rosenbrock2). The theorem should either assume fixed kernel/hyperparameters or provide a new argument for data-dependent hyperparameters.
  - The bound depends on ρ̄t, but there is no nontrivial upper bound on ρ̄t under the stated assumptions; remarks argue qualitatively about “useful” UCBs, but the exponential rate can be vacuous if ρ̄t grows with N. Some structural control (e.g., conditions guaranteeing ρ̄t=O(1) or O(log N)) would greatly strengthen the result.
  - The role and growth of Ξn and its effect on computational cost/regret need a clearer statement (e.g., a bound on the total number of UCB queries per iteration).
- Algorithmic clarity: The high-level idea is sound and well-motivated, but several notational/instructional glitches hinder reproducibility:
  - Several lines in Algorithm 1 appear inconsistent or mistyped: the acceptance condition in step (iii) uses z(h,i∗h) < g(ch+ξ,i∗h+ξ) but the definitions of indices and the direction of comparisons are hard to follow; ςM formula lacks context; variables N, n, t, Ngp, Ξ, and Ξmax are used with overlapping purposes; “Ξ← Ξ + 22” is unclear; “β = supx,x′ 1/2‖x−x′‖∞” is unusual (why 1/2?). A careful pass is needed.
- Experiments: Results are promising but raise methodological questions:
  - Hyperparameters are learned (empirical Bayes), which is realistic but contradicts theoretical assumptions; this discrepancy should be discussed explicitly.
  - GP-PI/EI optimization of acquisitions is handled by SOO/gradients; ensure fair computational budgets and report wall-clock times with a breakdown of f-eval vs UCB-eval overhead. Only a brief runtime table is mentioned.
  - Deterministic algorithms are run from a fixed initial point; PI/EI are averaged over 50 random restarts. For fairness, either randomize initializations for all or fix the same starting point across methods and report both fixed-start and multi-start results.
  - Provide details for the REMBO experiment: embedding dimension, bounds, and how IMGPO interacts with the embedding.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable, the motivation is clear, and the figure/algorithm help. However, the proof-dependent notation and several typos/ambiguities make the core claims hard to verify. I strongly suggest:
- Define all symbols once and stick to them; unify N (true evals), Ngp (virtual evals), n (node expansions), t (iterations).
- State Theorem 1 with all constants (C, β, λ) precisely and give assumptions on kernel/noise/hyperparameters.
- Improve the description of step (iii) and the construction of T′h+ξ.

Originality:
The idea of hedging over an infinite set of smoothness metrics while using GP UCB as a filter is novel and interesting. The analysis framing via ρt and the decomposition of the regret into N, Ngp, ρ̄t, and Ξn is insightful. If the exponential rate can be established under realistic conditions (e.g., fixed hyperparameters or justified learning), this would be a notable advance beyond BaMSOO. Related work is mostly covered (SOO/DOO/DIRECT, GP-UCB, REMBO), but please also cite results on convergence rates for EI/GP BO in deterministic settings (e.g., Bull 2011) and HOO/POO/StoSOO variants.

Significance:
Potentially high. A practically viable GP-based optimizer with exponential deterministic regret would be valuable. The empirical results suggest effectiveness even with non-ideal kernels/hyperparameters. The current theoretical gap around hyperparameter learning and the dependence on ρ̄t temper the impact.

Pros:
- Ambitious, well-motivated combination of optimistic partitioning and GP UCB.
- New analysis perspective (infinite-metric exploration loss) with interpretable terms.
- Claims to remove δ-cover and auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Open-source code; empirical gains on several benchmarks.

Cons:
- Theoretical guarantees appear to assume fixed GP prior; conflict with empirical Bayes updates and observed UCB violations.
- Ambiguities/typos in theorem and algorithm hinder verification.
- No bound/control on ρ̄t; “exponential” rate may be weak in practice without stronger conditions.
- Experimental protocol fairness and computational budget reporting need tightening.

Questions for authors:
- Can you restate Theorem 1 precisely, including λ and all constants, and specify whether kernel/hyperparameters are fixed?
- Can you provide a confidence guarantee when hyperparameters are learned, or restrict the theorem to the fixed-hyperparameter setting?
- Do you have empirical measurements of ρ̄t and Ξn over time to illustrate why the exponential rate is meaningful?
- Can you report wall-clock times with breakdowns and ensure comparable budgets across methods?

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is compelling and the empirical results are promising, but the current paper has theoretical and clarity issues (notably around the GP confidence with learned hyperparameters and the precise statement of the exponential bound) that should be addressed for a NIPS-level contribution. I would be inclined to accept a revised version that fixes the theorem/assumptions, clarifies the algorithm, and tightens the experimental protocol.
