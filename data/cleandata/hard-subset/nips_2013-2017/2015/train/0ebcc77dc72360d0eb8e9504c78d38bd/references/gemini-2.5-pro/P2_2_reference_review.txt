### Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a key open problem in Bayesian Optimization (BO): developing a Gaussian Process (GP)-based algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without relying on impractical assumptions like δ-cover sampling or requiring an auxiliary optimization procedure to maximize an acquisition function. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which elegantly synthesizes ideas from two distinct lines of global optimization research: the hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO, and the probabilistic modeling of GP-UCB.

The core idea of IMGPO is to maintain a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. At each iteration, it identifies promising hyperrectangles at all scales (depths) that could contain the global optimum. This is characteristic of "infinite-metric" methods that do not commit to a single smoothness assumption. The novelty lies in how the GP model is used to prune this search. Instead of solely relying on the GP's Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) to select the next point, IMGPO uses the UCB to discard candidate hyperrectangles from the partitioning scheme that are probabilistically unpromising. This allows the algorithm to focus its evaluations effectively while still benefiting from the theoretical guarantees of the partitioning method. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound, improving upon the polynomial bounds of prior practical GP-based methods like BaMSOO. They also present strong empirical results on several benchmark functions, demonstrating superior performance compared to SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-BO methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical result. It provides a positive answer to a well-recognized open problem highlighted by previous work [1, 2]: achieving exponential regret for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm. This improves significantly on the polynomial rates of the most closely related practical algorithm, BaMSOO. The analysis in Remark 3, which provides an intuitive condition for when their "infinite-metric" approach is superior to relying on a single (potentially misspecified) bound, is particularly insightful.

2.  **Novel and Sound Algorithmic Approach:** The synthesis of hierarchical partitioning with GP-based pruning is clever and novel. It successfully leverages the strengths of both paradigms: the robust convergence properties of partitioning methods that don't rely on a known Lipschitz constant, and the sample-efficiency of GPs which model the function globally. The mechanism for using the UCB to filter candidates (step iii) rather than to guide the search directly is the key insight that eliminates the need for an auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function—a major practical bottleneck for many BO methods.

3.  **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is more practical than its predecessors with similar theoretical guarantees. It dispenses with the δ-cover sampling requirement of de Freitas et al. [1], which is computationally infeasible in most settings. By avoiding the need to solve `argmax U(x)` at each step, it is also computationally more appealing than standard GP-UCB, as confirmed by the timing results in Table 1.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing IMGPO against the most relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI) on a variety of functions with different dimensionalities and characteristics. The results in Figure 2 consistently show IMGPO outperforming or matching the best alternative, providing compelling evidence for its practical effectiveness. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine hints at its broader applicability.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm Description:** The main weakness of the paper is the clarity of the algorithm's description in Section 3. While the high-level overview is clear, the detailed procedure in Section 3.2 and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 are quite dense and difficult to follow. The logic behind the selection and filtering steps (i-iii), involving comparisons of center values and UCBs across different depths (`υ_max`, `g(ch,i∗h)`, `z(h, i∗h)`), is complex. A more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of the example in Figure 1, explicitly mapping each decision to the conditions in Algorithm 1, would significantly improve readability. For example, clarifying how `υ_max` is used to propagate the "best-so-far" value from larger to smaller intervals (line 11) is crucial.

2.  **Complexity of the Pruning Step:** The computational cost of the UCB-based pruning step (lines 21-22) is not fully analyzed. This step requires finding the maximum UCB value within a potential subtree of depth ξ, which could involve many UCB calculations. While the authors state this rarely happens due to the `Ξmax` cap, the worst-case complexity and its practical implications could be discussed more explicitly.

3.  **Sensitivity to Parameters:** The algorithm introduces new parameters, notably `Ξmax` and the update rule for `Ξ`. The paper fixes `Ξmax = 22` and mentions that performance could be improved on some functions by increasing it, at the cost of more computation. A more thorough discussion or ablation study on the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance and runtime to this parameter would strengthen the paper.

**Detailed Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims appear sound and are a clear advance over prior work. The experimental methodology is fair and supports the claims, especially the decision not to hand-pick kernels for each function, which reflects a more realistic use case. The work is complete and self-contained.

*   **Clarity:** As mentioned, this is the paper's weakest point. The writing is generally good, but the core algorithm description is overly condensed. The paper would be much stronger if Section 3 were expanded and restructured for clarity. The motivation and high-level ideas are communicated well, but the low-level details needed for reproduction are difficult to extract.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon the established foundations of SOO and GP-UCB, the specific way these are combined is novel and not an incremental extension. It successfully bridges two important sub-literatures in global optimization that have largely evolved in parallel. The contribution is clearly delineated from previous work.

*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. This paper advances the state of the art in theoretical BO and provides a practical, high-performance algorithm. Given the widespread use of BO for hyperparameter tuning and other expensive optimization problems, a new algorithm that is both theoretically well-founded (offering exponential convergence) and practically efficient (avoiding auxiliary optimization) is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by both researchers and practitioners. The suggestion that this philosophy could apply to other bound-based search algorithms is thought-provoking and points to broader impact.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant advance in Bayesian Optimization. The authors solve a known open problem with a novel and practical algorithm, supported by strong theoretical and empirical evidence. The main drawback is the lack of clarity in the detailed algorithm description, which makes it difficult to understand the implementation details. However, this is a correctable flaw. I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper is a perfect fit for NIPS, offering a blend of new theory, a novel algorithm, and practical utility.
