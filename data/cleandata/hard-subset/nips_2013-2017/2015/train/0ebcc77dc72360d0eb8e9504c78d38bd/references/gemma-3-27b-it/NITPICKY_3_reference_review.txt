This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP) based global optimization algorithm, IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea revolves around simultaneously considering multiple possible bounds on the objective function, leveraging the GP prior to filter implausible bounds, and employing hierarchical partitioning for efficient exploration. The paper includes a theoretical analysis proving an exponential convergence rate and experimental results demonstrating superior performance compared to several baseline algorithms.

While the idea of considering multiple bounds is intriguing and the theoretical result is noteworthy, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws and inconsistencies that ultimately detract from its overall impact. My assessment is heavily influenced by the meticulous nature expected of a NIPS review, and thus reflects a critical evaluation of even seemingly small details. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The exponential convergence guarantee without δ-cover sampling is a significant theoretical advancement, addressing a key limitation of previous work.
*   **Novel Approach:** The concept of simultaneously considering multiple bounds and leveraging the GP prior for filtering is a unique and potentially powerful strategy.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results, while not exhaustive, generally demonstrate the superiority of IMGPO over several established algorithms.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and its importance, connecting it to relevant applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation is occasionally inconsistent. For instance, ` is introduced as a semi-metric in Assumption 1, but its usage throughout the analysis and algorithm description feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a clear geometrical interpretation. It isn’t clear *why* this particular semi-metric form is assumed, and the implications of other forms aren't discussed.
*   **Algorithm Description Clarity:** While Figure 1 attempts to illustrate the algorithm, it lacks sufficient detail to fully understand the decision-making process at each step. The explanation of steps (i)-(v) is somewhat convoluted and could benefit from more precise algorithmic pseudocode. The description of how the 'temporary dummy values' are handled is particularly unclear.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The role of Ξmax is not fully elucidated. The authors state that increasing it can improve performance, but the trade-off between computational cost and performance gain is not adequately discussed. The choice of Ξmax = 22 seems arbitrary and lacks justification.
*   **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The claim of using “blindly initialized” hyperparameters is misleading. While the initial values are fixed, the subsequent empirical Bayesian update *is* a form of hyperparameter optimization. The authors need to be more precise in their description. Furthermore, the lack of statistical significance testing in the experimental results is a concern. Reporting means and standard deviations is insufficient to conclude performance differences.
*   **Remark 1 & 2 Contradiction**: Remark 1 suggests that performance can be *worse* than the theoretical bound if the GP prior is poor. However, Remark 2 states the use of GP *reduces* the regret bound. This needs clarification and a more nuanced discussion of the interplay between the GP model and the proposed algorithm.
*   **Theoretical Assumptions:** The assumptions of the theorem (Assumption 1 and 2) feel somewhat strong without a deeper justification of their applicability to real-world problems. A discussion of how these assumptions might be relaxed or mitigated would be valuable.
*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors acknowledge a scalability issue in the analysis (λ approaching 1 with increasing dimensionality), the discussion remains superficial. The attempt to address this with REMBO is a brief mention and lacks substantial details.
*   **Minor Grammatical Errors and Typos:** Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor grammatical errors and typos that detract from its professionalism. 

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Significant theoretical contribution with an exponential convergence guarantee.
*   Novel algorithmic approach with potential for practical impact.
*   Generally positive experimental results.

**Con:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, algorithm description, and experimental setup.
*   Lack of robust statistical analysis.
*   Insufficient discussion of parameter sensitivity and scalability.
*   Potential contradictions in the analysis and discussion.
*   The theoretical assumptions may be too restrictive.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea of IMGPO and its theoretical result are promising, the paper’s numerous flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of clarity, the inconsistencies in notation, the superficial discussion of key parameters, and the weak experimental analysis all contribute to a sense of incompleteness and imprecision. A major revision addressing these issues is required before this paper could be considered for publication at NIPS. Specifically, the algorithm description needs to be more precise, the experimental validation needs to be more rigorous, and the theoretical assumptions and their implications need to be thoroughly discussed. The authors should also address the contradictions between Remarks 1 and 2.



**Rating:** Reject.
