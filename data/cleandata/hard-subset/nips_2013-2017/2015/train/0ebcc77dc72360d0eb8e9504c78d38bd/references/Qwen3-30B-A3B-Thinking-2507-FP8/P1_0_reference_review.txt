

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work. The authors address a significant limitation in the Bayesian optimization literature: while de Freitas et al. [1] achieved exponential convergence, their method relied on δ-cover sampling, which is computationally expensive. Wang et al. [2] combined GP-UCB with hierarchical partitioning (SOO) but only achieved polynomial regret bounds. The authors' key insight is to consider infinitely many possible candidates for an unknown bound (semi-metric) encoded in the continuity at the global optimizer, rather than relying solely on the bound provided by the GP.

The paper builds on the work of Munos [18] on SOO and Jones et al. [19] on DIRECT, but significantly advances the field by providing a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential regret bounds. The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which shows that IMGPO achieves regret bound O(λ^{N+Ngp}) with λ < 1, where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp is the number of GP evaluations.

## Strengths

### Quality
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-developed, with careful definitions and assumptions. The authors properly justify their approach by formalizing the relationship between the unknown bound and the GP-based bound.
- The paper thoroughly addresses the limitations of previous work, particularly the δ-cover sampling requirement of de Freitas et al. and the polynomial regret bound of Wang et al.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on eight different benchmark functions across multiple dimensions (1 to 1000), with appropriate baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI).
- The authors demonstrate practical advantages of their approach, showing that IMGPO outperforms other methods in most cases.

### Originality
- The core idea of considering infinitely many possible candidates for the unknown bound (semi-metric) is genuinely novel and represents a significant theoretical advancement over previous work.
- The paper provides a new theoretical framework that bridges the gap between bound-based search methods that rely on a specific bound (like GP-UCB) and those that consider multiple bounds (like DIRECT and SOO).
- The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work, particularly BaMSOO [2], by showing how IMGPO achieves exponential convergence where BaMSOO only achieves polynomial bounds.

### Significance
- The exponential convergence rate is a major theoretical advance over the polynomial convergence rates of previous GP-based methods.
- The paper provides a practical framework for understanding when and why not relying on a single bound (even a non-tight one) can be beneficial, which has broader implications beyond Bayesian optimization.
- The experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages of the proposed method across a range of benchmark problems.

## Weaknesses

### Quality
- The theoretical analysis relies on Assumption 2, which requires the semi-metric to be bounded by a specific form (L||x'-x||^α_p). It's not clear how restrictive this assumption is in practice, and the paper doesn't provide evidence of how often this condition holds for typical optimization problems.
- The scalability to high dimensions is acknowledged as a limitation (λ approaches 1 as D increases), but the paper doesn't provide a concrete strategy for addressing this challenge beyond a brief mention of "additional assumptions" like those in [14, 20].
- The computational complexity of the algorithm isn't fully analyzed, particularly regarding how the infinite-metric exploration loss ρ̄_t grows with the number of function evaluations.

### Clarity
- The concept of "infinite-metric exploration loss" (Definition 1) is introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation, making it difficult to grasp why this metric is important.
- The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is quite complex, and while it's accompanied by a detailed description, it would benefit from more explanatory comments to aid understanding.
- The relationship between the unknown bound ` and the GP-based bound isn't fully explained, making it hard to understand the conditions under which the algorithm will perform well.

### Significance
- While the paper demonstrates exponential convergence, it doesn't provide a clear roadmap for how to apply this method to extremely high-dimensional problems, which is a major application area for Bayesian optimization.
- The paper mentions that the algorithm could be used as a subroutine for higher-dimensional problems (e.g., with REMBO), but doesn't provide experimental evidence of this approach's effectiveness.

## Specific Comments

1. Theorem 1's regret bound depends on Ξ_n (the largest ξ used so far), but it's not clear how Ξ_n grows with N, which would help understand the practical behavior of the algorithm.

2. The paper mentions "the effect of GP" in Remark 2, but it's not clear whether the GP-based bound is always beneficial or if there are cases where it might be detrimental.

3. The experimental section could benefit from a more detailed analysis of why IMGPO outperforms other methods on specific functions. For example, why does it perform particularly well on Sin1 and Sin2 but not as well on Rosenbrock2 (where SOO is competitive)?

4. The authors could provide more intuition about how the Ξ_max parameter affects the algorithm's performance. The paper mentions that increasing Ξ_max improves performance for some functions at the cost of extra CPU time, but doesn't explain why.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature that successfully addresses a significant open problem: achieving exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides clear experimental validation of its theoretical claims.

The main weaknesses are related to the scalability to high dimensions and the lack of detailed explanation about the relationship between the unknown bound and the GP-based bound. However, these are common challenges in the field, and the paper makes a significant step forward in addressing the exponential convergence problem.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by providing a new theoretical framework that explains when not relying on a single bound can be advantageous, which has implications beyond Bayesian optimization.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, addressing a fundamental limitation of previous work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are comprehensive, and the paper is well-written. While the scalability to high dimensions is a limitation, this is acknowledged by the authors and is a common challenge in the field, not a flaw in the paper itself.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing a method that achieves exponential convergence without the impractical assumptions of previous work. The significance of this contribution is clear, as exponential convergence rates are much more efficient than polynomial ones for practical applications.

## Final Thoughts

This paper represents a meaningful advancement in Bayesian optimization theory and practice. It successfully bridges two approaches that have been pursued separately in the literature (methods with a specific bound and methods without), providing a theoretically sound framework for when and why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial. The authors have clearly demonstrated the practical advantages of their approach through comprehensive experiments, making this a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community.

The paper is well-positioned to influence future research in Bayesian optimization and related areas of global optimization, and I believe it would be a strong addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
