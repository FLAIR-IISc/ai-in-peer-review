

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work. The authors build upon the theoretical framework of GP-UCB and the SOO algorithm, introducing a method that considers infinitely many possible upper bounds rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior.

## Strengths

The paper addresses a genuine limitation in existing GP-based optimization methods, specifically the impractical requirement of δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis is detailed and appears mathematically sound, providing a regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1. The connection to previous work (BaMSOO, SOO, GP-UCB) is well-maintained, and the authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods.

The experimental section demonstrates that IMGPO outperforms several baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on multiple test functions, which is promising. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the theoretical implications of their approach in Remarks 1-3, explaining why considering infinitely many bounds can be beneficial even when the GP's UCB is not tight.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and validation. The primary issue is that the paper makes an extremely strong claim ("the first GP-based optimization method with an exponential convergence rate without the need of auxiliary optimization and the δ-cover sampling") without providing sufficient evidence to support it. The authors cite previous work by Wang et al. [2] as having an open problem regarding exponential convergence without δ-cover, but they do not sufficiently demonstrate that their method truly solves this problem in a way that is practically superior to existing approaches.

The experimental validation has several limitations. The authors use a single kernel (isotropic Matern with ν=5/2) and fixed hyperparameters for all experiments, which is not how these methods are typically evaluated in practice. The paper states they "blindly initialized" hyperparameters, but this is not a standard evaluation protocol. A proper comparison would involve tuning hyperparameters for each method and function, which the authors do not do.

Furthermore, the paper lacks statistical significance testing in the experiments. The results are presented as single runs without error bars or multiple runs to demonstrate consistency. The claim that IMGPO "outperformed the other algorithms in general" is not supported by sufficient statistical evidence.

The computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm are not adequately addressed. While the authors acknowledge that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, they do not provide evidence that their method scales well to higher dimensions. The paper's final function (Sin2 in 1000 dimensions) relies on REMBO as a subroutine, which undermines the claim of being a complete solution for high-dimensional problems.

The theoretical analysis relies on Assumption 1 (the existence of an unknown semi-metric) which is somewhat abstract and not clearly connected to practical optimization problems. The paper does not provide evidence that this assumption holds for typical objective functions encountered in practice.

## Originality and Significance

The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds rather than relying on a single bound is interesting and potentially valuable. However, this approach has been previously explored in the DIRECT algorithm [19] and generalized by Munos [18] in SOO. The paper does not sufficiently clarify how their method differs from these prior approaches or why their specific implementation provides meaningful improvements.

The significance of the work is questionable given the lack of thorough validation. The paper claims to achieve exponential convergence, but the experimental evidence does not convincingly demonstrate that this theoretical advantage translates to practical performance improvements over existing methods. The paper would be significantly stronger if it provided a more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods that have been specifically designed for high-dimensional problems.

## Conclusion

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical approach to Bayesian optimization, I have serious concerns about the validation and practical significance of the proposed method. The strong claim of achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling is not sufficiently supported by the experimental evidence provided. The experimental section lacks proper statistical validation and standard evaluation protocols.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More thorough experimental validation with proper hyperparameter tuning and multiple runs
2. A clearer connection between the theoretical assumptions and practical optimization problems
3. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability
4. A more rigorous comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of new approaches without extensive validation, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. The theoretical contribution is interesting, but the lack of thorough experimental validation and the overstatement of the practical benefits make it difficult to justify its significance for NIPS. I would encourage the authors to address these concerns before resubmission.
