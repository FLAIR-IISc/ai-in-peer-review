- Proposes IMGPO, a GP-based Bayesian optimization algorithm that attains exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.  
- Core idea: simultaneously consider multiple possible Lipschitz-like bounds via an unknown semi-metric, guided by GP predictions with hierarchical partitioning and an acceptance/rejection rule.  
- Theoretical contribution: proves an exponential convergence rate for the proposed algorithm.  
- Empirical claim: shows consistently strong performance on several standard benchmark functions compared to existing methods.  
- Related work: builds on de Freitas et al., links to SOO and BaMSOO, and situates itself among DIRECT, A*, and PAC-MDP literature.  
- Strength — theory: significant theoretical advance by removing δ-cover sampling while retaining exponential convergence.  
- Strength — novelty: clever idea of considering multiple bounds jointly with GP guidance rather than relying solely on the GP.  
- Strength — experiments: empirical validation across benchmarks provides encouraging evidence of effectiveness.  
- Strength — clarity: generally well-written, organized, with helpful pseudocode.  
- Strength — discussion: insightful analysis on the interaction between GP-based and unknown bounds.  
- Weakness — complexity: algorithm appears complex to implement and tune (parameters Ξmax, η) and division procedures not fully explored.  
- Weakness — scalability: limited discussion and solutions for scaling to higher-dimensional problems.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter sensitivity: GP hyperparameters (σ, l) fixed arbitrarily across tasks, with no thorough tuning or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness — experimental fairness: comparisons may be uneven (e.g., competing methods given handpicked kernels while IMGPO is not).  
- Weakness — limited benchmarks: uses standard test functions and a REMBO high-dimensional example that doesn’t fully demonstrate IMGPO’s standalone capability.  
- Weakness — analysis depth: full proofs are in supplementary material, making it harder to assess validity and tightness; Assumption 2 parameters could be more formalized.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important theoretical advance, addresses a practical limitation, solid empirical results, and valuable insights for the community.  
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about algorithmic complexity, scalability, experimental rigor, and limited access to detailed proofs.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant potential, but practical complexity and experimental limitations warrant concern.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept, contingent on addressing scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, experimental rigor, and providing fuller proof access.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10 — theoretical results convincing but practical implications remain somewhat uncertain.
