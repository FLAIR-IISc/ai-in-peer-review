

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential convergence rates without the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors propose Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which leverages an "unknown bound" assumption (Assumption 1) to consider infinitely many possible upper bounds rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior. This approach theoretically achieves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, while previous work like BaMSOO [2] only achieved polynomial regret bounds. The paper relates to recent NIPS work on Bayesian optimization (e.g., [13-15, 19, 20]) but addresses a specific limitation identified in Wang et al. [2].

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a significant theoretical advance with Theorem 1, establishing an exponential regret bound that matches or improves upon previous results. The analysis is thorough, with detailed remarks (Remarks 1-3) explaining how different components affect the bound.

2. **Practical Algorithm**: The authors develop a concrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) that avoids the δ-cover sampling procedure, making it more practical than prior theoretical approaches. The implementation is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The experimental evaluation across eight benchmark functions demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) in most cases. The authors appropriately address practical considerations by using a single kernel and hyperparameter initialization across all experiments, reflecting real-world scenarios.

4. **Insightful Analysis**: The paper provides valuable theoretical insights into why considering infinitely many bounds can be beneficial (Remarks 1-3), which advances the field's understanding beyond just providing a new algorithm.

## Weaknesses
1. **Motivation for Assumption 1**: The paper states "we do not expect the known upper bound due to GP to be tight" but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. A brief analysis showing how often the GP bound is loose in practice would strengthen the motivation.

2. **Algorithm-Theoretical Connection**: While the algorithm is well-described, the connection between Algorithm 1 and the theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) is not sufficiently explicit. A clear mapping of algorithmic components to the theoretical guarantees would improve readability.

3. **High-Dimensional Scalability**: The paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 as dimension D increases (Remark 1), which suggests practical limitations. The authors could provide more concrete guidance on handling high-dimensional cases, such as specific additional assumptions that would mitigate this issue.

4. **Experimental Rigor**: The paper reports performance across 8 functions but lacks statistical significance testing for the observed improvements. Including p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the experimental claims.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written. The work addresses a well-defined gap in the literature and makes a significant advance in both theory and practice.

## Recommendation
Accept. The paper meets all criteria for a high-quality NIPS contribution: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. While there are some weaknesses in the motivation and experimental rigor, they are not severe enough to outweigh the substantial theoretical and practical contributions.

## Constructive Suggestions for Improvement
1. Include a brief empirical analysis showing the frequency with which the GP bound is loose in practice to strengthen the motivation for Assumption 1.

2. Add a section explicitly mapping the algorithmic components of Algorithm 1 to the theoretical analysis in Section 4, clarifying how the algorithm implements the theoretical guarantees.

3. Expand Remark 1 to provide more concrete guidance on handling high-dimensional cases, perhaps by suggesting specific additional assumptions that could be leveraged (e.g., additive structure, low-dimensional embeddings).

4. Add statistical significance testing to the experimental results (e.g., p-values for performance differences) to strengthen the empirical claims.

5. Clarify the phrasing "exponential convergence rate" to emphasize that the bound is exponential in N+Ngp, not just N, to avoid potential confusion.

This paper represents a substantial advance in Bayesian optimization theory and practice. With the suggested minor improvements, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
