This paper proposes a novel Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, termed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results (de Freitas et al. [1]). The core idea is to consider a wider range of possible Lipschitz-like bounds than typically explored, leveraging the GP as a means to prune unlikely candidates. The authors present a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and experimental results on several benchmark functions.

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contribution. Achieving exponential convergence for GP-based Bayesian optimization without δ-cover sampling *is* a significant result, addressing a known open problem highlighted by Wang et al. [2]. The attempt to formalize the role of an "unknown bound" and explore multiple potential bounds is conceptually interesting and distinguishes this work from standard GP-UCB approaches. The inclusion of a theoretical analysis, even with assumptions (detailed below), is commendable.

However, significant weaknesses raise serious concerns. The theoretical analysis, while present, relies on several assumptions that limit its practical applicability. Specifically, Assumption 1 (the existence of an unknown semi-metric) feels rather strong and potentially untestable in many real-world scenarios. The dependency on parameters like `Ξmax` and η, along with the analysis showing how the convergence rate is affected by the “tightness” of the GP’s UCB, highlight the sensitivity of the method and make it difficult to predict performance in practice without extensive tuning. The discussion of the relationship between ρ̃t (exploration loss) and the quality of the GP bound is insightful, but doesn’t fully translate into clear guidance for practical implementation.

The experimental evaluation, while reasonably comprehensive for benchmark functions, feels somewhat insufficient to validate the claimed exponential convergence.  The authors acknowledge that the GP prior was "misleading" for Rosenbrock2, which casts doubt on the robustness of the algorithm.  Furthermore, comparing to BaMSOO and GP-UCB with *blindly initialized* hyperparameters is a disservice. These methods are highly sensitive to hyperparameter tuning. The authors’ justification that good hyperparameters are typically unknown in practice is valid, but a more rigorous comparison would involve performing hyperparameter optimization (even a limited grid search) for all algorithms, or explicitly stating the significant disadvantage the other algorithms were placed at. The claim that IMGPO is "much faster" than traditional GP optimization is not convincingly demonstrated – the results presented primarily show better final objective values, not runtime comparisons.  The scaling to 1000 dimensions is only performed with REMBO, obscuring the direct performance of IMGPO at high dimensionality.

The writing quality is generally good, but the description of Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to follow.  The numerous indices and symbols make it challenging to understand the flow of the algorithm without carefully tracing the pseudocode.  More illustrative examples or a simplified explanation would be beneficial. The motivation for choosing specific parameters (like Ξmax) feels arbitrary.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretical novelty: Addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   Potential for improved convergence: The core idea of exploring multiple bounds is intriguing.
*   Theoretical Analysis: Provides a formal convergence guarantee.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumptions in the theoretical analysis limit practical applicability.
*   Insufficient experimental validation, particularly regarding runtime and hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   Comparison to baselines is unfair due to hyperparameter initialization.
*   Algorithm description is overly complex and difficult to understand.
*   The practical benefits over existing methods (especially with properly tuned baselines) remain unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** with a request for significant revisions. While the theoretical contribution is noteworthy, the lack of robust experimental validation and the strong assumptions undermine the practical significance of the work.  The authors need to address the concerns regarding hyperparameter tuning, provide more convincing evidence of the claimed exponential convergence (perhaps through scaling experiments or comparisons on a wider range of problems), and clarify the algorithm description.  A more detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability of IMGPO would also be valuable. The paper, in its current form, feels more like a preliminary exploration of an interesting idea than a fully mature and validated contribution.
