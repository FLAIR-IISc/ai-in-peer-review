Summary
The paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization method for deterministic black-box functions that aims to achieve exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure or auxiliary global optimization of the acquisition. The key idea is to combine hierarchical partitioning (à la SOO/DOO/DIRECT) with a GP-derived UCB used only as a filter to prune intervals whose UCB is below the current best. Conceptually, IMGPO “hedges” over an infinite family of possible unknown semi-metric upper bounds around the global optimizer, instead of committing to a single (often loose) bound. The analysis introduces an infinite-metric exploration loss ρt—roughly, the number of intervals divided per iteration—and derives a regret bound of the form rN = O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, where N is the number of true function evaluations and Ngp counts instances where a GP-UCB surrogate value is used instead of actually evaluating f. The authors provide remarks on how GP influences ρ̄t and the bound, and empirical results on several benchmark functions showing that IMGPO often outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-PI/EI.

Relation to prior work
- DIRECT/SOO/DOO: IMGPO follows the optimistic, hierarchical partition paradigm but differs in not fixing a known smoothness and in using GP-UCB to safely skip evaluations. Compared to SOO, IMGPO aims for exponential rates by reducing exploratory overhead with GP-based pruning.
- GP-UCB and de Freitas et al. (δ-cover): The paper targets the open problem of obtaining exponential rates without δ-cover sampling; BaMSOO achieves polynomial rates. IMGPO attempts to bridge the “bound-based” BO path (GP-UCB) and the “unknown-metric optimistic partitioning” path (SOO/DOO).
- High-dimensional BO: The 1000-D experiment uses REMBO to reduce dimensionality, plugging in IMGPO or BaMSOO as the BO subroutine.

Strengths
- Interesting conceptual contribution: combining infinite-metric optimistic search with GP-based UCB pruning is novel and well motivated. The “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt provides a useful lens to analyze overhead incurred by not committing to a single bound.
- Ambitious theory: claims an exponential regret bound without δ-cover or auxiliary acquisition optimization, and discusses how GP impacts the constants via ρ̄t and Ngp.
- Practical algorithmic design: deterministic partition scheme; GP is used parsimoniously to avoid expensive evaluations; empirical Bayes for hyperparameters; code availability.
- Empirics suggest consistent gains over baselines on several benchmarks; good discussion of when GP can be misleading.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumption gap for UCB validity: The analysis relies crucially on a high-probability event f(x) ≤ U(x|D) globally during execution. This typically requires either (i) f is a sample from the GP prior, or (ii) f lies in the RKHS of the kernel with bounded norm and βt chosen using information gain. The paper does not state such an assumption. Using empirical Bayes to fit hyperparameters further complicates any such guarantee. Indeed, the authors note cases where this property “did not hold many times” (Rosenbrock2). The theoretical guarantees appear to require a stronger modeling assumption than stated.
- Regret bound counts Ngp: The main rate O(λN+Ngp) depends on the sum of true evaluations (N) and the number of GP “virtual” evaluations (Ngp). This is nonstandard: simple regret is defined in terms of N, and internal computations should not “buy” tighter rates. The remarks argue that GP reduces ρ̄t while increasing Ngp, but this still conflates sample efficiency with internal compute. A bound purely in N (or separate statements for N and Ngp) would be more meaningful.
- Dependence on ρ̄t and constants: The exponent depends on ρ̄t, a data- and algorithm-dependent quantity with weak control. In the worst case, the guarantee becomes loose, and no clean explicit bound in N, D, α emerges. This undermines the headline “exponential convergence” claim under general conditions.
- Notational/clarity issues in theory: The expression for λ appears incorrect as written (“λ = 3 − α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1” cannot hold generally). The paper later writes exponents like exp(−α[... ln 3]), suggesting λ = 3−α/(2CDρ̄t). Please correct and unify notation. The assumptions on the semi-metric (Assumptions 1–2) are essentially a local Hölder condition around x*, but the constants L, α, p, C, and β, and the geometry of the trisection scheme, are not easy to track; several proofs are deferred, limiting verifiability.
- Computational complexity of step (iii): Computing z(h,i*) as the max UCB over T′h+ξ requires enumerating centers in a fully expanded subtree to depth ξ. Even with Ξmax, this can be combinatorially expensive in higher D. Complexity and memory costs are not analyzed.
- Empirical setup details: While the choice to avoid hand-tuned kernels is fair, the optimization of GP-PI/EI’s acquisition is nonstandard (SOO + gradients) and may disadvantage these baselines. Budgets, stopping criteria, and evaluation counts should be reported clearly. An ablation showing IMGPO without GP pruning (or with different Ξmax) would help isolate contributions.
- Scope of “first exponential rate” claim: Please verify against the deterministic DOO literature (known smoothness) and recent GP-based optimistic partitioning variants whether similar exponential rates (perhaps under stronger assumptions) have been shown.

Quality
Technically promising but currently undermined by the missing assumption guaranteeing UCB validity under misspecification/EB hyperparameters and by the nonstandard inclusion of Ngp in the regret exponent. Proof sketch readability and notation need care. Experiments are suggestive but could be more rigorous and balanced.

Clarity
The high-level motivation is clear, and the algorithm description is helpful, but the pseudocode and Step (iii) constructs are hard to parse, and notation for constants/exponents is inconsistent. Please tighten the presentation, define all symbols upon first use, and give a precise computational complexity discussion.

Originality
Moderately high. The “infinite-metric” view and its analysis, coupled with GP-based pruning to approach exponential rates without δ-cover or known smoothness, is novel relative to SOO/BaMSOO.

Significance
Potentially significant if the theoretical guarantee can be made rigorous under clearly stated, realistic assumptions and if the dependence on Ngp is removed or separated. The practical approach is appealing and could influence BO in deterministic settings.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- State explicit conditions under which f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holds uniformly (e.g., f is a GP sample; RKHS norm bound + appropriate βt; fixed hyperparameters). Discuss the impact of empirical Bayes on these guarantees.
- Provide a regret bound in terms of N only (or give two statements: one in N and a computational complexity bound in Ngp), to avoid conflating internal compute with sample complexity.
- Clarify and correct λ and related constants. Provide an explicit upper bound on ρ̄t (or guidance ensuring N/ρ̄t = Ω(N)) under reasonable conditions on the GP prior/posterior.
- Analyze the time/memory complexity of step (iii) as a function of D, Ξmax, and depth, and show empirical scaling.
- Strengthen experiments: report evaluation budgets, wall-clock time, and acquisition optimization details for baselines; include ablations varying Ξmax and with/without GP pruning; consider more misspecified-kernel tests.
- Minor: fix typos, unify notation, and provide the promised general-domain extension beyond hyperrectangles.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a compelling idea and promising empirical results, but the current theoretical claims hinge on unstated assumptions about the GP model and use a nonstandard regret accounting (N+Ngp). With clarified assumptions, corrected/cleaner bounds in N, and strengthened empirical comparisons, this could become a strong contribution. Confidence: high.
