Summary
This paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions that achieves an exponential convergence rate without relying on the impractical δ-cover sampling required by prior work (de Freitas et al.) and without solving an auxiliary nonconvex optimization of the acquisition at each step. The key idea is to not commit to a single (possibly loose) bound from the GP; instead, IMGPO conducts an optimistic, hierarchical partitioning search over a continuum of candidate bounds implied by an unknown semi-metric around the global optimum. The GP posterior UCB is used to screen/prune candidates. The authors introduce the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt to quantify the price of not committing to a single bound and show a regret bound of the form rN = O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 under a Hölder-type assumption around the optimum (unknown L, α, p). They provide a detailed algorithm with a trisection-style partitioning, pruning via UCB, a cap (Ξmax) on the depth of local UCB lookahead, and empirical Bayes updates of GP hyperparameters. Experiments on standard benchmarks show that IMGPO typically outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI; code is provided.

Relation to prior work
IMGPO builds on optimistic partitioning methods (DIRECT, DOO/SOO) and GP-based BO with UCB. BaMSOO combined SOO and GP-UCB but obtained only polynomial rates. This paper claims to resolve an open problem raised by Wang et al. by achieving exponential simple regret without δ-covers, through a novel analysis that decomposes the contribution of the GP (Ngp) and the loss from not fixing a bound (ρ̄t). The analysis also clarifies when non-tight UCBs can still be useful and when considering infinitely many bounds is beneficial relative to committing to an estimated bound.

Strengths
- Bold, original idea: using an “infinite-metric” optimistic search that leverages an unknown local smoothness semi-metric and uses GP-UCB only to prune. Conceptually bridges the “with-bound” (GP-UCB) and “without-bound” (SOO/DIRECT) paradigms.
- Theoretical contribution: an exponential regret bound O(λN+Ngp), expressed in terms of D, α, p, the exploration loss ρ̄t, and an explicit accounting of the GP’s role (Ngp, Ξn). This advances the state of the art over BaMSOO’s polynomial rates and addresses a stated open problem.
- Analytical insights: the definitions and remarks around ρ̄t, the role of GP in reducing ρ̄t and increasing Ngp, and when “not relying on a particular bound” wins are useful to the broader global optimization community.
- Practical algorithmic design: avoids expensive auxiliary acquisition optimization; hierarchical trisection with pruning is simple and deterministic; code provided.
- Empirical results: across multiple standard benchmarks, IMGPO is generally superior to SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-PI/EI under a fixed kernel class and empirical-Bayes hyperparameter learning. Timing comparisons (referenced) suggest IMGPO is far faster than classic GP-BO with acquisition optimization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and constants: The exponential rate depends on D and ρ̄t; λ approaches 1 rapidly as D grows, and the bound can become loose even in moderate dimensions. The paper acknowledges this, but the practical regime where the theorem is informative could be clarified.
- GP-UCB validity with learned hyperparameters: The high-probability UCB guarantee typically assumes a fixed kernel/hyperparameters; here, hyperparameters are updated by empirical Bayes each iteration. The paper uses a union bound over M UCB calls, but does not reconcile the model-selection effect. Experiments even note violations of f ≤ U in Rosenbrock2. Please either (a) state the theorem under fixed hyperparameters and add an empirical-only discussion for learned hyperparameters, or (b) provide a robustness argument for the EB updates (even a heuristic stability condition).
- Computational complexity: Step (iii) requires evaluating UCBs over T′ up to a lookahead depth ξ, controlled by Ξmax. The worst-case cost and its scaling with D and iteration are not fully quantified. A more explicit time-complexity analysis (in terms of UCB evaluations and GP updates) would help.
- Experimental protocol/fairness: GP-PI/EI acquirements are optimized via SOO/gradients; this is atypical and may disadvantage these baselines. Including a standard multistart L-BFGS or DIRECT-based acquisition optimizer for EI/PI/UCB would improve fairness. Also, including GP-UCB itself as a baseline (with and without BaMSOO’s partitioning) would be informative.
- Scope: The work targets deterministic objectives; the noisy setting is important in practice. A discussion of how IMGPO might extend to noise (and what rate, perhaps reverting to polynomial) would be valuable.
- Clarity/typos: Some notation slips (ρt vs ρτ), and long algorithm descriptions could be tightened. A sketch of the main proof steps in the main text (beyond the supplement) would aid readability.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formalize the guarantee when hyperparameters are updated? If not, could you provide an ablation with fixed hyperparameters (matching the proof) to empirically verify the bound’s qualitative behavior?
- How sensitive is performance to Ξmax and η? Can you report ρ̄t, Ξn, and Ngp over time to validate the theoretical roles identified in Remarks 1–3?
- What is the time complexity per iteration in terms of D, ξ, and the number of rectangles, including GP posterior updates? How large can T′ get in practice before Ξmax truncation is necessary?
- Could you include experiments with a standard acquisition maximizer for EI/PI/UCB, and add GP-UCB as a baseline?
- Do you foresee a straightforward extension to bounded noise (e.g., by using GP-UCB for noise and a modified pruning test)?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a recognized open problem with a novel and principled approach.
- Provides new conceptual tools (infinite-metric exploration loss) and an analysis that will likely influence future work on optimistic optimization.
- Practical, auxiliary-optimization-free algorithm with competitive empirical performance and available code.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantee and practice diverge when hyperparameters are learned; this is common but should be clarified.
- Computational complexity of the pruning step and sensitivity to Ξmax are not fully characterized.
- Experimental baselines and protocol can be strengthened.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some theoretical and empirical caveats, the paper introduces a significant and original direction—“infinite-metric” GP optimization with exponential convergence without δ-covers or auxiliary optimization—and provides both analysis and promising empirical results. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the weaknesses, which can be addressed with clarifications and minor additional experiments.
