**Summary**

This paper presents IMGPO, a novel algorithm for Bayesian optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The work aims to combine the theoretical appeal of exponential convergence rates with practical applicability. The authors identify a key gap in the literature: prior methods either achieved exponential regret with impractical sampling procedures (de Freitas et al. [1]) or achieved polynomial regret with a practical algorithm (Wang et al. [2]). The proposed IMGPO algorithm is based on a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, similar to the SOO algorithm, but integrates a Gaussian Process (GP) prior to guide the search. The central idea is to avoid committing to a single, potentially loose, upper bound (from the GP or a known Lipschitz constant). Instead, the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite set of possible semi-metrics that define local smoothness around the optimum, using the GP-derived UCB to prune branches of the search tree that are provably suboptimal. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret and demonstrate strong empirical performance against several baselines, including SOO and BaMSOO.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addressing an Important Problem:** The paper tackles a well-established open problem in Bayesian optimization: achieving an exponential convergence rate without impractical assumptions like δ-cover sampling. This is a significant research direction.
2.  **Novel Algorithmic Contribution:** The core idea of integrating GP-UCB not as the primary driver of exploration but as a pruning mechanism within an optimistic, partitioning-based search framework is novel and interesting. This combination of two distinct lines of research in global optimization is a clear contribution.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper's main theoretical result (Theorem 1) establishes an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which represents a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO.
4.  **Sound Experimental Methodology:** The empirical evaluation is commendable. The authors compare against a strong set of relevant baselines. Critically, they avoid hand-picking kernels or hyperparameters for each test function, instead using a standard kernel and an empirical Bayes procedure for updates. This makes the experimental setting more realistic and the results more credible. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO also demonstrates the potential utility of IMGPO as a component in more complex systems.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the notable strengths, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in clarity, technical precision, and theoretical justification, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   The algorithmic description is exceedingly dense. The prose description in Section 3.2 is very difficult to follow without constantly referring to the pseudocode.
    *   The pseudocode itself (Algorithm 1) is poorly structured. For instance, the `while true` loop starting on Line 9 is an unintuitive way to implement what appears to be a single-pass check in most cases.
    *   There are numerous typographical and grammatical errors. For example, "forth diagram" instead of "fourth" (Sec 3.2), and "divide it along with the longest coordinate" (Sec 3.2) should be "along the longest coordinate."
    *   There is a glaring technical error in the standard definition of a Gaussian Process in Section 2: the covariance function is defined as `E[(f(x)−m(x))(f(x′)−m(x′))T ]`. The transpose `T` is incorrect for a scalar-valued function `f` and suggests carelessness or a copy-paste error from a multi-output context.

2.  **Algorithmic and Computational Claims:**
    *   The paper repeatedly claims the benefit of having "no need for auxiliary optimization." This claim is misleading. The algorithm replaces the continuous optimization of an acquisition function with a discrete search in step (iii) (Line 22), where it computes `max_{k:c_{h+ξ,k}∈T′_{h+ξ}(c_{h,i∗h})} U(c_{h+ξ,k}|D)`. The set `T'` can be large, and this maximization is itself a non-trivial search. The cost of this step is not properly analyzed, and it is not obvious that it is more efficient than standard methods like DIRECT or CMA-ES for optimizing the UCB.
    *   The algorithm introduces its own set of sensitive, heuristic parameters. The update rule for `Ξ` on Line 38 (`Ξ← Ξ + 22`, `Ξ← max(Ξ− 2−1, 1)`) is entirely ad-hoc and lacks any theoretical justification. For a paper that presents formal regret bounds, such a purely heuristic mechanism is a major weakness.

3.  **Theoretical Ambiguity and Justification:**
    *   The presentation of the main result, Theorem 1, is ambiguous. The exponential rate depends on `λ = 3^(−α / (2CDρ̄t)) < 1`. It is not made clear whether `λ < 1` is an assumption on the problem instance or a proven property of the algorithm. Since `ρ̄t` evolves during the run, `λ` is not a constant. Stating the bound as `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` is therefore confusing. The conditions under which the exponential convergence is guaranteed must be stated much more precisely.
    *   The analysis in the remarks, intended to provide intuition, is weak. Remark 3 attempts to justify the "infinite-metric" approach with a hand-wavy derivation that concludes with "For an intuitive insight...". The final inequality is difficult to parse, and the argument is not rigorous enough to be convincing.
    *   The footnote in Section 4 regarding a depth limit of `O(sqrt(N))` is strange. It suggests a modification that worsens the theoretical regret bound, and the justification for not adopting it is unpersuasive, leaving the reader to question the robustness of the analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   Presents a novel algorithm that cleverly combines ideas from optimistic optimization and GP-UCB.
*   The headline theoretical result is an exponential convergence rate, a major improvement over prior practical methods.
*   Empirical results are strong and based on a sound, realistic experimental protocol.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is marred by numerous clarity issues, including typos, grammatical errors, and a confusing algorithmic description.
*   A fundamental technical definition (GP covariance) is stated incorrectly.
*   The key claim of avoiding auxiliary optimization is misleading, as the algorithm contains its own expensive, unanalyzed search step.
*   The algorithm relies on a crucial, completely ad-hoc heuristic for updating the parameter `Ξ`.
*   The main theoretical result is stated ambiguously, obscuring the precise conditions required for the exponential convergence rate.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a promising core idea, the execution is unfortunately flawed. The combination of poor clarity, technical inaccuracies, questionable computational claims, and unjustified heuristics in the algorithm design is substantial. The work feels premature and does not meet the high standards of quality and rigor expected at NIPS. I therefore recommend **rejection**. The authors should be encouraged to thoroughly revise the manuscript to address these issues, as the underlying concepts are indeed interesting and potentially significant.
