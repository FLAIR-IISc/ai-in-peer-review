### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for deterministic black-box functions, a central topic in machine learning, particularly for hyperparameter tuning. The authors focus on Bayesian optimization using Gaussian Processes (GPs) and aim to develop an algorithm with a theoretically guaranteed exponential convergence rate for simple regret. The paper situates itself in the context of recent work. De Freitas et al. [1] proposed a GP-based procedure with an exponential regret bound but relied on a computationally impractical `δ-cover sampling` procedure. Subsequently, Wang et al. [2] introduced BaMSOO, which combined GP-UCB with the hierarchical partitioning SOO algorithm [18], but only achieved a polynomial regret bound. This paper identifies achieving an exponential rate without `δ-cover sampling` as a key open problem.

The main contribution is a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO). The core idea is to not rely solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP model, which may not be tight. Instead, IMGPO operates under the assumption that an unknown, but potentially much tighter, semi-metric `l` governs the function's smoothness around the global optimum (Assumption 1). Since this semi-metric is unknown, the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite family of possible bounds by simultaneously exploring promising regions at all scales of the hierarchical partition. The GP-UCB is then used as a powerful heuristic to prune this search, filtering out partitions that are unlikely to contain the optimum according to the GP posterior. This hybrid approach elegantly merges the bound-agnostic philosophy of algorithms like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18] with the probabilistic modeling of GPs.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving that IMGPO achieves an exponential convergence rate of `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` with `λ < 1` (Theorem 1), where N is the number of function evaluations. This result is a significant theoretical improvement over the polynomial bounds of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The paper complements the theory with an empirical evaluation on several benchmark functions, comparing IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods (GP-PI, GP-EI), demonstrating its superior performance.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is the main strength. Theorem 1 provides the first exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm that does not require `δ-cover sampling`. The analysis, which introduces the concept of "infinite-metric exploration loss" `ρ_t`, is sound and provides valuable insight into how the GP-based bound helps constrain the search (Remarks 1-3). The work appears complete, presenting the algorithm, its theoretical justification, and empirical validation. The experiments are well-designed, comparing against a strong set of relevant baselines under fair conditions (e.g., not assuming hand-tuned kernels).
*   **Weaknesses:** The algorithm's dependence on a tri-section partition of a hyperrectangular domain is a limitation, though this is common for algorithms in this class. The analysis also reveals that the convergence rate degrades with dimensionality (as the constant `λ` approaches 1), a crucial point that is mentioned but could be further emphasized as a primary limitation on scalability.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.
*   **Strengths:** The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The high-level overview in Section 3.1 and the illustrative example in Figure 1 are very helpful for building intuition about the algorithm's mechanics. The relationship to previous work is clearly delineated.
*   **Weaknesses:** The detailed description of the algorithm (Section 3.2 and Algorithm 1) is quite complex and dense. The interplay between the nested loops and the various conditions for selecting, keeping, and dividing intervals is difficult to follow. For instance, the purpose and update rule for the parameter `Ξ` (Line 38 in Algorithm 1) are not fully motivated in the main text, making it harder to grasp its role in balancing exploration. The pseudocode contains many variables and state-dependent logic (e.g., "GP-based" labels), which could pose challenges for reproducibility without access to the provided source code. A more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of the selection logic (lines 8-24) could significantly enhance clarity.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   **Strengths:** The core idea of synthesizing two major lines of thought in global optimization—the agnostic partitioning approach of DIRECT/SOO and the model-based approach of GP optimization—is novel and powerful. While BaMSOO also combines these, the way IMGPO uses the GP not just for local selection but to prune the "infinite-metric" search across all scales is a unique and key contribution. This conceptual advance directly leads to the improved theoretical guarantees.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant.
*   **Strengths:** The paper solves a well-defined and important open problem in the theory of Bayesian optimization. An algorithm with a practical implementation and an exponential convergence guarantee is a notable advance for the field. The results are likely to be of interest to both theorists and practitioners in Bayesian optimization. The analysis framework itself, particularly the formalization in Remarks 1-3 of how a loose (GP) bound can still accelerate search, provides a valuable conceptual tool that could influence future algorithm design in other bound-based search problems.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  **Theoretical Advance:** The paper provides the first exponential convergence rate for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm, solving a recognized open problem in the field. This is a substantial theoretical contribution.
2.  **Novel Algorithm Design:** The IMGPO algorithm presents a novel and insightful combination of hierarchical partitioning methods and GP-based search, where the GP serves to effectively prune a search over an infinite class of potential bounds.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The algorithm is shown to outperform state-of-the-art baselines on a variety of benchmark problems, validating the practical utility of the theoretical ideas.
4.  **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper clearly articulates its contribution relative to prior work (especially BaMSOO), making its novelty and significance easy to appreciate.

### Arguments against Acceptance
1.  **Algorithmic Complexity:** The description of the algorithm is dense and complex, which may hinder understanding and implementation by other researchers. The clarity of the pseudocode and the motivation for certain design choices (e.g., the `Ξ` update rule) could be improved.
2.  **Limited Scalability:** Like other methods based on space partitioning, the algorithm's performance is expected to degrade significantly with dimension. While this is acknowledged, it remains a primary practical limitation that is not fully overcome by the proposed method itself (the 1000D experiment relies on REMBO).

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its significant theoretical contribution and novel algorithmic approach, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The paper represents a clear and important advance in the field of Bayesian optimization.
