Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization method for deterministic black-box functions that combines hierarchical partitioning (in the spirit of DIRECT/DOO/SOO) with Gaussian process (GP) upper-confidence bounds for pruning. The key conceptual move is to avoid committing to a single Lipschitz/Hölder bound; instead, IMGPO conducts a joint global/local search “over all possible bounds,” using the GP’s UCB to screen/prune candidates. Theoretically, the paper introduces the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt (the number of intervals divided in iteration t) and proves a simple regret bound of the form rN ≤ const · exp(−α[(N+Ngp)/(2CD ρ̄t) − Ξn − 2] ln 3), where ρ̄t is a running average of ρt and Ngp counts “GP-based” evaluations skipped/converted by UCB. This is presented as an exponential convergence rate O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, without δ-cover sampling (unlike de Freitas et al., ICML’12), and with a tighter rate than BaMSOO (AISTATS’14), which had polynomial regret. The analysis assumes an unknown semi-metric around x* (Assumption 1) upper-bounded by a Hölder-type condition (Assumption 2), in line with the near-optimality assumptions in DOO/SOO (Munos, NIPS’11).

Empirically, IMGPO is compared to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on eight benchmarks (1–1000 dimensions, the latter via REMBO). With a fixed Matern-5/2 kernel and empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates, IMGPO often outperforms baselines in simple regret, while being faster than GP-based methods that solve auxiliary optimization problems.

Strengths:
- Novel algorithmic synthesis: The idea of using GP-UCB not as the sole driver but as a pruning signal layered on top of an infinite-metric (DIRECT/SOO-like) search is interesting, practical, and distinct from BaMSOO.
- Theoretical framing: Introducing ρt makes explicit the trade-off between not committing to a single metric and progress; the regret bound ties algorithmic behavior to ρ̄t, Ngp, and depth parameters, and clarifies roles of L, α, p.
- Removes δ-cover requirement while claiming exponential simple regret under stated conditions; if valid under realistic scenarios, this would be impactful.
- Practical appeal: Avoids auxiliary nonconvex optimization of the acquisition; code link provided; experiments suggest good performance on standard problems.

Weaknesses:
Quality/technical correctness:
- The exponential rate critically depends on ρ̄t = O(1). The paper does not provide sufficient conditions ensuring ρ̄t remains bounded (or grows slowly) under realistic modeling conditions. Remarks discuss cases when GP UCB is “useful,” but this is not formalized. Without control of ρ̄t, the exponential claim is conditional.
- Validity of the GP-UCB upper bound when hyperparameters are re-estimated online via empirical Bayes is not justified. Standard GP-UCB guarantees assume fixed kernel/hyperparameters and f in the RKHS of the kernel. With model misspecification and learned hyperparameters, f(x) ≤ U(x|D) “with probability ≥ 1−η” is not guaranteed. The analysis relies on this property across the run.
- Assumptions 1–2 essentially require a Hölder condition globally (since Assumption 2 is for all x, x′), which is strong and not discussed in relation to near-optimality dimension. The dependence on D via C and the exponential base approaching 1 with D is acknowledged but not quantified.
- Pseudocode and step (iii) appear inconsistent with the textual description; the inequality for pruning “accept if UCB within interval is ≥ center value of smaller intervals” vs the code that removes i*h when z(h,i*h) < g(ch+ξ,i*_{h+ξ}) is confusing. The update of Ξ (adding 2^2, subtracting 2^{-1}) mixes integers and fractions for a variable that elsewhere is an integer; please clarify.
- The role and computation of T′h+ξ(ch,i*h) (virtual full expansion for ξ levels) can be computationally heavy; complexity bounds for step (iii) are hand-waved with Ξmax.

Clarity:
- The paper is dense and notation-heavy; important definitions (C, β, ρ̄t) are scattered. Figures and Table 1 are referenced but not included, making it hard to assess empirical claims. Several typos/formatting issues in Algorithm 1 hinder reproducibility.

Originality:
- The combination is novel relative to BaMSOO/SOO, and the “infinite-metric exploration loss” is a fresh angle. However, the regret proof’s novelty rests on conditions that are not fully verifiable or controlled by the algorithm (ρ̄t, validity of UCB with hyperparameter learning).

Significance:
- If the exponential rate could be guaranteed under standard GP assumptions (or under verifiable conditions linking kernel, fill distance, and ρ̄t), this would be a substantial advance. As written, the significance is tempered by conditional guarantees and limited empirical scope (few, low-to-moderate dimensional problems; REMBO experiment is promising but brief).

Pros for acceptance:
- Interesting algorithmic idea that eliminates auxiliary optimization and uses GP information in a new way.
- Clear attempt to bridge DIRECT/SOO-style exploration with GP-UCB theory.
- Promising empirical results on several benchmarks.

Cons for acceptance:
- Exponential convergence depends on unbounded/undefined quantities (ρ̄t) without sufficient conditions; UCB validity under empirical Bayes is unproven.
- Clarity issues in algorithm description and pseudocode; some steps appear inconsistent.
- Experimental evaluation misses direct comparison to GP-UCB (with multi-start or global solvers) and more recent BO baselines; absent ablations measuring ρ̄t, Ngp, and the contribution of GP pruning.

Recommendation: Reject (encourage resubmission after strengthening theory and clarity)

Suggestions for improvement:
- Theoretical:
  - Provide sufficient conditions on the kernel, function class (e.g., f in a Hölder class or RKHS with bounded norm), and hyperparameter learning under which ρ̄t is bounded (or grows sublogarithmically). Even a result under fixed hyperparameters would be valuable.
  - Analyze the effect of online hyperparameter learning on the UCB validity, or freeze hyperparameters for the theoretical result (plus an empirical variant that learns).
  - Relate Assumption 2 to near-optimality dimension and compare to DOO/SOO rates; quantify constants C and the D-dependence of λ.
- Algorithm/clarity:
  - Fix Algorithm 1: ensure inequalities in step (iii) align with the prose; define T′ precisely; make Ξ integer and consistent; define all variables (C, β, p, norms) upon first use.
  - Provide computational complexity per iteration and the impact of Ξmax; include ablations varying Ξmax and measuring ρ̄t and Ngp empirically.
- Experiments:
  - Add GP-UCB with strong optimizers (multi-start L-BFGS, DIRECT/CMA-ES) as a baseline; include Thompson sampling/entropy search if feasible.
  - Report wall-clock time vs regret; show sensitivity to kernel choice and misspecification.
  - Include ablations: IMGPO without GP pruning (to isolate the gain), and with fixed hyperparameters.
  - Expand higher-dimensional evaluations (beyond REMBO) or clearly scope the intended dimensional regime.

Overall, the paper contains a promising idea and a potentially impactful direction. With more rigorous conditions for the exponential rate, clearer presentation, and stronger empirical evidence, it could become a strong contribution.
