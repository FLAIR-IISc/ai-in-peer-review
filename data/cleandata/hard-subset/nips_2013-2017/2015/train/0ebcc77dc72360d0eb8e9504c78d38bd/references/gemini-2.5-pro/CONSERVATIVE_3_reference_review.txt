**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of designing a practical Bayesian optimization algorithm with a guaranteed exponential convergence rate for the simple regret. The authors identify an open problem in the literature: previous methods either required an impractical sampling procedure (δ-cover sampling) to achieve exponential regret or settled for polynomial regret bounds with practical algorithms (e.g., BaMSOO). The proposed method, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), combines a hierarchical partitioning scheme, in the spirit of DIRECT and SOO, with a Gaussian Process (GP) surrogate model. The core idea is to not rely solely on the GP-derived upper confidence bound (UCB), but to simultaneously consider an implicit, infinite set of possible local bounds around the global optimum. The GP-UCB is then used to prune the search space explored by the hierarchical partitioning strategy. The main contribution is a theoretical proof (Theorem 1) showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound without needing auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors support their theoretical claims with experiments on several benchmark functions, comparing IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its theoretical contribution. It provides a positive answer to the open question of whether a practical GP-based optimization algorithm can achieve an exponential convergence rate. The derivation of the regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of closely related prior work like BaMSOO and represents a notable advance in the theory of Bayesian optimization.

2.  **Novel Algorithmic Concept:** The conceptual framing of the problem is interesting. Instead of treating the GP-UCB as the definitive bound to be exploited, the algorithm treats it as one source of information to accelerate a more general, bound-agnostic search strategy. The idea of using the GP to prune branches in a search that implicitly accounts for an "infinite" class of semi-metrics is a novel combination of ideas from the Lipschitz-free (e.g., SOO) and Bayesian optimization communities.

3.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated and clearly situates its contribution within the context of prior work. The introduction does a good job of explaining the limitations of existing approaches and framing the open problem that the paper aims to solve.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **High Algorithmic Complexity:** A major concern is the complexity of the proposed IMGPO algorithm. Algorithm 1 is substantially more intricate than established methods like GP-UCB or even BaMSOO. It involves multiple nested loops over the tree depth, complex selection criteria across different tree levels (steps i-iii), and dynamic parameter updates (e.g., for Ξ). This complexity is a significant barrier to implementation and practical adoption. It also introduces new parameters (η, Ξmax) whose sensitivity is not thoroughly explored. The elegance of standard GP-BO lies in its conceptual simplicity; IMGPO moves considerably away from this.

2.  **Limited and Inconclusive Experimental Validation:** For a paper that proposes a new algorithm intended for practical use, the experimental evidence is not sufficiently compelling.
    *   The set of baseline algorithms is rather small. A thorough validation would require comparison against a wider suite of modern Bayesian optimization methods and libraries.
    *   The performance advantage of IMGPO is not consistently demonstrated. On several functions (e.g., Branin, Hartmann3), its performance is nearly indistinguishable from BaMSOO. On Rosenbrock2, it performs worse than the non-Bayesian SOO algorithm, highlighting a critical failure mode when the GP prior is mismatched with the objective function. The claim of general outperformance seems overstated.
    *   The high-dimensional experiment (Sin2 in 1000D) relies on embedding IMGPO within the REMBO framework. While this shows potential compatibility, it is not a direct evaluation of IMGPO's scalability and does not address the paper's own acknowledgment that the convergence rate worsens with dimensionality. More extensive testing in moderately high dimensions (e.g., 10-20D) would be needed to understand its practical limits.

3.  **Practical Benefit vs. Theoretical Gain:** The analysis correctly identifies that the algorithm's performance hinges on the GP providing "useful" bounds to prune the search and reduce the exploration loss `ρ̄t`. As the Rosenbrock2 experiment shows, when the GP model is poor, the complex machinery of IMGPO provides little benefit and can even be detrimental compared to a simpler, more robust baseline like SOO. This raises a critical question: how much practical benefit does the exponential convergence guarantee provide over a polynomial one if the constant factors and dependence on GP model quality lead to comparable or worse performance in a finite budget setting? The theoretical improvement is clear, but its translation to consistent real-world performance is not.

**Rationale for Decision**

This paper presents a strong theoretical result that formally closes an open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature. The approach of combining hierarchical partitioning with GP-based pruning is conceptually novel. These aspects make the paper a solid candidate for NIPS.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by a skepticism rooted in the paper's practical side. The proposed algorithm is complex, and its empirical validation is not thorough enough to convincingly demonstrate a consistent and significant advantage over established, simpler methods. The performance gains appear to be benchmark-dependent and rely heavily on a well-specified GP model, a weakness shared by the very methods it aims to improve upon. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the paper would be much stronger if the experimental section provided more extensive evidence to support the claim that this novel complexity leads to tangible and robust performance improvements in practice.

**Summary of Pros & Cons**

*   **Pro:** A novel, practical algorithm with a proven exponential regret bound, addressing a known open problem.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the interplay between GP bounds and bound-free hierarchical search.
*   **Con:** The proposed algorithm is significantly more complex than standard baselines, hindering its practical appeal.
*   **Con:** The experimental validation is limited and does not provide conclusive evidence of superior performance across a wide range of problems compared to established methods.
*   **Con:** The practical effectiveness remains highly dependent on the quality of the GP surrogate, potentially negating the benefits of the complex search strategy.

I lean towards acceptance due to the strength of the theoretical contribution, but with reservations about the practical significance as presented.
