

# Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

## Summary
This paper proposes IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The key insight is to consider infinitely many possible semi-metric bounds (not just the GP-estimated bound) to balance global and local search. The authors prove an exponential regret bound O(λ^N+Ngp) with λ < 1, significantly improving upon previous polynomial bounds like BaMSOO's Õ(n^(-2α/D(4-α))). The paper demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across multiple benchmark functions.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous theoretical analysis. Theorem 1 provides a concrete exponential regret bound under reasonable assumptions (1 and 2), and the supplementary material contains complete proofs. The experimental design is sound, testing on eight diverse benchmark functions with appropriate parameter settings (using a single Matern kernel for all methods, as would be done in practice). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, particularly the dimensionality scaling issue where λ approaches 1 as D increases.

However, the theoretical analysis assumes Ω is a hyperrectangle, which restricts applicability to general domains. While the authors note this limitation in Section 3.3, they don't discuss how to extend the method to non-rectangular domains or quantify the impact of this assumption. Additionally, the paper doesn't address how the algorithm would perform with noisy function evaluations, which is a common scenario in Bayesian optimization.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experiments. Figure 1 and Algorithm 1 provide valuable visual and pseudocode support for understanding the algorithm. The key concept of "infinite-metric exploration loss" is well-defined, though the explanation of how Ξmax affects performance could be clearer.

The paper adequately references related work, particularly distinguishing itself from BaMSOO (which achieves polynomial regret) and GP-UCB (which requires δ-cover sampling). However, it could better connect to more recent Bayesian optimization literature, such as the 2017 NIPS paper "Bayesian Optimization with Unknown Constraints" by Gardner et al., which addresses a different but related challenge.

## Originality
The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by introducing the concept of considering infinitely many possible bounds rather than relying on a single GP-estimated bound. This approach resolves the open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] regarding exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The core insight in Section 3.1 is particularly elegant: "instead of relying on a single bound given by the GP, we leverage the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer."

The authors clearly establish how their method differs from previous approaches (Section 3.4), showing that IMGPO achieves exponential convergence while BaMSOO achieves only polynomial convergence. The theoretical framework in Remarks 1-3 provides deeper understanding of when considering multiple bounds is advantageous, which is a significant conceptual contribution beyond just the algorithm itself.

## Significance
The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in Bayesian optimization literature and makes a substantial theoretical advancement by achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. This is a major step toward making Bayesian optimization more practical, as δ-cover sampling is computationally prohibitive in many real-world applications.

The empirical results consistently show improvements over state-of-the-art methods (Figure 2), with IMGPO outperforming BaMSOO and other baselines across most test functions. The framework developed in Remarks 1-3 provides valuable theoretical insights that could inform future work in optimization, not just Bayesian optimization.

The main limitation is the dimensionality scaling issue noted in Remark 1. While the paper acknowledges this and suggests potential solutions (leveraging additional assumptions like in [14,20]), it doesn't provide concrete strategies for high-dimensional problems, which is a significant practical constraint for many applications.

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization. The exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling is a major advancement, and the theoretical framework provides valuable insights into bound-based optimization methods. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods.

The paper's main limitations are the restriction to hyperrectangular domains and the dimensionality scaling issue, but these are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current approach.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear theoretical advance with practical implications, addressing an important open problem in Bayesian optimization. The quality of the theoretical analysis, experimental validation, and clarity of the presentation make it a strong contribution to the field.

**Pro:** Strong theoretical contribution (exponential convergence without δ-cover), novel framework for understanding bound-based optimization, comprehensive experimental validation.

**Con:** Limited to hyperrectangular domains, dimensionality scaling issue acknowledged but not fully addressed, could better connect to very recent Bayesian optimization literature.
