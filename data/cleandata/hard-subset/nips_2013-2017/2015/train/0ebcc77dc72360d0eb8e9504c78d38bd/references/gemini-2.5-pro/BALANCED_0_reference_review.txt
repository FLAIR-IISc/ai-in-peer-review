### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for globally optimizing deterministic black-box functions. The main contribution is to provide the first Gaussian Process (GP)-based method that achieves a provably exponential convergence rate without relying on computationally expensive procedures like δ-cover sampling, which was a requirement for previous work with similar guarantees [1].

The proposed method addresses an open problem highlighted in recent literature [1, 2]. While GP-based methods like GP-UCB are popular, their theoretical guarantees often rely on strong assumptions or result in slower (polynomial) convergence rates. The key idea of IMGPO is to not rely solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP, which may not be tight. Instead, it combines the GP-UCB framework with the hierarchical partitioning strategy of algorithms like SOO [18]. The algorithm implicitly considers an infinite set of possible semi-metrics that could define the function's smoothness, as in the DIRECT algorithm [19], and uses the GP posterior to efficiently prune the search space. At each iteration, the algorithm selects promising regions to partition based on both the best-observed function values (corresponding to the search over unknown metrics) and the GP's UCB. This hybrid approach allows IMGPO to leverage the best of both worlds: the data-efficiency of GPs and the robust theoretical properties of partitioning-based methods.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1. This significantly improves upon the polynomial bounds of related algorithms like BaMSOO [2]. The paper also includes an empirical evaluation on several benchmark functions, comparing IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods (GP-EI, GP-PI), demonstrating its superior practical performance.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a significant theoretical leap in Bayesian optimization. Achieving an exponential convergence rate for a GP-based method without impractical assumptions has been a recognized open problem. The core idea of combining the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" from GP-UCB with the "simultaneous search over all possible smoothness moduli" from DIRECT/SOO is highly novel and insightful. This work successfully bridges two historically separate lines of research in global optimization.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) is strong and well-supported by the analysis provided in the supplementary material. The regret bound is a clear improvement over prior art. Furthermore, the analysis in Remarks 1-3 provides valuable intuition about *why* the proposed combination is powerful. It explains how a GP-based bound, even if loose, can still be useful for pruning the search, and quantifies the advantage of the infinite-metric approach over relying on a single, potentially poorly estimated, smoothness constant.

3.  **Good Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the practical effectiveness of IMGPO. The algorithm consistently outperforms strong baselines, including its closest relative BaMSOO, across a variety of standard benchmark functions with different dimensionalities and characteristics. The experimental setup is fair and practical, as it avoids hand-tuning kernels and hyperparameters for each function, which is a more realistic scenario.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and structured. The motivation is clearly laid out, the related work is appropriately discussed, and the high-level description of the algorithm, aided by Figure 1, is intuitive. The authors clearly articulate how their work fits into and extends the existing body of literature.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Algorithm Complexity and Clarity:** While the high-level idea is clear, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to follow. The logic behind the nested loops, the multiple conditions for selecting a cell to divide (lines 8-24), and the update rule for the parameter Ξ (line 38) are not immediately obvious. A more detailed walkthrough of the pseudocode's logic would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Computational Cost:** A potential concern is the computational cost of step (iii) of the algorithm (lines 18-23), which involves finding the maximum UCB within a hypothetical, fully expanded sub-tree of depth up to Ξmax. This seems computationally intensive, potentially requiring 3^Ξmax UCB evaluations in the worst case. While the authors state this rarely happens, a more formal analysis of the per-iteration complexity and how it scales with the dimension D and the parameter Ξ would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scalability:** The theoretical analysis notes that the convergence rate constant λ approaches 1 as the dimensionality D increases. This is a common limitation of partitioning-based methods. While the authors demonstrate scalability to 1000 dimensions by using IMGPO as a subroutine within REMBO, the performance of the core IMGPO algorithm in moderately high dimensions (e.g., D=20-50) remains unexplored. This leaves open questions about its practical applicability beyond low-dimensional problems.

4.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new parameter, Ξmax, which controls the depth of the UCB lookahead. The experiments use a fixed value (Ξmax = 22), with a brief comment that a larger value could improve performance at a computational cost. A sensitivity analysis showing the trade-off between performance and runtime for different values of Ξmax on a representative function would be a valuable addition.

### Constructive Suggestions

1.  To improve clarity, I strongly suggest the authors add a section or paragraph that walks through the pseudocode with a simple example. Explaining the purpose of variables like `υmax` and the flow of the three main for-loops (steps i-ii, step iii, steps iv-v) in more concrete terms would make the algorithm much easier to understand and implement.

2.  To address concerns about computational cost, the authors could add a brief discussion on the complexity of the UCB maximization step in line 22. Even an empirical analysis of how many UCB evaluations this step requires in practice across the benchmark functions would be very helpful.

3.  The experimental section could be strengthened by adding a benchmark problem with an intermediate dimension (e.g., the Hartmann6 function or Rosenbrock10) to better characterize the algorithm's scaling properties before resorting to methods like REMBO.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It solves an important open problem by providing the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. The theoretical results are strong and the empirical evidence is convincing. While there are minor weaknesses related to the clarity of the algorithm's description and questions about scalability, these do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. The paper is well-executed and will likely be of high interest to both theoreticians and practitioners in the NIPS community.

**Recommendation: Accept**
