This paper proposes a Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) that aims to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric representing the function’s continuity at the global optimizer, effectively considering a wider range of potential bounds during optimization. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing an exponential convergence rate and demonstrate empirical performance on several benchmark functions, showing IMGPO often outperforms existing methods like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of previous work in Bayesian optimization [13, 14, 15] and Lipschitz optimization [7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. It addresses a key limitation of [1] – the impracticality of δ-cover sampling – and improves upon the polynomial regret bound of Wang et al. [2]. The theoretical analysis is rigorously presented, and the connection to bound-based search methods [18, 19] is clearly articulated. The inclusion of experiments on standard benchmark functions and comparison against relevant baselines strengthens the paper’s evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of the “infinite-metric optimization” concept, considering a set of potential bounds rather than relying on a single estimate from the GP, is a novel contribution. This addresses a critical bottleneck in GP-based optimization.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving an exponential convergence rate under reasonable assumptions. The detailed analysis of the regret bound and the influence of various parameters (L, α, p) provides valuable insights.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its underlying principles. The diagrams in Section 3.2 are helpful for understanding the hierarchical partitioning process.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO on a range of benchmark functions. The comparison with existing algorithms supports the claim of improved performance.
*   **Connection to Previous Work:** The paper does a good job relating its contributions to existing literature, specifically referencing and building upon previous work in Bayesian optimization, Lipschitz optimization, and related search methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** While achieving exponential convergence is a significant result, the analysis reveals potential scalability challenges in higher dimensions (as λ approaches 1 with increasing D). The paper acknowledges this and suggests future work involving additional assumptions to address it. However, a more detailed discussion of potential mitigation strategies would be valuable.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of IMGPO may be sensitive to the choice of parameters like Ξmax. The experiments show that increasing Ξmax can improve performance for certain functions, but the optimal value may depend on the specific problem. More systematic analysis of parameter tuning and its impact on performance is needed.
*   **Computational Cost:** Although IMGPO avoids the δ-cover sampling, the algorithm appears to be computationally intensive due to the repeated UCB calculations and hierarchical partitioning. A more thorough computational complexity analysis and comparison with other algorithms (beyond just runtime in the tables) would be helpful.
*   **GP Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper uses a standard empirical Bayesian method for GP hyperparameter tuning. It would be beneficial to discuss the potential impact of hyperparameter optimization on the performance of IMGPO and whether more sophisticated optimization techniques could further improve results.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with a demonstrably improved convergence rate compared to existing methods.
*   The idea of infinite-metric optimization is a significant conceptual advancement in the field of Bayesian optimization.
*   The empirical results are convincing and support the claims of the paper.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and thoroughly references relevant work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The scalability concerns and potential parameter sensitivity of the algorithm warrant further investigation.
*   The computational cost of IMGPO should be more thoroughly analyzed and compared to other algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The novel concept of infinite-metric optimization, coupled with a rigorous theoretical analysis and convincing empirical results, makes this work worthy of acceptance. While there are some potential limitations regarding scalability and computational cost, these are acknowledged by the authors and provide directions for future research. I recommend accepting this paper.
