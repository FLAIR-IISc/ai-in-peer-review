Summary
The paper addresses deterministic global optimization of a black-box function via Bayesian optimization. It proposes IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), which combines hierarchical partitioning/direct-search ideas (as in DIRECT/SOO) with GP-based UCB to guide and prune exploration. The key idea is to not rely on a single, potentially loose bound (e.g., GP-UCB) but to implicitly consider an infinite family of unknown bounds induced by continuity around the global optimum. The GP posterior/UCB is then used to eliminate inferior candidates among these possible bounds and to avoid unnecessary function evaluations via “dummy” GP-based center values. The authors prove an exponential simple regret bound O(λN+Ngp), λ < 1, under an assumption of an unknown semi-metric bound around an optimizer and a Hölder-type upper bound `(x, x*) ≤ L||x−x*||_p^α. They introduce an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt to quantify the overhead from not committing to a single bound; the bound shows how GP predictions reduce this overhead and improve the rate. Experiments on several synthetic benchmarks (1–1000D, with REMBO for 1000D) compare IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI; IMGPO typically attains lower regret faster and avoids expensive auxiliary acquisition optimization. Code is reported as available.

Relation to prior work
- GP-UCB and related GP bandit work provide sublinear/polynomial regret rates and typically require optimizing an acquisition at each step; de Freitas et al. obtained exponential rates but required impractical δ-cover sampling. IMGPO claims exponential rates without δ-cover and without auxiliary optimization.
- SOO/DOO (Munos) and DIRECT motivate the hierarchical partitioning framework and assumptions near the optimum. BaMSOO combines SOO with GP-UCB but achieves only polynomial rates. IMGPO’s main novelty is the explicit “infinite-metric” view plus using GP UCB only to prune and to save evaluations, yielding exponential decay in N.
- The analysis borrows the near-optimality-style assumptions while threading through GP confidence and a new notion ρt to capture the cost of not fixing a bound.

Strengths
- Conceptually interesting and timely: bridges the “bound-based” GP-UCB and “unknown smoothness” partitioning paradigms, with a clear algorithmic instantiation that avoids auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Theoretical contribution: an exponential regret bound in the number of true function evaluations, without δ-cover, and with an interpretable decomposition showing how GP helps (increasing Ngp, decreasing ρ̄t, with a trade-off via Ξn). The dependence on L, α, p is transparent.
- Practical aspects: the algorithm is simple to implement on top of hierarchical partitioning; skipping function evaluations when UCB < incumbent is a useful speedup; code availability is a plus.
- Empirical results suggest consistent improvement over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI on a range of benchmarks, and demonstrate how to use IMGPO within REMBO for very high-dimensional settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions/validity of UCB guarantee: The analysis relies on the event f(x) ≤ μ(x|D)+ςσ(x|D) holding for all UCB calls with probability ≥ 1−η. The paper sets ςM via a union bound over M UCB calls, but the required modeling assumptions (is f a GP draw? or does f lie in an RKHS ball?) are not clearly stated. Prior GP-UCB analyses require either a GP-sample assumption or RKHS norm bounds plus information gain; this paper appears to bypass γT/information gain but still assumes simultaneous concentration. Please clarify the precise assumptions under which the high-probability UCB event holds and reconcile them with the empirical observation that the GP prior can be “misleading” (Rosenbrock2), which would violate assumptions.
- Exponential rate and constants: While exp(−cN) is compelling, the constant c depends on D, C, α, ρ̄t, and Ξn. The paper acknowledges that λ→1 as D grows. It would help to more explicitly compare to SOO/BaMSOO constants and to discuss whether exponential-in-N beats polynomial rates for realistic values of ρ̄t and Ξn. As it stands, the rate is partly self-referential (depends on ρ̄t induced by the algorithm).
- Algorithm clarity and complexity: The pseudocode has notational inconsistencies (n vs N, use of h, i*, T′ sets) and several typos, making it hard to reproduce. Step (iii) requires computing maxima over T′ up to depth ξ, which can blow up exponentially in ξ; Ξmax is introduced to cap this but is otherwise heuristic. A clearer accounting of per-iteration computational complexity and memory is needed, as well as how Ξ is adapted (Line 38) and its effect on the bound (via Ξn).
- Experimental methodology: 
  - Acquisition baselines (GP-EI/PI) depend heavily on the optimizer used; implementing them with SOO+gradient is unconventional and may bias comparisons. A standard multi-start L-BFGS or CMA-ES would be more typical.
  - Hyperparameter learning is done for all GP-based methods, but there is limited discussion of sensitivity to kernel choice/misspecification. Given that the theory assumes reliable UCBs, it would be valuable to report performance under model mismatch and to measure actual frequency of UCB violations.
  - Missing details: exact evaluation budgets, stopping criteria, and how “dummy” GP-based skips are counted. Please ensure a fair comparison on the number of true function evaluations and provide wall-clock times (Table 1 is mentioned but not shown here).
  - Ablations: it would strengthen the paper to include ablations without GP pruning, and with fixed finite-metric choice, to empirically validate the roles of GP pruning, ρ̄t, and Ξ.
- Related work and positioning: While the connections to DIRECT, SOO, and BaMSOO are discussed, the paper would benefit from a sharper comparison to GP-UCB-style deterministic bounds (e.g., Srinivas et al.) and to other recent BO algorithms that avoid heavy auxiliary optimization.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely state the probabilistic assumptions on f needed for the UCB event to hold uniformly over all UCB calls, and justify the choice of ςM. How does this relate to γT-based analyses?
- Can you provide a tighter bound or empirical estimate for ρ̄t and Ξn on your benchmarks? How large do they get in practice?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration as a function of D, depth(T), and Ξmax? How sensitive is performance to Ξmax and η?
- How are function evaluation budgets matched across methods when IMGPO skips evaluations via UCB?

Pros
- Novel and principled algorithm that marries “unknown smoothness” partitioning with GP guidance.
- First exponential regret guarantee for a practical GP-based optimizer without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization (to my knowledge).
- Clear insights via ρ̄t and remarks on the utility of non-tight bounds.
- Promising empirical results; code available.

Cons
- Clarity and rigor around the UCB assumption and proof details need strengthening.
- Practical complexity and parameterization (Ξ, Ξmax) are under-explained.
- Baseline implementation choices may disadvantage GP acquisition methods; missing ablations and some experimental details.
- Exponential rate constants may be weak in higher dimensions.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful idea with nontrivial theory and promising experiments. With clarifications on assumptions, improved exposition, and stronger experimental methodology (fairer baselines, ablations, and complexity reporting), it would make a solid NeurIPS contribution.
