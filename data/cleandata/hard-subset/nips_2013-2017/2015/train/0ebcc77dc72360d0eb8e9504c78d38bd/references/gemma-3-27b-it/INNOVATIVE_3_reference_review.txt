This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP) based Bayesian optimization algorithm, IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results. The core idea is to simultaneously consider multiple potential Lipschitz-like bounds, leveraging the GP to prune unlikely candidates, effectively performing a search over a space of possible semi-metrics. This is a bold approach, stepping away from relying on a single, potentially inaccurate bound estimated directly from the data. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing an exponential convergence rate and demonstrate empirical improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of simultaneously exploring multiple bounds is genuinely novel within the context of GP-based Bayesian optimization.  It’s a clever way to sidestep the limitations of directly estimating a tight Lipschitz constant or relying on impractical sampling techniques. This goes beyond incremental improvements.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The paper provides a theoretical analysis guaranteeing exponential convergence.  While the constants involved might be large in practice, the theoretical foundation is a significant accomplishment.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate IMGPO’s superior performance across a range of benchmark functions, validating the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The fact that the method works well even without explicitly tuning hyperparameters is a strong point.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The authors clearly position their work within the broader context of bound-based optimization and highlight the limitations of existing methods like BaMSOO and SOO.
* **Insightful Discussion:** The analysis section goes beyond simply stating the theorem and offers valuable insights into the interplay between the GP-estimated bound and the unknown semi-metric.  The discussion of the implications of a “tight” or “loose” GP bound is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:**  The algorithm is conceptually complex and the description, while generally clear, could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the hierarchical partitioning and bound selection process.  The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is dense and might be challenging for some readers to follow.
* **Parameter Sensitivity (Ξmax):** While the authors note that performance isn’t highly sensitive to Ξmax in the tested scenarios, a more systematic investigation of its impact would be valuable. The practical implications of choosing a suitable value for Ξmax are not fully explored.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The theoretical analysis identifies potential scalability issues as dimensionality increases. While the authors suggest potential remedies, addressing this concern more explicitly would strengthen the paper.  The experiment with 1000 dimensions, relying on REMBO, feels somewhat tacked on and doesn’t fully address the core scalability concern of IMGPO itself.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison to GP-EI and GP-PI is limited.  More comprehensive comparisons with recent, highly optimized GP-based Bayesian optimization frameworks would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is highly original and represents a significant departure from existing approaches to GP-based Bayesian optimization.
* The theoretical results provide a solid foundation for the algorithm.
* The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods.
* The insightful analysis provides a deeper understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.
* The paper addresses a long-standing open problem in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The algorithm's complexity and potential scalability issues could limit its practical applicability.
* The parameter sensitivity (especially concerning Ξmax) warrants further investigation.
* A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization frameworks is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly original contribution. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the promising empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. The paper addresses a significant problem in Bayesian optimization and offers a compelling solution. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference and recommend **acceptance**.  The potential impact of this work justifies overlooking the minor flaws and the incomplete exploration of all parameter spaces. The boldness of the approach and the potential to significantly advance the field are particularly compelling.
