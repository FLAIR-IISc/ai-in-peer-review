This paper proposes a new Gaussian Process (GP) based Bayesian optimization algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric defining a potentially tighter bound than that provided by the GP, and to simultaneously explore multiple possible bounds, effectively acting as a safeguard against a poorly calibrated GP.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation and methodology. The connection to existing bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*) is a useful framing. The presentation of the algorithm itself is clear, although the description of the hierarchical partitioning and the various steps (i)-(v) could benefit from a more concise and less repetitive explanation. The inclusion of a pseudocode is valuable. The theoretical analysis, aiming to establish an exponential convergence rate, is a significant undertaking, and the attempt to relate the performance to the tightness of the GP bound is insightful. 

However, despite the clear presentation, I harbor significant reservations regarding the novelty and, critically, the demonstrated impact of this work. My primary concern lies with the empirical evaluation. The experimental setup, while including some standard benchmarks, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The choice to fix hyperparameters (σ and l) across all methods, rather than performing proper hyperparameter tuning, is a serious limitation. This effectively puts IMGPO at a disadvantage, as GP performance is notoriously sensitive to kernel parameters.  The claim of outperforming other algorithms “in general” is not strongly supported by the provided results. While IMGPO performs well on some functions, its advantage is not consistent, and the results are not statistically significant enough to justify strong claims. The explanation for SOO’s performance on Rosenbrock2—that the GP prior was misleading—feels like a post-hoc rationalization rather than a principled understanding of the limitations. 

Furthermore, the theoretical contribution, while mathematically rigorous, feels somewhat detached from the practical aspects of the algorithm.  The reliance on Assumption 1 (the existence of an unknown semi-metric) is a strong assumption, and its impact on the performance in real-world scenarios remains unclear. The discussion in Remark 3 about the benefit of considering all possible bounds, while theoretically sound, is not convincingly demonstrated empirically. The presented empirical results do not clearly demonstrate *why* IMGPO's exploration strategy is superior to simply refining the GP model with better hyperparameters.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Interesting theoretical framework connecting GP-based optimization to broader bound-based search methods.
*   Attempt to address a practical limitation of existing theoretical results (the δ-cover sampling).
*   Pseudocode provided.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Empirical evaluation is limited by fixed hyperparameters and lacks statistical rigor.
*   Claims of outperformance are not strongly supported by the results.
*   Strong assumption about the existence of an unknown semi-metric.
*   Theoretical analysis feels somewhat disconnected from the practical implementation and empirical validation.
*   The benefit of the "infinite-metric" approach over standard GP optimization with proper hyperparameter tuning is not clearly demonstrated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian optimization with a rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   The connection to broader optimization paradigms is valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the claims of superior performance.
*   The theoretical assumptions may be unrealistic in practice.
*   The lack of a compelling demonstration of the benefits of the proposed algorithm compared to well-tuned standard methods. 

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the paper demonstrates technical competence and presents an interesting theoretical idea, the lack of a convincing empirical validation and the strong assumptions limit its impact. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a practical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors need to significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation, perform a proper hyperparameter search, and provide a more compelling justification for the proposed algorithm’s advantages over existing methods before it would be suitable for NIPS. A clearer connection between the theoretical analysis and the observed experimental results is also crucial.
