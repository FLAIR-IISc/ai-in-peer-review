Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles global optimization of a deterministic black-box objective using Bayesian optimization (BO) with Gaussian processes (GPs). The central claim is an algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves exponential simple regret convergence without relying on δ-cover sampling (as in de Freitas et al.) or auxiliary acquisition-function optimization. IMGPO combines hierarchical partitioning (DIRECT/SOO-style) with GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCB) to prune and defer evaluations. The key idea is to not commit to a single smoothness bound: assume an unknown semi-metric ℓ encoding Hölder-type continuity around the global maximizer (Assumptions 1–2), and consider “infinitely many” candidate bounds implicitly through a partition-and-bound procedure; use the GP UCB only to eliminate candidates looser than the GP bound. A new notion, the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt, quantifies the extra partitions induced by not committing to a specific bound. The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) bounds simple regret by O(λN+Ngp) for λ<1, where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp counts times the algorithm substitutes a GP-UCB in lieu of evaluating f. The paper connects to DIRECT and SOO/DOO (Munos), and contrasts with BaMSOO (Wang et al.), claiming exponential rather than polynomial rates. Experiments on several standard functions (plus a REMBO-1000D case) suggest IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-PI/EI under a uniform Matern-5/2 kernel choice with empirical Bayes hyperparameter learning.

Strengths:
- Problem importance: Removing δ-cover and auxiliary optimization while retaining fast convergence would be valuable in BO for expensive objectives.
- Algorithmic design: The integration of hierarchical partitioning with GP-UCB to skip or defer evaluations is elegant and practically motivated (objective evaluations dominate cost).
- Novel analysis angles: The “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt provides an interpretable bridge between unknown smoothness and algorithmic search overhead; remarks analyze how GP affects ρt and the regret.
- Practical aspects: Code availability, simple division scheme, and a procedure that avoids an inner nonconvex optimization are appealing for practitioners.
- Empirical results: On multiple test functions, IMGPO appears competitive or superior, and the REMBO-1000D example hints at composability with high-dimensional BO frameworks.

Weaknesses and concerns:
1) Validity of the UCB property under empirical Bayes: The analysis of step (iii) relies on f(x) ≤ μ(x|D)+ςσ(x|D) holding uniformly with probability ≥1−η. The paper sets ςM = sqrt(log(π^2 M^2/(12η))), but does not assume the standard GP-UCB conditions (e.g., f in the RKHS of the kernel with bounded norm and fixed hyperparameters). Hyperparameters are updated online via empirical Bayes, and the experiments acknowledge violations of the UCB property (e.g., Rosenbrock2). Without explicit assumptions ensuring a valid high-probability upper bound under hyperparameter learning/misspecification, the key probabilistic guarantee used in Theorem 1 is not justified.

2) Dependence of the rate on Ngp and ρ̄t: The main bound is O(exp(−c (N+Ngp)/(D ρ̄t) + …)). Two issues:
   - Regret is typically measured versus the number of function evaluations N. Counting Ngp (the number of “skipped evaluations” where only UCBs are computed) as if they contributed like real observations inflates the apparent convergence rate. The posterior is not updated at these points, so Ngp does not increase information about f; it only prunes. A bound purely in terms of N (with explicit dependence on D and algorithmic constants) would be more meaningful.
   - The exponential rate hinges on ρ̄t being O(1). When the GP bound is “not useful,” the paper concedes ρ̄t can grow with N/t, and then the bound becomes exponential in the number of outer iterations t rather than N, weakening the main headline. There is no a priori control of ρ̄t without additional assumptions.

3) Assumptions and interpretation: Assumption 1–2 essentially require Hölder continuity of the optimality gap, f(x*)−f(x) ≤ L ||x−x*||α_p, a standard local smoothness condition. The paper’s “infinite-metric” framing is insightful, but the theoretical improvement over SOO/DOO arises primarily from assuming and leveraging a tighter effective bound through pruning; the novelty is more in the analysis than in fundamentally new algorithmic principles beyond BaMSOO/SOO/DIRECT.

4) Sensitivity to Ξ and Ξmax: Step (iii) uses a lookahead depth ξ capped by Ξmax and dynamically updated Ξ. Theorem 1 degrades with Ξn, yet the paper provides heuristic updates and limited guidance. This parameter materially affects both runtime and constants in the bound; a more principled control or an analysis that removes Ξ would strengthen the contribution.

5) Experimental evaluation:
   - The comparison for GP baselines uses SOO + local gradient search to optimize PI/EI, which is atypical and might disadvantage them. There is no comparison to DIRECT, which is a natural baseline here.
   - No ablations on ρ̄t, Ξmax, or hyperparameter learning to correlate empirical behavior with theory.
   - UCB-validity violations are observed, but the impact on algorithm behavior is not analyzed.
   - The high-dimensional REMBO experiment is a single case; broader evidence would be needed to support claims about scalability.

6) Clarity and presentation: Several notational glitches and typos (e.g., definitions and indexing in the pseudo-code, the exact definition of λ) hinder readability. The dependence of constants (C, β, ρ̄t) on dimension and partition scheme could be presented more cleanly, and the relationship between N, t, and the number of partitions would benefit from a clear lemma.

Questions for the authors:
- Under what explicit assumptions on f, kernel, and hyperparameters does your UCB bound hold when hyperparameters are updated online? Can you provide a theorem with fixed hyperparameters to make the main guarantee rigorous?
- Can you restate the regret bound purely as a function of N (perhaps by upper bounding Ngp in terms of N and Ξmax), and provide conditions under which ρ̄t is bounded?
- How sensitive is IMGPO to Ξmax and to kernel misspecification? Can you include ablations and a DIRECT baseline?
- Can you quantify wall-clock time and the number of UCB calls versus function evaluations?

Pros:
- Addresses an important gap (δ-cover impracticality; no auxiliary optimization).
- Clever hybrid of SOO/DIRECT with GP pruning; concept of ρ̄t is insightful.
- Promising empirical performance; code available.

Cons:
- Key probabilistic assumption for UCB is not justified under the stated setting (empirical Bayes).
- Main rate depends on Ngp and ρ̄t; claim of exponential in N is contingent and may be misleading.
- Limited empirical analysis of design choices and missing baselines; clarity issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The ideas are interesting and potentially impactful, but the current theoretical justification relies on a UCB property that is not established under hyperparameter learning, and the main convergence claim depends on quantities not controlled in terms of N. With strengthened assumptions/analysis (or fixed-kernel theorem), a bound in N only, and more thorough experiments/ablations, this could be a strong contribution.
