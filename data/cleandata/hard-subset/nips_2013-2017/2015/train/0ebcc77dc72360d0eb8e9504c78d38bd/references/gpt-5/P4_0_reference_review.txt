Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses deterministic global optimization of a black-box function over a compact domain, using Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes (GPs). The goal is to achieve exponential simple-regret convergence without relying on the δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. (ICML’12), which is computationally impractical, and to improve upon the polynomial-rate guarantees of BaMSOO (AISTATS’14).

The proposed algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), combines hierarchical partitioning (as in DIRECT and SOO) with GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCB). The key conceptual shift is to avoid committing to a single, potentially loose, global smoothness bound; instead, IMGPO behaves “optimistically” over an implicit family of possible semi-metric bounds around the global optimum. The GP posterior UCB is used adaptively to prune candidates that are looser than what the data support.

Assumptions: (1) an unknown semi-metric ` such that f(x*) ≤ f(x) + `(x, x*), and (2) a Hölder-type control `(x, x*) ≤ L||x−x*||_p^α with unknown constants. Under these (standard in optimistic partitioning; cf. DOO/SOO), and under an event that the GP UCBs upper-bound f with high probability, the authors prove exponential regret: r_N = O(λ^{N+N_gp}) with λ < 1, where N is the number of function evaluations and N_gp counts deferred GP-based “virtual” evaluations. They introduce the infinite-metric exploration loss ρ_t (number of intervals divided per iteration), which modulates the constant λ via its average ρ̄_t; GP-based pruning reduces ρ̄_t and increases N_gp, tightening the bound. The analysis contrasts with BaMSOO’s polynomial rate and provides remarks quantifying how GP quality, infinite-metric exploration, and dimension affect the rate.

Algorithmically, IMGPO maintains a tree of hyperrectangles, selects per-depth candidates by center value, screens with GP UCBs, and performs trisection along the longest coordinate. It avoids auxiliary global optimization of the acquisition. Experiments on several standard test functions (1–6D) and a 1000D REMBO-embedded task compare IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-EI/PI, showing generally faster simple-regret decrease and lower wall-clock than GP-EI/PI (though slower than SOO).

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The analysis framework is careful about isolating the impact of GP bounds (via N_gp) and the exploration overhead (via ρ̄_t), yielding interpretable exponential-rate expressions.
- Assumptions (local Hölder continuity near the optimum) align with optimistic partitioning literature and are broadly applicable in deterministic settings.
- The algorithmic design is concrete, with pseudocode and practical details (e.g., capping lookahead depth Ξ_max, empirical Bayes updates).

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Validity of GP UCBs: The guarantee f(x) ≤ μ(x|D) + ςσ(x|D) with high probability typically requires either (i) f is a GP sample from the chosen kernel, or (ii) f lies in the RKHS with bounded norm and appropriate β_t (as in Srinivas et al., ICML’10). The paper sets ς_M = sqrt(log(π^2 M^2/(12η))) without specifying assumptions linking f and the GP (noise-free, RKHS norm, sampling from prior). Indeed, the experiments note many violations when the prior is “misleading.” Since the main theorem depends on this high-probability UCB event, the conditions under which it holds need to be clearly stated and aligned with established results.
- The “unknown semi-metric” Assumption 1 is effectively vacuous without Assumption 2; combined, they reduce to standard Hölder smoothness near x*. The framing as “infinite-metric” is conceptual; the technical newness lies in using GP-UCB for pruning rather than driving the bound.
- Constants and dimensional dependence: λ = 3^{-α/(2 C D ρ̄_t)} can be very close to 1 for moderate D or large ρ̄_t, limiting practical impact of the exponential form. The analysis remarks acknowledge this but do not provide mechanisms to control ρ̄_t beyond heuristic reliance on GP usefulness.
- Computational complexity: Step (iii) may compute many UCBs; Ξ_max caps this, but no complexity bound in terms of N and D is provided. The effect of Ξ on both time and regret is only qualitatively discussed.
- Some steps/notations in the pseudocode are difficult to follow (e.g., definitions of T′, index handling, updates of Ξ, typos such as “Ξ ← Ξ + 22”, unclear line references), which hinders reproducibility from the text alone.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable but contains notational inconsistencies, missing definitions, and typos in the algorithm that should be corrected for precision. The role and required properties of the semi-metric should be explicitly stated, and all constants (C, β, ς_M) and sets (T′) precisely defined. The probabilistic assumption for UCB validity must be clearly stated with citations to appropriate concentration results.

Originality
- Combining optimistic partitioning with GP-UCB solely for pruning, and analyzing its effect via ρ̄_t and N_gp to obtain exponential regret without δ-covers, appears novel relative to SOO/DOO, DIRECT, and BaMSOO. The “infinite-metric exploration loss” is a useful conceptual addition.

Significance
- If the UCB validity is established under standard assumptions (e.g., RKHS-norm boundedness), the result provides the first exponential simple-regret rate for GP-based BO without δ-covers and without auxiliary acquisition optimization, which is of interest to the BO community.
- Empirically, IMGPO seems competitive to strong deterministic baselines and avoids costly inner optimizations. However, experimental scope is limited (few functions, mainly low-D, deterministic), and stronger baselines/tuning for GP-EI/PI and more ablations (effect of GP vs no-GP, ρ̄_t, Ξ_max) would strengthen claims.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel analysis yielding exponential regret without δ-covers, bridging GP-BO and optimistic partitioning.
- Intuitive framework (ρ̄_t, N_gp) clarifying how GP information helps.
- Practical algorithm avoiding auxiliary optimization; code availability.
- Competitive empirical performance.

Cons against acceptance
- Incomplete or unclear probabilistic assumptions for GP UCB validity; potential gap relative to established GP-UCB theory.
- Strong dimensional dependence; practical advantage of exponential form may be limited.
- Clarity issues in algorithm description and notation.
- Limited experimental breadth and baseline strength.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- State precisely the conditions under which f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holds with probability 1−η, and align ς_M (or β_t) with standard results (e.g., RKHS-norm bounds or GP-sample assumptions). If assuming f is a GP draw, say so; otherwise provide an RKHS-based bound and required constants.
- Clarify Assumption 1 (properties of the semi-metric) and emphasize that Assumption 2 is the operative smoothness condition; relate to near-optimality dimension.
- Provide a bound (or empirical scaling) on ρ̄_t and computational complexity as functions of D, Ξ_max, and N.
- Correct and streamline the pseudocode; define all symbols, sets, and constants; fix typos; add a worked 1D example with exact indices.
- Expand experiments: include additional benchmarks (including noisy settings), stronger/tuned GP baselines, and ablations isolating the effect of GP pruning (Ngp, ρ̄_t), and sensitivity to Ξ_max and kernel mis-specification.

Overall, the paper presents a promising and potentially significant idea, but clarification of the probabilistic assumptions underlying the UCB, improved exposition, and stronger empirical validation would substantially strengthen it.
