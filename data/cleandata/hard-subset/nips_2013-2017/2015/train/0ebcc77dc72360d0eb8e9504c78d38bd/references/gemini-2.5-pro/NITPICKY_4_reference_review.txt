**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The main contribution is to provide a GP-based method that provably achieves an exponential convergence rate without relying on the computationally impractical δ-cover sampling procedure, a limitation of previous work by de Freitas et al. [1]. The authors combine ideas from hierarchical partitioning methods like SOO [18], which are agnostic to the function's smoothness, with a standard GP-UCB framework. The core idea is that while a GP provides a useful (but potentially loose) upper bound on the function, there exists a tighter, unknown bound related to the function's local smoothness around the optimum. The proposed algorithm, IMGPO, implicitly considers an infinite set of possible smoothness properties (via hierarchical partitioning) and uses the GP-UCB bound to prune the search space, thereby accelerating convergence. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing an exponential regret bound and experimental results on several benchmark functions suggesting IMGPO outperforms existing methods like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-EI/PI.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-defined open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature: achieving an exponential convergence rate with GPs without impractical sampling assumptions. The proposed method of synergistically combining the "known bound" from a GP with the "unknown bound" framework of SOO is novel and insightful. If the claims hold, this represents a significant theoretical and practical advancement.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a formal regret analysis leading to an exponential convergence rate (Theorem 1). The analysis in the remarks, which attempts to disentangle the effects of the GP component and the "infinite-metric" component, provides a useful conceptual framework for understanding the algorithm's behavior.
3.  **Experimental Design:** The empirical evaluation is reasonably thorough. The choice to use a standard kernel with fixed initial hyperparameters, rather than function-specific tuning, makes the experimental setup more realistic and convincing. The inclusion of an experiment in high dimensions (via REMBO) is a welcome addition. The authors are also commendably transparent about a case where their method underperformed (Rosenbrock2) and provided a plausible explanation.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, despite its promising core ideas, suffers from a substantial number of flaws in clarity, technical precision, and presentation. These issues are severe enough to preclude a confident assessment of the work's correctness and reproducibility.

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm:** The algorithm's description is convoluted.
    *   The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is exceedingly complex, featuring three separate loops over the tree depth `h` and a nested `while true` loop whose purpose is not immediately obvious. The overall flow is difficult to parse, and it is unclear if a simpler implementation exists.
    *   The description of step (iii) and its implementation in lines 18-23 is particularly alarming. The computation of `z(h, i*h)` requires finding the maximum UCB over `T'h+ξ(ch,i*h)`, which is defined as the set of centers in a "fully expanded tree" of depth `ξ`. This implies an exponential number of UCB evaluations (`3^ξ` in this partitioning scheme), which would render the algorithm computationally intractable for even modest `ξ`. The paper mentions this as a worst-case scenario but does not explain how it is avoided in practice or how this search is actually performed. This critical detail is glossed over.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Precision:** There are several instances of technical sloppiness that undermine confidence in the results.
    *   The statement of the main result, Theorem 1, appears to contain a significant typo. The convergence rate is given as `λ = 3 − α/(2CDρ̄t)`. This expression is nonsensical as it would be greater than 1. Presumably, it should be an exponent, e.g., `λ = 3^(-α/(2CDρ̄t))`. This is a critical error in the paper's central claim.
    *   Following this, the claim of achieving a rate of `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` is an oversimplification. The full regret bound in Theorem 1 contains other terms (`Ξn`, constants), meaning the base of the exponent is not constant but depends on `N`. This is misleading.
    *   The adaptive update rule for `Ξ` in line 38 of Algorithm 1 (`Ξ← Ξ + 22`) appears to be a typo for `Ξ ← Ξ + 2^2`, as mentioned in the text. Another typo appears in the same line (`Ξ← max(Ξ− 2−1, 1)` in the pseudocode vs. `2^{-1}` in the text). Such inconsistencies are concerning.

3.  **Formatting and Presentation:** The manuscript exhibits a general lack of care, particularly in the bibliography.
    *   The reference list is in an unacceptable state for a formal submission. Nearly every reference [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, etc.] is incomplete, missing venue, year, page numbers, or other critical bibliographic information. Reference [21] is malformed ("Retrieved November, , ,"). This level of sloppiness makes it difficult for a reviewer to verify the context of prior work and reflects poorly on the submission's quality.
    *   There are numerous minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., "fsin1(x) = (sin(13x) sin+1)/2" in Section 5; "the access to the δ-cover sampling procedure" in the Introduction).
    *   The matrix notation for the joint GP distribution in Section 2 concatenates `k` and `kT` without a separator, making it ambiguous.

**Recommendation**

While the paper tackles an important problem with a novel approach, the execution is severely lacking. The unclear and potentially intractable algorithm description, coupled with significant technical typos in the main theorem and an exceptionally poorly formatted bibliography, makes it impossible to recommend acceptance. The sheer number of minor and major flaws suggests a rushed submission. The work has potential, but it requires a very substantial revision to address the issues of clarity, correctness, and professionalism. I recommend **rejection**.
