This paper presents a novel GP-based Bayesian optimization algorithm (IMGPO) aiming for exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure. The core idea – simultaneously considering a range of possible Lipschitz-like bounds, guided by the GP’s uncertainty estimates – is a significant and refreshing departure from standard Bayesian optimization approaches. It directly addresses a known limitation of theoretical results guaranteeing exponential convergence [1, 2] and attempts to bridge the gap with practical applicability. The authors convincingly argue that existing methods often struggle when the GP-estimated bound is weak, and their approach aims to mitigate this by exploring a broader space of potential bounds.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of exploring multiple bounds concurrently is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. This shifts the paradigm from relying on a single, often imperfect, bound to leveraging a distribution of possible bounds, effectively encoding uncertainty about the function’s smoothness.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence, which is a strong result. The connection to infinite-metric optimization and the analysis considering the interplay between the GP-estimated bound and the unknown semi-metric are well-articulated.
* **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate IMGPO’s superior performance across a variety of benchmark functions, often outperforming SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods. The careful consideration of hyperparameter tuning and the acknowledgment of limitations (e.g., the impact of Ξmax) add to the credibility of the experiments.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm, its motivation, and the theoretical underpinnings. The inclusion of a figure illustrating the algorithm's operation is helpful.
* **Addresses a Core Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental issue in Bayesian optimization – balancing exploration and exploitation when the function’s properties are unknown.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The algorithm, while theoretically sound, appears relatively complex compared to simpler GP-UCB implementations. The hierarchical partitioning and the management of multiple bounds could introduce practical overhead.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper addresses the impact of Ξmax, the sensitivity to other parameters and the practical guidelines for setting them remain somewhat unclear. A more detailed discussion of parameter tuning strategies would be beneficial.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The analysis acknowledges potential scalability issues in high dimensions (λ approaching 1). While mentioning potential mitigation strategies (e.g., leveraging additional assumptions [14, 20]), the paper doesn't explore these in detail. The experiments, while covering a range of dimensions up to 1000 (with REMBO), don’t fully address scalability in the core IMGPO algorithm itself.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper notes IMGPO is slower than SOO but doesn't provide a detailed computational complexity analysis or a comparative runtime assessment against other methods beyond the overall performance plots.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and represents a significant conceptual advance in Bayesian optimization.
* The theoretical guarantee of exponential convergence is a strong result, especially given the elimination of the δ-cover sampling requirement.
* The experimental results are compelling and support the claims made in the paper.
* The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach and suggest avenues for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm is complex and might be difficult to implement and tune in practice.
* Scalability to very high dimensions remains a concern.
* The paper lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis and a thorough runtime comparison with other methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly original idea. While the algorithm is complex and has potential scalability concerns, the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. The novelty and potential impact of the approach significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization and is worthy of acceptance. The paper prioritizes a bold new idea over exhaustive validation, which, given the potential impact, is a reasonable trade-off. Minor revisions addressing the points raised above (parameter sensitivity, scalability discussion, and computational cost analysis) would further strengthen the paper.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
