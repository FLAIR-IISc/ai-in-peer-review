**Summary**

This paper introduces a new algorithm for Bayesian global optimization, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO). The work addresses the open problem of designing a Gaussian Process (GP)-based optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without relying on computationally impractical procedures like δ-cover sampling. The proposed method builds upon the line of research on hierarchical partitioning methods, such as SOO [18] and BaMSOO [2]. The core idea is to combine the strengths of two optimization paradigms: one that relies on a known (but potentially loose) bound provided by a GP-UCB model, and another that implicitly searches over an infinite family of unknown semi-metrics, as in DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]. IMGPO uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. At each iteration, it identifies promising regions based on observed function values (the "infinite-metric" part) and then uses the GP-UCB bound to prune candidate regions that are provably suboptimal, thus avoiding unnecessary function evaluations. A key practical advantage is that IMGPO, unlike many standard GP-based methods, does not require solving an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next query point. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound, improving upon the polynomial bounds of prior practical methods like BaMSOO. The algorithm's empirical performance is demonstrated to be superior to several state-of-the-art methods on a suite of benchmark functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a clearly defined and important open problem in Bayesian optimization, as articulated by Wang et al. [2]. Achieving an exponential convergence rate without impractical assumptions is a significant theoretical advancement for GP-based optimization.
2.  **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** The central idea of synthesizing two distinct approaches to global optimization is highly novel. Instead of relying solely on the GP's upper confidence bound, the algorithm uses it as a pruning mechanism for a more general, bound-agnostic search strategy. This provides a new and effective way to leverage a GP model within a hierarchical search framework.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) establishes an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which is a substantial improvement over the polynomial bounds of related practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The analysis, through Remarks 1-3, provides valuable intuition about the interplay between the GP model's utility and the "infinite-metric exploration loss," clarifying when and why the proposed approach is beneficial.
4.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing IMGPO against relevant and strong baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI). The choice to use a standard kernel and an empirical Bayesian approach for hyperparameter tuning, rather than hand-tuning for each function, reflects a realistic use case and strengthens the claims of practical applicability. The results consistently show IMGPO outperforming or being competitive with the state of the art across a diverse set of problems.
5.  **Practical Advantages:** By embedding the point selection within the partitioning scheme, the algorithm cleverly sidesteps the need to solve an auxiliary optimization problem over the acquisition function at each step. This is a known bottleneck for methods like GP-UCB, GP-EI, and GP-PI, and the runtime comparison in Table 1 confirms that IMGPO is computationally more efficient.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** While the high-level idea is well-motivated, the detailed description of the algorithm in Section 3.3 and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 are dense and challenging to parse. Specifically:
    *   The mechanism for screening candidates using `z(h, i*h)` at Line 22 is complex. The concept of creating a "fully expanded tree" locally to compute a maximum UCB value could be explained more intuitively, perhaps with a small graphical illustration.
    *   The update rule for the parameter `Ξ` in Line 38 appears heuristic. While it may work well in practice, the paper provides little justification for its specific form (e.g., why powers of 22?). A sensitivity analysis or ablation study regarding `Ξ` and `Ξ_max` would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Scalability:** The paper correctly notes in the analysis that the constant `λ` in the regret bound depends on the dimensionality `D` and can approach 1 for large `D`, implying a degradation in performance. While the paper includes a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine, this primarily demonstrates the utility of the REMBO wrapper. The scalability limitations of the core IMGPO algorithm itself could be discussed more explicitly as a primary area for future work.
3.  **Connection between Assumptions and Model:** The analysis relies on standard assumptions about the existence of a semi-metric `l` that bounds the function near its optimum (Assumptions 1 and 2). However, the relationship between these function properties and the choice of the GP kernel is not discussed. It would be insightful to explore whether a well-specified kernel (i.e., one that accurately reflects the function's smoothness) translates to more favorable constants (`L`, `α`) in the regret bound, thereby tightening the connection between the learning model and the theoretical guarantees.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a strong theoretical solution to a known open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   The core idea is novel, elegant, and combines two previously separate lines of research.
*   The theoretical claims of an exponential regret bound are a significant improvement over prior art.
*   The algorithm is shown to be practically effective and computationally efficient through solid empirical evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The algorithmic description is complex and could be improved for clarity and reproducibility.
*   The scalability of the core algorithm with increasing dimensionality remains a significant challenge, as is common in this class of methods.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its significant theoretical contribution and novel approach, substantially outweigh its weaknesses, which are primarily related to presentation clarity and known challenges of scalability. This is a high-quality contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
