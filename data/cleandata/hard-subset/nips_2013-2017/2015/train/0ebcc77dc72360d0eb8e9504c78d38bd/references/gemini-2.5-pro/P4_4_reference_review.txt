### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for deterministic black-box functions using a Bayesian optimization framework. The authors aim to develop an algorithm that achieves an exponential rate of convergence for the simple regret, a goal that has been theoretically achieved before [1] but only with impractical assumptions such as access to a `δ-cover` sampling procedure. More practical approaches that combine Gaussian Process (GP) models with hierarchical partitioning schemes, such as BaMSOO [2], have so far only demonstrated polynomial regret bounds. The authors identify this gap as an important open problem.

The main contribution is a novel algorithm named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO). IMGPO integrates a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, similar to that used in SOO [18], with a GP-UCB model. The core idea is to not rely exclusively on the bound provided by the GP, which may be loose. Instead, the algorithm implicitly operates over an infinite set of possible semi-metrics that define local smoothness around the optimum (Assumption 1), a concept pioneered by algorithms like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]. The GP model is not used to guide the search directly by maximizing an acquisition function, but rather as a mechanism to prune sub-optimal regions within the hierarchical partitioning scheme. This hybrid approach allows the algorithm to simultaneously leverage the data-driven bounds from the GP and the robust multi-scale search of partitioning methods.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis of IMGPO, proving that it achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` with `λ < 1`, without requiring `δ-cover` sampling or an auxiliary optimization step. This result formally closes the aforementioned open problem. The analysis introduces the notion of an "infinite-metric exploration loss" to characterize the trade-offs in the algorithm. Empirically, IMGPO is compared against SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP optimization methods (GP-PI, GP-EI) on several benchmark functions, demonstrating superior or competitive performance.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The primary strength is the theoretical contribution: Theorem 1 provides the first exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm. This is a significant advance over the polynomial bounds of related methods like BaMSOO. The analysis is detailed and introduces an insightful framework for understanding the interplay between the GP model and the partitioning scheme through the "infinite-metric exploration loss" `ρ_t`. The remarks following the main theorem clearly articulate how the tightness of the GP bound and the "infinite-metric" approach contribute to the overall performance, strengthening the theoretical narrative. The experimental evaluation is sound, comparing against relevant and state-of-the-art baselines on a standard set of benchmark problems. The claim of avoiding auxiliary optimization is a major practical advantage.

A minor weakness lies in the discussion of scalability. The analysis correctly points out that the convergence rate `λ` worsens with increasing dimensionality `D`, a known issue for space-partitioning methods. While an experiment is shown on a 1000D problem by embedding IMGPO within the REMBO framework, this demonstrates its utility as a component rather than its inherent scalability. The lack of experiments on moderately high-dimensional problems (e.g., 10-20 dimensions) makes it difficult to assess its practical limits.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is well-written and structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The high-level overview of the algorithm in Section 3.1 and the illustration in Figure 1 are very helpful for building intuition. The relationship to previous work is thoroughly explained.

However, the detailed description of the algorithm and its pseudocode (Algorithm 1) are quite complex and could be improved for clarity. Specifically:
- The procedure for selecting candidate intervals (lines 8-24) involves multiple nested loops and conditions that are difficult to parse without careful study.
- The term `T'_{h+ξ}(c_h,i*_h)` at line 21, representing a fully expanded subtree, is a critical component but its computation and role could be explained more intuitively.
- The update rule for the parameter `Ξ` in line 38 appears heuristic. While it may work well in practice, its introduction lacks theoretical grounding in the main text, which may leave readers wondering about its necessity and sensitivity.

#### **Originality**

The paper is highly original. While it builds upon established ideas from both GP optimization (GP-UCB) and deterministic global optimization (SOO, DIRECT), the synthesis is novel and effective. The central idea of using the GP not to greedily select points but to prune branches in a multi-scale optimistic search is a new perspective. This work successfully bridges two distinct lines of research in global optimization that, as the authors note, have largely developed in parallel. The resulting algorithm and its accompanying analysis significantly differ from previous contributions and solve a clearly articulated open problem posed by prior work [2].

#### **Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant for both the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization.
- **Theoretically:** It provides a definitive answer to an open question regarding the achievable convergence rates of practical GP-based algorithms, strengthening the theoretical foundations of the field.
- **Practically:** It delivers a competitive algorithm that outperforms standard methods in the experiments. By eliminating the need to solve an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem for the acquisition function, IMGPO addresses a significant computational bottleneck in conventional GP optimization, making it an attractive practical alternative.

The ideas presented, particularly the concept of using a probabilistic model to complement a robust deterministic search strategy, could inspire further research in other bound-based search and planning problems.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Solves an Open Problem:** The paper provides the first practical GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential regret bound, addressing a known gap in the literature.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The exponential convergence proof is a significant theoretical result that advances the state of the art.
3.  **Novel Algorithm Design:** The combination of hierarchical partitioning with GP-based pruning is a novel and well-motivated approach.
4.  **Practical Advantages:** The algorithm avoids the costly auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function, making it computationally appealing compared to standard GP-BO.
5.  **Strong Empirical Results:** Experiments show that IMGPO is highly competitive with or superior to existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Algorithmic Complexity:** The proposed algorithm is complex, and its description could be improved for clarity and reproducibility. The role and justification for all its components (e.g., the `Ξ` update) are not perfectly clear.
2.  **Limited Scalability:** Like many hierarchical methods, the algorithm's performance is expected to degrade significantly with dimensionality, which is not fully explored experimentally beyond its use as a subroutine in REMBO.
