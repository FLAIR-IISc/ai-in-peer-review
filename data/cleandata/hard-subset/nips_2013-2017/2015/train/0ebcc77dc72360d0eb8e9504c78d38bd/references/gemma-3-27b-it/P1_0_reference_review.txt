## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results (de Freitas et al., 2017).  The authors argue that existing GP-based Bayesian Optimization methods struggle due to difficulties in obtaining tight bounds on the objective function. IMGPO addresses this by simultaneously exploring a multitude of potential bounds, guided by the information provided by the GP, and employing a hierarchical partitioning scheme to efficiently navigate the search space. They provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and empirical validation showing performance competitive with, and often exceeding, other state-of-the-art methods.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly on the theoretical foundation laid by de Freitas et al. (2017), addressing the practical limitation of δ-cover sampling. It also acknowledges and improves upon the polynomial regret bound achieved by Wang et al. (2018) through the combination of GP-UCB with SOO.  The authors position their work within the broader context of bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*, PAC-MDPs) and draw connections to DIRECT (Jones et al., 1993) and SOO (Munos, 2013), highlighting the benefits of considering multiple potential bounds.  The paper also relates to recent advances in high-dimensional Bayesian Optimization, like REMBO (Kandasamy et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate, a significant result in the field of black-box optimization.
* **Practicality:**  IMGPO avoids the δ-cover sampling requirement, making it more readily applicable to real-world problems.
* **Novel Approach:** The concept of simultaneously exploring multiple possible bounds is a clever and potentially powerful strategy for addressing the limitations of relying on a single, potentially inaccurate GP-derived bound.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a diverse set of benchmark functions demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO, consistently outperforming other algorithms, particularly in higher dimensions. The comparison with well-established methods like SOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI provides a strong basis for evaluation.
* **Detailed Analysis & Discussion:** The paper includes a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance, discussing the impact of different parameters (e.g., Ξmax) and the interplay between the GP and the infinite-metric exploration.  The “Remarks” section provides insightful discussion on the benefits and potential drawbacks.
* **Code Availability:** Making the source code publicly available is a major plus, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The algorithm’s description is dense and the interplay between hierarchical partitioning, UCB, and the infinite-metric exploration can be difficult to grasp without careful reading.  The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is helpful, but could benefit from further simplification or annotations.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  While the authors address the role of Ξmax, the sensitivity of the algorithm to other parameters (e.g., the GP kernel hyperparameters, η) is not thoroughly investigated. The choice of blindly initialized hyperparameters is somewhat concerning.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  Although the experiments demonstrate good performance on functions up to 1000 dimensions (using REMBO), the theoretical analysis points to potential scalability issues as the dimensionality increases (due to the λ term). This is acknowledged by the authors, but a more detailed discussion of mitigation strategies would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines in High Dimensions:** The higher-dimensional experiments rely on REMBO, making a direct comparison to other state-of-the-art high-dimensional Bayesian Optimization methods difficult.  

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical guarantees (exponential convergence).
* Novel and potentially impactful approach to Bayesian Optimization.
* Compelling empirical results demonstrating superior performance.
* Clear connection to and improvement over existing literature.
* Publicly available code.

**Con:**

* Algorithm complexity and potentially steep learning curve.
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity.
* Theoretical scalability concerns.
* The high-dimensional comparison relies on REMBO and isn't a direct evaluation of IMGPO's inherent scalability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, the proposed algorithm is novel and practical, and the empirical validation is convincing. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work.  The paper addresses a crucial limitation of previous theoretical results and offers a promising new direction for research in black-box optimization.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

* **Improve Clarity:** Streamline the description of the algorithm and potentially add a visual aid (beyond Figure 1) to illustrate the hierarchical partitioning and infinite-metric exploration process.
* **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a more thorough analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to key parameters, particularly the GP kernel hyperparameters.
* **Scalability Discussion:** Expand the discussion of scalability concerns and explore potential mitigation strategies (e.g., leveraging dimensionality reduction techniques).
* **High-Dimensional Baselines:** If feasible, include a more direct comparison to other state-of-the-art high-dimensional Bayesian Optimization methods in the experiments.
