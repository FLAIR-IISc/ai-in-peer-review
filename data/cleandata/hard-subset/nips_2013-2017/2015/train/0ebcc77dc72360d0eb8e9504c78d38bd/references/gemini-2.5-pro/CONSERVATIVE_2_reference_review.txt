**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for deterministic black-box functions. The authors situate their work within the context of Bayesian optimization (BO), particularly methods based on Gaussian Processes (GPs). They identify a key open problem in the literature: designing a GP-based algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate (in terms of simple regret) without relying on impractical assumptions like the δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1], or settling for the polynomial regret bounds achieved by methods like BaMSOO [2].

The main contribution is a novel algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO). IMGPO combines principles from GP-based upper confidence bound (GP-UCB) methods with the hierarchical partitioning strategy of optimistic optimization algorithms like SOO [18] and DIRECT [19]. The core idea is to simultaneously consider an infinite set of possible smoothness assumptions (semi-metrics) for the objective function, a concept inherited from SOO, while using the bound provided by the GP posterior to prune the search space and accelerate convergence. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound, O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which represents a theoretical improvement over prior practical methods. They support their claims with experiments on several benchmark functions, comparing IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-based methods using Expected Improvement and Probability of Improvement acquisition functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Significance:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important theoretical question in the Bayesian optimization literature. Proving an exponential convergence rate for a practical GP-based algorithm without δ-cover sampling is a significant theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in BO analysis.
2.  **Novel Approach:** The combination of optimistic optimization (which is agnostic to a specific smoothness constant) with GP-based bounds is an interesting and non-trivial synthesis of two distinct lines of research in global optimization.
3.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated, clearly positioning its contribution against the limitations of recent, closely related work [1, 2]. The introduction effectively frames the problem and the paper's proposed solution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Complexity and Soundness:** The proposed IMGPO algorithm is exceedingly complex. The procedure described in Algorithm 1 involves multiple nested loops over the tree depth, a series of heuristic-feeling selection rules (steps i-iii), temporary "dummy" values, and an adaptive parameter Ξ. This intricate machinery stands in stark contrast to the conceptual simplicity of established methods like standard GP-UCB or GP-EI. The justification for the specific filtering rules feels post-hoc and lacks a clear, principled derivation. This complexity raises significant concerns about the algorithm's practicality, robustness, and ease of implementation. Furthermore, the algorithm introduces new hyperparameters (e.g., Ξmax) whose practical impact and recommended settings are not adequately explored.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the proposed method's practical superiority.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The most striking omission is a comparison against a standard GP-UCB algorithm [15]. Given that IMGPO is fundamentally a UCB-style method, GP-UCB is its most direct and relevant competitor. The authors claim an advantage in not requiring an auxiliary optimizer for the acquisition function, but this claim can only be substantiated by a direct comparison (in terms of both performance and runtime) against a method that does.
    *   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation relies heavily on a small set of low-dimensional (mostly 1D and 2D) benchmark functions. The algorithm's poor performance on the Rosenbrock2 function is attributed to a "misleading" GP prior. This is a critical weakness. In practice, the prior is frequently misspecified, and a robust algorithm should degrade gracefully rather than fail. This result calls into question the algorithm's utility in real-world scenarios. The single high-dimensional experiment using REMBO feels preliminary and does not constitute a thorough investigation of the method's scalability.
    *   **Experimental Setup:** The use of a single kernel with fixed initial hyperparameters for all methods may not provide a fair comparison. While this mimics a scenario of user ignorance, established GP-based methods are often used with at least some minimal problem-specific kernel selection. It would be more convincing to show that IMGPO's advantage holds across a range of kernel choices.

3.  **Unclear Practical Advantage:** While the theoretical improvement from a polynomial to an exponential regret bound is notable, the experiments do not show a correspondingly dramatic improvement in practical performance over the simpler BaMSOO algorithm. It is questionable whether the modest performance gains observed justify the substantial increase in algorithmic complexity. For a new method to be adopted, it must offer a compelling advantage over existing, well-understood, and simpler alternatives. Based on the evidence presented, this case has not been made.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting algorithm that formally solves an open problem in BO. The derivation of an exponential regret bound is a solid contribution. However, I have serious reservations about the work in its current form. The proposed method is highly complex, and its design includes several components that appear ad-hoc. The empirical evaluation is not rigorous enough to support the claims of practical superiority; it lacks a crucial baseline (GP-UCB) and relies on a narrow set of test problems, one of which reveals a significant vulnerability to prior misspecification.

While the theoretical novelty is appreciated, the work does not yet convince me of its practical significance or robustness when compared to established methods. The gap between the theoretical promise and the presented empirical evidence is too wide. Therefore, I lean towards recommending rejection. The paper could be substantially improved by simplifying the algorithm or providing a more principled justification for its complexity, and by conducting a much more thorough and convincing empirical study that includes standard baselines and a more challenging suite of test functions.
