

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process-based optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential convergence rates without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors address an open problem identified by Wang et al. (2013) regarding the creation of a GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure.

The paper builds upon two strands of work: (1) GP-based optimization methods like GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., 2010) that provide logarithmic regret bounds but require strong assumptions, and (2) bound-based search methods like SOO (Munos, 2011) that provide polynomial regret bounds. The authors' key insight is to simultaneously consider all possible bounds encoded in an unknown semi-metric ` (Assumption 1) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior.

This work is positioned as a novel combination of these two approaches, with the authors claiming to be the first to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The paper extends the literature on Bayesian optimization by providing a theoretical framework to understand why not relying on a particular bound can be beneficial.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proving an exponential regret bound (Theorem 1) that is stronger than the polynomial bounds of previous GP-based methods like BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2013). The analysis provides valuable insights into how the GP prior can be leveraged to improve the regret bound.

2. **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept (Definition 1) and the analysis in Remarks 1-3 provide a compelling theoretical justification for why considering infinitely many possible bounds can be beneficial, even when a single bound (from GP) is available. This is a significant conceptual contribution that extends beyond just this algorithm.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental results on eight benchmark functions generally support the theoretical claims, with IMGPO outperforming SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the performance on Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading).

4. **Practical Implementation**: The algorithm is well-specified with a clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and the authors provide a public code repository, which enhances reproducibility.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Theoretical Concerns with the Regret Bound**: The key theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) has a critical issue with the condition for λ < 1. The authors define λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1, which requires α/(2CDρ̄t) > 2, meaning ρ̄t < α/(4CD). However, ρ̄t is defined as the maximum of averages of the exploration loss ρ1:t', and ρt itself is the number of intervals to be divided during iteration t. For this condition to hold, ρ̄t would need to be very small (less than α/(4CD)), which may not be achievable in practice. If ρ̄t is too large, λ would exceed 1, making the regret bound meaningless (as the regret would increase with N). The authors acknowledge that λ approaches 1 as D increases, but they don't sufficiently address how often λ < 1 actually holds in practice.

2. **Confusing Notation and Terminology**: The paper uses several confusing notations and terms that make the theoretical analysis difficult to follow:
   - The definition of ρ̄t as "the maximum of the averages of ρ1:t'" is not well-explained and is counterintuitive.
   - The claim of "exponential convergence" is stated as O(λ^(N+Ngp)), but Ngp (number of GP evaluations) is typically much smaller than N (number of function evaluations), so the effective convergence rate is not truly exponential in the number of function evaluations.
   - The relationship between the GP prior and the unknown semi-metric ` is not clearly established. How does the GP prior help identify the "tighter" bounds? The paper states they "leverage the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer," but this is not operationalized.

3. **Experimental Limitations**: 
   - The experimental setup uses a single kernel (isotropic Matern with ν = 5/2) and fixed hyperparameters for all test functions, which may not be optimal for each function. This could disadvantage the comparison methods that might benefit from better kernel selection.
   - For GP-PI and GP-EI, the authors used SOO as the auxiliary optimization method, which might not be the most efficient approach. The comparison with BaMSOO is somewhat misleading since BaMSOO already uses GP-UCB with SOO, while IMGPO is a different approach.
   - The paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational cost of the algorithm. While the authors mention that IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods," they don't provide concrete runtime comparisons.

4. **Lack of Discussion on Practical Limitations**: The paper notes that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality D increases, suggesting scalability issues, but doesn't provide sufficient discussion on how severe this limitation is. For instance, they don't estimate for which D values λ would exceed 1, making it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the method.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Clarify the Theoretical Conditions**: The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the conditions under which λ < 1 actually holds. They should also include a concrete example showing how ρ̄t behaves in practice for a specific problem, to demonstrate that the condition is achievable.

2. **Improve Notation and Terminology**: The paper should redefine the key terms (ρ̄t, λ, etc.) to make them more intuitive. They should explicitly state that the exponential convergence is in terms of N+Ngp, not N, and discuss how this affects the practical interpretation of the regret bound.

3. **Expand Experimental Analysis**: The authors should include a more comprehensive experimental comparison that:
   - Tests different kernel choices for each function
   - Compares runtime with other methods
   - Examines how the regret bound behaves with increasing dimensionality

4. **Provide More Context on Related Work**: The paper should more explicitly contrast their approach with other recent work on Bayesian optimization with exponential convergence. For example, the paper by Wang et al. (2013) on "Bayesian Multi-Scale Optimistic Optimization" claims to achieve near-exponential convergence but with a different theoretical guarantee.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proposing a novel approach to Bayesian optimization that combines GP priors with infinite-metric exploration. The conceptual framework for understanding why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial is valuable and could extend to other domains beyond optimization.

However, the theoretical result has a critical condition (λ < 1) that may not hold in practice for many problems, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address this limitation. The experimental results support the claims but are not comprehensive enough to fully validate the theoretical claims.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions:

1. The authors must address the theoretical concerns about the condition λ < 1 in the revision. They should provide a more detailed analysis of when this condition holds and how it relates to practical problems.

2. The authors should improve the clarity of the theoretical presentation, particularly regarding the definition of ρ̄t and the relationship between the GP prior and the unknown semi-metric.

3. The authors should expand the experimental section to include more comprehensive comparisons, particularly regarding runtime and scalability with dimensionality.

This paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field if the theoretical concerns can be adequately addressed. The conceptual framework for considering infinitely many bounds is particularly valuable and could influence future work in Bayesian optimization and beyond.

## Final Comments

This is a promising paper that addresses an important open problem in Bayesian optimization. While the theoretical claims require more careful justification, the conceptual insights provided are valuable and the experimental results support the main claims. With the suggested revisions, this paper could be a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.

The authors have made a notable contribution to the understanding of how to combine GP priors with bound-based search methods, and their "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept provides a useful framework for analyzing such algorithms. The paper also offers a compelling argument for why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial, which has implications beyond just this algorithm.
