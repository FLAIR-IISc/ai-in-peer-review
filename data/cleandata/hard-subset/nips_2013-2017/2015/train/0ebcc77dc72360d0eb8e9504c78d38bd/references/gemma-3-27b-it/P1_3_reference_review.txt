## Peer Review for "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work (de Freitas et al. [1]). The core idea revolves around simultaneously considering a set of potential bounds on the objective function, leveraging the GP prior to prune unlikely candidates, and employing a hierarchical partitioning scheme for exploration. The authors argue that existing GP-based methods often struggle with obtaining tight bounds, and IMGPO addresses this by implicitly exploring a range of possible bounds dictated by an unknown semi-metric. They provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence and empirical results showcasing competitive performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the work of de Freitas et al. [1], aiming to circumvent the practical limitations of their δ-cover sampling requirement for exponential convergence. It also positions itself in relation to Wang et al. [2], acknowledging their polynomial regret bound achieved through a combination of GP-UCB and the SOO algorithm.  The authors appropriately contextualize their approach within the broader class of bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*, PAC-MDP), highlighting the importance of bound tightness and drawing connections to DIRECT [19] and SOO [18].  The work acknowledges and attempts to address the practical challenges of GP hyperparameter tuning, which is often glossed over in theoretical analyses.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence, a significant achievement. The proof, while detailed (and largely relegated to the supplementary material), appears sound.
* **Novelty:** The core idea of exploring a space of possible bounds, rather than relying on a single estimate from the GP, is a clever and potentially impactful approach.  This addresses a practical limitation of many GP-based Bayesian optimization methods.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge and address the importance of hyperparameter tuning, opting for a practical approach rather than assuming perfect knowledge.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, although limited, demonstrate that IMGPO performs competitively with other state-of-the-art algorithms on a range of benchmark functions.  The comparison to SOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI is valuable.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods and motivates the need for a more robust approach to bound estimation.
* **Connection to Existing Frameworks:** Framing the algorithm within the broader context of bound-based search methods strengthens its positioning and provides valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  While the benchmarks are standard, the parameter tuning is kept simple (isotropic Matern kernel with fixed initial hyperparameters), potentially masking performance differences. More extensive experimentation with different kernels, hyperparameter optimization strategies, and higher-dimensional problems would strengthen the claims.  The comparison to REMBO in higher dimensions is a good step, but more details regarding the integration and performance would be beneficial.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  The analysis identifies scalability as a potential challenge, particularly in high dimensions. While the paper acknowledges this, the discussion lacks concrete strategies for mitigating the issue.  Further investigation into techniques for reducing the search space or improving the efficiency of the hierarchical partitioning is needed.
* **Complexity of Algorithm:** The algorithm is relatively complex, with several parameters (Ξmax, η) and a multi-stage process involving hierarchical partitioning and UCB calculations. This complexity may make it challenging to implement and tune in practice.
* **Dependence on Assumptions:** The exponential convergence guarantee relies on Assumption 1 (the existence of an unknown semi-metric) and Assumption 2 (Lipschitz continuity of the semi-metric). The practical implications of these assumptions and their validity in real-world applications warrant further discussion.
* **Figure 1 Clarity:**  While intended to illustrate the algorithm’s operation, Figure 1 is quite dense and difficult to follow without careful reading of the accompanying text.  A more streamlined visual representation would be helpful.




**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical result: exponential convergence is a significant advance.
* Novel and well-motivated approach to bound estimation.
* Clear connection to existing work and established frameworks.
* Competitive empirical performance.

**Con:**

* Limited experimental scope.
* Scalability concerns not fully addressed.
* Algorithmic complexity may hinder practical adoption.
* Reliance on potentially strong assumptions.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The authors have presented a novel and well-motivated algorithm with a rigorous analysis and promising empirical results. While the experiments are somewhat limited and scalability remains a concern, the potential impact of this work is considerable.  The paper is of high quality and is appropriate for acceptance at NIPS, contingent on addressing some of the reviewer's comments in a rebuttal. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Expand the experimental section:** Include results on more challenging benchmark functions, explore different kernel choices and hyperparameter optimization strategies, and provide a more detailed analysis of performance in higher dimensions.
* **Address scalability concerns:** Discuss potential strategies for mitigating the scalability issues, such as dimensionality reduction techniques or more efficient partitioning schemes.
* **Clarify Figure 1:** Simplify the visualization of the algorithm's operation for better clarity.
* **Discuss the practical implications of Assumptions 1 and 2:** Provide examples of real-world problems where these assumptions are likely to hold and address potential limitations.
* **Provide a more detailed discussion of computational cost:** Compare the computational cost of IMGPO to other algorithms (SOO, GP-UCB, etc.) in terms of function evaluations and runtime.
