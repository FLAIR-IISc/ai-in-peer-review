This paper proposes a new Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to simultaneously consider multiple potential Lipschitz-like bounds, leveraging the GP to filter out overly restrictive ones, and combining this with hierarchical partitioning.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, presenting the problem, related work, and proposed algorithm in a logical flow. The motivation for addressing the δ-cover sampling issue is well-articulated. The algorithm description, including Figure 1 and Algorithm 1, provides a reasonable level of detail. However, the theoretical analysis, while attempting to demonstrate exponential convergence, feels somewhat detached from the practical implementation details and relies heavily on assumptions (Assumption 1, Assumption 2) that may not always hold in real-world optimization scenarios. 

The main strength of this work lies in its attempt to bridge the gap between theoretical exponential convergence guarantees and practical applicability of GP-based Bayesian optimization. The idea of simultaneously exploring multiple bounds is conceptually interesting, particularly the notion of exploiting an unknown, potentially tighter, bound encoded in the function's continuity. However, the practical benefit of this approach isn’t convincingly demonstrated through the experimental results. While IMGPO generally outperforms other algorithms in the experiments, the gains are often marginal, and the configuration (fixed kernel and hyperparameters) feels ad-hoc and limits the generalizability of the findings.  The claim of improved performance over BaMSOO hinges on the impracticality of the δ-cover sampling, but doesn't demonstrably outperform it *given* equivalent computational resources and a fair comparison in hyperparameter tuning. The comparison to SOO is less meaningful, as the GP-based methods operate in a fundamentally different space. The discussion of the impact of Ξmax and the analysis of the effect of tighter UCBs (Remarks 1 and 2) are insightful but contribute more to future research directions than bolstering the current contribution.

The originality of the approach lies in the combination of multiple bound estimation with hierarchical partitioning, driven by the GP. However, the core concepts – GP-based Bayesian optimization and hierarchical partitioning – are well-established. The novelty comes from the specific method of simultaneously exploring multiple bounds, but the theoretical justification feels somewhat convoluted and relies on assumptions which weaken the claim of a broadly applicable advantage. The paper thoroughly cites relevant literature, demonstrating awareness of the existing work in the field.

The significance of this work is questionable. While the theoretical analysis provides an exponential regret bound, it is contingent on several assumptions and doesn’t translate into a dramatic performance improvement in the presented experiments. The computational overhead of exploring multiple bounds isn't thoroughly investigated. The algorithm appears to be sensitive to the choice of Ξmax, further complicating its practical use. While the idea of leveraging an unknown bound is intriguing, the implementation doesn’t showcase a clear and substantial advantage over existing methods in real-world scenarios.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important practical limitation of existing Bayesian optimization algorithms (δ-cover sampling).
*   Presents a novel combination of techniques (multiple bound estimation, hierarchical partitioning, GPs).
*   Provides a theoretical analysis with an exponential regret bound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results show only marginal improvements over existing methods.
*   The algorithm’s performance appears sensitive to parameter choices (Ξmax).
*   The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions that may not hold in practice.
*   The practical benefit of exploring multiple bounds is not convincingly demonstrated.
*   The comparison to other algorithms isn't fully fair (fixed hyperparameters vs. optimized).
*   Scalability to higher dimensions remains a concern, despite the mention of REMBO.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. 

While the paper presents an interesting idea and a technically sound analysis, the limited practical gains, reliance on assumptions, and sensitivity to hyperparameters make it difficult to justify acceptance to NIPS at this time. The work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a significant advancement in the field. I encourage the authors to perform a more thorough experimental evaluation, exploring the sensitivity of the algorithm to different parameter settings and comparing it to a wider range of benchmarks using properly tuned hyperparameters. Further clarification of the assumptions and their implications would also strengthen the work.
