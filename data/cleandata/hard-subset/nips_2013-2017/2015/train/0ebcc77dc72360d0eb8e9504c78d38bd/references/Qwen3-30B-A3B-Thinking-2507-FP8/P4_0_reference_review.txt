

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work. The authors address a gap identified by Wang et al. [2] where existing GP-based methods either relied on impractical sampling procedures (de Freitas et al. [1]) or achieved only polynomial regret bounds (BaMSOO [2]). The key insight is to consider "all possible bounds" encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer (Assumption 1), rather than relying solely on the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB). The algorithm maintains a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, using the GP's UCB to prune candidates that are too loose, while simultaneously considering multiple candidates for intervals that might contain the global optimum. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, and demonstrates superior empirical performance compared to baselines on multiple benchmark functions.

**Strengths**

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with clear assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) and a well-structured proof of the exponential regret bound (Theorem 1). The authors thoughtfully analyze the effect of different components (GP, infinite-metric exploration) through Remarks 1-3, showing how the regret bound depends on various parameters. The experimental section is comprehensive, testing on eight benchmark functions across dimensions 1-1000, with fair comparisons against relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) using standardized hyperparameters.

2. **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the algorithm (Section 3.2) and detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The figure (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the algorithm's operation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

3. **Novelty**: The "infinite-metric" approach of considering all possible bounds encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer is a clear contribution that distinguishes this work from previous GP-based methods. The authors correctly identify the gap in the literature and position their work relative to prior research, particularly highlighting the contrast with GP-UCB (which relies on a single bound) and DIRECT/SOO (which consider multiple bounds but without leveraging GP).

4. **Significance**: Achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling is a significant theoretical advance in Bayesian optimization. The empirical results consistently show improvements over baselines (Figure 2), and the paper provides a new framework for understanding why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial. The approach could potentially be applied to other bound-based methods (A*, PAC-MDP algorithms) as noted in the conclusion.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Scalability Concerns**: The theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) notes that λ can get close to 1 as input dimension D increases, suggesting scalability challenges for higher dimensions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper doesn't provide concrete strategies for addressing it beyond a brief mention of "additional assumptions" (Section 4, Remark 1). The experimental results don't include comprehensive tests on high-dimensional problems beyond the 1000-dimensional Sin2 example.

2. **Algorithm Complexity**: The algorithm's computational complexity isn't thoroughly analyzed. The infinite-metric exploration loss ρt (Definition 1) represents a trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational cost, but the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how ρt scales with problem dimension or complexity.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The effect of Ξmax (a key parameter) is only briefly discussed in the experiments (Section 5). While Figure 2 shows that increasing Ξmax can improve performance for some functions, the paper lacks a systematic analysis of optimal parameter selection across different problem types.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization by providing the first GP-based method with exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines, and the paper is well-written and clear. The weaknesses noted are relatively minor in the context of the core contribution.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments**

*Pro Acceptance:*
- First GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear regret bound
- Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks
- Novel theoretical framework for understanding bound-based optimization

*Con Acceptance:*
- Scalability concerns for higher dimensions not fully addressed
- Limited analysis of parameter sensitivity (Ξmax)
- Some details about computational complexity could be expanded

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It makes a clear theoretical contribution, provides strong empirical validation, and is well-written and clear. The weaknesses noted are relatively minor in the context of the significant contribution. The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature and advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. The authors' theoretical framework for understanding the benefits of considering multiple bounds rather than a single one represents a valuable conceptual contribution to the field.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
