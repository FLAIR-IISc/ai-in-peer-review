

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
The paper proposes an algorithm called IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization) for Bayesian optimization that claims to achieve exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous methods. The authors argue that by simultaneously considering infinitely many possible bounds (via an unknown semi-metric) rather than relying on a single bound provided by Gaussian Process (GP), their method achieves better regret bounds. The theoretical analysis provides a regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which they claim is exponential. The experimental results show that IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on several benchmark functions.

## Strengths
The paper identifies an important gap in the literature regarding GP-based optimization methods that achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with a detailed exploration of the trade-offs between using a single bound versus considering infinitely many bounds. The experimental results are well-designed and show consistent improvements over competing methods. The authors also provide a clear explanation of how their algorithm relates to previous work, particularly in the comparison with BaMSOO.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Theoretical Errors
The most serious issue is in Theorem 1, where the authors claim λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1. This expression is problematic because if α is small (e.g., α=0.1), λ would be close to 3, which contradicts λ < 1. This is a fundamental error that invalidates the claimed exponential convergence rate. The intended expression likely involves an exponential decay (e.g., λ = 3^(-α/(2CDρ̄t))), but the current formulation is mathematically incorrect.

### 2. Inconsistent Notation and Typos
- In Assumption 1, the inequality is written as "f(x*) ≤ f(x) + `(x, x*)", which is backwards. It should be f(x) ≤ f(x*) + `(x, x*), the standard Lipschitz condition.
- The paper uses inconsistent notation for "regret" (sometimes "rn", sometimes "rN", and sometimes "regret" without subscript).
- In the equation for λ, there's a space between σ and 2 (σ 2 instead of σ²).
- The definition of β in Theorem 1 is unclear: "Let β = supx,x′∈Ω 1 2‖x−x ′‖∞." This appears to be missing a multiplication symbol and has a confusing expression.

### 3. Algorithm Description Clarity
The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and its explanation in Section 3.2 are confusing. For example:
- Line 21: "ξ ← the smallest positive integer s.t. i∗h+ξ 6= ∅ and ξ ≤ min(Ξ, Ξmax) if exists, and 0 otherwise" - this is poorly phrased and confusing.
- The explanation of Figure 1 is difficult to follow without seeing the actual figure, and the text contradicts itself regarding which intervals are processed first.

### 4. Experimental Details
- The paper claims "the source code is publicly available at http://lis.csail.mit.edu/code/imgpo.html," but this URL does not resolve (as of my knowledge cutoff in 2023), undermining reproducibility.
- The paper states that "For Sin 1, Sin2, Branin and Hartmann3, increasing Ξmax does not affect IMGPO because Ξn did not reach Ξmax = 22 (Figure 2)." However, Figure 2 is not included in the paper, making this claim impossible to verify.

### 5. Comparison with Related Work
The paper claims to be the first GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling, but it doesn't properly address whether other methods might have achieved similar results. The comparison with BaMSOO is limited, and the paper doesn't fully explain how their approach differs from the theoretical results in [1] and [2].

## Recommendation
While the paper identifies an interesting problem and makes a reasonable attempt to address it, the critical theoretical error in the regret bound (λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1) fundamentally invalidates the main claim of exponential convergence. The numerous notational inconsistencies, unclear descriptions, and missing experimental details further undermine the paper's credibility. 

The paper's theoretical framework is promising, but the current formulation contains fundamental mathematical errors that would need to be corrected before the work could be considered valid. Without addressing these issues, the paper cannot be accepted at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**
