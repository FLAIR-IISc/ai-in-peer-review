

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence rates without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work. The authors address an open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] regarding the lack of a GP-based method with exponential convergence that avoids impractical sampling. The key insight is to consider infinitely many possible upper bounds (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing exponential regret bounds, a detailed algorithm description, and empirical results demonstrating superior performance over existing methods like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on multiple benchmark functions.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with a rigorous analysis of the regret bound. The proof of Theorem 1 establishes an exponential convergence rate O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which represents a significant improvement over the polynomial regret bounds of previous GP-based methods (BaMSOO achieves Õ(n^(-2α/D(4-α))) as noted in the paper). The authors properly acknowledge limitations, including the scalability issue in higher dimensions (Remark 1) and the need for empirical validation of the theoretical assumptions.

However, the paper could strengthen its theoretical claims by explicitly comparing the exponents in the regret bounds. While the authors state that "we have improved the regret bound," the specific improvement in the convergence rate isn't quantified for practical settings. The relationship between the theoretical analysis and the practical performance (particularly the log-scale plots in Figure 2) could be more clearly articulated.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and generally clear. The algorithm description is supported by pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and an illustrative figure (Figure 1). The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their approach, particularly in Section 3.1 where they discuss the limitations of relying on a single bound. The relationship to prior work (especially BaMSOO and SOO) is well-established.

A minor clarity issue is the explanation of the infinite-metric exploration loss ρ_t in Section 4. While the concept is theoretically important, its practical implications for implementation and the trade-offs it represents aren't sufficiently emphasized. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how ρ_t affects implementation choices.

## Originality
This work makes a genuinely novel contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The core idea of considering infinitely many possible upper bounds (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior is original and well-motivated. The authors correctly position their work as addressing an open problem identified in Wang et al. [2]. While previous work has considered multiple bounds (e.g., DIRECT and SOO), this is the first to combine this approach with GP in a way that achieves exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.

The paper properly cites related work, including Munos [18] for the SOO algorithm and de Freitas et al. [1] for the δ-cover sampling approach. The distinction between this work and previous GP-based methods is clearly articulated.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Achieving exponential convergence without the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure represents a major practical advance for Bayesian optimization. The paper provides a theoretically grounded framework for understanding when not relying on a single bound is beneficial (Remarks 1-3), which has implications beyond Bayesian optimization for other bound-based methods like A* search.

The empirical results (Figure 2) demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods across diverse benchmark functions. The authors appropriately acknowledge that their GP prior can sometimes be misleading (as in the case of Rosenbrock2), showing thoughtful evaluation of both strengths and weaknesses.

The main limitation in significance is the scalability issue in higher dimensions (Remark 1), which the authors acknowledge but don't fully address. This is a common challenge in Bayesian optimization, but the paper could better articulate how their approach might be combined with dimensionality reduction techniques (as mentioned in Section 6) to address this limitation.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- First GP-based method achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Theoretical analysis with clear regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp))
- Practical improvements demonstrated across multiple benchmark functions
- Insightful theoretical framework explaining when not relying on a single bound is beneficial
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Weaknesses:**
- The practical implications of the theoretical exponential rate aren't fully connected to the experimental results
- Limited discussion of how to address scalability in higher dimensions
- The relationship between the theoretical analysis and the log-scale experimental results could be clearer
- The claim of "exponential regret" could be more precisely quantified against previous work

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to Bayesian optimization. The novel approach of considering infinitely many possible upper bounds rather than relying on a single GP-based bound represents an important advancement that addresses a clear open problem in the field. The theoretical analysis is sound, the empirical results are convincing, and the paper provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between different bound-based search strategies. While the scalability issue in higher dimensions is acknowledged, the paper's core contribution is substantial and represents a meaningful advance over previous work. The paper is well-written, properly contextualized within the literature, and makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
