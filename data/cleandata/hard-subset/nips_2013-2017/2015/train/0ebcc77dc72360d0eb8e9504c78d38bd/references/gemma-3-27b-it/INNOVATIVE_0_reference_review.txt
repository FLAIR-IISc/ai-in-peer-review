This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP) based Bayesian Optimization algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling required by previous theoretical guarantees. The core idea is to leverage an unknown, potentially tighter, semi-metric governing the function's smoothness alongside the GP’s estimated bound, effectively considering a broader range of possible bounds during optimization. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and experimental results showcasing IMGPO’s performance on several benchmark functions.

This is a *very* interesting paper. The motivation is excellent – bridging the gap between theoretical exponential convergence guarantees and practical applicability by eliminating the δ-cover sampling bottleneck. The core idea of implicitly considering a family of possible bounds, guided by the GP, is genuinely novel and represents a potentially significant contribution. The theoretical analysis appears solid, and the experimental results, while not overwhelmingly decisive, provide promising evidence supporting the algorithm’s effectiveness. The authors correctly identify the limitations of relying solely on the GP's bound and propose a clever workaround. The connection to bound-based search methods and the discussion of the infinite-metric exploration loss provide valuable insights. The inclusion of remarks dissecting the impact of GP tightness and the exploration loss is particularly insightful.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. The presentation, while generally clear, could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the algorithm's mechanics. Figure 1 is helpful but complex and requires careful study. The description of the division procedure in Algorithm 1, specifically the roles of Ξmax and the handling of temporary dummy values, is a bit convoluted. While the theoretical analysis establishes exponential convergence, the constant factor hidden within the O(λ^(N+Ngp)) bound is not explored. The experiments lack a detailed ablation study. For instance, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of Ξmax? The discussion regarding the influence of the kernel hyperparameters is somewhat superficial – a more thorough investigation of their impact would be beneficial. The scaling to 1000 dimensions is achieved using REMBO, and the contribution of IMGPO within that framework isn't clearly isolated. Finally, the discussion on the division procedure’s generalization to non-hyperrectangular search spaces feels rather cursory.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of leveraging an unknown semi-metric alongside the GP prior is genuinely novel and addresses a significant limitation in existing Bayesian Optimization algorithms.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence, a strong result.
*   **Potential Significance:**  If validated by further research, this approach could significantly broaden the applicability of Bayesian Optimization, making it more practical for real-world problems.
*   **Bold Approach:** The authors tackle a challenging problem and propose a non-trivial solution that challenges the conventional wisdom of relying solely on GP-estimated bounds.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Presentation:** The algorithm's description and the explanation of some implementation details could be clearer and more intuitive.
*   **Experimental Thoroughness:** The experiments lack a detailed ablation study and a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters.
*   **Practical Scalability:** While promising, the scalability to very high dimensions relies on REMBO and isn't fully demonstrated within IMGPO itself.
*   **Constant Factors:** The theoretical analysis lacks exploration of the constant factors within the convergence bound.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor flaws, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the potential for significant impact outweigh the concerns regarding presentation and experimental thoroughness. The authors have identified a crucial limitation in existing Bayesian Optimization methods and proposed a clever and potentially groundbreaking solution. While further work is needed to refine the algorithm and fully explore its capabilities, this paper represents a substantial contribution to the field and warrants publication at NIPS. I believe the potential for impact is high, and prioritizing novel ideas, even with minor imperfections, aligns with the spirit of NIPS. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the field and a willingness to tackle a challenging problem with a bold and innovative approach.
