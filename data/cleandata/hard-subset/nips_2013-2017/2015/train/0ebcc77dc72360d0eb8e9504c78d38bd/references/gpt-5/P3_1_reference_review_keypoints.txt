- Paper proposes IMGPO: GP-guided hierarchical partitioning that considers an infinite family of unknown upper bounds and prunes via GP-UCB without auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Builds on δ-cover exponential regret (de Freitas), BaMSOO (Wang et al.), and optimistic partition methods (DIRECT/DOO/SOO); novelty is exponential convergence without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization.
- Strength: original “infinite-metric” perspective blending partition optimism with GP posterior information.
- Strength: insightful theoretical device ρt quantifying the cost of not committing to a single bound and relating regret to unknown Hölder constants.
- Strength: practical design avoids δ-cover and auxiliary acquisition optimization; UCB-based skip-evaluation is attractive for expensive objectives.
- Strength: empirical results show competitive or superior performance on deterministic benchmarks; code released.
- Major concern: high-probability UCB guarantee is invalidated by adaptive (empirical Bayes) hyperparameter learning, undermining pruning and the regret theorem unless stronger assumptions are stated.
- Major concern: main regret bound depends on N+Ngp, where Ngp counts GP-imputed nodes; including Ngp inflates the effective budget and weakens claims comparing to standard BO measured by true evaluations N.
- Major concern: complexity of step (iii) is ambiguous—paper claims limited UCB calls but tree-node growth might be exponential (O(3^ξ)), making Ξmax values potentially infeasible; needs precise analysis.
- Major concern: exponential rate base λ depends unfavorably on dimension and ρ̄t, so λ→1 in high dimensions and practical exponential contraction may be negligible.
- Technical concern: derivation and justification of ςM and its union-bound-based concentration are heuristic and ignore adaptive sampling and hyperparameter tuning.
- Notational/statement issues: ambiguous use of Ξ, Ξn, Ξmax, inconsistent line references, and unclear linkage of proof quantities to computable measures.
- Experimental gaps: missing comparisons to DIRECT and well-optimized GP-UCB baselines, and missing ablations isolating infinite-metric selection, GP pruning, and Ξ schedule.
- Empirical diagnostics missing: report how often UCB prunes, scaling of Ngp, and sensitivity to kernel mis-specification.
- Quality: core idea is promising and analysis ambitious, but theoretical guarantees do not match the implemented adaptive-hyperparameter algorithm.
- Clarity: generally well written, but definitions, step (iii) complexity, and probability conditions need clearer statements and fixes to typos.
- Originality: high — formalizing infinite-metric exploration loss ρt and combining it with GP pruning is novel beyond BaMSOO/SOO/DIRECT.
- Significance: potentially significant if theory is made robust to adaptive GP modeling or claims are properly scoped and computational issues resolved.
- Suggestion: explicitly state assumptions for Theorem 1 (fixed kernel/hyperparameters or RKHS/Gaussian prior) and align experiments or weaken claims accordingly.
- Suggestion: provide bounds in terms of N alone or upper-bound Ngp in terms of N and Ξ; precisely analyze complexity of step (iii) (O(ξ) vs O(3^ξ)).
- Suggestion: add ablations for Ξ/Ξmax, report Ngp/pruning rates, and include DIRECT and optimized GP-UCB baselines.
- Suggestion: clarify derivation of ςM, relate to known GP-UCB concentration results (information gain, RKHS norm), and fix notation inconsistencies.
- Pros for acceptance: novel algorithmic idea, ambitious theory with potential impact, empirical competitiveness, and no auxiliary acquisition optimization needed.
- Cons for acceptance: theoretical claims not applicable under adaptive hyperparameter learning, Ngp dependence undermines evaluation-by-N, ambiguous computational complexity, and limited ablations/baselines.
- Recommendation: Weak reject — close to acceptance if assumptions/complexity clarified and theory aligned with practice.
