This paper presents a novel GP-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results. The core idea is to simultaneously consider a multitude of potential Lipschitz-like bounds, effectively leveraging the assumption of an unknown, potentially tighter, bound than that captured by the GP alone. This is a surprisingly elegant and bold approach, sidestepping a significant bottleneck in translating theoretical guarantees to practical algorithms. The authors appropriately connect their work to existing methods like GP-UCB, SOO, and BaMSOO, highlighting the limitations of the latter and positioning IMGPO as an improvement.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of exploring multiple potential bounds concurrently is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from standard GP-based Bayesian optimization. It directly addresses the practicality concerns surrounding δ-cover sampling.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence, a strong result, and importantly connects this convergence to the effectiveness of the GP bound. The discussion around the impact of a "tight" vs. "loose" GP bound is insightful.
* **Potential Significance:** If the empirical results hold up, this algorithm could significantly improve the efficiency of Bayesian optimization, particularly in high-dimensional or computationally expensive settings. The scalability discussion, though acknowledging limitations, points towards future research directions.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The algorithm is explained reasonably well, and the illustrations (Figure 1) are helpful in understanding the hierarchical partitioning process.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on a variety of benchmark functions demonstrate promising performance, exceeding that of several baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the algorithm's concept is clear, the implementation appears complex, involving multiple parameters (Ξmax, η) and a potentially sensitive hierarchical partitioning scheme. The code availability is positive, but the learning curve will likely be steep.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The results show that performance *can* be affected by Ξmax. While the paper acknowledges this and provides a discussion, further analysis of parameter sensitivity would be valuable.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While acknowledging the scaling issues with increasing dimensionality, the discussion feels somewhat hand-wavy. The REMBO integration is a positive step, but a more detailed analysis of computational cost with increasing dimension is needed.
* **Clarity of Relation to SOO:** While the paper correctly identifies SOO as a related work, the connection could be strengthened.  Specifically, clarifying how IMGPO’s hierarchical partitioning differs functionally from SOO's would be helpful.  The benefits of *simultaneously* considering bounds vs. SOO's more directed search are not fully articulated.
* **Theoretical assumptions:** The assumption of a semi-metric ` is strong, and its practical implications are not fully explored. Real-world functions may not neatly satisfy this.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is substantial. The authors address a core limitation of existing theoretical results in Bayesian optimization.
* The theoretical guarantees (exponential convergence) are impressive.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate a clear benefit over several strong baselines.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity might limit the practical adoption of the algorithm.
* The theoretical assumptions might be limiting in certain applications.
* While the experimental results are good, they do not definitively demonstrate overwhelming superiority across all test functions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a truly novel idea. While some aspects of the implementation and theoretical assumptions raise concerns, the potential benefits of IMGPO are significant. The authors have presented a compelling case for their algorithm, and the theoretical guarantees, combined with the promising empirical results, make this a valuable contribution to the field.  I am willing to overlook the minor flaws in the validation and methodological detail, given the boldness and potential impact of the approach.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in Bayesian optimization and is worthy of inclusion in NIPS.  I would encourage the authors to address the comments regarding parameter sensitivity and scalability in a future version.
