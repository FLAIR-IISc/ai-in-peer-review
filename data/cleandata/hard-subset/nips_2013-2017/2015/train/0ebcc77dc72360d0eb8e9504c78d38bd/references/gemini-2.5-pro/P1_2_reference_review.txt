This paper addresses the problem of global optimization of a deterministic black-box function, a central topic in machine learning and beyond. The authors propose a novel Gaussian Process (GP) based algorithm, called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret without relying on impractical assumptions such as δ-cover sampling. The key idea is to combine the hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like SOO with GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCBs). Crucially, instead of relying solely on the GP-derived bound, the algorithm operates as if it is searching over an infinite family of possible semi-metrics that could define the function's smoothness. The GP-UCB is then cleverly used not as the primary bound, but as a mechanism to prune the search space and avoid expensive function evaluations in unpromising regions. This synthesis of two major lines of work in global optimization—one based on known bounds (like GP-UCB) and one robust to unknown smoothness (like DIRECT/SOO)—is the main conceptual contribution. The authors provide a theoretical proof of an exponential regret bound and support their claims with strong experimental results on a variety of benchmark functions, demonstrating superior performance against several state-of-the-art algorithms.

### Strengths

*   **Significance:** The paper solves a well-defined and important open problem in Bayesian optimization theory. Previous work either achieved an exponential rate with the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure [1] or achieved a polynomial rate without it [2]. This work provides the first practical algorithm that provably achieves an exponential convergence rate, which is a major theoretical advance for this field.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of IMGPO is highly novel and insightful. Instead of treating the GP-UCB as the definitive upper bound, the authors reframe its role as a pruning mechanism for an underlying "bound-free" search. The concept of leveraging the existence of an unknown, potentially tighter bound (Assumption 1) and implicitly searching over all possibilities is a powerful paradigm. This unification of the DIRECT/SOO philosophy with modern GP-based methods is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   **Quality (Theoretical):** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. Theorem 1 provides the main result of an exponential regret bound, explicitly connecting the convergence rate to problem-dependent constants and a new quantity, the "infinite-metric exploration loss" `ρ̄t`. The remarks in Section 4 are particularly valuable, as they provide clear intuition about how the GP's utility affects the bound (Remark 1), the benefit of using a GP versus not (Remark 2), and the advantage of the infinite-metric approach over relying on a single, potentially misspecified bound (Remark 3). This analysis is both rigorous and illuminating.
*   **Quality (Empirical):** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. IMGPO is compared against a strong set of relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI). The setup is practical and fair, using a standard kernel and online hyperparameter tuning rather than hand-picking optimal settings for each function. The results in Figure 2 clearly demonstrate that IMGPO consistently outperforms or matches the best baseline across a diverse set of test functions, including a high-dimensional problem (via REMBO), which validates the practical utility of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses

*   **Clarity:** While the high-level overview in Section 3.1 is excellent, the detailed description of the algorithm in Sections 3.2 and 3.3 is very dense and difficult to follow. The multi-step procedure for selecting an interval to divide—involving comparisons with intervals of larger and smaller sizes—lacks sufficient intuition. For example, the motivation for steps (ii) and (iii) is not immediately obvious from the text. The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is particularly challenging to parse due to its nested loops, multiple conditions, and state variables (`υmax`, `i*h`, `Ξ`). A more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough with a simpler example could significantly improve the reader's understanding.
*   **Reproducibility:** Certain algorithmic details feel heuristic and could be better justified. The update rule for `Ξ` in line 38, which controls the lookahead for the UCB check, seems ad-hoc. A clearer explanation of its role and the reasoning behind this specific update (`Ξ ← Ξ + 22` or `Ξ ← max(Ξ − 2−1, 1)`) would strengthen the paper and aid in reproducibility.
*   **Scalability:** The authors are commendably upfront about the algorithm's limitations with respect to dimensionality, noting that the convergence rate `λ` can approach 1 as `D` increases. While they demonstrate a successful application within the REMBO framework for a high-dimensional problem, the inherent scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm remains a concern. The performance of hierarchical partitioning methods typically degrades rapidly with dimension, and it would be beneficial to include a more direct discussion of how `ρ̄t` is expected to scale with `D`.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Significance:** The paper presents a major theoretical breakthrough by providing the first practical GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate.
2.  **Excellent Novelty:** The core idea of combining "bound-free" (infinite-metric) search with GP-based pruning is a novel and powerful concept that elegantly unifies two distinct lines of research.
3.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The claims are well-supported by both a rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments showing state-of-the-art performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Clarity of Algorithm:** The detailed description and pseudocode of the algorithm are overly complex and difficult to understand, which could hinder comprehension and adoption by the community.

### Final Recommendation

Despite the issues with the clarity of the algorithm's presentation, the significance and novelty of this work are outstanding. The paper solves an important open problem and introduces a powerful new idea for designing global optimization algorithms. The theoretical and empirical results are very strong. I strongly recommend acceptance. I urge the authors to use the camera-ready version to significantly improve the clarity of Section 3, perhaps by adding more intuitive explanations and a simpler illustrative example to guide the reader through the complex selection logic.
