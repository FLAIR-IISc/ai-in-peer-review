

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper proposes IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Gaussian Process-based global optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors address a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization (posed by Wang et al. [2]), where previous GP-based methods either required impractical sampling procedures or achieved only polynomial regret bounds. The core insight is to consider an infinite set of possible bounds (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior, which enables exponential convergence while maintaining practicality.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first GP-based optimization method with exponential regret bound without δ-cover sampling. Theorem 1 establishes a regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which represents a clear improvement over BaMSOO's Õ(n^(-2α/(D(4-α)))) bound.

2. **Novel Framework**: The paper introduces a new conceptual framework for understanding when it's beneficial to consider multiple bounds rather than relying on a single one. The analysis in Remarks 1-3 provides valuable theoretical insights into how the GP prior interacts with the unknown bound, which is insightful for the broader optimization community.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments comprehensively compare IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on eight benchmark functions. The results consistently show IMGPO outperforming baselines, with particularly strong results on most test functions (Figure 2).

4. **Practical Implementation**: The algorithm is designed to be practical, avoiding the computational burden of δ-cover sampling while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is well-structured and clear.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of High-Dimensional Performance**: The paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 as dimension D increases (Remark 1), suggesting scalability challenges. However, it doesn't provide a clear strategy for handling high-dimensional problems beyond the REMBO example, which is limited to 1000 dimensions. This is a significant limitation given the importance of high-dimensional optimization in practice.

2. **Insufficient Justification of Infinite-Metric Exploration Loss**: While the paper introduces the concept of infinite-metric exploration loss (ρt), it doesn't convincingly explain why this approach is beneficial in practice. The theoretical analysis shows it can improve regret bounds, but the practical implications aren't clearly demonstrated through experiments or additional discussion.

3. **Kernel Selection Justification**: The paper states they use the isotropic Matern kernel with ν=5/2 without discussing why this specific kernel was chosen over alternatives. Given that kernel choice significantly impacts GP performance, this lack of justification is notable.

4. **Rosenbrock2 Case**: The paper mentions that SOO outperformed IMGPO on Rosenbrock2 because "our GP prior was misleading" (p. 12), but doesn't discuss how to detect such cases or whether the algorithm could be modified to handle them. This is a practical limitation that deserves more attention.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand on High-Dimensional Strategy**: Provide a more concrete discussion of how to handle high-dimensional problems, perhaps by connecting to recent work on dimensionality reduction for Bayesian optimization (e.g., REMBO [14]) and discussing the trade-offs between dimensionality reduction and regret bounds.

2. **Clarify Practical Benefits of ρt**: Include additional experiments or analysis demonstrating how the infinite-metric exploration loss (ρt) specifically improves performance in practical scenarios, not just theoretically.

3. **Justify Kernel Choice**: Discuss why the isotropic Matern kernel with ν=5/2 was selected, and consider including experiments with alternative kernels to demonstrate robustness.

4. **Address the GP Prior Misleading Case**: Provide insights on how to detect when the GP prior is misleading (as in the Rosenbrock2 case) and potential strategies to handle such scenarios.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization by solving a well-known open problem. The exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling is a significant improvement over previous GP-based methods. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the paper provides valuable insights into the relationship between GP priors and unknown bounds.

However, the limited discussion of high-dimensional scalability and insufficient justification of key design choices (particularly regarding ρt and kernel selection) prevent this from being a truly outstanding contribution. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these limitations.

The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning optimization with both theoretical and practical significance. The quality is high, the clarity is good, the originality is strong, and the significance is substantial.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution with practical implications for Bayesian optimization. With minor revisions addressing the high-dimensional scalability discussion and additional justification for key design choices, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
