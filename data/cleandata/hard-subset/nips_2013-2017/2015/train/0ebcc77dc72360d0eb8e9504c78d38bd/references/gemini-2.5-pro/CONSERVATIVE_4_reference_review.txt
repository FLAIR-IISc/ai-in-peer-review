**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for black-box functions using a Bayesian optimization framework. The authors aim to develop a Gaussian Process (GP)-based algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without relying on impractical assumptions like the δ-cover sampling procedure, a known open problem in the field. The proposed algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), combines a hierarchical partitioning scheme, similar to the SOO algorithm, with GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCBs). The core idea is to avoid committing to a single, potentially loose bound provided by the GP. Instead, the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite set of possible smoothness assumptions (or semi-metrics) and uses the GP-UCB to prune the search space, effectively balancing global and local search. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound, an improvement over the polynomial bounds of prior art like BaMSOO. Experimental results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that IMGPO generally outperforms established methods like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based optimization with EI and PI acquisition functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical result. It successfully addresses a recognized open problem by providing the first GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate that does not require δ-cover sampling. Theorem 1 formally establishes this bound, representing a significant advance over the polynomial rates of closely related methods like BaMSOO.

2.  **Problem Significance:** The goal of developing theoretically sound and practically efficient Bayesian optimization algorithms is of high importance to the machine learning community. Improving convergence guarantees from polynomial to exponential is a meaningful contribution.

3.  **Novel Combination of Ideas:** The paper proposes an interesting synthesis of two major lines of work in global optimization: the partitioning-based approach of DIRECT and SOO, which does not require an explicit smoothness constant, and the probabilistic modeling approach of GP-based Bayesian optimization. Using the GP-UCB not as the sole driver of exploration but as a mechanism to prune candidates generated by the partitioning scheme is a novel algorithmic concept.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity:** The proposed IMGPO algorithm is exceedingly complex. The description in Section 3.2 and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 are dense and difficult to follow. The procedure involves multiple filtering steps (i-iii), nested loops, dynamic updates to a parameter Ξ, and management of "GP-based" dummy function values. This complexity is a significant drawback compared to the conceptual elegance of standard GP-UCB or the relative simplicity of SOO and BaMSOO. It is not clear from the text that this intricate machinery is fully justified or if a simpler heuristic might achieve similar results. The reliance on many interacting parts makes the algorithm's behavior difficult to reason about and raises concerns about its robustness and ease of implementation.

2.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation, while showing favorable results for IMGPO, has some weaknesses that undermine the conclusions.
    *   The paper states that for the GP-PI and GP-EI baselines, the auxiliary optimization problem (i.e., maximizing the acquisition function) was solved using "the SOO algorithm and a local optimization method using gradients." This is a highly unconventional and likely suboptimal choice. Standard practice is to use a powerful global optimizer like DIRECT or CMA-ES for this subproblem, as the acquisition function is itself non-convex. By using a potentially weak optimizer for the baselines, their performance may have been artificially handicapped, thereby inflating the relative performance of IMGPO, which does not require this step.
    *   The paper claims an advantage in not requiring hand-picked hyperparameters. However, all GP-based methods tested, including IMGPO, used the same Matern kernel and an empirical Bayesian method for hyperparameter tuning. This is standard and good practice, but it is not a unique advantage of IMGPO. The observation that SOO performs better on Rosenbrock2 due to a "misleading" GP prior is telling; it confirms that IMGPO is just as susceptible to model mismatch as any other GP-based method, a point that is not sufficiently emphasized.

3.  **Clarity and Framing:** The central concept of "Infinite-Metric Optimization" is somewhat opaque. The algorithm does not literally optimize over an infinite space of metrics. Rather, it uses a specific, heuristic filtering procedure based on function values at different scales of the partition. While motivated by the idea of being robust to an unknown semi-metric, the name and explanation might overstate the conceptual novelty. The paper would be clearer if it were framed as a novel and sophisticated cell selection criterion for partitioning-based optimization that leverages a GP surrogate.

4.  **Practicality vs. Performance Gain:** While IMGPO shows improved performance on the selected benchmarks, the improvement is not always dramatic when compared to BaMSOO or SOO. Given the substantial increase in algorithmic complexity, a practitioner might reasonably prefer a simpler, more established, and better-understood algorithm unless the performance gains are consistently an order of magnitude better, which does not appear to be the case here. The runtime analysis is also incomplete; a direct comparison with BaMSOO is missing from Table 1.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a strong theoretical result on an important problem. However, the proposed method is highly complex, and its empirical validation against established baselines is not entirely convincing due to questionable experimental choices. The practical value of such a complex algorithm is not clearly demonstrated, as the performance gains over simpler, related methods like BaMSOO appear incremental rather than transformative.

While the theoretical contribution is noteworthy, the lack of clarity in the algorithm's motivation and the concerns about the experimental methodology prevent a confident endorsement. I am skeptical of endorsing a new, highly complex algorithm when its superiority over established methods has not been demonstrated with unimpeachable rigor.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Solves a known open problem with a novel theoretical result (exponential convergence).
*   The core idea of combining partitioning schemes with GP-based pruning is interesting.
*   Shows strong performance on a range of benchmark functions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The algorithm is overly complex and its design choices are not well-justified.
*   The experimental comparison is potentially flawed due to the use of a weak optimizer for the baselines.
*   The practical gains over simpler, established methods are not compelling enough to justify the added complexity.

**Final Decision:** Weak Reject. I would be willing to reconsider if the authors can provide a more convincing experimental comparison against properly optimized baselines and a much clearer, more intuitive justification for their algorithm's intricate design.
