**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for deterministic black-box functions using Bayesian Optimization (BO). The main goal is to develop a Gaussian Process (GP)-based algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate of the simple regret, a long-standing challenge in the field. Previous work either achieved this rate with impractical assumptions like δ-cover sampling (de Freitas et al., 2012) or achieved a weaker polynomial rate without such assumptions (Wang et al., 2014; BaMSOO).

The authors propose a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first to provably achieve an exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling or requiring an auxiliary optimization step to maximize an acquisition function. The core idea is a synthesis of two major approaches to global optimization: the GP-UCB framework, which relies on a probabilistic upper bound, and the hierarchical partitioning scheme of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO, which implicitly search over a family of possible function smoothnesses (or semi-metrics). IMGPO uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space and selects cells for expansion through a novel filtering process that leverages both the observed function values (as in SOO) and the GP-UCB (to prune unpromising regions). The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis culminating in an exponential regret bound and supports its claims with experiments comparing IMGPO to several strong baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-BO with EI/PI acquisition functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization. Achieving an exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based algorithm is a significant theoretical advancement. The proposed method is highly original in its elegant unification of the GP-UCB framework with the "infinite-metric" philosophy of SOO/DIRECT. This conceptual contribution, which shows how to use a (potentially loose) probabilistic bound to accelerate a search that makes no explicit smoothness assumptions, is a key strength.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The theoretical analysis is a major highlight. Theorem 1 provides the main result of an exponential convergence rate, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`. The analysis is made more insightful by the introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (`ρ_t`), which formalizes the cost of exploring under unknown smoothness and clearly shows how the GP component helps by reducing this loss (Remark 1 and 2). This framework provides a clear and valuable perspective on why combining these two approaches is beneficial.

3.  **Clarity of Motivation and High-Level Idea:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem and situating the work within the existing literature. Section 3.1 clearly explains the core intuition: instead of trusting a single, potentially loose GP bound, the algorithm simultaneously considers a vast space of possible bounds (via the unknown semi-metric `l`) and uses the GP only to prune candidates from this space. Figure 1 is also very effective at providing a visual intuition for the algorithm's behavior.

4.  **Good Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing IMGPO against relevant and strong baselines on a range of benchmark problems with varying dimensionalities (1D to 1000D). The results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of IMGPO in terms of sample efficiency. The authors are also honest about failure modes, such as the Rosenbrock2 case where a poor GP prior hurts performance, which strengthens the credibility of the evaluation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm's Technical Details:** While the high-level idea is clear, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to follow.
    *   The flow of control through the three separate loops over depth `h` (lines 8, 19, 26) is complex. It would be much clearer if these were either combined or abstracted into sub-functions with descriptive names (e.g., `SelectPromisingNodes`, `PruneWithGPUCB`, `ExpandNodes`).
    *   The procedure in lines 21-23 is critical but its computational cost is not well analyzed. It requires computing the maximum UCB over a set of points `T'` from a "fully expanded tree" up to a depth `ξ`. This seems like a potentially expensive local search that replaces the global auxiliary optimization of standard GP-UCB. The claim of having "no need for auxiliary optimization" should be nuanced; the algorithm replaces a generic global optimization with a structured, but still potentially costly, local one. A discussion of the computational complexity of this step would be valuable.
    *   The adaptive update rule for `Ξ` (line 38) is heuristic. While it seems reasonable, its introduction and role could be better explained in the main text, especially its relationship to `Ξn` in the theoretical bound.

2.  **Discussion of Scalability:** The paper rightly notes that `λ` can approach 1 as the dimension `D` increases, limiting scalability. However, the dependence on `D` in the bound is quite severe (via the constant `C` in the exponent), a common issue for hierarchical partitioning methods. It would be beneficial to be more explicit about this limitation and discuss the practical range of dimensionality where IMGPO is expected to be effective on its own (without embeddings like REMBO).

3.  **Experimental Details:**
    *   While the use of a fixed, non-ideal kernel is a good step towards a realistic evaluation, the performance of the baselines (GP-PI/EI) is highly dependent on the quality of the auxiliary optimizer. The paper states it used SOO and a local gradient method. A brief justification of why this constitutes a strong and fair baseline optimizer would strengthen the comparison.
    *   The paper's discussion about the parameter `Ξmax` is brief. While some functions did not hit the limit, for others the performance could be improved "at the cost of extra CPU time." It would be helpful to include a small sensitivity analysis on one of the latter functions (e.g., Levy or Ackley) to show the trade-off between performance and computation time for different `Ξmax` values.

**Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. The core idea is clever, the theoretical results are strong, and the experiments are convincing. The main weaknesses relate to the clarity of the algorithm's presentation and a need for a more detailed discussion of its computational complexity and limitations. These are largely presentational issues that can be addressed in a final version. The paper's strengths, particularly its success in solving a well-known open problem, far outweigh its weaknesses. It will be of great interest to both the Bayesian optimization community and the broader global optimization community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
