- Proposes IMGPO, a GP-based global optimization algorithm that attains exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling by considering infinitely many semi-metric bounds.  
- Provides a formal exponential regret bound (rN = O(λ^{N+Ngp})) and theoretical analysis accounting trade-offs between GP-based and unknown semi-metric bounds.  
- Experimental evaluation on 8 objective functions (dimensions 1–1000) shows IMGPO outperforming SOO, BaMSOO, GP-UCB, GP-PI, and GP-EI on most benchmarks.  
- Theoretical contribution is rigorous and introduces the infinite-metric exploration loss concept with clear remarks.  
- Novelty: the core idea of using infinitely many possible bounds is a significant conceptual advance over prior GP-based methods.  
- The approach differs from BaMSOO and previous work by achieving exponential rather than polynomial regret without impractical sampling.  
- Experimental scope is limited to 8 functions and may not represent broader real-world Bayesian optimization problems.  
- Assumes the search domain Ω is a hyperrectangle, limiting applicability to complex-shaped domains.  
- Computational cost and time complexity are not clearly analyzed, particularly the runtime dependence on parameter Ξmax.  
- Missing comparisons with more recent Bayesian optimization frameworks (e.g., BoTorch and other modern acquisition strategies).  
- Paper is well-organized with clear sections, reproducible pseudocode, and well-explained theory.  
- Description of Algorithm 1 is harder to follow without Figure 1; some terms (e.g., semi-metric) lack sufficient intuition or examples.  
- Relationship between the theoretical analysis and practical implementation could be made more explicit.  
- Relation to DIRECT and SOO is not fully clarified, and comparisons to recent related methods are lacking.  
- Significance: exponential convergence is a major theoretical advance with broader implications for combining GP and bound-based search.  
- Limitation: scalability to high-dimensional problems remains a significant practical concern.  
- Recommendation: accept—the paper makes a strong, well-supported theoretical contribution with promising empirical results despite acknowledged limitations.  
- Suggested improvements: broaden experimental benchmarks (more high-D problems and modern baselines), analyze runtime/Ξmax trade-offs, give intuitive examples of semi-metrics, and address non-hyperrectangular domains and integration into BO pipelines.
