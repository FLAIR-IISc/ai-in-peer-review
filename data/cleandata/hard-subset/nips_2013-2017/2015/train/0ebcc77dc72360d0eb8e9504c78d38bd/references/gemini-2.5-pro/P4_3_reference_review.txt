### Summary

This paper presents a novel algorithm for the global optimization of deterministic black-box functions, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO). The goal is to develop a Gaussian Process (GP)-based optimization method that achieves an exponential rate of convergence for the simple regret, without resorting to computationally expensive procedures like δ-cover sampling. This addresses an open problem identified in prior work (e.g., Wang et al., 2017).

The key idea is to combine a hierarchical partitioning scheme, similar to that used in the SOO algorithm (Munos, 2011), with GP-based upper confidence bounds (GP-UCB). The algorithm does not solely rely on the bound provided by the GP model. Instead, it operates under the assumption that an unknown, potentially tighter bound exists, characterized by a semi-metric `l` describing the function's continuity around a global optimizer. IMGPO implicitly considers an infinite family of such bounds by simultaneously exploring regions at different scales (depths in the partition tree). The GP-UCB bound is then used as a mechanism to prune branches of the search tree that are provably suboptimal, thereby accelerating the search. The algorithm proceeds by iteratively selecting and partitioning hyperrectangles based on a set of criteria that balance exploration (guided by GP uncertainty) and exploitation (guided by observed function values). Theoretically, the authors prove that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of related practical algorithms like BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2017).

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by presenting the first practical GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. This directly addresses a well-defined open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature. The conceptual framework, which combines the "infinite-metric" philosophy of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO with the probabilistic modeling of GPs, is novel and insightful. This synthesis of two distinct lines of research in global optimization is a primary strength. The analysis in Remarks 1-3, which explains how a non-tight GP bound can still be useful and why not committing to a single bound can be advantageous, provides valuable theoretical perspective.

2.  **Technical Quality (Theory):** The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, is strong and well-supported by the analysis provided in the supplementary material. It establishes an exponential convergence rate, improving upon the state-of-the-art polynomial bounds for practical GP-based algorithms. The analysis clearly articulates the dependence of the convergence rate on properties of the function (through `L`, `α`, `p`) and the dimensionality `D`, even though these properties are unknown to the algorithm.

3.  **Technical Quality (Experiments):** The empirical evaluation is conducted on a diverse set of benchmark functions, with dimensions ranging from 1 to 1000. IMGPO is compared against relevant and strong baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI. The results generally show superior performance for IMGPO, demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO showcases a potential path for scaling the algorithm. Furthermore, the authors are transparent about a failure case (Rosenbrock2) and provide a plausible explanation related to a misleading GP prior, which adds credibility to the evaluation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm:** While the paper provides a high-level overview, a 1D illustration, and pseudocode, the mechanics of the algorithm remain difficult to parse. The interplay between the three nested for-loops in Algorithm 1, the role of variables like `υ_max`, and the precise conditions for selecting, keeping, and dividing intervals are complex. For example, the description of `T'h+ξ` (line 21) as "the set of all center points of a fully expanded tree" is dense and could benefit from further clarification. The update rule for the parameter `Ξ` (line 38) feels somewhat heuristic and its motivation is not fully explained. Improved exposition in this section would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Scalability:** The theoretical analysis correctly identifies that the convergence rate can degrade as the input dimension `D` increases (the base of the exponent, `λ`, approaches 1). This is a known limitation of methods based on hierarchical space partitioning. While the paper acknowledges this challenge, the experimental section does not thoroughly investigate it. The 1000-dimensional experiment uses REMBO, which primarily tests the algorithm's effectiveness on a low-dimensional random embedding rather than its intrinsic scalability. Experiments on benchmark functions with intermediate dimensions (e.g., D=10, 20) would be valuable for understanding the practical limits of the proposed method.

3.  **Experimental Details:** The comparison to baselines could be strengthened. The performance of SOO and BaMSOO depends on the parameter `h_max`, which was set to `sqrt(n)`. While this is a theoretically justified choice, its practical optimality is not guaranteed. Similarly, the performance of GP-PI and GP-EI is highly sensitive to the auxiliary optimization routine used. A more detailed discussion of these design choices and their potential impact on the performance of the baseline algorithms would provide a more complete picture.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper solves a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   The proposed method is novel, combining two major paradigms in global optimization.
*   The work is supported by strong theoretical guarantees (exponential convergence), a substantial improvement over prior art.
*   Empirical results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on a variety of benchmarks.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   The description of the algorithm is complex and lacks clarity, potentially hindering understanding and reproducibility.
*   The issue of scalability with dimensionality, a key challenge for this class of algorithms, is not sufficiently addressed in the experiments.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a high-quality and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. Its primary strengths are the novel synthesis of ideas, the strong theoretical result of exponential convergence for a practical algorithm, and compelling empirical performance. The weaknesses, primarily related to the clarity of the algorithm's description and a limited investigation of scalability, are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. The paper successfully advances the state of the art and is likely to be of high interest to researchers and practitioners in machine learning and global optimization.
