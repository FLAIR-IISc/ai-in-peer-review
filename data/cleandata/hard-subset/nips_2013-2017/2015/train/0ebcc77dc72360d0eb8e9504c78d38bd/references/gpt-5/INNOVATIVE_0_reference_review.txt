Summary
The paper tackles deterministic global optimization of black-box functions with Gaussian process (GP) Bayesian optimization. It targets a key open question raised by Wang et al. (BaMSOO): can we achieve exponential simple-regret decay without the impractical δ-cover sampling? The authors propose Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a hierarchical partitioning algorithm that blends two sources of optimism: (i) a GP-derived UCB bound, and (ii) an implicit, unknown Hölder-type semi-metric around the global optimum. Rather than committing to a single Lipschitz/Hölder bound, IMGPO “hedges” over infinitely many potential bounds and uses the GP to prune candidates. The algorithm avoids auxiliary inner optimization (unlike GP-UCB), leverages dummy GP-based center values to skip evaluations until needed, and adaptively updates GP hyperparameters.

The core theoretical claim is an exponential convergence rate O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ<1 for simple regret, where N is the number of true function evaluations and Ngp counts placements of GP-based “dummy” centers. The bound depends on an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρ̄t that measures the cost of hedging over many possible bounds; GP pruning is argued to keep ρ̄t small in practice. The analysis assumes an unknown Hölder semi-metric near the optimum (no known constants). Experiments compare IMGPO with SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI across several multimodal benchmarks and a 1000-D REMBO setting, showing generally superior performance and reasonable runtime.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: The “infinite-metric” idea—explicitly refusing to commit to a single smoothness bound and using GP optimism to prune—is a fresh and compelling synthesis of DIRECT/SOO-style partitioning and GP acquisition logic. It offers a principled explanation of when non-tight bounds can still help (via ρ̄t), and why hedging may dominate committing to a possibly wrong bound.
- Theoretical advance: An exponential simple-regret rate without δ-cover is, to my knowledge, new in GP-based BO for deterministic functions. The bound is stated for the best (unknown) Hölder constants, which aligns with the algorithm’s philosophy of not requiring them. Remarks dissect how GP helps via Ngp and ρ̄t, and when hedging beats a fixed-bound approach.
- Practicality: No auxiliary nonconvex acquisition optimization; hierarchical partitioning with selective evaluation is attractive for expensive-objective regimes. The use of common kernels with blind initialization and empirical Bayes updates matches realistic practice better than tuned hyperparameters per function.
- Empirical results: Across diverse functions and a high-dimensional REMBO setup, IMGPO typically outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI. The runtime comparison suggests IMGPO is far faster than classic GP-BO methods and only somewhat slower than SOO.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Confidence guarantee for UCB: The algorithm and proof sketch hinge on “with probability ≥1−η, f(x) ≤ μ+ςσ for all queried x,” but the paper does not state assumptions (e.g., f sampled from the GP prior or belongs to an RKHS with bounded norm and suitable βt as in Srinivas et al., 2010). In experiments (Rosenbrock2), the authors note the UCB property “did not hold many times,” which undermines the stated high-probability guarantee. Please make explicit the function class and conditions under which the UCB property holds and the theorem is valid; otherwise, the claimed probability bound is not justified.
- Exponential rate in N+Ngp: Counting Ngp (instances where evaluation is skipped and a GP UCB is inserted) in the exponent may confuse readers. While Ngp affects pruning, the main practical resource is the number of expensive evaluations N. It would strengthen the contribution to also state a bound purely in terms of N (perhaps with looser constants), or to characterize Ngp/N under mild conditions.
- Notational/typographical issues: λ is written as “λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t < 1,” which appears dimensionally off; presumably λ = 3^{−α/(2CDρ̄t)} or similar. There are other minor typos (e.g., Ξ← Ξ + 22?). The notation for n, N, Ngp, t is occasionally confusing. Cleaning this up will help reproducibility and readability.
- Computational complexity: Step (iii) can require many GP predictions over a fully expanded subtree up to depth ξ; although Ξmax caps this, a more explicit worst-case and typical-case complexity analysis (both in number of GP predictions and matrix ops per update) would be valuable.
- Baselines and fairness: GP-EI/PI are optimized using SOO plus a local gradient method, which is nonstandard and may be suboptimal relative to multi-start or global optimizers used in prior work. BaMSOO and SOO are run with hmax = √n per theory; practical tuning may yield better baselines. Please discuss sensitivity to these choices or add ablations.
- Noise robustness: The setting is deterministic; many real BO problems are noisy. Even if the theory targets deterministic problems, a small empirical section on moderate noise would strengthen the practical relevance.
- High-dimensional behavior: The authors acknowledge λ→1 as D grows. The REMBO experiment is promising; additional results or discussion on combining IMGPO with additive/low-dimensional structure methods would help.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a genuinely new and insightful direction (infinite-metric hedging plus GP pruning) that could influence both BO and broader optimistic search.
- Claims and analysis push the frontier on exponential regret without δ-cover.
- Practical algorithm with no auxiliary optimization; code available; strong empirical performance.

Cons for acceptance
- Key probability assumption for GP-UCB-style bounds is not stated and appears violated in experiments, casting doubt on the theorem’s stated probability guarantee.
- Some theoretical and notational rough edges (typos, unclear λ, complexity).
- Baseline choices and optimization of EI/PI may not be fully competitive.

Questions for authors
- Under what precise assumptions on f and κ does the UCB validity hold throughout the run? Can you align with RKHS-based βt conditions and update the proof accordingly?
- Can you provide a bound purely in terms of N, or characterize Ngp/N under mild conditions?
- What is the empirical sensitivity to Ξmax and η? Any guidance for users?
- Can you report runtime broken down into GP prediction time vs. evaluation time, and the typical ξ used?

Recommendation
Accept (7). Despite some theoretical gaps and experimental choices that could be strengthened, the paper offers a bold, novel idea with a compelling analysis framework and strong practical results. With clarifications on the UCB assumption and cleanup of notation, this work will be impactful. Confidence: 3/5.
