This paper proposes a new Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, termed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work [1]. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric alongside the GP’s uncertainty estimates to guide exploration, effectively considering a broader range of potential bounds on the objective function.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the motivation, algorithm, and analysis. The connection to existing bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*) is insightful, and the authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, provides a regret bound demonstrating exponential convergence. The experimental validation, while limited, shows promising results across a range of benchmark functions.

However, despite these strengths, I have significant reservations about the novelty and overall contribution of this work. My primary concern revolves around the lack of a truly compelling demonstration that IMGPO significantly outperforms established baselines in a practical setting. While the experiments *show* improved performance, the reasons aren’t entirely convincing. The authors acknowledge the performance of SOO on Rosenbrock2 when the GP prior is uninformative, highlighting a weakness in relying heavily on the GP’s output. Furthermore, the experimental setup appears to avoid a fair comparison.  The baselines (GP-PI, GP-EI) are run with a fixed, relatively unoptimized kernel and hyperparameters, while IMGPO benefits from parameter updates after each iteration. This creates an uneven playing field.  The claim that this setup reflects a “realistic” scenario (where good kernel/hyperparameter knowledge is unavailable) feels somewhat contrived, as proper hyperparameter tuning is standard practice in the field.

The theoretical contribution, while mathematically sound, feels incremental. The improvement over existing work hinges on the assumption of an unknown, tighter semi-metric. While this assumption is reasonable, the practical benefit isn't definitively demonstrated. The analysis relies on parameters (L, α, p) characterizing this metric, yet there’s little discussion on how these influence the algorithm’s performance or how they might be estimated in practice. The paper spends significant effort explaining why considering “all possible bounds” is beneficial, but this feels more like a philosophical justification than a concrete algorithmic advantage.  The effect of Ξmax, a key algorithmic parameter, is also only briefly discussed.

The introduction adequately summarizes related work, but it would be beneficial to more directly contrast IMGPO with other recent advancements in Bayesian optimization, such as those incorporating deep learning or multi-fidelity optimization.  The discussion of the limitations (scalability to high dimensions) feels rather brief and could be expanded.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to Bayesian optimization incorporating an unknown semi-metric.
*   Theoretical guarantee of exponential convergence.
*   Promising experimental results on benchmark functions.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental theoretical contribution.
*   Unconvincing demonstration of significant practical improvement over established baselines.
*   Unfair experimental setup with suboptimal hyperparameter settings for baselines.
*   Limited discussion on the estimation and impact of parameters characterizing the unknown semi-metric.
*   Scalability concerns not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound algorithm and analysis, it lacks a compelling demonstration of its practical significance. The improvements over existing methods appear marginal, and the experimental validation is not sufficiently rigorous to justify acceptance into NIPS. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration of a potentially interesting idea than a significant advancement in the field of Bayesian optimization. I encourage the authors to strengthen their experimental evaluation, address the concerns regarding the baseline comparison, and provide a more compelling justification for the practical benefits of their approach.
