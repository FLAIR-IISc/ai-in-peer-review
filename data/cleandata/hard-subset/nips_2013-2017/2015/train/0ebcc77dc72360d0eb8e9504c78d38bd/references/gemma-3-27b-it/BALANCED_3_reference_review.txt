This paper proposes a novel Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work (de Freitas et al., 2017). The core idea is to consider an infinite number of possible bounds, leveraging the GP prior to prune unlikely candidates, and perform both global and local searches simultaneously. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence and empirical results showcasing IMGPO's performance on several benchmark functions.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation of prior theoretical results in Bayesian optimization. The problem of achieving exponential convergence *without* δ-cover sampling is clearly stated and is a valuable contribution. The proposed approach, IMGPO, is conceptually interesting, blending ideas from bound-based search methods and GP optimization. The theoretical analysis, while dense, appears rigorous and demonstrates a clear understanding of the convergence properties. The experimental validation on a diverse set of functions supports the claims, and the comparison with several baseline algorithms is fair. The inclusion of a publicly available code repository is a strong positive.  The attempt to connect the analysis to practical considerations, such as the impact of a “tight” GP bound, is also commendable.

However, the paper also suffers from some weaknesses. The description of the algorithm itself (Section 3.2) is quite dense and difficult to follow. Figure 1, while attempting to illustrate the process, is rather complex and requires considerable effort to decipher.  More illustrative examples, perhaps with lower dimensionality, would be beneficial. The connection between the “infinite-metric” concept and the actual implementation is not entirely clear; the algorithm still employs a hierarchical partitioning scheme, which feels somewhat conventional and doesn’t immediately highlight the ‘infinite’ aspect. While the authors acknowledge scalability concerns, the discussion feels somewhat limited. The impact of the various parameters (Ξmax, η) on performance could be explored more thoroughly. The discussion of related work, while present, feels a little cursory. For instance, the connection to multi-armed bandit algorithms with optimism in the face of uncertainty, beyond a passing mention, could be elaborated. The experimental section would benefit from statistical significance tests to clearly demonstrate the improvements.  Finally, the experimental setup assumes a known kernel and hyperparameter initialization, which is a significant simplification. A sensitivity analysis on these choices would strengthen the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed algorithm presents a novel approach to Bayesian optimization, addressing a known limitation in the field.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with a provable exponential convergence rate.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO on a range of benchmark functions.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  The algorithm avoids the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure.
*   **Open Source:** The availability of the source code encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The algorithmic description is dense and difficult to follow.
*   **Scalability:** The potential scalability issues, particularly in high dimensions, are not fully addressed.
*   **Experimental Detail:** The experimental section lacks statistical significance tests and a more thorough sensitivity analysis.
*   **Simplifications in Experiments:** The assumption of known kernel and hyperparameters is a significant simplification.
* **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of relevant work in bandit algorithms and related optimization techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are strong, and the empirical validation is encouraging. However, the clarity of the algorithmic description and the lack of a thorough scalability analysis are significant concerns.  While the paper is not *quite* at the level of a strong accept, it has the potential to be after addressing these concerns.  

**Recommendation:** Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the algorithmic description, providing a more detailed analysis of scalability, and strengthening the experimental section with statistical significance tests and a sensitivity analysis. A more extensive discussion of related work would also be beneficial.
