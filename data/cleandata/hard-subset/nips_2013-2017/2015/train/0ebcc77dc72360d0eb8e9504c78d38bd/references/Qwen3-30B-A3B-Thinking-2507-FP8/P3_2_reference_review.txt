

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that aims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors claim to be the first to achieve exponential convergence (regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1) for a GP-based method without the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure, which was required in previous work by de Freitas et al. [1].

The core idea is to consider infinitely many possible upper bounds (rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior) and use hierarchical partitioning to explore these bounds. The authors introduce the concept of "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρ_t) to measure the cost of not committing to a particular upper bound. Their theoretical analysis shows that by considering all possible bounds (rather than a single bound), the algorithm can achieve exponential convergence even when the GP's UCB bound is not tight.

The paper compares IMGPO with several baselines including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on several benchmark functions. The experimental results show that IMGPO generally outperforms the baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing an exponential regret bound for a GP-based method without δ-cover sampling. The introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept provides a novel analytical framework for understanding the trade-offs between using a single bound versus multiple bounds.

2. **Novel Perspective**: The authors present a compelling argument that not relying on a single (potentially loose) bound can be advantageous. Their analysis in Remarks 1-3 provides valuable theoretical insights into why this approach can be beneficial, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization.

3. **Clear Experimental Comparison**: The experimental section provides a comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines, and the use of a single kernel (isotropic Matern with ν=5/2) and fixed hyperparameters across all functions is a realistic setting that reflects practical Bayesian optimization scenarios.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The algorithm is well-motivated and the pseudocode is provided, making it possible for others to implement and reproduce the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Soundness Concerns**: The key theoretical claim (exponential convergence) depends critically on the value of λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄_t) < 1. However, the paper does not provide sufficient conditions to ensure λ < 1. When ρ̄_t is small (meaning few intervals are divided per iteration), λ could be greater than 1, which would invalidate the exponential convergence claim. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of how ρ̄_t behaves as a function of N, which is crucial for establishing the exponential bound.

2. **Ambiguous Regret Bound**: The regret bound in Theorem 1 is O(λ^(N+Ngp)), but the value of λ depends on ρ̄_t, which is defined as the maximum of averages of ρ_1:t' for t' = 1, 2, ..., t. This is an unusual definition and makes it difficult to interpret the bound. The paper does not clarify how ρ̄_t scales with N in practice, which is essential for understanding the practical convergence rate.

3. **Experimental Limitations**: While the paper claims IMGPO is "much faster" than traditional GP optimization methods, it doesn't provide concrete runtime comparisons. The paper also doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the experimental results (e.g., p-values for the performance differences).

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper uses Ξ_max = 22 as a fixed value, but doesn't adequately discuss how this parameter affects performance or how it should be chosen in practice. The experimental results show that increasing Ξ_max can improve performance for some functions (Table 1), but the paper doesn't provide guidance for selecting this parameter.

5. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper acknowledges in Remark 1 that λ can get close to one as dimensionality D increases, suggesting scalability challenges. However, the paper doesn't provide any concrete evidence of how the algorithm's performance degrades with increasing dimensionality beyond the 1000-dimensional test case (which uses REMBO as a subroutine).

6. **Comparison with Baselines**: While the paper cites BaMSOO as the closest related work, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the regret bounds. The paper claims BaMSOO achieves polynomial regret while IMGPO achieves exponential, but doesn't explicitly state the exact form of BaMSOO's regret bound for comparison.

## Detailed Technical Critique

### Theoretical Analysis (Section 4)

The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which claims an exponential regret bound. However, there are several concerns:

1. The condition λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄_t) < 1 is problematic. If ρ̄_t is small (e.g., ρ̄_t = 1), then λ = 3 - α/(2CD) which could be greater than 1 for small D (e.g., D=1, α=1, C=1). The paper doesn't provide conditions under which λ < 1 is guaranteed.

2. The constant C is defined as "a constant in N" but not specified. The paper references previous work for this constant, but it's not clear how it relates to the specific problem. For example, in the case of SOO [18], C is related to the branching factor of the search tree.

3. The regret bound depends on the unknown parameters L, α, and p. While the paper claims the bound holds for the best values of these parameters, it doesn't explain how these parameters would be estimated in practice.

### Algorithm Description (Section 3)

The algorithm description is somewhat confusing in places. For example:

- The meaning of "ξ" in the algorithm (line 21) and its relationship to "T'_h+ξ" (line 21) is not clearly explained.
- The purpose of the "GP-based" label (lines 13-17) and how it affects the algorithm's behavior isn't fully clear.
- The description of step (v) in Section 3.2 ("For each new interval, if the UCB of the evaluation point is less than the best function value found so far, skip the evaluation...") seems to contradict the pseudocode, where lines 33-35 show that evaluation occurs when UCB ≥ f+.

### Experimental Section (Section 5)

The experimental results are generally convincing, but there are some issues:

1. The paper claims IMGPO is "much faster" than traditional GP optimization methods, but doesn't provide concrete runtime comparisons. It only states that IMGPO is slower than SOO but faster than GP-PI/GP-EI.

2. The use of Ξ_max = 22 as a fixed parameter is not justified. The paper states that "for Sin1, Sin2, Branin and Hartmann3, increasing Ξ_max does not affect IMGPO because Ξ_n did not reach Ξ_max = 22," but doesn't explain why Ξ_n didn't reach Ξ_max for these functions.

3. The experimental results for the 1000-dimensional function (which uses REMBO as a subroutine) are interesting but don't directly demonstrate the scalability of IMGPO itself.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization by providing a novel approach to achieving exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure. The concept of considering infinitely many possible bounds is novel and the analysis in Remarks 1-3 provides valuable insights.

However, the theoretical claim of exponential convergence is not fully supported due to the dependence of λ on ρ̄_t, which is not well-characterized. The paper lacks sufficient conditions to ensure λ < 1, which is crucial for the exponential convergence claim.

The experimental results are generally positive but could be strengthened with more detailed runtime comparisons and statistical significance tests.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

The paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of Bayesian optimization. The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds is novel and well-motivated. However, the theoretical analysis has some significant gaps that need to be addressed before the exponential convergence claim can be fully accepted.

The authors should:
1. Provide a more detailed analysis of how ρ̄_t scales with N to ensure λ < 1
2. Clarify the relationship between λ and the practical convergence rate
3. Provide more detailed experimental comparisons including runtime data
4. Discuss how to choose Ξ_max in practice

With these improvements, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the theoretical contribution is promising but not yet fully substantiated.

## Final Comments

The paper addresses an important open problem in Bayesian optimization (creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling). The authors' solution approach is novel and their theoretical analysis provides valuable insights. The experimental results support their theoretical claims, but the theoretical proof of exponential convergence requires more rigorous treatment. I believe this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field, but needs to address the theoretical concerns before it can be accepted as a major contribution to NIPS.
