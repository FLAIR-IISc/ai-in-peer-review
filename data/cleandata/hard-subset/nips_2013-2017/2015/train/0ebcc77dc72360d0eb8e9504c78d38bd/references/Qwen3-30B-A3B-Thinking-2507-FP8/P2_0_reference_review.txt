

# NIPS Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel GP-based global optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors address a key limitation in previous Bayesian optimization methods (such as GP-UCB) that either require a tight Lipschitz constant or rely on impractical sampling procedures. The core innovation is the idea of simultaneously considering "infinitely many possible bounds" (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior. The authors prove an exponential regret bound for their algorithm and demonstrate through experiments that it outperforms previous methods like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-UCB, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

The paper builds on previous work by de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], who identified the δ-cover sampling procedure as a bottleneck for achieving exponential convergence in GP-based methods. While BaMSOO [2] combined GP-UCB with SOO to achieve polynomial regret, the authors claim this is the first method to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported. The authors provide a clear regret bound (Theorem 1) showing exponential convergence: rN = O(λ^N+Ngp) with λ < 1.
- The analysis properly accounts for the trade-offs between the GP-based bound and the unknown semi-metric bound (see Remarks 1-3), which is a significant theoretical contribution.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive for the test functions used, with 8 different objective functions across dimensions 1-1000, and the authors provide meaningful comparisons against relevant baselines.
- The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., scalability for higher dimensions) and provide theoretical explanations for when their method might not be advantageous.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental section is limited to 8 test functions, which may not be sufficiently representative of the broader class of optimization problems where Bayesian optimization is applied.
- The paper assumes Ω is a hyperrectangle, which is a limitation for many practical applications where the search domain may have complex shapes.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the algorithm, particularly regarding the parameter Ξmax. While they note that increasing Ξmax improves performance at the cost of CPU time, they don't provide a clear analysis of the time complexity.
- The paper doesn't compare against more recent Bayesian optimization methods beyond GP-UCB, GP-EI, and GP-PI (e.g., recent methods like BoTorch, which have become standard in the field).

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation.
- The algorithm description (Section 3.2) is detailed with a clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1) that should be reproducible.
- The theoretical analysis is well-explained, with the authors taking care to define key concepts (e.g., infinite-metric exploration loss).

**Weaknesses:**
- The description of Algorithm 1 is somewhat difficult to follow without Figure 1 (which is referenced but not provided in the text). A more detailed explanation of the algorithm's operation would benefit readers.
- Some technical terms (e.g., "semi-metric" in Assumption 1) are used without sufficient intuition, which might make the paper challenging for readers unfamiliar with the concept.
- The relationship between the theoretical analysis and the practical implementation could be more explicitly connected.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of considering "infinitely many possible bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior is genuinely novel and represents a significant conceptual advance.
- The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work (e.g., BaMSOO only achieves polynomial regret), and the authors provide a thorough discussion of how their approach differs from related work.
- The theoretical framework they develop (see Remarks 1-3) provides a new perspective on why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The idea of considering multiple bounds is related to DIRECT [19] and SOO [18], but the paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how their approach differs from these methods in the context of GP-based optimization.
- The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison with more recent methods that might have addressed similar challenges.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The exponential convergence rate is a major theoretical advance in Bayesian optimization, addressing a key open problem identified by Wang et al. [2].
- The paper provides a new theoretical framework for understanding how to combine GP with bound-based search methods, which has broader implications beyond Bayesian optimization.
- The practical results demonstrate consistent improvement over previous methods on most test functions, with significant performance gains on several benchmark problems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The scalability limitations for high-dimensional problems (noted in Theorem 1) are a significant limitation for many real-world applications.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method could be integrated into existing Bayesian optimization pipelines or how it might be used in practice beyond the test cases presented.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-supported, and the experimental results provide strong evidence for the practical advantages of the proposed method. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding scalability for high dimensions and the scope of the experimental evaluation), these are acknowledged by the authors and represent important directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the paper.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the experimental evaluation** to include more challenging high-dimensional problems (beyond the 1000-dimensional Sin2 example) and compare with more recent Bayesian optimization methods (e.g., BoTorch, which has become standard in the field).

2. **Clarify the computational complexity** of the algorithm, particularly the relationship between Ξmax and runtime. A more detailed analysis of the time complexity would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

3. **Provide more intuition** for the "semi-metric" concept in Assumption 1. A simple example illustrating what this means in practice would help readers understand the theoretical framework.

4. **Address the generalization to non-hyperrectangular domains** more thoroughly in the main paper (rather than just mentioning it as a possibility in the supplementary material).

5. **Include a comparison with the most recent Bayesian optimization methods** (e.g., those that use more sophisticated acquisition functions or parallelization strategies) to better establish the state-of-the-art.

This is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization that makes a clear theoretical advance while providing practical benefits. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the literature.
