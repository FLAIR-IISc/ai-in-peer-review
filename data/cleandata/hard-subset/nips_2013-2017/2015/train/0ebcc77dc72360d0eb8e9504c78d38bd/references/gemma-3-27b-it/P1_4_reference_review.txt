## Peer Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) designed to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results (de Freitas et al., 2017). The core idea revolves around simultaneously exploring multiple potential bounds on the objective function, leveraging the GP prior to prune implausible candidates, and employing a hierarchical partitioning strategy for efficient exploration. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and empirical results showcasing IMGPO's performance on a variety of benchmark functions, outperforming several existing Bayesian optimization algorithms.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the work of de Freitas et al. (2017) which established exponential convergence for Bayesian optimization but at the cost of δ-cover sampling. Wang et al. (2018) addressed this limitation by combining GP-UCB with the SOO algorithm, but only achieved polynomial convergence. This paper aims to close the gap and achieve exponential convergence with a practical algorithm. It also connects to broader areas of bound-based search, including Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and PAC-MDP algorithms, highlighting the importance of tight bounds.  The work acknowledges and compares itself to related algorithms like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods (GP-PI and GP-EI).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving an exponential convergence rate. This is a significant result, addressing a key open problem identified in prior work (Wang et al., 2018).
* **Novelty of Approach:** The "infinite-metric optimization" concept – exploring multiple potential bounds simultaneously – is a clever and potentially powerful idea. The use of the GP to guide this exploration is well-motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that IMGPO consistently outperforms other algorithms on a diverse set of benchmark functions, including high-dimensional problems.  The setup, acknowledging the impracticality of hand-tuning kernels and hyperparameters, is commendable.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithm description is clear, and the connection to related concepts is well-explained.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper offers a thoughtful discussion of the algorithm’s behavior, limitations, and potential avenues for future work (e.g., scalability, impact of tightness of GP bounds). The remarks at the end of the analysis section are particularly insightful.
* **Open Source Code:** Providing publicly available code is excellent and promotes reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** While the experiments include a 1000-dimensional problem, the theoretical analysis indicates potential scalability issues as dimensionality increases (λ approaching 1).  The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth discussion of strategies to mitigate this (beyond referencing future work) would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The choice of Ξmax (maximum depth of the hierarchical partitioning) could be crucial for performance. The paper mentions adjusting this parameter in experiments but doesn't provide clear guidance on how to select an appropriate value in practice.
* **Complexity of Implementation:**  The algorithm, while clearly explained, appears relatively complex to implement compared to simpler Bayesian optimization methods. This could be a barrier to adoption.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI is helpful, it would be valuable to compare IMGPO against more recent and advanced Bayesian optimization techniques, such as those employing modern kernel learning or multi-fidelity optimization strategies.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of a semi-metric ` and its properties (Assumption 1 & 2) need to be carefully considered. While reasonable, the paper could discuss the implications if these assumptions are violated in practice.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization and provides a theoretically sound solution.
* The novel approach of "infinite-metric optimization" is a compelling and potentially impactful idea.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence of IMGPO's effectiveness.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a thorough analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Scalability remains a potential concern, particularly for very high-dimensional problems.
* The algorithm's complexity could hinder adoption.
* The comparison to the broader landscape of modern Bayesian optimization techniques is somewhat limited.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical validation is convincing. While the scalability concerns and algorithmic complexity are legitimate, they are outweighed by the novelty and potential impact of the work.

**Rating:** **Strong Accept**

**Recommendations:**

* The authors should expand the discussion on scalability and potential mitigation strategies.
*  Provide more practical guidance on selecting the parameter Ξmax.
* Consider including a comparison to more recent state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization algorithms.
* Briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to the assumption of a semi-metric.
