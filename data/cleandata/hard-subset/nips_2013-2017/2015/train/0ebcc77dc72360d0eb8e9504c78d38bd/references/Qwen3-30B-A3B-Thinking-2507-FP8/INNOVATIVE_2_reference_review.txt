

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian optimization that achieves exponential convergence rates without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors introduce "Infinite-Metric GP Optimization" (IMGPO), which fundamentally shifts the paradigm from relying on a single upper bound (as in GP-UCB) to simultaneously considering infinitely many possible bounds encoded by an unknown semi-metric. This approach resolves a long-standing open problem identified by Wang et al. [2], who noted that creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling remained unsolved.

The core innovation lies in the theoretical framework that leverages the existence of an unknown semi-metric bound while using the GP-estimated bound to prune irrelevant candidates. The authors prove an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which is a significant theoretical improvement over previous polynomial regret bounds (e.g., BaMSOO's Õ(n^(-2α/D(4-α)))). The analysis is rigorous, with Theorem 1 providing a concrete bound that accounts for the interplay between the unknown semi-metric and the GP's estimated bound. The authors also provide insightful theoretical analysis in Remarks 1-3, explaining how the use of GP and the infinite-metric exploration benefit the algorithm.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by bridging two previously divergent paths in global optimization: methods that rely on a single known bound (like GP-UCB) versus methods that consider multiple bounds (like DIRECT and SOO). The experimental results validate the theoretical claims, showing that IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on eight benchmark functions, including high-dimensional cases. The authors demonstrate that IMGPO is faster than traditional GP optimization methods while maintaining superior performance.

The paper's strengths are its bold conceptual shift, theoretical novelty, and strong experimental validation. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work and provide a compelling argument for why considering infinitely many bounds can be advantageous. The connection to the DIRECT algorithm [19] and SOO [18] is well-established, and the authors properly situate their work within the broader optimization literature.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, suggesting scalability limitations that aren't fully addressed. While the authors note this as a remaining challenge, they don't propose a concrete solution for high-dimensional cases beyond mentioning REMBO.
2. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is quite complex, and some aspects of the algorithm (particularly the role of Ξmax) could be clarified with additional explanation.
3. The supplementary material is referenced for the proof, which is acceptable but slightly diminishes the paper's completeness.

Despite these minor concerns, the paper's contribution is substantial and addresses a fundamental limitation in Bayesian optimization. The authors have successfully achieved what they set out to do: providing a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without the impractical δ-cover sampling. The theoretical framework they develop is not just a technical improvement but represents a conceptual shift in how we think about bound-based optimization.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It represents a bold approach that prioritizes a novel conceptual framework over incremental improvements, which aligns perfectly with NIPS's preference for novel and impactful work.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- First GP-based method achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Novel theoretical framework considering infinitely many possible bounds
- Strong theoretical analysis with exponential regret bound
- Clear experimental validation showing superior performance
- Insightful theoretical discussion in Remarks 1-3
- Well-situated within the broader optimization literature

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of scalability for high-dimensional problems
- Some algorithmic details could be clearer
- Supplementary material required for full proof

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by resolving a long-standing open problem with a novel theoretical approach that has both strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The conceptual shift toward considering infinitely many possible bounds represents a fundamental advance that could inspire new directions in global optimization and related fields.
