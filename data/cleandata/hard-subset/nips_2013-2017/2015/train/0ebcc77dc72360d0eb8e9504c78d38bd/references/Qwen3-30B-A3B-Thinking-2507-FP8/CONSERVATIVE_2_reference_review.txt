

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper proposes a new Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work. The authors argue that by simultaneously considering all possible bounds (via an "infinite-metric" approach) rather than relying on a single estimated bound, their method achieves better performance than existing approaches.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis with regret bounds (Theorem 1), which is valuable for the field. The experimental section compares the proposed method against several baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on eight test functions, and the authors acknowledge limitations like scalability issues with higher dimensions.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main weakness is the lack of sufficient evidence to support its strong claim of exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis relies on several strong assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) that lack practical justification. Assumption 1 posits the existence of an unknown semi-metric ` that bounds the function, but the authors provide no evidence that this assumption is reasonable for real-world optimization problems.

The experimental evaluation is insufficient to validate the claimed exponential convergence. The paper only tests on eight benchmark functions, which is a small number for claiming a general advantage. More critically, the experimental results don't clearly demonstrate the exponential convergence claimed in the theory. The plots in Figure 2 show better performance for some functions, but it's not clear if this represents exponential convergence or merely better practical performance.

The paper fails to adequately address key questions: Why would considering "infinitely many possible bounds" be beneficial in practice when a single tight bound would be sufficient? How does the algorithm handle cases where the GP-estimated bound is actually tight? The paper's analysis suggests that in such cases, the method would be no better than GP-UCB, but it doesn't provide evidence that the GP-estimated bound is typically not tight.

The authors claim to be the first GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling, but don't sufficiently distinguish themselves from BaMSOO [2], which is referenced as achieving a polynomial bound. The theoretical analysis doesn't clearly establish why their bound is better than BaMSOO's.

The algorithm appears significantly more complex than existing methods (with detailed pseudocode and multiple new parameters like Ξmax), but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this complexity is justified by substantially better performance. The experimental results show IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods," but it's unclear if this speedup is due to the novel approach or simply the absence of δ-cover sampling.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the lack of convincing evidence for the practical benefits of the proposed approach makes it difficult to endorse. The paper does not sufficiently validate its strong claims with rigorous empirical evidence. The theoretical contribution appears marginal compared to the existing literature, and the experimental evidence does not support the claims made in the paper.

**Summary of pros and cons:**
Pros: Clear theoretical analysis with regret bounds; comparison with relevant baselines; acknowledgment of limitations.

Cons: Theoretical analysis relies on unrealistic assumptions; insufficient experimental validation; lack of convincing evidence for exponential convergence claim; algorithm complexity not adequately justified; failure to adequately distinguish from related work.

As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and rigorous validation, I am skeptical of the paper's claims. The theoretical contribution is not sufficiently compelling given the lack of empirical evidence demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The paper would need substantially more evidence to support its strong claims before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
