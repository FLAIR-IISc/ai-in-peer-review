### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for deterministic black-box functions, a key challenge in machine learning and other scientific domains. The authors focus on Bayesian optimization using Gaussian Processes (GPs) and aim to develop an algorithm with an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret. The paper identifies a critical gap in the existing literature: prior work by de Freitas et al. [1] achieved an exponential rate but required an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure, while subsequent work by Wang et al. [2] (BaMSOO) removed this requirement but only achieved a polynomial rate.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first GP-based method to achieve a provable exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling or an auxiliary optimization subroutine. IMGPO cleverly hybridizes two major approaches to global optimization. It uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, similar to the SOO algorithm [18], which implicitly considers an infinite set of possible function smoothnesses (or semi-metrics). This is combined with a GP-UCB criterion, which is used not to guide the search directly (by maximizing an acquisition function) but to prune unpromising branches of the hierarchical search tree. This synthesis allows the algorithm to leverage the information from the GP posterior to accelerate convergence while retaining the robustness of methods that do not rely on a single, potentially misspecified, smoothness assumption. The authors provide a theoretical analysis culminating in an exponential regret bound and support their claims with experiments on several benchmark functions, demonstrating superior performance against relevant baselines like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important open problem in Bayesian optimization theory: achieving exponential regret without impractical assumptions. The proposed solution is novel and elegant, successfully merging two distinct lines of research—GP-based optimization and Lipschitz-style optimization without a known Lipschitz constant (like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]). This combination is non-trivial and provides a new perspective on how to effectively utilize GP surrogates in global optimization.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary result, Theorem 1, establishes an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) for IMGPO. This is a significant theoretical improvement over the polynomial bounds of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The analysis provided in the remarks is particularly insightful, offering a clear intuition on how the GP-based bound and the "infinite-metric" exploration interact. For instance, Remark 1 clarifies how a "useful" GP bound improves the convergence rate, while Remark 3 provides a compelling argument for why considering infinitely many bounds can be superior even when a single bound is known.

3.  **Well-Written and Clear Motivation:** The paper is clearly structured and well-written. The introduction effectively situates the work within the literature and motivates the problem. The core idea of the algorithm is explained intuitively in Section 3.1 before the technical details are presented, which greatly aids reader comprehension. The relationship to prior work is thoroughly discussed.

4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments compare IMGPO against strong and relevant baselines across a variety of test functions, including a high-dimensional problem using REMBO. The results generally show IMGPO outperforming its competitors, providing empirical validation for the theoretical claims. The practical setup, which avoids hand-picking kernels and hyperparameters for each function, reflects a more realistic use case and strengthens the findings.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity and Clarity:** While the high-level concept is clear, the specific implementation details in Algorithm 1 are quite complex and difficult to parse. The nested loops, multiple conditions for selecting and pruning cells (steps i-iii), the use of "GP-based" labels for dummy function values, and the update rule for the parameter `Ξ` (line 38) are intricate. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind some of these specific design choices, as the current description is very dense.

2.  **Computational Cost of Pruning Step:** The authors claim the method avoids auxiliary optimization. While it is true that it does not require a continuous optimization of an acquisition function, the pruning step (iii), specifically line 22, involves finding the maximum UCB value over a potentially large set of points `T'_{h+ξ}` in a sub-partition. The cost of this step is not fully analyzed but could become significant, especially as `Ξ` grows. This feels like a discrete form of auxiliary optimization, and a discussion of its computational trade-offs would be welcome.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The hierarchical partitioning approach underlying IMGPO is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The theoretical analysis reflects this, as the constant `C` in the regret bound typically depends exponentially on the dimension `D`, which in turn pushes `λ` towards 1. While the 1000-D experiment is included, it relies on the random embedding technique of REMBO. The performance of the core IMGPO algorithm on moderately high-dimensional problems (e.g., D = 20–50) remains an open question that is not addressed experimentally.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Improving Algorithmic Clarity:** I strongly recommend that the authors add a more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of one or two iterations of the algorithm in the main text or an appendix. This could use a simple 1D example to illustrate how a cell is selected by steps (i)-(ii) and then potentially rejected by step (iii), and how the dummy "GP-based" values are placed and later resolved. Further justification for the specific update rule for `Ξ` would also be beneficial.

2.  **Addressing Computational Complexity:** A brief analysis or discussion of the computational complexity per iteration would strengthen the paper. Specifically, what is the cost of the UCB maximization in line 22, and how does `Ξmax` affect this? Comparing this cost to the typical cost of auxiliary optimization in GP-EI/PI would provide a more nuanced picture of the algorithm's practicality.

3.  **Expanding Experimental Analysis:** To better characterize the algorithm's limitations, it would be valuable to include experiments on benchmark functions with intermediate dimensions (e.g., the Hartmann6 function, or Rosenbrock/Ackley in D=10 or D=20). This would provide a clearer picture of how gracefully performance degrades as dimensionality increases, before specialized techniques like REMBO become necessary. An ablation study showing the sensitivity to the new hyperparameter `Ξmax` would also be very useful for practitioners.

4.  **Discussion on Model Misspecification:** The authors rightly note that SOO was competitive on Rosenbrock2 because the GP prior was misleading. This is a crucial point. A slightly expanded discussion on the robustness of IMGPO to GP model misspecification would be valuable. Does the "infinite-metric" aspect provide some resilience compared to pure GP-UCB, even when the GP is not perfect? The results seem to suggest so, but making this point more explicit would be a strength.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. The strengths in originality, theoretical depth, and significance are substantial. The weaknesses are primarily related to the clarity of the complex algorithm and the need for a more thorough exploration of its practical limits, but these are addressable and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
