

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Gaussian process-based global optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence rates without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure previously used in similar methods. The authors address a key open problem in Bayesian optimization: creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. Their key insight is to leverage the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer (Assumption 1), while simultaneously considering all possible candidates for this bound (the "infinite-metric" approach), using the GP's UCB to reduce the number of candidates. The paper provides theoretical guarantees of exponential regret bounds and demonstrates superior empirical performance compared to state-of-the-art methods like BaMSOO, SOO, GP-UCB, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

This work builds upon and extends the literature on bound-based search methods (A*, SOO) and GP-based optimization (GP-UCB). It directly addresses the gap identified by Wang et al. (2014) who noted that "creating a GP-based algorithm with an exponential convergence rate without the impractical sampling procedure remained an open problem."

## Strengths

### 1. Theoretical Contribution (Quality and Significance)
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by proving an exponential regret bound for their algorithm (Theorem 1) with the form O(λ^(N+Ngp)) where λ < 1. This is a significant improvement over previous results like BaMSOO which achieved only polynomial regret bounds (Õ(n^(-2α/(D(4-α))))). The authors provide a clear analysis of how the unknown bound (semi-metric `), the GP's UCB, and the infinite-metric exploration loss affect the regret bound.

The theoretical framework is well-constructed, with interesting remarks (Remarks 1-3) that provide valuable insights into why the approach works and how different components contribute to the performance. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the λ < 1 condition and scalability issues with increasing dimensionality).

### 2. Novelty (Originality)
The paper's core innovation - simultaneously considering all possible candidates for the bound while using the GP's UCB to prune the search space - is genuinely novel. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from previous work, particularly BaMSOO which combines GP-UCB with SOO but only achieves polynomial regret bounds. The "infinite-metric" concept is a significant theoretical contribution that bridges two previously divergent approaches in optimization (bound-based methods with a single bound vs. those considering multiple bounds).

### 3. Experimental Validation (Quality)
The experimental results are well-designed and support the theoretical claims. The authors compare IMGPO against multiple relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on eight different benchmark functions across various dimensions. The results consistently show that IMGPO outperforms other methods, particularly for the majority of test functions. The experimental setup is appropriate (using a single kernel type for all methods with randomly initialized hyperparameters) and reflects practical scenarios where the optimal kernel is unknown.

The authors also include a thoughtful discussion of how the Ξmax parameter affects performance, which demonstrates their understanding of the algorithm's practical implementation considerations.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Theoretical Clarity (Quality)
The paper's main theoretical contribution is potentially undermined by a critical issue in Theorem 1. The authors define λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1, but they do not provide a clear argument for why λ must be less than 1 for the regret bound to be meaningful. For λ to be less than 1, we need α/(2CDρ̄t) > 2, which may not hold for reasonable parameter values. This is a significant gap in the theoretical argument that needs to be addressed. The supplementary material should clarify how this condition is ensured in practice.

### 2. Experimental Rigor (Quality)
While the experimental results support the paper's claims, the lack of statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests or confidence intervals for the performance comparisons) weakens the empirical evidence. The paper reports mean performance for 50 runs for GP-PI and GP-EI, but not for the other methods. This makes it difficult to assess whether the observed performance differences are statistically significant.

### 3. Scalability Concerns (Significance)
The paper correctly acknowledges that the exponential convergence rate becomes less effective as dimensionality increases (Remark 1). The authors note that λ can get close to one as D increases, suggesting a challenge in scalability for higher dimensions. While they mention potential strategies to address this (leveraging additional assumptions), they don't provide any experimental evidence or theoretical analysis of how their algorithm performs in high-dimensional settings (beyond the single 1000-dimensional test function where they use REMBO as a subroutine).

### 4. Clarity of Algorithm Description
The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is quite complex with many nested loops and conditions. While well-commented, the pseudocode could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the key steps (i) through (v) that the authors describe in Section 3.2. A more detailed walkthrough of how the algorithm progresses through a few iterations would help readers understand the practical implementation.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to Bayesian optimization with exponential convergence, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds while using GP to prune the search space is novel and well-motivated. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the authors provide a thoughtful analysis of the method's strengths and limitations.

The main weakness is the potential issue with the λ < 1 condition in Theorem 1, which needs to be clarified. The experimental results could also be strengthened with statistical significance testing. However, these are fixable issues that could be addressed in the camera-ready version.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling procedure is a substantial advance over previous work, and the theoretical framework provides valuable insights into why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial. The experimental validation is strong, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized.

The main weakness (the λ < 1 condition) is a theoretical detail that can be clarified in the revision. The authors have already demonstrated a good understanding of their method's limitations and have provided a thoughtful discussion of the challenges. With minor clarifications to the theoretical argument, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

- First GP-based algorithm to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Novel theoretical framework for understanding bound-based optimization
- Strong experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions
- Clear distinction from previous work (BaMSOO, GP-UCB, SOO)
- Thoughtful analysis of the method's limitations and practical considerations

## Summary of Arguments Against Acceptance

- Potential theoretical gap in the λ < 1 condition
- Lack of statistical significance testing in experiments
- Limited discussion of high-dimensional performance beyond one test case

The theoretical contribution is substantial, and the paper makes a clear, valuable advancement in the field. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed in revision. This paper represents a meaningful contribution to the state of the art in Bayesian optimization.
