### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for black-box functions, a central topic in machine learning. The authors aim to develop a Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret, without relying on impractical assumptions like the δ-cover sampling procedure required by prior work [1]. The key contribution is a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which elegantly synthesizes ideas from hierarchical partitioning methods (like SOO [18]) and GP-based optimization (like GP-UCB).

The core idea of IMGPO is to not solely rely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP model, which may be loose. Instead, it presupposes the existence of an unknown but tighter semi-metric `l` that bounds the function's variation around the global optimum (Assumption 1). The algorithm then implicitly performs a search over an infinite family of such possible metrics. This is operationalized through a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. At each iteration, for every partition size (depth), it identifies a promising candidate region. The GP-UCB is then used not as the primary driver of exploration, but as a mechanism to prune unpromising candidates from this multi-scale search. This synergy allows IMGPO to achieve an exponential regret bound, O(λ^(N+Ngp)), which represents a significant theoretical improvement over the polynomial bounds of related methods like BaMSOO [2]. The authors support their theoretical claims with experiments on several benchmark functions, showing that IMGPO generally outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-EI/PI methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a clearly articulated and important open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature: achieving exponential convergence for GP-based methods without impractical sampling schemes. The proposed solution is highly novel. The conceptual leap of moving from relying on a *single* (GP-UCB) bound to simultaneously considering an *infinite class* of unknown bounds, using the GP as a pruning tool, is both insightful and powerful. This effectively unifies two successful but previously distinct lines of research in global optimization—the DIRECT/SOO family and the GP-UCB family.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The primary contribution is the theoretical result in Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential convergence rate. The proof appears sound and the analysis provides valuable insights. The introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt) is a useful tool for formalizing the cost of the multi-scale search and understanding the algorithm's behavior. The remarks following the theorem are particularly helpful, clearly explaining how the GP's quality affects the bound and why the "infinite-metric" approach can be superior even to using a known, but potentially loose, explicit bound.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is solid and convincing. The authors compare IMGPO against the most relevant and state-of-the-art baselines (SOO, BaMSOO). The choice to not hand-pick kernels and hyperparameters for each function makes the evaluation more realistic and compelling. The results in Figure 2 consistently show IMGPO's superior performance across a variety of functions with different dimensionalities and characteristics, validating the practical benefits of the theoretical design. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO demonstrates a plausible path to scalability.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Algorithmic Complexity:** The algorithm's description is the weakest part of the paper. While the high-level overview in Section 3.2 is intuitive, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to follow. The logic, involving multiple passes through the tree depths (lines 8, 19, 26), temporary "GP-based" labels, and complex conditions for selecting and pruning nodes (`i*h`), is non-trivial. A more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of a single iteration (`t`) accompanying the pseudocode would greatly enhance reproducibility and understanding. For example, the interaction between `υmax` across the different loops is not immediately clear.

2.  **Practicality and Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new hyperparameter, Ξmax, which controls the depth of the UCB-based lookahead for pruning. The paper states it was fixed to 22 and mentions that increasing it could improve performance at the cost of computation, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis is missing. A practitioner would be left wondering how to set this parameter. Furthermore, while the algorithm avoids the auxiliary optimization of standard GP-UCB, the pruning step (line 22) requires maximizing UCB over a potentially large set of points in a sub-tree (`T'`), which could be computationally expensive, especially as Ξmax grows. This computational cost is not fully analyzed.

3.  **Assumptions and Limitations:** The analysis hinges on Assumption 1, which posits the existence of a semi-metric `l` related to the function's smoothness at the optimum. While this is a standard type of assumption, a brief discussion on the class of functions for which it holds would strengthen the paper. The authors also correctly point out the scalability challenge with respect to dimension `D`, as the base of the exponent `λ` approaches 1. This is a crucial limitation that, while acknowledged, deserves emphasis as it confines the direct applicability of IMGPO to low-to-moderate dimensional problems.

### **Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It solves a well-defined open problem with a novel and elegant approach, backed by solid theoretical analysis and convincing empirical results. The weaknesses, primarily related to the clarity of the algorithm's presentation and a lack of hyperparameter analysis, are addressable and do not detract from the core importance of the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses and provides a solution to a recognized open problem.
*   Presents a highly novel algorithm that cleverly synthesizes ideas from two different optimization paradigms.
*   Provides a strong theoretical result (exponential convergence) that improves upon the state of the art.
*   Demonstrates superior empirical performance against relevant baselines in a realistic setting.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The description of the algorithm is complex and hard to follow, potentially hindering reproducibility.
*   The practical implications of the new hyperparameter `Ξmax` and the computational cost of the pruning step are not fully explored.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research and could become an important algorithm in the Bayesian optimization toolkit. I recommend acceptance.
