This paper introduces a novel Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) aiming for exponential convergence without relying on computationally expensive δ-cover sampling, a limitation of previous theoretical results (de Freitas et al. [1]). The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric encoding a potentially tighter bound than the one provided by the GP, effectively conducting both global and local searches based on all possible bounds, while using the GP bound to prune less promising candidates. The algorithm incorporates hierarchical partitioning and a UCB acquisition function. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence and empirical results showcasing IMGPO’s performance on several benchmark functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a strong theoretical result, achieving exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling requirement. This addresses a significant open problem highlighted in Wang et al. [2].  The analysis and derivation of the regret bound appear rigorous, and the inclusion of assumptions (Assumption 1 & 2) and their impact on the bound is well-explained.
* **Novelty:** The concept of simultaneously considering multiple bounds, using the GP to prune candidates, is a novel approach.  This combines ideas from bound-based search methods with GP optimization in a creative manner. The distinction between leveraging the GP's *estimated* bound and the *existence* of an unknown, potentially tighter bound is clearly articulated.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The algorithm is explained with a detailed step-by-step description and a helpful illustrative figure (Figure 1). The relation to existing work (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-UCB) is clearly explained.
* **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation compares IMGPO against several relevant baselines on a variety of benchmark functions.  The discussion of the experimental setup (kernel choice, hyperparameter initialization) and the results, including the impact of Ξmax, are insightful.
* **Detailed Analysis & Discussion:** The authors go beyond presenting the main result, providing a detailed analysis of the convergence rate, discussing the effect of the GP's tightness, and exploring connections to related algorithms.  The discussion of potential future work and scalability concerns is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While avoiding δ-cover sampling, the proposed method introduces a potentially significant computational cost due to the consideration of multiple bounds. While the GP helps prune candidates, the complexity of managing and evaluating these bounds isn’t fully addressed.  The experiments show IMGPO is slower than SOO.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm has several parameters (e.g., Ξmax, η) that require tuning. The sensitivity of the performance to these parameters is discussed, but a more systematic investigation (e.g., a parameter study) would be beneficial.
* **Scalability:** Although the authors acknowledge scalability concerns, the experiments are limited to relatively low-dimensional problems (up to 1000 dimensions with REMBO). The practical applicability of IMGPO to truly high-dimensional optimization problems remains unclear.
* **Assumptions:** Assumption 1 (existence of a semi-metric) is crucial for the theoretical guarantees but may not hold for all real-world optimization problems. A discussion of the practical implications of this assumption and potential strategies for dealing with violations would be valuable.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It would be helpful to understand the contribution of each component of the algorithm. For instance, what is the performance gain achieved by considering the set of possible bounds compared to simply using the GP-UCB algorithm?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant theoretical open problem in Bayesian optimization.
* The proposed algorithm achieves exponential convergence without the impractical δ-cover sampling.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm on benchmark functions.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its theoretical foundations.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost of the algorithm may limit its practical applicability.
* The scalability to high-dimensional problems is uncertain.
* The reliance on Assumption 1 could be problematic in some real-world scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical evaluation supports the claims made by the authors. While the computational cost and scalability concerns are valid, the novelty and theoretical importance of the work outweigh these limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section (e.g., a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, a parameter sensitivity analysis, and a more thorough exploration of the implications of Assumption 1).
