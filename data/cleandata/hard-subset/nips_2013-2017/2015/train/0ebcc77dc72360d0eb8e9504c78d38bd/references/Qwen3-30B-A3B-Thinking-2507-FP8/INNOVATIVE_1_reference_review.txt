

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure previously needed for exponential regret bounds. The authors address a well-known open problem identified by Wang et al. (2014) - creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling - by proposing a bold conceptual shift: instead of relying on a single bound from the GP prior, they consider all possible bounds encoded by unknown semi-metrics.

The core novelty lies in the "infinite-metric exploration" concept, where the algorithm simultaneously searches using all possible bounds (semi-metrics) while leveraging GP to reduce the number of candidates. The authors formalize this through Assumption 1 (unknown bound) and define the "infinite-metric exploration loss" ρt, which quantifies the cost of not committing to a single bound. Their main theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which proves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, significantly improving upon the polynomial regret bound of BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014).

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across eight benchmark functions, showing consistent outperformance of SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI. The experimental design is thoughtful, using a single Matern kernel with default hyperparameters across all functions to simulate practical scenarios where optimal kernels are unknown. The authors correctly note that GP's bound can be "useful" or "useless," and their analysis shows how IMGPO adapts to both cases.

This work stands out in the Bayesian optimization literature, which has largely focused on improving GP-UCB variants with tighter bounds or more efficient sampling. The authors correctly position their contribution against previous work, particularly noting that de Freitas et al. (2012) achieved exponential convergence but with impractical δ-cover sampling, while Wang et al. (2014) only achieved polynomial regret without it.

Strengths:
1. The core idea of considering all possible bounds (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound is genuinely novel and bold.
2. The paper solves a well-documented open problem in the field (Wang et al., 2014).
3. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with clear definitions and theorems.
4. The experimental results robustly support the theoretical claims across multiple benchmarks.
5. The paper thoughtfully discusses limitations (e.g., scalability in high dimensions) and broader implications for other bound-based methods.

Weaknesses:
1. The theoretical analysis is complex, and some notation (e.g., ρ̄t, Ξn) could be clarified for broader accessibility.
2. The explanation of how the algorithm handles "temporary dummy values" (step v) in Figure 1 and Algorithm 1 could be more intuitive.
3. The paper could provide more insight into when IMGPO would be particularly advantageous compared to other methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. Bayesian optimization is widely used in machine learning, and the ability to achieve exponential convergence without impractical sampling procedures represents a major practical advancement. The authors' analysis framework for understanding when not relying on a single bound is beneficial (Remarks 1-3) provides a new perspective on bound-based optimization that could influence future work beyond Bayesian optimization.

While the paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality D increases (Remark 1), this is a known challenge in high-dimensional optimization and doesn't invalidate the core contribution. The authors appropriately frame this as an area for future work rather than a flaw in their current approach.

In terms of originality, this paper clearly represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work. The authors have correctly positioned their contribution against the literature, and the "infinite-metric exploration" concept appears to be genuinely new. The paper is not just an incremental improvement but a fundamental rethinking of how to leverage GP priors in optimization.

The quality of the work is high, with solid theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) providing sufficient detail for reproduction.

Given my preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection, and the paper's clear solution to a known open problem with significant theoretical and practical implications, I strongly recommend acceptance. This work makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization and provides a new conceptual framework for thinking about bound-based search methods. The paper's bold approach to a long-standing problem in the field is exactly the kind of innovation NeurIPS values.
