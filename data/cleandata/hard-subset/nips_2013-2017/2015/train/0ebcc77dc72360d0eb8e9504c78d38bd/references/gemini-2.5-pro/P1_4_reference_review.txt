### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to solve global optimization problems for black-box functions. The main contribution is to present the first Gaussian Process (GP)-based optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without relying on impractical assumptions like the δ-cover sampling procedure or requiring an auxiliary optimization step to maximize the acquisition function. The core idea is to combine the strengths of two distinct lines of research: bound-based optimization with GPs (like GP-UCB) and partitioning-based methods that do not assume knowledge of the function's smoothness (like SOO/DIRECT). IMGPO maintains a hierarchical partitioning of the search space and, at each iteration, simultaneously considers an "infinite" family of potential smoothness assumptions (semi-metrics). The GP posterior is then cleverly used not as the sole driver of exploration, but as a mechanism to prune unpromising regions from this simultaneous search, thereby accelerating convergence. The authors provide a theoretical proof for the exponential regret bound and demonstrate strong empirical performance against several state-of-the-art baselines on a suite of benchmark functions.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature: achieving an exponential convergence rate for GP-based methods under practical assumptions. The proposed solution is highly original. The central idea of not fully committing to the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB) but instead using it to guide a search over an implicit, infinite set of possible function bounds is a conceptual breakthrough. This successfully unifies two historically separate approaches to global optimization and provides a new perspective on how to leverage probabilistic models like GPs.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous proof of an exponential regret bound (Theorem 1), which is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of related practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The analysis introduces the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (`ρ_t`), a novel and insightful quantity that elegantly captures the trade-off inherent in the algorithm. The remarks following the main theorem are particularly valuable, as they clearly explain the practical implications of the theory: how a "useful" GP improves the bound, the precise role of the GP in the algorithm, and why the infinite-metric approach can outperform methods that rely on a single, potentially poorly-specified bound.

3.  **Solid and Realistic Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. IMGPO is compared against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI. The choice of test functions covers a range of dimensionalities and characteristics. Crucially, the experimental setup is realistic and practical; the authors use a standard kernel and an empirical Bayes method to tune hyperparameters, rather than hand-picking optimal settings for each function, which makes the strong results more compelling. The demonstration of using IMGPO within the REMBO framework to tackle a 1000-dimensional problem is a nice addition that points toward its utility in more complex scenarios.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the literature. Section 3.1 provides an excellent high-level overview of the algorithm's core intuition, which is essential for understanding the complex mechanics that follow. The use of a 1D illustrative example (Figure 1) is also very effective in building intuition about the algorithm's behavior.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Algorithm Complexity and Reproducibility:** While the high-level idea is explained well, the algorithm's implementation details are quite complex. The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 involves multiple nested loops, several state variables (`υ_max`, `i*_h`), and non-trivial conditional logic. For example, the process of resolving "GP-based" labels and the exact mechanism for checking UCBs against smaller intervals (lines 18-23) are dense. While the authors provide a link to the source code (which is commendable), the paper itself could benefit from a more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of a single iteration to improve clarity and self-contained reproducibility.

2.  **Heuristic Parameter Update:** The update rule for the parameter `Ξ` (line 38), which controls the depth of the UCB check, appears somewhat ad-hoc. The paper does not provide a theoretical justification for the specific update values (`+2^2`, `-2^-1`). While this adaptive mechanism seems reasonable in practice, its heuristic nature stands in contrast to the rest of the paper's rigorous analysis. A discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to this update rule and the choice of `Ξ_max` would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scalability with Dimensionality:** The analysis in Theorem 1 shows that the convergence rate `λ` depends on constants `C` and `D` (dimension), and `λ` can approach 1 as `D` increases. The authors acknowledge this limitation. While the REMBO experiment suggests a path forward, it also highlights that IMGPO, on its own, is not a direct solution for high-dimensional problems. This is a common limitation in the field, but it should be framed more explicitly as a primary weakness and an important direction for future work.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It successfully bridges a gap between theory and practice by providing the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. The core idea is elegant, the theoretical analysis is sound and insightful, and the empirical results are strong. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to the complexity of the algorithm's description and some heuristic components, which do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. The work is of high quality and is likely to have a substantial impact, inspiring other researchers to explore this new hybrid approach to global optimization. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
