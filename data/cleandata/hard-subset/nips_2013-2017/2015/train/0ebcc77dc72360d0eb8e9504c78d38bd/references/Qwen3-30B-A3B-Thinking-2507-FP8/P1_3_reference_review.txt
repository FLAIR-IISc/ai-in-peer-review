

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors address an open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] regarding creating a GP-based method with exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling. The key insight is to consider infinitely many possible upper bounds (instead of relying on a single bound from the GP) to leverage the unknown continuity at the global optimizer. The authors prove an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which improves upon the polynomial regret bounds of previous methods like BaMSOO [2]. The paper relates to the broader literature on bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*, PAC-MDP) and extends the DIRECT [19] and SOO [18] algorithms with GP information.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions with a rigorous regret bound analysis. The proof techniques are sound, and the authors properly address the limitations of previous work. The exponential regret bound represents a clear theoretical advance over previous GP-based methods, which had polynomial regret bounds. The experimental results on standard benchmarks confirm the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvement over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

**Originality:** The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds rather than relying on a single estimated bound is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly articulates how this approach differs from previous bound-based methods, including GP-UCB and SOO. The authors demonstrate how their approach combines the best of both worlds: the theoretical guarantees of bound-based methods and the practical flexibility of GP priors. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the global optimization literature.

**Significance:** The paper solves a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization, establishing the first GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. This is particularly important for practical applications where δ-cover sampling is computationally prohibitive. The results provide a new theoretical framework for understanding when and why not to rely on a single upper bound, which could influence other bound-based methods beyond optimization.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm description is supported by a helpful figure (Figure 1), and the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is well-structured. The theoretical analysis is presented with appropriate definitions and theorems. The authors effectively explain the relationship to prior work, particularly BaMSOO [2] and SOO [18].

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis assumes that the semi-metric ` satisfies Assumption 2 (which is a strong Lipschitz-like condition). While the paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases (suggesting scalability challenges), this limitation needs more thorough discussion. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence about how the algorithm performs in high-dimensional settings beyond the 1000-dimensional Sin2 example, which uses REMBO as a subroutine.

**Clarity:** The paper could better explain the intuition behind the infinite-metric exploration loss (ρt) and why it's necessary. The description of how the algorithm handles "temporary dummy values" (lines 33-37 in Algorithm 1) is somewhat opaque. The connection between the theoretical analysis and the practical implementation could be strengthened.

**Significance:** While the theoretical improvement is significant, the practical impact on real-world problems could be better articulated. The paper demonstrates improved performance on standard benchmarks, but it doesn't discuss how the computational overhead of the infinite-metric approach compares to other methods in terms of wall-clock time (not just function evaluations).

**Originality:** The paper cites related work appropriately, but the connection to the DIRECT algorithm [19] could be more precisely articulated. The paper states that DIRECT has consistency but not finite regret bounds, but doesn't clearly explain how their method improves upon this.

## Detailed Assessment

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an important open problem in Bayesian optimization. The exponential regret bound is a clear improvement over previous work, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing consistent performance gains over baselines.

However, the paper could strengthen its discussion of scalability. The analysis shows that λ approaches 1 as dimension D increases, suggesting that the algorithm may not scale well to very high dimensions. While the paper mentions this limitation, it doesn't provide concrete evidence of how quickly the performance degrades as dimension increases, nor does it propose practical strategies for high-dimensional problems beyond the REMBO example.

The paper also doesn't fully address the computational cost of the infinite-metric approach. While Figure 2 shows that IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods," it doesn't compare wall-clock times between methods. This is important for practitioners who care about total runtime, not just the number of function evaluations.

The paper's greatest strength is its theoretical novelty. The idea of considering infinitely many bounds rather than relying on a single GP-based bound is conceptually elegant and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate that this approach can outperform methods that rely on a single (potentially loose) bound, which is a valuable insight for the broader optimization community.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and addresses a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization. The paper is well-written, the analysis is sound, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. While there are some limitations regarding scalability and computational cost that could be addressed in the final version, these don't detract from the paper's main contribution.

The paper makes a clear and important advance in the theoretical understanding of GP-based optimization, and its insights about not relying on a single upper bound could influence future work in optimization and related areas. This represents a meaningful contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of computational overhead (wall-clock time) in the experiments and providing more concrete evidence of how the algorithm performs in higher-dimensional settings beyond the REMBO example. These would strengthen the paper further, but they are not required for acceptance given the paper's strong theoretical contribution.

This paper represents a high-quality contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
