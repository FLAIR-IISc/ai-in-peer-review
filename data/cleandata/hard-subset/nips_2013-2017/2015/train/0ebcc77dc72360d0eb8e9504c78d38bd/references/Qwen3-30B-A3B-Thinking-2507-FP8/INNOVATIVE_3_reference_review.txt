

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential convergence rates without relying on impractical sampling procedures like δ-cover. The authors propose IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel GP-based algorithm that maintains exponential regret bounds without the δ-cover sampling requirement that previously limited practical implementation of exponential convergence results.

The core innovation lies in the "infinite-metric" approach, where the algorithm simultaneously considers all possible upper bounds encoded by unknown semi-metrics rather than relying on a single bound provided by the GP. This represents a bold theoretical shift from the standard GP-UCB framework. The authors formally prove an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which is a significant improvement over the polynomial regret bounds of previous GP-based methods like BaMSOO. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with the authors carefully examining the relationship between the GP's uncertainty bounds and the unknown semi-metric, providing valuable insights into when this approach provides advantages over standard methods.

The paper makes a compelling case for the significance of this work. It directly addresses a specific open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] and provides the first GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The theoretical framework developed (particularly in Remarks 1-3) offers a novel perspective on bound-based search methods that could potentially influence other areas beyond Bayesian optimization.

The experimental results demonstrate that IMGPO consistently outperforms existing methods (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) across multiple benchmark functions. The authors make a reasonable practical assumption that good kernel parameters are unavailable, using a standard Matern kernel with default hyperparameters across all experiments. The results are well-presented with clear comparisons, and the paper acknowledges limitations (such as the algorithm's performance in high dimensions and the sensitivity to Ξmax).

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel theoretical contribution:** The infinite-metric approach represents a genuinely new direction in Bayesian optimization with clear theoretical advantages.
2. **Addresses a concrete open problem:** The paper directly tackles a specific gap identified in prior work [2].
3. **Theoretical insight:** The analysis provides valuable understanding of when not relying on a single bound is advantageous.
4. **Strong experimental validation:** The algorithm demonstrates consistent performance improvements across multiple test functions.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper could better clarify how this differs from de Freitas et al.'s [1] exponential convergence result, which also claimed exponential rates but required δ-cover sampling. The distinction between "exponential convergence" in [1] versus the practical implementation in this work needs clearer articulation.
2. The claim that IMGPO "does not require auxiliary optimization" is slightly misleading, as the hierarchical partitioning procedure still requires optimization of the acquisition function, though it avoids the δ-cover sampling.
3. The experimental section could better quantify the computational cost of the algorithm relative to baselines, particularly the tradeoff between Ξmax and performance.

Despite these minor weaknesses, this paper makes a significant contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization. The core idea of considering infinitely many bounds rather than relying on a single estimated bound is bold, novel, and well-justified by the theoretical analysis. The exponential regret bound represents a major theoretical advance over previous GP-based methods, and the practical implementation demonstrates clear empirical advantages.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical directions, I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the literature and provided a fundamentally new approach to addressing it. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. While the paper could be strengthened by better distinguishing its approach from prior exponential convergence results, this does not diminish the significance of the core contribution.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a genuinely novel approach that significantly advances the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical benefits. This work opens up new directions for research in Bayesian optimization and potentially other bound-based search methods. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the substantial theoretical and practical contributions.
