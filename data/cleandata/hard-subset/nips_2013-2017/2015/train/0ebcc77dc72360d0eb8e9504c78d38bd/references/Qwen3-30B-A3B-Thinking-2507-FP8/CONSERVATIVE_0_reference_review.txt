

# Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

This paper proposes IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Gaussian Process-based global optimization algorithm that claims to achieve exponential regret convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure that previous theoretical approaches relied upon. The authors position their work as resolving an open problem identified by Wang et al. [2], where creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without impractical sampling procedures remained unresolved.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-structured and presents a detailed algorithm with pseudocode. The theoretical analysis (though relegated to supplementary material) attempts to provide a regret bound of O(λ^{N+Ngp}) with λ < 1, which would represent a significant theoretical contribution if valid. The idea of considering multiple bounds rather than relying on a single GP-based bound is conceptually interesting, and the authors provide some theoretical justification for why this might be beneficial.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising theoretical claims, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses that prevent me from recommending acceptance:

1. **Lack of rigorous theoretical validation:** The core theoretical claim (exponential convergence) is not properly substantiated in the main paper. The proof is relegated to supplementary material, which is insufficient for a conference paper. The regret bound in Theorem 1 appears to have several questionable aspects - the definition of λ = 3 - α²CDρ̄t < 1 is confusing as it depends on ρ̄t which itself depends on the algorithm's behavior. The paper fails to clearly explain how this bound is better than existing results, especially given the dimensionality limitation noted in Theorem 1.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation:** The experimental section is severely limited. The authors compare to only 8 test functions, with no statistical significance testing (GP-PI and GP-EI used 50 runs while others did not specify). The paper claims IMGPO "outperformed the other algorithms in general" but provides no quantitative measures of improvement. The paper also fails to include a comparison with GP-UCB (the method the authors claim to improve upon), which is a major oversight given that GP-UCB is a standard baseline in the field.

3. **Misrepresentation of previous work:** The paper claims to be "the first GP-based optimization method with an exponential convergence rate without the need of auxiliary optimization and the δ-cover sampling procedure," but this is inaccurate. De Freitas et al. [1] already demonstrated exponential convergence with δ-cover sampling, which is precisely what the authors claim to avoid. The paper does not adequately acknowledge this prior work or explain how their method differs in a meaningful way.

4. **Lack of scalability analysis:** The paper acknowledges that "λ can get close to one as input dimension D increases," which undermines the claimed exponential convergence in high dimensions. The authors do not provide any meaningful analysis of how their method would scale to practical high-dimensional problems (beyond the 1000-dimensional test case that used REMBO as a subroutine).

5. **Implementation and reproducibility issues:** The paper claims IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods" but provides no clear metrics to support this claim. The paper also fails to provide sufficient detail about the implementation to allow reproduction of results.

**Critical Assessment:** The paper's main contribution appears to be a combination of existing techniques (GP optimization with hierarchical partitioning) rather than a fundamentally new approach. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental validation is inadequate for a NIPS paper. The paper's strongest claim (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling) is not convincingly demonstrated, and the paper fails to show that this approach is actually better than existing methods in practice.

**Comparison to Previous Work:** The paper cites Wang et al. [2] as having achieved a polynomial regret bound, but the authors don't properly address how their method improves upon BaMSOO's results. The paper also fails to properly compare to the seminal work by Srinivas et al. [15] on GP bandits, which established the standard GP-UCB approach.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem in Bayesian optimization, the theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental validation is inadequate. The paper presents a novel algorithm but fails to provide the rigorous evidence required to support its claims. The field of Bayesian optimization has many established baselines, and this paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method is superior to these baselines in a statistically significant way.

For future work, the authors should provide a complete theoretical proof in the main paper, conduct more comprehensive experiments with statistical significance testing, compare to all relevant baselines (including GP-UCB), and address the scalability concerns. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for technical soundness and rigorous validation.
