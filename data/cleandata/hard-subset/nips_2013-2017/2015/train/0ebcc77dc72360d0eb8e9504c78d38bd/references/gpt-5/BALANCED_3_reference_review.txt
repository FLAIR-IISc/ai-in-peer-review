Summary
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process–based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions. The key idea is to combine hierarchical partitioning (as in SOO/DIRECT) with GP-based optimistic bounds, while not committing to a single smoothness/Lipschitz metric. Instead, the method implicitly considers an infinite family of possible semi-metrics around the global optimum (Assumption 1), and uses GP upper confidence bounds (UCB) to prune candidates and to avoid expensive function evaluations by “temporarily filling” node values with UCBs. The authors define an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt to quantify the cost of not committing to one bound, and prove a simple-regret bound of the form rN = O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, i.e., exponential in the total number of true evaluations N plus the number of GP-based (proxy) evaluations Ngp. They argue the bound adapts to the best unknown Hölder parameters (L, α, p) and analyze the effect of GP on ρ̄t and Ngp. Empirically, IMGPO is compared against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on standard benchmarks and shows favorable performance; a REMBO+IMGPO variant is also tested in 1000D.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on DIRECT (Jones et al., 1993), DOO/SOO (Munos, NIPS 2011), GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., ICML 2010), the exponential-regret result of de Freitas et al. (ICML 2012) that used δ-covers, and BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS 2014), which combined SOO with GP-UCB and obtained polynomial regret. The claimed novelty is to remove δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization while proving an exponential simple-regret rate.

Strengths
- Ambitious and potentially impactful goal: an exponential-regret GP-based optimizer without δ-covers or acquisition-optimization subroutines would be a substantial advance over BaMSOO and classical GP-UCB.
- Interesting conceptual contribution: the “infinite-metric” view and the exploration-loss ρt provide a lens to quantify the cost/benefit of not committing to a specific smoothness constant, connecting the Lipschitz-free (SOO-style) and GP-optimism strands.
- Algorithmic design has practical elements (hierarchical partitioning, using UCB to skip costly evaluations), and the empirical results are competitive across several standard functions.
- The analysis highlights how GP contributes (via decreasing ρ̄t and increasing Ngp) and how dimensionality degrades constants, which is valuable for understanding limitations.

Weaknesses
- Core theoretical assumption/justification gap: The property f(x) ≤ U(x|D) “with probability ≥ 1−η” is asserted using a choice of exploration parameter ςM, but this only holds under nontrivial assumptions (e.g., the true f is a GP sample from the chosen kernel, or f lies in an RKHS with bounded norm and the kernel/hyperparameters are fixed; see Srinivas et al., 2010). The paper updates hyperparameters by empirical Bayes during optimization and even notes cases where the GP prior is “misleading” and the UCB property “did not hold many times.” As written, the main probabilistic invariant used in the analysis appears unjustified under the stated setup. This is a major concern for soundness.
- Ambiguity/typos in the bound: Theorem 1 states “Let λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t < 1,” which is dimensionally inconsistent; from the displayed exponential, it seems λ should be 3^{−α/(2CDρ̄t)}. Such errors make it hard to verify the claim. Several notational issues (e.g., “sin+1,” inconsistent use of norms, occasionally unclear definitions like Ngp) hinder clarity.
- Assumptions essentially reintroduce smoothness: Assumption 2 is a global Hölder-type bound `(x′,x) ≤ L||x′−x||_p^α. While the algorithm is “infinite-metric,” the regret analysis still relies on such a condition. The novelty over SOO/DOO is then concentrated in the GP-assisted pruning and the way ρ̄t enters the exponent; a crisper comparison to the best-known rates under Hölder assumptions would help (e.g., does exponential hold for any α>0 and dimension D, or only when ρ̄t is bounded?).
- Computational aspects of step (iii): The algorithm may need to evaluate UCBs over a depth-ξ fully expanded subtree, with worst-case cost exponential in Ξmax; yet Ξmax=22 is used in experiments. More precise complexity accounting and an ablation on Ξmax would strengthen the practical story.
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat limited: Only deterministic functions, a single kernel family (isotropic Matérn 5/2), and acquisition solvers for GP-PI/EI that differ from common practice. No comparison to GP-UCB itself, and no noisy or constrained settings. The REMBO experiment is illustrative but does not isolate IMGPO’s contribution (e.g., compare to REMBO+EI/UCB under identical settings).

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The algorithm is well-motivated and experiments are promising. However, the main bound seems to crucially rely on a UCB validity condition that is not properly assumed or verified given hyperparameter learning and potential model misspecification. This undermines the claimed exponential guarantee.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but several formula/notation typos, dense pseudocode, and insufficiently explicit assumptions about the GP-UCB property reduce clarity. The role of ρ̄t and its typical scaling deserve clearer discussion.
- Originality: Combining infinite-metric reasoning with GP-based pruning is a novel angle relative to BaMSOO, and an exponential simple-regret claim would be new if correct.
- Significance: If the theory were fully sound under realistic assumptions, the contribution would be significant. As is, the practical algorithm may still be useful, but the theoretical advance is not convincingly established.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel conceptual framing (infinite-metric exploration loss) and an appealing algorithm that often outperforms strong baselines.
- Ambitious theoretical target; if fixed, could influence the BO literature.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees appear to rely on an unproven global UCB property under EB hyperparameter updates and potential model misspecification.
- Typos/ambiguities in the main theorem.
- Limited empirical scope and missing key baselines/ablations.

Suggestions for improvement
- State explicit conditions under which f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holds uniformly over the algorithm’s queries. If you require f to be a GP sample or to lie in an RKHS with bounded norm B under a fixed kernel, say so, and align ςM (or βt) with existing results (e.g., Srinivas et al., 2010) that depend on information gain and fixed hyperparameters. Alternatively, separate the practical EB variant from the theoretical fixed-kernel analysis.
- Correct Theorem 1’s statement of λ and clean up notation. Provide a succinct, self-contained statement of assumptions and the resulting bound, with all constants defined.
- Provide complexity analysis of step (iii) and empirical scaling with Ξmax; include an ablation varying Ξmax and reporting wall-clock time and regret.
- Add baselines: GP-UCB (with the same kernel/hyperparameter learning), and REMBO with standard acquisitions. Include a noisy setting to probe robustness.
- Clarify how ρ̄t typically behaves in practice (plots of ρt over t), and how the bound degrades with D. If exponential decay becomes vacuous for moderate D, discuss when the result is meaningful.
- Improve presentation: tighten pseudocode, fix minor typos, and expand on the intuition for Assumptions 1–2 and their relation to near-optimality dimension.

Recommendation: Reject (encourage resubmission after addressing the theoretical gaps and strengthening the empirical and presentation aspects).
