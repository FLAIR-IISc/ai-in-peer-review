## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) designed to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work (de Freitas et al., 2017). The core idea is to simultaneously consider a wide range of potential Lipschitz-like bounds (captured by an “infinite-metric” approach) and leverage the GP prior to prune unlikely candidates. The authors provide a theoretical analysis guaranteeing exponential convergence and demonstrate empirical performance on a variety of benchmark functions, outperforming several baselines including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods.

**Relationship to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the context of Bayesian Optimization and the challenges of achieving theoretical guarantees without impractical assumptions. They acknowledge the limitations of previous exponential convergence results relying on δ-cover sampling and build upon the work of Wang et al. (2018) by aiming for a similar convergence rate without their reliance on the SOO algorithm. The connection to bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*) is well articulated, highlighting the importance of tight bounds for performance.  The paper also accurately frames its approach in relation to Munos’ (2013) work on SOO and its limitations.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential convergence, a significant achievement in the field of Bayesian Optimization. The analysis is detailed and considers the impact of various parameters.
* **Novel Approach:** The “infinite-metric” optimization concept is a compelling and potentially impactful idea. Addressing the difficulty of obtaining tight bounds by simultaneously considering many possibilities is a clever strategy.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO across a diverse set of benchmark functions.  The authors thoughtfully compare against relevant baselines.  The acknowledgement that the GP needs to provide *some* useful information is a strength, and the analysis reflects this.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithm is explained clearly, and the mathematical notation is mostly accessible.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge limitations and potential avenues for improvement, such as scalability to higher dimensions.  They also discuss the sensitivity of performance to parameter settings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While the algorithm is explained well, the overall approach feels somewhat complex and relies on several parameters (Ξmax, η) that require tuning. The interplay between these parameters isn’t fully explored in the text.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance gains observed in the experiments appear somewhat sensitive to the choice of Ξmax. While the authors address this in the discussion, it suggests a potential practical challenge.
* **Scalability Concerns:** Despite acknowledging the scalability limitations, the paper doesn’t offer concrete solutions beyond suggesting further research.  The theoretical analysis also points to potential issues with convergence rate as dimensionality increases.
* **Limited Novelty in Experiments:** The experimental setup largely reuses benchmarks and baselines from previous work. While this facilitates comparison, it doesn’t necessarily showcase the unique strengths of IMGPO in novel scenarios.
* **Hyperparameter tuning details:** While the authors state they used standard values, details regarding the empirical Bayesian hyperparameter updates would be helpful for reproducibility.



**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The completeness of the work is high, though scalability remains a concern. The authors are careful in their evaluation, discussing both strengths and weaknesses. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized, but the complexity of the algorithm and its parameters could be made more accessible with additional explanations and visualizations. **(Good)**
* **Originality:** The “infinite-metric” optimization approach is novel and represents a significant departure from previous work in Bayesian Optimization. The combination of ideas is well-motivated and clearly differentiates this work. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:** The results are important because they address a fundamental challenge in Bayesian Optimization – achieving theoretical guarantees without impractical assumptions.  IMGPO has the potential to be a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers alike. **(Good)**

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical foundation with an exponential convergence guarantee.
* Novel and well-motivated algorithmic approach.
* Consistent empirical performance improvements over established baselines.
* Addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian Optimization.

**Con:**

* Algorithm complexity and parameter sensitivity.
* Scalability concerns limit the applicability to high-dimensional problems.
* Experimental setup could be more innovative.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical performance is promising. While the algorithm’s complexity and scalability concerns are valid, they do not outweigh the benefits of the novel approach and the strong theoretical guarantees. I recommend acceptance after the authors address the following minor revisions:

1.  **Expand the discussion of parameter sensitivity:** Provide more guidance on how to choose appropriate values for Ξmax and other key parameters.
2.  **Address scalability more directly:** Explore potential strategies for mitigating the scalability limitations, even if only in the form of future research directions.
3.  **Provide more detail on hyperparameter tuning:** Add more details on the empirical Bayesian method and the initial hyperparameter choices to improve reproducibility.
