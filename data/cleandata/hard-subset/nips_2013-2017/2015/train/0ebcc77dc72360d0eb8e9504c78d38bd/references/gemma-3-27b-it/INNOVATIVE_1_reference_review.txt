This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling required by previous theoretical guarantees. The core idea is ingenious: instead of relying solely on the GP's potentially weak bound on the objective function, IMGPO simultaneously explores possibilities based on an *unknown* tighter bound encoded by the function’s continuity at its optimum. This is achieved through a hierarchical partitioning scheme combined with a clever filtering mechanism guided by the GP’s uncertainty estimates. The theoretical analysis claims an exponential convergence rate and highlights the benefits of leveraging this unknown bound.

The strength of this work lies in its novelty and ambition. The problem of achieving exponential convergence in GP-based Bayesian optimization *without* δ-cover sampling is a significant one, acknowledged by leading researchers in the field (referenced in the introduction). IMGPO directly addresses this and proposes a genuinely new approach. The theoretical analysis, while detailed, supports the claim of improved convergence and offers insightful discussion on the interplay between GP-derived bounds and the unknown, potentially tighter, semi-metric. The experiments, while not exhaustive, demonstrate promising performance across a range of benchmark functions, consistently outperforming established methods. The inclusion of a publicly available codebase is commendable.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The description of the algorithm, particularly the iterative process in Section 3.2, is somewhat convoluted and relies heavily on Figure 1, making it difficult to fully grasp without careful study. The parameters Ξmax and η, while explained, could benefit from more justification for their specific settings in the experiments. The analysis, while rigorous, feels somewhat detached from the practical implementation; bridging this gap with a discussion on computational complexity and parameter sensitivity would strengthen the contribution. The discussion in Remarks 1, 2, and 3 is valuable, but a bit dense and could be presented more clearly. While the experimental results are positive, the choice of kernels and initial hyperparameter settings appears somewhat arbitrary given the emphasis on avoiding hand-tuning. Furthermore, the scaling to 1000 dimensions is achieved through REMBO, and the contribution of IMGPO within that framework isn’t fully disentangled. The paper acknowledges the scalability challenges, but a more detailed discussion of potential solutions would be appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of leveraging an unknown, tighter bound is genuinely new and addresses a significant open problem.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The exponential convergence rate is theoretically justified, and the analysis provides valuable insights.
*   **Promising Experimental Results:** The algorithm outperforms existing methods on benchmark functions.
*   **Publicly Available Code:** This promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.
*   **Bold Approach:** This paper tackles a difficult problem with a non-trivial solution, demonstrating a willingness to explore novel ideas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The algorithmic description is complex and requires careful study.
*   **Limited Scalability Analysis:** While acknowledged, a deeper discussion on scaling to higher dimensions would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the algorithm to parameter settings (Ξmax, η) isn’t thoroughly explored.
*   **Kernel/Hyperparameter Choice:** The arbitrary choice of kernel and hyperparameters could be more rigorously justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses in clarity and practical considerations, the originality and potential impact of this work are significant. The paper proposes a genuinely novel approach to Bayesian optimization with a strong theoretical foundation and promising experimental results. I believe this work represents a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. I am willing to overlook the relatively minor flaws given the boldness of the approach and the importance of the addressed problem. This is a paper that could genuinely move the field forward.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
