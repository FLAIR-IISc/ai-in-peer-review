### **Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization for deterministic black-box functions, focusing on the simple regret performance measure. The authors identify a key open problem in the Bayesian Optimization (BO) literature: achieving an exponential convergence rate without resorting to impractical sampling procedures like the δ-cover. Previous work by de Freitas et al. [1] achieved this rate but required δ-cover sampling, while follow-up work like BaMSOO [2] removed this requirement but only proved a polynomial convergence rate.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first GP-based method to provably achieve an exponential regret bound without δ-cover sampling or requiring an auxiliary optimization step to maximize the acquisition function. The core idea is a clever synthesis of two distinct lines of global optimization research: the hierarchical partitioning approach (seen in algorithms like DIRECT and SOO) and the probabilistic modeling of GP-based BO. Instead of relying solely on the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB), IMGPO implicitly considers an infinite set of possible local smoothness conditions (semi-metrics) around the optimum. It uses the GP-UCB not as the definitive bound, but as a tool to efficiently prune the search space of this "infinite-metric" exploration. The paper provides a theoretical proof for its exponential convergence rate and presents empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrating its superior performance against relevant baselines.

---

**Evaluation and Reasoning**

Here I evaluate the paper based on the standard NIPS criteria: Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance.

**1. Quality: Excellent**

The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, directly supports the central claim of the paper: an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret. The proof structure appears sound (though proofs are deferred to supplementary material). The analysis is particularly strong in the remarks that follow the theorem. Remarks 1-3 provide excellent intuition into the algorithm's mechanics, clarifying how the GP's UCB quality affects the regret bound (Remark 1), the dual benefit of the GP in reducing regret (Remark 2), and why the "infinite-metric" approach can outperform methods that commit to a single, potentially mismatched, smoothness assumption (Remark 3). This level of analysis shows a deep understanding of the problem.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete contribution. It identifies an open problem, proposes a novel algorithm, provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of its performance, and validates it with a solid set of experiments. The authors also provide code, which is a significant plus for reproducibility.
*   **Evaluation Honesty:** The experimental evaluation is well-conducted and honest. The authors compare against the most relevant prior work (SOO, BaMSOO) and standard BO methods (GP-EI, GP-PI). They acknowledge cases where their method is not the best (e.g., Rosenbrock2) and correctly attribute this to a misleading GP prior, which is a known failure mode for GP-based methods and strengthens the credibility of their analysis.

**2. Clarity: Good, but could be improved**

The paper is generally well-written and organized, but the description of the algorithm itself is very dense and could be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with hierarchical partitioning algorithms.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction and motivation are exceptionally clear. The authors do an excellent job of situating their work within the existing literature and precisely stating the open problem they solve. The high-level overview in Section 3.1 is also helpful.
*   **Areas for Improvement:** The main weakness in clarity lies in Sections 3.2 and 3.3, which describe the algorithm's mechanics. While the 1D illustration in Figure 1 is useful, connecting its steps directly to the complex pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is difficult. The interplay between the nested loops, the `υ_max` variable, the selection/pruning of candidate intervals `i*_h` across different depths, and the dynamic update of the parameter `Ξ` is complex. A more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of the pseudocode for a simple 2-3 iteration example would significantly improve readability and aid in reproducibility. For instance, explicitly showing how the sets of `i*_h` are populated and then pruned in the loops from lines 8-24 would be invaluable.

**3. Originality: Excellent**

The work is highly original. While it builds upon established concepts (GPs, UCB, hierarchical partitioning), the synthesis is novel and insightful.

*   **Novel Approach:** The core idea of "infinite-metric" optimization, where the algorithm simultaneously entertains all possible local smoothness assumptions and uses the GP to prune them, is a new and powerful concept in the BO space. It elegantly combines the "all-scales" search of DIRECT/SOO with the probabilistic guidance of a GP. This is a significant departure from the standard BO paradigm, which typically commits fully to the model specified by the GP kernel.
*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** The paper clearly articulates how IMGPO differs from its closest relatives. Unlike BaMSOO, which uses SOO as a subroutine to optimize a GP acquisition function, IMGPO integrates the GP directly into the partitioning and selection logic. This integration is the key that enables the exponential convergence proof. The work successfully bridges two previously separate algorithmic philosophies in a non-trivial way.

**4. Significance: Very High**

The results presented in this paper are highly significant for both the theoretical and practical BO communities.

*   **Theoretical Advancement:** Resolving a major open problem by proving the first exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based algorithm is a landmark theoretical achievement. This advances our fundamental understanding of what is possible in global optimization with GPs. The provided analysis framework could also inspire new theoretical tools for other hybrid algorithms.
*   **Practical Implications:** The algorithm does not require solving an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem for the acquisition function, which is a major practical bottleneck for standard GP-UCB, GP-EI, and GP-PI. IMGPO's deterministic partitioning scheme replaces this with a series of comparisons, making it computationally faster per iteration (as shown in Table 1). The strong empirical performance suggests it could be a highly effective, off-the-shelf optimizer for low-to-moderate dimensional problems.
*   **Future Impact:** The core idea of using a probabilistic model to prune a more robust, non-parametric search strategy is compelling and could be influential beyond this specific algorithm. As the authors suggest in the conclusion, this principle might be applicable to other domains involving bound-based search, such as planning and reinforcement learning.

---

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Solves a Major Open Problem:** Provides the first GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate without impractical assumptions.
2.  **High Originality:** The "infinite-metric" approach represents a novel and powerful synthesis of hierarchical partitioning and Bayesian optimization.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper offers a rigorous proof of its main claim, accompanied by insightful analysis that clarifies the algorithm's behavior.
4.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The algorithm outperforms relevant state-of-the-art baselines on a variety of benchmark problems.
5.  **Practical Advantages:** Eliminates the need for an auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function, a common bottleneck in standard BO.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
1.  **Clarity of Algorithm:** The description of the algorithm's mechanics is dense and complex, potentially hindering understanding and implementation. (This is a moderate issue that can be addressed in a revision).
2.  **Scalability:** Like other partitioning-based methods, the algorithm's performance is expected to degrade with dimensionality, a point the authors acknowledge but do not extensively explore empirically beyond using it as a subroutine for REMBO.

**Conclusion:**

This is an outstanding paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The strengths—solving a major open problem with a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically strong algorithm—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the clarity of the algorithm's presentation. This work is of high quality, originality, and significance, and I strongly recommend its acceptance.
