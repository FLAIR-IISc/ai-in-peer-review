### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of global optimization of a black-box function, a central task in Bayesian Optimization (BO). The authors identify a key open problem in the literature: achieving an exponential convergence rate for a Gaussian Process (GP)-based optimization algorithm without resorting to impractical sampling schemes like the δ-cover. The current state-of-the-art practical algorithms, such as BaMSOO, only achieve polynomial convergence rates.

The main contribution is a novel algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which successfully achieves an exponential regret bound. The core idea is a conceptual shift from relying solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP. Instead, IMGPO assumes the existence of an unknown, potentially much tighter, semi-metric that bounds the function's variation near the global optimum. The algorithm then simultaneously considers an infinite family of such possible semi-metrics by using a hierarchical partitioning scheme similar to the SOO algorithm. The GP-UCB is not used as the primary driver of optimism but rather as a powerful tool to prune branches of the search tree that are provably suboptimal, thereby making the search over the "infinite metrics" tractable.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes the exponential convergence rate `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` with `λ < 1`. The analysis insightfully shows how the GP's quality affects the convergence rate through a term called the "infinite-metric exploration loss." The paper supports its theoretical claims with experiments on several benchmark functions, demonstrating that IMGPO generally outperforms existing methods like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

### Review

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant theoretical and conceptual advance in the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed algorithm, IMGPO, elegantly combines ideas from optimistic partitioning algorithms and GP-based BO to solve a well-known open problem. The theoretical analysis is thorough and the experimental results are convincing.

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the analysis presented in Section 4 and the supplementary material. Theorem 1 is the central result, proving the claimed exponential convergence rate. The analysis is particularly strong because it provides intuition through Remarks 1-3, which dissect the contributions of the different components of the algorithm: the GP model and the "infinite-metric" optimization philosophy. This helps the reader understand *why* the algorithm works and under what conditions it is most beneficial (e.g., when the GP provides a useful but not perfectly tight bound).

The experimental methodology is solid. The authors compare against a strong set of relevant baselines on a variety of standard optimization benchmarks. The choice to not hand-pick kernels and hyperparameters for each function makes the experimental setting more realistic and compelling. The results in Figure 2 generally demonstrate the superiority of IMGPO. The inclusion of runtime analysis (Table 1) is a welcome addition, honestly positioning the algorithm's computational cost relative to its peers.

**Clarity: Good, with room for improvement**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The high-level overview of the algorithm in Section 3.1 is excellent and provides a clear intuition for the novel "infinite-metric" idea.

However, the detailed description of the algorithm in Section 3.3 and the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) are very dense and difficult to follow. The sequence of steps for selecting, filtering, and dividing cells (lines 8-24) involves multiple loops and conditions that are hard to trace. For example, the meaning of `T'h+ξ(ch,i*h)` and the logic behind the update of `υmax` are not immediately obvious. The paper would be significantly improved if the authors could provide a more detailed walkthrough of the pseudocode, perhaps connecting it more explicitly to the visual example in Figure 1.

The parameter `Ξ` and its update rule (line 38) feel somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from more justification or intuitive explanation. Why these specific update constants? An ablation study on its effect would be beneficial.

**Originality: Very High**
The core contribution of this paper is highly original. While it builds upon the hierarchical partitioning framework of SOO/BaMSOO and uses standard GP-UCB machinery, the central idea of "Infinite-Metric Optimization" is novel. It reframes the role of the GP in optimistic algorithms: instead of being the sole source of the upper bound, the GP acts as a pruning mechanism for a search over a much richer class of implicit smoothness assumptions. This is a clever and powerful combination of two distinct lines of research in global optimization (one based on a fixed, known bound structure, and one that makes no assumptions, like DIRECT). This work successfully bridges that gap, which is a significant conceptual contribution. The paper clearly articulates its relationship to prior work, especially BaMSOO, and correctly identifies its key advance over it (exponential vs. polynomial regret).

**Significance: High**
The results are important for both the theoretical and practical BO communities.
*   **Theoretically,** providing the first practical GP-based algorithm with an exponential convergence rate is a major milestone. This closes an important gap in the theory of BO. The analytical framework, particularly the "infinite-metric exploration loss," provides a new tool for understanding the trade-offs in these types of algorithms.
*   **Practically,** IMGPO appears to be a state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free global optimization of expensive black-box functions. It does not require auxiliary optimization to maximize an acquisition function, which can be a practical bottleneck for standard GP-BO methods. The strong empirical performance suggests it could be widely adopted by practitioners. The broader idea of using a surrogate model to prune a more general search process could inspire new algorithms in other areas of AI, such as planning and reinforcement learning, as the authors suggest.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Addresses and solves a significant open problem** in Bayesian Optimization theory.
2.  **Highly novel core idea** of combining an "infinite-metric" search with GP-based pruning.
3.  **Strong theoretical result** (Theorem 1) proving exponential convergence, with insightful analysis of the algorithm's components.
4.  **Convincing empirical validation** against strong baselines on a range of problems.
5.  **No need for auxiliary optimization** of an acquisition function, a practical advantage over many BO methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Clarity of the algorithm's detailed description** and pseudocode could be improved. The logic is complex and requires significant effort to parse.
2.  The curse of dimensionality, a known issue for partitioning-based methods, is present here (as noted by the authors in Section 4). While they demonstrate a high-dimensional application using REMBO, the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm is a limitation that warrants a more prominent discussion.
3.  Some algorithmic details, like the update rule for the parameter `Ξ` and the choice of a trisection for partitioning, feel heuristic and could be better motivated.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees and empirical performance. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely related to the clarity of presentation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
