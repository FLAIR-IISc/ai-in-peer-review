

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents a novel algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence for Bayesian optimization without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors address an open problem stated in Wang et al. [2] by proposing a method that maintains exponential regret bounds while eliminating the need for auxiliary optimization procedures.

The key innovation lies in the authors' conceptual shift from relying on a single upper bound (as in GP-UCB) to simultaneously considering infinitely many possible bounds (semi-metrics) encoded in the continuity at the global optimizer. This "infinite-metric exploration" approach allows the algorithm to leverage the GP prior to reduce the search space while maintaining exponential convergence. The authors formally analyze this approach, proving an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which significantly improves upon the polynomial regret bounds of previous methods like BaMSOO.

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by providing a framework for understanding why not relying on a single upper bound can be advantageous. The three remarks in the analysis section offer particularly insightful theoretical perspectives on the interplay between the GP's bound, the unknown semi-metric, and the algorithm's performance. This conceptual contribution extends beyond Bayesian optimization to potentially influence other bound-based search methods like A* search and PAC-MDP algorithms.

The experimental results demonstrate that IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across most benchmark functions, with the exception of Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading. The authors provide a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods while using a common kernel and hyperparameter initialization approach to ensure fair comparison.

Strengths:
- The paper solves a meaningful open problem in Bayesian optimization with a theoretically sound and novel approach
- The exponential convergence result is a significant theoretical advance over previous polynomial bounds
- The conceptual framework (infinite-metric exploration) provides valuable theoretical insight applicable beyond the specific algorithm
- The paper demonstrates practical effectiveness through comprehensive experiments
- The authors carefully analyze the theoretical implications of their approach (Remarks 1-3)

Weaknesses:
- The theoretical analysis is somewhat dense, which may make it challenging to grasp for some readers
- The paper could provide more intuitive explanation of why the infinite-metric approach outperforms single-bound methods
- The discussion of high-dimensional scalability (mentioned in Remark 1) could be expanded
- The experimental section could more thoroughly analyze when IMGPO might underperform

I find this paper to be highly innovative and conceptually rich. While the methodological details could be clarified in places, the core idea of "infinite-metric exploration" represents a bold theoretical advance that significantly changes how we think about bound-based optimization. The exponential convergence result is particularly impressive, as it resolves a long-standing open problem in the field.

The paper's contribution is not merely incremental but represents a fundamental shift in how we approach the trade-off between exploration and exploitation in Bayesian optimization. The authors successfully bridge the theoretical gap between methods that use a single bound (like GP-UCB) and those that consider multiple bounds (like DIRECT/SOO), providing a unified framework that achieves exponential convergence.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. The authors have provided a new conceptual lens through which we can view Bayesian optimization, and their algorithm achieves a theoretically superior convergence rate that is also practically effective.

The paper meets all the quality criteria: it is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis, clearly written (with some dense sections), original (introducing the infinite-metric exploration concept), and significant (solving an open problem with exponential convergence).

I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a novel and important direction for Bayesian optimization research that will likely inspire further work in the field. The exponential convergence result is particularly compelling, and the authors' theoretical framework provides valuable insights that extend beyond their specific algorithm.
