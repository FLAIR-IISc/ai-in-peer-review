

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Gaussian Process-based global optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence rate without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors address a key limitation in previous work where de Freitas et al. [1] achieved exponential convergence but relied on δ-cover sampling, while Wang et al. [2] proposed BaMSOO that achieved polynomial regret bounds. The paper's main contribution is a theoretically grounded method that leverages the existence of an unknown semi-metric bound (Assumption 1) to achieve exponential convergence.

The authors build upon the foundation of bound-based search methods (including Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and PAC-MDP algorithms) by proposing a strategy that considers infinitely many possible upper bounds rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior. The key insight is that even when the GP-estimated bound is not tight, the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the function's continuity at the global optimizer can be exploited. The IMGPO algorithm uses hierarchical partitioning with a novel selection procedure that simultaneously considers both the GP-based bound and the unknown semi-metric bound.

The paper demonstrates technical soundness through Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 under Assumptions 1 and 2. The authors provide detailed analysis of how the tightness of the GP-estimated bound affects the regret (Remarks 1-3), offering valuable theoretical insights beyond the main result. The experimental section compares IMGPO with SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight benchmark functions, showing consistent improvements for most test cases.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a helpful figure (Figure 1) illustrating the algorithm's operation and detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The authors properly contextualize their work against related methods, particularly BaMSOO [2] and SOO [18], and clearly articulate how their approach differs. The supplementary material provides additional theoretical results, including an abstract regret bound for different division procedures.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contribution is significant: IMGPO achieves exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure, addressing a key open problem identified in [2].
2. The analysis provides valuable insights into why considering infinitely many bounds can be beneficial (Remarks 1-3), extending the understanding of bound-based search methods.
3. The experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements over baselines across multiple benchmark functions, supporting the theoretical claims.
4. The paper is technically sound with clear assumptions and well-motivated theoretical analysis.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims "exponential convergence" but doesn't explicitly clarify that the bound is exponential in the number of function evaluations (N+Ngp), which is standard but could be more explicit.
2. The scalability challenge for high-dimensional problems (λ approaching 1 as D increases) is acknowledged but not adequately addressed. The paper mentions "one strategy for addressing this problem would be to leverage additional assumptions," but doesn't explore how this could be implemented practically.
3. The paper states that "the use of GP reduces the regret bound by increasing Ngp and decreasing ρ̄t, but may potentially increase the bound by increasing Ξn ≤ Ξ," yet doesn't provide guidance on how to optimally balance these effects in practice.
4. The 1000-dimensional Sin2 experiment uses REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine, but lacks sufficient detail on how this combination was implemented, potentially limiting reproducibility.

Originality assessment: The paper makes a clear contribution by being the first GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The idea of considering infinitely many bounds (rather than a single bound) represents a novel perspective on bound-based optimization. While the concept of considering multiple bounds was previously explored in DIRECT [19] and SOO [18], the paper's adaptation to GP-based optimization with theoretical guarantees is novel. The authors properly cite related work and clearly differentiate their approach.

Significance assessment: The paper addresses a practical limitation in Bayesian optimization (the need for δ-cover sampling) while achieving a theoretically superior regret bound. The theoretical framework provided (Remarks 1-3) offers valuable insights that could be applied to other bound-based methods beyond Bayesian optimization. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, suggesting practical utility for real-world applications.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- First method to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Theoretical analysis provides valuable insights beyond just the regret bound
- Clear experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions
- Well-written and well-organized paper

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of high-dimensional scalability challenges
- Some trade-offs between GP usage and regret bound aren't fully explored
- Insufficient detail on the 1000-dimensional experiment implementation

Overall, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with clear practical implications. The exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling procedure addresses a significant limitation in previous work, and the theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the design of bound-based optimization methods. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The identified weaknesses are important but do not undermine the core contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization and is well-suited for NeurIPS.
