This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which aims to achieve an exponential convergence rate without requiring an auxiliary optimization of an acquisition function or the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The core idea is to integrate a Gaussian Process Upper Confidence Bound (GP-UCB) criterion into a hierarchical partitioning scheme reminiscent of the SOO algorithm. Unlike standard GP-UCB which relies on a single bound, IMGPO implicitly considers an infinite set of potential upper bounds corresponding to an unknown semi-metric around the global optimum. The GP-UCB information is used not for direct selection, but to prune branches of the search tree that are provably suboptimal. The authors provide a theoretical analysis culminating in an exponential regret bound, which improves upon the polynomial bounds of related algorithms like BaMSOO. Experiments on several benchmark functions suggest that IMGPO is competitive with or superior to existing methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  The main contribution is the novel synthesis of two powerful but distinct approaches to global optimization: the partitioning-based strategy of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO, and the probabilistic modeling of Bayesian optimization. This combination leads to an algorithm that cleverly uses the GP to accelerate the search without needing to solve the difficult subproblem of maximizing the acquisition function.
2.  The paper addresses a significant open problem identified in prior work [2]: achieving an exponential convergence rate for a GP-based method without impractical sampling assumptions. The theoretical result (Theorem 1), if sound, represents a substantial advance in the theory of Bayesian optimization.
3.  The experimental evaluation appears promising. The algorithm demonstrates strong performance across a variety of test functions, and the authors are transparent about a case where the method underperforms due to a misleading GP prior.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, technical details, and theoretical claims, which preclude a recommendation for acceptance in its current form.

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm:** The description of the algorithm is confusing and lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.
    *   The textual description in Section 3.2 is difficult to parse and relies heavily on a "Figure 1," which is not included in the manuscript.
    *   The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 contains several obscure or poorly justified steps. For instance, the mechanism for computing the max UCB `z(h, i*h)` over a "fully expanded tree" (Line 22) is not clearly explained and seems computationally intractable, even with the `Ξmax` limit. The logic of the `break` statement on Line 24 is also unclear; it suggests that rejecting a candidate at one depth level prematurely terminates the selection process for all finer levels in that iteration.
    *   The adaptive update rule for `Ξ` on Line 38 appears ad-hoc, with no justification provided for the specific update values (`+22`, `-2⁻¹`).

2.  **Flawed Theoretical Foundation:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, contains a critical flaw that undermines the paper's central claim.
    *   The regret is bounded as a function of `N + Ngp`, where `N` is the number of expensive function evaluations and `Ngp` is the number of cheap "GP-based" placeholder values used. This formulation incorrectly equates the cost of a true function evaluation with the cost of a UCB computation. The central premise of Bayesian optimization is that `f(x)` is expensive to evaluate while the model is cheap. A meaningful regret bound must be a function of `N` alone. As presented, the theorem does not properly characterize the algorithm's efficiency.
    *   The definition of the average infinite-metric exploration loss, `ρ̄t`, is non-standard (a maximum of running averages). The rationale for this specific definition over a simple average is not provided.
    *   The specific form chosen for `ςM` requires a citation or derivation, as it is a critical component for the high-probability bound to hold.

3.  **Technical Inaccuracies and Lack of Polish:** The manuscript suffers from a general lack of care in its preparation.
    *   There is a significant notational error in the definition of the covariance function in Section 2: `E[(f(x)−m(x))(f(x′)−m(x′))T ]`. The transpose `T` is meaningless for a scalar function `f`.
    *   Multiple figures and tables referenced in the text (Figure 1, Figure 2, Table 1) are missing, making it impossible to fully evaluate the algorithm's behavior and experimental results.
    *   The paper contains numerous typographical and formatting errors (e.g., "forth diagram," inconsistent use of `arg max`, incomplete reference formatting). While minor individually, their sheer number detracts from the paper's scientific credibility.

4.  **Questionable Experimental Comparison:** The setup for the baseline GP-PI and GP-EI algorithms is unconventional. The use of "the SOO algorithm ... to solve the auxiliary optimization" is highly unusual and likely inefficient, potentially presenting an unfair comparison that favors the proposed method.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the paper presents a genuinely novel and promising direction for Bayesian optimization, the execution is severely lacking. The opaque algorithm description, the critically flawed regret bound in Theorem 1, and the pervasive lack of polish make the work unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should undertake a major revision to: (1) provide a clear, unambiguous, and well-motivated description of the algorithm; (2) re-derive the theoretical bounds strictly in terms of `N`, the number of true function evaluations; and (3) thoroughly proofread and complete the manuscript, including all figures, tables, and properly formatted references. The core idea is strong, but the paper requires significant work to meet the technical and presentational standards of the conference.
