Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses global optimization of a deterministic black-box function over a bounded domain using Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes (GPs). It proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a hierarchical partitioning algorithm that combines (i) optimism via GP upper confidence bounds (UCB) and (ii) a nonparametric “infinite-metric” search that does not commit to a single Lipschitz/Hölder bound. The algorithm selects and splits hyperrectangles using a three-step screening: best-center among same-size cells; dominance over larger cells (leveraging an unknown semi-metric bound around the optimum); and a GP-UCB-based check to avoid exploring cells that are provably suboptimal. It also performs “lazy” GP-based surrogate evaluations to defer expensive function calls.

The main analysis assumes the existence of an unknown semi-metric ` that upper-bounds suboptimality at the global optimizer (Assumption 1) and a Hölder condition on ` with parameters (L, α, p) (Assumption 2). Under these, the authors prove a simple regret bound rN = O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, where N is the number of true function evaluations and Ngp counts deferred “GP-based” center values used in the search. The bound depends on a newly introduced “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt, which measures how many rectangles are expanded per iteration. The paper argues that using GP-UCB reduces ρ̄t (the average of ρt) and thus improves constants in the exponential rate, and also discusses pros/cons of relying on a single, possibly loose bound versus considering infinitely many potential bounds.

Empirically, IMGPO is compared to SOO [18], BaMSOO [2], and GP-EI/PI [3] on standard benchmarks (1–6D), a 1000D REMBO setup [14], and functions from the literature. With an isotropic Matérn-5/2 kernel and empirical Bayes hyperparameter tuning, IMGPO typically achieves lower regret with fewer evaluations; a code link is provided.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Positive:
  - The algorithmic idea of combining hierarchical partitioning with GP-UCB screening and lazy evaluations is clearly outlined and plausibly effective.
  - The analysis framework introduces ρt and cleanly separates the effects of GP-based bounding, partitioning depth, and unknown smoothness parameters.
  - The regret expression is explicit in L, α, p, dimension D, and ρ̄t, clarifying the dependence on function regularity and search overhead.

- Concerns:
  - Validity of UCB coverage. The property f(x) ≤ μ(x|D) + ςσ(x|D) with high probability typically requires either a correct GP prior (Bayesian assumption that f is a GP sample) or that f lies in an RKHS with bounded norm together with a specific βt schedule (e.g., Srinivas et al., 2010). The paper sets ςM = sqrt(log(π^2 M^2/(12η))) but does not state assumptions on f beyond Hölder continuity at x∗, nor does it connect ςM to an RKHS norm bound. Moreover, empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates generally break nominal GP coverage. The analysis needs a precise assumption under which the event “f(x) ≤ U(x|D) for all UCB calls” holds with probability ≥ 1−η.
  - Dependence on ρ̄t without a nontrivial bound. The exponential rate constant uses ρ̄t, but no general upper bound on ρ̄t is proved; remarks discuss best/worst cases qualitatively. As a result, the theorem certifies an exponential form but the exponent may be arbitrarily small if ρ̄t grows.
  - Use of Ngp in the exponent. The regret bound is expressed in terms of N + Ngp, where Ngp counts GP-based “virtual” center values. Since regret is measured after N true evaluations, incorporating Ngp into the exponent inflates the apparent evaluation efficiency; a bound purely in terms of N would be more informative and comparable to prior work.
  - Minor but important clarity issue: λ is written as “3 − α2CDρ̄t,” which reads as a subtraction; given the preceding derivation exp(−α(·) ln 3), λ should be 3 to the power of a negative constant (e.g., λ = 3−α/(2CDρ̄t)). This should be fixed to avoid confusion.
  - The analysis focuses on deterministic functions; no extension to noisy settings is provided, limiting scope relative to much of the BO literature.

Clarity
- The high-level algorithm and pseudocode are described in detail, with a helpful running example.
- However, there are multiple notation/typographical issues (e.g., λ expression, mismatched indices, footnote marker in Remark 1) that impede readability.
- The probabilistic setting is not clearly defined (probability over what randomness? GP prior? hyperparameter estimation?), which is crucial for the main guarantee.
- Computational complexity of step (iii) is only cursorily discussed via Ξmax; a more explicit cost analysis would help.

Originality
- The “infinite-metric” perspective, introduction of the exploration loss ρt, and the specific way GP-UCB is used to vet partitions without δ-cover sampling are novel within GP-based BO. The closest prior, BaMSOO [2], achieves only polynomial rates; the attempt to obtain exponential rates without δ-covers is new and valuable if the assumptions are clarified.

Significance
- If the exponential rate can be made rigorous under standard assumptions (e.g., RKHS norm bound) and expressed in terms of N alone, this would be a meaningful advance. The empirical results are promising and the algorithm appears practical. However, the current theoretical gaps (UCB validity and ρ̄t) weaken the significance of the claimed rate.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear and interesting algorithm that blends hierarchical partitioning with GP screening and lazy evaluation; publicly available code.
- New analysis elements (ρt) that may inform future designs and clarify the role of bound tightness.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines on a range of benchmarks using standard kernels and hyperparameter learning.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- The key probabilistic assumption ensuring GP-UCB coverage is not stated or justified; empirical Bayes updates further complicate coverage.
- Exponential rate constant depends on an unspecified quantity ρ̄t; Ngp in the exponent makes comparison to prior sample-complexity results problematic.
- Notational and typographical issues obscure parts of the theory (e.g., λ).
- No treatment of noisy observations; limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability beyond noting degradation with D.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please state explicitly the assumption under which f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holds for all UCB queries with probability ≥ 1−η. Is f assumed to be a sample from the GP prior, or to lie in an RKHS with bounded norm? How do empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates affect this guarantee?
- Can you provide a bound on ρ̄t (even under additional assumptions), or a condition that ensures N/ρ̄t = Ω(N) with high probability?
- Can the main regret bound be restated in terms of N only, possibly by upper-bounding Ngp as a function of N and Ξmax?
- Please correct the expression for λ and clean up typographical issues in Section 4 and the remarks.
- Include a more detailed time/space complexity analysis of step (iii) as a function of D, Ξmax, and the number of rectangles, and report wall-clock comparisons consistently.
- Consider adding results with GP-UCB as a baseline, and ablations on Ξmax and the impact of mis-specified kernels; discuss extensions to noisy settings.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a promising algorithmic contribution and an insightful analysis framework, with strong empirical performance. However, the theoretical guarantee as written relies on an unstated coverage assumption for GP-UCB and on algorithm-dependent quantities without bounds, and the use of Ngp in the exponent complicates interpretation. Clarifying and tightening these aspects would substantially strengthen the contribution.
