Summary
The paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box global optimization. IMGPO combines hierarchical partitioning (as in SOO/DOO/DIRECT) with a GP posterior used to compute UCBs that prune evaluations. The key conceptual move is to “not commit” to a single smoothness/Lipschitz metric: instead, the method selects intervals using rules that are consistent with any semi-metric satisfying a mild Hölder-type assumption at a global optimizer, while using the GP-UCB only to avoid unnecessary function evaluations. The authors introduce an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt to quantify how much extra exploration is incurred by not committing to a bound. They claim a simple-regret bound of the form rN = O(λN+Ngp) with λ<1 (exponential rate) without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling, and present experiments on standard test functions comparing to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI/EI.

Relation to prior work
- GP-UCB and de Freitas et al. (δ-cover) established exponential rates under strong sampling assumptions; BaMSOO integrates SOO with GP-UCB but achieves only polynomial rates. IMGPO aims to recover exponential rates without δ-cover by blending partition-based optimism (Munos’ DOO/SOO) with GP-UCB for pruning.
- The idea of exploring over “many possible” bounds traces back to DIRECT; SOO formalized near-optimality-dimension guarantees without a known Lipschitz constant. IMGPO’s “infinite-metric” framing fits this lineage but tries to use the GP to reduce exploration loss.
- The assumed Hölder-type regularity (Assumption 2) is in the same spirit as DOO/SOO; the paper claims the bound accommodates the best unknown (L,α,p).

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing hybrid of SOO/DIRECT-style partitioning with GP-based pruning, avoiding auxiliary global optimization over acquisition functions.
- Attempts to formalize when GP-UCB information is helpful even if the GP bound is not tight, via ρt and Ngp.
- Claims exponential convergence without δ-cover, which, if correct and practically realizable, would be a notable theoretical advance.
- Code is promised; some ablation around Ξmax is discussed.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality and theoretical soundness
- The key theorem’s statement and notation have issues. For example, λ is written as “λ = 3 − α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1”, which seems incorrect; from the displayed exponential form, it appears λ should be 3^{-α/(2CDρ̄t)}. There are several similar typographical or definitional inconsistencies (e.g., ρτ vs ρt, β definition).
- The “exponential” rate is in N+Ngp, where Ngp counts uses of the GP to set UCB placeholders. Because the algorithm can generate many GP UCB computations per iteration, this substantially weakens the claim when measured against the standard metric (number of function evaluations N). The paper should provide a regret bound purely in terms of N (or at least control Ngp relative to N) to make the claim comparable to prior art.
- The bound depends on ρ̄t and Ξn, algorithm-dependent quantities not controlled in terms of problem properties (e.g., near-optimality dimension). Without a priori bounds on ρ̄t and Ξn, the “exponential” rate can become vacuous (λ→1), especially as D grows, which the authors acknowledge. The remarks are qualitative and do not close this gap.
- Assumption 1–2 effectively require Hölder continuity near the optimum, similar in spirit to DOO/SOO; it is not clear that they are weaker than standard assumptions or that IMGPO’s analysis yields strictly stronger guarantees under comparable conditions.

Empirical validation
- The experimental suite is limited and does not include key baselines: DIRECT and GP-UCB are notably absent; DOO is not compared. Since IMGPO builds on DIRECT/SOO principles, including those baselines is necessary.
- The GP baselines are potentially handicapped: a single kernel choice and blind hyperparameter initialization may favor partitioning methods; no sensitivity analysis over kernels/hyperparameters is shown. UCB-parameter selection (η=0.05) is fixed; when empirical Bayes is used, the theoretical high-probability UCB property is not guaranteed.
- Comparisons mix deterministic (IMGPO/SOO/BaMSOO) and stochastic (GP-PI/EI) procedures with different initialization protocols; fairness and variance handling need more care.
- No results on noisy objectives (even though many BO applications are noisy), and the only “high-dimensional” case uses REMBO, making IMGPO a subroutine rather than a standalone high-D method.
- Computation: step (iii) requires expanding subtrees up to depth ξ; theoretical and empirical computational complexity are not characterized. While a Ξmax cap is introduced, the trade-off vs. regret is not systematically evaluated.

Clarity and reproducibility
- Several notation and presentation issues impede understanding (typos in theorem, inconsistent symbols, reliance on a figure/pseudocode that is hard to parse). The domain restriction to “square” is ad hoc. The supplementary proofs are referenced but not sketched.
- Important constants (C, β) and their roles are not clearly explained; the conditions under which λ is meaningfully less than 1 are not made transparent.

Originality
- The “infinite-metric” framing and the way GP-UCB is used to avoid evaluations are interesting. However, the algorithmic components build directly on SOO/DOO/DIRECT and GP-UCB, and the novelty largely rests on the specific combination and the analysis.

Significance
- If the exponential guarantee could be made in terms of N alone, with clear conditions on ρ̄t and Ξn tied to problem complexity, the result would be significant. As written, the theoretical contribution is undermined by uncontrolled factors and typographical errors, and the empirical evidence is not strong enough to compensate.

Questions for the authors
- Please correct the theorem statement (λ) and provide a clean, self-contained statement with explicit conditions, and a sketch of the proof. How is Ngp precisely defined and bounded relative to N?
- Can you bound ρ̄t and Ξn in terms of near-optimality dimension or other standard complexity measures? Under what conditions can you guarantee λ ≤ 3^{-c/D} with a concrete c independent of runtime-tunable quantities?
- Provide regret vs. N only (not N+Ngp), and runtime vs. N for all methods. What is the worst-case and typical scaling of step (iii)?
- Add comparisons to DIRECT, DOO, and GP-UCB; include sensitivity to kernel choice/hyperparameter learning; and ablations varying Ξmax.
- Discuss and, if possible, evaluate the noisy setting.

Pros
- Interesting hybrid idea; avoids auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Attempted theoretical framework to quantify benefit of GP pruning.
- Promising empirical results on some benchmarks; code availability.

Cons
- Theoretical claims hinge on quantities not controlled; typographical errors in key theorem.
- Exponential rate is in N+Ngp, not clearly comparable to prior art.
- Limited and potentially biased empirical evaluation; missing key baselines.
- Clarity issues and incomplete complexity analysis.

Recommendation
I lean to reject in its current form. The idea is promising, but the main claims require a cleaner, more controlled theory and stronger, fairer empirical validation.

Confidence
High.
