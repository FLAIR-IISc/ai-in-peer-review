This paper presents IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel algorithm for the global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The main contribution is a Gaussian Process (GP) based method that achieves an exponential convergence rate without requiring an auxiliary optimization step for the acquisition function or an impractical sampling scheme like a δ-cover. This work positions itself as a solution to an open problem identified by previous research [1, 2], which either required impractical assumptions for exponential rates or achieved only polynomial rates with practical algorithms.

The core idea of IMGPO is an elegant synthesis of two distinct lines of research in global optimization: the bound-agnostic, hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18], and the probabilistic, model-based approach of GP-UCB. Instead of solely relying on the upper confidence bound (UCB) from the GP to guide the search, IMGPO uses a hierarchical partitioning scheme that implicitly considers an infinite family of possible semi-metrics (smoothness assumptions). The GP-UCB bound is then cleverly used as a pruning mechanism to eliminate unpromising branches in the search tree, thereby accelerating convergence. This hybrid approach allows the algorithm to benefit from the strong theoretical guarantees of partitioning methods while leveraging the data efficiency of GPs.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical result. Theorem 1 provides an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, for a practical GP-based algorithm. This represents a substantial improvement over the polynomial bounds of the most closely related practical algorithm, BaMSOO [2], and achieves the same desirable rate as the theoretical procedure of de Freitas et al. [1] but without its impractical δ-cover sampling requirement. This effectively closes an important theoretical gap in the field.

2.  **Novel and Insightful Methodology:** The fusion of hierarchical partitioning with GP-based pruning is novel and well-motivated. The concept of an "infinite-metric exploration loss" (`ρ_t`) provides a clear and formal way to understand the trade-off. The analysis in Remarks 1-3 is particularly insightful, clearly articulating how the GP serves to reduce this loss (Remark 2), why being agnostic to a specific bound can be superior to relying on a misspecified one (Remark 3), and how the algorithm maintains strong performance even if the GP provides a loose bound (Remark 1).

3.  **Practical Advantages:** Unlike standard GP-BO methods (GP-EI, GP-PI, GP-UCB), IMGPO does not require solving an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next query point. This is a significant practical advantage, as optimizing the acquisition function can be computationally expensive and problematic in its own right. The algorithm's efficiency is further enhanced by a clever scheme to defer expensive function evaluations by temporarily using UCB values, which are cheaper to compute.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are convincing. The authors compare IMGPO against a strong and appropriate set of baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI. A notable strength of the experimental design is the avoidance of hand-tuning kernels and hyperparameters for each benchmark function, which reflects a more realistic use case. IMGPO demonstrates superior or competitive performance across a range of functions with varying dimensionalities and characteristics. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO showcases a path toward scaling the approach.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity and Complexity of the Algorithm Description:** The algorithm's description is dense and challenging to parse. The interplay between the nested loops (lines 8, 19, 26 in Algorithm 1) and the multiple conditions for selecting and pruning intervals (steps i-iii) is complex. The notation `T'h+ξ(ch,i*h)` in line 21, representing a "fully expanded tree," could be explained more intuitively before its formal introduction. A more detailed walkthrough of the conditions in lines 11, 23, and 27, perhaps with a visual aid alongside the pseudocode, would greatly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Scalability and Dependence on Dimensionality:** While the exponential rate is impressive, the base of the exponent, `λ`, has a dependency on the dimension `D` (hidden within the constant `C`). Hierarchical partitioning methods are known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality, as the number of cells grows exponentially. The paper briefly acknowledges this as a "remaining challenge," but a more explicit discussion of how `C` and `ρ_t` scale with `D` would be valuable. The 1000-D experiment is encouraging, but it relies on REMBO, which leverages a low-dimensional effective subspace assumption, so it doesn't demonstrate the scalability of IMGPO itself in high dimensions.

3.  **Sensitivity to New Hyperparameters:** The algorithm removes the need for an auxiliary optimizer but introduces new hyperparameters, namely `Ξ_max` and `η`. The paper fixes `η=0.05` and `Ξ_max=22` with limited justification. While the discussion on the effect of `Ξ_max` is helpful, a more systematic sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claim of the algorithm's practicality and robustness.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. It addresses a well-defined and important open problem with a novel and technically sound solution. The theoretical result is a clear advance over the state of the art, and it is supported by a convincing set of experiments. While the presentation could be improved for clarity, the core ideas are powerful and the implications are substantial for both researchers and practitioners in global optimization. The work successfully bridges two previously separate approaches to optimization in a synergistic way, offering new insights and a high-performance algorithm.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept.**
