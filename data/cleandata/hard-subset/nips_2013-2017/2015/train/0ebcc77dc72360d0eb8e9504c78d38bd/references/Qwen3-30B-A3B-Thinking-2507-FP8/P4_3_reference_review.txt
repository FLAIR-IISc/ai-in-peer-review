

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work. The authors address an open problem identified by Wang et al. (2014) regarding creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without impractical sampling. The key innovation lies in considering infinitely many possible candidates for a semi-metric bound (Assumption 1), rather than relying on a single bound from GP. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for exponential regret (O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1), demonstrates superior empirical performance on benchmark functions compared to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, and offers a new analytical framework for understanding bound-based optimization methods.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with sound theoretical analysis. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous regret bound that improves upon the polynomial bound of BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014) and other prior work. The supplementary material contains the complete proof, and the analysis carefully considers how the tightness of the GP-UCB bound affects performance (Remarks 1-3). The authors appropriately discuss limitations, noting that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, suggesting scalability challenges. The experimental setup is comprehensive, using eight benchmark functions across dimensions 1-1000, with fair comparison using a single kernel (Matern ν=5/2) and blind hyperparameter initialization for all methods. The paper is careful to acknowledge cases where the GP prior is misleading (e.g., Rosenbrock2), demonstrating honesty in evaluation.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The algorithm is thoroughly explained with pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and a detailed figure (Figure 1) illustrating the hierarchical partitioning process. The relationship to previous work (BaMSOO, SOO, GP-UCB) is clearly articulated, and the connection between the infinite-metric exploration loss (ρ_t) and algorithm performance is well-explained. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the supplementary material provides necessary technical details. The experimental results (Figure 2) are presented effectively with appropriate metrics (log10(f(x*) - f(x+))). The writing is precise and accessible to the Bayesian optimization community.

## Originality
This work makes a significant original contribution by addressing an open problem in Bayesian optimization. The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds (rather than a single bound from GP) is novel, as evidenced by the clear distinction from previous work (e.g., GP-UCB, BaMSOO). The theoretical framework (Remarks 1-3) provides new insights into why not relying on a particular bound can be advantageous, which is a meaningful contribution to the understanding of bound-based optimization. The paper is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on Bayesian optimization (e.g., Srinivas et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2014), with the exponential convergence result being a key novelty. The connection to broader optimization methods (A* search, PAC-MDP) in the conclusion further demonstrates the significance of the theoretical perspective.

## Significance
The exponential convergence rate represents a substantial theoretical improvement over previous polynomial regret bounds in Bayesian optimization. The empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages across most benchmark functions, with IMGPO outperforming competitors in general. The paper provides a new analytical framework that could influence future work in Bayesian optimization and related bound-based methods. The potential application to higher dimensions through REMBO (as shown for the 1000-dimensional Sin2 function) demonstrates practical relevance. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation of the approach.

## Strengths
- Solves an important open problem (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling)
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with a novel regret bound
- Demonstrates superior empirical performance on multiple benchmarks
- Offers a new perspective on bound-based optimization methods
- Clear and well-structured presentation

## Weaknesses
- The scalability limitation with increasing dimensionality (λ approaching 1) is significant but only briefly acknowledged
- The performance depends on the parameter Ξmax, which requires tuning (though this is discussed)
- The paper doesn't deeply analyze the computational complexity of the hierarchical partitioning
- The "blind" kernel selection (Matern ν=5/2) might not be optimal for all functions, though this is a fair comparison approach

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Addresses a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization
- Provides the first exponential convergence result without δ-cover sampling
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified
- Empirical results consistently show improved performance
- Offers a new analytical framework that could influence future work

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The scalability limitation with increasing dimensionality is a notable practical constraint
- The algorithm's performance depends on a parameter (Ξmax) that requires tuning
- The paper doesn't fully address computational complexity

## Recommendation
The paper makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to Bayesian optimization by providing the first GP-based method with exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the work advances the field in a meaningful way. While the scalability limitation with increasing dimensionality is noted, this is a common challenge in global optimization and is appropriately acknowledged. The paper's novelty, technical quality, and significance outweigh the minor weaknesses, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
