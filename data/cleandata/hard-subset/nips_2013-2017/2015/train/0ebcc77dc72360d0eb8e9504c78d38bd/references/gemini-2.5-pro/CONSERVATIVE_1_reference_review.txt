**Summary**

This paper proposes a new algorithm for Bayesian optimization, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which aims to achieve an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret without relying on impractical sampling procedures. The method combines a Gaussian Process (GP) prior with a hierarchical partitioning scheme similar to the SOO algorithm. The core idea is to not depend solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP, which may be loose. Instead, it operates under the assumption of an unknown, tighter bound described by a semi-metric (an assumption inherited from the SOO literature). The algorithm simultaneously explores partitions at all scales, using the GP-UCB to prune branches that are provably suboptimal. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing an exponential regret bound and present experimental results comparing IMGPO to several established baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Clear Open Problem:** The paper directly tackles a well-defined open problem articulated in prior work [2]: achieving an exponential convergence rate for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm.
2.  **Theoretical Improvement:** The main theoretical contribution is an exponential regret bound, which, on its face, is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds demonstrated for related practical methods like BaMSOO.
3.  **Novel Algorithmic Approach:** The synthesis of optimistic partitioning methods (like SOO) with the probabilistic bounds of GP-UCB is a novel direction. Using the GP not as the sole driver of exploration but as a mechanism to prune the search of an optimistic algorithm is an interesting concept.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity and Lack of Robustness:** The proposed IMGPO algorithm is significantly more complex than the established methods it seeks to improve upon. The procedure involves a multi-step filtering process within each iteration (steps i-v) and introduces new, non-intuitive hyperparameters and ad-hoc update rules. Specifically, the parameter `Ξmax` and the adaptive rule for `Ξ` (line 38) appear arbitrary and lack clear justification. The paper offers no practical guidance on setting `Ξmax`, yet acknowledges that performance on several test functions could be improved by increasing it, at the cost of more computation. This suggests that the reported results are not necessarily optimal and that the algorithm's performance is sensitive to a parameter that a practitioner would not know how to set. This lack of robustness and reliance on new tuning parameters is a significant drawback compared to more established methods.

2.  **Questionable Rigor of the Theoretical Bound:** While the paper claims an exponential convergence rate of `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, this result warrants careful scrutiny. The base of the exponent, `λ`, is a function of `ρ̄t`, the "infinite-metric exploration loss." This quantity is not a fundamental property of the optimization problem but rather a measure of the algorithm's own exploratory behavior in a given run. As acknowledged in Remark 1, if the GP prior is not well-suited to the objective function (a common occurrence in practice), `ρ̄t` can grow, and the convergence rate can degrade substantially. This makes the "exponential" claim conditional and less impactful than it first appears. A truly rigorous analysis would provide a clearer characterization of the behavior of `ρ̄t` or bound it in terms of more fundamental problem properties. As it stands, the bound feels somewhat self-referential.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is not thorough enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
    *   The decision to use a single "blindly initialized" set of GP hyperparameters for all methods is problematic. While framed as a practical choice, it may disproportionately penalize the baseline algorithms (BaMSOO, GP-EI, GP-PI), whose performance can be sensitive to initialization. A more rigorous comparison would involve either a more robust initialization protocol or a sensitivity analysis.
    *   The experiments fail to investigate the impact of the crucial new hyperparameter, `Ξmax`. Without a sensitivity analysis, it is impossible to assess the trade-off between performance and computation or to understand how critical its setting is to the algorithm's success.
    *   The claims of scalability are unsubstantiated. The majority of the benchmarks are low-dimensional. The single high-dimensional experiment uses REMBO as a wrapper, which is a dimensionality reduction technique. Therefore, the experiment does not test IMGPO in a high-dimensional setting but rather its effectiveness as a subroutine for a low-dimensional problem. Furthermore, the paper’s own analysis notes that `λ` approaches 1 as the dimension `D` increases, suggesting the method suffers from the curse of dimensionality, a critical point that is not addressed empirically.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and recognized open problem in Bayesian optimization theory.
*   It provides a novel algorithm that combines two distinct lines of research in global optimization.
*   The main theorem presents an exponential regret bound, which is a theoretical improvement over prior work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The proposed algorithm is overly complex and introduces new, poorly motivated hyperparameters (`Ξmax`), making it less practical and robust than established methods.
*   The headline theoretical result (exponential convergence) is qualified by its dependence on an algorithm-specific term (`ρ̄t`), making its practical implications unclear.
*   The experimental evidence is not sufficiently rigorous, with concerns about the fairness of the baseline comparisons and a lack of sensitivity analysis for new hyperparameters.
*   Claims regarding scalability to high dimensions are not supported by the experiments or the theory.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an interesting theoretical question, the proposed solution is a complex heuristic with a theoretical guarantee of questionable practical significance. The work, in its current state, does not provide sufficient evidence to justify adopting this new, complicated method over well-established and better-understood baselines. The experimental validation is not rigorous enough to support the claims of superior performance.
