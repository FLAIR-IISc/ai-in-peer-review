

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization) that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors argue that their method improves upon previous work by considering "infinitely many possible candidates of the bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the Gaussian process (GP) prior. They provide theoretical regret bounds and experimental comparisons against several baselines including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

## Strengths
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a detailed description of the algorithm and its relationship to previous work. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, and the authors acknowledge limitations in their approach (e.g., scalability issues with high dimensions). The experimental results show consistent improvements over baselines on multiple benchmark functions, which is a positive empirical contribution.

## Weaknesses

### Theoretical Claims
The paper's most significant claim is that IMGPO achieves exponential convergence (O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1) without δ-cover sampling. However, this claim is problematic for several reasons:

1. The regret bound in Theorem 1 depends on ρ̄t, which is defined as the maximum of averages of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt) over iterations. The paper acknowledges that λ can get close to 1 as dimension D increases, which means the convergence rate is not truly exponential for high-dimensional problems. This is a critical limitation that undermines the main theoretical claim.

2. The paper does not provide a rigorous comparison with the only method that actually achieves exponential convergence (GP-UCB with δ-cover sampling, as cited in [1]), which is the method they claim to improve upon. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if their method truly achieves exponential convergence without the impractical sampling procedure.

3. The theoretical analysis relies on Assumption 2, which posits that the semi-metric ` satisfies `(x', x) ≤ L||x' − x||^α_p. This is a strong assumption that may not hold in practice, and the paper does not provide evidence that this assumption is reasonable for real-world optimization problems.

### Experimental Validation
The experimental validation has several shortcomings:

1. The paper uses a fixed Ξmax = 22 for all experiments, but acknowledges that increasing this parameter can improve performance at the cost of extra CPU time. This parameter sensitivity weakens the claim that the method is robust and easy to use.

2. The comparison does not include GP-UCB with δ-cover sampling, which is the only method known to achieve exponential convergence (per the authors' own citation of [1]). Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the claimed exponential convergence is actually achieved.

3. The paper claims to use "blindly initialized" hyperparameters for all algorithms, but doesn't provide evidence that these initializations were truly unbiased or representative of typical practice.

### Methodological Soundness
The paper's claim that "the use of GP reduces the regret bound" is not well-supported by the analysis or experiments. The authors acknowledge in Remark 2 that GP may potentially increase the bound by increasing Ξn ≤ Ξ, suggesting that the benefit of GP is not guaranteed. This contradicts the paper's main claim about the advantage of their method.

The paper also claims to "maintain an exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling procedure," but their algorithm still requires solving an additional non-convex optimization problem (as stated in Section 2), which contradicts their claim of avoiding auxiliary optimization.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong theoretical claim that is not sufficiently supported by the analysis or experiments. The exponential convergence rate is not rigorously demonstrated to be achievable in practice, especially for higher-dimensional problems where the paper acknowledges λ approaches 1. The lack of comparison with the only method that actually achieves exponential convergence (GP-UCB with δ-cover sampling) is a critical flaw.

While the experimental results show modest improvements over baselines, these improvements do not justify the paper's main theoretical claim. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence rate in practice, a comparison with the δ-cover sampling method, and a clearer demonstration of why considering infinitely many bounds provides a practical advantage over standard GP-based methods.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. The paper makes an extraordinary claim (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling) that is not sufficiently supported by the theoretical analysis or experimental evidence. The claims are overstated, and the paper fails to provide the necessary evidence to justify its main contribution. Bayesian optimization is a well-established field, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that it has made a significant theoretical advance. The authors should either provide stronger theoretical justification for their exponential convergence claim or revise their claims to be more modest.
