

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work. The authors address a key limitation in existing GP-based optimization methods, where de Freitas et al. [1] established exponential convergence but relied on impractical sampling, while Wang et al. [2] developed BaMSOO with polynomial regret. The paper builds on the DIRECT algorithm [19] and SOO [18] but introduces a theoretical framework for considering infinitely many possible bounds encoded in an unknown semi-metric rather than relying on a single bound from GP. This represents a significant theoretical advance in the Bayesian optimization literature, particularly relevant to the NIPS community's focus on practical and theoretically sound optimization methods.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with Theorem 1, establishing an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1. This is the first GP-based method to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling, directly addressing a long-standing open problem identified by Wang et al. [2].

2. **Novel Insight**: The core insight—leveraging all possible bounds encoded in an unknown semi-metric rather than relying on a single GP-based bound—is conceptually elegant and well-justified through detailed theoretical analysis (Remarks 1-3). This provides a new perspective on bound-based optimization methods.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments consistently demonstrate superior performance of IMGPO over multiple baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) across eight different benchmark functions. The results in Figure 2 provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims.

4. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the algorithm (Figure 1), detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1), and thorough theoretical analysis. The supplementary material appears to provide additional rigor for the proofs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity**: While the paper shows IMGPO outperforms other methods in terms of regret, it lacks a detailed analysis of computational complexity. The paper mentions that IMGPO is "much faster than traditional GP optimization methods" but doesn't quantify this or compare the computational cost per iteration with other methods. This is particularly important for a method that involves hierarchical partitioning.

2. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality D increases (Remark 1), suggesting scalability challenges. However, it doesn't provide concrete evidence of how the algorithm performs on high-dimensional problems beyond the 1000-dimensional embedding example. The connection between the 1000D example and practical high-dimensional problems needs further clarification.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions that Ξmax affects performance (Section 5) but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how to choose Ξmax in practice. The recommendation to fix Ξmax=22 appears arbitrary without justification or sensitivity analysis.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper references "GP-UCB" but doesn't compare with more recent GP-based optimization methods like those in [20, 25] that address high-dimensional optimization. This weakens the claim of novelty in the context of recent NIPS literature.

## Constructive Suggestions

1. **Expand Computational Analysis**: Include a table comparing computational complexity (time per iteration, memory requirements) across methods, particularly for the hierarchical partitioning procedure in IMGPO versus BaMSOO's SOO implementation.

2. **Address Scalability**: Provide additional experiments on moderate-dimensional problems (e.g., D=10, 20) to better understand the scalability limitations. Discuss how the algorithm might be combined with dimensionality reduction techniques for higher-dimensional problems.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis**: Add a section analyzing how Ξmax affects performance across different problem types, including a recommendation for how to choose this parameter in practice.

4. **Clarify High-Dimensional Connection**: Expand on how the 1000D example relates to practical high-dimensional problems, and discuss how IMGPO might be integrated with other high-dimensional optimization techniques like REMBO [14] beyond the single embedding example.

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a well-known limitation in Bayesian optimization. The paper makes a clear and novel contribution to the field with a theoretically sound algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without the impractical δ-cover sampling. The experimental results provide good empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

While the paper has some weaknesses regarding computational complexity analysis and scalability, these are primarily presentation issues rather than fundamental flaws. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization with a theoretically rigorous and practically promising method. The core insight about considering all possible bounds encoded in an unknown semi-metric is both novel and valuable, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its benefits. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria—quality (sound theory and experiments), clarity (well-written with good pseudocode), originality (novel approach), and significance (addresses an important open problem with practical implications).
