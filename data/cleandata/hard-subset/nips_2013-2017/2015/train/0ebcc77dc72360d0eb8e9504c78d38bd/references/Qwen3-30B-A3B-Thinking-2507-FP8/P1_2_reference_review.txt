

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous methods. The authors address an important open problem identified by Wang et al. [2]: creating a GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling procedure. The key innovation is to consider an infinite number of possible upper bounds (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound provided by the Gaussian process (GP) prior. The paper establishes a theoretical exponential regret bound (Theorem 1) and demonstrates through experiments that IMGPO outperforms existing methods like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across multiple benchmark functions.

The work relates to several key contributions in the literature: de Freitas et al. [1] established exponential convergence but required δ-cover sampling, Wang et al. [2] combined GP-UCB with SOO (BaMSOO) but achieved only polynomial regret, and Munos [18] introduced SOO with a finite regret bound. The paper builds on these works while introducing a fundamentally different approach to bounding the optimization problem.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper solves an important open problem in Bayesian optimization by providing the first GP-based method with exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling. Theorem 1 establishes a regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which is a substantial improvement over previous polynomial bounds.

2. **Novel Conceptual Approach**: The idea of considering an infinite number of possible bounds (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound is conceptually novel and well-motivated. The authors provide a compelling theoretical explanation (in Remarks 1-3) for why this approach is beneficial, especially when the GP's bound is not tight.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experimental results are comprehensive and well-designed, comparing IMGPO with multiple relevant baselines across eight different objective functions with varying dimensions (1 to 1000). IMGPO consistently outperforms the baselines, with the exception of Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading.

4. **Clear Discussion of Limitations**: The authors appropriately discuss the limitations of their approach, including the scalability issue with increasing dimensionality (Remark 1) and the trade-off between using GP to reduce ρ̄t and increasing Ξn (Remark 2).

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Details**: While the main result is presented, some technical details of the proof (particularly the relationship between the semi-metric ` and the GP's UCB) are not fully elaborated in the main paper. The authors mention these details are in the supplementary material, but the main paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of this relationship.

2. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 as dimension D increases, suggesting scalability challenges. While this is noted, the authors don't provide a concrete strategy for addressing this issue, which is important for practical applications. The paper mentions "leveraging additional assumptions such as those in [14, 20]" but doesn't elaborate on how this could be practically implemented.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper fixes Ξmax to 22 in experiments, but the impact of this choice on performance is not thoroughly explored. The authors mention that increasing Ξmax can improve performance for some functions at the cost of extra CPU time, but this needs more quantitative analysis.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with BaMSOO and other methods, but doesn't explicitly compare with more recent Bayesian optimization methods that might have addressed similar challenges, such as those in Kandasamy et al. (2015) or Wang et al. (2017). This would strengthen the position of the paper in the current literature.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach. The paper is a complete work, not merely a position paper. The supplementary material contains the proofs, making the paper a complete scientific contribution.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, motivation, and algorithm description are clear. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is helpful for understanding the algorithm. However, some technical details (like the exact relationship between the semi-metric and the GP's UCB) could be clarified for readers less familiar with the field. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt relates to the algorithm's practical performance.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a significant original contribution by introducing the concept of considering an infinite number of possible bounds in Bayesian optimization. This is a novel departure from previous work like GP-UCB and BaMSOO, which rely on a single bound. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work and provide a compelling theoretical justification for why this approach is beneficial. The paper's main idea is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers, particularly the works by Wang et al. [2] and de Freitas et al. [1].

## Significance Assessment

The paper addresses an important open problem in Bayesian optimization with significant theoretical and practical implications. Achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling is a substantial improvement over previous methods. The results have the potential to influence future research in Bayesian optimization and related areas. The paper's framework for understanding when not relying on a single bound is beneficial provides valuable theoretical insights for the broader optimization community. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Provide more detailed explanation of the relationship between the semi-metric ` in Assumption 1 and the GP's UCB bound. How exactly does the GP help in reducing the number of candidates?

2. Expand on the scalability concerns. While the paper acknowledges that λ approaches 1 with increasing D, it would be helpful to discuss potential strategies for addressing this issue (e.g., leveraging additional assumptions like those in Kandasamy et al. (2015)).

3. Include a more thorough exploration of the impact of Ξmax on performance. The current paper only mentions that increasing Ξmax can improve performance for some functions, but doesn't quantify this effect.

4. Consider adding a comparison with more recent Bayesian optimization methods that might have addressed similar challenges, such as those in Kandasamy et al. (2015) or Wang et al. (2017).

5. Clarify the relationship between ρ̄t and the algorithm's practical performance. How does the choice of Ξmax affect ρ̄t, and how does this impact the regret bound?

## Summary of Arguments for and Against Acceptance

*Arguments for Acceptance:*
- First GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Novel conceptual approach of considering infinite number of bounds
- Strong theoretical results with clear regret bound
- Comprehensive experimental validation showing consistent improvement
- Clear explanation of the theoretical benefits of the approach
- Well-written and well-organized paper

*Arguments against Acceptance:*
- Some theoretical details not fully elaborated in main paper
- Scalability concerns with increasing dimensionality not fully addressed
- Impact of Ξmax parameter not thoroughly explored
- Comparison with more recent Bayesian optimization methods is limited

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are novel and important, and the experimental validation is solid. The paper addresses an important open problem in the field with a novel and well-motivated approach. While there are some areas for improvement, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's significant contributions. The paper would be of interest to the NIPS community and would be a valuable addition to the conference proceedings.

## Recommendation

Accept

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with a novel theoretical approach that solves an important open problem. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in a camera-ready version but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.
