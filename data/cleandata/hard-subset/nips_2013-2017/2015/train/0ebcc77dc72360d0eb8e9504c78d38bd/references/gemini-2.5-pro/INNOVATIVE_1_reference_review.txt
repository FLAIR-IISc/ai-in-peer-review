### Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for Bayesian optimization, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which aims to achieve an exponential convergence rate without relying on impractical sampling schemes. The central problem addressed is a known open question in the field: how to bridge the theoretical gap between GP-based methods with strong, practical performance but only polynomial regret bounds (like BaMSOO), and those with exponential bounds that depend on computationally prohibitive assumptions (like the δ-cover sampling required by de Freitas et al.).

The core idea of IMGPO is a conceptually bold synthesis of two distinct lines of work in global optimization. It combines the hierarchical, partitioning-based approach of algorithms like SOO, which optimistically search based on an *unknown* semi-metric, with the probabilistic upper confidence bounds (UCBs) derived from a Gaussian Process posterior. Crucially, instead of relying solely on the GP's UCB to guide the search, IMGPO uses the UCB primarily as a pruning mechanism to eliminate regions that are provably suboptimal. The main search driver remains the optimistic, multi-scale exploration characteristic of SOO, which implicitly considers an infinite family of possible function smoothness profiles. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this hybrid approach achieves an exponential regret bound and demonstrate its effectiveness empirically against several strong baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual novelty. The idea of not fully trusting the GP's bound and instead using it to accelerate a search that is agnostic to the true function smoothness is a significant departure from standard GP-UCB-style thinking. While BaMSOO combined GPs and SOO, it did so more superficially. This paper presents a deeper integration, creating a new algorithmic principle: use the GP's probabilistic model to prune the search space of an "infinite-metric" optimistic algorithm. This is a genuinely new and exciting idea in the Bayesian optimization landscape.

2.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper delivers a major theoretical result by proving an exponential convergence rate for a practical GP-based algorithm. This directly addresses and solves an open problem explicitly mentioned by previous work [2]. The analysis, particularly the introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt) and the subsequent remarks, provides valuable insights into the trade-offs at play. Remark 3, which analyzes when it is beneficial to consider infinitely many bounds even if a single (potentially loose) bound is known, is especially insightful and could have implications beyond this specific algorithm.

3.  **Bold and Ambitious Approach:** This work is not an incremental improvement. It tackles a difficult theoretical problem with a non-obvious and sophisticated algorithmic solution. The design of Algorithm 1, with its interlocking mechanisms for selection, pruning, and deferred evaluation, reflects a thoughtful and ambitious attempt to build a practical algorithm from a powerful theoretical idea. I highly value this kind of bold research direction.

4.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The experimental results are convincing. IMGPO generally outperforms its closest competitors, including BaMSOO and standard GP-BO algorithms, across a range of benchmark functions with varying dimensionalities and characteristics. The fact that this performance is achieved without hand-tuning kernels for each function strengthens the claim of practical relevance.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity and Clarity:** The proposed algorithm is quite complex, with multiple nested loops, conditional checks, and parameters (e.g., Ξ, Ξmax) that interact in non-trivial ways. While the high-level overview in Section 3.2 is helpful, understanding the precise mechanics from the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is challenging. The exposition could be improved to make the flow and the role of each component (especially the parameter Ξ and the search for `T'`) more intuitive for the reader. However, this complexity seems to be a necessary consequence of the novel mechanism and does not invalidate the approach.

2.  **Practicality of New Parameters:** The algorithm introduces new parameters, most notably `Ξmax`, which controls the depth of the UCB lookahead for pruning. While the experiments show good performance with a fixed value, the sensitivity to this parameter and guidance on how to set it could be explored further. The heuristic update rule for Ξ also feels a bit ad-hoc. These are minor methodological points that could be polished in future work but do not detract from the core contribution.

3.  **Scalability:** The authors are commendably transparent about the algorithm's potential scalability issues, noting that the convergence rate degrades with dimensionality `D`. While the 1000-D experiment using REMBO is a nice proof of concept, it demonstrates a hybridization strategy rather than IMGPO's intrinsic high-dimensional performance. This is a common challenge for partitioning-based methods and is an acceptable limitation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a highly novel and significant conceptual advance by elegantly synthesizing two powerful optimization paradigms.
*   **Pro:** It solves a recognized open problem in the field by providing the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical analysis provides deep and generalizable insights into the benefits of being agnostic to a specific function bound.
*   **Pro:** The strong empirical results validate that the theoretical benefits translate into excellent practical performance.
*   **Con:** The algorithm's implementation is complex, and the clarity of its description could be improved.
*   **Con:** The introduction of new, somewhat heuristic parameters slightly reduces the algorithm's elegance.

**Recommendation:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that I would argue strongly for accepting. Its primary contribution is a bold and novel idea that pushes the boundary of what is theoretically possible in Bayesian optimization. While there are minor weaknesses in terms of algorithmic clarity and the introduction of new parameters, these are far outweighed by the significance of the conceptual and theoretical advances. This work successfully combines the strengths of optimistic and Bayesian approaches to create something genuinely new and powerful. It provides a definitive answer to an open question while also opening up new avenues for research, which is exactly the kind of paper NIPS should feature.
