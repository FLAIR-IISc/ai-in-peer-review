- Summary: Proposes IMGPO, a Bayesian optimization method claiming exponential convergence by considering "infinitely many" bounds instead of a single GP-derived bound.  
- Strength: Provides a theoretical exponential regret bound, improving over prior polynomial bounds.  
- Strength: Introduces a novel conceptual framework of using many possible bounds for bound-based optimization.  
- Strength: Empirical results on standard test functions show IMGPO often outperforms baselines.  
- Strength: Algorithm description (pseudocode and 1D example) is relatively clear and illustrative.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 and the regret bound are written unclearly with confusing notation and poor interpretation.  
- Weakness: Practical behavior of λ (the convergence factor) is not well explained; λ may approach 1 depending on ρ̄t, undermining exponential claims.  
- Weakness: Comparison to BaMSOO’s bound is unclear due to notation and lack of direct interpretation.  
- Weakness: Algorithm explanation (Section 3.2) is confusing, especially interval selection and the role of Ξmax.  
- Weakness: Ξmax’s effect and tuning guidance are not provided despite being important for performance and runtime.  
- Weakness: Experiments are limited (only 8 functions), use fixed kernel/hyperparameters making comparisons potentially unfair, and lack statistical significance testing.  
- Weakness: Scalability concerns—λ may approach 1 with increasing dimension, and 1000-D experiment lacks sufficient detail.  
- Weakness: Claim of being faster than traditional GP methods is contradictory to being slower than SOO; practical speedups are unclear.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to related work (notably Srinivas et al. 2010) and incomplete literature support for the “first” claim.  
- Specific comment: Provide a clearer, simplified interpretation of the regret bound and dependence on key parameters (α, L, p).  
- Specific comment: Simplify and intuitively explain Algorithm 1 before presenting detailed pseudocode.  
- Specific comment: Expand experiments with more and higher-dimensional benchmarks and compare to hand-tuned baselines.  
- Specific comment: Provide more details on the 1000-D test and address high-dimensional performance explicitly.  
- Specific comment: Discuss relation to Srinivas et al. (2010) and other GP-based theoretical results.  
- Overall assessment: Interesting theoretical idea but unclear exposition, limited experiments, and unresolved scalability concerns reduce impact.  
- Recommendation: Reject with major revisions; paper has potential but needs clearer theory, stronger experiments, and practical analysis.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Exponential regret bound is significant, the infinite-bounds idea is novel, and empirical results are promising.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Poorly explained theory, insufficient and potentially unfair experiments, unaddressed scalability, and questionable practical significance.  
- To improve: Clarify and simplify theoretical analysis; broaden and fairify experiments; address scalability/high-dimensional issues; compare thoroughly with related GP work.
