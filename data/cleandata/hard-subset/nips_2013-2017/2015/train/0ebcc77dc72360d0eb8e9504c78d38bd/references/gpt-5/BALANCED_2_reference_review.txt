Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions that seeks to achieve exponential simple-regret convergence without the impractical δ-cover sampling requirement of de Freitas et al. (ICML’12) and without auxiliary global optimization of acquisition functions. IMGPO combines hierarchical partitioning (DIRECT/SOO-style) with UCBs from a GP posterior to filter candidate rectangles. The central conceptual move is to “not commit” to a single smoothness/Lipschitz bound; instead, the algorithm maintains candidates consistent with any of infinitely many possible bounds (formalized via an unknown semi-metric satisfying a Hölder-type condition near x*), while using the GP’s UCBs to prune and to sometimes avoid function evaluations.

The analysis defines an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt (the number of rectangles split per iteration), and proves an exponential regret bound O(λN+Ngp) with λ<1, where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp is the number of deferred GP-based “virtual” evaluations. The bound depends on unknown Hölder parameters (L, α, p) at the optimum and on ρ̄t. Remarks discuss how GP UCBs can help by reducing ρ̄t and increasing Ngp. Experiments on 1–6D test functions (plus a 1000D REMBO setup) compare IMGPO to SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-EI/PI, generally showing faster decrease in regret.

This work builds on and connects: DIRECT (Jones et al.), SOO/DOO/HOO (Munos), GP-UCB (Srinivas et al.), BaMSOO (Wang et al.), and δ-cover sampling (de Freitas et al.). The claimed main novelty is an exponential regret rate without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization by blending infinite-metric selection with GP-based pruning.

Strengths
- Ambitious goal and potentially significant theoretical advance: exponential simple-regret under deterministic observations without δ-cover sampling.
- Clear algorithmic idea marrying two strands of the literature (infinite-metric/DIRECT-like selection and GP-UCB filtering), with a detailed pseudocode.
- The notion of infinite-metric exploration loss ρt is insightful and offers an interpretable lens on the cost of not committing to a bound.
- Empirical results are promising across several multimodal benchmarks, with practical speedups relative to standard GP BO that require solving nonconvex inner problems.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Validity of the UCB coverage used in the theory is unclear. The paper sets ςM = sqrt(log(π^2 M^2/(12η))) and updates GP hyperparameters via empirical Bayes. Without assuming the function is a sample from the exact GP with fixed hyperparameters or, alternatively, that f lies in an RKHS ball with known norm (as in Srinivas et al.), it is not evident that f(x) ≤ μ + ςσ holds uniformly with probability ≥ 1−η. Adaptive hyperparameter learning typically breaks standard UCB coverage guarantees; the paper should state precise assumptions under which the high-probability UCB property holds.
- The main rate is stated in terms of N+Ngp, where Ngp counts the number of deferred GP “virtual” evaluations. Standard BO rates are measured in terms of black-box evaluations N; counting inexpensive GP computations in the exponent can overstate the effective convergence in the standard oracle model. A regret bound purely as a function of N (with explicit constants depending on GP usage) would be more comparable to prior work.
- The dependence on dimension and on ρ̄t can make λ close to 1 as D grows; the conditions under which ρ̄t remains O(1) or o(N) are not characterized, so the regime where the exponential rate is meaningful remains somewhat opaque.
- Assumption 1 and 2 effectively impose local Hölder continuity around x*. This aligns with DOO/SOO assumptions, but the paper claims the bound “for the best unknown L, α, p” without the algorithm adapting to those; it would be helpful to more explicitly connect to near-optimality dimension or to provide intuition on when the constants are favorable.
- Experimental setup could be strengthened: GP baselines are handicapped by using SOO/local methods to optimize the acquisition; modern multi-start quasi-Newton or DIRECT-based acquisition optimization might change the picture. Moreover, only low-dimensional benchmarks (plus a REMBO wrapper) are shown; no real-world tuning tasks are included. Ablations on Ξmax and ρ̄t, and diagnostics on how often UCBs prevent evaluations, would clarify where the gains come from.
- Clarity: some parts of Algorithm 1 are hard to parse (e.g., the “while true” loop, the definition and computation of T′h+ξ, index notation i∗h+ξ). The computational complexity in terms of calls to U, GP hyperparameter fits, and worst-case dependence on Ξmax should be more explicit.

Quality
- The algorithm is well-motivated and carefully described. However, the central theoretical guarantee appears to hinge on an unproven UCB coverage property under empirical Bayes and possible kernel mis-specification. Without a precise assumption (e.g., RKHS norm bound and βt as in GP-UCB) the probabilistic statements are not fully justified.
- Empirical evaluation is reasonable and suggests practical gains, but broader and fairer baselines are needed to support strong claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and situates the contribution within prior work. Some notational/algorithmic details could be clarified; provide a succinct high-level schematic and a complexity section. Make the relationship to DIRECT/SOO selection criteria more explicit.

Originality
- The combination of infinite-metric selection with GP-based pruning to target exponential rates is novel to my knowledge. The analysis framing via ρt is also interesting. The algorithmic components draw from established ideas but are combined in a new way.

Significance
- If the exponential rate can be established under practically verifiable assumptions (and measured in terms of N), this would be a meaningful advance. As written, the theoretical conditions and the counting of Ngp limit the immediate impact. The empirical benefits are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and broader tasks.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and interesting idea; bridges two influential lines of work.
- Promising empirical performance without auxiliary optimization.
- Attempts to address an open problem (exponential convergence without δ-cover).

Cons:
- Theoretical guarantee seems to rely on a UCB property that is not justified under empirical Bayes/mis-specified kernels.
- Regret rate counts Ngp, blurring comparability to standard evaluation budgets.
- Limited empirical scope and some baseline choices disadvantage GP methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- State and prove the precise assumptions needed for UCB coverage (e.g., RKHS norm bound B and appropriate βt depending on information gain), or keep hyperparameters fixed and analyze the misspecified case.
- Restate the main regret bound purely as a function of N (number of f-evaluations), with separate results showing the effect of GP usage on constants, and report wall-clock complexity.
- Provide a tighter characterization or upper bound on ρ̄t under reasonable smoothness/near-optimality dimension assumptions.
- Expand experiments: include standard BO baselines with strong acquisition optimizers, additional kernels, and real-world tuning tasks; add ablations on Ξmax, effect of hyperparameter learning, and counts of deferred evaluations.
- Improve algorithm clarity and provide implementation guidance (e.g., handling of T′h+ξ, numerical stability in GP fits).

Recommendation
Leaning reject in current form due to concerns about the theoretical assumptions (UCB coverage under empirical Bayes) and the way the rate is measured. The paper has interesting ideas and promising empirical results; with clarified and strengthened theory and more comprehensive experiments, it could be a strong contribution. My decision is primarily driven by rigor rather than lack of interest.
