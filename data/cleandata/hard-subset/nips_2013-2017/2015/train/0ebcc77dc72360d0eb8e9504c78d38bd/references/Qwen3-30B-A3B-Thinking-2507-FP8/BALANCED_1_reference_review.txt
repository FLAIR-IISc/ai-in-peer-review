

# Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

## Summary
This paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Gaussian Process-based global optimization algorithm that achieves exponential regret convergence without requiring the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors address a gap identified by Wang et al. [2] regarding the lack of GP-based methods with exponential convergence rates that avoid δ-cover sampling. The key insight is to consider infinitely many possible upper bounds (using an unknown semi-metric) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior, while using the GP to reduce the number of candidate bounds. The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing exponential regret bounds and demonstrates superior empirical performance over state-of-the-art methods like BaMSOO, SOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical advancement by providing the first GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence rate (O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ<1) without δ-cover sampling. This resolves an important open problem identified in previous work [2].

2. **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The infinite-metric exploration loss concept and the analysis of how considering infinitely many bounds can be beneficial (Remarks 1-3) provide valuable theoretical insights that go beyond previous work. The paper successfully bridges the theoretical gap between methods using a single bound (like GP-UCB) and methods that don't rely on a specific bound (like DIRECT/SOO).

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental results across eight benchmark functions consistently show IMGPO outperforming other methods, including BaMSOO (which has a polynomial regret bound) and traditional GP optimization approaches. The results are well-presented and demonstrate practical significance.

4. **Clear Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides a rigorous regret bound analysis with clear definitions and proofs (in supplementary material). The authors carefully discuss the relationship between the bound parameters (L, α, p) and the theoretical performance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Clarity of Exponential Convergence Claim**: The paper claims "exponential convergence" but fails to sufficiently clarify that λ < 1 depends on dimensionality D (λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1), and λ approaches 1 as D increases. This is a critical limitation that should be more prominently discussed, as it directly impacts the practical applicability in high dimensions.

2. **Experimental Details**: The paper mentions that for the 1000D Sin2 test function, they used REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine but provides no details on how this integration was implemented or how it affected the overall performance. More details on computational complexity and wall-clock time comparisons would strengthen the empirical claims.

3. **Parameter Selection Justification**: The paper fixes Ξmax = 22 but provides insufficient justification for this choice. While the paper mentions that increasing Ξmax can improve performance for some functions, it doesn't provide a principled way to select this parameter in practice.

4. **Mechanism of GP Advantage**: The paper states that "the use of GP reduces the regret bound by increasing Ngp and decreasing ρ̄t," but doesn't clearly explain *how* the GP achieves this reduction in ρ̄t. A more detailed explanation of the mechanism by which GP information helps reduce the infinite-metric exploration loss would strengthen the theoretical argument.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization by resolving a significant open problem in the field. The exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling is a notable advancement that addresses a practical limitation in previous work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

The paper's main limitation is the dimensionality-dependent convergence rate, where λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases. This is a well-known challenge in global optimization, but the paper could better address how this limitation impacts practical applications and what strategies might mitigate it (as mentioned in Remark 1). The paper also lacks sufficient detail on the computational trade-offs and parameter selection.

## Recommendations

1. **Clarify the dimensionality limitation**: In the abstract and introduction, explicitly state that the exponential convergence rate depends on the dimensionality D, with λ approaching 1 as D increases. Discuss practical implications and potential mitigation strategies.

2. **Provide more experimental details**: Include computational complexity analysis and wall-clock time comparisons with other methods. For high-dimensional cases, elaborate on the REMBO integration and its impact on the overall performance.

3. **Justify Ξmax selection**: Provide a more principled approach to selecting Ξmax, or at least discuss how this parameter should be chosen in practice for different problem settings.

4. **Explain the GP's role in reducing ρ̄t**: Elaborate on the mechanism by which GP information helps reduce the infinite-metric exploration loss, which is central to the exponential convergence claim.

## Final Recommendation

The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in Bayesian optimization with a novel approach that resolves an important open problem. While the dimensionality limitation is notable, it's a common challenge in global optimization rather than a flaw in the proposed method. The paper's theoretical insights and empirical results are strong, and the suggested improvements are implementable.

I recommend **acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the clarity issues regarding the dimensionality dependence and provide more detailed experimental justification for parameter selection in the final version. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field that advances our understanding of how to effectively combine GP information with bound-based optimization methods.
