Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions that aims to achieve exponential convergence (simple regret decays exponentially) without the impractical δ-cover sampling of de Freitas et al. The key idea is to combine a hierarchical partitioning scheme (in the spirit of DIRECT/SOO) with GP upper confidence bounds (UCB) to prune partitions. Instead of committing to a single Lipschitz/semi-metric bound, the algorithm conceptually considers infinitely many possible bounds (an “infinite-metric” view), using GP-UCB as a way to discard candidates that are looser than what the GP posterior would allow. The paper provides a regret analysis under a local Hölder-type assumption around the global optimizer (Assumptions 1–2), yielding a bound of the form rN = O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, where N is the number of objective evaluations and Ngp counts intervals whose center values are replaced by GP-UCB instead of evaluating f. Empirically, IMGPO is compared against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on standard benchmarks; the method generally performs best and avoids the auxiliary nonconvex optimization that GP-UCB requires.

This sits at the intersection of optimistic partitioning methods (DIRECT/DOO/SOO) and GP-based Bayesian optimization (GP-UCB/PI/EI). Compared to BaMSOO (SOO + GP-UCB), the claimed novelty is an exponential simple regret bound (BaMSOO provides polynomial rates), together with a pruning mechanism that does not rely on δ-cover sampling.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: The infinite-metric viewpoint is a neat way to explain why not committing to a single (often loose) bound can be beneficial, while still leveraging GP information to prune. The remarks in Section 4 (effects of GP, of ρ̄t, and of considering all bounds) are insightful.
- Theoretical contribution: If the proof holds as stated, achieving exponential simple regret in the deterministic setting without δ-cover is a nontrivial improvement over prior GP-based methods (e.g., BaMSOO’s polynomial rate).
- Algorithmic practicality: Avoids solving an auxiliary global optimization of the acquisition at each step; hierarchical splitting is deterministic and simple to implement; the skip-evaluation rule using UCB is an intuitive efficiency trick.
- Empirical performance: On a suite of multi-modal benchmarks (including a REMBO-style high-dimensional example), IMGPO typically outperforms the baselines. The use of a single, common kernel class and blind initialization is a fairer stress test than function-specific tuning.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on Ngp and ρ̄t in the bound: The main bound scales with N + Ngp and depends on the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρ̄t and the lookahead parameter Ξn. Counting Ngp (number of GP-based “dummy” placements) alongside true function evaluations makes the theoretical rate look stronger than a standard rN vs. N guarantee. In practice, the user pays only for N; the theory should ideally relate Ngp to N (e.g., show Ngp = O(N)) or provide a pure N-only bound.
- Potential typo/clarity in Theorem 1: The definition “Let λ = 3 − α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1” appears dimensionally and conceptually inconsistent with the subsequent exponential form; this likely intends λ = 3^(−α/(2CDρ̄t)). This should be corrected.
- High-probability UCB under adaptive hyperparameters: The guarantee f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with probability 1−η is asserted using a specific ςM schedule. However, the algorithm updates GP hyperparameters at every iteration via empirical Bayes. Standard GP-UCB analyses require fixed kernel or RKHS assumptions with βt schedules that account for information gain; re-estimating hyperparameters adaptively can invalidate the high-probability envelope unless carefully justified. The paper should clarify the stochastic model and conditions under which the claimed UCB property holds with adaptive hyperparameters.
- Scalability and constants: The bound degrades with dimension D (authors acknowledge λ → 1 as D grows). The role and magnitude of constants C, L, α, p, and the effect of Ξn are not quantified in practice, making it hard to assess the practical rate. A complexity analysis of step (iii) and the effect of Ξmax on runtime vs. accuracy would help.
- Assumptions: Assumption 1 is vacuous without Assumption 2; with Assumption 2 it reduces to a standard local Hölder condition at the optimum (akin to DOO/SOO’s weak Lipschitz/near-optimality assumptions). The paper should connect this explicitly to near-optimality dimension to aid comparison.
- Clarity/notation: There are several notational inconsistencies and typos that impede understanding (e.g., λ definition, ρt vs ρτ, update rule for Ξ at line 38 “Ξ ← Ξ + 22?”, details of T′h+ξ construction and its computational cost). The algorithm description is dense and would benefit from a clearer, self-contained pseudocode with consistent symbols and a small worked example table.
- Experimental limitations: Only deterministic settings are tested; many applications of BO are noisy. Baselines for GP-PI/EI require solving auxiliary optimization; here SOO plus a gradient-based local method is used, which may not be a standard or strongest optimizer, potentially affecting fairness. Including GP-UCB with a robust acquisition optimizer and reporting wall-clock time would strengthen claims. More ablations on Ξmax, kernel sensitivity, and the impact of mis-specified GP hyperparameters would be valuable.

Quality
- Theoretical claims are promising but hinge on the correctness of the high-probability UCB under adaptive hyperparameters and on the interpretation of Ngp in the rate. The λ typo should be fixed. Proofs are in the supplement; given the above concerns, I would like stronger discussion of assumptions and a statement of the exact probabilistic model used in the proofs.
- Empirical results are encouraging but could be more comprehensive and include runtime analysis.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but there are several typos and notational ambiguities that should be addressed. The algorithm description and the roles of ρ̄t, Ξ, C should be explained more plainly.

Originality
- The combination of infinite-metric optimistic partitioning with GP-based pruning and an exponential rate (without δ-cover) appears novel relative to BaMSOO/SOO/GP-UCB.

Significance
- If the analysis gap regarding adaptive hyperparameters is resolved and the exponential rate is sound in the standard BO setting, this is a meaningful theoretical advance with practical implications. The method could influence subsequent work in deterministic BO and hybrid optimistic/GP approaches.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please correct the λ definition and ensure consistency between theorem statement and exponential bound.
- Precisely state the probabilistic assumptions behind the UCB guarantee when hyperparameters are re-estimated. Does the high-probability envelope still hold? If so, under what conditions?
- Can you relate Ngp to N, or provide a regret bound purely in terms of N? Alternatively, justify counting Ngp as a resource and compare methods on a combined cost metric.
- Provide complexity bounds for step (iii) and empirical runtime vs. Ξmax curves; clarify the update rule for Ξ (line 38).
- Discuss extension to noisy observations and whether rates or guarantees can be retained.
- Add ablations on kernel mis-specification and comparisons with a strong acquisition optimizer for GP baselines; report wall-clock time.

Verdict
Borderline accept. The idea and claimed result are compelling and potentially impactful, but the presentation has clarity issues and some theoretical assumptions (notably adaptive hyperparameters and the counting of Ngp) need clarification. With revisions addressing these points, this would be a strong contribution.
