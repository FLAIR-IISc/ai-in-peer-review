

# Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP) optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors build on the theoretical work of de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], claiming to be the first GP-based method with exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling. The key idea is to consider all possible upper bounds (via a semi-metric) rather than relying on a single GP-based bound.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Fundamental Flaw in Assumption 1**: The "Unknown Bound" assumption (Assumption 1) is mathematically flawed. The formulation "f(x*) ≤ f(x) + `(x, x*)" is problematic because it implies f(x*) is less than or equal to f(x) plus a non-negative quantity, which contradicts x* being a global optimizer (where f(x*) ≥ f(x) for all x). This fundamental error undermines the entire theoretical foundation of the paper.

2. **Theoretical Bound Inconsistency**: The regret bound in Theorem 1 has a critical error in its formulation. The expression λ = 3 - α/2CDρ̄t < 1 requires α > 4CDρ̄t for λ to be less than 1. Given that CDρ̄t is a positive constant (likely greater than 1), this implies α must be very large for the bound to hold, which is unrealistic for most practical optimization problems. The paper does not address this significant issue.

3. **Algorithmic Inconsistency**: The definition of Ngp (number of GP evaluations) is inconsistent with its usage. In the algorithm, Ngp is incremented when using GP-based predictions (lines 36-37), but these are not actual function evaluations - they're GP predictions. This mislabeling creates confusion about the computational cost.

4. **Formatting and Notational Errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies in mathematical expressions throughout, such as "exp ( − 12 (x − x ′)T Σ−1(x − x′) )" (should be "exp(-1/2(x-x')^T Σ^{-1}(x-x'))"), inconsistent spacing around operators, and poorly formatted mathematical expressions in Theorem 1.

## Minor Technical Issues

1. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses both "ρ̄t" and "ρ̄_t" for the average infinite-metric exploration loss. The same symbol should be used consistently.

2. **Clarity Issues**: The description of how the algorithm handles "temporary dummy values" (step v) is unclear. The explanation in Section 3.2 and the corresponding algorithm (Algorithm 1) are not perfectly aligned.

3. **Experimental Rigor**: The paper claims IMGPO "outperformed the other algorithms in general" but does not provide statistical significance testing for these claims, which is standard in machine learning research.

4. **Parameter Selection**: The paper states Ξmax = 22 was "fixed" but admits "increasing Ξmax can improve performance." The choice of 22 seems arbitrary without justification, and the paper doesn't discuss how to select this parameter in practice.

## Strengths

1. The paper clearly identifies a gap in the literature regarding exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.
2. The algorithmic approach of considering multiple possible bounds (rather than relying on a single bound) is conceptually interesting.
3. The experimental results show promising empirical performance compared to baselines.

## Weaknesses

1. The fundamental error in Assumption 1 is a major theoretical flaw that cannot be easily fixed. This undermines the entire theoretical contribution.
2. The regret bound in Theorem 1 is mathematically problematic as stated.
3. The paper lacks sufficient detail on how to practically implement the algorithm, particularly regarding the selection of Ξmax.
4. The comparison to previous work (e.g., BaMSOO [2]) is not sufficiently detailed in terms of the specific regret bounds achieved.

## Overall Assessment

This paper has an interesting conceptual idea that could be valuable if the theoretical foundation were sound. However, the fundamental error in Assumption 1 and the problematic regret bound in Theorem 1 represent substantial theoretical flaws that cannot be overlooked. The paper also contains multiple minor technical issues that indicate a lack of careful proofreading.

While the experimental results are promising, they cannot compensate for the serious theoretical flaws. The paper does not make a demonstrable contribution to the state of the art because the main theoretical claim is based on an incorrect assumption.

Given the stringent standards for NIPS, the theoretical flaws in this paper are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper would need to be completely reworked to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
