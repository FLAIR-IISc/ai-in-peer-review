Summary
This paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a GP-based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions. The key idea is to reduce reliance on a single, potentially loose bound (e.g., UCB from GP) by simultaneously considering an infinite set of unknown semi-metric upper bounds around a global optimizer. The algorithm uses hierarchical partitioning with pruning via GP-based UCB to avoid the impractical δ-cover sampling of de Freitas et al. The authors claim an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 under Assumptions 1–2, and present experiments on several benchmarks comparing IMGPO with SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

Relation to prior work
- DIRECT and SOO/DOO: The “unknown smoothness” perspective and hierarchical partitioning are in the spirit of DIRECT and SOO (Munos). IMGPO’s “infinite-metric” framing generalizes this idea theoretically.
- BaMSOO: Combines GP-UCB with SOO to obtain polynomial regret. IMGPO aims to improve to exponential regret without δ-covers by leveraging GP to prune candidate rectangles.
- GP-UCB and GP Bayesian optimization: The paper departs from standardized GP-UCB analyses (RKHS norm bounds or sample-from-GP assumptions) and instead uses GP primarily as a device to filter candidates.

Strengths
- Ambitious goal: removing δ-cover sampling while targeting exponential convergence is an important and timely objective.
- Interesting conceptual contribution: using GP to reduce the effective “search over unknown bounds” (the infinite-metric idea) is novel relative to prior SOO/DOO/BaMSOO formulations.
- Algorithmic design is well-motivated: hierarchical partitioning plus UCB-based pruning and “temporary GP-based values” to reduce expensive function calls is plausible.
- Some empirical gains: reported stronger performance than SOO/BaMSOO/GP-EI/PI on a suite of functions, and discussion of computational trade-offs (Ξmax).

Weaknesses and concerns
Theory and assumptions:
- The central “exponential” bound depends critically on ρ̄t (infinite-metric exploration loss), Ξn, D, C, α, and L. There is no a priori control of ρ̄t; the remarks acknowledge it can grow unfavorably. Without a data-independent bound on ρ̄t, the “exponential” claim is largely nominal—λ can be arbitrarily close to 1, weakening the guarantee.
- The statement “bound holds for the best unknown L, α, p” is unclear: in standard analyses L, α, p must be those that actually satisfy the Hölder condition. Picking them post hoc “to tighten the bound” is only valid among admissible triples; this should be stated precisely.
- The use of GP-UCB-style bounds with hyperparameters updated by empirical Bayes at every iteration is problematic. The standard high-probability domination f(x) ≤ μ + β1/2 σ depends on fixed kernels/parameters and either RKHS-norm assumptions or “sample from GP” assumptions. None are made explicit. It is unclear whether the stated union bound over calls remains valid under adaptive hyperparameter updates.
- Notational issues/typos in the theorem (e.g., λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t vs presumably 3^{-α/(2CDρ̄t)}). Several expressions involving N, t, and ρ̄t are hard to parse (e.g., ρ̄t ≤ ∑i i / t involving N). This makes it hard to verify the derivations.
- Computational complexity of step (iii): worst-case 3^{Ξmax} UCB evaluations per accepted interval can be large; theory does not relate Ξmax to ρ̄t or provide guidance for safe settings.

Empirics:
- Fairness and completeness: GP baselines (PI/EI) are handicapped by using SOO plus a local optimizer for acquisition maximization; this is atypical and may bias against GP baselines. Standard practice includes multi-start L-BFGS or global optimizers tailored to acquisitions.
- Hyperparameter learning and UCB calibration: The paper uses empirical Bayes and η = 0.05 but does not show sensitivity, nor consider standard βt schedules as in GP-UCB. There is no check that the UCB-domination events hold empirically.
- Deterministic vs stochastic averaging: IMGPO/SOO/BaMSOO are deterministic given Ω; EI/PI are averaged over 50 runs. The comparison is not apples-to-apples; deterministic methods should be repeated with different seeds or perturbations to report dispersion.
- Missing details: Table 1 (CPU times) and Figure 1/2 references are crucial but not in the submission text. Reproducibility is limited: precise acquisition solvers, stopping criteria, and code details beyond a URL are not fully specified.

Quality
- The high-level ideas are interesting, but the theoretical justification hinges on quantities not controlled by the algorithm (ρ̄t), and on GP-UCB-style events without clear assumptions to ensure them under adaptive hyperparameter updates.
- Proofs are deferred; the main text has several notational and typographical issues that impede verification.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally readable, but important expressions are ambiguous or appear malformed. Several key definitions and constants (C, ρ̄t vs ρ̃t, relationship between N and t) need clearer presentation.

Originality
- The “infinite-metric” framing and integration of GP as a pruning device rather than a sole bound are novel combinations of known ingredients (SOO/DOO/DIRECT + GP-UCB).

Significance
- If the exponential bound were robust and the empirical gains reproducible under fair baselines, this would be impactful. As written, the theoretical novelty is undermined by gaps, and the empirical section is not strong enough to compensate.

Questions for the authors
1. Under what precise assumptions on the GP prior, kernel, and hyperparameter adaptation does f(x) ≤ U(x|D) hold uniformly with probability ≥ 1 − η? Please specify whether you assume the function is a GP sample or lies in an RKHS with bounded norm and how β/ς is chosen accordingly.
2. Can you provide algorithm-independent upper bounds on ρ̄t (or relate it explicitly to Ξmax and D), so the exponential term translates into a concrete rate?
3. Please fix the notational issues in Theorem 1 (definition of λ) and the bounds in Remark 1, and clarify the relationships among N, t, and ρ̄t.
4. Provide ablations: IMGPO without GP pruning; varying Ξmax; fixed vs learned hyperparameters; and standardized acquisition optimizers for EI/PI. Include wall-clock time and number of UCB evaluations.
5. Can you include standard GP-UCB baselines with theoretically calibrated βt and proper acquisition optimization?

Pros
- Novel conceptual blend of SOO/DIRECT-style search with GP-based pruning.
- Ambitious theoretical target (exponential regret without δ-cover).
- Promising empirical results on several benchmarks.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantee depends on quantities not controlled or bounded; unclear GP assumptions with adaptive hyperparameters.
- Several notational/technical issues hinder verification.
- Empirical comparisons may be unfair to GP baselines; missing details and ablations.

Recommendation
Reject. The ideas are intriguing, but the current version lacks a sound, verifiable theoretical foundation and sufficiently careful empirical validation against established baselines. I encourage the authors to tighten assumptions and proofs, provide algorithm-independent control of ρ̄t (or remove it from the main bound via explicit upper bounds), clarify the role of adaptive hyperparameters in UCB validity, and strengthen and standardize the empirical evaluation.

Confidence: High.
