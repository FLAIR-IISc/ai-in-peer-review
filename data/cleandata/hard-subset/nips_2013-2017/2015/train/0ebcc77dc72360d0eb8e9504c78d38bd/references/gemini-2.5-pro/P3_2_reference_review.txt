### Summary

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first to provably achieve an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret without relying on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The core problem of finding a practical GP-based algorithm with exponential regret was explicitly stated as an open problem in prior work (e.g., Wang et al., 2017). The key idea is to combine the hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like SOO with GP-UCB in a novel way. Instead of solely relying on the GP's upper confidence bound (which may not be tight), the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite set of possible smoothness assumptions (or semi-metrics). It uses the observed function values to optimistically select promising regions under these implicit assumptions, and then uses the GP-UCB bound to prune away regions that are provably suboptimal. This hybrid strategy allows the algorithm to enjoy the best of both worlds: the strong theoretical guarantees of optimistic methods based on unknown smoothness and the practical data efficiency of GPs. The authors provide a theoretical proof of an exponential regret bound and demonstrate strong empirical performance against several state-of-the-art baselines on a suite of benchmark functions.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clearly defined and important open problem in Bayesian optimization theory. The resulting exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) is a significant theoretical improvement over the polynomial bounds of the most closely related practical algorithm, BaMSOO. The core conceptual contribution—using a GP not as the sole source of confidence bounds, but as a tool to prune a search over an implicit, infinite family of bounds—is highly novel. It elegantly unifies two major lines of research in global optimization: those that assume a known (or single estimated) smoothness class (like GP-UCB) and those that do not (like DIRECT/SOO).

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, is impressive. It not only establishes the exponential convergence rate but also provides an insightful decomposition of the factors influencing performance. The introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρ_t) is a useful analytical tool for understanding the trade-offs. The remarks in Section 4 are particularly valuable, clearly articulating the benefits of the GP component (Remark 2) and the infinite-metric approach (Remark 3), even when compared to an oracle with a known (but potentially non-optimal) smoothness constant.

3.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing IMGPO against relevant and strong baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI). The results on a variety of benchmark functions, ranging in dimensionality and modality, convincingly demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. It generally outperforms its competitors, often by a significant margin. The choice to not hand-tune kernels for each function makes the experimental setup more realistic and the results more compelling. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO showcases a path towards scaling the method.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Clarity and Complexity of the Algorithm:** While the high-level overview in Section 3.1 is clear, the detailed algorithm description in Section 3.3 and Algorithm 1 is extremely dense and difficult to parse.
    *   The interplay between the two main `for h` loops (lines 8-17 and 19-24) and the management of the candidate indices `i*_h` is complex. A more detailed, step-by-step example on a very simple problem could significantly improve comprehension.
    *   The procedure in line 22, `z(h, i*_h) = max_{k:c_{h+ξ,k}∈T'_{h+ξ}(...)} U(...)`, seems computationally intensive. It requires identifying all nodes in a virtual, fully expanded subtree of depth `ξ`. What is the computational complexity of this step, and how does it scale with the dimensionality `D` and the parameter `Ξ`? While the paper notes that `Ξ_max` limits this, the cost could still be substantial, potentially undermining the claim of being faster than methods that require auxiliary optimization. A more detailed complexity analysis is warranted.

2.  **Analysis of the Exploration Loss `ρ̄_t`:** The theoretical bound hinges critically on the average infinite-metric exploration loss, `ρ̄_t`. The analysis in Remark 1 states that if the GP is "useful," then `N/ρ̄_t = Ω(N)`, which is ideal. However, it also notes that if the GP is "useless," `ρ̄_t` can be large. This is a crucial point. The paper would be strengthened by a more formal characterization of the conditions under which the GP is "useful" in this context. How does `ρ̄_t` depend on the mismatch between the chosen GP kernel and the true function's smoothness, or on the problem dimensionality `D`? Without a better understanding of `ρ̄_t`'s behavior, the tightness of the bound remains somewhat abstract.

3.  **Practicality and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces new parameters, `Ξ_max` and `η`. The paper provides some discussion on `Ξ_max` in the experiments, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. More importantly, the adaptive update rule for `Ξ` (line 38) feels somewhat heuristic. While it is intuitively sensible (increase exploration after finding a better point), is there any theoretical justification for this specific update rule? How sensitive is the performance to this schedule?

### Overall Evaluation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It successfully closes an important theoretical gap by presenting a practical algorithm with an exponential convergence rate. The core idea is elegant and novel, and the work is supported by both rigorous theory and strong empirical evidence. While the algorithm's description could be clearer and a deeper analysis of its complexity and key theoretical terms would be welcome, these are points for improvement rather than fatal flaws. The paper is well-written, clearly situated within the literature, and its results are likely to be influential for future research in global optimization.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
