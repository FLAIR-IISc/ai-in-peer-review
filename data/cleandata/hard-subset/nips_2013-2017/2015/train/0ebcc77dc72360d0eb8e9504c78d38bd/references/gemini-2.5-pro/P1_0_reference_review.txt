This paper proposes a new algorithm for Bayesian optimization, called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which is the first Gaussian Process (GP)-based method to achieve an exponential convergence rate without relying on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The core idea is to combine a hierarchical partitioning scheme (akin to SOO) with a GP-UCB model in a novel way. Instead of solely relying on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP, the algorithm operates under the assumption that an unknown, tighter bound defined by a semi-metric exists. It then effectively performs a search over an infinite class of possible bounds implied by this unknown metric, using the GP-UCB not as the primary driver of exploration, but as a mechanism to prune the search space and accelerate convergence. This hybrid approach elegantly bridges two major lines of work in global optimization: those that rely on a specific (e.g., GP-based) model of smoothness and those that make weaker assumptions (e.g., SOO, DIRECT). The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis culminating in a high-probability exponential regret bound and demonstrates strong empirical performance against state-of-the-art methods.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses and solves a significant open problem explicitly mentioned in prior work [1, 2]: achieving an exponential convergence rate for GP-based optimization without impractical sampling assumptions. This represents a major theoretical advance for Bayesian optimization. The core conceptual contribution—using the GP to prune a search over an infinite class of implicit bounds rather than strictly following the GP's own bound—is highly novel and insightful. It provides a new and powerful way to think about combining model-based and model-free optimization ideas.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary theoretical result, Theorem 1, establishes an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which is a substantial improvement over the polynomial bounds of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO [2]. The analysis is particularly strong because it provides deep intuition through the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρ_t) and the subsequent remarks. Remarks 1-3 clearly dissect the contribution of each component of the algorithm: they show how a useful GP model helps (by reducing ρ_t), how the infinite-metric search provides a robust baseline even if the GP is poor, and why this combined approach can outperform methods that commit to a single, potentially misspecified, bound.

3.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. IMGPO is benchmarked against strong and relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI) across a variety of test functions with different characteristics and dimensionalities. The choice to use a standard kernel and online hyperparameter tuning, rather than hand-picking them for each function, makes the results more realistic and compelling. The results in Figure 2 clearly show that IMGPO is either the best or among the best performers on nearly all problems.

4.  **Clarity of Motivation and High-Level Idea:** The paper does an excellent job in Section 3.1 of motivating the algorithm and explaining the high-level intuition. The analogy to A* search and the general class of bound-based methods is effective in positioning the work and explaining the problem of loose bounds.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity and Clarity:** While the high-level idea is clear, the algorithm itself is quite complex, and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense and difficult to parse. The interplay between the nested loops, the `υmax` variable, the selection of `i*h` across different depths, and the conditions for breaking the loops is intricate. The paper would be significantly improved by a more detailed walkthrough of a single iteration (`t`) of the main loop, explaining how the `i*h` candidates are selected, filtered, and finally acted upon. For example, the meaning of `T ′h+ξ(ch,i∗h)` is non-obvious and requires careful rereading to understand. This complexity could be a barrier to implementation and adoption.

2.  **Scalability:** The authors rightly note that the method's performance may degrade with dimensionality. The reliance on hierarchical partitioning makes it susceptible to the curse of dimensionality. While the experiment using REMBO as a wrapper is a nice proof of concept, it would be beneficial to discuss the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm more directly. The analysis shows the dimension `D` appearing in the base of the exponential term `λ`, highlighting this dependency, but a more explicit discussion of the practical limits would be welcome.

3.  **New Hyperparameters:** The algorithm introduces a new hyperparameter, `Ξmax`, which bounds the lookahead search for the UCB check (step iii). While the experiments use a fixed value, some discussion on its sensitivity and guidance on how to set it would be valuable for practitioners. The update rule for `Ξ` (line 38) is also heuristic and could benefit from more justification.

### Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Pros:**
*   Solves a well-defined open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   Presents a novel and elegant idea of combining model-based and optimistic optimization.
*   Provides the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   The theoretical analysis is insightful and clearly explains the benefits of the approach.
*   Strong empirical results against state-of-the-art baselines in a realistic setting.

**Cons:**
*   The algorithm's implementation details and pseudocode are complex and hard to follow, potentially hindering reproducibility.
*   Like related methods, it is likely limited to low-to-moderate dimensions, a point that could be discussed more extensively.
*   Introduces new parameters (`Ξmax`, `Ξ` update rule) with limited guidance on their setting.

### Final Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The strengths—a novel core idea, a strong theoretical result solving an open problem, and compelling empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarity of presentation and practical implementation details. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and the underlying ideas are likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance, and believe it is a candidate for a spotlight or oral presentation. Minor revisions to improve the clarity of the algorithm's description would further strengthen an already outstanding paper.
