

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary
This paper addresses a key challenge in Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential convergence rates without the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure required by prior work. The authors propose Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which leverages the idea of considering "infinitely many possible candidates" of bounds rather than relying on a single bound (as in GP-UCB). The theoretical contribution is a rigorous proof of an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, representing a significant improvement over previous polynomial bounds (e.g., BaMSOO's Õ(n^(-2α/D(4-α)))). The approach builds on earlier work in DIRECT and SOO algorithms while incorporating Gaussian process priors to reduce the number of candidates.

## Strengths

**Originality and Theoretical Contribution (High):** The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by introducing the "infinite-metric exploration" framework, which provides a novel perspective on bound-based optimization. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with a clear proof of the exponential regret bound and insightful remarks (Remarks 1-3) that explain the benefits of considering multiple bounds. This addresses a long-standing open problem in the field (as noted by Wang et al. [2]).

**Significance (High):** The exponential convergence rate represents a substantial theoretical advancement over existing GP-based methods, which have only achieved polynomial regret bounds. The practical implications are significant, as the method eliminates the need for δ-cover sampling, which is computationally prohibitive in many applications.

**Experimental Validation (Moderate-High):** The experiments demonstrate consistent performance improvements over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across multiple benchmark functions. The authors appropriately test with a single kernel and default hyperparameters, reflecting realistic application scenarios.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity and Presentation (Moderate):** The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is complex and could benefit from clearer exposition. The explanation of steps (i)-(iii) and the role of Ξmax is somewhat opaque. A more intuitive visualization of the hierarchical partitioning process would aid understanding.

**Theoretical Limitations (Moderate):** While the exponential bound is theoretically impressive, Remark 1 reveals that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality D increases, suggesting scalability limitations for high-dimensional problems. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't provide a compelling solution, which is a significant drawback for a field where high-dimensional optimization is common.

**Experimental Scope (Moderate):** The experiments are limited to relatively low-dimensional problems (with the highest dimension being 1000, using REMBO as a subroutine). The paper lacks experiments on more challenging high-dimensional problems where GP-based methods typically struggle, making it difficult to assess the method's practical utility in these settings.

**Parameter Sensitivity (Moderate):** The performance appears sensitive to the Ξmax parameter (as noted in the experiments), which could be a practical drawback. The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on selecting this parameter for different problem classes.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- First GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with a clear exponential regret bound
- Significant improvement over previous polynomial regret bounds
- Well-designed experiments demonstrating consistent performance gains
- Addresses a long-standing open problem in the field

**Con:**
- Limited scalability to high dimensions (λ→1 as D increases)
- Algorithm complexity and parameter sensitivity may hinder practical adoption
- Experiments don't adequately test high-dimensional cases
- Some theoretical aspects (e.g., the role of Ξmax) require further clarification

## Constructive Suggestions

1. **Clarify the intuition behind the algorithm:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of why considering infinitely many bounds is beneficial, potentially with a simple 1D visualization.

2. **Address high-dimensional limitations:** Discuss potential extensions to handle higher dimensions (e.g., incorporating dimensionality reduction techniques or additive assumptions) and provide experiments on more challenging high-dimensional problems.

3. **Improve parameter selection guidance:** Provide more concrete recommendations for selecting Ξmax or propose an adaptive mechanism for this parameter.

4. **Expand experimental scope:** Include results on more challenging high-dimensional problems where GP-based methods typically struggle, to better demonstrate practical utility.

5. **Simplify the algorithm description:** Consider breaking down Algorithm 1 into more intuitive components or providing pseudocode for the core steps.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel approach to achieving exponential convergence. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the experimental results support the claims. However, the scalability limitations to high dimensions and the complexity of the algorithm present practical challenges that need to be addressed. The paper represents an important step forward in the field, but the limitations in scalability and practical implementation should be acknowledged and addressed in future work.

**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions to address clarity and scalability concerns)

This paper is a strong contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization. While the high-dimensional limitations are notable, the theoretical novelty and practical improvements over previous methods make this a valuable contribution to the field. With minor revisions to improve clarity and address scalability concerns, this paper would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS.
