

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel Gaussian process-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential regret convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors frame their approach as considering "infinitely many possible candidates of the bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior. They provide theoretical analysis showing an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, and experimental results demonstrating superior performance compared to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on various benchmark functions.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by addressing the open problem of achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis is well-structured, with clear definitions and theorems. The experimental comparison is comprehensive across multiple benchmark functions, and the authors provide a good discussion of parameter choices (e.g., Ξmax=22).

## Weaknesses

Despite the promising theoretical claim, the paper contains several significant technical and presentation flaws that undermine its credibility and clarity:

1. **Notational inconsistencies**: The paper uses "f(x+)" to denote the best found value in the introduction, but later uses "f(x+)" without explanation. The standard notation would be f(x*) or f(x^*). This inconsistency appears multiple times throughout the paper.

2. **Critical mathematical error in Theorem 1**: The paper claims λ = 3 - α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1 for exponential convergence. However, this expression cannot be less than 1 for typical values of the parameters (e.g., α > 0, C, D, ρ̄t > 0). The correct expression should likely be λ = 3^(-α/(2CDρ̄t)) < 1, which would yield the exponential decay. This is a fundamental error that invalidates the theoretical claim.

3. **Ambiguous parameter definitions**: The paper introduces Ξmax as a parameter but never explains how to choose it in practice. In Section 5, they state "For IMGPO, Ξmax was fixed to be 22 (the effect of selecting different values is discussed later)" but the discussion of different Ξmax values is minimal and lacks statistical justification.

4. **Algorithm description errors**: Line 31 of Algorithm 1 contains a typo: "g(ch,i∗h)← g(ch,i∗h)" which is nonsensical (it's assigning a value to itself). This is a critical implementation error that would prevent reproduction.

5. **Inconsistent dimensionality references**: In Section 3.2, they state "the center of the input region Ω ⊂ R" when Ω is D-dimensional (D ≥ 1), creating confusion about the algorithm's dimensionality.

6. **Missing explanation of key assumptions**: Assumption 2 is defined in Theorem 1 but not properly introduced in the main text. The paper claims to work for "the best values of the unknown L, α and p" but doesn't explain how these are determined in practice.

7. **Experimental methodological flaws**: The paper claims IMGPO "outperformed the other algorithms in general" but fails to provide statistical significance tests for these comparisons. The experimental section also lacks proper error bars or confidence intervals for the reported results.

8. **Formatting and citation issues**: The reference list contains inconsistent formatting (e.g., "In Proceedings of the 29th International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML),, , , " with multiple commas), and the paper cites [1] and [2] as pointing out the open problem without clearly explaining how their solution differs from prior work.

9. **Confusing notation in regret bounds**: In Remark 1, they state "N/ρ̄t = Ω(N)" but N is the number of function evaluations while ρ̄t is the average exploration loss. This notation is mathematically confusing and makes the bound interpretation difficult.

10. **Lack of practical guidance**: The paper claims exponential convergence but doesn't provide guidance on how to choose parameters for real-world applications. The experimental section shows good results but doesn't explain how to apply the algorithm to new problems.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization and contains interesting theoretical insights, the technical errors (particularly the fundamental mistake in Theorem 1's λ definition) and numerous notational inconsistencies significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The paper claims to achieve "exponential regret" but the mathematical expression for the regret bound is incorrect, which is a critical flaw.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with such fundamental mathematical errors. These are not minor typos but rather critical errors that invalidate the main theoretical contribution. The paper also contains numerous other inconsistencies that would confuse readers and prevent reproduction of the results.

I recommend rejection due to the fundamental mathematical error in Theorem 1, which is a critical flaw in the paper's main claim. The authors would need to completely revise the theoretical analysis to correct this error before the paper could be considered for acceptance.
