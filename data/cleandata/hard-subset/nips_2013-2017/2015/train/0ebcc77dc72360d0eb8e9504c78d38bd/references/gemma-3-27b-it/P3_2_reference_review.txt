## NIPS Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1].  The core idea is to simultaneously consider a range of possible Lipschitz-like bounds (through an unknown semi-metric) on the objective function, guided by the information provided by the GP. This is implemented using hierarchical partitioning and a clever acceptance/rejection criterion informed by both GP predictions and the potential for tighter, unknown bounds. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and empirical results showcasing performance gains compared to existing methods on several benchmark functions.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on Bayesian Optimization, Lipschitz optimization, and global optimization. It builds upon the work of de Freitas et al. [1] by addressing the practical limitations of δ-cover sampling.  The connection to SOO [18] and BaMSOO [2] is also clearly articulated, with the authors highlighting the improvements in convergence rate achieved by IMGPO. The references to related work in global optimization (DIRECT, A*) and reinforcement learning (PAC-MDP) provide valuable context.  The discussion of related work is generally thorough.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength is the derivation of an exponential convergence rate for a GP-based Bayesian optimization algorithm without δ-cover sampling. This is a significant theoretical advance in the field.
* **Novel Approach:**  The concept of simultaneously considering multiple possible bounds, leveraging the GP as a guide but not strictly relying on it, is a clever and potentially impactful idea. It addresses a fundamental limitation in traditional bound-based optimization methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate consistently strong performance across a range of benchmark functions. While the experimental setup is relatively standard, the results provide encouraging evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
* **Clear Presentation (generally):** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clear, and the algorithm is described in a reasonably accessible manner. The inclusion of a pseudocode is also helpful.
* **Insightful Discussion:** The concluding remarks and the “Remarks” section within the analysis are particularly strong. They provide valuable insights into the interplay between the GP-based bound, the unknown bound, and the overall performance of the algorithm.  The discussion around the advantages of considering multiple bounds is well articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Scalability:** While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the algorithm itself appears complex to implement and tune. The parameters Ξmax and η require careful consideration, and the effect of different division procedures isn't fully explored.  The discussion of scalability is limited, acknowledging potential challenges in higher dimensions but lacking concrete solutions beyond referencing additional assumptions found in other works.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments fix hyperparameters (σ, l) across all functions. While this simplifies the comparison, it doesn’t address potential sensitivity to hyperparameter choices. A more thorough hyperparameter tuning procedure would strengthen the empirical results.  The choice of these initial hyperparameters also seems somewhat arbitrary.
* **Experimental Setup:** The justification for the initial hyperparameters for GP is weak (“blindly initialized”). The comparison with other algorithms sometimes appears uneven (e.g., SOO benefiting from a handpicked kernel while IMGPO does not). A more rigorous and fair comparison is needed.
* **Limited Novelty in Experiments:** The benchmark functions used are fairly standard. While this allows for comparison with existing methods, it would be beneficial to evaluate IMGPO on more challenging, real-world problems.  The experiment with high-dimensional Sin2 using REMBO doesn’t fully showcase IMGPO’s standalone performance.
* **Analysis Depth:** The proof sketch is mentioned, but the full mathematical details are relegated to supplementary material. While standard practice, this makes it harder for reviewers to fully assess the validity and tightness of the theoretical results. The connection between the parameters of Assumption 2 (L, α, p) and the difficulty of the optimization problem is intuitively explained, but could be further formalized.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in Bayesian Optimization.
* The proposed algorithm addresses a key practical limitation of existing methods.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the algorithm.
* The insights provided in the discussion section are valuable for the research community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm's complexity and potential scalability issues are concerning.
* The experimental setup could be more rigorous and fair.
* The analysis, while promising, is difficult to fully assess without access to detailed proofs.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to advance the field of Bayesian Optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the proposed algorithm offers a promising solution to a practical problem. However, the complexity of the algorithm and the limitations of the experimental setup raise some concerns.

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept**.

I recommend accepting this paper with the caveat that the authors address the reviewers' concerns regarding scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, and the rigor of the experimental evaluation. A more detailed explanation of the parameter choices and a discussion of potential solutions for scaling to higher dimensions would significantly strengthen the paper.  Providing access to the full proofs (beyond the supplementary material) would also be beneficial.



**Confidence:** 8/10.  The theoretical results are convincing, but the practical implications remain somewhat uncertain due to the complexity of the algorithm.
