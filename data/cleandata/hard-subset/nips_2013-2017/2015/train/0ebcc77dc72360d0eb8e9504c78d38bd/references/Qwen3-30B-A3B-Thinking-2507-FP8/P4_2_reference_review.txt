

# Review: Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence

## Summary
This paper addresses the open problem of achieving exponential convergence rates in Bayesian optimization without requiring the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors propose Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which leverages an unknown semi-metric bound at the global optimizer (Assumption 1) rather than relying solely on the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB). The key insight is that considering infinitely many possible bounds can yield better performance than committing to a single (potentially loose) bound. This work builds on de Freitas et al.'s δ-cover sampling approach [1], Wang et al.'s BaMSOO algorithm [2], and Munos' SOO algorithm [18], but differs by eliminating the need for δ-cover sampling while achieving exponential regret bounds.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with Theorem 1 establishing an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) (λ < 1), which is superior to BaMSOO's polynomial bound. The authors carefully address the technical challenges of the infinite-metric exploration loss (Definition 1) and provide detailed proofs in supplementary material. The experimental section demonstrates consistent performance improvements over baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) across eight benchmark functions, with clear results shown in Figure 2. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding scalability to higher dimensions (Remark 1).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The main algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented with comprehensive pseudocode and step-by-step explanation in Section 3.2. The theoretical concepts (e.g., infinite-metric exploration loss) are carefully defined and motivated. The connection between the algorithm and the theoretical analysis is well-maintained throughout. The supplementary material provides additional details that would help reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a specific open problem in Bayesian optimization literature (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling). The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds rather than relying on a single bound is novel and well-justified. The authors effectively position their work against related literature, particularly differentiating from BaMSOO [2] and GP-UCB [13-15]. The theoretical insights in Remarks 1-3 provide valuable understanding of when and why not relying on a single bound is advantageous.

**Significance:** The exponential convergence rate is a significant theoretical advance over previous polynomial bounds. The practical implementation shows clear performance benefits across multiple test functions. The framework provides a new perspective on bound-based search methods that could potentially extend to other domains like planning algorithms (A* search) or reinforcement learning (PAC-MDP), as noted in the conclusion. The paper successfully bridges theoretical guarantees with practical implementation.

## Weaknesses

**Technical Details:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the connection between the infinite-metric exploration loss and the practical implementation could be more explicit. The paper mentions Ξmax as a parameter but doesn't sufficiently explain how to select it in practice beyond noting that "increasing Ξmax can improve performance at the cost of extra CPU time" (Section 5).

**Scalability Discussion:** The paper acknowledges dimensionality limitations (Remark 1) but doesn't provide concrete suggestions for addressing them. While the paper mentions REMBO [14] for high-dimensional problems (Section 5), it would be beneficial to have more specific guidance on how IMGPO could be integrated with existing high-dimensional Bayesian optimization techniques.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper focuses on comparing with BaMSOO and GP-UCB, but doesn't explicitly compare with more recent Bayesian optimization methods that have achieved improved performance, such as those using sparse GPs or other approximation techniques. The paper's claim of being "the first GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence" could be strengthened by more comprehensive comparison with the most recent literature.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Solves a well-defined open problem in Bayesian optimization (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling)
- Provides the first theoretical guarantee of exponential convergence for a GP-based method without δ-cover sampling
- The theoretical analysis offers valuable insights into the trade-offs of bound-based search methods
- Strong experimental results across multiple benchmark functions
- Well-written with clear organization and thorough technical details

**Con:**
- The practical implications of the Ξmax parameter could be better explained
- Limited discussion of how to handle the dimensionality challenge in high-dimensional problems
- The comparison with more recent Bayesian optimization methods could be more comprehensive

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, the experimental results are convincing, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. While the paper could strengthen its discussion of parameter selection and high-dimensional extensions, these are minor concerns relative to the paper's substantial contribution. The work addresses an important open problem in the field and provides both theoretical advances and practical benefits. This paper meets all four criteria for high-quality NIPS papers and makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
