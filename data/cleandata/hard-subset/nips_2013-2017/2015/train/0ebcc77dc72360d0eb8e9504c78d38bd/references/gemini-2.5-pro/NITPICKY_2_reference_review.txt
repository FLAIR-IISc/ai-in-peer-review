This paper addresses the important problem of designing a practical Bayesian optimization algorithm with a guaranteed exponential convergence rate. The authors propose a new algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which combines a hierarchical partitioning scheme, reminiscent of the SOO and DIRECT algorithms, with Gaussian Process (GP) bounds. The core idea is to not rely solely on the GP-derived upper confidence bound (UCB), which may be loose, but to simultaneously consider an infinite set of potential smoothness assumptions, embodied by an unknown semi-metric. The GP model is then used to prune the search space under these assumptions. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound, O(λ^(N+Ngp)), without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior theoretical work. This result is supported by experiments on several benchmark functions, where IMGPO generally outperforms existing methods like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential regret without impractical assumptions. The proposed method of integrating the SOO/DIRECT philosophy (robustness to unknown smoothness) with the GP framework is a novel and conceptually appealing contribution. It provides a principled way to bridge two influential but previously separate lines of work in global optimization.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, provides an exponential regret bound for a practical algorithm. This is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of related methods like BaMSOO. The analysis framework that explicitly models the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt) is insightful.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough, comparing against relevant baselines on a variety of test functions, including a high-dimensional problem via the REMBO framework. The reported results demonstrate that IMGPO is not only theoretically sound but also practically effective, often outperforming its competitors.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the significance of the contributions, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in clarity, technical detail, and presentation that detract from its quality.

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm:** The description of the IMGPO algorithm is exceedingly difficult to follow.
    *   The prose description in Section 3.2 is dense. The relationship between the number of iterations `t`, function evaluations `N`, and divisions `n` is not clearly established.
    *   The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is highly complex. The `while true` loop (lines 9-18) for resolving "GP-based" labels is convoluted; its purpose and efficiency are not well-motivated. It seems to iteratively query the same point `ch,i*h` if it was previously assigned a UCB value.
    *   Several key design choices lack justification. Why is the hyperrectangle divided into three new ones (line 29) instead of a more standard bisection? The update rule for Ξ in line 38 appears entirely heuristic, with magic numbers (`22`, `2^-1`) that are not explained.

2.  **Contradiction on "Auxiliary Optimization":** The paper repeatedly claims an advantage of IMGPO is that it avoids the "additional non-convex optimization problem" required by standard GP-UCB. However, line 22 of Algorithm 1, `z(h, i*h) = max_k U(ch+ξ,k|D)`, appears to be exactly such a problem. The set of points `T'_{h+ξ}` can be large (growing as `3^ξ`), and finding the maximum UCB over this set is a non-trivial optimization. The paper completely fails to explain how this maximization is performed, which is a critical omission. If it is done by exhaustive search, it could be computationally prohibitive. This unresolved point undermines a central claim of the paper.

3.  **Minor Technical and Notational Issues:**
    *   In Section 2, the definition of the covariance function includes a transpose, `(f(x′)−m(x′))^T`, which is incorrect for a scalar-valued function `f`.
    *   The footnote in Section 4 introduces a modification to the algorithm (limiting tree depth) that is not part of the main proposal, discusses its pros and cons, and includes a typo (`tN` instead of likely `t/N`), all of which creates confusion.
    *   In Remark 3, the final inequality `ρ̃−1t ≥ 1− (Cc2D/N) ln(...)` seems to suggest `ρ̃t=1` is always a good choice, which feels overly simplistic and may be a consequence of the high-level algebraic manipulation. The remark provides little tangible intuition.

4.  **Reproducibility and Presentation:**
    *   The source code is unavailable, with the provided URL (`http://lis.csail.mit.edu/code/imgpo.html`) being a dead link.
    *   The choice of `Ξmax = 22` for the experiments is stated without any justification or sensitivity analysis.
    *   There are several typographical errors (e.g., "forth diagram" instead of "fourth").
    *   The formatting of references is inconsistent. For example, [1] and [2] lack venue and year information, which is present in other entries like [3].

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization theory.
*   Presents a novel and powerful synthesis of hierarchical partitioning and GP models.
*   Provides the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The algorithm's description is extremely unclear and the pseudocode is difficult to implement from the text.
*   A central claim of the paper—avoiding auxiliary optimization—appears to be contradicted by a crucial step in the algorithm (line 22), which is left unexplained.
*   The paper is littered with minor technical inaccuracies, notational errors, unjustified parameter choices, and formatting inconsistencies, indicating a lack of careful preparation.
*   The work is not reproducible due to the complexity and the dead code link.

**Decision:**

While the core ideas in this paper are novel and potentially very significant, the execution is unfortunately marred by a large number of flaws, ranging from minor typos to a critical contradiction in its claims. The lack of clarity in the algorithm's description and the unresolved question of the auxiliary optimization in line 22 are major obstacles to understanding and accepting the work in its current form. Given my stringent criteria, the accumulation of these issues outweighs the paper's strengths. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should be strongly encouraged to perform a major revision to clarify the algorithm, address the contradiction, justify their design choices, and polish the manuscript before resubmission.
