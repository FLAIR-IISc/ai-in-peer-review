This paper proposes a Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, termed IMGPO, aiming for exponential convergence without the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure required by de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to simultaneously consider multiple potential bounds on the objective function, leveraging the GP’s estimate to prune less plausible bounds, guided by an unknown semi-metric. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and experimental results showcasing IMGPO’s performance on several benchmark functions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper achieves a theoretical result of exponential convergence, addressing a key open problem identified in Wang et al. [2]. This is a significant contribution, as it provides a strong guarantee of performance for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of considering a distribution over possible bounds, rather than relying on a single estimated bound, is a novel idea and intuitively appealing. It attempts to address the issue of tight bound estimation inherent in many optimization algorithms.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results generally support the theoretical claims, demonstrating IMGPO’s competitive performance compared to existing methods across a range of benchmark functions.
*   **Clear Algorithm Description:** The algorithm is described in a reasonably clear manner, with a helpful illustrative example (Figure 1) and pseudocode (Algorithm 1).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Excessive Detail & Notational Burden:** The paper is dense and burdened with an excessive level of detail in both the theoretical analysis and the algorithmic description. Numerous parameters (e.g., Ξmax, η, ρ̄t) are introduced without sufficiently motivating their necessity or providing clear guidelines for their setting. This hinders readability and makes it difficult to grasp the core concepts.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity & Practicality Concerns:** While the theoretical analysis is presented, the sensitivity of the convergence rate to the various parameters (L, α, p, C, ρ̄t) is not adequately discussed. The choice of Ξmax, for instance, appears crucial but lacks a robust tuning strategy in the experimental section. The statement that increasing Ξmax *can* improve performance, but at a computational cost, is insufficient.
*   **Lack of Thorough Comparison:** The experimental comparison, while showing generally good performance, lacks depth. The claim that the authors used a “blind” initialization of hyperparameters is concerning. The impact of different kernel choices beyond the isotropic Matern kernel is not explored. The comparison to GP-PI and GP-EI, while included, feels cursory, and a more thorough ablation study examining the contribution of each component of IMGPO would be valuable.
*   **Assumptions & Scalability:** The analysis relies on Assumption 1 (existence of a global optimizer and an unknown semi-metric) and Assumption 2 (Lipschitz continuity). While standard, the implications of these assumptions on real-world problem applicability are not fully discussed. The discussion of scalability to high dimensions, while mentioning REMBO, feels tacked on and lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations.  The remark about scaling to 1000D via REMBO requires a far more comprehensive justification.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:**
    *   The statement following Equation (16) (regarding the definition of the third function) is incomplete and unclear.
    *   In Algorithm 1, line 37 mentions labeling g(ch+1,inew) as GP-based, but the usage of this label is not explicitly clarified.
    *   The use of the symbol “≡” (identity) is used inconsistently throughout the paper.
    *   The definition of ρ̄t as the *maximum* of averages seems counterintuitive and warrants further justification.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical result of exponential convergence is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The proposed approach offers a potentially more robust alternative to existing GP-based optimization methods.
*   The experimental results provide some evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The paper is overly detailed and burdened with excessive notation, hindering readability.
*   The practical considerations, such as parameter tuning and scalability, are not adequately addressed.
*   The experimental comparison lacks depth and a thorough ablation study.
*   Numerous minor technical issues detract from the overall quality of the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically promising algorithm for Bayesian optimization. However, its presentation is convoluted, and practical considerations are insufficiently addressed. While the theoretical contribution is significant, the lack of clarity, depth in experimentation, and sensitivity analysis raise serious concerns. Given the density and numerous minor flaws, I recommend **rejection**. A substantial revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, with a focus on clarity, practical relevance, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, would be necessary for reconsideration. The current manuscript, despite its merits, falls short of the quality expected for a NIPS publication.
