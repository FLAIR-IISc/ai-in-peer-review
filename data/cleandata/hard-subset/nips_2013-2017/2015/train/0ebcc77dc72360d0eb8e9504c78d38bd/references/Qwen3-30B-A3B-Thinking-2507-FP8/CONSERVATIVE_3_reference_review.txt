

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process-based Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work (de Freitas et al. [1]). The authors argue that by simultaneously considering all possible bounds (rather than relying on a single GP-based bound), their method can achieve exponential regret bounds while avoiding the computational burden of δ-cover sampling.

## Strengths
The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature regarding exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis is well-structured, with a clear definition of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept. The experimental results on 8 benchmark functions show that IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI in most cases, particularly on the more challenging functions. The algorithm description is detailed, and the supplementary material provides additional theoretical support.

## Critical Weaknesses

### Theoretical Claims vs. Evidence
The paper's central claim of "exponential convergence" is problematic. The regret bound is O(λ^N+Ngp) with λ = 3 - α/2CDρ̄t < 1. However, λ depends on unknown parameters (α, C, D, ρ̄t) and approaches 1 as dimensionality D increases (Remark 1). In practice, ρ̄t (the average infinite-metric exploration loss) could be large enough that λ is very close to 1, making the "exponential" convergence rate practically indistinguishable from polynomial convergence. The paper fails to provide empirical evidence that λ is significantly less than 1 in real-world scenarios.

### Experimental Validation
The experimental evaluation is insufficiently thorough. The authors test only 8 functions, most with low dimensionality (1-10D), and the 1000D function is an artificial embedding. Crucially, they don't compare to state-of-the-art GP-based methods like GP-UCB with modern acquisition functions (e.g., Knowledge Gradient), which have demonstrated strong performance in practice. The comparison to GP-UCB is limited to the specific implementation used in BaMSOO (which already has limitations), not to the standard GP-UCB approach. The paper also doesn't test on higher-dimensional problems where Bayesian optimization is most commonly applied.

### Methodological Soundness
The core theoretical contribution relies on Assumption 2 (a bound on the semi-metric `), which is extremely strong and likely unrealistic for many practical problems. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how frequently this assumption holds in real-world applications. The algorithm's complexity (Algorithm 1) with its many parameters (Ξmax, η, etc.) and the need for multiple hierarchical partitioning steps makes it significantly more complex than standard GP-UCB without clear justification for the added complexity.

### Novelty Assessment
While the paper correctly cites related work (DIRECT, SOO, BaMSOO), the core idea of considering multiple bounds isn't truly novel. Jones et al.'s DIRECT algorithm and Munos' SOO already explored similar concepts. The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how IMGPO differs substantially from these prior methods in a way that justifies the exponential convergence claim. The "infinite-metric exploration loss" concept doesn't appear to provide a meaningful theoretical or practical advantage over existing methods.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses a genuine problem in Bayesian optimization but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its central claim of exponential convergence. The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions that are unlikely to hold in practice, and the experimental validation is limited to a small set of low-dimensional test functions. The authors don't adequately compare to the standard GP-UCB approach (which is the most widely used GP-based Bayesian optimization method) or to other state-of-the-art methods.

The paper's claim of "exponential convergence" is overstated. The regret bound O(λ^N+Ngp) with λ < 1 is only meaningful if λ is significantly less than 1, which the paper fails to demonstrate empirically. In high dimensions (where Bayesian optimization is most needed), the bound becomes increasingly weak as λ approaches 1 (Remark 1).

The paper would benefit from:
1. More thorough comparisons to standard GP-UCB and other established methods
2. Experiments on higher-dimensional problems
3. A clearer demonstration that λ < 1 in practical scenarios
4. A more detailed discussion of when the strong assumption (Assumption 2) is likely to hold

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a valid problem, the theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported by evidence, and the practical significance is questionable given the limitations of the analysis and experiments. The paper presents a method that may be marginally better in some specific cases but fails to demonstrate the substantial improvement in convergence rate that the title and claims suggest. The authors would need to provide stronger theoretical justification and more comprehensive experimental validation to support their central claim of exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.
