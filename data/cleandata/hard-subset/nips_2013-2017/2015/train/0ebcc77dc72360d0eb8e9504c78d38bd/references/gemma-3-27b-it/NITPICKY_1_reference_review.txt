This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally demanding δ-cover sampling procedure. The core idea is to consider an infinite number of possible bounds, leveraging the assumption of an unknown semi-metric governing the function’s continuity around its optimum. While the motivation is sound – addressing a practical limitation of previous theoretical results – and the presented analysis suggests exponential convergence, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor yet significant flaws hindering its overall impact and necessitating substantial revisions.

The introduction adequately frames the problem and positions the work within the existing literature. However, referencing “simple regret” as “regret” throughout feels unnecessarily terse and potentially confusing for readers less familiar with the optimization literature. The discussion of previous work is reasonably thorough, but lacks nuance. For example, the criticisms of Lipschitz constant estimation are glossed over – these methods often work well in practice despite theoretical limitations.

The GP optimization background (Section 2) is standard and serves its purpose, but is overly verbose and lacks conciseness. Equations are presented without sufficient context; for instance, the definition of *k* could be expanded. A minor, but irritating, inconsistency exists: *f(x1:N)* is used as both a function and a vector of function values. 

Section 3, detailing IMGPO, is where the most significant concerns arise. The algorithm’s description lacks sufficient clarity. While Figure 1 attempts to illustrate the process, it’s difficult to follow and doesn’t adequately convey the interplay between hierarchical partitioning, UCB values, and the unknown semi-metric. The algorithm description is also plagued by ambiguous phrasing (e.g., “skip the evaluation and use the UCB value as the center value until the interval is accepted…”) and lacks sufficient detail regarding implementation specifics. The purpose of Ξmax is poorly explained – its role in limiting computation is acknowledged, but the potential impact on convergence isn’t discussed. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is overly complex and difficult to parse, with numerous unexplained variables and nested loops. The use of indentation is inconsistent. More importantly, several lines of the pseudocode (e.g., lines 13-17) are only vaguely linked to the corresponding text description, undermining the algorithm's accessibility. The claim that the source code is publicly available is a positive, but wasn't accessible at the time of review.

The analysis (Section 4) presents a theoretical result claiming exponential convergence. While the mathematical derivation may be correct (requires expert verification), the assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) are strong and their practical implications are not fully explored. The discussion of how the GP bound affects the regret bound (Remark 1) is insightful, but the provided intuition feels incomplete. Remark 2’s discussion on omitting GP feels somewhat contradictory, stating it could be disadvantageous. The justification for excluding a randomness in the evaluation is not strong. Furthermore, the connection between the theoretical analysis and the practical performance observed in the experiments isn’t clearly articulated.

The experiments (Section 5) are adequate, but lack rigor. The justification for using a fixed set of hyperparameters is weak – a proper parameter sensitivity analysis is missing. The comparison to other algorithms is hampered by the use of identical hyperparameters across all methods, a choice made due to the assumption of unavailable hyperparameter knowledge, but ultimately diminishing the value of the comparison. The claim of outperforming other algorithms "in general" is overstated, as the performance differences are not always statistically significant. The discussion of Ξmax's effect is brief and lacks detailed results.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to GP-based optimization, addressing a known limitation in existing theoretical results.
*   Theoretical analysis suggesting exponential convergence.
*   Publicly available code (though currently inaccessible).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in algorithm description and pseudocode.
*   Numerous minor flaws in writing, notation, and technical details.
*   Strong assumptions in the theoretical analysis and limited discussion of practical implications.
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation.
*   Overstated claims about performance improvements.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea of IMGPO is promising, the paper is currently unsuitable for acceptance. The numerous minor flaws, coupled with the lack of clarity and rigor, significantly detract from the work’s impact. Substantial revisions, including a clearer algorithm description, a more robust experimental evaluation, and a more nuanced discussion of the theoretical assumptions, are required. I recommend **rejection** with the strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The potential contribution warrants further consideration, but the current manuscript falls short of NIPS quality standards.
