

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper proposes a novel Gaussian process-based optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the δ-cover sampling procedure. The authors argue that their approach leverages an unknown semi-metric bound in addition to the GP-based bound, allowing for exponential regret bounds.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Misrepresentation of Prior Work**: The paper cites de Freitas et al. [1] as having "proposed a theoretical procedure that maintains an exponential convergence rate (exponential regret)" without δ-cover sampling. However, the cited paper is titled "Exponential regret bounds for Gaussian process bandits with deterministic observations" and concerns bandit settings, not general global optimization. This misrepresentation undermines the paper's premise and should be corrected.

2. **Theoretical Flaw in Theorem 1**: The main result (Theorem 1) claims an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, but λ = 3 - α/2CDρ̄t where ρ̄t is defined as "the maximum of the averages of ρ1:t' for t' = 1, 2, ..., t." This means λ depends on N (since ρ̄t depends on N), making it impossible for λ to be a constant less than 1. The claimed "exponential convergence" is therefore invalid as stated.

3. **Algorithmic Ambiguity**: Algorithm 1 contains several notational inconsistencies and undefined terms:
   - Line 21: "ξ ← the smallest positive integer s.t. i∗h+ξ 6= ∅..." uses non-standard notation (6= instead of ≠) and the meaning of "if exists" is unclear.
   - T' is used in line 22 without prior definition.
   - The description in Section 3.2 contradicts the algorithm: "at n = 16, the far right interval is divided, but no function evaluation occurs" conflicts with lines 33-37 which state evaluation occurs when U(...) ≥ f+.

4. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout (DN vs. D, f+ vs. f(x+), 6= vs. ≠), which significantly hinders understanding of the algorithm.

## Minor but Significant Issues

1. **Theoretical Analysis Weaknesses**: Remark 1 claims "if UCB computed by GP is 'useful' such that N/ρ̄t = Ω(N), then our regret bound becomes O(exp(-N+Ngp2CD α ln 3))". This is inconsistent with Theorem 1's bound and lacks proper justification.

2. **Experimental Weaknesses**: The paper claims IMGPO "outperformed the other algorithms in general" but provides no statistical significance tests for the results in Figure 2. The claim that "increasing Ξmax does not affect IMGPO for Sin1, Sin2, Branin and Hartmann3" is vague without explaining why Ξn didn't reach Ξmax = 22.

3. **Reference Inconsistency**: The paper cites Wang et al. [2] as "Bayesian Multi-Scale Optimistic Optimization" but the reference shows it as "Bayesian Multi-Scale Optimistic Optimization" with a different title. The reference list contains formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Retrieved November, , , " for Surjanovic and Bingham).

4. **Misleading Claims**: The paper states "our method still maintains an exponential convergence rate and an advantage over GP-UCB (no need for auxiliary optimization)" but the paper does not clearly establish why the exponential convergence claim holds when the theoretical analysis is flawed.

## Strengths

The paper makes a legitimate attempt to address a practical limitation in Bayesian optimization (the need for δ-cover sampling). The concept of leveraging multiple potential bounds is theoretically interesting, and the experimental results do suggest practical improvements over existing methods for some test functions.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in Bayesian optimization, the theoretical claims are undermined by fundamental mathematical errors in Theorem 1. The algorithm description contains critical ambiguities that would prevent reproducibility. The misrepresentation of prior work and inconsistent notation further diminish the paper's credibility.

The authors have made a good attempt at a novel approach, but the flaws in the theoretical analysis and algorithm description are substantial enough to outweigh the potential significance of the contribution. The paper would require complete reworking of the theoretical analysis and algorithm description to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**
