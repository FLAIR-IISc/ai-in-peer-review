This paper proposes a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical results (de Freitas et al. [1]). The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric that may provide a tighter bound than that directly estimated by the GP, simultaneously conducting global and local searches based on all candidate bounds, filtering out looser ones using the GP estimate. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and empirical validation on several benchmark functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a novel theoretical result, achieving exponential convergence for GP-based Bayesian optimization without δ-cover sampling. This addresses a significant open problem highlighted by Wang et al. [2]. The proof appears rigorous, and the analysis of the infinite-metric exploration loss is insightful.
* **Novelty of Approach:**  The concept of leveraging an unknown semi-metric to refine the search process is innovative. The algorithm effectively balances exploration and exploitation by considering a wider range of potential bounds, mitigating the limitations of solely relying on the GP estimate.  The connection to bound-based search methods (Lipschitz optimization, A*) is well-articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO across a diverse set of benchmark functions, consistently outperforming other algorithms (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) in many cases. The experimental setup, including the use of a fixed kernel and hyperparameters (reflecting a realistic scenario where optimal choices are unknown), strengthens the credibility of the results.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The algorithm description, while complex, is accompanied by a helpful illustrative figure (Figure 1). The discussion of related work is thorough, and the analysis section is clear and concise.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the scalability limitations in high dimensions and suggest potential avenues for improvement (e.g., leveraging additional assumptions).  The publicly available source code (http://lis.csail.mit.edu/code/imgpo.html) is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:**  While avoiding δ-cover sampling, the algorithm introduces a hierarchical partitioning scheme and the consideration of multiple bounds, potentially increasing the computational cost per iteration. The experiments do not comprehensively analyze the runtime performance of IMGPO compared to other methods, which is crucial. Table 1 only mentions it's faster than traditional GP optimization, but doesn't compare to other methods in terms of wall-clock time.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm has several parameters (e.g., Ξmax, η) that could influence its performance. The sensitivity of the results to these parameters is not thoroughly investigated. The choice of Ξmax=22 appears somewhat arbitrary; a more systematic study of its impact would be valuable.
* **Clarity of the Infinite-Metric Exploration Loss:** While the concept of ρt is clearly defined, its practical interpretation and relationship to other performance metrics (e.g., regret, convergence speed) could be elaborated further. The discussion around its impact on the regret bound could be simplified for better understanding.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors acknowledge the scalability issue in higher dimensions, the discussion is somewhat limited. Exploring the applicability of IMGPO with dimensionality reduction techniques or other strategies to enhance scalability would be beneficial. The use of REMBO in the 1000-dimensional case is a step in the right direction, but further analysis is needed.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of an unknown semi-metric ` (Assumption 1) is strong. While the algorithm is designed to work even if the GP bound is less tight, the existence of such a semi-metric is crucial.  A discussion on the types of functions where this assumption is likely to hold, and potential implications if it doesn't, would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization, providing a novel and theoretically sound solution with proven exponential convergence.
* The proposed algorithm demonstrates strong empirical performance on a diverse set of benchmark functions.
* The clarity of presentation and the availability of source code contribute to the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the algorithm could be a limiting factor in practical applications.
* The sensitivity to parameter choices and the scalability limitations require further investigation.
* The strong assumption regarding the existence of an unknown semi-metric warrants further discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution and promising empirical results. The proposed IMGPO algorithm represents a valuable addition to the toolkit of Bayesian optimization methods. While some weaknesses related to computational cost, parameter sensitivity, and scalability exist, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the runtime performance, investigate the sensitivity of the results to key parameters, and elaborate on the assumptions underlying the algorithm.  Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact on the field.
