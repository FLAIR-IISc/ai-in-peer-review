

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in previous work by de Freitas et al. [1]. The authors address a known gap in the literature (pointed out by Wang et al. [2]) where existing GP-based methods either fail to achieve exponential convergence or rely on impractical sampling procedures.

The key insight of the paper is to consider "infinitely many possible bounds" rather than relying on a single bound provided by the GP prior. The authors formalize this approach through an unknown semi-metric ` that encodes the continuity at the global optimizer. The algorithm simultaneously conducts global and local searches based on all possible candidates of this bound, using the GP's known upper bound to prune less promising candidates.

The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, and demonstrates through experiments that IMGPO outperforms existing methods like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on a variety of benchmark functions.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical analysis is well-developed, with Assumptions 1 and 2 providing a solid foundation for the main result (Theorem 1). The regret bound is properly derived with a clear probability guarantee (1-η), and the paper appropriately acknowledges limitations (e.g., the bound on λ as dimension increases).

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on eight different objective functions across dimensions from 1 to 1000. The setup is reasonable for real-world practice, using a standard isotropic Matern kernel with blind initialization rather than hand-picked hyperparameters. The results clearly show IMGPO outperforming other methods in most cases, with appropriate discussion of the exceptions (like Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading).

The paper is honest about its limitations, particularly in Remark 1 where it discusses the trade-offs between the tightness of the GP's bound and the algorithm's performance. The authors also acknowledge that their algorithm is slower than SOO (Table 1), which is an important practical consideration.

*Minor concern*: The paper claims "the source code of the proposed algorithm is publicly available" but doesn't include a URL in the text, which would be helpful for reproducibility.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and the gap in the literature. The description of the algorithm (Section 3) is supported by a helpful figure (Figure 1) and detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The technical details in Section 3.3 are sufficiently precise for a reader familiar with GP and hierarchical partitioning.

The presentation of the theoretical results (Section 4) is logically structured, with the main theorem followed by helpful remarks that explain the implications of different parameters. The experimental results are clearly presented in Figure 2, with appropriate discussion of the findings.

*Minor suggestion*: The paper could improve clarity by providing a more intuitive explanation of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" ρt and its relationship to the algorithm's performance.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The key novelty is the "infinite-metric" approach that considers infinitely many possible bounds rather than relying on a single bound. This is distinct from previous work in several important ways:

1. It's the first GP-based algorithm to achieve exponential convergence without the δ-cover sampling procedure.
2. It provides a theoretical framework explaining why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial (Remarks 1-3).
3. It bridges two previously divergent paths in global optimization literature (those with a specific bound vs. those without).

The paper effectively positions itself against related work, clearly explaining how it differs from BaMSOO [2], GP-UCB, and DIRECT [19]. The theoretical framework they provide for understanding when not relying on a single bound is beneficial is particularly novel.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Achieving exponential convergence in Bayesian optimization is a major theoretical advance, and doing so without the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure makes the method more applicable to real-world problems.

The paper makes a strong case for the practical importance of the theoretical framework they provide, showing how it can be used to understand the trade-offs between different optimization approaches. The potential for extending this principle to other bound-based methods (A* search, PAC-MDP algorithms, etc.) mentioned in the conclusion suggests broader impact beyond Bayesian optimization.

The experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods, with IMGPO outperforming competitors on most benchmark functions. The paper's approach could influence future research in Bayesian optimization and related fields.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a clear and important gap in the literature (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling)
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees with a well-motivated analysis
- Demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods
- Offers a novel theoretical framework that explains why their approach is beneficial
- Honesty about limitations and trade-offs

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical analysis, while sound, may be complex for some readers
- The paper doesn't directly compare with some more recent Bayesian optimization methods (e.g., those using sparse GPs)
- The scalability to very high dimensions (beyond what's tested) isn't fully addressed
- The source code URL is missing from the text

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with a theoretically sound and practically useful algorithm. The theoretical framework they provide is particularly valuable, as it offers a principled way to understand when and why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial.

The paper's strengths in quality, originality, and significance clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the authors have appropriately acknowledged limitations.

This paper represents a clear advance over previous work and provides both a practical algorithm and a theoretical framework that will likely influence future research in Bayesian optimization and related areas. It meets all the criteria for a high-quality NeurIPS paper.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
