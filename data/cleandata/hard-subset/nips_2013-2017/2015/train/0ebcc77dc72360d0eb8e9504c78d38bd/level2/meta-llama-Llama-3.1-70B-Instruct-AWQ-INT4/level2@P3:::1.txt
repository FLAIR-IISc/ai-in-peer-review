This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The method leverages the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer, allowing it to consider infinitely many possible candidates of bounds simultaneously.

**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper addresses the problem of global optimization with non-convex black-box functions. The authors propose a GP-based optimization method that eliminates the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, which are requirements of existing Bayesian optimization methods with exponential convergence. The method, IMGPO, uses a hierarchical partitioning approach to maintain hyperintervals and selects the next query point based on the upper confidence bound (UCB) generated by a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to consider infinitely many possible candidates of bounds simultaneously, rather than relying on a single bound given by the GP.

**Relationship to Previous Work**

The authors relate their work to previous Bayesian optimization methods, including GP-UCB, which requires auxiliary optimization, and BaMSOO, which combines SOO with GP-UCB but only achieves a polynomial regret bound. The authors also mention the DIRECT algorithm, which considers a set of infinitely many bounds, but only maintains the consistency property (i.e., convergence in the limit) from a theoretical viewpoint.

**Strengths**

1. **Exponential convergence rate**: The paper presents a theoretical analysis that shows IMGPO achieves an exponential convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over existing Bayesian optimization methods.
2. **No auxiliary optimization**: The method eliminates the need for auxiliary optimization, which is a requirement of existing Bayesian optimization methods with exponential convergence.
3. **No δ-cover sampling**: The method does not require δ-cover sampling, which is considered impractical in many cases.
4. **Practical analysis framework**: The paper provides a practically oriented analysis framework that enables the understanding of why not relying on a particular bound is advantageous and how a non-tight bound can still be useful.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: The method requires the computation of UCBs for multiple intervals, which can be computationally expensive.
2. **Hyperparameter tuning**: The method requires the tuning of hyperparameters, such as Ξmax and η, which can be challenging in practice.
3. **Scalability**: The method may not be scalable to high-dimensional problems, as the number of intervals to be considered grows exponentially with the dimensionality of the problem.
