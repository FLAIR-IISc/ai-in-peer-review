This paper introduces IMGPO, a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without requiring an auxiliary optimization step or the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The work situates itself clearly in the context of previous literature, particularly building on hierarchical partitioning methods like SOO and addressing an open problem identified in BaMSOO, which combined SOO with GP-UCB but only achieved a polynomial regret bound. The core idea is to use the Gaussian Process upper confidence bound (GP-UCB) not to guide a separate optimization for the next query point, but to prune branches in a hierarchical partitioning search, thereby combining the strengths of both approaches.

The primary strength of the paper lies in this novel synthesis. By leveraging the GP posterior to eliminate unpromising regions of the search space, IMGPO avoids the computationally expensive auxiliary optimization common to many GP-based methods, a significant practical advantage. This claim is well-supported by both strong theoretical analysis and a thorough empirical evaluation. The authors provide a formal proof of an exponential regret bound (Theorem 1), which marks a clear theoretical advance over related work. Experimentally, IMGPO is shown to consistently outperform strong baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-EI/PI, across a variety of benchmark functions with different dimensionalities. The work is original, significant, and presents a complete and reproducible contribution, with code made available.

The main weakness is the complexity of the algorithm, which makes the description in Section 3.2 and the pseudocode somewhat dense and difficult to parse on first reading. A more high-level, intuitive walkthrough of the selection logic (steps i-iii) before presenting the full algorithm could improve clarity. Additionally, while the authors commendably acknowledge scalability challenges in their analysis, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to the Ξmax parameter, which balances exploration and computational cost, would be beneficial for practitioners.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-supported, and practically significant contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. It solves a well-known problem and its theoretical and empirical results are convincing. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the importance of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
