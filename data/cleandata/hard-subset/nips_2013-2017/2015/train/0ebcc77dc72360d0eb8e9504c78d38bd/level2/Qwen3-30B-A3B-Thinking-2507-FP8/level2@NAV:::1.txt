

This paper presents IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The main contribution is a theoretical framework demonstrating that considering infinitely many possible bounds (rather than relying on a single estimated bound) enables exponential convergence while eliminating practical implementation barriers of prior methods.

The paper's key strength lies in its strong theoretical foundation. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous proof of exponential convergence rate O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ<1, supported by detailed analysis in Remarks 1-3 that explain why considering multiple bounds can outperform single-bound approaches. The experimental evaluation on eight benchmark functions demonstrates consistent performance improvements over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, with IMGPO being significantly faster than traditional GP methods while achieving better regret.

The paper demonstrates solid field knowledge, properly situating its work within the Bayesian optimization literature and correctly addressing limitations of prior approaches (particularly the impracticality of δ-cover sampling noted by Wang et al.). The novelty is well-justified by showing how IMGPO bridges the gap between bound-based methods that rely on a single bound (like GP-UCB) and those that consider multiple bounds (like DIRECT and SOO).

However, the paper has some limitations. The theoretical analysis reveals that the convergence rate deteriorates with increasing dimensionality (λ approaches 1 as D increases), suggesting scalability challenges. The experimental section doesn't adequately address how to choose the parameter Ξmax in practice, which significantly impacts performance. While the paper mentions that increasing Ξmax improves performance at the cost of computational time, it lacks clear guidance for practitioners.

The paper would be strengthened by including a more detailed discussion of parameter selection for Ξmax, along with a more comprehensive analysis of the computational complexity trade-offs. The authors should also clarify how their method scales to higher-dimensional problems, as this is a critical concern for real-world applications.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization. The exponential convergence rate without impractical requirements represents a meaningful advancement. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the paper provides valuable insights into the benefits of considering multiple bounds. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the parameter selection discussion and scalability concerns.
