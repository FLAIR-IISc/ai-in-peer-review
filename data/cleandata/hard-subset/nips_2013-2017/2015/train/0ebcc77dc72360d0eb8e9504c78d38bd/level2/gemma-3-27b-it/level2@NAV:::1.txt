## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization (BO) method aiming for exponential convergence without relying on auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, both limitations of existing approaches like those by de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The core idea is to leverage the assumption of an unknown semi-metric bound on the objective function, effectively considering a wider range of potential bounds during the optimization process, while still utilizing the information provided by the Gaussian Process (GP) prior to reduce the search space.

The paper clearly articulates the motivation – the impracticality of δ-cover sampling and the computational cost of auxiliary optimization – and positions its contribution within the broader landscape of BO and global optimization. The authors effectively build upon previous work on bound-based search methods [18, 19] and highlight the advantage of not relying on a single, potentially inaccurate, bound. The technical description of the Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) algorithm is reasonably detailed, and the connection to existing GP-UCB methods is well explained. The inclusion of pseudocode is helpful. 

A significant strength lies in the theoretical analysis, demonstrating an exponential convergence rate. The discussion around the effect of the GP bound’s tightness, and the insights provided in Remarks 1, 2, and 3, are valuable for understanding the algorithm's behavior and limitations. The experiments on eight different objective functions demonstrate competitive performance against established algorithms like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, particularly in scenarios where the GP prior is not particularly informative. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The description of the hierarchical partitioning and division procedure could benefit from greater clarity. While the pseudocode is present, visualizing the process in more detail (beyond Figure 1, which is limited) would improve understanding.  Additionally, the scalability concerns raised in the analysis – the convergence rate approaching 1 as dimensionality increases – are acknowledged but not fully addressed. While the authors suggest leveraging additional assumptions, further discussion on potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach achieving exponential convergence without problematic requirements of existing methods.
*   Solid theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees.
*   Competitive empirical performance against established algorithms.
*   Clear articulation of motivations and connections to existing work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity of the division procedure could be improved.
*   Scalability to high dimensions is a concern.
*   Limited discussion of potential solutions to scalability issues.

**Overall:**

Despite some minor shortcomings, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors successfully address important limitations of existing methods and provide a theoretically sound and empirically validated algorithm. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to the authors to further refine the description of the partitioning procedure and explore solutions for improving scalability.
