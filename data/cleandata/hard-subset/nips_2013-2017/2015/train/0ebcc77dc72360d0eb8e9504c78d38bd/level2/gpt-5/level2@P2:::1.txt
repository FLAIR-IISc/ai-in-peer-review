Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces IMGPO, a Bayesian optimization algorithm that combines hierarchical optimistic partitioning with GP-based upper confidence pruning, and claims an exponential simple-regret rate without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary acquisition optimization. The method assumes an unknown semi-metric that upper-bounds the suboptimality around a global optimizer (Assumption 1) and that this semi-metric is Hölder-dominated by an L, α, p condition (Assumption 2). The algorithm explores a hierarchy of hyperrectangles, keeps multiple candidates per depth (“infinite-metric” exploration), and uses GP-UCB values to avoid some function evaluations and to prune subtrees. A regret bound of the form rN = O(λN+Ngp) is proved, where λ < 1 depends on constants, dimension D, and an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρ̄t capturing the cost of not committing to a single bound. Empirically, IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on several deterministic benchmarks and remains competitive in a REMBO setup for 1000-D embedded problems. The work builds on DIRECT and SOO/DOO (Jones et al.; Munos) and on GP-UCB and BaMSOO (Srinivas et al.; Wang et al.), positioning itself as a bridge: it uses GP upper bounds to reduce the “infinite-metric” exploration overhead and achieves an exponential rate like de Freitas et al. but without δ-cover sampling.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: A clear and interesting idea of decoupling correctness (requiring only an unknown, potentially tighter local bound) from tightness (leveraging GP-UCB to screen/prune), thereby combining strengths of DIRECT/SOO and GP-UCB.
- Theoretical contribution: An exponential convergence guarantee under deterministic observations without δ-cover sampling is significant, and the analysis exposes how GP affects Ngp, ρ̄t, and a tunable parameter Ξ. The remarks give useful intuition about when relying on many candidate bounds is preferable to committing to one.
- Practical relevance: Avoids auxiliary global optimization of the acquisition; uses standard kernels and EB hyperparameter updates; code is available. Empirically strong on several multimodal test functions.
- Relation to prior work is generally well-covered, and the positioning vs BaMSOO/SOO/DIRECT is appropriate.

Weaknesses:
- Assumption/pruning-vs-UCB correctness gap: The algorithm critically relies on high-probability UCB validity (f(x) ≤ μ + ςσ) while simultaneously adapting GP hyperparameters via empirical Bayes. This data-dependent adaptation can invalidate uniform UCB guarantees absent additional conditions. Indeed, the authors note cases where “the property did not hold,” which undermines the theoretical pruning guarantees as implemented.
- Notational/technical clarity issues in the main theorem: λ is written ambiguously (e.g., “λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t < 1”), and the bound mixes N, Ngp, t, ρ̄t, and Ξn in a way that is hard to interpret without the supplement. The role and growth control of Ξ/Ξmax (subtractive in the exponent) are not transparent. The definition and empirical behavior of ρ̄t (depending on t while the bound is in N) deserve clearer exposition.
- Scope of assumptions: Assumption 2 effectively enforces a Hölder-type global domination of the semi-metric by an ℓp norm. This is stronger than “unknown local smoothness at the optimum” and narrows applicability relative to SOO’s near-optimality framework. The analysis would be more compelling if stated in terms of near-optimality dimension or if local variants of Assumption 2 suffice.
- Experimental design details: GP-PI/EI optimization is reportedly done with SOO plus local gradient-based optimization; this is an unusual and potentially weak optimizer for acquisitions relative to standard multi-start L-BFGS-B or DIRECT. GP-UCB itself is not included as a baseline. There is limited sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying Ξmax, kernels, or hyperparameter learning strategies) and no ablation (IMGPO without GP pruning, or with fixed hyperparameters) to validate the claimed mechanisms (Ngp, ρ̄t reductions).
- Scalability: The bound degrades quickly with D (authors acknowledge λ → 1 as D increases). High-dimensional experiments rely on REMBO; this is reasonable but warrants more systematic study.

Quality:
- The high-level idea and algorithmic design are sound and well-motivated. However, several theoretical steps hinge on uniform UCB validity with adaptive hyperparameters; this needs to be stated as an assumption or handled formally (e.g., by freezing hyperparameters, using priors on hyperparameters, or citing robust confidence schemes).
- The main bound’s constants/notation should be corrected and unified to be checkable in the main text. A discussion of computational complexity per iteration and a proof sketch would aid reproducibility.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally good but marred by typos and notational inconsistencies. N vs n vs t, the definition and use of ρ̄t, the precise form of λ, and the role of Ξ/Ξmax need cleanup. Pseudocode line references and the definition of T′h+ξ should be simplified. Clarify what “label as GP-based” entails and when these placeholders are guaranteed to be resolved.

Originality:
- The “infinite-metric” framing and the use of GP to reduce exploration while achieving exponential regret (without δ-cover) appear novel relative to BaMSOO/SOO/DOO/DIRECT.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful for deterministic BO, especially when acquisition optimization is costly or impractical. If the theoretical guarantees can be solidified under realistic GP hyperparameter learning, this could influence practice. Current empirical evidence is promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and ablations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel idea that unifies two lines of work (optimistic partitioning and GP-based BO).
- Exponential regret without δ-cover is a meaningful theoretical advance.
- Practical algorithm avoiding auxiliary optimization; good empirical performance; released code.

Cons against acceptance:
- Theoretical guarantee appears to rely on UCB validity that is questionable under EB hyperparameter adaptation; clarity of theorem/notation needs revision.
- Experimental baselines/optimizers for acquisitions may be suboptimal; limited ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Assumptions and constants’ dependence on D may limit applicability; analysis not framed in near-optimality terms.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- State clearly the assumptions under which UCB holds with adaptive hyperparameters, or modify the algorithm to ensure theoretical validity (e.g., fix hyperparameters, use hierarchical priors, or derive confidence bounds robust to adaptation).
- Correct and unify the notation in Theorem 1; explicitly define λ; align N, t, Ngp, ρ̄t; provide a concise proof sketch in the main text. Discuss bounds in terms of near-optimality dimension if possible.
- Provide ablations: (a) IMGPO without GP pruning; (b) different Ξmax; (c) fixed vs EB hyperparameters; (d) measure ρ̄t and Ngp empirically. Include GP-UCB with strong acquisition optimizers as a baseline and DIRECT.
- Expand sensitivity to kernels and hyperparameters; report wall-clock times in more detail.
- Clarify computational complexity per iteration and stopping criteria. Fix typos and improve pseudocode readability.

Overall: promising and creative contribution; with improved theoretical clarity around UCB validity and stronger experiments, it would be a solid accept. As is, it is borderline due to theoretical/clarity gaps.
