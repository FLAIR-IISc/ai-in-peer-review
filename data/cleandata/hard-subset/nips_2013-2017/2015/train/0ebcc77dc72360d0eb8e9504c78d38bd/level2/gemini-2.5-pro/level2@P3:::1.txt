**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for the global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The main contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret, a significant improvement over the polynomial rates of many existing methods. The work positions itself as solving a known open problem by achieving this strong theoretical guarantee without two major limiting requirements of previous approaches: (1) it eliminates the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization of an acquisition function, a common and computationally expensive step in many GP-based methods; and (2) it does not rely on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure, which was a requirement for a prior method with a similar convergence guarantee [1].

The core idea of IMGPO is a synthesis of two prominent lines of work in global optimization: hierarchical partitioning methods (like SOO [18]) and GP-based Bayesian optimization. The algorithm maintains a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. Instead of directly optimizing a GP-based acquisition function like the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB), it uses the UCB to prune branches of the search tree that are provably suboptimal. The selection of which partition to refine is guided by both the observed function values (which implicitly accounts for an unknown function smoothness, akin to SOO) and the GP's uncertainty quantification. This novel combination allows the algorithm to enjoy the strong theoretical guarantees of GP models while retaining the practical efficiency of partitioning-based methods that avoid auxiliary optimization.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. Achieving an exponential convergence rate without the impractical δ-cover assumption or the costly auxiliary optimization step represents a major theoretical and practical advancement. This work effectively closes an open problem highlighted by previous research [2].

2.  **Originality:** The proposed approach is highly original. While it builds upon established concepts like hierarchical partitioning and Gaussian processes, the method of integration is novel. Using the GP-UCB not as an objective to be maximized, but as a criterion for pruning branches in a partitioning scheme that implicitly handles a wide range of smoothness assumptions, is a clever and powerful idea. This successfully unifies two distinct paradigms in global optimization.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and well-supported.
    *   **Theory:** The formal analysis culminates in Theorem 1, which provides a clear exponential regret bound. The analysis is further strengthened by insightful remarks that dissect the contribution of each component of the algorithm. For instance, Remark 1 clarifies how the algorithm gracefully degrades if the GP provides a "useless" bound, and Remark 3 provides a compelling argument for why the "infinite-metric" approach can be superior even to methods that use a known (but potentially loose) smoothness constant.
    *   **Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. IMGPO is benchmarked against a strong set of relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI) across a variety of standard test functions with varying dimensionalities. The results consistently demonstrate the superior sample efficiency of IMGPO. The inclusion of runtime comparisons in Table 1 effectively validates the practical benefit of avoiding auxiliary optimization. The demonstration of IMGPO's use within a high-dimensional method (REMBO) is a nice addition.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature and articulates its contributions. The core idea is presented with good intuition before diving into the algorithmic details.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a very strong paper, a few points could be clarified or strengthened to further improve its impact.

1.  **Algorithmic Complexity:** The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is dense, and some steps could benefit from additional explanation. In particular, the mechanism for selecting a cell to expand, involving the nested loops (lines 8-23) and the computation of `z(h, i*h)` (line 22), is intricate. This step, which appears to involve a limited-depth lookahead search, is critical to the algorithm's performance but its motivation and computational cost are not fully transparent. A more detailed intuitive explanation of this selection and pruning logic would enhance the paper's clarity and aid in reproducibility.

2.  **Scalability with Dimension:** The paper correctly notes in the analysis that the base of the exponent in the regret bound, `λ`, can approach 1 as the input dimension `D` increases. This dependence on dimensionality is a key challenge for this class of methods. While the 1000-D experiment is a good proof-of-concept for embedding IMGPO in other frameworks, a more explicit discussion of the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm itself would be valuable. For instance, clarifying how terms like `C` and the exploration loss `ρ̄_t` are expected to behave with increasing `D` would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's limitations.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new hyperparameter, `Ξmax`, which controls the depth of the UCB lookahead for pruning. The experiments use a fixed value, with a brief note that performance could be tuned at the cost of computation time. A small sensitivity analysis or ablation study showing the trade-off between `Ξmax`, regret, and wall-clock time on one or two benchmark functions would provide valuable practical guidance and strengthen the experimental section. Similarly, the update rule for `Ξ` in line 38 has a heuristic feel; a brief justification for this specific choice would be helpful.

These suggested revisions are relatively minor and intended to polish an already excellent piece of work. They are not essential for acceptance.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the literature on Bayesian optimization. It provides a novel algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees that are supported by a solid empirical study. The work successfully bridges two important sub-fields of global optimization and solves a well-defined open problem. The paper is clear, well-executed, and is likely to be influential. It is a strong candidate for acceptance.
