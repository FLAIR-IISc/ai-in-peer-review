This paper presents a Bayesian optimization (BO) method with exponential convergence that avoids auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, both limitations of prior work. The authors address a significant challenge in BO – achieving strong theoretical guarantees without impractical requirements. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric to encode potential tightness of bounds, effectively considering a wider range of possible function behaviors. The approach is complemented by a hierarchical partitioning scheme for efficient exploration.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, proposed method, and experimental results. It connects well to existing literature in BO, particularly the work of de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], highlighting the limitations they address. The theoretical analysis, while somewhat dense, provides a crucial foundation for understanding the convergence properties of the algorithm. The experimental validation, across a variety of test functions, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to several baselines.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The exponential convergence rate without the reliance on δ-cover sampling is a significant theoretical result. This addresses a known bottleneck in previous BO methods.
* **Novel Approach:** The idea of incorporating an unknown semi-metric and considering a broader range of possible bounds is innovative and well-motivated.
* **Practical Relevance:** Eliminating auxiliary optimization makes the method more readily applicable in real-world scenarios where defining and solving such problems can be difficult.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide strong evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness across diverse test functions, including a 1000-dimensional problem.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained in a reasonably accessible manner.
* **Availability of Code:** Publicly available code is a major plus for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** While the experiments demonstrate good performance, the discussion acknowledges scalability concerns with increasing dimensionality, and the proposed solution (leveraging additional assumptions) isn’t fully elaborated upon.  The impact of the λ parameter approaching 1 as dimension increases needs further discussion.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems somewhat sensitive to parameters like Ξmax, and further analysis on robust hyperparameter tuning strategies would be beneficial. While a discussion of this is present, it's limited.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While several baselines are included, a comparison to more recent and advanced BO algorithms (e.g., those utilizing deep Gaussian processes or more sophisticated acquisition functions) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Analysis Depth:** The theoretical analysis, while providing a convergence guarantee, could be made more accessible with a more intuitive explanation of the key assumptions and their implications.
* **Assumption 1:** The assumption of the existence of an unknown semi-metric ` is relatively strong and its practical implications are not fully discussed. Is this assumption always reasonable in real-world optimization problems?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian optimization with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The exponential convergence guarantee is a valuable contribution to the field.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm in practice.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Scalability concerns remain a potential limitation.
* The theoretical analysis could be more accessible and the assumptions more thoroughly justified.
* The comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art BO algorithms is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are significant, the proposed method is innovative, and the experimental validation is compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the scalability concerns and potentially expand the experimental comparison in a future version. The authors should also consider adding a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results and a discussion of the practicality of Assumption 1.
