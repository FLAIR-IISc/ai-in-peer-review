

This paper proposes an Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) algorithm for Bayesian optimization that claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors argue that existing methods like GP-UCB require auxiliary optimization (which is computationally expensive) and δ-cover sampling (which is considered impractical), and that their method overcomes both limitations while maintaining exponential convergence.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a compelling theoretical contribution by providing an exponential regret bound for a GP-based optimization method without the δ-cover sampling requirement. The idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds (instead of relying on a single bound from GP) is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis in Section 4 is thorough, with careful examination of how different parameters affect the regret bound. The experimental results (Section 5) demonstrate that IMGPO outperforms baselines like SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on multiple benchmark functions, with the exception of Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The exponential convergence rate (O(λ^(N+Ngp)) where λ < 1) is theoretically interesting but has a critical limitation: λ = 3^(-α/(2CDρ̄t)) approaches 1 as the dimension D increases. This suggests the algorithm may not scale well to higher dimensions, which is a significant drawback for Bayesian optimization where high dimensions are common. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't provide a clear solution for high-dimensional problems.

2. The algorithm's computational complexity is not sufficiently addressed. The "infinite-metric exploration loss" ρt (the number of intervals to be divided during each iteration) could be large, potentially making the algorithm slower than claimed. The paper states "IMGPO is much faster than traditional GP optimization methods although it is slower than SOO," but lacks concrete runtime comparisons or scalability analysis.

3. The paper's claim of eliminating the need for δ-cover sampling isn't fully substantiated. While the algorithm doesn't explicitly use δ-cover sampling, it's not clear how the algorithm avoids the underlying requirements that made δ-cover sampling necessary in previous work.

4. The experimental section lacks sufficient detail on parameter sensitivity. While the paper mentions Ξmax = 22, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how different Ξmax values affect performance across various functions.

5. The theoretical analysis relies on Assumption 1 (Unknown Bound) which is vague. The paper doesn't clearly explain how to verify this assumption for real-world problems or how often it's satisfied in practice.

**Overall assessment:**
The paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel approach to bound-based search. The exponential convergence rate without δ-cover sampling is a significant theoretical advance. However, the practical limitations in high dimensions and the unclear computational complexity of the algorithm are substantial concerns that need to be addressed before this method can be considered practically useful for real-world Bayesian optimization tasks.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More detailed analysis of the dimensionality limitation and potential solutions for high-dimensional problems
2. Concrete runtime comparisons with baselines
3. Clearer explanation of how the algorithm avoids the δ-cover sampling requirement in practice
4. A more thorough investigation of the parameter Ξmax across different functions

The significance of the paper is somewhat limited by the practical scalability concerns, though the theoretical contribution is strong. The paper is well-written and makes a genuine contribution to the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization, but the practical impact is uncertain due to the high-dimensional scaling issue.

The paper is a solid theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of Bayesian optimization, but the practical limitations need to be addressed before it can be considered a major contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the scalability concerns and provide more concrete experimental comparisons.
