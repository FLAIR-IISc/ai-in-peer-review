### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for globally optimizing deterministic black-box functions. The main contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret without requiring two common but problematic components of previous methods: (1) an auxiliary non-convex optimization step to maximize an acquisition function, and (2) an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure.

The proposed method, IMGPO, builds upon the framework of hierarchical partitioning algorithms like SOO and DIRECT. The core idea is to simultaneously consider an infinite set of possible smoothness assumptions (or semi-metrics) for the objective function, avoiding reliance on a single, potentially poorly-specified model. The algorithm maintains a tree of hyper-rectangles over the search space. At each iteration, it selects candidate rectangles for subdivision based on their observed function values. The key novelty lies in how it incorporates a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. Instead of using the GP to define an acquisition function to be maximized, IMGPO uses the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB) as a pruning mechanism to eliminate sub-regions of the search space that are unlikely to contain the global optimum. This integration allows the algorithm to theoretically achieve an exponential convergence rate, a significant improvement over the polynomial rates of related methods like BaMSOO. The authors provide a theoretical analysis culminating in this exponential regret bound and empirically demonstrate that IMGPO outperforms several state-of-the-art methods on various benchmark functions.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a clearly stated open problem from prior work [2]: developing a GP-based optimization algorithm with an exponential convergence rate that does not rely on the impractical δ-cover sampling scheme [1]. The achievement of an `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` regret bound is a strong theoretical result that advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. The analysis provided is thorough and connects the new algorithm to existing theoretical frameworks.

2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Algorithm:** The IMGPO algorithm is an elegant synthesis of ideas from two distinct lines of work in global optimization: hierarchical partitioning methods that are robust to unknown function smoothness (e.g., SOO) and Bayesian optimization methods that leverage probabilistic models (GPs). The idea of using the GP-UCB not for direct maximization but as a pruning tool within the partitioning scheme is novel and clever. It directly tackles the weaknesses of both parent approaches: it gives the partitioning scheme a more principled way to prune branches, and it removes the need for the costly auxiliary optimization step common in GP-based methods.

3.  **Practical Advantages:** By design, the algorithm avoids the need to solve a difficult auxiliary non-convex optimization problem at each step. This is a significant practical advantage over standard GP-UCB, GP-EI, or GP-PI methods, as the cost of optimizing the acquisition function can be substantial. The experimental results in Table 1 confirm that IMGPO is computationally much faster per iteration than these standard methods.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, and the algorithm is described with both a high-level intuitive walkthrough (Section 3.2, Figure 1) and detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The relationship to previous work is clearly articulated, placing the contributions in proper context.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Intuition Behind Exponential Rate:** While the formal proof is provided in the supplement, the main paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* the specific GP-UCB pruning step (step iii) is the key that elevates the convergence rate from polynomial (as in BaMSOO) to exponential. The discussion around the "infinite-metric exploration loss" `ρ_t` in Section 4 is quite technical. A paragraph providing a higher-level argument—for example, explaining how the GP-UCB check allows the algorithm to discard entire subtrees of a certain minimum size with high confidence, leading to an exponential reduction in the search space—would greatly enhance the reader's understanding.

2.  **Scalability with Dimensionality:** The paper correctly notes that the constant `λ` in the regret bound approaches 1 as the input dimension `D` increases, implying a performance degradation in higher dimensions. This is a known issue for space-partitioning methods. While the authors demonstrate a high-dimensional application by embedding IMGPO within REMBO, this feels more like a proof of concept. It would be beneficial to be more explicit about the limitations of the core IMGPO algorithm in moderate-to-high dimensions and to frame the REMBO experiment as a promising direction for future work on scaling, rather than as a complete solution.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new parameter, `Ξ_max`, which limits the depth of the UCB check. The authors state it was fixed to 22 and that increasing it could improve performance at the cost of computation time. For the algorithm to be more readily usable by practitioners, a more detailed discussion on setting this parameter would be valuable. For instance, a small sensitivity analysis on one or two of the test functions showing the trade-off between performance and runtime as `Ξ_max` is varied would strengthen the experimental section. Additionally, the update rule for `Ξ` in line 38 of the algorithm seems somewhat heuristic; a brief justification for this specific update rule would be helpful.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It solves a recognized open problem with a novel, well-motivated algorithm that has both strong theoretical guarantees and practical benefits. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to clarifying intuition and exploring parameter sensitivity, which can be addressed in a future version. The paper is of high quality and will likely be of great interest to both theorists and practitioners in the NIPS community.
