Summary
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Bayesian optimization algorithm that combines Gaussian process (GP) posteriors with a hierarchical partitioning scheme inspired by SOO/DIRECT, and claims an exponential simple-regret rate without requiring the δ-cover sampling of de Freitas et al. [1] or an auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function. The key idea is to avoid committing to a single, possibly loose, bound on f by simultaneously considering an infinite family of bounds (captured via an unknown semi-metric around the global optimum), while using the GP-UCB as a filter to prune overly loose candidates. The algorithm partitions the domain into hyperrectangles, always evaluates at cell centers, and uses UCBs to decide whether to evaluate or to defer with a “GP-based” surrogate value. The analysis introduces an “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt to quantify the price of not fixing a bound, and proves an exponential convergence rate O(λN+Ngp) with λ<1 under Hölder-type smoothness around the optimum, capturing the benefit of GP pruning through both Ngp and a reduction in ρ̄t. Empirically, IMGPO is compared against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI and GP-EI on several standard benchmarks, generally outperforming baselines in simple regret while requiring no inner optimization.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: The “infinite-metric” perspective is a principled way to hedge against bound misspecification, and the ρt notion makes the trade-off analyzable.
- Theoretical advance: An exponential regret bound without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization addresses a known open question raised by Wang et al. [2]. The analysis carefully tracks the impact of L, α, p, dimension D, and GP’s role via ρ̄t and Ngp.
- Algorithmic practicality: The method uses only evaluations at rectangle centers, avoids nonconvex inner solves, and includes heuristics (Ξmax, dummy GP-based labels) that reduce evaluations.
- Empirical evidence: Across multiple deterministic benchmarks, IMGPO typically improves regret faster than baselines, and it slots into REMBO in a high-dimensional example.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Assumptions and constants: Assumption 1–2 essentially impose a Hölder condition near the optimum (standard in SOO), but the tightness and global vs local scope could be stated more precisely. The theorem claims bounds “for the best unknown L, α, p,” which is correct in the sense of existing minimal constants, but the interpretation of the rate in practice would benefit from discussion of how conservative these best-case constants might be. The dependency on D makes λ approach 1 in higher dimensions; the practical meaning of “exponential” then becomes weak in moderate-to-high D.
- Theorem notation/typo: The definition of λ appears misprinted as “λ = 3 − α/(2CDρ̄t) < 1.” It seems intended as λ = 3^{−α/(2CDρ̄t)}. Please correct and restate the bound with all constants defined in one place.
- Role of hyperparameter learning: The UCB validity is asserted with parameter ςM chosen to ensure f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with probability ≥ 1−η. However, with empirical Bayes refitting of kernel hyperparameters at every iteration, the martingale-type concentration used in GP-UCB analyses can break. The paper acknowledges that the property can fail in practice (e.g., Rosenbrock2). Please clarify the probability space and whether the theorem holds under data-dependent hyperparameters, or restrict the theory to fixed hyperparameters and discuss the practical implications.
- Computational complexity: Step (iii) entails computing UCBs over a fully expanded subtree up to depth ξ, capped by Ξmax. A tighter complexity analysis (time and GP-related costs per iteration) and guidance on setting Ξmax and η would help practitioners. An ablation on Ξmax beyond a few test functions would strengthen the empirical section.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are deterministic and low-dimensional (except REMBO). Since many BO tasks are noisy, it would help to discuss whether IMGPO extends to sub-Gaussian observation noise and how the analysis would change. Also, GP-PI/EI performance depends strongly on acquisition optimization; here they are solved by SOO or gradient methods, which may not reflect strong contemporary implementations. Reporting wall-clock along with evaluation counts (you mention a table) and a small ablation measuring ρ̄t and Ngp would make the empirical story more complete.
- Clarity: The algorithm is intricate; the indexing and resolution of “GP-based” labels are easy to get lost in. A small worked example with explicit values (complementing Fig. 1) and a schematic of the accept/reject logic for steps (i)–(v) would aid readability.

Quality
The analysis is careful and connects to prior work (SOO/DOO, BaMSOO, GP-UCB). The main proof ingredients and remarks are insightful, though the hyperparameter-learning caveat needs to be addressed for a fully airtight claim. The empirical evaluation is reasonable for a NIPS paper, albeit limited in scope and lacking ablations on key knobs.

Clarity
Generally well written, but several notational slips (notably λ) and dense pseudocode hinder quick comprehension. Providing a consolidated list of constants, assumptions, and a brief complexity section would improve clarity.

Originality
High. The infinite-metric approach plus GP pruning is a novel synthesis of the “bound-committing” and “bound-free” lines, and the exponential rate without δ-cover/auxiliary optimization is new to my knowledge.

Significance
If the theory stands (with clarified assumptions) and given the observed empirical gains, the contribution is significant for the BO community. It addresses a known gap and offers practical algorithmic ideas likely to be reused.

Pros for acceptance
- Exponential regret bound without δ-cover or inner optimization
- Clear positioning vs SOO, BaMSOO, GP-UCB
- Practical, deterministic algorithm with code release
- Generally strong empirical results

Cons for acceptance
- Bound interpretation weakens in higher D; limited experimental exploration of this
- Theorem statement needs correction and clearer treatment of hyperparameter learning
- Limited ablations and noisy-setting discussion; computational complexity not fully characterized

Questions for the authors
- Please correct and restate Theorem 1 with a precise definition of λ and all constants.
- Does the stated probability guarantee hold under empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates? If not, can you provide a version for fixed hyperparameters and discuss practical implications?
- What is the per-iteration time complexity as a function of D, depth, Ξmax, and GP kernel matrix operations?
- Can IMGPO be extended to noisy observations, and would the rate remain exponential under sub-Gaussian noise?
- Can you report ρ̄t and Ngp over time to empirically validate Remarks 1–2?

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel, well-motivated algorithm and nontrivial theory. With clarification of the theorem statement, discussion of hyperparameter learning, and a brief complexity/ablation addition, it would make a strong NIPS contribution.
