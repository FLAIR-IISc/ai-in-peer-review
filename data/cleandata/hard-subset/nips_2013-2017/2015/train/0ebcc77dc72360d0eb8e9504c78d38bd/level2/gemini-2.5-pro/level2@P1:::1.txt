This paper proposes a new Bayesian optimization algorithm, IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), which achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret. This is a significant result as it addresses a key open problem in the field. Most standard Bayesian optimization methods, like GP-UCB, require solving a difficult auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next query point. While a recent theoretical method [1] achieved an exponential rate, it relied on an impractical `δ-cover` sampling scheme. The proposed IMGPO algorithm elegantly avoids both of these drawbacks.

The core idea is to combine a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, similar to the SOO algorithm, with a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. The novelty lies in its "infinite-metric" approach. Instead of committing to a single smoothness assumption (e.g., a known Lipschitz constant or the one implied by the GP kernel), the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite family of possible semi-metrics that could define the upper bound on the function. It does this by optimistically selecting promising regions at all scales (depths of the partition tree). The GP-UCB is then used not to find the single best point to sample, but rather to prune the set of candidate regions generated by the infinite-metric search. The paper provides a theoretical analysis proving an exponential regret bound and empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrating its superior performance compared to related algorithms like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a substantial contribution by presenting the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. The core concept of combining an "infinite-metric" search, which adapts to unknown smoothness, with a GP-based pruning mechanism is highly novel. This effectively unifies two successful but previously separate lines of research in global optimization and provides a new perspective on how to leverage GP models.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. Theorem 1 provides an exponential regret bound, `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`, which is a clear improvement over the polynomial bounds of the most closely related work (BaMSOO [2]). Furthermore, the analysis in Remarks 1-3 is particularly insightful. It clearly explains the trade-offs involved, such as the role of the GP in reducing the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (`ρ_t`) and why this combined approach can outperform methods that rely on a single, potentially poorly-specified bound.

3.  **Empirical Quality:** The experiments are well-designed and support the paper's claims. IMGPO is compared against a strong set of baselines on a diverse collection of test functions. The results consistently show that IMGPO is either the best or among the best-performing algorithms. The choice to not hand-tune kernels and hyperparameters for each function makes the experimental setting more realistic and compelling. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO also points towards the potential applicability of IMGPO as a subroutine in more complex settings.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the motivation is clearly articulated. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem and related work. The illustrative example in Figure 1 is very helpful for building an intuition of how the algorithm works.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Algorithm Complexity and Clarity:** While the high-level idea is clear, the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is quite dense and challenging to parse. The logic for selecting and filtering candidate nodes (lines 8-24), with its nested loops and conditional breaks, could benefit from a more detailed, step-by-step explanation or a higher-level flowchart. For example, the purpose of the `while true` loop and the mechanism for resolving "GP-based" labels are not immediately obvious.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The algorithm appears to be more computationally intensive per iteration than methods like SOO due to the multiple loops over the tree depth and the UCB computations in step (iii). A more formal discussion of the computational complexity per iteration would be valuable. Specifically, how does the cost of line 22 scale with the new parameter `Ξmax` and the number of nodes in the tree?

3.  **Scalability with Dimension:** The paper commendably notes that the convergence rate degrades with dimensionality `D` (as `λ` approaches 1). While the REMBO experiment is a good demonstration of a potential solution, the limitation of the core algorithm deserves a bit more discussion. It would be helpful to clarify how the constant `C` in the regret bound depends on `D`, as this is key to understanding the severity of this curse of dimensionality.

4.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a new parameter, `Ξmax`. The authors state that increasing it could improve performance at the cost of computation time. To improve the practical utility of the paper, it would be beneficial to include a brief sensitivity analysis or provide more guidance on how to set this parameter.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. The strengths, particularly the novel methodology and strong theoretical results, far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the clarity of the algorithm's presentation and practical implementation details. This work will likely be of great interest to researchers in optimization and machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
