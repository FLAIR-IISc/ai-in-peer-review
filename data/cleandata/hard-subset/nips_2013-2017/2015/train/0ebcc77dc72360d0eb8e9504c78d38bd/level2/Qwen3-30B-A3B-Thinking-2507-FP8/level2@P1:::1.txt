

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which claims to achieve exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of existing methods: the need for auxiliary optimization (which involves solving an additional non-convex optimization problem) and the impracticality of δ-cover sampling. The main contribution is a method that considers "infinitely many possible candidates of the bounds" encoded in a semi-metric, allowing for exponential convergence.

The paper is well-structured and builds appropriately on previous work. The authors clearly position their contribution relative to de Freitas et al.'s exponential convergence result [1] (which requires δ-cover sampling) and Wang et al.'s BaMSOO [2] (which achieves polynomial regret bounds). The theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) provides a regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which is stronger than previous results. The authors also provide insightful remarks that clarify how the tightness of the GP-based bound affects performance (Remark 1), the role of GP (Remark 2), and why considering multiple bounds can be advantageous (Remark 3).

The experimental section compares IMGPO with several baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) across eight test functions. The results show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly for the higher-dimensional functions. The authors properly acknowledge that their GP prior can be misleading in some cases (e.g., for Rosenbrock2), which demonstrates appropriate self-critique.

The paper's strengths include its clear theoretical contribution, comprehensive experimental validation, and detailed algorithm description with pseudocode. The authors have effectively addressed a practical limitation in Bayesian optimization that has been recognized in the literature [1, 2].

Some weaknesses are worth noting. First, while the paper claims to be the first to achieve exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, it would be helpful to more explicitly contrast with related work that might have achieved similar results. For example, the paper cites Kandasamy et al. [20] on high-dimensional Bayesian optimization, but doesn't clarify how IMGPO differs from or relates to their approach. Second, the theoretical analysis relies on Assumption 2 (about the semi-metric), which might be restrictive for some practical applications. The paper mentions scalability challenges for higher dimensions (D increases), but could more thoroughly discuss how this limitation affects practical utility. Third, while the paper mentions that the GP prior might be misleading (as seen in the Rosenbrock2 case), it would be valuable to discuss how to detect when this occurs and what alternatives might be available.

The paper's clarity is generally good, though some technical details (like the precise role of Ξmax) could be clarified for readers less familiar with the field. The writing is accessible and well-organized, with the pseudocode being particularly helpful for implementation.

In terms of significance, the paper makes an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization. The exponential convergence rate is theoretically valuable, and the practical benefits demonstrated in experiments suggest potential for real-world applications. The authors' insight that "not relying on a particular bound can be advantageous" offers a broader perspective that could influence other optimization methods beyond Bayesian optimization.

The paper's main limitation is its focus on deterministic functions, which may limit applicability to more common noisy optimization scenarios. While the authors cite Srinivas et al. [15] as foundational work on Gaussian process optimization, they don't explicitly discuss how their method would extend to the noisy case.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear experimental validation. The paper makes a novel and valuable addition to the Bayesian optimization literature by resolving two practical limitations of previous exponential-convergence methods. While there are some minor weaknesses in the theoretical framing and experimental scope, these do not undermine the paper's significant contribution.

Pro: Novel approach to Bayesian optimization that achieves exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling; clear theoretical analysis with concrete regret bound; well-executed experiments demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines.

Con: Some theoretical assumptions (Assumption 2) might be restrictive; limited discussion of how to handle cases where GP prior is misleading; minimal discussion of extension to noisy optimization settings.

The paper meets all four NIPS evaluation criteria: it is technically sound (quality), well-written (clarity), makes a novel contribution (originality), and addresses a practically important problem with significant theoretical advances (significance). I recommend acceptance.
