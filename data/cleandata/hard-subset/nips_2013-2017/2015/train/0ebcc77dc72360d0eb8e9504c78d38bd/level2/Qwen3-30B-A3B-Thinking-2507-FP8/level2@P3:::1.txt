

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing two key limitations in prior work. The authors introduce a theoretical framework that considers infinitely many possible bounds encoded by an unknown semi-metric, rather than relying on a single upper bound from the Gaussian process prior. The algorithm uses hierarchical partitioning to simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all candidate bounds, with the GP prior used to prune less promising candidates.

The theoretical contribution is significant, with Theorem 1 establishing an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+N_gp)) where λ < 1. This improves upon the polynomial regret bounds of previous methods like BaMSOO. The authors provide a clear analysis of how the tightness of the GP-based bound affects performance (Remarks 1-3), offering valuable theoretical insights into when and why the infinite-metric approach outperforms single-bound methods. The experimental results on eight benchmark functions demonstrate consistent improvements over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, with IMGPO showing faster convergence in most cases.

The paper excels in theoretical rigor, providing a well-structured analysis that connects the algorithm to previous work on DIRECT and SOO. The relationship to prior research is clearly articulated, particularly regarding the limitations of GP-UCB and the significance of eliminating δ-cover sampling. The algorithm description is detailed with comprehensive pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and sufficient technical depth to allow reproduction.

A key strength is the practical perspective the authors provide. They address the tension between theoretical guarantees and practical implementation by demonstrating that the algorithm can be implemented without the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure while maintaining exponential convergence. The discussion of how the GP prior's quality affects performance (e.g., the misleading case with Rosenbrock2) shows careful consideration of real-world applicability.

Several aspects could be improved to strengthen the paper. The dependence of the convergence rate on dimensionality (λ approaching 1 as D increases) warrants more discussion of practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies beyond the brief mention of additional assumptions. The paper could provide clearer guidance on selecting Ξmax in practice, as the experiments show performance improvements with larger values but at the cost of increased computation. While the authors note that Ξmax = 22 worked well for most functions, the relationship between Ξmax and problem characteristics is not fully explored.

The experimental section is generally strong but could be enhanced by including more recent Bayesian optimization methods in the comparison, such as those leveraging additive structure or other dimensionality reduction techniques. The paper's claim that the GP prior can be "blinded" to good kernel parameters (using a default Matern kernel with default hyperparameters) is reasonable, but the paper could provide more justification for this assumption in practical settings where prior knowledge might be available.

The paper's main contribution is well-positioned within the Bayesian optimization literature. It successfully bridges the theoretical gap between methods that rely on a single bound (like GP-UCB) and those that consider multiple bounds (like DIRECT and SOO), demonstrating that combining both approaches can yield better theoretical guarantees and practical performance. The authors' insight that a non-tight bound can still be useful represents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with appropriate mathematical notation and sufficient detail for reproduction. The supplementary material appears to contain necessary proofs, though the main paper could benefit from a more concise presentation of the key theoretical insights. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate benchmark functions and careful implementation details.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, particularly by addressing a long-standing open problem regarding exponential convergence without impractical sampling procedures. The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the relationship between bound tightness and optimization performance, which could inform future work in Bayesian optimization and related areas like bandit algorithms and reinforcement learning. While there are some practical considerations to address (dimensionality limitations, parameter selection), the core contribution is significant and well-supported.

The paper meets the quality standards expected at NIPS, with a technically sound theoretical analysis, clear exposition, and compelling experimental results. The originality is strong, with a novel algorithmic approach that addresses a clear limitation in the literature. The significance is high, as exponential convergence represents a major improvement over previous polynomial regret bounds, with practical implications for applications requiring efficient global optimization of black-box functions.
