- Proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization algorithm achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization.  
- Builds on de Freitas et al. (2012) and Wang et al. (2013), addressing their practical limitations.  
- Key innovation: “infinite-metric” approach that considers all possible semi-metric bounds instead of a single GP-estimated bound.  
- Theoretically rigorous: defines infinite-metric exploration loss ρ̄_t and proves regret bounds O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 (Theorem 1).  
- Provides insightful analysis on how GP-bound tightness affects performance (Remarks 1–3), showing non-tight bounds can still help.  
- Resolves an open problem by obtaining exponential convergence for GP-based optimization without impractical assumptions.  
- Algorithm presentation is clear with intuitive hierarchical partitioning, a detailed example (Fig.1), and comprehensive pseudocode (Alg.1).  
- Experimental evaluation is broad and strong: comparisons to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI across eight benchmarks and dimensions 1–1000.  
- IMGPO consistently outperforms baselines, notably on multimodal functions like Branin and Hartmann3.  
- Practical experiment setup: single isotropic Matern kernel with default hyperparameters to reflect real-world unknown-kernel scenarios.  
- Ablation study on Ξ_max shows its effect on performance and computation trade-offs.  
- Originality: extends “multiple bounds” ideas (DIRECT/SOO) to Bayesian optimization with rigorous support, distinct from BaMSOO and GP-UCB.  
- Significance: removes key practical barriers in Bayesian optimization, making exponential convergence more applicable for hyperparameter tuning.  
- Main limitation: exponential convergence weakens in high dimensions due to a dimension-dependent λ term; scalability not fully resolved.  
- Suggested improvement: more thorough discussion and analysis of dimensionality effects, especially on ρ̄_t, and mitigation strategies beyond brief mentions.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-supported by theory and experiments; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address scalability discussion.
