

The paper introduces a novel Gaussian process-based global optimization algorithm (IMGPO) that achieves exponential regret convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing a key limitation in prior work by de Freitas et al. (2012) and Wang et al. (2013). The authors situate their contribution within the bound-based search framework (Munos 2011, Jones et al. 1993), clearly differentiating IMGPO from BaMSOO (Wang et al. 2013) and SOO (Munos 2011) through its infinite-metric exploration mechanism. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) rigorously establishes the exponential regret bound and provides valuable insights into how the GP prior's looseness interacts with the unknown semi-metric bound, addressing a significant gap in the literature.

Strengths include a well-structured exposition of the algorithm and its theoretical foundations, clear articulation of the problem's context within Bayesian optimization, and the insightful analysis of the trade-offs between bound tightness and exploration (Remarks 1–3). The algorithm's implementation details are sufficiently precise for reproducibility.

Weaknesses center on the experimental evaluation, which falls short of NIPS standards for Bayesian optimization papers. Specifically: (1) The comparison lacks statistical rigor, with no multiple initializations or significance testing across random seeds (standard practice in BO, e.g., Snoek et al., NIPS 2012); (2) Critical state-of-the-art baselines are omitted, including information-theoretic methods like Entropy Search (Srinivas et al., ICML 2010); (3) All experiments use synthetic functions up to 6 dimensions, with no validation on real-world parameter tuning tasks or higher-dimensional problems (e.g., 1000D Sin2 as in Wang et al., AISTATS 2013), limiting the demonstration of practical utility. While the algorithm's theoretical novelty is compelling, the experimental section fails to convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing methods in meaningful scenarios.
