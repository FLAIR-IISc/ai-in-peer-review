Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a Bayesian optimization algorithm that uses hierarchical space partitioning (hyperrectangles) to choose future evaluations, guided by a GP posterior through an upper confidence bound (UCB). This design avoids the usual auxiliary nonconvex optimization over an acquisition function and removes the need for the δ-cover sampling procedure required by earlier exponential-convergence analyses. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing an exponential simple-regret bound, improving over prior GP-based methods that either required δ-covers or achieved only polynomial rates.

The approach is closely related to bound-based global optimization and, in particular, to DIRECT, SOO, and BaMSOO. Compared to BaMSOO, which combines SOO with GP-UCB and yields polynomial regret, the proposed method claims exponential regret by leveraging GP information to prune an infinite family of candidate bounds. Conceptually, the work bridges lines of research that rely on fixed smoothness/Lipschitz-type bounds and those that avoid committing to a particular bound.

Strengths:
- Eliminates the inner global optimization typically used to maximize UCB/EI, which can be costly and brittle in practice.
- Provides a clear algorithmic description with an illustrative 1D example; the procedure is understandable and implementable.
- Presents a theoretically stronger convergence guarantee (exponential regret), addressing a notable open issue from prior work.
- The contribution is novel and relevant to the Bayesian optimization community.

Main reservation:
- The experiments appear to be restricted to 1D test functions (this is not explicitly stated). A space-partitioning scheme based on hyperrectangles typically suffers in higher dimensions because the number of partitions required to maintain resolution grows exponentially. DIRECT, a close relative, is known to degrade badly as dimension increases, and similar concerns likely apply here. While this does not diminish the theoretical contribution, it is important for practical impact. At a minimum, the paper should explicitly discuss scalability, relate the bound’s dependence on dimension to practical performance, and, if possible, provide experiments beyond 1D.

Quality:
- The theoretical development appears sound, and the claimed exponential regret is a meaningful advance. The algorithmic components are standard (GP posterior, UCB) combined with a principled partitioning and pruning scheme.
- The empirical evaluation, as presented, seems too limited to support practical claims, especially regarding scalability.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and the algorithm is clearly explained with helpful figures. Some statements would benefit from clarification (see minor comments).
- It would be helpful to be explicit about the dimensionality of all experiments and to discuss computational complexity as dimension increases.

Originality:
- The idea of integrating GP-based optimism with a partition-based search that avoids auxiliary optimization is novel in this precise form. The infinite-metric perspective and analysis that lead to exponential rates are original relative to SOO/BaMSOO and DIRECT.

Significance:
- If the exponential regret translates into empirical gains beyond low-dimensional settings, the impact would be substantial. As it stands, the theoretical advance is significant, but the practical significance is uncertain without higher-dimensional evidence or a more detailed discussion of scaling.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contribution: exponential convergence without δ-cover and without auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Clear algorithm and analysis; good connection to prior methods (DIRECT/SOO/BaMSOO).
- Practical motivation: avoiding inner global optimization is valuable.

Cons for acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation appears limited to 1D; practical scalability is unclear.
- Lack of discussion about high-dimensional behavior and computational costs inherent to the partitioning scheme.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- UCB “for brevity”: Could the method be instantiated with other acquisition functions such as EI or PI while preserving the key guarantees? Or is UCB essential for the analysis?
- “We simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds”: Please clarify this statement. Concretely, how does the algorithm operationalize “all candidates” of the semi-metric bound, and how does GP-based pruning affect this set?
- In the 1D example, “At n = 16, the far right interval is divided, but no function evaluation occurs”: Please explain why the evaluation is skipped (i.e., because the UCB at the new center is below the current best, so a GP-based surrogate value is temporarily used) to make the mechanism explicit.
- Please state the dimensionality of all benchmarks and, if possible, include results beyond 1D or a detailed discussion of expected scaling, including the relationship between depth/branching, the regret bound’s constants, and wall-clock cost.

Minor comments:
- p.2: If UCB is chosen “for brevity,” specify whether EI/PI could be substituted and what would change in the analysis.
- p.3: Clarify the phrase about “simultaneously” conducting global and local searches with “all candidates of the bounds.”
- p.4: Add a sentence explaining why no function evaluation occurs at n = 16 in the example.

Overall assessment:
A technically strong and clearly presented paper with a novel algorithm and a compelling theoretical result. The main weakness is the apparent limitation of empirical validation to 1D and the lack of discussion about high-dimensional scaling. Addressing the scalability concerns—at least via discussion and, ideally, with experiments beyond 1D—would substantially strengthen the submission.
