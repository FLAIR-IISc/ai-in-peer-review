

The paper introduces a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization that generates future samples through space partitioning via interval subdivision. By employing UCB, this approach avoids the internal global optimization required to select the next sample. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that the method achieves exponential regret bounds, representing a significant theoretical improvement over prior work. The algorithm is clearly described, with a well-explained and illustrated example. The contribution is novel, and the paper is generally well-presented.  

My primary concern is the experimental evaluation, which appears limited to 1D test functions (not explicitly stated). I suspect the interval partitioning strategy does not scale effectively to higher dimensions, as the required resolution would grow exponentially with dimensionality. This limitation is particularly concerning given the approach's conceptual similarity to the DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from poor scalability in high dimensions. The paper should address this practical limitation, as it is significant for real-world applications despite not affecting the theoretical contribution.  

Minor comments:  
- p.2: UCB is introduced "for brevity." The paper implies UCB is mandatory without justification; could alternative acquisition functions (e.g., expected improvement) be considered?  
- p.3: The statement "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is ambiguous and requires clarification.  
- p.4: The note "At n=16, the far right... but no function evaluation occurs" lacks explanation; please clarify why no evaluation is performed at this point.  

The work is theoretically sound and novel but is currently evaluated only on 1D problems.
