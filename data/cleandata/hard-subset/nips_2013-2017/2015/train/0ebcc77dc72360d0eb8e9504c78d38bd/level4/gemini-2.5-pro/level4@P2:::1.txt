### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) algorithm, a novel method for Bayesian optimization (BO) of deterministic black-box functions. The main contribution is to provide a GP-based algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without two common and problematic requirements: 1) solving an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next query point, and 2) relying on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The latter requirement was a significant limitation of prior work by de Freitas et al. (2012) which also established an exponential rate. The core idea of IMGPO is to hybridize a GP-UCB approach with a hierarchical partitioning strategy inspired by algorithms like SOO. This allows the method to simultaneously consider an infinite family of potential (and unknown) semi-metric bounds on the function, using the GP-derived bound to efficiently prune the search space. This approach aims to be robust to a misspecified GP prior while still benefiting from the information it provides.

### **Review and Justification**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The idea of combining GP-based methods with the infinite-metric optimization framework is novel and addresses a well-defined open problem. However, the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous or comprehensive to fully support the claims of the paper's practical advantages, making it fall marginally below the acceptance threshold for NIPS.

#### **Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths)**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The work tackles an important open problem identified in previous literature (Wang et al., 2014)—achieving exponential convergence in GP-based optimization without δ-cover sampling. The proposed unification of the probabilistic GP approach with the deterministic partitioning approach of SOO/DIRECT is an elegant and novel contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound, culminating in an exponential regret bound (Theorem 1). The discussion in Remarks 1-3 is particularly insightful, providing clear intuition on the trade-offs involved and why the proposed hybrid strategy can be more robust than methods that rely solely on a single (potentially loose) bound provided by the GP.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors effectively situate their work within the broader context of bound-based search methods, making the contribution and its novelty clear to the reader.

#### **Arguments against Acceptance (Weaknesses)**

The primary weakness of this submission is the experimental evaluation, which does not meet the standard of empirical work in the BO field at a top-tier conference like NIPS.

*   **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** Standard practice in BO is to evaluate algorithms over multiple runs with different random initializations to account for sensitivity to the initial query points. The results should be presented with means and confidence intervals (e.g., standard error). The paper shows results from what appears to be a single run for the deterministic methods and a single seed for the others. This makes it impossible to assess whether the observed performance differences are statistically significant or merely anecdotal.
*   **Incomplete Baselines:** The comparison is made against a limited set of baselines. A convincing BO paper should compare against the current state of the art. The omission of information-theoretic acquisition functions, such as Entropy Search (ES) or Predictive Entropy Search (PES), is a major gap, as these methods are often considered top performers in terms of sample efficiency.
*   **Absence of Real-World Application:** The evaluation is confined to synthetic benchmark functions, most of which are in low dimensions. A key selling point for BO is its success in complex, real-world problems like hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance on at least one such practical task would significantly strengthen the paper and substantiate its claims of utility.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper could be substantially improved by addressing the weaknesses in the experimental section. Specifically, the authors should:
1.  Rerun the experiments with multiple random initializations for all algorithms and report the average performance with error bars.
2.  Include stronger, more contemporary baselines, particularly from the information-theoretic family (e.g., Entropy Search).
3.  Add an experiment on a real-world problem, such as tuning the hyperparameters of a neural network or another machine learning model, to showcase the practical benefits of the proposed method.

While the theoretical ideas in this paper are interesting and deserve attention, the current empirical evidence is not strong enough to warrant publication at NIPS.
