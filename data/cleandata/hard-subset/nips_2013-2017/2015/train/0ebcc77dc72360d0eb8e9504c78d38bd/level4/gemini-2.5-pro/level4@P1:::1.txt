This paper presents IMGPO, a novel global optimization algorithm based on Gaussian Processes. The key contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without requiring auxiliary optimization or the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. This work extends the theoretical results of de Freitas et al. (2012) by removing the reliance on this impractical sampling assumption, which has been a noted open problem. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of situating their algorithm within the broader context of bound-based search methods, making the paper's contribution and novelty clear. The theoretical analysis appears sound and represents a solid contribution that should be of interest to the Bayesian optimization community.

However, while the theoretical contributions are strong, my enthusiasm is tempered by an experimental section that, in my opinion, falls short of the current standards for empirical work in this field. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  **Lack of Statistical Comparison:** The experiments appear to be based on a single run for each algorithm. Standard practice in Bayesian optimization is to perform multiple runs with different random initializations to account for sensitivity to the initial design points. Reporting the mean performance and a measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation or error bars) is crucial for a robust and meaningful comparison. Without this, it is difficult to confidently conclude that the observed performance differences are statistically significant.

2.  **Missing State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper compares IMGPO against relevant baselines like SOO and BaMSOO. However, it omits comparisons against several other state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods. To make a convincing case for the practical superiority of a new algorithm, it should be benchmarked against the strongest existing competitors. In particular, information-theoretic approaches such as Entropy Search (ES) or Predictive Entropy Search (PES) are often considered top performers and would be essential baselines for a thorough comparison.

3.  **Absence of Real-World Applications:** All experiments are conducted on synthetic benchmark functions. While these functions are useful for controlled analysis, a key motivation for Bayesian optimization is solving real-world problems like hyperparameter tuning of machine learning models, robotics, or experimental design. Demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on at least one such practical application would significantly strengthen the paper by illustrating its utility beyond synthetic test cases.

In summary, the paper introduces a novel algorithm with interesting and significant theoretical properties. However, the empirical evaluation is not rigorous enough to fully substantiate the claims of its practical advantages. Strengthening the experimental section by incorporating multiple runs for statistical validation, including stronger state-of-the-art baselines, and adding a real-world application would make this a much more compelling submission.
