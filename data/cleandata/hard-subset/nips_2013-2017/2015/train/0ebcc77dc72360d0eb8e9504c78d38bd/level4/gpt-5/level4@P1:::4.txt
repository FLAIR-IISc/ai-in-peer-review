Summary
The paper proposes a new Gaussian process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that claims exponential convergence without two common requirements: (i) auxiliary nonconvex optimization to maximize an acquisition function and (ii) δ-cover sampling used in prior theoretical work. The key idea is to combine GP-derived upper confidence bounds with an “infinite-metric” search that considers all possible (unknown) semi-metric upper bounds near the global optimizer. The algorithm uses hierarchical partitioning, selectively subdividing hyperrectangles based on both GP UCBs and comparisons of center values across scales, and skips expensive function evaluations when the UCB is provably below the current best.

The authors provide a regret bound of the form O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 (with probability at least 1 − η), improving on the polynomial rates in prior GP-based methods that avoid δ-cover sampling (e.g., BaMSOO). They also analyze how the tightness of the GP UCB and the use of infinite-metric exploration affect convergence. Empirically, the method is compared against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI and GP-EI on several benchmark functions, showing faster convergence in most cases, and significant wall-clock savings relative to traditional GP optimization that requires solving auxiliary acquisition optimizations.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: removing both δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization while retaining exponential convergence is a compelling goal.
- Conceptual novelty: the “infinite-metric” perspective and the way GP UCBs are used to prune candidate bounds are interesting and well-motivated.
- Theoretical contribution: an exponential simple-regret bound under stated assumptions, with informative discussion of how GP tightness and exploration losses enter the rate.
- Practicality: avoids solving a separate nonconvex optimization at each iteration and exploits GP only where it helps, which appears to reduce computation in practice.
- Presentation: the paper is generally well written and didactic, with helpful figures illustrating the algorithm’s behavior.

Weaknesses and limitations (as I understand them)
- Expertise: I am not an expert in GP optimization, so I cannot fully verify the proofs or compare all nuances with the latest literature; my positive assessment of the theoretical contribution is based on the clarity and plausibility of the arguments presented.
- Scalability: the bound degrades with dimension D (λ can approach 1 as D grows), and the paper notes that additional assumptions may be needed for high-dimensional problems. While this is candidly discussed, it limits immediate applicability in very high dimensions.
- Hyperparameter learning: as with many GP-based methods, performance can depend on kernel choice and hyperparameter estimation; the paper uses a standard Matern kernel and empirical Bayes, but robustness to mis-specification is only indirectly illustrated.

Quality
- The claims are supported by a clear algorithm description, a formal regret bound with assumptions stated, and experiments on standard benchmarks. While I cannot check all technical details, nothing in the presentation raised red flags.

Clarity
- The paper is largely clear and well organized. The figures are effective at explaining the selection and subdivision process.

Originality
- To my knowledge (again, with the caveat of limited expertise), the combination of avoiding both δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization while achieving an exponential rate is novel, and the “infinite-metric” analysis provides a new lens on bound-based search.

Significance
- If the theoretical and empirical findings hold broadly, the approach could influence practical Bayesian optimization by reducing overhead and improving convergence on multimodal problems without requiring δ-cover sampling.
