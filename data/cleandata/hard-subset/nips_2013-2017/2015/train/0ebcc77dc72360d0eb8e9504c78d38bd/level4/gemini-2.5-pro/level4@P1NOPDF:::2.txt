This paper introduces a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization that employs a space-partitioning algorithm to select subsequent evaluation points. A key advantage of this approach is that, by using an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) criterion on the partitions, it circumvents the difficult sub-problem of globally optimizing an acquisition function to choose the next sample. The theoretical analysis is a strong point of the paper, demonstrating that the proposed method achieves an exponential regret bound, which represents a significant improvement over prior art. The algorithm is described clearly and effectively illustrated with a running example. Overall, the paper presents a novel contribution in a well-written and clear manner.

My primary reservation concerns the scalability of the proposed method and the scope of the experimental evaluation. The experiments appear to be limited to one-dimensional test functions, although this is not explicitly stated. I am concerned that the interval-partitioning strategy may not scale effectively to higher-dimensional problems. As the dimensionality increases, the number of hyperrectangles required to partition the space would likely grow exponentially to maintain a given resolution. This issue is reminiscent of the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to scale poorly with increasing problem dimension.

While this potential limitation does not diminish the paper's theoretical contribution, it is a crucial consideration for the method's practical applicability. The authors should address this scalability issue in the paper, perhaps by discussing the expected performance in higher dimensions or by including experiments on at least a low-dimensional (e.g., 2D or 3D) problem.

Minor comments:

*   p. 2: The paper states that UCB is used "for brevity." This phrasing is slightly ambiguous. Is the use of UCB fundamental to the theoretical analysis, or could other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, also be used within this framework?

*   p. 3: The statement "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" was unclear. Could the authors please elaborate on what this means in the context of their algorithm?

*   p. 4: In the example, it is stated that "At n=16, the far right... but no function evaluation occurs." For the reader's benefit, it would be helpful to briefly explain why no evaluation takes place in that interval at that step.
