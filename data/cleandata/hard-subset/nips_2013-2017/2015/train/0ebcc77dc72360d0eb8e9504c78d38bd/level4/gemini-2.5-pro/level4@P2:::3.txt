This paper presents the Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) algorithm, a novel method for Bayesian optimization of black-box functions. The primary contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without requiring two common but often impractical components of previous methods: auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling.

The problem of global optimization is central to many fields, and Bayesian optimization using Gaussian Processes (GPs) is a standard approach. However, many state-of-the-art GP-based methods, such as GP-UCB, require solving a non-convex auxiliary optimization problem at each step to select the next query point. While often computationally cheaper than evaluating the true objective function, this can still be a significant burden. Furthermore, the main prior work demonstrating exponential convergence for GP optimization [1] relied on a δ-cover sampling procedure, which is widely considered computationally impractical. A subsequent algorithm, BaMSOO [2], successfully removed the need for δ-cover sampling but could only achieve a polynomial regret bound, leaving the development of a practical, exponential-rate algorithm as an open problem.

This paper closes that gap. The authors' key insight is to combine the strengths of two distinct lines of research: bound-based methods that rely on a known model of smoothness (like GP-UCB) and those that do not assume a known smoothness constant, instead implicitly considering an infinite family of possibilities (like the DIRECT and SOO algorithms). IMGPO uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, similar to SOO, but leverages the GP-UCB to prune branches of the search tree that are unlikely to contain the optimum. This synergy allows the algorithm to avoid auxiliary optimization while using the GP posterior to accelerate the search, leading to the proven exponential convergence rate.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contribution is significant, providing the first exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm (Theorem 1). The analysis is insightful, particularly the remarks in Section 4 which clearly articulate how the GP component and the "infinite-metric" search strategy each contribute to the algorithm's performance. This provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding why combining these approaches is beneficial. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on eight benchmark functions, with dimensions ranging from 1 to 1000. The empirical results are strong, showing that IMGPO generally outperforms relevant baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods (GP-PI/EI). The authors are also careful to note the one case where SOO was competitive and provide a reasonable explanation (a misleading GP prior), demonstrating an honest evaluation of their method's performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction by contextualizing the work within the existing literature. The core ideas are explained with remarkable clarity, particularly through the illustrative 1-D example in Figure 1 and Section 3.2, which provides an excellent intuition for how the algorithm operates. The detailed pseudocode in Algorithm 1, combined with the description in Section 3.3, provides sufficient information for an expert to reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. It directly addresses and solves an open problem explicitly stated in previous work [2]. The primary novelty lies in the elegant synthesis of hierarchical partitioning methods (which do not require a known smoothness constant) with GP-based Bayesian optimization (which provides a probabilistic smoothness model). While BaMSOO [2] also combined these elements, this paper's formulation is what enables the exponential convergence guarantee. This unification of two previously parallel research paths is a significant conceptual contribution.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. By removing the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, the authors have developed an algorithm that is both theoretically powerful (exponential convergence) and practically appealing. Researchers and practitioners working on hyperparameter tuning, engineering design, or any problem involving expensive black-box optimization are likely to find this method useful. The analytical framework provided could also inspire similar hybrid approaches in other bound-based search problems, such as planning and reinforcement learning, as the authors suggest.

A minor weakness is that the scalability with respect to dimensionality remains a challenge, as the authors honestly acknowledge in Section 4. The convergence rate parameter λ depends on the dimension D, suggesting performance will degrade in very high-dimensional spaces without additional structural assumptions. However, their experiment successfully using IMGPO within the REMBO framework [14] demonstrates a promising path forward for this issue.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Solves an important and explicitly stated open problem in the field.
*   **Pro:** Provides the first practical GP-based optimization algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   **Pro:** The novel combination of hierarchical partitioning with GP-based pruning is elegant and effective.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, with strong theoretical analysis backed by convincing empirical results.
*   **Pro:** The work is of high significance and is likely to be widely used and built upon by the community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Con (minor):** Scalability in high dimensions remains a concern, though this is a common challenge for most global optimization algorithms and the authors address it appropriately.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong and clear contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It is well-executed, well-written, and addresses a significant problem. I strongly recommend acceptance.
