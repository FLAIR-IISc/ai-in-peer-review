

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing a significant limitation in existing methods. The authors build upon the GP-UCB framework while resolving a key open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] and de Freitas et al. [1] regarding the impracticality of δ-cover sampling for exponential convergence.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization. The core innovation lies in the authors' insight that instead of relying on a single bound from the GP prior, their method simultaneously considers all possible bounds encoded by an unknown semi-metric, thereby avoiding the need for δ-cover sampling. This approach elegantly combines two distinct strands of optimization research: the bound-based methods (like SOO [18]) and Bayesian methods (like GP-UCB), providing a more flexible framework that can leverage the strengths of both.

The theoretical analysis (Section 4) is particularly impressive. The authors prove an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, significantly improving upon the polynomial regret bound of BaMSOO [2]. The analysis carefully examines how different components of the algorithm (UCB tightness, GP usage, and infinite-metric exploration) affect the convergence rate, providing valuable theoretical insights that go beyond previous work. The authors also provide nuanced discussions of the effects of various parameters through Remarks 1-3, offering a deeper understanding of the algorithm's behavior.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. Section 3.2 provides an excellent visual explanation of the algorithm's operation with a simple 1D example, making the technical content accessible. Section 3.3's pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is well-structured and would enable straightforward implementation, fulfilling the requirement for reproducibility. The authors effectively contextualize their work against previous methods, clearly explaining how IMGPO differs from BaMSOO [2] and other related approaches.

The experimental results (Section 5) are compelling and well-designed. The authors compare IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight multimodal test functions of varying dimensionality (1-1000). The results consistently demonstrate IMGPO's superiority, particularly in the more challenging high-dimensional cases. The authors' decision to use a single kernel (isotropic Matern with ν=5/2) with default hyperparameters across all experiments reflects practical considerations, as the knowledge of optimal kernel and hyperparameters is typically unavailable in real-world applications.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a fundamental limitation in Bayesian optimization that has persisted since the introduction of GP-UCB. The exponential convergence rate achieved without the need for impractical δ-cover sampling represents a substantial theoretical and practical advancement. The authors' framework also provides a valuable new perspective on when and why a non-tight bound can still be useful, with potential implications beyond Bayesian optimization for other bound-based methods like A* search or PAC-MDP algorithms.

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few minor limitations worth noting. The authors acknowledge that the exponential convergence rate may become less effective in very high dimensions (D > 10), as λ approaches 1. The paper could further explore how to address this dimensionality challenge through additional assumptions or modifications. Additionally, while the authors discuss the effect of Ξmax in experiments, a more systematic analysis of how to optimally select this parameter would strengthen the practical implementation guidance.

The paper's quality is excellent, with rigorous theoretical analysis, clear exposition, and thorough experimental validation. The contribution is clearly original and significant, advancing the state of the art in Bayesian optimization by resolving a long-standing open problem. The work is well-positioned to influence both theoretical and practical approaches to global optimization in machine learning.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization and aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-quality scientific contributions. It successfully addresses a fundamental limitation in the literature while providing valuable theoretical insights. The clarity of presentation, strength of theoretical results, and compelling experimental validation make this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

Pro: Addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization with exponential convergence without impractical assumptions; provides rigorous theoretical analysis with novel insights; clear, well-organized presentation with helpful examples and pseudocode; compelling experimental results across diverse test functions.

Con: Some scalability challenges in very high dimensions remain (though the paper acknowledges this); more detailed guidance on selecting Ξmax would be beneficial for practitioners.

The paper meets all four criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers a novel approach to a longstanding problem (Originality), and provides a significant advancement with practical implications (Significance). I recommend acceptance.
