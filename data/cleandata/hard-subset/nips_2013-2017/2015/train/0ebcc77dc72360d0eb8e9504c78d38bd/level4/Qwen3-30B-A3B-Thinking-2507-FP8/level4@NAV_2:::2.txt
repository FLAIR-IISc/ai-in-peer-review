

The paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing a key limitation of prior work by de Freitas et al. [1]. This contribution builds upon and extends the theoretical framework established in recent NIPS publications, particularly the work on Bayesian optimization with exponential convergence [1] and the SOO algorithm [18], while bridging the gap between methods that rely on specific upper bounds and those that consider multiple possibilities.

Strengths:
- The theoretical contribution is significant, with a rigorous proof demonstrating exponential regret bounds (O(λ^N+Ngp) with λ < 1), which improves upon the polynomial regret bounds of BaMSOO [2].
- The algorithmic design is clearly explained with a well-illustrated example (Figure 1), making the approach accessible to readers.
- The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the benefits of considering multiple bounds through Remarks 1-3, offering a more nuanced perspective than previous work.
- The implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility, and the authors provide publicly available code.

Weaknesses:
- The experimental evaluation is limited to 1D test functions, which fails to demonstrate the algorithm's practical utility for higher-dimensional problems where most real-world applications reside.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability concerns associated with the interval partitioning approach, particularly given the known limitations of similar methods like DIRECT [19] in high dimensions.
- The statement in Section 3.1 ("we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds") lacks clarity and requires elaboration.
- The explanation for why no function evaluation occurs at certain points (e.g., "At n=16, the far right interval is divided but no function evaluation occurs" on p.4) is insufficient.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The theoretical contribution represents a clear advancement in Bayesian optimization with a rigorous proof of exponential convergence.
- The paper provides a principled approach to combining multiple bounds that has implications beyond Bayesian optimization.
- The technical content is sound and well-justified with proofs.

Con arguments for rejection:
- The experimental evaluation is insufficient for a conference paper, as it fails to demonstrate practical utility in higher dimensions where the method's claims would be most relevant.
- The paper does not adequately address scalability concerns that would be critical for practitioners.

The authors should address these concerns before publication by:
1) Expanding the experimental evaluation to include higher-dimensional problems (at least 2D, preferably 5D or higher) to demonstrate practical utility.
2) Discussing the scalability limitations of their partitioning approach in the context of high-dimensional optimization, potentially comparing with methods like REMBO [14].
3) Clarifying the statement about "simultaneously conducting global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" in Section 3.1.
4) Providing a more detailed explanation for why function evaluations are skipped at certain points.

While the theoretical contribution is strong and represents an important advancement, the lack of higher-dimensional experiments significantly weakens the paper's practical impact, which is a key consideration for NIPS. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
