### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to maximize non-convex black-box functions. The main contribution is an algorithm that achieves a provable exponential convergence rate without two major practical drawbacks of previous methods: the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization to maximize an acquisition function (common in most GP-based methods like GP-UCB) and the reliance on an impractical `δ-cover` sampling procedure (required by the one prior method with a similar convergence guarantee [1]).

The core idea of IMGPO is to elegantly synthesize two distinct lines of research in global optimization. It combines the hierarchical partitioning approach of algorithms like SOO [18], which operate by assuming the existence of an unknown semi-metric that bounds the function, with the probabilistic upper confidence bounds (UCB) derived from a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. The algorithm simultaneously explores the space based on a family of potential unknown bounds while using the GP-UCB to prune unpromising regions of the search space efficiently. This hybrid approach allows it to retain the strong theoretical guarantee of exponential convergence while being practically implementable and efficient.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clearly stated open problem in the literature [2]: developing a practical GP-based optimization algorithm with an exponential convergence rate. By eliminating the need for both auxiliary optimization and `δ-cover` sampling, the authors have made a significant practical and theoretical contribution. The unification of the bound-based GP approach with the unknown metric-based partitioning approach is a novel and powerful concept.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential regret bound of `O(λ^(N+Ngp))`. This is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of related practical algorithms like BaMSOO [2]. The analysis in Remarks 1, 2, and 3 is particularly insightful, providing a clear framework to understand the interplay between the GP-based bound and the "infinite-metric" exploration, and explaining why their combination is advantageous.

3.  **Excellent Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear motivation for the problem and a high-level overview of their approach in Section 3.1. The illustrative 1D example in Section 3.2 and Figure 1 is extremely effective at building intuition for the algorithm's mechanics, particularly the interactions between the partitioning scheme and the GP-UCB pruning steps. The detailed pseudocode in Algorithm 1 appears complete and sufficient for reimplementation.

4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing IMGPO against relevant and strong baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) across a diverse set of benchmark functions with varying dimensionalities. The results in Figure 2 consistently demonstrate the superior performance of IMGPO in terms of simple regret. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO showcases the potential for IMGPO to serve as a core subroutine in more complex systems. The runtime comparison in Table 1 further supports the claim of efficiency by avoiding expensive auxiliary optimization.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability with Dimensionality:** The authors rightly acknowledge that the constant in the exponential rate, `λ`, approaches 1 as the input dimension `D` increases. This suggests that, like many partitioning-based methods, the "curse of dimensionality" remains a challenge for the core algorithm. While the 1000-D experiment with REMBO is a good proof-of-concept for tackling this, the scalability of the standalone IMGPO algorithm is a limitation.

2.  **Sensitivity to New Parameters:** The algorithm introduces a key parameter, `Ξmax`, which limits the lookahead depth for the UCB-based pruning step. While the experiments show good performance with a fixed value, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion or ablation study on the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance and runtime to this parameter. Similarly, the heuristic update rule for `Ξ` in line 38 could be further motivated or analyzed.

3.  **Practical Cost of Hyperparameter Updates:** The algorithm updates GP hyperparameters in each iteration (line 39), which typically involves a non-trivial optimization of the marginal likelihood. This computational cost is not reflected in the function evaluation counts or the runtime table (which focuses on algorithm overhead minus function evaluations). While standard practice, a brief comment on the practical impact of this step on total wall-clock time would add completeness.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

*   The paper solves a significant and explicitly stated open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   It is the first practical, GP-based algorithm to achieve a provable exponential convergence rate.
*   The core idea of unifying two major paradigms in global optimization is novel, elegant, and impactful.
*   The work is supported by strong theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical results.
*   The paper is written with exceptional clarity, making a complex algorithm and its analysis highly accessible.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**

*   The scalability of the core algorithm with high dimensionality remains a concern, though this is a common challenge in the field and the authors are transparent about it.
*   The analysis of the new algorithm parameters (e.g., `Ξmax`) could be more thorough.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a clear and significant advance in the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. The work is of high quality, original, and will likely be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
