

# Review for "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations in the current literature: (1) most Bayesian optimization methods with exponential convergence (e.g., de Freitas et al., 2012) require impractical δ-cover sampling, and (2) standard GP-based approaches like GP-UCB require solving an additional non-convex optimization problem (auxiliary optimization) at each iteration. The paper proposes a theoretically grounded approach that considers infinitely many possible bounds rather than relying on a single GP-derived bound, thereby eliminating both requirements while maintaining exponential convergence.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, which has been a prominent topic at NIPS since the seminal work by Srinivas et al. (2010) on GP-UCB. It builds upon Munos' SOO algorithm (2011) and Wang et al.'s BaMSOO (2013), which combined GP with hierarchical partitioning but achieved only polynomial regret bounds. The authors demonstrate that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which represents a significant theoretical improvement over previous methods. The paper also provides a nuanced theoretical analysis explaining when the infinite-metric approach is beneficial compared to relying on a single bound.

The paper excels in clarity and presentation. The authors provide a thorough, well-structured explanation of the algorithm, supported by a helpful figure (Figure 1) that illustrates the hierarchical partitioning process. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is well-documented and clearly explains the key components of the algorithm, including the novel steps for handling temporary dummy values and the criteria for interval selection. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with a clear definition of the infinite-metric exploration loss and a detailed examination of how the algorithm's parameters affect the regret bound.

The experimental section is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare IMGPO with several state-of-the-art methods (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI) on eight benchmark functions spanning dimensions from 1 to 1000. The experimental setup is realistic, with the authors using a single kernel (isotropic Matern with ν=5/2) and default hyperparameters for all methods, reflecting typical practical implementation scenarios. The results consistently show IMGPO outperforming the baselines in terms of regret reduction, with particularly strong performance on the more challenging multimodal functions. The authors also provide insightful analysis of the impact of the Ξmax parameter on performance, demonstrating careful consideration of implementation trade-offs.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution: providing the first GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence that eliminates two key practical limitations of previous approaches. The authors' analysis of when the infinite-metric approach is beneficial (Remarks 1-3) provides valuable theoretical insights for the broader optimization community. This work represents a meaningful synthesis of two different optimization paradigms (bound-based search with a single bound and bound-based search with multiple bounds), which is conceptually elegant and theoretically sound.

The primary weakness of the paper is the limited discussion of scalability in high dimensions. While the authors note in Remark 1 that λ can approach 1 as the dimension D increases, they do not provide concrete strategies for addressing this challenge. The paper's experimental section includes a 1000-dimensional benchmark, but the authors simply embed the 2D Sin2 function into higher dimensions without addressing how the algorithm would scale to truly high-dimensional problems. The connection to REMBO (Wang et al., 2013) for high-dimensional problems is mentioned but not elaborated upon.

Another minor weakness is the lack of comparison with more recent Bayesian optimization methods that have emerged since the conference dates (2013-2015), particularly those focusing on high-dimensional optimization (e.g., Kandasamy et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2013). While the authors' experimental setup is realistic, a comparison with these more recent methods would strengthen the paper's significance.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. The authors successfully address a well-known gap in the literature and provide a practically implementable solution that eliminates two key practical barriers to using exponential-convergence Bayesian optimization methods.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear explanation of the infinite-metric exploration loss
- Comprehensive experimental results demonstrating consistent performance improvements
- Well-written with clear explanations and helpful figures
- Addresses a well-known limitation in the literature

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of scalability to very high dimensions
- Could include more recent optimization methods for comparison
- The practical impact of the Ξmax parameter could be more thoroughly analyzed

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with clear practical implications. The work is of high quality, well-executed, and represents an important advance over previous methods. While the scalability discussion could be strengthened, the paper's core contribution is compelling and well-supported. I recommend acceptance.
