This paper introduces IMGPO, a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm based on Gaussian Processes (GPs). The core idea is to combine a hierarchical partitioning of the search space with GP-derived upper confidence bounds (UCB) to guide the selection of subsequent evaluation points. This approach cleverly avoids two major practical challenges in the field: it eliminates the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to maximize an acquisition function, and it circumvents the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous work [1] to guarantee exponential convergence. The main contribution is a theoretical one: the paper presents the first GP-based optimization algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without these demanding requirements. This work directly addresses an open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] and improves upon the polynomial regret bounds of related methods like BaMSOO.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by solving a known open problem in the theoretical analysis of Bayesian optimization. Achieving an exponential regret bound without impractical assumptions is a notable advance. The approach of integrating GP bounds to prune the search of an "infinite-metric" partitioning algorithm (in the spirit of SOO [18] and DIRECT [19]) is a novel and effective combination of ideas.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous analysis. Theorem 1 provides a clear regret bound that improves upon previous results. The discussion in Remarks 1-3 offers valuable insights into the interplay between the GP-based bound and the partitioning scheme, theoretically justifying why this combined approach is advantageous.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The algorithm is described formally and also illustrated with an intuitive 1D example that effectively explains its mechanics. The motivation and connection to the existing literature are well-established.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability with Dimensionality:** The primary concern with this work is the scalability of the proposed method. The algorithm's foundation in hierarchical space partitioning is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality, a weakness shared by related algorithms like DIRECT. The authors rightly acknowledge in their analysis that the convergence rate degrades as the dimension D increases.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while thorough on the chosen problems, is largely confined to low-dimensional functions (mostly D ≤ 6). The inclusion of a 1000-dimensional experiment using REMBO is interesting, but it demonstrates IMGPO's utility as a subroutine within a specific high-dimensional framework rather than the scalability of the core partitioning algorithm itself. A more direct empirical analysis on moderately higher-dimensional (e.g., 10-20 dimensions) benchmark functions would significantly strengthen the paper's practical claims. The authors should be more explicit about this limitation in the experiments and conclusion.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   On page 2, the paper states that UCB is used "for brevity." Could the authors clarify if the theoretical framework is critically dependent on the high-probability upper bound property of UCB? Or could it be feasibly extended to other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement?
*   The sentence on page 3, "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is central to the method's motivation. For readers less familiar with this line of work, it could be made clearer by briefly explaining that this refers to how the partitioning implicitly explores a family of possible function smoothnesses (semi-metrics `l`) without committing to a single one.
*   In the walk-through of Figure 1 (page 4), the paper mentions that at n=16 a new interval is created "but no function evaluation occurs." It would be helpful to explicitly link this back to step (v) of the algorithm's description, reminding the reader that this is a key feature where the GP's UCB is used to provably avoid a costly function evaluation.
