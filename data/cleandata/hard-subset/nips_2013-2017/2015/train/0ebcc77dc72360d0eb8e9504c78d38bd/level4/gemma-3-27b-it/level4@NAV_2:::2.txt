The paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence, circumventing the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling – requirements of existing approaches like [1]. The core idea involves a GP-based optimization algorithm coupled with an infinite-metric approach and hierarchical partitioning to guide sample selection, avoiding an internal optimization loop for choosing the next evaluation point. The theoretical analysis demonstrates an exponential regret bound, representing an improvement over previous work such as [2]. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the algorithm, supported by an illustrative example. The contribution is original, and the presentation is generally strong. The authors appropriately relate their work to existing Bayesian optimization techniques, including those utilizing Gaussian processes [3, 14, 15] and comparisons to methods like BaMSOO [2] and DIRECT [19].

However, a key concern is the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. While the results demonstrate performance on several benchmark functions (Sin1, Sin2, Branin, Hartmann3, etc.), the dimensionality of these problems appears relatively low, and the scalability to higher dimensions is not thoroughly addressed. The interval partitioning strategy, reminiscent of DIRECT [19], is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality, potentially requiring exponential growth in resolution with increasing problem dimension. This limitation should be acknowledged and discussed, as it significantly impacts the practical applicability of the proposed method.

Minor comments: On page 2, the justification for focusing on UCB acquisition function could be clarified. While presented “for brevity,” exploring the potential applicability of other acquisition functions, like Expected Improvement, would strengthen the discussion. On page 3, the statement regarding simultaneous global and local searches based on all bound candidates is unclear and could benefit from further explanation. Finally, on page 4, the rationale behind the lack of function evaluation at n=16 could be made more explicit for enhanced clarity. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed method offers a novel approach to Bayesian optimization, achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the algorithm’s performance guarantees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its underlying principles.
*   **Significance:** The elimination of δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization addresses practical limitations of existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The lack of evaluation on high-dimensional problems raises concerns about the algorithm's scalability due to the potential for the curse of dimensionality inherent in interval partitioning.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while positive, do not convincingly demonstrate performance in realistic, high-dimensional settings.
