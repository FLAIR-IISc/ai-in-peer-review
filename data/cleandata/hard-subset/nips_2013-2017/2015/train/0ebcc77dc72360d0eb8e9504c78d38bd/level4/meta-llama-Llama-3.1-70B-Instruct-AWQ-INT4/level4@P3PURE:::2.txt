The paper presents a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization where future samples are generated through a space/interval partitioning algorithm. This approach leverages the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method to avoid the internal global optimization problem of choosing the next sample. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, demonstrating that this method achieves exponential regret, thereby improving upon previous work. The algorithm is clearly described, explained, and illustrated with an example, making a novel contribution to the field.

The paper is well-presented overall, but I have one major reservation. The experimental results appear to be limited to 1D test functions, which is not explicitly stated. I suspect that the interval partitioning approach may not scale well to higher dimensions, as the required "resolution" would grow exponentially with the number of dimensions (hyperrectangles). This concern is reinforced by the fact that the approach is related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from poor performance as the dimensionality of the problem increases. I believe that the authors should address this limitation in the paper, as it has significant implications for practical applications, even if it does not affect the theoretical contribution.

Minor comments:

On page 2, the authors mention that UCB is considered "for brevity." It would be helpful to clarify whether alternative acquisition functions, such as expected improvement, could be used instead. The current presentation suggests that UCB is the only viable option.

The statement on page 3, "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is unclear and requires further explanation.

On page 4, the authors mention that "At n=16, the far right...but no function evaluation occurs." It would be helpful to provide a brief explanation for this observation to improve clarity.
