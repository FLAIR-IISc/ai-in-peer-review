The paper proposes a variant of Bayesian global optimization in which future samples are generated via a space/interval partitioning scheme. By using UCB, the method sidesteps the internal global optimization problem of choosing the next sample. Theoretical analysis indicates an improvement over prior work, yielding exponential regret. The algorithm is clearly described and effectively illustrated with an example. Overall, the contribution is novel and the presentation is clear.

I have one reservation. The experiments appear to be conducted only on 1D test functions (this is not stated explicitly). I suspect the interval-partitioning strategy will not scale well to higher dimensions (hyperrectangles), since the required resolution would grow exponentially. The approach is related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to degrade severely as dimensionality increases. The paper should at least discuss this issue. It does not diminish the theoretical contribution, but it is important for practical applications.

Minor comments:
- p.2: UCB is considered “for brevity.” Does this mean other acquisition functions (e.g., expected improvement) could also be used, or is UCB essential? I had the impression it had to be UCB.
- p.3: “...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds.” This statement was unclear to me.
- p.4: “At n=16, the far right... but no function evaluation occurs.” Please explain why, for clarity.

In summary, the paper presents a sound and novel method that achieves theoretical improvements (exponential regret), but it appears to be evaluated only on 1D test problems.
