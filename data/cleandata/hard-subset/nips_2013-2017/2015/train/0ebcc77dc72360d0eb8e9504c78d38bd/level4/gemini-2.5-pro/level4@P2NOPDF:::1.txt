This paper introduces a novel algorithm for global optimization that achieves exponential regret while avoiding the need for delta-cover sampling. This successfully generalizes the work of Freitas et al. (2012), which relied on a sampling procedure that is often impractical.

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the existing literature on bound-based search methods, making the context and contribution of their algorithm clear. The theoretical results are compelling, and the development of a practical algorithm that removes a significant limitation of prior work is a valuable contribution. For these reasons, this work certainly deserves attention from the optimization community.

However, I am recommending a score of 5 (borderline) because the experimental section is not sufficiently rigorous and falls below the standard expected for a top-tier publication in this field. My primary concerns with the empirical evaluation are as follows:

1.  **Lack of Statistical Comparison:** The experiments appear to be based on single runs or initializations for each method. Standard practice in the Bayesian Optimization (BO) literature is to perform multiple runs with different random initializations to account for the stochastic nature of the process and the sensitivity to initial conditions. Without multiple runs, it is impossible to conduct a meaningful statistical comparison (e.g., reporting means and standard errors), and the presented results may not be representative of the methods' average performance.

2.  **Insufficient Baselines:** Although the proposed algorithm is a bound-based search method, it operates in the same problem space as other state-of-the-art BO methods. The comparison should therefore include a broader set of strong baselines. The omission of prominent information-theoretic approaches, such as Entropy Search (ES) or Predictive Entropy Search (PES), is a significant weakness, as these are often considered to be top-performing algorithms in the field. A comparison against these methods is necessary to properly situate the performance of the proposed algorithm.

3.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation is confined to synthetic benchmark functions in low dimensions (up to D=6). While these are useful for initial validation, a strong BO paper should also demonstrate the algorithm's utility on more challenging, real-world problems. Including experiments on tasks such as hyperparameter tuning of machine learning models or other established real-world optimization benchmarks would provide much stronger evidence of the method's practical relevance and scalability.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound and interesting algorithm with clear contributions. The clarity of the writing and the novelty of the approach are significant strengths. However, the work is currently let down by a weak experimental section that lacks statistical rigor, omits key baselines, and fails to demonstrate performance in realistic scenarios. While I value the theoretical contribution, the empirical validation needs to be substantially improved to warrant a stronger recommendation for acceptance.
