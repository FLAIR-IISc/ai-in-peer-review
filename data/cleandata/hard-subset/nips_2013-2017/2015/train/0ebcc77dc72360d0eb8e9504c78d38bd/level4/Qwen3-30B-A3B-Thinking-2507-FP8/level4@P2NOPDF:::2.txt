

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian global optimization method that replaces the standard inner optimization step with a space partitioning algorithm for generating future samples. By leveraging UCB acquisition, the approach avoids solving the computationally intensive global optimization problem inherent in typical Bayesian methods. The theoretical analysis demonstrates exponential regret bounds, providing a significant improvement over prior work. The algorithm is clearly described, with a well-illustrated example enhancing comprehension. The contribution is novel, and the paper is generally well-structured and accessible.

The primary reservation concerns the experimental evaluation, which is limited to 1D test functions without explicit justification. This raises significant practical concerns, as the interval partitioning strategy appears unlikely to scale to higher dimensions. Specifically, the required partition resolution would need to grow exponentially with dimensionality to maintain accuracy, mirroring the well-documented dimensionality limitations of the DIRECT algorithm (which shares conceptual similarities). While the theoretical contribution remains sound, this scalability issue substantially impacts practical utility. The paper should explicitly address this limitation and discuss potential extensions or alternatives for higher-dimensional problems.

Minor comments:  
- Page 2: UCB is introduced "for brevity," but the justification for excluding alternatives like expected improvement is unclear. The text implies UCB is necessary; this should be clarified.  
- Page 3: The statement "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is ambiguous. Please rephrase for precision.  
- Page 4: The note "At n=16, the far right...but no function evaluation occurs" requires explanation. Why does the partitioning algorithm not trigger an evaluation at this stage?  

The paper presents a theoretically compelling advance in Bayesian optimization. Its core contribution—avoiding inner optimization via partitioning—is well-motivated and rigorously analyzed. However, the lack of higher-dimensional experiments and discussion of scalability constitutes a notable gap. Addressing this would significantly strengthen the work’s practical relevance. The minor clarifications requested are straightforward to implement.  

Overall, the paper is technically sound and makes a valuable theoretical contribution. With minor revisions to address the scalability concern and clarify the points above, it would be suitable for publication. The current version is strong in theory but requires stronger justification for its applicability beyond 1D.
