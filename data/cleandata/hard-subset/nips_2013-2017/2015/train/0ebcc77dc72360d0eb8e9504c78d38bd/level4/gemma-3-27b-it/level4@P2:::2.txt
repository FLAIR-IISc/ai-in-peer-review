This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence, addressing limitations of existing approaches that require auxiliary optimization or impractical δ-cover sampling. The core idea is an infinite-metric Gaussian process (GP) optimization algorithm that leverages hierarchical partitioning to efficiently explore the search space, achieving an exponential convergence rate without these prior requirements. The method effectively balances global and local searches by considering a range of potential bounds on the objective function.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm, illustrated with a helpful example. The theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential regret is a significant contribution, building upon prior work in Bayesian optimization [1, 2] and related areas like Lipschitz optimization [7, 8, 9]. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the field, referencing relevant papers such as those by de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], and acknowledging connections to established algorithms like DIRECT [19].

A key strength of this work is its theoretical grounding and the explicit addressing of limitations in previous methods. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of their approach, particularly in scenarios where obtaining a tight Lipschitz constant or performing δ-cover sampling is challenging. The inclusion of a publicly available code repository is also a positive aspect.

However, a potential limitation is the empirical evaluation. While the results demonstrate strong performance on the tested functions, the experiments appear primarily focused on 1D or low-dimensional problems (as indicated by Figure 2 and the inclusion of Sin1 and Sin2). This raises concerns about the scalability of the proposed interval partitioning strategy to higher-dimensional spaces. Similar partitioning-based methods, like DIRECT [19], are known to suffer from the “curse of dimensionality” as the required resolution grows exponentially with dimension. A discussion of this potential limitation and perhaps some preliminary exploration of higher-dimensional problems would strengthen the paper. The connection to DIRECT is noted in the paper, but a more detailed discussion regarding scalability would be valuable. 

Regarding minor points, the justification for choosing UCB as the acquisition function (p.2) could be expanded; mentioning the potential applicability of other acquisition functions like Expected Improvement would be beneficial. The statement on p.3 regarding simultaneously conducting global and local searches is somewhat unclear and could benefit from further clarification. The explanation on p.4 regarding the lack of function evaluation at n=16 could be made more explicit to improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed algorithm presents a novel approach to Bayesian optimization with a strong theoretical guarantee of exponential convergence.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the algorithm’s convergence properties.
*   **Clarity:** The algorithm is clearly described and explained, making it accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian optimization.
*   **Significance:** The method addresses a practical limitation of existing Bayesian optimization techniques, namely the need for auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** The empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional problems, raising concerns about the algorithm's scalability to higher dimensions. This is a significant practical consideration that needs further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the scalability concerns, this is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The authors have developed a novel and well-justified algorithm with a clear explanation and promising results. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the scalability concerns in a discussion within the paper, acknowledging the potential limitations in high-dimensional spaces, and potentially providing preliminary results or analysis on higher-dimensional benchmarks.
