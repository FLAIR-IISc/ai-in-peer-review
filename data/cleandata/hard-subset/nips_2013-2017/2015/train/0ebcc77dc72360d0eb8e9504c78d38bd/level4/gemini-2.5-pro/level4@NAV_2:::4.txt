This paper introduces IMGPO, a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm utilizing Gaussian Processes (GPs). The central contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate while circumventing two significant practical challenges: the need for auxiliary non-convex optimization sub-problems, a common and costly step in methods like GP-UCB, and the reliance on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure, which was required by previous work [1] to guarantee exponential convergence. The proposed method ingeniously combines the hierarchical partitioning strategy from algorithms like SOO [18] with the probabilistic bounds furnished by GP-UCB. The core idea is to implicitly consider an infinite set of potential semi-metrics that could define the function's smoothness at the optimum, while leveraging the GP's upper confidence bound to efficiently prune branches of the search tree. This work directly addresses an open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] and provides a compelling synthesis of two major approaches to global optimization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper presents a significant theoretical advance. It offers what appears to be the first GP-based optimization method to achieve an exponential convergence rate without impractical assumptions (δ-cover sampling) or computationally intensive subroutines (auxiliary optimization). The combination of hierarchical partitioning with GP-based pruning is novel and effectively resolves a well-known open problem in the field. The analysis framework, particularly in Remarks 1-3, provides valuable and generalizable insights into the trade-offs of using known versus unknown function bounds.

*   **Technical Quality:** The claims are rigorously supported by a formal analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, which clearly establishes the exponential regret bound. The theoretical result is particularly strong as it holds for the best unknown smoothness parameters (L, α, p). The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing IMGPO against several state-of-the-art and relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI/EI) on a diverse set of standard benchmark functions. The experimental results consistently and convincingly demonstrate the proposed method's superior performance in terms of sample efficiency.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and didactic. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and carefully positioning their work within the context of existing literature. The algorithm's mechanics, though intricate, are made accessible through a clear step-by-step description in Section 3.2 and an excellent illustrative diagram in Figure 1. The provision of pseudocode further enhances the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The parameter `Ξ_max` is fixed in the experiments. While the authors note that performance could be improved by increasing it at a computational cost, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to this parameter or a heuristic for setting it would add practical value for future users of the algorithm.

*   The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness. An interesting addition would be an ablation study that further isolates the contribution of the GP component. For example, how does IMGPO perform compared to a version where the GP-based pruning (step iii / lines 19-24 in Algorithm 1) is disabled? This would empirically validate the theoretical claims in Remark 2 about the benefit of using the GP to reduce the "infinite-metric exploration loss".

*   The paper's clarity is a major strength, though a final proofreading pass could polish some minor points. For example, the phrasing in the abstract could be slightly tightened to avoid repetition.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves an important and explicitly stated open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   Provides a novel algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees (exponential convergence), a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of closely related methods like BaMSOO.
*   Enhances the practicality of Bayesian optimization by eliminating the need for expensive auxiliary optimization and impractical sampling assumptions.
*   Empirical results are strong, consistent, and demonstrate superior performance over established baselines.
*   The paper is very clearly written, presenting its complex ideas in an accessible and effective manner.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The scalability to very high-dimensional problems remains a challenge, as acknowledged by the authors. However, this is a general limitation of most methods in this class, and the paper rightly points to hybrid approaches like REMBO as a potential path forward. This does not detract from the core contribution.
