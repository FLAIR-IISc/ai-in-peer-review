Summary
The paper proposes a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization (BGO) that selects future evaluations via a space/interval partitioning strategy. By pairing this partitioning with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) rule, the method avoids solving the typically expensive inner global optimization problem over the acquisition function. Theoretical analysis indicates improved performance guarantees—specifically, exponentially decreasing regret—relative to prior work. The algorithm is clearly described and illustrated with an example. Overall, the contribution appears novel, sound, and well presented.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic description with an intuitive partitioning mechanism and illustrative example.
- The use of UCB within the partitioned search eliminates the need for inner acquisition optimization, addressing a common computational bottleneck in BGO.
- Theoretical results claim exponential regret bounds, which, if correct under the stated assumptions, represent a notable improvement over typical sublinear rates.
- The paper situates the method within the BGO literature and articulates the motivation well.

Concerns and limitations
- Empirical evaluation appears to be limited to one-dimensional test functions (this is not explicitly stated). This is a significant limitation for a global optimization method intended for general use.
- Interval/space partitioning in higher dimensions (into hyperrectangles) is likely to suffer from exponential growth in the number of regions, leading to poor scalability. This concern is well documented for related methods such as DIRECT, which can degrade rapidly as dimensionality increases.
- The paper should discuss the computational and statistical consequences of scaling the partitioning strategy to d > 1, including:
  - How the number of candidate regions grows with dimension and budget.
  - Any heuristics or pruning rules that mitigate the curse of dimensionality.
  - Whether the theoretical guarantees meaningfully extend to higher dimensions or rely on dimension-dependent constants that become prohibitive.
- While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the lack of multi-dimensional experiments or a clear discussion of scalability limits the practical impact.

Requests for clarification
- Use of UCB “for brevity” (p.2): Does the framework permit other acquisition functions (e.g., Expected Improvement, Probability of Improvement, Thompson sampling)? If so, which parts of the analysis rely on specific UCB properties (e.g., confidence bounds, optimism), and would the guarantees carry over or change?
- Statement on p.3: “we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds.” This is hard to parse. Please clarify precisely what constitutes “global” versus “local” in your partitioning scheme, and how “candidates of the bounds” are generated and prioritized.
- Comment on p.4: “At n=16, the far right … but no function evaluation occurs.” Please explain why no evaluation is triggered. Is it due to pruning, dominance by other intervals under the UCB criterion, or a tie-breaking rule?
- Assumptions for exponential regret: Clearly state the conditions under which the exponential rate holds (e.g., noise-free observations, smoothness or analyticity of the objective, specific kernel assumptions, known/unknown hyperparameters). This will help readers understand when the guarantees apply.
- Computational complexity: Provide per-iteration complexity in terms of (i) Gaussian process updates, (ii) number of candidate subregions maintained, and (iii) partitioning/pruning operations. A discussion of memory requirements would also be helpful.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Include at least a modest set of higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., d=2–5) on standard benchmarks to illustrate behavior beyond 1D. If runtime is an issue, report wall-clock time and evaluation budgets, and compare against baselines such as DIRECT, standard GP-UCB with inner optimization, and EI-based methods.
- If full multi-dimensional experiments are infeasible, add a thorough analysis of scalability: growth of the candidate set, pruning effectiveness, and any dimensional dependence in the regret bounds.
- Discuss connections and distinctions from DIRECT more explicitly. For example, how does the use of GP posteriors and UCB alter the region-selection criteria relative to Lipschitz-based or center-sampling rules in DIRECT? Are there conditions where your method provably avoids DIRECT’s pathological behaviors in higher dimensions?
- Clarify hyperparameter handling for the GP (e.g., whether hyperparameters are fixed, re-estimated online, or marginalized). Since UCB depends on posterior variance, instability from hyperparameter learning could affect both performance and theoretical claims.
- Provide stopping criteria and guidance for practical users, including recommended budgets, tie-breaking rules, and sensitivity to initialization.

Minor comments
- p.2: If UCB is chosen “for brevity,” please state explicitly whether EI or other acquisitions are compatible and what changes in analysis would be required.
- p.3: Rephrase the sentence about “global and local searches” to avoid ambiguity; a schematic or pseudocode comment would help.
- p.4: Explain why “no function evaluation occurs” at n=16 in the figure/example.
- Throughout: Ensure that dimensionality of experiments is stated explicitly in the setup. If only 1D, say so and motivate why; otherwise, add higher-dimensional cases.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and theoretically interesting paper that removes a key practical bottleneck in BGO by sidestepping inner acquisition optimization and offers strong regret guarantees. However, the current empirical evaluation and discussion do not address scalability, which is critical for partition-based methods and for practical adoption. With added clarity on the acquisition choice, assumptions for the regret analysis, and a more complete discussion (and ideally experiments) in higher dimensions, the paper would be significantly stronger.
