

This paper proposes a novel GP-based global optimization algorithm, IMGPO, which achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address a key limitation in existing Bayesian optimization methods: while de Freitas et al. [1] achieved exponential convergence, their method required impractical δ-cover sampling, and Wang et al. [2] developed a method with polynomial convergence that avoided this but still needed auxiliary optimization. The authors' key insight is to consider "infinitely many possible bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior, which allows them to maintain exponential convergence without the impractical sampling procedure.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a rigorous proof of exponential convergence (Theorem 1), establishing a regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1. This advances the state of the art over previous methods that only achieved polynomial regret bounds. The authors provide a clear analysis of how their approach differs from existing methods, particularly showing that IMGPO can outperform BaMSOO [2] by leveraging the GP prior to reduce the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρ̄t). The theoretical analysis also includes insightful remarks (Remarks 1-3) that explain why not relying on a single bound can be advantageous, providing valuable theoretical perspective for the broader optimization community.

The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and effective use of figures to illustrate the algorithm's operation. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is detailed and accessible, and the authors do an excellent job explaining the intuition behind their approach. The experimental results are comprehensive, comparing IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight benchmark functions of varying dimensions (1-1000). The results consistently demonstrate IMGPO's superior performance, with the algorithm outperforming others in most cases. The authors also provide useful insights into the effect of parameters like Ξmax, showing that increasing this parameter can improve performance at the cost of additional computation.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough experimental validation. The regret bound in Theorem 1 is a significant contribution that advances the state of the art. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that the exponential convergence rate becomes less effective as dimensionality increases (λ approaches 1), and they suggest potential directions for addressing this challenge.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors carefully define all necessary concepts, provide appropriate citations to relevant work, and structure the paper to build understanding progressively. The figures effectively illustrate the algorithm's operation, making complex concepts accessible to readers. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by introducing the "infinite-metric" approach to Bayesian optimization. While the concept of considering multiple bounds has appeared in other optimization contexts (e.g., DIRECT [19], SOO [18]), the authors' application to GP-based optimization with exponential convergence is both novel and well-justified. The paper adequately references previous work, including the relevant NIPS proceedings (e.g., [18] from NIPS 2011).

The significance of this work is substantial. It solves a long-standing open problem in Bayesian optimization (creating a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling [2]), which has practical implications for real-world applications where computational efficiency matters. The paper's theoretical framework also provides valuable insights applicable beyond Bayesian optimization, suggesting potential for adaptation to other bound-based methods like A* search or PAC-MDP algorithms.

Strengths for acceptance:
- First GP-based method achieving exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear regret bound
- Comprehensive experimental validation across diverse benchmarks
- Well-written with excellent explanations and illustrative figures
- Provides valuable theoretical insights about the benefits of not relying on a single bound

Weaknesses to consider:
- The connection to previous work could be strengthened by more explicitly contrasting IMGPO with other bound-based methods beyond SOO
- The practical implications of the semi-metric assumption (Assumption 1) in real-world applications could be discussed in more depth
- The scalability to higher dimensions is acknowledged as a limitation, but the paper doesn't provide concrete suggestions for addressing this beyond mentioning "additional assumptions"

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization that advances both theoretical understanding and practical applicability. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and addresses an important open problem in the field. The authors have provided a compelling solution that demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and would be valuable to both researchers and practitioners in the field. I recommend acceptance.
