Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a Gaussian process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function and without relying on the impractical δ-cover sampling used in earlier theory. This addresses a key limitation in de Freitas et al. (ICML’12), which established exponential regret under δ-cover sampling, and complements BaMSOO (AISTATS’14), which avoids δ-cover but attains only polynomial rates. The paper frames IMGPO as a bound-based search that uses GP information to prune an infinite family of implicit bounds derived from local continuity at the (unknown) optimum, and proves an exponential regret bound O(λN+Ngp), λ < 1, alongside empirical comparisons against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.

Strengths:
- Clear, didactic exposition with helpful figures illustrating the hierarchical partitioning and pruning strategy.
- Theoretical contribution: an exponential convergence guarantee without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary acquisition optimization; analysis also clarifies how GP tightness and the “infinite-metric exploration loss” affect regret.
- Empirical results across several benchmarks generally favor the proposed method, supporting the theory.
- Algorithmic design avoids the costly inner optimization typically required by GP-UCB-style methods.

Weaknesses:
- Minor presentation issues (see “Minor comments” below).
- Some dependence of constants and performance on dimensionality remains; the paper notes that λ approaches 1 as D grows, suggesting scalability challenges without additional structural assumptions.
- My subject-matter expertise in GP bandits/global optimization is limited; the assessment below is therefore based on careful reading rather than deep field expertise.

Evaluation by review criteria:
- Quality: The technical development appears sound. The probability guarantees for UCB usage, the definition and role of exploration loss, and the final regret bound are coherently presented. Proofs are deferred to the supplement, but the main ideas are well-motivated. Experiments are fair given fixed, non-hand-tuned kernels and shared settings across methods.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable. The algorithm is described both intuitively and via pseudocode, and figures effectively convey the selection/division rules.
- Originality: Combining GP posteriors with an “infinite-metric” pruning idea to attain exponential rates without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization seems novel relative to GP-UCB, DIRECT, SOO, and BaMSOO.
- Significance: If the guarantees and empirical advantages hold broadly, the method is practically appealing for deterministic black-box optimization, especially where auxiliary acquisition optimization is costly or brittle.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a known open issue: exponential rates for GP-based optimization without δ-cover.
- Strong alignment of theory and practice; method is competitive or superior on benchmarks.
- Clear contribution that bridges bound-based global optimization and GP Bayesian optimization.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of robustness to kernel misspecification and to high-dimensional settings; practical guidance on choosing Ξmax and handling adverse GP priors could be expanded.
- Proof details are in the supplement; readers may desire more intuition or tighter constants in the main text.

Minor comments (presentation/typos):
- Abstract: some phrasing is repetitive; consider tightening to avoid redundancy.
- Around line 49: the phrase “the function” reads awkwardly; rephrase for clarity.
- Around line 72: there appears to be a misplaced parenthesis; please correct.

Reviewer confidence:
I am not a specialist in GP optimization or global optimization; my evaluation reflects an informed reading rather than deep domain expertise. Nonetheless, the paper’s exposition, theoretical claims, and experiments collectively make a compelling case for the proposed approach.
