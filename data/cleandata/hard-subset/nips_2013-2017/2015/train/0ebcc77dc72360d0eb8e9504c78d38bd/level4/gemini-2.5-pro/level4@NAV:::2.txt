This paper introduces a variation of Bayesian Global Optimization that employs a space-partitioning algorithm to select subsequent samples. By leveraging the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) criterion, the method circumvents the common sub-problem of optimizing an acquisition function to determine the next evaluation point. The theoretical analysis provides evidence of improved performance, specifically demonstrating exponential regret, which surpasses prior results. The proposed algorithm is well-described, with a helpful illustrative example. Overall, the paper is clearly presented and constitutes a novel contribution.

My primary reservation concerns the experimental validation. The experiments appear to be conducted exclusively on one-dimensional test functions, although this is not explicitly stated. This raises concerns about the scalability of the interval partitioning strategy to higher-dimensional spaces (i.e., hyperrectangles), as the number of partitions required to maintain a given resolution would likely grow exponentially with the dimension. This potential limitation is reminiscent of the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which also struggles with performance degradation as dimensionality increases. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this potential scalability issue. While this does not detract from the theoretical contribution, it is a crucial consideration for the method's practical applicability.

Minor comments:
*   p.2: The text states that UCB is used "for brevity." Could the framework accommodate other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement? The current presentation seems to imply that UCB is integral to the method.
*   p.3: The statement "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" was unclear. Please consider rephrasing or elaborating on this part of the process.
*   p.4: In the example where n=16, it is noted that "the far right...but no function evaluation occurs." For clarity, it would be helpful to briefly explain why a function evaluation is not performed at this location.
