This paper introduces a novel algorithm for global optimization, addressing limitations of existing methods by circumventing the need for delta-cover sampling. The authors demonstrate exponential regret, effectively extending the work of Freitas et al. (2012) and overcoming the impracticality of its sampling procedure.

The paper is clearly written and well-structured, making it easy to understand the algorithm and its positioning within the broader landscape of bounded-based search methods. I believe this work has the potential to be of significant interest to the research community.

My overall assessment is a score of 5. However, I believe the experimental evaluation prevents a higher rating. While the theoretical contributions are promising, the empirical analysis falls short of the standards typically expected for a publication in NIPS, particularly within the field of Bayesian Optimization (BO). 

Specifically, my concerns are as follows:

*   **Lack of Multiple Initial Evaluation Comparisons:** The experiments do not evaluate the methods across varying numbers of initial function evaluations. This is a standard practice in BO research and crucial for enabling statistically meaningful comparisons. Without this, drawing definitive conclusions about the relative performance of the proposed algorithm is difficult.

*   **Missing Comparison to State-of-the-Art BO Methods:** Despite presenting a bounded-based search method, the authors do not compare their results against established state-of-the-art Bayesian Optimization techniques. The omission of information-theoretic approaches, such as Entropy Search, is particularly noteworthy. Including such comparisons would strengthen the evaluation and better contextualize the contribution.

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** All experiments are conducted using synthetic functions, with a maximum dimensionality of 6. While valuable for initial validation, the absence of experiments on real-world parameter tuning problems limits the demonstration of the method’s practical utility and generalizability. Applying the algorithm to a "wet lab" or other realistic scenario would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting theoretical advancement in global optimization, offering a compelling alternative to methods relying on delta-cover sampling. However, the limited and somewhat unrepresentative experimental evaluation weakens the overall impact of the work. Addressing these experimental shortcomings would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential contribution to the field.
