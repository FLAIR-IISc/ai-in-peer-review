This paper introduces a novel Bayesian Global Optimization method that utilizes a space/interval partitioning strategy to determine subsequent samples, circumventing the need for an internal global optimization step typically found when employing Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) acquisition functions. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating improved performance, specifically exponential regret, compared to existing methods. The algorithm is well-defined and effectively illustrated with a clarifying example, representing a valuable contribution to the field and exhibiting a high degree of clarity in its presentation.

However, I have one significant concern. The experimental evaluation appears limited to one-dimensional test functions (though this isn’t explicitly stated). I anticipate that the interval partitioning approach may not scale favorably to higher-dimensional problems, as maintaining sufficient resolution would likely require exponential growth in partitioning complexity. This limitation is reminiscent of the DIRECT algorithm, which is known to struggle with increasing dimensionality. While this does not diminish the theoretical advancements presented, it is a crucial consideration for practical applicability and should be addressed within the manuscript.

Regarding minor points: On page 2, the justification for using UCB “for brevity” raises the question of whether alternative acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, were considered. The text implies UCB was the natural choice, but a brief discussion would be beneficial. On page 3, the statement “…we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” lacks clarity and requires further explanation. Finally, on page 4, the observation regarding n=16 and the lack of function evaluation would benefit from a more explicit explanation of the underlying reason. 

Overall, the paper presents a sound and novel approach, but the lack of higher-dimensional evaluation is a notable limitation.
