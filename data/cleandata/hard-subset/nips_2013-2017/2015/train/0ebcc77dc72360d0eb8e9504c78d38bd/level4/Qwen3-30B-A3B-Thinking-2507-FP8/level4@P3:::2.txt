

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents an innovative Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing key limitations in previous approaches. The authors build upon the recent work of de Freitas et al. (2013) which established exponential convergence but relied on impractical δ-cover sampling, and Wang et al. (2013) which combined GP-UCB with hierarchical partitioning but achieved only polynomial regret bounds. The proposed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) algorithm introduces a novel approach to global optimization by simultaneously considering infinitely many candidate bounds, rather than relying on a single upper bound provided by the GP prior. This theoretical insight enables exponential convergence while eliminating the need for the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is strong and well-justified. The authors prove an exponential regret bound (O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1) under reasonable assumptions, and provide detailed analysis of how different components (GP, infinite-metric exploration, bound tightness) affect the regret bound. The supplementary material contains complete proofs. The experimental evaluation demonstrates consistent improvement over baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on 1D test functions, with clear quantitative results showing the advantage of IMGPO. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, including the potential dimensionality challenge noted in Remark 1. The experimental setup is well-motivated (using a single kernel with fixed hyperparameters across all functions rather than hand-tuned parameters) and the results are presented clearly.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core idea is introduced through a clear conceptual framework (bound-based search methods), followed by a precise mathematical formulation. The algorithm description is supported by a helpful example (Figure 1) and pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The technical details are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor while remaining accessible to the NIPS audience. The connection between the algorithm's operation and the theoretical analysis is well-maintained throughout. The minor issues noted in the draft review (e.g., clarification of "all candidates of bounds" in Section 3.1) could be addressed with a brief sentence or two.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The key insight of simultaneously considering infinitely many candidate bounds (rather than relying on a single GP-derived bound) is novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work: BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2013) achieves polynomial regret, while IMGPO achieves exponential regret; DIRECT (Jones et al., 2001) and SOO (Munos, 2011) lack the exponential convergence guarantee; and GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., 2010) requires auxiliary optimization. The theoretical framework for understanding when it's beneficial to consider multiple bounds (Remarks 1-3) is particularly valuable.

**Significance:** The exponential convergence rate represents a substantial theoretical advance over existing GP-based optimization methods. The practical implications are significant: eliminating the need for auxiliary optimization (which can be time-consuming) and δ-cover sampling (which is computationally impractical) makes the algorithm more accessible for real-world applications. While the experimental results are limited to 1D functions, the theoretical guarantees hold for higher dimensions, and the authors appropriately discuss the dimensionality challenge in Remark 1. The potential for scalability in higher dimensions through additional assumptions (e.g., REMBO for high-dimensional embedding) is promising.

**Strengths:**
- First GP-based method with exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling
- Clear theoretical analysis with precise regret bound
- Well-structured algorithm description with illustrative example
- Practical implementation details (public code)
- Thoughtful discussion of theoretical insights (Remarks 1-3)

**Weaknesses:**
- Experimental evaluation limited to 1D functions (though the theoretical results apply to higher dimensions)
- The dimensionality scaling challenge needs more explicit discussion in the main text (beyond Remark 1)
- Minor clarification needed on the "infinitely many candidates" concept (Section 3.1)
- The paper could briefly address why other acquisition functions (like expected improvement) might not work as well with the IMGPO framework

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. The exponential convergence rate is a significant improvement over previous work, and the algorithm is both theoretically sound and practically implementable. The minor limitations noted (dimensionality scaling and limited experimental validation) are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the theoretical contribution.

While the experimental results are currently limited to 1D problems, the authors have appropriately noted this limitation and provided a promising direction for higher-dimensional applications (via REMBO). For a conference like NIPS, where theoretical advances are highly valued, the exponential convergence rate alone makes this a compelling contribution. The paper provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding when and why it's beneficial to consider multiple bounds in optimization, with potential implications beyond Bayesian optimization (as noted in the conclusion).

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a substantial theoretical contribution to an important area of machine learning with clear connections to previous NIPS work (Srinivas et al., 2010; Munos, 2011; Wang et al., 2013). The writing is clear, the technical content is rigorous, and the contribution is significant. I believe this paper would be of interest to the NIPS community and would advance the field.
