The paper introduces a novel variant of Bayesian Global Optimization that generates future samples using a space/interval partitioning algorithm. By employing an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) acquisition function, the method circumvents the internal global optimization problem inherent in selecting the next sample point. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating exponential regret, establishing improvements over existing approaches. The algorithm is well-described and effectively illustrated with an example. This work represents a novel contribution and is presented with commendable clarity. 

I have one primary concern regarding the practical scalability of the proposed method. The experimental evaluation appears limited to one-dimensional test functions (this is not explicitly stated). I anticipate that the interval partitioning approach may not scale effectively to higher dimensions, as the necessary resolution would likely grow exponentially with dimensionality. This limitation is reminiscent of the DIRECT algorithm, which is known to perform poorly as problem dimensionality increases. While this does not diminish the theoretical contributions, it is a significant consideration for practical application and should be addressed in the paper.

Minor comments:

p.2: The statement that UCB is considered “for brevity” raises a question. Is this to suggest that other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, could also be utilized within this framework? The presentation strongly implies a reliance on UCB.

p.3: The phrase “…we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” is unclear. Could this statement be clarified?

p.4: The explanation for why no function evaluation occurs at n=16 (“At n=16, the far right...but no function evaluation occurs”) lacks sufficient detail. Providing the rationale for this behavior would improve clarity. 

Overall, the paper presents a sound and novel approach to Bayesian Global Optimization, but the scope of experimental validation appears limited to 1D problems.
