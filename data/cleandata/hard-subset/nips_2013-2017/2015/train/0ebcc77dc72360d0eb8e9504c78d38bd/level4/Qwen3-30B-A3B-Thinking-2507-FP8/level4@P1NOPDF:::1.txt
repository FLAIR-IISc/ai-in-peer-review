

The paper introduces a novel global optimization algorithm that avoids delta-cover sampling while achieving exponential regret bounds, thereby generalizing the work of Freitas et al. (2012), which relies on an impractical sampling procedure. The theoretical contributions are well-motivated, and the paper is clearly written with a strong contextualization of the method within existing bounded-search literature. The authors effectively demonstrate the algorithm’s theoretical advantages, and I believe this work deserves significant attention from the community.

I rate the paper a 5 (highest score), though it would have warranted a higher score had the experimental section met NIPS standards. The empirical evaluation has three critical shortcomings:  
1. **Lack of standard BO benchmarks**: The authors fail to compare their method across multiple initial evaluations of $f$, which is standard practice in Bayesian optimization (BO). This omission prevents statistically rigorous comparison of performance.  
2. **Missing state-of-the-art BO baselines**: Despite proposing a bounded-search method, the experiments omit key BO approaches, such as information-theoretic techniques (e.g., Entropy Search), which are widely adopted benchmarks.  
3. **Over-reliance on synthetic functions**: All experiments use synthetic functions (maximum dimension 6), with no validation on real-world applications or parameter-tuning tasks. This limits the demonstration of practical utility.  

The theoretical results are compelling, but the experimental section falls short of the empirical rigor expected for a top-tier NIPS paper. A stronger evaluation—including comparisons with standard BO baselines, multiple initialization strategies, and real-world validation—would substantially strengthen the contribution. As it stands, the paper presents an interesting theoretical advance but lacks the empirical depth required to fully convince the community of its practical impact.
