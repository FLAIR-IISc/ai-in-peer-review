The paper presents a Bayesian optimization method, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which eliminates the need for both auxiliary optimization – a common requirement in many approaches – and the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work [1]. The core idea is to maintain an exponential convergence rate without these limitations.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear motivation and a concise introduction to the problem of global optimization, relating it to existing challenges in Lipschitz continuity assumptions and the limitations of previous Bayesian optimization methods. The main contributions are clearly articulated. Section 3.2 provides a helpful illustrative example, enhancing understanding of the algorithm’s mechanics. Furthermore, Section 3.3 details the algorithmic procedure in a way that facilitates potential implementation and replication. The experimental results presented demonstrate promising performance. Notably, the authors highlight the synergy achieved by integrating concepts from both continuity-estimating and non-continuity-estimating Bayesian optimization approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method addresses a significant practical limitation of existing Bayesian optimization techniques, namely the reliance on δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, establishes an exponential convergence rate, a strong result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The concepts are presented logically, and the inclusion of an illustrative example is particularly helpful. The programmatic description in Section 3.3 adds to the reproducibility of the work.
*   **Originality:** The approach of leveraging an unknown semi-metric and considering infinitely many potential bounds represents a novel contribution. This distinguishes the work from previous attempts to achieve exponential convergence in Bayesian optimization, like those in [1, 2].
*   **Significance:** The elimination of δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization makes IMGPO a potentially valuable tool for real-world applications where these requirements are prohibitive. The demonstrated performance improvements, particularly in comparison to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, further underscore its significance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the discussion acknowledges potential scalability issues as input dimension increases. The authors suggest that additional assumptions, as explored in [14, 20], may be necessary to address this.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments fix Ξmax and hyperparameters to specific values. A more thorough investigation of the algorithm’s sensitivity to these parameters and guidance for their selection would strengthen the practical utility of the method.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** While the paper cites related work, a deeper discussion of how IMGPO relates to broader research on robust optimization or methods that explicitly handle model uncertainty could be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant practical limitation in Bayesian optimization.
*   The theoretical results (exponential convergence) are strong and well-justified.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
*   The experimental results demonstrate promising performance gains.
*   The approach offers a novel perspective by considering infinitely many bounds.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Potential scalability limitations in high dimensions remain a concern.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors have developed a novel and theoretically sound method that addresses a practical limitation of existing approaches. The clarity of the presentation and the encouraging experimental results further enhance its merit. While some concerns regarding scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity exist, they do not outweigh the significant benefits of the proposed method. I recommend acceptance.
