This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm with exponential convergence that circumvents the need for both auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, building upon the work of de Freitas et al. [1] which relied on the impractical δ-cover procedure. The authors propose an Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) method, and provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating its exponential convergence rate. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, algorithm, and theoretical contributions. The connection to existing bound-based search methods is well-articulated, and the authors effectively position their work within the broader literature. The inclusion of a publicly available codebase is a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The achievement of exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling is a significant theoretical advance in Bayesian optimization. This addresses a key limitation of previous work [1, 2] and expands the applicability of these techniques.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally easy to follow, with a logical structure and clear explanations of key concepts. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful for illustrating the algorithm’s operation.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach of simultaneously considering multiple potential bounds, leveraging the GP prior to prune implausible candidates, is a novel idea and a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors accurately situate their work within the context of bound-based search methods and provide relevant citations to prior research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while present, is a significant weakness. The reliance solely on synthetic functions, with a maximum dimensionality of 6, limits the generalizability of the findings.  The lack of real-world application demonstrations hinders assessment of practical utility.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to other Bayesian optimization methods is incomplete.  Crucially, the authors do not evaluate against information-theoretic approaches like Entropy Search, which are commonly used and often outperform GP-based methods in certain scenarios. 
*   **Missing Standard BO Practice:** The experiments lack a standard practice in Bayesian Optimization: evaluating performance across multiple initial random evaluations of the objective function. This omission prevents a statistically rigorous comparison between algorithms, as initial random points can significantly affect performance. 
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors state they used a standard kernel and hyperparameters, a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning (or lack thereof) and sensitivity would strengthen the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution to Bayesian optimization. The removal of the δ-cover sampling requirement is a significant step forward. However, the limited experimental validation substantially weakens the impact of the work. While the theoretical results are compelling, the lack of demonstration on real-world problems and comparison to more comprehensive baselines makes it difficult to fully assess the practical significance of IMGPO.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results guaranteeing exponential convergence.
*   Novel algorithmic approach addressing a known limitation in the field.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the material.
*   Publicly available code promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Weak experimental evaluation with limited scope and missing statistical rigor.
*   Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods, particularly information-theoretic approaches.
*   Absence of real-world application demonstrations.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper with a request for significant revisions to be made prior to publication.  The authors should prioritize expanding the experimental section to include:

1.  **Real-world benchmark problems:**  Demonstrations on parameter tuning tasks or other relevant applications would greatly strengthen the paper.
2.  **Comparison to stronger baselines:**  Include comparisons to methods like Entropy Search and other state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization algorithms.
3.  **Statistical rigor:** Perform experiments with multiple random initializations and report statistical significance of the results.
4.  **Hyperparameter analysis:**  Discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to hyperparameter settings and provide justification for the choices made.
