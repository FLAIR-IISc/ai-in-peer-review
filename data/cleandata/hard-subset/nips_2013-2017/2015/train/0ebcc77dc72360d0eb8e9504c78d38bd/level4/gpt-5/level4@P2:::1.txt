Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm that attains an exponential simple regret rate without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling of de Freitas et al. (2012) and without auxiliary global optimization of acquisition functions. The method combines hierarchical partitioning with a bound-based search strategy that does not commit to a single smoothness bound; instead, it explores a family of possible bounds (“infinite-metric” idea) while using GP posteriors to prune candidates via UCB screening. The authors prove a regret bound of the form O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, improving on the polynomial rates shown for BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014), which integrates SOO with GP-UCB. The paper provides a clear linkage to DIRECT, SOO, and optimism-based methods (UCB/A*), and explains how GP information tightens the search without relying on δ-covers. Experiments compare IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on several synthetic functions, including a REMBO-style high-dimensional example.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Removes δ-cover sampling and auxiliary acquisition optimization while retaining an exponential convergence guarantee.
- Theoretical depth: Provides a nonasymptotic analysis, clarifies the roles of GP-UCB, the infinite-metric exploration loss, and how GP information affects the bound. The remarks give insight into when not committing to a single bound is beneficial.
- Clarity and organization: The algorithm is described in detail with a stepwise explanation; the connection to prior bound-based search methods is well articulated.
- Practicality: Code is available; the approach avoids solving an additional nonconvex optimization at each iteration.

Weaknesses:
- Experimental design is below common BO standards:
  - No evaluation with multiple random initializations for all methods, which limits statistical comparisons. Standard BO practice reports means/uncertainty over multiple random seeds or initial designs.
  - Missing comparisons to strong information-theoretic BO baselines (e.g., Entropy Search, Predictive Entropy Search, Max-value Entropy Search) and Thompson sampling or GP-UCB with robust optimizers, which would better position the method against current state of the art.
  - All benchmarks are synthetic (mostly ≤6D), with only a REMBO-embedded synthetic high-dimensional task; no real-world tuning or experimental design problems are included.
- Limited ablations: The impact of key parameters (e.g., Ξmax) and the contribution of GP screening versus the infinite-metric mechanism are not deeply analyzed. Runtime and wall-clock comparisons are only briefly discussed.
- Scalability: While the analysis acknowledges the dimension dependence (λ approaches 1 as D increases), there is little empirical exploration of performance beyond the REMBO example or of strategies to mitigate high-dimensional effects.

Quality:
- The theoretical results appear technically sound and clearly presented, with appropriate references and discussion of assumptions. The experimental section, however, lacks breadth and statistical rigor relative to NIPS BO papers.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is generally well written and organized. Some minor editorial issues (e.g., consistent reference formatting, occasional typos) could be addressed. The pseudocode is detailed, and the illustrative figure (as described) clarifies the mechanics.

Originality:
- The central idea—combining GP-based pruning with a search that considers infinitely many candidate bounds to avoid relying on a single (possibly loose) bound—is original relative to BaMSOO, SOO, and DIRECT, and addresses a previously noted open problem.

Significance:
- Theoretical significance is high: an exponential regret bound without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization is a notable advance. Practical significance would be strengthened by comprehensive experiments on real tasks and comparisons to stronger BO baselines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide statistically sound evaluations: multiple random initial designs for all methods, report mean/variance across runs, and include wall-clock time.
- Add comparisons to information-theoretic approaches (Entropy Search, PES, MES) and Thompson sampling/GP-UCB with strong optimizers.
- Include real-world benchmarks (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for ML models, engineering design tasks), even small-scale, to demonstrate practical impact.
- Add ablation studies: effect of Ξmax, η, kernel choices, and a version without GP screening to isolate contributions.
- Discuss computational complexity and memory usage per iteration, especially versus acquisition-optimization-based methods.
- Expand discussion on high-dimensional settings and potential integrations (e.g., additive models, embeddings) beyond a single REMBO example.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical contribution eliminating δ-cover and auxiliary optimization with exponential convergence.
- Clear connection to and extension of prior bound-based methods.
- Accessible presentation and publicly available code.

Cons for acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation is limited, lacks strong baselines and statistical rigor.
- No real-world applications demonstrated.
- Scalability and practical performance under varied conditions are not thoroughly assessed.

Overall rating: 5. The paper offers solid theoretical innovation and a clear algorithmic contribution, but the empirical section falls short of NIPS standards for BO. Strengthening the experiments as suggested would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
