### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel variant of Bayesian Global Optimization (BGO) that employs a hierarchical space-partitioning scheme to select subsequent evaluation points. By leveraging an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) criterion on the resulting intervals, the method circumvents the need to solve an auxiliary global optimization problem to choose the next sample—a common challenge in standard BGO. The primary contribution is a theoretical analysis demonstrating that this approach achieves exponential regret, which represents a significant improvement over existing BGO methods. The algorithm is presented clearly and is well-supported by an illustrative example.

### **Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to sample selection in BGO. The theoretical contribution, particularly the proof of exponential regret, is strong and advances the state-of-the-art in the theoretical understanding of these algorithms.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The proposed algorithm is described with excellent clarity. The inclusion of a step-by-step example is particularly helpful for understanding the mechanics of the partitioning and selection process.
*   **Methodological Advantage:** The framing of the problem, especially how the partitioning strategy avoids the typical inner-loop optimization of the acquisition function, is a compelling and practical aspect of the work.

### **Weaknesses**

My primary reservation concerns the scalability of the proposed method to higher-dimensional problems.

The experimental evaluation appears to be conducted exclusively on one-dimensional test functions, although this is not explicitly stated. The core mechanism of the algorithm is interval partitioning, which in higher dimensions would translate to partitioning hyperrectangles. This type of approach is known to suffer severely from the curse of dimensionality, as the number of partitions required to maintain a certain resolution grows exponentially with the number of dimensions.

The authors' approach is conceptually related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is also based on hyperrectangle partitioning and is known to perform poorly as dimensionality increases. It is highly likely that the proposed method would face similar scaling challenges.

While this limitation does not invalidate the paper's important theoretical contribution, it has significant implications for the practical applicability of the algorithm. The paper would be substantially strengthened if the authors explicitly addressed this issue. At a minimum, they should discuss the expected performance in higher dimensions and acknowledge the potential scalability limitations. This is a critical point for assessing the method's utility for practical purposes.

### **Questions and Minor Comments for the Authors**

1.  **Choice of Acquisition Function:** On page 2, the use of UCB is justified "for brevity." This phrasing is slightly ambiguous. Is UCB a fundamental requirement for the theoretical results to hold, or could the framework be extended to other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement? If so, what challenges would this entail?
2.  **Clarification of Statement on p.3:** The statement, "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," was difficult to understand. Could you please rephrase or elaborate on what is meant by simultaneous global and local searches in this context?
3.  **Clarification of Example on p.4:** In the example, it is stated that at step n=16, a partition occurs "but no function evaluation occurs." For the reader's benefit, it would be helpful to briefly explain why the algorithm chose to partition this interval without sampling from it at that step.
