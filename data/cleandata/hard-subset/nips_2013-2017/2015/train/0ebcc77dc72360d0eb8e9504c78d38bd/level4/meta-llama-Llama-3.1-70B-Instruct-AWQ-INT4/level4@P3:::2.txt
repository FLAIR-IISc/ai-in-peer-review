The paper presents a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization where future samples are generated through a space/interval partitioning algorithm, which yields theoretical improvements (exponential regret). The work appears sound and novel but seems to be only evaluated on 1D test problems.

The main idea of the paper is to eliminate the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling in Bayesian optimization methods. The proposed algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), achieves an exponential convergence rate without these requirements. The authors provide a clear description of the algorithm, along with a theoretical analysis that demonstrates its improvements over previous work.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors do a good job of explaining the algorithm and its underlying principles. The use of a simple example to illustrate the algorithm's operation is helpful in understanding the method. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the authors provide a clear discussion of the results.

One potential reservation with this paper is the scalability of the interval partitioning approach to higher dimensions. The authors mention that the algorithm is related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from poor performance as the dimensionality of the problem increases. While the authors do provide some experimental results for higher-dimensional problems, it would be beneficial to discuss this limitation in more detail and provide some insight into how the algorithm might be modified to improve its performance in these cases.

Some minor comments:

* On page 2, the authors mention that they consider the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) "for brevity." It would be helpful to clarify whether other acquisition functions, such as expected improvement, could be used in place of UCB.
* On page 3, the authors state that they "simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds." This statement is not entirely clear, and it would be beneficial to provide more explanation or context.
* On page 4, the authors mention that at n=16, the far right interval is divided, but no function evaluation occurs. It would be helpful to clarify why this is the case.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and sound contribution to the field of Bayesian Global Optimization. While there are some limitations and areas for improvement, the work is well-presented and provides a clear discussion of the results.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and sound contribution to the field of Bayesian Global Optimization.
* The algorithm is well-described, and the theoretical analysis is thorough.
* The authors provide a clear discussion of the results and their implications.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's experimental results seem to be limited to 1D test problems, which may not be representative of more complex real-world problems.
* The scalability of the interval partitioning approach to higher dimensions is not fully addressed.
* Some minor points of clarification are needed to improve the paper's overall clarity and readability.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but encourage the authors to address the minor points of clarification and provide more insight into the scalability of the algorithm to higher dimensions.
