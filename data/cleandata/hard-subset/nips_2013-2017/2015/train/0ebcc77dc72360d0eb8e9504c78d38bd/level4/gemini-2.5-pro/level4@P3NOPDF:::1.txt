This paper introduces a novel global optimization algorithm that achieves exponential regret while avoiding the need for delta-cover sampling. This work serves as a generalization of the method proposed by Freitas et al. (2012), which relied on a sampling procedure that is often impractical.

The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of situating their algorithm within the existing literature on bound-based search methods. The theoretical contributions are interesting and warrant the attention of the optimization community.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the experimental section, which I believe falls short of the standards expected for a top-tier publication in this field. Consequently, I am recommending a borderline score. My primary concerns with the experiments are detailed below:

1.  **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** The experiments are conducted without multiple random initializations for the function evaluations. This is a departure from standard practice in the Bayesian optimization literature, where results are typically averaged over many trials to account for the stochasticity of the process. Without this, it is impossible to perform a meaningful statistical comparison between the proposed algorithm and the baselines, making it difficult to assess the robustness and statistical significance of the reported performance gains.

2.  **Insufficient Baselines:** While the proposed algorithm is a bound-based search method, its performance should be benchmarked against the broader state-of-the-art in Bayesian optimization. The current comparisons are limited. Notably, the experiments do not include comparisons to prominent information-theoretic approaches, such as Entropy Search or Predictive Entropy Search, which are considered strong baselines in the field.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is confined to synthetic benchmark functions of low dimensionality (up to 6 dimensions). To demonstrate the practical utility and scalability of the proposed method, it is crucial to include experiments on real-world problems. For instance, applying the algorithm to a hyperparameter tuning task for a machine learning model would provide a much more compelling case for its relevance and effectiveness in realistic scenarios.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically interesting global optimization algorithm with a valuable contribution in generalizing prior work and avoiding impractical sampling requirements. However, the promising theoretical results are not supported by a sufficiently rigorous or comprehensive experimental evaluation. The lack of statistical comparisons, state-of-the-art baselines, and real-world applications significantly weakens the paper's overall impact. While the theoretical core is strong, the empirical validation needs substantial improvement to meet the standards of a NIPS publication.
