

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian global optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary global optimization. The authors address two key limitations of prior work: the impracticality of δ-cover sampling (noted in Wang et al. [2]) and the computational overhead of solving an additional non-convex optimization problem (e.g., in GP-UCB). IMGPO overcomes these by leveraging a hierarchical partitioning strategy that simultaneously evaluates all candidate bounds derived from an unknown semi-metric, while using the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB) to prune irrelevant candidates. Theoretical analysis proves an exponential regret bound O(λ^{N + N_{gp}}) with λ < 1, significantly improving upon BaMSOO's polynomial regret. The paper is well-structured, with clear algorithmic description (including pseudocode), illustrative examples, and thorough theoretical justification.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical contribution is strong and novel. The authors convincingly demonstrate that IMGPO achieves exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling—a major practical barrier in prior work [1, 2]. The regret bound analysis (Theorem 1) explicitly quantifies how the GP's UCB tightness and the semi-metric exploration loss ρ̄_t affect performance (Remarks 1–3), offering valuable insights beyond prior methods. The algorithmic design is elegant: it avoids auxiliary optimization by using GP to filter candidates for hierarchical partitioning (Section 3.2), and the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is precise and executable. The paper also thoughtfully connects to related work (e.g., DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]), clarifying how IMGPO bridges the gap between bound-based methods with fixed bounds and those leveraging unknown bounds.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary concern is the experimental scope. The results (Section 5) focus exclusively on 1D test functions (Sin1, Sin2, Branin, Hartmann3, Rosenbrock2), with the 1000D example relying on REMBO as a subroutine rather than direct evaluation. Hierarchical partitioning (like DIRECT [19]) suffers from the curse of dimensionality due to exponential growth in required partition resolution. The authors should explicitly address this scalability limitation, as it directly impacts practical applicability. While the paper notes that higher dimensions may require additional assumptions (Section 6), this warrants stronger discussion in the experimental section, including a brief analysis of how dimensionality affects ρ̄_t or computational cost (e.g., via runtime measurements on 2D/3D problems).

**Minor Issues:**  
- Page 2: The remark "for brevity, we consider Bayesian optimization with UCB" is slightly misleading. The paper *does* use UCB throughout (Section 2), so clarifying its role (e.g., "we focus on GP-UCB as a canonical acquisition function") would avoid confusion.  
- Page 3: "We simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all candidates of the bounds" is ambiguous. The text clarifies that candidates are filtered by the GP's UCB (Section 3.1), so rephrasing to "we filter candidates using the GP’s UCB while maintaining a set of potential bounds" would improve clarity.  
- Page 4: The explanation for n=16 (no evaluation) is clear in the paper, but the review should acknowledge this is a deliberate design choice (using GP-estimated UCB as a placeholder) rather than a flaw.

**Overall Assessment:**  
IMGPO makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving a long-standing open problem: achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The core idea—leveraging the GP to dynamically prune candidates for an infinite set of bounds—is novel and well-justified. While the experimental validation is currently limited to low dimensions, the theoretical advances are substantial and would be a valuable addition to NIPS. The paper’s clarity, rigorous analysis, and connections to prior work (e.g., DIRECT, SOO) meet NIPS standards. The scalability concern is important but does not undermine the theoretical contribution; it merely highlights a direction for future work.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept.** The paper’s theoretical novelty, technical soundness, and clear presentation outweigh the limited experimental scope. The authors should address dimensionality concerns in the final version (e.g., by adding a brief scalability analysis or discussing trade-offs), but this is a minor revision. The work advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization and aligns with NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful contributions.
