

This paper introduces a novel global optimization algorithm that avoids delta-cover sampling while achieving exponential regret bounds, extending the framework of Freitas et al. (2012) by circumventing their computationally intractable sampling procedure. The theoretical contributions are sound, with clear exposition of the algorithm’s novelty within the context of bounded-search methods. The writing is polished, logically structured, and effectively situates the work relative to existing literature, making it accessible and valuable for the optimization community.  

Despite these strengths, the experimental evaluation significantly undermines the paper’s impact. The evaluation fails to meet the standard expectations for Bayesian optimization (BO) research at NIPS. Specifically:  
1. **Lack of multi-initial-evaluation comparison**: The authors evaluate only a single initial set of function evaluations, whereas BO benchmarks typically require comparisons across multiple initial points to assess robustness and statistical significance. This omission prevents meaningful statistical validation of the proposed method’s performance gains.  
2. **Insufficient baselines**: The authors omit comparisons with key state-of-the-art BO approaches, including information-theoretic methods like Entropy Search. Given the paper’s focus on bounded-search optimization, this gap is critical, as Entropy Search represents a dominant paradigm in modern BO literature.  
3. **Over-reliance on synthetic functions**: All experiments use synthetic test functions (with a maximum dimension of 6), lacking real-world validation. The absence of practical applications—such as hyperparameter tuning in machine learning or experimental design in scientific domains—prevents demonstration of the method’s utility beyond theoretical settings.  

While the theoretical advances are promising, the experimental section’s shortcomings prevent a stronger recommendation. The paper would benefit substantially from incorporating multi-initial evaluations, comparing against established BO baselines (e.g., Entropy Search, Expected Improvement), and including at least one real-world case study. Without these, the empirical claims remain under-supported, and the practical relevance of the method is unclear.  

In summary, the paper makes a technically rigorous contribution to global optimization theory but falls short in empirical validation. The writing and theoretical foundation warrant attention, yet the experimental limitations prevent it from meeting NIPS’ standards for methodological impact. I recommend a score of 5 (weak accept) as the work is solid but requires stronger empirical support to fully convince the community of its practical value.
