

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential regret convergence without requiring the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure or auxiliary optimization problems that characterize prior work. The authors address a significant limitation identified by Wang et al. (2013) regarding the practicality of de Freitas et al.'s (2012) exponential convergence result, which relied on δ-cover sampling. By considering infinitely many possible upper bounds rather than relying on a single GP-derived bound, IMGPO achieves exponential regret while maintaining computational feasibility.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly positions the contribution within the broader literature on bounded-based search methods. The authors effectively contextualize their work by explaining how IMGPO bridges the theoretical gap between bound-based methods that rely on a single known bound (like GP-UCB) and those that consider multiple bounds (like DIRECT and SOO). The algorithm description is thorough, with a detailed pseudocode, step-by-step explanation, and clear visualization of the hierarchical partitioning approach.

Quality: The theoretical contribution is strong and well-supported. The authors provide a rigorous analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp is the number of GP evaluations. The analysis carefully examines the relationship between bound tightness and regret, with insightful remarks that illuminate when and why the approach outperforms alternatives. The proofs provided in the supplementary material appear sound and address the key theoretical questions.

Clarity: The paper excels in clarity. The introduction effectively establishes the problem context and the limitations of existing approaches. The mathematical notation is consistently used, and the technical explanations are accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian optimization. The algorithm description is particularly well-structured, with each component of the hierarchical partitioning process clearly explained.

Originality: The core idea of simultaneously considering infinitely many possible upper bounds (rather than relying on a single GP-derived bound) is novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate how this differs from prior work, particularly BaMSOO, which achieves only polynomial regret bounds. The theoretical framework they develop provides valuable insights into the relationship between bound tightness and regret, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Significance: The theoretical advances have important practical implications for Bayesian optimization. By eliminating two major computational barriers (δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization), IMGPO could significantly improve the efficiency of Bayesian optimization in real-world applications. The paper's framework for analyzing bound tightness provides a valuable theoretical lens for future work.

However, the experimental section falls significantly below NIPS standards for a paper making such strong theoretical claims. The authors fail to address three critical issues:

1. They do not conduct multiple initial evaluations of the objective function, which is standard practice in Bayesian optimization to account for algorithmic stochasticity and obtain statistically meaningful comparisons. Without this, the reported performance differences cannot be reliably assessed.

2. The experimental comparison omits several state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly information-theoretic methods like Entropy Search (Hennig & Schuler, 2012) and Predictive Entropy Search (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014), which represent major paradigms in the field. This omission limits the paper's ability to demonstrate the relative advantages of IMGPO.

3. All experiments are conducted on synthetic functions with dimensionality up to 6, with no real-world applications or parameter tuning experiments to demonstrate the method's practical utility. Bayesian optimization is primarily used for practical applications, and the absence of such validation significantly weakens the paper's claim about the method's practical value.

The paper presents a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of Bayesian optimization, but the experimental section does not adequately support the claims made. For a NIPS paper, we expect both strong theoretical results and compelling empirical validation, particularly in a field where practical applications are the primary motivation.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution addressing a significant limitation in the literature; well-written paper that clearly contextualizes the work; novel approach that combines benefits of bound-based search methods with GP priors; valuable theoretical insights into the relationship between bound tightness and regret.

Con arguments against acceptance: Experimental section fails to meet NIPS standards; lacks statistical validation through multiple initial evaluations; omits key baseline methods (like Entropy Search); no real-world applications or parameter tuning experiments to demonstrate practical utility.

Given the strong theoretical contribution but inadequate experimental validation, I recommend a score of 4 (Weak Accept). The paper would be an excellent candidate for acceptance with substantial improvements to the experimental section, including multiple initial evaluations, comparisons with information-theoretic baselines, and at least one real-world application demonstrating practical utility. As it stands, the experimental limitations prevent me from recommending a higher score.
