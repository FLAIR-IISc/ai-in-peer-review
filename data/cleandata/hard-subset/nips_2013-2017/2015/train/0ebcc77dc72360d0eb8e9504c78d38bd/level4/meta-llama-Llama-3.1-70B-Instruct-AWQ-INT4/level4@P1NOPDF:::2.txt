The paper presents a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization, where future samples are generated through a space/interval partitioning algorithm. This approach avoids the internal global optimization problem of choosing the next sample by utilizing Upper Confidence Bound (UCB). The theoretical analysis demonstrates that this method achieves exponential regret, thereby improving upon previous work. The algorithm is clearly described, explained, and illustrated with an example, making a novel contribution to the field. Overall, the paper is well-presented.

However, I have one major reservation regarding this paper. The experimental results appear to be limited to 1D test functions, although this is not explicitly stated. I suspect that the interval partitioning approach may not scale well to higher dimensions, as the "resolution" would be required to grow exponentially. This concern is exacerbated by the fact that the approach is related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from poor performance as the dimensionality of the problem increases. I believe it is essential to address this limitation in the paper, as it significantly impacts the practical applicability of the proposed method. Although this does not detract from the theoretical contribution, it is crucial to discuss the potential limitations of the approach.

Additionally, I have a few minor comments. On page 2, the authors mention that UCB is considered "for brevity." This raises the question of whether alternative methods, such as expected improvement, could be employed. The text seems to imply that UCB is the only viable option, but it would be beneficial to clarify this point. On page 3, the statement "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is unclear. A more detailed explanation of this process would be helpful. Finally, on page 4, the authors mention that "At n=16, the far right...but no function evaluation occurs." A brief explanation of why this is the case would enhance the clarity of the text.
