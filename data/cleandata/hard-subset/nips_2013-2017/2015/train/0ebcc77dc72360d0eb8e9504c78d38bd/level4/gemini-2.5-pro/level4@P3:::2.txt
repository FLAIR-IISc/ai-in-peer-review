### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO). The primary goal is to solve the global optimization problem for non-convex, black-box functions. The proposed method makes two significant contributions over prior work. First, it achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret, improving upon the polynomial bounds of similar practical algorithms like BaMSOO. Second, it accomplishes this without resorting to two common but problematic components of other Bayesian optimization methods: (i) it avoids the need to solve an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next query point, and (ii) it does not rely on an impractical `δ-cover` sampling procedure, which was a requirement for a previous method with a similar theoretical guarantee [1].

The core idea of IMGPO is to combine a hierarchical partitioning of the search space (similar to algorithms like SOO and DIRECT) with a Gaussian Process (GP) model. The algorithm maintains a tree of hyperrectangles and, at each iteration, selects promising ones to subdivide. This selection process cleverly balances exploration and exploitation by considering both the observed function values at the centers of the rectangles (local search) and the GP-derived Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) to prune unpromising regions (global search). By not relying solely on a single, potentially loose GP-derived bound, but implicitly considering an infinite family of possible semi-metrics (Assumption 1), the algorithm robustly seeks the optimum.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a well-defined open problem in the Bayesian optimization literature: achieving an exponential convergence rate without the impractical `δ-cover` sampling assumption. The theoretical analysis culminating in Theorem 1, which proves an `O(λ^(N+Ngp))` regret bound, is the main strength of this work. This represents a clear theoretical advance over related methods like BaMSOO [2], which only has polynomial regret bounds.

2.  **Novelty and Originality:** The approach of integrating the "infinite-metric" optimization idea from DIRECT/SOO with the probabilistic framework of GP-UCB is novel. This synthesis allows the algorithm to leverage the best of both worlds: the robust partitioning strategy that doesn't rely on a known smoothness constant, and the GP's ability to use its posterior to efficiently prune large portions of the search space.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The algorithm is described clearly, and the inclusion of a detailed 1D example with illustrations (Figure 1) is extremely effective at building intuition for how the various steps of the algorithm interact to select, divide, and prune intervals. The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 provides a complete specification.

4.  **Practical Advantage:** By design, the algorithm avoids the need for an auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function (e.g., UCB or EI). This is a significant practical advantage, as this inner optimization loop can be computationally expensive and may have its own local optima issues, complicating the overall procedure.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability with Dimensionality:** The primary reservation with this paper concerns the scalability of the proposed method to higher-dimensional problems. The algorithm relies on a hierarchical partitioning of the search space into hyperrectangles. This class of methods, including the related DIRECT algorithm [19], is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality. As the dimension `D` increases, the number of partitions required to cover the space at a given resolution grows exponentially, severely limiting practical applicability.

    While the authors acknowledge this limitation in their analysis (noting that λ can approach 1 as `D` increases), the experimental evaluation does not sufficiently address this concern. Most of the benchmark functions are of low dimensionality (1D to 6D). The single high-dimensional experiment (1000D) relies on embedding IMGPO within the REMBO algorithm [14], which is a framework specifically designed to handle high dimensionality by using random projections. This experiment demonstrates the utility of IMGPO as a subroutine for a low-dimensional optimizer but does not provide evidence for the scalability of IMGPO itself. A more upfront discussion of this limitation and the practical range of dimensionality for which IMGPO is suitable would strengthen the paper.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

-   **On the Role of UCB:** The paper states that UCB is used "for brevity" (p. 2). This suggests that other acquisition functions, like Expected Improvement (EI), might be applicable. However, the theoretical analysis appears to rely heavily on the high-probability upper bound property of UCB (`f(x) ≤ U(x|D)`). Could the authors clarify if the exponential convergence proof holds for other acquisition functions, or if UCB is, in fact, essential to the theory?

-   **Clarification of "Infinite Candidates":** The statement in Section 3.1, "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is central to the paper's philosophy but may be difficult for some readers to grasp. It would be beneficial to expand on how the hierarchical partitioning scheme, by selecting cells based on function values at different scales (depths), implicitly explores the space as if it were operating under the "best" possible (but unknown) semi-metric from Assumption 1.

-   **Explanation of Figure 1:** The example in Figure 1 is excellent. To improve clarity further, the caption or text could explicitly state *why* no function evaluation occurs for the red asterisks at `n=16`. As described in step (v) of the algorithm, this is because the UCB at these points was lower than the best function value found so far (`f+`), which is a key efficiency mechanism of the algorithm worth highlighting.

### Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for Bayesian optimization that makes a significant contribution by achieving an exponential convergence rate without requiring auxiliary optimization or impractical sampling schemes. The work is well-presented and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The primary weakness is the inherent lack of scalability of the partitioning method, which limits its direct applicability to low-to-moderate dimensional problems.

Despite this limitation, the theoretical contribution is strong and the proposed framework is elegant. I recommend acceptance. I strongly encourage the authors to include a more explicit discussion in the final version regarding the practical limitations imposed by the curse of dimensionality.
