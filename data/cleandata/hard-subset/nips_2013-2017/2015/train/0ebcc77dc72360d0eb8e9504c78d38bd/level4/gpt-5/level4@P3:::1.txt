Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Infinite-Metric Gaussian Process Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization method for deterministic black-box functions that achieves an exponential convergence rate without the δ-cover sampling assumption and without auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function. The method combines a hierarchical partitioning strategy (in the spirit of SOO/DOO) with a GP posterior to prune partition candidates, while explicitly reasoning over an infinite family of possible upper bounds induced by unknown Hölder-type semi-metrics around the global optimum. Theoretical analysis yields an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 under mild smoothness conditions, improving upon the polynomial rates of BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS 2014), which coupled GP-UCB with SOO, and avoiding the impractical δ-cover sampling required by the exponential result of de Freitas et al. (ICML 2012). The algorithm is positioned relative to bound-based search (DIRECT, SOO/DOO) and GP-based BO (EI/PI/UCB), and it emphasizes how GP-derived UCBs can be useful even when not tight, by reducing the exploration loss across infinitely many candidate bounds.

Quality
- Theory: The main contribution is a nontrivial regret analysis establishing exponential convergence under an unknown semi-metric with Hölder continuity. The role of the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt, the impact of GP UCBs on pruning, and the dimensional dependence are clearly articulated. Assumptions are standard for deterministic BO, though the dependence on dimension D (λ approaching 1 as D grows) highlights scalability challenges. The proofs are deferred to the supplement; the high-level argument is plausible and carefully motivated.
- Algorithm: IMGPO is well specified, with a hierarchical partitioning scheme, a mechanism to defer expensive function evaluations when UCB is provably below the incumbent, and periodic hyperparameter updates. The interplay between steps (i)–(iii) (candidate selection and pruning) and the role of parameters η, Ξ, Ξmax is explained.
- Empirics: Experiments include comparisons against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on standard low-dimensional synthetic benchmarks and a high-dimensional (1000-D) random embedding example (REMBO). Results generally favor IMGPO, with some failures when the GP prior is misleading. However, the empirical section falls short of BO standards (see weaknesses).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and generally easy to follow. The positioning among bound-based methods and GP BO is well done. Algorithm 1 is detailed, though the notation could be streamlined: define all symbols at first use (e.g., ς, M, Ξ, Ngp, ρt, ρ̄t), use consistent indices (n, N, t), and consolidate definitions of sets such as T′h+ξ. The narrative around Figure 1 is helpful; a small schematic highlighting accept/reject decisions in steps (ii)–(iii) would further aid intuition.

Originality
The central idea of “infinite-metric” reasoning—eschewing commitment to a single smoothness bound while using GP UCBs to reduce exploration—is novel relative to SOO/DOO and prior GP-based methods. Achieving exponential regret without δ-cover is a notable theoretical advance. The analysis also makes explicit how GP calibration, ρ̄t, and Ξn trade off in the bound.

Significance
The theoretical contribution is significant for deterministic BO, resolving an open question raised by BaMSOO about exponential rates without δ-cover. Practically, removing auxiliary acquisition optimization can simplify implementations and reduce overhead. That said, the current empirical evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong modern BO baselines across realistic tasks, which tempers the practical impact.

Strengths
- First GP-based BO with exponential convergence in the deterministic setting without δ-cover and without auxiliary optimization.
- Clear connection to and extension of DIRECT, SOO/DOO, GP-UCB, and BaMSOO; insightful discussion of when non-tight GP bounds are still useful.
- Practical algorithmic design (deferred evaluations via UCB; hierarchical partitioning) with accessible implementation.
- Transparent theoretical dependence on smoothness and dimension.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited experimental protocol:
  - No multiple random initializations or statistical tests; BO comparisons typically average over many starts to account for stochasticity in hyperparameter learning and acquisition optimization.
  - Missing comparisons to information-theoretic BO (Entropy Search, Hennig & Schuler, NIPS 2012; Predictive Entropy Search, NIPS 2014), which are common strong baselines.
  - All tasks are synthetic (≤6D, except REMBO). Include at least one real tuning task (e.g., ML hyperparameters, engineering/wetlab experiment) to demonstrate practical utility.
- Deterministic-only setting: Many BO applications are noisy. Please discuss whether the analysis or algorithm extends to noise (e.g., robust UCBs, replication strategies) and provide a small noisy benchmark if feasible.
- Sensitivity analysis: Provide ablations on Ξmax, η, kernel choice and hyperparameter learning (including mis-specified kernels), and the cost-accuracy trade-off from using GP-derived deferrals in step (v).
- Scalability: The bound degrades with D; discuss mitigation (additive models, embeddings, or structure learning) and provide more evidence beyond a single REMBO example.
- Clarity/notation: Unify notation for iteration/depth counters, define all symbols in the main text, and move key definitions (e.g., ρt) closer to where they are first used.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical result (exponential regret without δ-cover) on an important BO problem.
- Conceptually interesting “infinite-metric” perspective that bridges GP BO and optimistic partition-based search.
- Algorithmic contribution that removes reliance on auxiliary global optimization, simplifying practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation does not meet common BO standards: no multi-start statistical comparisons, no information-theoretic baselines, no real-world tasks.
- Deterministic-only validation, with limited discussion of noise robustness.
- Scalability and sensitivity aspects are not thoroughly explored.

Overall assessment
A well-written paper with a compelling theoretical advance and a thoughtful algorithmic design. However, the experimental section is below the bar for NIPS BO papers. I lean toward a borderline recommendation: acceptable if the authors substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation (multi-start comparisons, ES/PES baselines, and at least one real-world task), or otherwise defer to a future iteration with more comprehensive experiments.
