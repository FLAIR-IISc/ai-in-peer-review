### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO). The primary contribution is a method that achieves an exponential convergence rate for global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The authors state that their approach overcomes two significant limitations of prior work: 1) it avoids the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization step to select the next sample point, which is a common and potentially costly subroutine in many Bayesian optimization methods; and 2) it eliminates the requirement for a δ-cover sampling procedure, an assumption deemed impractical in previous work that also achieved exponential convergence [1].

The core idea of IMGPO is to combine a GP-UCB framework with a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. Instead of relying solely on the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP, the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite family of potential Lipschitz-like bounds. It uses the function values at the centers of hyperrectangles to guide the search (akin to the DIRECT [19] and SOO [18] algorithms) and leverages the GP's UCB to prune branches of the search tree that are unlikely to contain the global optimum. This synthesis of ideas leads to the main theoretical result: a high-probability regret bound that decreases exponentially with the number of function evaluations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important open problem in Bayesian optimization: achieving a fast (exponential) convergence rate without impractical assumptions or computationally expensive subroutines. The theoretical result (Theorem 1) represents a significant improvement over the polynomial regret bounds of related methods like BaMSOO [2]. The conceptual contribution of blending a bound-free partitioning approach (like DIRECT/SOO) with a model-based one (GP-UCB) is novel and compelling.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and provides a formal proof for the claimed exponential regret bound. The introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" is a useful analytical tool for understanding the trade-offs in this hybrid approach. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical derivations.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The algorithm is described with both a high-level step-by-step procedure and detailed pseudocode. The inclusion of Figure 1, which provides a 1-dimensional visual walkthrough of the algorithm's execution, is particularly effective at building intuition for its mechanics.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / for revision):**

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The primary reservation with this work concerns its scalability to higher-dimensional problems. The algorithm's core is based on hierarchical space partitioning, a technique that is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality. As the dimension `D` increases, the number of partitions required to cover the space at a given resolution grows exponentially. This is a well-known limitation of related algorithms like DIRECT.

    While the authors briefly acknowledge this limitation at the end of Section 4 and present experimental results on problems with up to 6 dimensions, the scalability remains a major practical concern. The 1000-dimensional experiment is encouraging, but it relies on using IMGPO as a subroutine within the REMBO framework [14], which uses random embeddings to handle high dimensionality. This makes it difficult to assess the performance of the core IMGPO algorithm in high dimensions. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more direct analysis or discussion of how the performance of standalone IMGPO is expected to degrade as dimensionality increases.

### Questions and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  The statement on page 3, "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is central to the paper's motivation but is difficult to fully grasp intuitively. Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the algorithm's mechanics (specifically the interplay between steps (i)-(ii) and step (iii)) translate to searching over this "infinite set of candidate bounds"?

2.  In Section 2, the UCB acquisition function is chosen "for brevity." This implies other acquisition functions might be suitable. However, the theoretical analysis seems to rely on the specific properties of the UCB criterion (`f(x) ≤ U(x|D)` with high probability). Is the framework extensible to other popular acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, or is the UCB choice fundamental to the theoretical guarantees?

3.  The experimental evaluation is solid for low-dimensional problems. To better address the scalability concerns, it would be beneficial to include results on moderately-dimensional benchmark functions (e.g., 10-20 dimensions) without the REMBO framework, to illustrate the standalone performance curve of IMGPO as `D` grows.

4.  For improved clarity in the illustrative example (Figure 1), could the authors briefly explain *why* no function evaluation occurs at `n=16`? The text points to step (v) of the algorithm, which means the UCB at the new candidate points was less than the best function value found so far. Explicitly confirming this for the depicted step would help solidify the reader's understanding.
