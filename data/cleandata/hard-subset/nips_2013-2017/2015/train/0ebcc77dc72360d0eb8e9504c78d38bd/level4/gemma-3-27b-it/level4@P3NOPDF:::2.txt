## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission – Bayesian Global Optimization with Interval Partitioning

This paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian Global Optimization, utilizing a space/interval partitioning algorithm to strategically generate future samples. By leveraging an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) acquisition function in conjunction with this partitioning scheme, the authors effectively circumvent the internal global optimization problem inherent in many Bayesian optimization methods. The theoretical analysis demonstrates promising results, establishing exponential regret bounds and thus showcasing an improvement over existing techniques. The algorithm is clearly described, well-motivated, and illustrated with a helpful example. Overall, this is a sound and novel contribution to the field.

However, I have one significant reservation regarding the practical scalability of the proposed method. The experimental evaluation appears limited to one-dimensional test functions; while not explicitly stated, this is strongly suggested by the presented results. I am concerned that the interval partitioning approach may not scale effectively to higher-dimensional problems. As the dimensionality increases, maintaining sufficient resolution within the hyperrectangles generated by the partitioning would likely require an exponential increase in partitioning points. This potential scaling issue is reminiscent of the DIRECT algorithm, a related method known to suffer performance degradation with increasing dimensionality.

While the theoretical results remain valid regardless of dimensionality, this scalability limitation is crucial to acknowledge for any potential practical application of the algorithm. I recommend the authors address this point in the paper, discussing the expected performance in higher dimensions and potentially outlining strategies to mitigate the scaling concerns (e.g., adaptive partitioning, dimensionality reduction techniques).

Regarding minor points, on page 2, the authors state that UCB is considered “for brevity.” This phrasing is somewhat unclear. Could the approach be generalized to other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement? While the presentation effectively centers around UCB, briefly acknowledging the potential for alternatives would enhance clarity. On page 3, the statement “…we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” is difficult to interpret. A more precise explanation of this process would be beneficial. Finally, on page 4, the explanation of the behavior at n=16 (“At n=16, the far right...but no function evaluation occurs”) lacks sufficient detail. Clarifying *why* no function evaluation is performed in this case would improve understanding.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a valuable contribution to Bayesian Global Optimization with a well-defined algorithm and strong theoretical guarantees. However, the lack of evaluation on higher-dimensional problems and the potential scalability issues of the partitioning scheme raise concerns about its practical applicability. Addressing these points, particularly by acknowledging the dimensionality limitations and discussing potential mitigation strategies, would significantly strengthen the paper. Despite this reservation, I believe the theoretical contribution is significant and the paper is overall clearly presented.
