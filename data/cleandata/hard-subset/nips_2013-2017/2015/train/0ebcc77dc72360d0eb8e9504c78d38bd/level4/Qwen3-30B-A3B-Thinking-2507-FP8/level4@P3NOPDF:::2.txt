

This paper introduces a novel variant of Bayesian global optimization that replaces the standard internal global optimization step for selecting next samples with a space partitioning algorithm. The key innovation lies in using Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) acquisition to avoid solving a separate global optimization problem, yielding a theoretically sound approach with exponential regret bounds—a significant improvement over prior work. The algorithm is clearly described, well-motivated, and illustrated with a concrete example, making the contribution accessible and the presentation strong. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and constitutes a clear novelty.

However, a major limitation concerns the experimental evaluation. The results are exclusively demonstrated on 1D test functions (e.g., the Branin function), without explicit acknowledgment or justification for this restriction. This raises critical questions about scalability, as the interval partitioning approach inherently faces exponential growth in required resolution with dimensionality. Specifically, to maintain the same "grid" density in higher dimensions (e.g., hyperrectangles), the number of partitions must increase exponentially with the input dimension. This directly parallels the well-documented dimensionality curse in methods like DIRECT, which suffers from poor performance in high-dimensional spaces due to similar combinatorial explosion. The authors should explicitly address this limitation in the paper, as it significantly impacts practical applicability—while the theory remains valid, the method’s utility for real-world problems (typically >2D) is questionable without evidence of scalability. A brief discussion of this trade-off, perhaps with a small 2D experiment or reference to related work on high-dimensional BO, would substantially strengthen the contribution.

Minor points for clarification:  
- Page 2: The note that UCB is used "for brevity" warrants expansion. The authors should justify why UCB is preferred over alternatives (e.g., Expected Improvement) for this specific partitioning framework, as the choice impacts the algorithm’s behavior and theoretical guarantees.  
- Page 3: The phrase "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is ambiguous. Clarify how the partitioning mechanism enables simultaneous global/local exploration—does it refer to partitioning the search space to cover both broad regions and local neighborhoods?  
- Page 4: The comment that "no function evaluation occurs" at n=16 (e.g., for the Branin function) lacks context. Briefly explain why the partitioning step at this iteration does not trigger an evaluation (e.g., the partitioning criterion identifies a subinterval where the UCB upper bound is sufficiently low to avoid sampling).

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with a clean algorithmic design. The exponential regret guarantee is compelling, and the writing is clear. However, the absence of higher-dimensional experiments and the lack of discussion on scalability represent a notable gap. For a conference like NIPS, where practical relevance is highly valued, this limitation must be addressed to fully justify the method’s significance. The authors should either extend the experiments to higher dimensions (even if preliminary) or explicitly discuss the dimensionality constraints and potential mitigation strategies. Without this, the work risks being perceived as primarily theoretical with limited applicability to the broader optimization community.  

The paper is technically sound and novel, but its practical impact hinges on resolving the scalability concern. With minor revisions addressing the dimensionality limitation and clarifying the points above, this would be a strong submission. As is, the current evaluation scope significantly undermines the practical motivation, despite the solid theoretical foundation.
