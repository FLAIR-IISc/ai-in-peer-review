This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence, addressing limitations of existing approaches that require auxiliary optimization or impractical δ-cover sampling. The core idea is to combine Gaussian process optimization with an infinite-metric approach and hierarchical partitioning to efficiently explore the search space. This avoids the need for an inner optimization loop often present in other Bayesian optimization algorithms. The theoretical analysis demonstrates an exponential convergence rate, representing an improvement over previous results, particularly those relying on δ-cover sampling.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed algorithm. The inclusion of an illustrative example (Figure 1) significantly aids understanding. The authors appropriately relate their work to existing literature, including discussions of Lipschitz optimization, Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes, and previous approaches to achieving exponential convergence (e.g., de Freitas et al. [1], Wang et al. [2]). The connection to bound-based search methods and the DIRECT algorithm is also well-articulated.

A key strength of this work is its theoretical contribution. The authors rigorously analyze the algorithm, proving an exponential convergence rate and discussing the impact of various parameters and assumptions. The detailed analysis in Section 4, including the discussion of the infinite-metric exploration loss and the effect of the GP prior, is particularly valuable.

However, I have a reservation regarding the scalability of the proposed approach to higher dimensions. The experimental evaluation focuses on relatively low-dimensional problems, and the interval partitioning strategy, while effective in these cases, may become computationally expensive and less efficient as the dimensionality increases. This is because maintaining sufficient resolution in high-dimensional hyperrectangles would likely require an exponential increase in the number of partitions, similar to the challenges faced by the DIRECT algorithm. While the theoretical contribution remains valid, this limitation is significant for practical applications. The paper would benefit from a discussion acknowledging this potential scalability issue and perhaps outlining potential avenues for addressing it (e.g., using dimensionality reduction techniques or adaptive partitioning strategies).

Minor comments:

*   Page 2: The statement that UCB is considered “for brevity” is slightly unclear. It would be helpful to clarify whether other acquisition functions could be used within the proposed framework.
*   Page 3: The sentence “…we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” is difficult to interpret. Rephrasing this sentence to more clearly explain the interplay between global and local search would improve readability.
*   Page 4: The explanation of why no function evaluation occurs at n=16 would benefit from additional detail. Clarifying *why* the algorithm skips evaluation in this case would aid understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to Bayesian optimization.
*   Proven exponential convergence rate, improving upon existing results.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the algorithm and analysis.
*   Strong connection to relevant literature and appropriate acknowledgement of related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation, primarily focused on low-dimensional problems.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the scalability of the method to higher dimensions, a potential limitation for practical applications.



**Overall:**

Despite the limitation regarding scalability and the need for minor clarifications, this is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. I believe it warrants acceptance, provided the authors address the scalability concerns by adding a discussion section acknowledging the issue and potentially suggesting future research directions.
