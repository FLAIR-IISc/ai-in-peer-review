This paper proposes a variant of Bayesian global optimization that generates future evaluations via a space/interval partitioning scheme. By using UCB, the method avoids the inner global optimization typically required to select the next query. Theoretical analysis indicates improved performance—specifically, exponentially decreasing regret—relative to prior work. The algorithm is clearly described and supported by an illustrative example. Overall, the contribution appears novel and the presentation is generally clear.

My main reservation concerns empirical validation and scalability. The experiments seem to involve only one-dimensional test functions (this is not stated explicitly). I am concerned that the interval/space partitioning strategy will not scale well to higher dimensions, where hyperrectangle resolution must grow exponentially. The approach is closely related to the DIRECT algorithm, which is known to degrade significantly as dimensionality increases. At minimum, the paper should discuss this limitation, its practical implications, and any possible mitigations (e.g., adaptive partitioning, dimensionality reduction, or structure-exploiting assumptions). While this does not detract from the theoretical contribution, it is important for practical relevance.

Minor comments and questions:
- p.2: UCB is used “for brevity.” Is the method inherently tied to UCB, or could it be adapted to other acquisition functions such as Expected Improvement or Thompson sampling? The current presentation suggests UCB may be essential to the analysis and algorithm.
- p.3: The sentence “we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” is unclear. Please clarify what “candidates of the bounds” means and how the method balances global versus local search in practice.
- p.4: “At n=16, the far right... but no function evaluation occurs.” Please explain why no evaluation is performed at this step.

In summary, the paper offers a clear, well-motivated algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees and a novel perspective on acquisition optimization via partitioning. The primary weakness is the apparent restriction of experiments to 1D and the lack of discussion about scalability to higher dimensions, which is crucial for practical impact.
