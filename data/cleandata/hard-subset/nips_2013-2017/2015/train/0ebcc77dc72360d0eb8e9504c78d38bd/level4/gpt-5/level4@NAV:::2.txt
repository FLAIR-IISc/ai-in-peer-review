The paper introduces a variant of Bayesian global optimization in which future evaluations are selected via a space/interval partitioning scheme. By employing UCB, the method avoids the internal global optimization step typically required to choose the next sample. Theoretical analysis demonstrates improved guarantees (exponentially fast regret), surpassing prior work. The algorithm is described clearly and illustrated with an example. The contribution is novel and the paper is overall well written.

I have one reservation. The experiments appear to focus solely on 1D test functions (this is not stated explicitly). I suspect that interval-based partitioning will not scale favorably to higher dimensions (hyperrectangles), since the required resolution would grow exponentially. The approach is related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to degrade substantially as dimensionality increases. The paper should at least discuss this issue. It does not diminish the theoretical contribution but is important for practical applicability.

Minor comments:
- p.2: UCB is used “for brevity.” Does this mean the method could also be instantiated with expected improvement, for instance? My impression was that UCB is essential here.
- p.3: “...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds.” This sentence was unclear to me.
- p.4: “At n=16, the far right... but no function evaluation occurs.” Please explain why no evaluation is performed for clarity.

In sum, the paper proposes a Bayesian global optimization variant using interval partitioning with exponentially fast regret guarantees; the work seems sound and original, but the evaluation appears limited to 1D problems.
