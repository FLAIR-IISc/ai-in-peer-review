The paper presents a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization where future samples are generated through a space/interval partitioning algorithm. This approach avoids the internal global optimization problem of choosing the next sample, instead utilizing the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) to guide the sampling process. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, demonstrating that their method achieves exponential regret, thus improving upon previous work. The algorithm is clearly described, explained, and illustrated with an example, making it easy to follow.

The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian Global Optimization, and its overall presentation is clear and well-organized. However, I do have one significant reservation regarding the experimental results. It appears that the evaluation is limited to 1D test functions, which is not explicitly stated in the paper. I suspect that the interval partitioning approach may not scale well to higher dimensions, as the required "resolution" would need to grow exponentially. This concern is exacerbated by the fact that the approach is related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from poor performance as the dimensionality of the problem increases.

While this limitation does not diminish the theoretical contribution of the paper, it is crucial to acknowledge and discuss its implications for practical applications. I recommend that the authors address this concern in their paper, providing insight into the potential scalability of their approach.

Minor comments:

* On page 2, the authors mention that they consider UCB "for brevity." I would like to know if other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, could be used in place of UCB.
* On page 3, the statement "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is unclear. I would appreciate a more detailed explanation of this concept.
* On page 4, the authors mention that at n=16, the far right interval is divided, but no function evaluation occurs. A brief explanation of why this is the case would be helpful for clarity.

In summary, the paper presents a sound and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian Global Optimization. However, it is essential to address the potential scalability concerns and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the approach.
