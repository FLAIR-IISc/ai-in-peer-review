

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of existing methods: GP-UCB's need for an additional non-convex global optimization problem and the impractical δ-cover sampling required by previous methods with exponential convergence [1, 2]. The proposed approach leverages a "semi-metric" assumption to simultaneously consider infinitely many possible bounds, avoiding reliance on a single potentially loose bound from the GP prior.

The paper is well-motivated and makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization. The theoretical analysis demonstrates an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, improving upon the polynomial regret bounds of related methods like BaMSOO [2]. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of how the GP prior and infinite-metric exploration interact to achieve this improvement, with insightful remarks on the effects of bound tightness, GP usage, and infinite-metric optimization.

The paper excels in clarity: the algorithm is well-described with a detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1), and the example in Figure 1 effectively illustrates the interval partitioning procedure. The technical explanations are thorough, and the relationship to prior work (particularly DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]) is well-articulated. The supplementary material provides necessary theoretical proofs, enhancing the paper's quality.

Strengths:
- Theoretical contribution is strong and novel, with clear improvements over prior work
- The paper clearly explains the intuition behind the approach (considering infinitely many bounds)
- The algorithm is well-described with a helpful illustrative example
- The analysis provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between bound tightness and regret
- The paper addresses limitations of prior work (no auxiliary optimization, no δ-cover sampling)

Weaknesses:
- The experimental evaluation is limited to 1D test functions (Sin1, Sin2, Branin, Hartmann3), which does not adequately demonstrate the practical utility of the method for higher-dimensional problems
- The authors do not address the well-known dimensionality curse of partitioning-based methods (like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]), which is a significant concern for practical applications
- The paper claims to be "practically oriented" but lacks experiments on higher-dimensional problems (2D or higher) to validate this claim
- The theoretical analysis suggests scalability challenges (λ approaching 1 as dimension D increases), but the paper does not explore practical solutions to this

The paper would be significantly strengthened by experiments on higher-dimensional problems (2D, 3D, etc.) to demonstrate practical utility beyond 1D. The authors should also discuss how their method might scale to higher dimensions (e.g., by leveraging additional assumptions like those in REMBO [14]), rather than simply stating that the method is applicable to high-dimensional problems without evidence.

Regarding minor points:
- The statement that "for brevity, we consider Bayesian optimization with UCB" (p.2) is misleading; UCB is central to the algorithm design, not merely a simplification. The paper should clarify why UCB was chosen over other acquisition functions like EI.
- The phrase "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" (p.3) is confusing; the paper should clarify what "candidates of the bounds" means and how the search is conducted.
- The explanation for why no function evaluation occurs at n=16 (p.4) is insufficient; the paper should clarify the conditions under which GP-based UCB values are used instead of actual function evaluations.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization, but its practical relevance is currently limited by the lack of evaluation on higher-dimensional problems. Addressing this limitation would significantly strengthen the paper's impact and make it more compelling for the NIPS audience. The theoretical advances are substantial, but without evidence of practical applicability beyond 1D, the contribution's significance for real-world applications remains questionable.
