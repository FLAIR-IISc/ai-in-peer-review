This paper proposes a variant of Bayesian global optimization in which future samples are generated by a space/interval partitioning algorithm. By using a UCB acquisition, the method avoids the inner global optimization problem usually required to select the next sample. Theoretical analysis shows exponential regret, improving on prior work. The algorithm is clearly described and well illustrated with an example. Overall, the contribution is novel and the presentation is clear.

My main reservation concerns empirical validation. The experiments appear to be conducted only on 1D test functions (this is not stated explicitly). I suspect that the interval-partitioning approach will not scale well to higher dimensions (i.e., to hyperrectangles), because the resolution required would grow exponentially. The approach is closely related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to perform poorly as dimensionality increases. The paper should at least discuss this issue; it does not diminish the theoretical contribution but is important for practical relevance.

Minor comments:
- p.2: You mention using UCB “for brevity.” Does the approach also accommodate other acquisition functions such as expected improvement, or is UCB essential to the method and analysis? As written, it feels like it must be UCB.
- p.3: The sentence “we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” is unclear; please rephrase or elaborate.
- p.4: “At n=16, the far right … but no function evaluation occurs.” Please explain why no evaluation occurs for clarity.

In summary, the paper presents a clear and novel Bayesian optimization method based on interval partitioning with theoretical improvements (exponential regret), but the experiments seem restricted to 1D test problems, raising concerns about scalability to higher dimensions.
