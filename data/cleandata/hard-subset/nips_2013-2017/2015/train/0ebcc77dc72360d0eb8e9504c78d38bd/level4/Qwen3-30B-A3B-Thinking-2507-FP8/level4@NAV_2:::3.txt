

The paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization—a significant advancement over prior work. The authors address two key limitations in the field: de Freitas et al.'s [1] method relies on impractical δ-cover sampling (critiqued by Wang et al. [2]), while standard GP-UCB requires solving an additional non-convex optimization problem for the acquisition function. IMGPO overcomes both by leveraging an infinite-metric exploration framework that simultaneously considers all possible upper bounds encoded in the function's continuity, rather than relying on a single GP-derived bound. This approach builds on Munos' SOO [18] and Jones' DIRECT [19] but uniquely integrates GP priors to achieve exponential regret bounds, contrasting with BaMSOO [2], which only attains polynomial regret. The paper's theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) proves an exponential regret bound O(λ^{N+N_gp}) with λ<1, improving upon BaMSOO's Õ(n^{-2α/D(4-α)}) and providing a principled analysis of how bound tightness affects performance (Remarks 1–3). The algorithmic description in Sections 3.2–3.3 is exceptionally clear, with a detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and illustrative examples that enable direct replication. Experiments across eight multimodal benchmarks (Figure 2) demonstrate consistent superiority over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, particularly for low-dimensional problems (Sin1, Sin2, Branin, Hartmann3), with runtime efficiency (Table 1) confirming the elimination of auxiliary optimization.  

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality:** Solves a well-documented open problem (Wang et al. [2]) by combining infinite-metric exploration with GP, offering a novel perspective on bound-based optimization that bridges two prior literature strands.  
- **Quality:** Theoretical analysis is rigorous (supplementary material), with clear connections between Assumption 1 (unknown semi-metric bound) and the regret bound. The regret bound explicitly quantifies the trade-off between GP-derived bounds and unknown function properties.  
- **Significance:** Provides a practically viable path to exponential convergence in Bayesian optimization, directly addressing a major limitation in real-world applications where δ-cover sampling is infeasible. The framework could generalize to other bound-based methods (e.g., A* search, PAC-MDP).  
- **Clarity:** The algorithm description (Section 3.2) and pseudocode (Algorithm 1) are exceptionally well-structured, with intuitive explanations of steps (i)–(v) and parameter roles (e.g., Ξmax).  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Scalability:** The regret bound's dependence on dimensionality (Theorem 1, Remark 1) suggests exponential convergence weakens as D increases. While the authors acknowledge this, the experimental section lacks high-dimensional tests beyond the 1000D Sin2 embedding (Section 5), which relies on REMBO. A more thorough analysis of scalability (e.g., 10D–100D benchmarks) would strengthen the significance claim.  
- **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments show Ξmax=22 is sufficient for most functions, but Section 5 notes performance gains from increasing Ξmax at the cost of CPU time. The paper does not discuss how to automatically select Ξmax in practice, which could impact usability.  
- **Comparison Scope:** The comparison omits recent BO methods like GP-UCB with adaptive kernels [20] or high-dimensional approaches (e.g., [14]), which might contextualize IMGPO's advantages more fully.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Solves a critical practical barrier (δ-cover sampling) while maintaining exponential convergence, with a theoretically sound and clearly articulated algorithm. The work directly advances the state-of-the-art in Bayesian optimization and aligns with NIPS' focus on rigorous, impactful methods.  
*Con:* Limited scalability analysis for high-dimensional problems and a parameter (Ξmax) whose optimal setting requires empirical tuning.  

The paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS, delivering both theoretical novelty and practical relevance. The minor weaknesses are well-justified within the paper and do not undermine the core advancement. I recommend acceptance.
