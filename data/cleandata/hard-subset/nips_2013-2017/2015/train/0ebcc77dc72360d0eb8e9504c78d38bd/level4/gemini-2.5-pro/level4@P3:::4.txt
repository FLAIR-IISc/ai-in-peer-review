### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for non-convex black-box functions. The primary contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without two major practical drawbacks of previous methods: it does not require solving an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next point, and it does not rely on an impractical "δ-cover" sampling scheme.

The context for this work is the long-standing goal of developing practical and theoretically sound global optimization algorithms. Standard Gaussian Process (GP) based methods, such as GP-UCB, typically require a computationally expensive sub-routine to maximize an acquisition function at each step. While de Freitas et al. (2012) proposed a method with an exponential convergence guarantee, it depended on δ-cover sampling, which is considered computationally infeasible. More recent work, like BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014), removed this dependency but at the cost of achieving only a polynomial convergence rate.

The proposed IMGPO algorithm elegantly bridges the gap between these approaches. It combines a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, similar to algorithms like DIRECT and SOO, with GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCBs). The key idea is to not rely solely on the GP-derived bound, which may be loose. Instead, the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite family of potential smoothness assumptions (or "semi-metrics") around the global optimum, a concept central to the SOO algorithm. The GP-UCB is then used not for direct maximization, but as an efficient mechanism to prune unpromising regions of the search space, thereby accelerating the search guided by the hierarchical partitioning. This novel combination allows IMGPO to achieve the desired exponential convergence rate in a practical algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and well-recognized open problem in the field of Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential regret bounds with a practical algorithm. By successfully removing the need for both auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, the authors have made a substantial contribution that advances the state of the art. This work is likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian optimization.

2.  **Originality:** The proposed method is a novel and insightful synthesis of two distinct lines of research in global optimization: bound-based methods that rely on an explicit model (like GP-UCB) and partitioning-based methods that make weaker assumptions about the function's smoothness (like DIRECT/SOO). The idea of using the GP not to guide the search directly, but to prune branches in a search tree that considers an infinite set of metrics, is elegant and powerful.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The claims are well-supported by strong theoretical analysis and convincing empirical results. Theorem 1 provides a formal proof of the algorithm's exponential convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing IMGPO against several relevant and strong baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) across a variety of benchmark functions with different dimensionalities and characteristics. The results clearly demonstrate the superior performance of IMGPO in most cases.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and clear. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and situating their contribution within the relevant literature. The description of the algorithm in Section 3.2, aided by the excellent Figure 1, is particularly effective at providing an intuitive understanding of a complex procedure. This high level of clarity makes the paper accessible to a broader audience beyond experts in global optimization.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a very strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve it:

1.  **Discussion on Scalability:** The analysis in Section 4 correctly points out that the convergence rate constant λ can approach 1 as the input dimension D increases, implying a potential curse of dimensionality. The authors demonstrate a promising approach by embedding IMGPO within the REMBO framework for a high-dimensional experiment. However, a more explicit discussion of the algorithm's practical limitations as D grows would be valuable. For instance, how does the infinite-metric exploration loss `ρ̄t` behave with increasing dimensionality? This would provide readers with a clearer understanding of the regimes where IMGPO is most effective.

2.  **Guidance on Hyperparameters:** The algorithm introduces new parameters, primarily `Ξmax` and `η`. While the experimental setup fixes these values and briefly discusses the effect of `Ξmax`, the paper would be of greater practical value if it included a more detailed sensitivity analysis or at least some intuition and practical guidance on how to set these parameters for new problems.

3.  **Minor Refinements:**
    *   The abstract contains some redundancy. The first and last sentences state nearly the same contribution. This could be rephrased for greater impact. For example: "This paper presents a Bayesian optimization method that achieves an exponential convergence rate without the need for auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. Most existing methods require solving an additional non-convex optimization problem, and the only prior method with an exponential convergence guarantee relies on the impractical δ-cover assumption. Our approach, IMGPO, eliminates both of these demanding requirements."
    *   The use of `l` (lowercase L) as a hyperparameter for the Matern kernel in Section 5 can be easily confused with `1` (the number one). Using a different symbol, such as `ℓ`, would improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper solves an important and long-standing open problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   **Pro:** It presents the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential rate of convergence, a significant theoretical and practical advance.
*   **Pro:** The core idea is novel, elegantly combining techniques from two different subfields of optimization.
*   **Pro:** The claims are rigorously supported by both a formal proof and strong, comprehensive experimental results.
*   **Pro:** The presentation is exceptionally clear and didactic, making the work accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The discussion on the algorithm's scalability to higher dimensions, a known challenge for this class of methods, could be more detailed.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
