### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to achieve an exponential convergence rate for global optimization of black-box functions. The main contribution is that IMGPO accomplishes this without two significant practical drawbacks of previous methods: it eliminates the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization step to maximize an acquisition function (common in methods like GP-UCB), and it avoids the computationally impractical δ-cover sampling procedure required by the only other GP-based method with a proven exponential convergence rate [1].

The core idea is to combine the strengths of two distinct lines of optimization research. On one hand, it leverages a Gaussian Process (GP) prior to model the objective function and provide an upper confidence bound (UCB). On the other hand, inspired by algorithms like DIRECT and SOO, it does not rely solely on this explicit bound. Instead, it implicitly considers an infinite family of possible upper bounds derived from an unknown semi-metric that characterizes the function's continuity near the global optimum. The algorithm uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, and the GP-UCB is cleverly used to prune branches that are provably suboptimal, thereby accelerating the search for the intervals containing the global optimizer.

### Quality

The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work.

**Theoretical Contributions:** The primary theoretical result, Theorem 1, establishes an exponential regret bound for IMGPO. This is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of related algorithms like BaMSOO [2]. The analysis is insightful, particularly the introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" (ρt), which elegantly quantifies the cost of not committing to a single, known bound. The remarks following the theorem are very helpful, providing clear intuition on how the GP's tightness affects the convergence rate and why the "infinite-metric" approach can be superior even when a known Lipschitz-like bound exists.

**Experimental Contributions:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. IMGPO is compared against a strong set of baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, on a diverse suite of benchmark functions with dimensionalities ranging from 1 to 1000. The experiments are well-designed, particularly in their realistic assumption that a good kernel and hyperparameters are not known a priori. Across the majority of test cases, IMGPO demonstrates superior performance, often converging much more quickly to the global optimum. The inclusion of performance timings (Table 1) further substantiates the practical advantage of avoiding auxiliary optimization.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and clear. Even for a reader not deeply specialized in Bayesian optimization, the paper's core ideas are presented with remarkable clarity.

The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem in the introduction by clearly situating their work relative to the limitations of prior art. Section 3.1 provides a superb high-level overview of the algorithm's strategy before diving into the details. The use of Figure 1 is highly effective in providing a visual, step-by-step walkthrough of the algorithm's mechanics, making the complex interactions between the hierarchical partitioning and the GP-UCB pruning easy to follow. The pseudocode is detailed and appears sufficient for implementation.

A few minor points could further improve clarity:
- The abstract's first and last sentences convey nearly the same information. The final sentence could be rephrased to emphasize the core idea or key result instead.
- The manuscript would benefit from a final proofreading pass to correct minor typographical errors (e.g., a potential misplaced parenthesis was noted on a quick read, and some phrasing could be tightened).

### Originality

The work is highly original. While it builds upon established concepts—hierarchical partitioning from SOO [18] and GP-based bounds from GP-UCB [15]—the synthesis is novel and non-trivial. The key innovation is using the GP-UCB not to guide the search directly via auxiliary optimization, but as a mechanism to prune the search space explored by the optimistic, multi-bound approach of SOO. This successfully bridges two distinct approaches to global optimization. To my knowledge, this is the first practical algorithm to achieve an exponential convergence rate for GP-based optimization without requiring δ-cover sampling, directly addressing what was stated as an open problem in [2].

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and global optimization.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by resolving a known open problem. Furthermore, it provides a practical, high-performance algorithm. The elimination of the auxiliary optimization loop makes IMGPO much faster per iteration than standard GP-BO methods (as shown in Table 1) and simpler to implement, lowering the barrier to adoption. Practitioners tuning hyperparameters for machine learning models or solving other expensive black-box optimization problems are likely to find this method very useful. The analytical framework, particularly the concept of the infinite-metric exploration loss, also provides a new lens through which to understand the trade-offs in bound-based optimization algorithms, which could inspire future theoretical work.

### Arguments For Acceptance:
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a novel algorithm that solves a well-established open problem in Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential convergence without impractical sampling assumptions.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The exponential regret bound is proven and supported by an insightful analysis that clarifies the benefits of the proposed approach.
3.  **Convincing Empirical Evidence:** The algorithm is shown to outperform several state-of-the-art and widely used methods on a comprehensive set of benchmark problems.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written and accessible, with excellent motivation and a clear, intuitive explanation of the algorithm's mechanics.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Scalability:** The paper acknowledges that the convergence rate can degrade with high dimensionality (as λ approaches 1). While a 1000-D experiment is included using REMBO, a more in-depth discussion of the algorithm's limitations in high-dimensional settings would be beneficial.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically effective solution to an important problem. I recommend acceptance.
