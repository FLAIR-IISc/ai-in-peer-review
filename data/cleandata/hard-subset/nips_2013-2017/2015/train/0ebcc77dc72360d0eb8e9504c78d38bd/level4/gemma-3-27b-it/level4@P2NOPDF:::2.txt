This paper introduces a novel Bayesian Global Optimization method that utilizes a space/interval partitioning algorithm to determine future sampling locations, effectively circumventing the internal global optimization problem inherent in Upper Confidence Bound (UCB)-based approaches. The authors demonstrate a theoretical improvement over existing methods, achieving exponential regret bounds. The algorithm is presented clearly and is well-illustrated with an example, representing a genuine and novel contribution to the field.

My primary concern lies with the scope of the experimental evaluation. The results appear to be limited to one-dimensional test functions, a detail that isn’t explicitly stated but is strongly implied. I anticipate that the interval partitioning strategy may not scale effectively to higher-dimensional problems. Maintaining sufficient resolution within hyperrectangles would likely necessitate exponential growth in partitioning complexity as dimensionality increases. This limitation is particularly relevant given the similarities between this approach and the DIRECT algorithm, which is known to degrade significantly with increasing dimensionality. While the theoretical guarantees remain valid, this scalability issue is a crucial consideration for practical applications and warrants discussion within the manuscript.

Despite this concern, the theoretical results are compelling and the presentation is generally strong. However, a few minor points require clarification. On page 2, the authors state UCB is considered “for brevity.” It would be beneficial to understand if other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, were considered and why UCB was ultimately chosen. The phrasing suggests UCB was the natural or only viable option, and clarifying this would strengthen the justification.

Furthermore, the statement on page 3 – “…we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” – is somewhat unclear. Expanding on this process would improve comprehension. Finally, on page 4, the description of the behavior at n=16 (“At n=16, the far right…but no function evaluation occurs”) lacks sufficient explanation. Specifying *why* no evaluation occurs at that point would enhance clarity.

In summary, this is a sound and innovative work with a solid theoretical foundation. Addressing the scalability concerns and clarifying the minor points mentioned above would significantly strengthen the paper and broaden its impact. The lack of higher-dimensional experimentation is a notable limitation, but does not invalidate the theoretical contribution.
