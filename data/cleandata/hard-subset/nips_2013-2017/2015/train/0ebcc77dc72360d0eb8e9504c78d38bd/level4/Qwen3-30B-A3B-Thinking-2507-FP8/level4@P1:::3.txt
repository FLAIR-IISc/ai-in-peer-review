

This paper presents Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization—two significant practical limitations in previous approaches. The authors build upon the theoretical work of de Freitas et al. (2012) who established exponential convergence but relied on impractical δ-cover sampling, and Wang et al. (2013) who introduced BaMSOO with polynomial regret bounds by combining GP-UCB with SOO but still required auxiliary optimization. The key innovation is the "infinite-metric" approach, where the algorithm considers all possible semi-metric bounds rather than relying on a single upper bound estimated by the GP prior. This enables exponential convergence while eliminating both major practical barriers.

The paper excels in theoretical rigor and clarity. The authors provide a precise definition of the infinite-metric exploration loss ρ̄_t and prove that IMGPO achieves regret bounds of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1 (Theorem 1). The analysis includes insightful remarks about how the tightness of the GP-based bound affects performance (Remarks 1-3), demonstrating that even a non-tight bound can be beneficial when considering all possible bounds. The theoretical contribution is significant as it resolves a key open problem identified by Wang et al. (2013).

The algorithm is exceptionally well-explained with a clear example in Figure 1 and comprehensive pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The hierarchical partitioning mechanism is intuitive, and the step-by-step explanation of the algorithm's operation in Section 3.2 makes it accessible for implementation. The paper's organization is logical, moving from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experimental validation without unnecessary detours.

The experimental section is strong and comprehensive. The authors compare IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on eight diverse benchmark functions across dimensions 1-1000. The results consistently show IMGPO outperforming other methods, with particularly strong results on challenging multimodal functions like Branin and Hartmann3. The authors appropriately address practical considerations by using a single isotropic Matern kernel with default hyperparameters (σ=1, l=0.25) for all experiments, reflecting real-world scenarios where optimal kernels are unknown. The ablation study on Ξ_max parameter demonstrates how this hyperparameter affects performance, with the authors noting that increasing Ξ_max improves results for most functions at the cost of additional computation.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a genuine contribution by extending the "multiple bounds" concept from DIRECT and SOO to Bayesian optimization with rigorous theoretical support. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from BaMSOO (which achieves polynomial regret) and GP-UCB (which requires auxiliary optimization), demonstrating that IMGPO's exponential convergence is both theoretically sound and practically superior.

The significance of this work is high. Bayesian optimization is widely used in machine learning for hyperparameter tuning, and eliminating the need for δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization substantially increases its applicability. The exponential convergence rate represents a meaningful theoretical advancement over previous GP-based methods.

The main limitation is that the exponential convergence rate becomes less effective in higher dimensions due to the dimension-dependent term λ = 3 - α/2CDρ̄_t. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 4 and provide a 1000-dimensional test case using REMBO, a more thorough discussion of scalability challenges and potential mitigation strategies (beyond the brief mention of additional assumptions) would strengthen the paper. The experimental section could also include more detailed analysis of how the dimensionality affects the infinite-metric exploration loss ρ̄_t.

The paper is technically sound with claims well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to address limitations (e.g., the dimensionality challenge) and provide appropriate comparisons to relevant prior work. The writing is clear, well-organized, and accessible to experts in the field.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a key open problem in Bayesian optimization with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with clear insights about the relationship between bound tightness and performance
- Demonstrates superior empirical performance across diverse benchmark functions
- Clear and well-organized presentation with sufficient detail for reproducibility

Arguments against acceptance:
- The dimensionality limitation for exponential convergence is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed
- More detailed discussion of scalability to high-dimensional problems would strengthen the practical contribution

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization with strong theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. It addresses an important practical limitation in current methods while advancing the theoretical understanding of the field. The work aligns with NIPS' focus on high-quality scientific contributions that meaningfully advance the state of the art. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the dimensionality scalability discussion.
