The paper proposes a variant of Bayesian global optimization in which future samples are generated via a space/interval partitioning scheme. By using UCB, the method sidesteps the inner global optimization typically required to choose the next sample. Theoretical analysis shows improved guarantees—specifically, exponentially decaying regret—relative to prior work. The algorithm is clearly described and well illustrated with an example. Overall, the contribution appears novel and the presentation is clear.

My main reservation concerns the experiments, which seem to be conducted only on 1D test functions (this is not stated explicitly). I suspect the interval-partitioning strategy will scale poorly in higher dimensions, where partitions become hyperrectangles and the required “resolution” grows exponentially with dimension. The approach is closely related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to degrade severely as dimensionality increases. While this does not diminish the theoretical contribution, it is highly relevant for practical use; the paper should explicitly discuss scalability and the connection to DIRECT, and either provide higher-dimensional experiments or a clear rationale for the focus on 1D.

Minor comments:
- p.2: You state that UCB is considered “for brevity.” Does this mean the approach could also be used with other acquisition functions, such as expected improvement? My impression is that the method requires UCB; please clarify whether and how it extends to other criteria.
- p.3: “...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds.” This sentence is unclear. Please rephrase to explain precisely what is meant by “candidates of the bounds” and how global vs. local search are being performed concurrently.
- p.4: “At n=16, the far right ... but no function evaluation occurs.” Please explain why no evaluation occurs at that step, for clarity.

In summary, the paper presents a sound and novel BO variant based on interval partitioning, with clear exposition and strong theoretical results (exponentially decaying regret). However, the empirical evaluation appears limited to 1D, and the likely scalability issues in higher dimensions—closely related to known limitations of DIRECT—should be addressed explicitly.
