This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, dubbed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling – both limitations of existing approaches like those of de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The core idea is to combine Gaussian Process (GP) modeling with a hierarchical partitioning strategy to efficiently explore the search space, leveraging an unknown semi-metric to guide the partitioning process. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential convergence rate and validate their approach through experiments on several benchmark functions.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and theoretical results. It effectively positions itself within the existing literature on Bayesian optimization, specifically addressing the drawbacks of previous methods relying on δ-cover sampling [1, 2] or auxiliary optimization. The inclusion of an illustrative example (Figure 1) is particularly helpful for understanding the algorithm’s mechanics. The theoretical contribution – achieving exponential convergence without the problematic δ-cover sampling – is significant. The connection to bound-based search methods like DIRECT [19] is also well-articulated.

A key strength of this work is its attempt to circumvent the need for a precisely known Lipschitz constant, a common challenge in Bayesian optimization [7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. By leveraging an unknown semi-metric and simultaneously considering multiple potential bounds, IMGPO aims to achieve robustness and efficiency. The analysis is thorough, with careful consideration given to the impact of the GP prior and the exploration loss (ρt). The discussion of limitations and potential avenues for improvement, such as addressing scalability to higher dimensions, is commendable.

However, the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While the results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness on a suite of test functions, the experiments predominantly feature low-dimensional problems. The authors acknowledge the potential for scalability issues related to the interval partitioning strategy, drawing parallels to the DIRECT algorithm [19], but do not adequately address this concern. The performance in higher dimensions (e.g., the 1000-dimensional problem) is tested with REMBO [14], but the contribution of IMGPO *within* that framework isn’t isolated and examined thoroughly. A more extensive evaluation on higher-dimensional problems, potentially incorporating techniques to mitigate the curse of dimensionality, is crucial to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of IMGPO.

Regarding minor points, the justification for selecting UCB as the acquisition function (p.2) could be slightly expanded. While the authors state it is “for brevity,” explaining why other acquisition functions were not considered or why UCB was particularly well-suited would strengthen the argument. The statement on p.3 regarding “simultaneously conduct[ing] global and local searches” remains unclear and could benefit from further explanation. The explanation for the lack of function evaluation at n=16 (p.4) is also somewhat obscure; elaborating on the role of the UCB value in this context would improve clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant theoretical contribution: exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Novel approach that addresses a key limitation of existing Bayesian optimization methods.
*   Thorough theoretical analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation, primarily focused on low-dimensional problems.
*   Scalability concerns related to the interval partitioning strategy are not adequately addressed through experimental validation.
*   Some statements within the text require clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in the experimental evaluation, the theoretical contribution and clear presentation of IMGPO make it a worthwhile submission. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors addressing the scalability concerns by either: 1) Conducting experiments on a wider range of higher-dimensional problems, or 2) Providing a more detailed discussion of strategies to mitigate the dimensionality issues, and clarifying the points mentioned in the minor comments. Addressing the experimental limitations would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
