The paper presents a novel variant of Bayesian Global Optimization that generates future samples through a space/interval partitioning algorithm. By leveraging an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) acquisition function, the method avoids the internal global optimization problem inherent in many Bayesian Optimization approaches concerning the selection of the next sample point. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that this approach achieves improved performance, specifically exhibiting exponential regret, and thus represents an improvement over existing methods. The algorithm is clearly described and well-illustrated with a helpful example. Overall, the paper makes a novel contribution and is presented in a clear and accessible manner.

I do have one significant reservation regarding the scope of the experimental evaluation. The results appear to be limited to one-dimensional test functions; this is not explicitly stated but implied by the presented figures. I suspect that the interval partitioning approach may not scale effectively to higher dimensions, as maintaining sufficient resolution within hyperrectangles would likely require an exponential increase in partitioning complexity. This limitation is particularly relevant given the relationship to the DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from performance degradation as dimensionality increases. While this does not diminish the theoretical contribution, it is a crucial consideration for practical applicability and should be addressed within the paper, perhaps through a discussion of potential mitigation strategies or an acknowledgement of the scaling limitations.

Minor comments:

p.2: The authors state UCB is considered “for brevity.” Could this be clarified? Does this imply the approach is compatible with other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement? The presentation leads me to believe UCB is a necessary component, and elaboration here would be beneficial.

p.3: The statement “…we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” is unclear. Could this be rephrased for improved clarity?

p.4: Concerning the statement at n=16, “the far right…but no function evaluation occurs,” providing a brief explanation for this behavior would enhance understanding. 

In summary, this paper presents a sound and novel contribution to Bayesian Global Optimization with promising theoretical properties. However, the lack of evaluation on higher-dimensional problems raises concerns about its practical scalability.
