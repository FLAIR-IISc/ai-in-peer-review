This paper introduces a novel approach to Bayesian Global Optimization. The method bypasses the standard sub-problem of maximizing an acquisition function by instead generating future query points through a hierarchical space-partitioning algorithm guided by the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) criterion. The primary theoretical contribution is a proof of exponential regret, which represents a significant improvement over existing bounds in the literature. The algorithm is described with excellent clarity and is helpfully illustrated with a step-by-step example. Overall, the paper presents a novel and clearly articulated contribution.

My primary reservation, however, concerns the scalability of the proposed method and the scope of the experimental evaluation. The experiments appear to be conducted exclusively on one-dimensional test functions, although this is not explicitly stated. This is a potentially significant limitation, as the interval-partitioning strategy is unlikely to scale gracefully to higher-dimensional problems. As dimensionality increases, the number of hyperrectangles to consider would grow exponentially, a classic manifestation of the curse of dimensionality.

This concern is reinforced by the method's similarity to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from poor performance as the dimensionality of the problem increases. While this limitation does not diminish the paper's theoretical contribution for the 1D case, its practical implications are substantial. The authors should explicitly discuss the scalability of their approach. Addressing this issue, perhaps by presenting a theoretical argument or preliminary empirical results in higher dimensions, would significantly strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

**Minor Comments:**

*   On page 2, the use of UCB is justified "for brevity." This phrasing is slightly ambiguous. Does the theoretical analysis hold for other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, or is the choice of UCB fundamental to the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees?
*   On page 3, the paper states, "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds." I found this sentence difficult to parse. Could the authors please clarify the meaning of this statement?
*   On page 4, in the discussion of the example at n=16, it is mentioned that a region is partitioned "but no function evaluation occurs." For the reader's benefit, it would be helpful to briefly explain why a function evaluation is skipped in this specific step of the algorithm.
