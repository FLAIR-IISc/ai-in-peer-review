Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a Gaussian process (GP)–based Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves an exponential simple-regret rate for deterministic black-box functions while avoiding two common drawbacks: (i) the need to solve an auxiliary nonconvex optimization problem to select the next query (as in GP-UCB/EI/PI), and (ii) the impractical δ-cover sampling assumed in de Freitas et al. for exponential regret. The method combines hierarchical space partitioning with GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCB) to prune and prioritize intervals. A key conceptual contribution is to view bound-based optimization through an “infinite-metric” lens: instead of committing to a single (often loose) bound, the algorithm hedges over an infinite family of candidate bounds induced by an unknown semi-metric around the global optimizer, and uses the GP posterior to discard candidates that are provably looser.

The paper clearly situates IMGPO relative to DIRECT (Jones et al.) and SOO/DOO (Munos), which are partition-based and make minimal smoothness assumptions, and BaMSOO (Wang et al.), which combines SOO with GP-UCB but only yields polynomial regret. The theoretical analysis introduces an infinite-metric exploration loss that quantifies the extra cost of not committing to one bound, and shows that IMGPO still attains O(λN+Ngp) regret with λ < 1 under mild continuity assumptions (unknown semi-metric with Hölder-type growth).

Quality:
- Theory: The regret analysis is careful and novel. It cleanly separates the roles of the GP posterior (reducing exploration loss and contributing Ngp) and the partitioning (controlling interval refinement), and explicitly shows the dependence on dimension and Hölder parameters. The assumptions (existence of a global optimizer and unknown semi-metric with Hölder growth) are standard in optimistic optimization.
- Algorithmic clarity: The algorithm is well specified, with pseudocode and an instructive 1D figure. The role of steps (i)–(v) and the “GP-based” placeholders is explained.
- Empirics: The experimental section appears focused on low-dimensional benchmarks (this is not entirely clear; please clarify dimensionalities in the text and captions). Given that hyperrectangular partitioning is known to degrade with dimension (as in DIRECT), a discussion and/or experiments in moderate dimensions would strengthen the practical case. If higher-dimensional tests were performed (e.g., via REMBO), please make this explicit and summarize those results more prominently.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but a few passages would benefit from clarification:
- Section 3.1: “we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds.” Consider rephrasing to: “We hedge over an infinite family of possible upper bounds induced by unknown local regularity around the global optimum, and use both global exploration (across depths) and local refinement (within promising rectangles) without committing to a single bound.”
- Figure walkthrough (Section 3.2): At n=16, the far-right interval is divided but not evaluated. Please state explicitly: “By step (v), if the UCB at the new center is below the current best value f+, we skip the function evaluation and retain the UCB as a temporary placeholder (‘GP-based’) until the interval becomes competitive.”
- Section 2: You restrict to UCB “for brevity.” Please clarify whether the analysis or algorithm fundamentally requires UCB, or whether other acquisition functions (EI/PI) could be used in step (iii) and still preserve the main guarantees. If UCB is essential for the high-probability upper bound property used in the proof, say so.

Originality:
The main novelty is the infinite-metric viewpoint combined with GP posteriors to prune candidate bounds, yielding exponential regret without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization. This is a nontrivial advance over BaMSOO (polynomial rates) and over classical partition-based methods (DIRECT/SOO) that either only guarantee consistency or do not leverage GP information in this way.

Significance:
From a theoretical standpoint, showing exponential regret under realistic assumptions and without δ-cover is significant. Practically, avoiding an internal global optimization makes the method appealing, especially when function evaluations are not orders-of-magnitude more expensive than acquisition maximization. The dimension dependence, however, remains a concern: the rate constant worsens with D, and hyperrectangular partitioning typically scales poorly. Addressing or mitigating this (e.g., with random embeddings, additive structure, or learned low-dimensional subspaces) would increase impact.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled algorithm that eliminates auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Strong theoretical guarantees (exponential simple-regret) under mild, interpretable assumptions.
- Useful conceptual tools (infinite-metric exploration loss) that illuminate when GP bounds are helpful even if loose.
- Code availability improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Scalability: How does IMGPO perform in D ≥ 5–10? Can you provide experiments or a simulation study showing the dependence on D, and comparisons with BaMSOO/SOO/GP-UCB in moderate dimensions? If you already include higher-D results (e.g., via REMBO), please surface them prominently and discuss the cost-quality trade-offs.
- Sensitivity and tuning: Provide guidance on η and Ξmax. How robust is performance to these choices, and how should a practitioner set Ξmax relative to dimension and budget?
- Kernel/hyperparameters: The theory assumes deterministic observations; in practice, EB updates may be unstable early on. Any safeguards (priors, regularization) used? Briefly discuss the noisy case and whether your ideas extend (perhaps with sub-exponential rates).
- Acquisition choice: If UCB is essential for correctness of step (iii), state explicitly. If EI/PI could be substituted with calibrated confidence surrogates, that would be useful to note.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify dimensionalities and make the scope of experiments explicit in text and figure captions; add moderate-D tests if feasible or provide a careful discussion of scalability and remedies (e.g., REMBO, additive GPs).
- Add a brief complexity discussion: per-iteration cost in terms of GP updates, number of UCB evaluations, and how Ξ controls this.
- Improve the explanations in Sections 3.1–3.2 as noted above, including the rationale for skipping evaluations.
- Explicitly state where UCB is required in the analysis and whether alternatives are possible.

Minor comments:
- “For brevity we consider UCB”: clarify whether this is a methodological necessity.
- Section 3.1 sentence on “global and local searches”: rephrase as suggested for clarity.
- Section 3.2 n=16 event: add the explicit reason from step (v) as noted.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong and novel theoretical contribution (exponential regret without δ-cover and without acquisition maximization).
- Clear algorithmic design with code and practical insights on when GP bounds help.
- Advances the connection between GP-based BO and optimistic partitioning methods.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Unclear empirical scope and potential lack of moderate/high-dimensional evaluation.
- Practical scaling concerns with hyperrectangular partitioning not fully addressed.
- Some exposition areas need clarification (role of UCB, skipped evaluations, infinite-metric intuition).

Overall, this is a solid theoretical paper with a well-motivated algorithm. Clarifying the empirical scope and addressing scalability—either experimentally or through discussion and guidance—would materially strengthen the submission.
