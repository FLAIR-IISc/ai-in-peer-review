The paper presents a new algorithm for global optimization that avoids delta-cover sampling and achieves exponential regret, generalizing the work of de Freitas et al. [1] that relies on an impractical sampling procedure. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the role of the new algorithm within the current literature of bound-based search methods is well-explained. This work deserves attention from the community.

The authors propose a novel GP-based global optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which maintains an exponential convergence rate without the need for auxiliary optimization and delta-cover sampling. The algorithm leverages the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer and simultaneously conducts global and local searches based on all possible candidates of the bounds. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the algorithm, showing that it achieves an exponential convergence rate with a regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1.

The experimental section compares the performance of IMGPO with other algorithms, including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, on various synthetic functions. The results show that IMGPO outperforms the other algorithms in general. However, the experimental section has some limitations. The authors do not compare the methods for multiple initial evaluations of f, which is the standard in Bayesian optimization (BO) methods. Therefore, no meaningful statistical comparison of the methods can be obtained. Additionally, the authors do not compare their results with state-of-the-art BO approaches, such as information-theoretic approaches like Entropy Search. Finally, all the experiments are carried out on synthetic functions, with a maximum dimension of 6, and no real-world experiment is used to illustrate the performance of the method in real scenarios.

In conclusion, the paper presents a new global optimization algorithm that avoids delta-cover sampling and achieves exponential regret. The paper is well-written, with some interesting theoretical results. However, the experimental section is below the standards of a NIPS paper. I vote 5, but not a higher score, due to the limitations of the experimental section.
