

This paper presents Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization—a significant advancement over prior work. The authors address two key limitations in existing methods: de Freitas et al.'s [1] approach, while achieving exponential regret bounds, relies on impractical δ-cover sampling, and standard GP-UCB requires computationally expensive auxiliary optimization. Wang et al. [2] identified the open problem of resolving both limitations simultaneously, which IMGPO directly solves. The paper positions itself within the broader context of bound-based optimization (e.g., Lipschitz optimization, SOO [18]), unifying the "known-bound" approach (e.g., GP-UCB) with the "unknown-bound" strategy (e.g., DIRECT [19], SOO [18]) through a novel infinite-metric exploration framework.

**Strengths**  
*Originality & Significance (NIPS Criteria):* The core contribution—leveraging *all possible* semi-metric bounds via hierarchical partitioning—represents a substantial theoretical advance. This resolves Wang et al.'s [2] open problem and provides the first GP-based method with exponential regret bounds (Theorem 1: $r_N = O(\lambda^{N + N_{gp}})$, $\lambda < 1$) without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization. The analysis (Section 4) rigorously demonstrates *why* this approach outperforms prior work: by allowing the GP-estimated bound to dynamically reduce the search space over infinitely many candidate bounds (Remarks 1–3), IMGPO achieves tighter regret bounds than BaMSOO [2] ($\tilde{O}(N^{-2\alpha / D(4-\alpha)})$) and avoids the scalability pitfalls of relying solely on a single bound (Remark 3). The significance is further underscored by practical experiments (Section 5), where IMGPO consistently outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across 8 multimodal benchmarks (including high-dimensional Sin2 in 1000D), with the exception of Rosenbrock2 (where GP mismodeling caused suboptimal performance). Crucially, the authors validate their method under realistic assumptions (e.g., no prior knowledge of optimal kernels/hyperparameters), making the results broadly applicable.

*Quality & Clarity:* The theoretical foundation is exceptionally strong, with a complete regret bound proof (supplementary material) and precise characterization of the infinite-metric exploration loss ($\rho_t$). The algorithm description (Section 3.2–3.3) is exemplary: Figure 1 provides an intuitive 1D visualization of hierarchical partitioning, while Algorithm 1 offers executable pseudocode with clear notation (e.g., $g(\cdot)$ for GP-based values, $\Xi_{\text{max}}$ for computational limits). The connection to prior work is thorough (Section 3.4), contrasting IMGPO with BaMSOO [2] (polynomial regret) and DIRECT [19] (consistency only). The experimental setup is rigorous (50 runs for stochastic methods, standardized kernel/hyperparameters), and results are presented transparently (Figure 2, Table 1).

**Weaknesses**  
*Scalability in High Dimensions:* While Theorem 1 establishes exponential convergence, Remark 1 notes that $\lambda \to 1$ as dimension $D$ increases, implying diminishing returns for high-dimensional problems. The paper acknowledges this as a remaining challenge but does not propose a concrete solution beyond referencing [14, 20]. For NIPS, this is a notable gap, as scalability is a central concern in Bayesian optimization. The 1000D Sin2 experiment (Section 5) uses REMBO as a subroutine to mitigate this, but this does not fully resolve the core limitation.

*Implementation Nuance:* The algorithm's reliance on GP-based labels (e.g., "skip evaluation if UCB < $f^+$") adds complexity not fully justified in the main text. While Section 3.3 explains the logic, the practical burden of managing these labels (e.g., for large $N$) could affect reproducibility. A brief discussion of computational overhead (beyond Table 1's speed comparison) would strengthen the clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance**  
- **Pro:** Resolves a key open problem in Bayesian optimization (Wang et al. [2]) with a theoretically rigorous, experimentally validated method.  
- **Pro:** Provides a new framework for analyzing bound-based optimization (infinite-metric exploration), with implications beyond Bayesian methods (e.g., A* search, PAC-MDP).  
- **Pro:** Demonstrates clear practical advantages over state-of-the-art (e.g., 30–50% faster convergence on Sin1/Sin2 vs. BaMSOO).  
- **Pro:** Exceptional clarity in presentation (algorithms, figures, analysis) and adherence to NIPS standards.  

**Arguments Against Acceptance**  
- **Con:** High-dimensional scalability remains unresolved, though this is acknowledged as a future direction.  
- **Con:** Implementation details (GP label management) could be clarified for reproducibility.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to Bayesian optimization and global search. It addresses a fundamental limitation in the field with a novel, theoretically sound solution that outperforms existing methods. The clarity of exposition, strength of analysis, and practical validation align perfectly with NIPS' emphasis on high-quality scientific work. While scalability in high dimensions warrants further investigation, this does not diminish the paper's core achievement. The work is original, impactful, and clearly written—meeting all four NIPS criteria. I strongly recommend acceptance.
