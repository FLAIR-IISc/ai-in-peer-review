Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles deterministic global optimization of black-box, non-convex functions using Bayesian optimization. The main contribution is IMGPO, a GP-based algorithm that attains an exponential simple-regret rate without (i) auxiliary non-convex optimization of an acquisition function and (ii) the impractical δ-cover sampling required by de Freitas et al. (ICML’12). IMGPO blends hierarchical partitioning with “infinite-metric” optimism: instead of committing to a single smoothness bound, it considers a family of possible bounds and uses GP-derived UCBs only to prune candidates. The analysis introduces the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt to quantify the cost of considering multiple bounds and proves a regret bound of order O(λN+Ngp), λ < 1, where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp counts “virtual” GP-based evaluations. Empirically, the method compares favorably to SOO (Munos, NIPS’11), BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS’14), and standard GP-PI/EI, and a REMBO-style experiment suggests potential in high dimensions.

The work is well-situated within the literature: it leverages the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty paradigm (GP-UCB; Srinivas et al., ICML’10), hierarchical optimistic optimization (SOO/DOO; Munos), and the practical philosophy of DIRECT (Jones et al.). It advances the open problem raised by Wang et al. regarding achieving exponential rates without δ-cover.

Quality
- Theory: The paper provides a clear set of assumptions. Assumption 1 posits the existence of a global optimizer and an unknown semi-metric ` that upper-bounds suboptimality. Assumption 2 upper-bounds ` by L||x−x′||p^α. Under these, Theorem 1 yields an exponential regret bound that adapts to the “best unknown” L, α, p. The analysis nicely explains roles of Ngp, ρ̄t, and the lookahead parameter Ξn, and includes insightful remarks on when GP-based UCBs help or hurt.
- Algorithm: The pseudocode is explicit, and the hierarchical tri-partition scheme is standard and implementable. The use of GP-derived UCBs to avoid unnecessary function evaluations is a practical gain.
- Experiments: The benchmarks cover 1–6D standard functions, a nontrivial custom function, and a 1000D REMBO scenario. IMGPO generally outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, PI/EI and is faster than classic GP BO with auxiliary optimization.

Concerns and suggestions
- Assumptions and scope: The guarantee requires a deterministic setting and a semi-metric that is Hölder-dominated (Assumption 2). Please make the function class explicit early (e.g., “Hölder-smooth around x* with parameters (L, α, p)”) and discuss how this relates to near-optimality dimension used in SOO/DOO analyses. Clarify whether the “best unknown” L, α, p means the bound holds uniformly over all triples satisfying Assumptions 1–2 (i.e., for the function class).
- Confidence bounds under hyperparameter learning: The claim f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with probability ≥ 1−η is used while recalibrating GP hyperparameters after each iteration via empirical Bayes. This can invalidate standard high-probability UCB guarantees unless model-selection effects are controlled. Please clarify whether the probability statement holds under data-dependent hyperparameters (e.g., via conditioning arguments or by citing results guaranteeing robustness to modest kernel mismatch).
- Computational complexity: Step (iii) appears to compute UCBs for descendants up to depth ξ. The text says “3Ξmax times,” which may intend 3^Ξmax. Please correct this and provide a clear per-iteration and total complexity bound in terms of N, D, and Ξmax. Also, line 38 sets Ξ ← Ξ + 22; confirm whether this is literally +22 or +2^2.
- Dimensional dependence: The bound’s λ worsens with D via factors like CD and base 3 partitioning, and the authors note scalability as an open issue. It would be useful to quantify empirically how performance degrades with D and to discuss practical remedies (e.g., additive kernels (Kandasamy et al.), random embeddings (REMBO), ARD).
- Behavior of ρ̄t: The bound depends critically on ρ̄t. While the remarks are insightful, providing empirical traces of ρt across runs and functions would help the reader see when GP pruning significantly reduces exploration loss.
- Experimental setup fairness: All GP methods use an isotropic Matérn 5/2 with blind initialization. While this avoids hand-tuning, PI/EI and BaMSOO are known to benefit from ARD and better kernel selection. Please include a sensitivity analysis (e.g., ARD Matérn, SE, or a small kernel set with model selection) and report whether relative rankings persist. Also report wall-clock budgets and the ratio of true evaluations to GP-based skips, since IMGPO’s advantage includes fewer expensive evaluations.
- Noisy objectives: The method and bound target deterministic functions. Please discuss how IMGPO would extend to noisy settings (e.g., modified UCB, replication, or noise-aware GP posterior), even if theory is deferred.
- Minor clarity issues: Notation and indexing are dense in Algorithm 1. A small schematic or glossary (Th, T′h+ξ(·), Ngp, Ξ, ρt) would help. Also confirm whether Ω must be a hyperrectangle; if not, summarize the general-case division.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and didactic. Figures are helpful. A few editorial nits:
- Abstract: some redundancy; consider tightening the first two sentences.
- Line 49: stray phrase “the function”.
- Line 72: misplaced parenthesis.
- Section 3.3: “3Ξmax” likely means 3^Ξmax; “Ξ ← Ξ + 22” is unclear; “resolve the the” (double “the”).
- Algorithm 1 formatting (line numbers and set operations) could be streamlined.

Originality
The combination of infinite-metric optimism with GP pruning is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel contribution. The notion of infinite-metric exploration loss provides a useful handle for analysis and distinguishes this from BaMSOO’s polynomial-rate guarantees.

Significance
Achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling and without auxiliary acquisition optimization is both theoretically notable and practically attractive. The empirical results are strong and the code release enhances impact. The dimensionality caveat tempers the scope but does not diminish the contribution.

Pros
- Clear motivation; removes two major practical limitations (δ-cover and auxiliary optimization).
- Novel analysis (ρt, Ngp) yielding exponential regret under reasonable assumptions.
- Practical algorithm that reduces real evaluations via GP-based screening.
- Strong empirical performance; code available.

Cons
- Deterministic-only analysis; unclear behavior under noise.
- Dimensional dependence in theory and practice; limited high-D evidence beyond REMBO.
- Confidence bounds with data-dependent hyperparameters need justification.
- Some algorithmic details and complexity claims need clarification; minor notation/typos.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper advances the state of the art in GP-based global optimization with a compelling blend of theory and practice. Addressing the noted clarifications and adding modest experimental sensitivity analyses would further strengthen an already strong submission.
