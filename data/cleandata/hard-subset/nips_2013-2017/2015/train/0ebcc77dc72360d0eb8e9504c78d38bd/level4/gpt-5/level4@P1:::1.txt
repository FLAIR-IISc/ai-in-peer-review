Summary
This paper proposes a Gaussian process (GP)-based global optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves an exponential simple regret rate without relying on δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization of an acquisition function. Building on the bound-based search paradigm (e.g., Lipschitz optimization, SOO, DIRECT), IMGPO departs from GP-UCB’s reliance on a single admissible bound. Instead, it leverages the existence of an unknown but tighter semi-metric bound around the global optimum and explores across an infinite family of potential bounds, while using the GP posterior’s UCB to prune candidates. The algorithm employs hierarchical partitioning, avoids auxiliary nonconvex optimization per iteration, and provides a regret bound of the form O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, where N is the number of true evaluations and Ngp is the number of “skipped” evaluations replaced by GP-UCB values. The analysis also explains how the GP helps by reducing the infinite-metric exploration loss and how the approach relates to SOO, BaMSOO, and DIRECT. Code is publicly available.

Relation to prior work
The work generalizes the exponential-regret result of de Freitas et al. (2012) for deterministic GP bandits by eliminating the δ-cover sampling assumption, which is generally impractical. Relative to BaMSOO (which combines SOO with GP-UCB and achieves only polynomial rates), IMGPO attains an exponential rate by using GP information to reduce uncertainty over the unknown semi-metric. It also connects to DIRECT and SOO by adopting hierarchical partitioning and optimism, while introducing a principled way to benefit from GP bounds without committing to a potentially loose single bound.

Quality
- Theory: The paper presents a clear theoretical framework, including Assumption 1 (existence of an unknown semi-metric bound around x*) and Assumption 2 (a Hölder-type condition on that semi-metric). The main theorem gives an exponential regret bound that holds with high probability and clarifies the roles of dimension D, α, p, and constants. The remarks make the analysis intuitive, explaining how GP-derived UCBs affect Ngp and the exploration loss, and when infinite-metric exploration is preferable to committing to a single estimated bound. One limitation is the dependence on D (λ approaches 1 as D increases), which suggests scalability challenges absent further structure; the paper notes possible remedies via methods like REMBO.
- Algorithm: The hierarchical partitioning scheme and the skip-evaluation mechanism are well described. Using GP-UCB to avoid unnecessary evaluations is attractive in practice. The computational cost of step (iii) is controlled via Ξmax, but there is limited empirical discussion of the trade-off between Ξmax, runtime, and regret in challenging settings.
- Assumptions: The unknown semi-metric assumption is reasonable and aligns with near-optimality analyses, but it would help to discuss its scope in real applications and sensitivity to kernel misspecification.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The bound-based perspective is articulated clearly, and the relationship to prior algorithms (SOO, BaMSOO, DIRECT) is informative. A few suggestions:
- Unify terminology (“bound-based,” not “bounded-based”) and ensure consistent notation for regret and counts (N, Ngp, t).
- Provide a concise summary of constants (C, β, ρ̄t, Ξn) near the main theorem for quick reference.
- Clarify the practical frequency and cost of recomputing UCBs in step (iii), and report wall-clock time alongside evaluations.

Originality
IMGPO’s combination of (i) infinite-metric exploration, (ii) GP-based pruning without auxiliary acquisition optimization, and (iii) an exponential regret bound without δ-cover is novel relative to GP-UCB, BaMSOO, SOO, and DIRECT. The analysis yielding O(λN+Ngp) and the interpretive remarks are original and insightful.

Significance
If empirically validated, the ability to achieve exponential simple regret without δ-cover or inner-loop optimization would be impactful for deterministic black-box optimization. However, the current experimental evaluation limits the strength of the empirical case.

Main weaknesses in the experiments
- No multiple independent initializations: BO papers typically report means and variability across multiple random initial designs or restarts. Here, several methods are run deterministically or with a single initialization, precluding meaningful statistical comparisons.
- Missing comparisons to strong BO baselines: Information-theoretic methods such as Entropy Search (ES) and Predictive Entropy Search (PES), as well as Thompson sampling, are not included. Given the claim of stronger convergence, these comparisons would be informative.
- Scope and realism: Experiments are almost entirely on synthetic, low-dimensional functions (≤6D), with one 1000D example only via REMBO. There are no real-world hyperparameter tuning or wet-lab/model calibration tasks that would demonstrate practical benefits.
- Reporting: Results focus on simple regret curves. Please add wall-clock time, number of GP hyperparameter updates, and ablations showing sensitivity to η, Ξmax, kernel choice, and hyperparameter learning method. Clarify whether GP-UCB or EI/PI baselines used auxiliary optimizers comparable in strength, and include their runtime.

Pros
- Clear and principled theoretical contribution with exponential regret without δ-cover.
- Eliminates auxiliary acquisition optimization, simplifying implementation.
- Insightful analysis on when not committing to a single bound is advantageous.
- Well-positioned within the literature; connections to SOO, BaMSOO, and DIRECT are clear.
- Open-source code.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is below BO standards: limited baselines, no multiple seeds, synthetic-only benchmarks, minimal runtime analysis.
- Scalability to higher dimensions remains uncertain; dependence on D in the bound is acknowledged but not thoroughly explored empirically.
- Limited discussion of sensitivity to kernel misspecification and hyperparameter learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add multiple-seed evaluations with confidence intervals and statistical tests.
- Include ES, PES, Thompson sampling, and a strong GP-UCB baseline; report both evaluation and wall-clock budgets.
- Add real-world tasks (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for ML models or a wet-lab calibration) to demonstrate practical relevance.
- Provide ablations on Ξmax and η, and a breakdown of time spent in step (iii) and GP fitting. Assess sensitivity to kernel choice and hyperparameter learning.
- Explore higher-dimensional cases beyond REMBO, or demonstrate improved performance under additive or low-dimensional structure assumptions.

Score: 5 (borderline). The paper makes an interesting and well-argued theoretical contribution with a clean algorithmic design, but the experimental section is not yet up to the standard expected for NIPS BO papers. Strengthening the empirical evaluation as suggested would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
