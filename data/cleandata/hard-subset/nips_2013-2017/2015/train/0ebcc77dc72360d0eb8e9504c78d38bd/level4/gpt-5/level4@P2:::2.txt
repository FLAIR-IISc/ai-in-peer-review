Summary
The paper proposes a Gaussian process–based Bayesian optimization algorithm that replaces the usual auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function with a hierarchical space-partitioning scheme. Using an upper confidence bound (UCB) and a novel “infinite-metric” perspective, the method screens and subdivides hyperrectangles to select future evaluations, thereby avoiding δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization. The authors prove an exponential simple-regret rate O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 under an assumption that there exists an unknown semi-metric capturing local smoothness near the optimum. The approach relates closely to DIRECT (bound-based partitioning), SOO (smoothness-agnostic optimistic optimization), BaMSOO (SOO + GP), and GP-UCB. Compared to BaMSOO’s polynomial regret, the paper claims an exponential rate by leveraging GP information to reduce the “infinite-metric exploration loss.”

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: combining GP posterior information with an infinite family of potential smoothness bounds within a hierarchical partitioning framework.
- Theoretical contribution: an exponential convergence guarantee without δ-cover sampling or acquisition-function maximization; clear discussion of how GP affects exploration loss and regret.
- Practical angle: avoids solving a difficult nonconvex inner optimization problem; the algorithm is well described with a helpful 1D illustration and pseudocode.
- Clarity of the main ideas: the motivation and connections to DIRECT/SOO/BaMSOO/GP-UCB are well framed.

Reservations and weaknesses
- Empirical scope and scalability: The experiments appear to focus on 1D test functions (this is not explicitly stated). A hyperrectangle partitioning scheme typically incurs exponential growth in cells with dimension, a known limitation in DIRECT-like methods. Without evidence or discussion on higher-dimensional behavior, the practical significance is unclear for typical Bayesian optimization use cases (hyperparameter tuning, engineering design).
- Computational aspects: The paper would benefit from a clearer accounting of per-iteration costs (number of UCB evaluations, effect of Ξ and Ξmax, and the cost of frequent GP hyperparameter updates). While the method avoids inner optimization, the screening and subdivision steps may still be heavy in higher dimensions.
- Assumption and interpretation: The “unknown semi-metric” assumption is reasonable but abstract. More guidance on when it holds and how it relates to common kernels or smoothness classes would help readers assess applicability.

Quality
- The theoretical analysis appears careful and improves on prior regret bounds. However, the empirical validation does not convincingly address performance beyond low dimensions, where hierarchical partitioning can struggle.

Clarity
- Overall well-written, with a clear algorithmic description and figures. Some statements (noted below) would benefit from clarification.

Originality
- The synthesis of GP information with an infinite-metric optimistic partitioning strategy is new relative to SOO/BaMSOO and DIRECT.

Significance
- Potentially significant if the approach scales or can be paired with dimensionality-reduction techniques. As presented, its practical impact may be constrained by dimensionality.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical advance (exponential regret without δ-cover and without inner optimization).
- Clear algorithm and analysis; good positioning relative to prior work.
- Potentially useful design pattern for blending GP bounds with partition-based optimistic search.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evidence; unclear scalability beyond low dimensions.
- Insufficient discussion of computational cost and sensitivity to parameters (e.g., Ξmax) in higher dimensions.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify the dimensionality of the test problems and, if possible, include higher-dimensional benchmarks or discuss pairing with dimensionality reduction (e.g., random embeddings) in more depth.
- Provide a complexity analysis per iteration (number of UCB calls, effect of Ξ/Ξmax, and GP hyperparameter updates).
- Elaborate on when the unknown semi-metric assumption is expected to hold and how it relates to common kernel smoothness assumptions.
- Could the method work with acquisition functions other than UCB (e.g., EI/PI)? If not, clarify what aspect of the analysis requires UCB.

Minor comments
- p.2: “UCB is considered ‘for brevity.’” Please clarify whether the framework extends to EI/PI or relies on UCB’s concentration properties.
- p.3: “we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds.” This is hard to parse—consider rephrasing to explain concretely how the infinite set of bounds is operationalized via the partition-selection rules and how GP screening prunes candidates.
- p.4: “At n=16, the far right… but no function evaluation occurs.” Please add a brief explanation that the UCB value is used as a temporary placeholder to defer expensive evaluations until the interval passes the acceptance test in step (ii)/(iii).

Overall assessment
A novel and well-analyzed contribution that removes two practical barriers to GP-based BO and establishes exponential regret. However, I am concerned about empirical scope and scalability. I encourage the authors to expand the experimental evaluation or, at minimum, provide a thorough discussion of high-dimensional behavior and computational costs.
