- Proposes a Bayesian optimization algorithm using hierarchical hyperrectangle partitioning guided by a GP-UCB, avoiding auxiliary acquisition optimization and δ-cover sampling.
- Theoretical contribution: proves exponential simple-regret bounds, improving over prior GP-based methods that required δ-covers or had polynomial rates.
- Conceptually relates to DIRECT, SOO, and BaMSOO, bridging fixed smoothness/Lipschitz approaches and GP-based methods.
- Novelty: integrates GP-based optimism with partition-based search and an infinite-metric analysis to obtain exponential rates.
- Strength: removes costly/brittle inner global optimization for acquisition maximization.
- Strength: clear, implementable algorithm description with illustrative 1D example.
- Strength: meaningful theoretical advance (exponential regret) addressing an open issue.
- Main reservation: experiments appear restricted to 1D; partitioning schemes typically scale poorly with dimension.
- Practical impact unclear without discussion or experiments on high-dimensional scaling and computational costs.
- Quality: theory appears sound; algorithmic components are standard but combined principledy.
- Empirical evaluation is too limited to support practical claims about scalability.
- Clarity: well organized and clear, but some statements need clarification (dimensionality and complexity).
- Significance: high if exponential regret yields empirical gains beyond low dimensions; uncertain otherwise.
- Pros for acceptance: strong theory, clear algorithm/analysis, useful practical motivation.
- Cons for acceptance: limited experiments (likely 1D) and lack of discussion on high-dimensional behavior and costs.
- Question: Can the method use other acquisition functions (EI/PI) while preserving guarantees, or is UCB essential?
- Question: Clarify how the algorithm operationalizes “all candidates” of the semi-metric bound and how GP pruning modifies this set.
- Question: Explain why no function evaluation occurs at n=16 in the 1D example (mechanism for skipping evaluations).
- Request: State dimensionality of all benchmarks and, if possible, include >1D results or a detailed scaling discussion relating depth/branching, regret constants, and wall-clock cost.
- Minor comment: Explicitly state whether EI/PI substitutions affect analysis.
- Minor comment: Reword/clarify “simultaneously” conducting global and local searches with “all candidates of the bounds.”
- Minor comment: Add a sentence explaining the skipped evaluation in the 1D example.
- Overall: technically strong and clearly presented with a novel theoretical result; main weakness is limited empirical validation and missing discussion/experiments on scalability.
