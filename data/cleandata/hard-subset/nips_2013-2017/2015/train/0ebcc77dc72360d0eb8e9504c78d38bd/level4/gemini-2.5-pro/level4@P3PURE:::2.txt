This paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian Global Optimization that utilizes a space-partitioning algorithm to select subsequent points for evaluation. By leveraging the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) criterion, this method cleverly circumvents the common need to solve an internal, and often difficult, global optimization problem to choose the next sample. The theoretical analysis is a key strength, demonstrating that the proposed algorithm achieves exponential regret, which is a significant improvement over previous work. The algorithm is described clearly and is well-illustrated with a helpful example. Overall, the paper makes a novel contribution and is presented with clarity.

However, I have one major reservation regarding the experimental evaluation. The results appear to be exclusively for one-dimensional test functions, although this is not explicitly stated. This raises concerns about the scalability of the interval partitioning approach to higher-dimensional problems. I suspect that as the dimensionality increases, the number of hyperrectangles required to maintain a certain resolution would grow exponentially, severely limiting the method's applicability. This approach is reminiscent of the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to perform poorly as the dimensionality of the search space increases.

The authors should address this potential limitation in the paper. While this concern does not invalidate the theoretical contribution, it is highly significant for assessing the practical utility of the proposed method.

Minor Comments:
*   On page 2, the use of UCB is justified "for brevity." Could other acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement, be used instead? My impression was that the algorithm's design might be fundamentally linked to UCB. Please clarify this point.
*   The statement on page 3, "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," was unclear. Please consider rephrasing this for better understanding.
*   On page 4, it is mentioned that "At n=16, the far right...but no function evaluation occurs." It would improve clarity to briefly explain why no evaluation is performed at this particular step.
