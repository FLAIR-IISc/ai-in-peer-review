This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which employs a hierarchical partitioning of the search space to select subsequent evaluation points. By combining this partitioning strategy with a Gaussian Process Upper Confidence Bound (GP-UCB) criterion, the method cleverly avoids the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization step, a common and often costly component of standard GP-based optimization. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that IMGPO achieves an exponential convergence rate without relying on impractical assumptions like access to a δ-cover sampling procedure. This work builds upon and improves the theoretical guarantees of related algorithms like BaMSOO, which only established a polynomial regret bound.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement. To my knowledge, this is the first GP-based optimization method that achieves an exponential regret bound without requiring either an auxiliary optimization routine or δ-cover sampling. This resolves an open problem in the field and provides a new, practical algorithm with strong theoretical backing.
*   **Clarity:** The algorithm is described clearly, and the inclusion of a detailed 1D example with illustrations (Figure 1) is very effective at building intuition for the procedure. The relationship to previous work (SOO, BaMSOO, DIRECT) is also well-articulated.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and rigorously supports the central claim of an exponential convergence rate. The analysis in Remarks 1-3, which discusses the trade-offs between relying on the GP bound versus the "infinite-metric" approach, provides valuable insight into why the proposed method is effective.

**Weaknesses**

My primary reservation concerns the practical scalability of the proposed method with respect to dimensionality. The core of the algorithm is a hierarchical partitioning of the search space into hyperrectangles. This approach is conceptually related to well-known algorithms like DIRECT, which are known to struggle as the dimensionality of the problem increases due to the curse of dimensionality—the number of partitions required to cover the space grows exponentially.

While the paper presents results on problems with dimensions greater than one (e.g., Hartmann6, Rosenbrock10, and even a 1000D problem via REMBO), the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more direct discussion of this potential limitation. The theoretical analysis hints at this issue (noting that λ can approach 1 as D increases), but a more explicit discussion of the practical implications would be valuable for potential users of the algorithm. For instance, how does the performance degrade as dimension increases, and at what point might standard GP-UCB with an auxiliary optimizer become more efficient despite its higher per-iteration cost? Addressing this scalability question is crucial for understanding the algorithm's practical applicability.

**Minor Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   On page 2, the paper states that UCB is used "for brevity." Does this imply that the theoretical framework could be extended to other popular acquisition functions, such as Expected Improvement or Probability of Improvement? Or is the analysis fundamentally tied to the specific properties of the UCB criterion?
*   The statement on page 3, "...we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is a key conceptual point but was difficult for me to fully understand. A rephrasing or further explanation of this idea could improve clarity for the reader.
*   In the explanation of the example on page 4, it is noted that for t=9, n=16, "the far right interval is divided, but no function evaluation occurs." For clarity, it would be helpful to explicitly state the reason for this, presumably by referencing step (v) of the algorithm (i.e., the UCB was below the best value found so far).
