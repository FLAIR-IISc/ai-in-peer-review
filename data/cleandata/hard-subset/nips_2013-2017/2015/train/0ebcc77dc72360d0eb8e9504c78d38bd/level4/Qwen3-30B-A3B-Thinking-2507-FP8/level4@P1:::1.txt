

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which achieves exponential convergence without requiring delta-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization—a significant advancement over previous approaches that relied on these impractical components. The authors build upon the theoretical framework of de Freitas et al. (2012), who established exponential regret bounds but required delta-cover sampling, which Wang et al. (2013) identified as computationally prohibitive. The paper makes a compelling case for their approach by demonstrating how leveraging multiple potential bounds (rather than relying on a single GP-derived bound) enables exponential convergence while maintaining practicality.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with an excellent contextualization of the proposed method within the broader literature of bound-based search methods. The authors effectively distinguish their work from related approaches like SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-UCB, particularly highlighting how IMGPO's theoretical framework provides a more flexible approach to handling unknown bounds. The technical exposition is thorough, with careful definitions and a solid theoretical foundation that supports their claims. The supplementary material provides rigorous proofs of the exponential convergence rate, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contribution and clear presentation. The authors successfully address an important open problem in Bayesian optimization—achieving exponential convergence without impractical sampling procedures—while providing valuable insights into why their approach outperforms previous methods. The analysis in Remarks 1-3 offers particularly insightful theoretical perspectives on the interplay between the GP-derived bound and the unknown semi-metric, which will be valuable to researchers working on optimization algorithms.

However, the experimental section falls below the standards expected for a NIPS publication. While the authors present results on several synthetic benchmark functions, their experimental design lacks several key elements required for a comprehensive evaluation in Bayesian optimization:

First, the comparison with baseline methods does not include multiple initial evaluations of the objective function, which is standard practice in Bayesian optimization literature. Without this, the results cannot be properly evaluated for statistical significance, and the reported performance differences may not reflect the algorithm's true behavior under realistic conditions.

Second, the experimental comparison omits several state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly information-theoretic methods like Entropy Search (Hennig & Schuler, 2012) and Predictive Entropy Search (Shahriari et al., 2014), which represent the current frontier in Bayesian optimization research. The absence of these comparisons makes it difficult to assess IMGPO's relative position in the field.

Third, the paper relies exclusively on synthetic test functions with maximum dimensionality of 6, without demonstrating performance on real-world applications or high-dimensional problems. While the paper does mention a 1000-dimensional test function using REMBO as a subroutine, this is insufficient to establish the algorithm's practical utility. A single synthetic function is not enough to convince the community that the method has real-world applicability.

The authors should also clarify the computational overhead of their approach. While they note that IMGPO is faster than traditional GP optimization methods, they do not provide sufficient detail about the trade-off between convergence rate and computational cost, particularly for higher-dimensional problems where the exponential convergence rate might be less advantageous.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel approach that addresses a significant limitation in the literature. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-presented, and the authors provide valuable insights into the relationship between bound-based search and Bayesian optimization. However, the experimental evaluation fails to meet the standards expected for a NIPS publication, lacking statistical validation, comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and real-world applications.

Pro: Theoretical contribution is significant, addressing an important open problem in Bayesian optimization; clear and well-written presentation; valuable insights into the relationship between bound-based search and Bayesian optimization.

Con: Experimental section lacks statistical validation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods; limited to synthetic functions with low dimensionality; no real-world application examples.

Given the strong theoretical contribution but weak experimental validation, I recommend a borderline acceptance. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more comprehensive experimental evaluation that includes statistical comparisons, comparisons with contemporary Bayesian optimization methods, and at least one real-world application.
