

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing an open problem identified by Wang et al. [2]. The authors build upon recent work by de Freitas et al. [1] who proposed a GP-based method with exponential convergence but relied on impractical δ-cover sampling. While Bayesian optimization with GP-UCB has become standard practice in the field, the need for auxiliary optimization remains a practical limitation. The paper's key insight is to leverage the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer rather than relying solely on the GP's upper bound, which can be loose in practice.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical rigor with a clear analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential regret bound O(λ^{N+Ngp}) with λ < 1 under reasonable assumptions. The authors provide a detailed proof of their regret bound in the supplementary material, showing how their approach improves upon the polynomial regret bound of BaMSOO [2] and other related work. The analysis is particularly compelling in Remarks 1-3, where they theoretically demonstrate the benefits of not committing to a single upper bound.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing IMGPO against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight benchmark functions with varying dimensions (1-1000). The results consistently show IMGPO outperforming other methods, with particularly strong performance on multimodal functions. The experimental setup is well-justified, using a single kernel (isotropic Matern with ν=5/2) without handpicked hyperparameters, which better reflects practical scenarios.

The paper excels in clarity with well-structured exposition, helpful figures (especially Figure 1), and a well-written pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their approach, particularly the tradeoff between the GP's upper bound and the unknown bound from continuity assumptions. The connection to previous work (DIRECT, SOO, GP-UCB) is well-established, and the paper properly situates its contribution within the broader Bayesian optimization literature.

The main strength of this paper is its theoretical contribution: resolving the open problem of achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization. The practical significance is high, as these are known limitations in existing Bayesian optimization methods. The authors' theoretical framework for understanding when a loose bound can still be useful (Remarks 1-3) provides valuable insights for the broader optimization community.

The paper has minimal weaknesses. The most minor concern is that the experimental section could have included a more detailed analysis of how Ξmax affects performance across different functions, though this is addressed in the supplementary material. The theoretical analysis assumes Ω is a hyperrectangle, which is a reasonable simplification but could be noted as a limitation for more general domains.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a core area of machine learning with clear practical implications. It addresses the four key criteria effectively:
- Quality: Theorems are well-supported with proofs in supplementary material, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations, helpful figures, and logical flow.
- Originality: The approach of considering infinitely many possible bounds is novel and resolves a known limitation in the field.
- Significance: The exponential convergence rate without impractical requirements represents a meaningful advance over existing methods.

The authors have made an important contribution to Bayesian optimization by demonstrating how to combine the strengths of bound-based search methods with GP priors. Their analysis framework provides deeper theoretical insights that could potentially inform other optimization approaches beyond Bayesian optimization.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Resolves a known open problem in Bayesian optimization (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling)
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported
- Experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written
- Theoretical framework provides valuable insights beyond the specific algorithm

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Minor limitation in assuming Ω is a hyperrectangle (though this is standard in many optimization papers)
- The practical implementation details of Ξmax selection could be more thoroughly discussed

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with clear theoretical and practical advantages. The authors have successfully resolved an important open problem while providing deeper theoretical insights into why their approach works. The paper is well-written, well-structured, and makes a strong case for its contributions. I recommend acceptance.
