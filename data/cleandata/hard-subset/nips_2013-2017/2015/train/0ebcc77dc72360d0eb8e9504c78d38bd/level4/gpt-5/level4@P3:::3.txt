Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Infinite-Metric Gaussian Process Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions that achieves an exponential convergence rate without two common practical burdens: (i) auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function (as in GP-UCB/EI/PI), and (ii) δ-cover sampling (required by the theoretical procedure in de Freitas et al. [1]). IMGPO combines a hierarchical partitioning scheme (in the spirit of SOO/DOO [18]) with GP-based upper confidence bounds used only for pruning and deferring evaluations rather than for posing a secondary optimization problem. A key conceptual idea is to avoid committing to a single regularity bound; instead, the algorithm is “optimistic” over an infinite family of potential semi-metric bounds around a global optimizer (Assumption 1), while using GP UCBs to reduce the candidate set. The analysis introduces the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt to quantify the cost of not committing to a specific bound.

Relative to prior work: (a) GP-UCB [15] provides sublinear (polynomial/log) regret via acquisition optimization; (b) de Freitas et al. [1] established exponential regret for deterministic observations but relied on δ-cover sampling deemed impractical; (c) BaMSOO [2] integrated GP-UCB with SOO to avoid δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization, but achieved only polynomial regret; (d) SOO/DOO [18] provide optimistic partition-based global optimization without requiring known smoothness but do not use GP priors. IMGPO bridges these lines by using GPs to prune while preserving a partition-based search that does not require acquisition optimization, and the paper proves an exponential-rate simple regret bound O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 under Hölder-type regularity (Assumptions 1–2).

Quality (technical soundness):
- Theory: The main theorem states an exponential simple regret bound that depends on N (true function evaluations), Ngp (deferred GP “virtual” evaluations), the dimension D, and the average infinite-metric exploration loss ρ̄t, along with Hölder constants (L, α, p). The role of GP is made explicit: it can both increase Ngp and reduce ρ̄t, tightening the bound; the analysis discusses when GP UCBs help and how their tightness matters. The scaling with D is transparent: λ approaches 1 as D grows, highlighting remaining challenges in high dimensions.
- Algorithm: The algorithm is clearly specified (Section 3.2–3.3), uses a deterministic division rule, and avoids auxiliary optimization. The use of UCB to skip evaluations (placing GP-based placeholders that are resolved later) is elegant and computationally efficient.
- Caveats: The theoretical guarantee assumes deterministic observations and requires confidence bounds of the form f(x) ≤ U(x|D) holding with high probability; however, hyperparameters are re-estimated online via empirical Bayes, which can invalidate fixed-confidence guarantees unless controlled (this is a general issue in GP-UCB-style analyses). The proof details are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and well organized. Section 3.2’s example and the pseudocode make the procedure understandable and reproducible. A few minor presentation issues remain (notation for n, N, t; “the forth” → “the fourth”; duplicate words in 3.3), and the computational cost of step (iii) and the choice/impact of Ξmax could be explained more succinctly in the main text.

Originality:
The “infinite-metric” perspective—optimizing over an implicit family of semi-metric bounds while using GP UCBs only to prune and defer—is a novel and interesting synthesis of optimistic partition methods (SOO/DOO/DIRECT) and GP-based BO. The introduction of the infinite-metric exploration loss is a useful analytical device that clarifies the benefit of not committing to a single (possibly loose) bound.

Significance:
Avoiding auxiliary acquisition optimization while retaining exponential convergence is an appealing advance both theoretically and practically. The approach may influence future design of BO methods that separate search control (via partitions) from statistical modeling (via GP-based pruning). The empirical results on standard benchmarks and on high-dimensional REMBO demonstrate competitive performance and runtime advantages over GP-EI/PI and BaMSOO in many cases.

Strengths:
- Exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling and without auxiliary optimization.
- Clear algorithmic description with reproducible pseudocode; code availability noted.
- Insightful analysis introducing ρt and discussing when GP UCBs help, and why not committing to a single bound can be beneficial.
- Strong empirical performance on multiple benchmarks; reasonable runtime characteristics.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Assumptions and scope: Guarantees are for deterministic objectives under Hölder continuity around a global optimum; no noisy/stochastic extension is analyzed.
- Confidence bounds vs. hyperparameter learning: Empirical Bayes updates can compromise the stated high-probability UCB property; this should be discussed and, if possible, mitigated.
- Dimensionality: The regret constant degrades with D; only a single high-dimensional case is shown via REMBO. More evidence on higher-D problems or an explicit discussion of practical limits would help.
- Baselines and fairness: No direct GP-UCB baseline is shown; the acquisition optimization for EI/PI is partly delegated to SOO/gradient-based local search, which may disadvantage them relative to common multistart global optimizers. DIRECT (conceptually related) is not included.
- Ablations: It would be useful to separate the effects of GP pruning vs. infinite-metric search (e.g., no-GP ablation), and to show sensitivity to kernel choice and Ξmax beyond brief remarks.
- Minor clarity/typos and notation consistency; a brief complexity analysis of step (iii) in the main text would be helpful.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Please discuss how empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates interact with the confidence parameter η and whether any safeguards (priors, freezing hyperparameters after a burn-in) can preserve guarantees.
- Can you provide a no-GP ablation and a GP-only bound-committing variant to empirically validate Remarks 2–3?
- Include a GP-UCB baseline and, if possible, DIRECT; ensure EI/PI acquisition optimization is competitive (e.g., multistart global search).
- Expand on computational complexity per iteration and the practical role/schedule of Ξmax; report sensitivity curves for Ξmax on functions where it binds.
- Clarify notation (N vs. n vs. t) and correct minor typos.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and principled algorithmic contribution that removes two practical bottlenecks.
- Solid and informative theoretical analysis with clear insights.
- Strong empirical results with practical runtime benefits.
- Clear writing and implementation details; code available.

Cons for acceptance:
- Deterministic-only analysis; unclear robustness to noise and to hyperparameter learning in practice.
- Limited high-dimensional evidence; some baselines and ablations missing.
- Dependence on D in constants may limit practical exponential gains.

Recommendation:
Leaning toward accept. The paper offers a compelling and well-motivated advance in Bayesian optimization, with clear theoretical and practical contributions. Addressing the noted experimental and clarity concerns would further strengthen the submission.
