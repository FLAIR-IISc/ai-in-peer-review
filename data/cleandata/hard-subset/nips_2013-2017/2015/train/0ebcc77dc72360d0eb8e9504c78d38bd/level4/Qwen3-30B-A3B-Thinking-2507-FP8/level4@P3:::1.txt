

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing a key limitation in previous work by de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The authors propose a bound-based search method that leverages the existence of an unknown semi-metric bound while simultaneously considering infinitely many candidate bounds, rather than relying on a single upper bound provided by Gaussian process (GP) priors. This approach generalizes and improves upon the theoretical results of BaMSOO [2] by achieving an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, whereas BaMSOO only achieves a polynomial regret bound.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions. The authors establish a rigorous regret bound (Theorem 1) that reveals how the tightness of the GP-based bound affects performance, providing valuable theoretical insights into when and why their approach outperforms existing methods. The analysis (Remark 1-3) carefully dissects the trade-offs between using GP information, considering multiple bounds, and the cost of infinite-metric exploration. This theoretical framework is particularly significant as it provides a principled understanding of why not committing to a single bound can be beneficial, a perspective that extends beyond Bayesian optimization to other bound-based search methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The relationship to prior work is well-explained, particularly in Section 3.4 where the authors carefully distinguish their approach from related algorithms like BaMSOO, SOO, and DIRECT. The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the literature, properly citing relevant work including Munos' SOO [18] and Jones' DIRECT [19].

Regarding quality, the theoretical results are sound and well-supported. The regret bound is derived with appropriate mathematical rigor, and the supplementary material provides additional proof details. The paper carefully addresses the strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly in Remark 3, where they analyze the conditions under which their method outperforms standard bound-based approaches. The authors are appropriately honest about the limitations of their approach (e.g., scalability challenges in higher dimensions as noted in Theorem 1).

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The algorithm description (Section 3.2) is well-explained with a helpful figure (Figure 1), and the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is presented in a clear, structured manner. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the algorithm, though the experimental setup could be more detailed.

The originality of the work is significant. While bound-based search methods have been extensively studied (e.g., Lipschitz optimization, A*, SOO), the authors' key insight—simultaneously considering infinitely many candidate bounds while using GP to reduce the search space—represents a novel approach to the problem of balancing global and local search. The paper clearly differentiates their contribution from previous work, particularly in how their method improves upon GP-UCB [1] without requiring impractical δ-cover sampling.

In terms of significance, the work addresses a long-standing open problem in Bayesian optimization: achieving exponential convergence without impractical sampling procedures. This has both theoretical importance (providing a rigorous exponential regret bound) and practical implications for real-world applications where δ-cover sampling is infeasible. The authors' framework for understanding when not relying on a particular bound is beneficial could influence future work in optimization and related fields.

However, the experimental evaluation has notable weaknesses that prevent the paper from fully demonstrating its significance. The authors compare IMGPO with SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on eight synthetic functions, but the experimental design lacks several standard elements expected in NIPS papers. First, the authors do not conduct multiple initial evaluations of f, which is standard practice in Bayesian optimization to ensure fair comparison. Without multiple initializations, the statistical significance of the results cannot be properly assessed, as noted in the draft review.

Second, the authors do not compare with state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization approaches such as Entropy Search [25] or Information-Directed Sampling [26], which are now standard benchmarks in the field. This omission weakens the demonstration of IMGPO's practical significance.

Third, the experiments are limited to synthetic functions with maximum dimensionality of 6, without any real-world applications to validate the approach in practical scenarios. While synthetic functions are acceptable for theoretical validation, NIPS papers typically include at least one real-world application to demonstrate practical utility, especially for optimization methods.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Adding multiple initializations for statistical significance
2. Including comparisons with more recent BO methods (e.g., Entropy Search, Information-Directed Sampling)
3. Adding at least one real-world optimization problem (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for a machine learning model)
4. Providing more detailed analysis of the Ξmax parameter's effect on performance

In summary, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel approach to achieving exponential convergence without impractical sampling. The theoretical framework is sound, well-explained, and provides valuable insights into bound-based search methods. However, the experimental section falls short of NIPS standards, lacking statistical rigor and real-world validation. The paper would benefit from addressing these experimental limitations to fully demonstrate the practical significance of the theoretical advances.

I recommend a score of 5 (accept), as the theoretical contribution is substantial and the paper makes a clear, meaningful advance in the field. However, the experimental section should be strengthened in a revised version to better support the claimed significance. The authors should address the experimental limitations noted above, particularly by including multiple initializations, comparing with more recent BO methods, and adding at least one real-world application. With these improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
