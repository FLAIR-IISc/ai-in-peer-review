The paper presents a variant of Bayesian Global Optimization where future samples are generated through a space/interval partitioning algorithm, which yields theoretical improvements (exponential regret). The work appears sound and novel but seems to be only evaluated on 1D test problems.

The algorithm is clearly described and explained/illustrated with an example. However, I have some concerns regarding the experimental results. It appears that the evaluation is limited to low-dimensional test functions (not explicitly stated), which raises concerns about the scalability of the approach to higher dimensions. The interval partitioning method may require an exponentially growing "resolution" to maintain its effectiveness, which could lead to computational issues.

The approach is related to the well-known DIRECT algorithm, which is known to suffer from poor performance when the dimensionality of the problem increases. I believe it is essential to discuss this limitation in the paper, as it has significant implications for practical applications.

Additionally, I have a few minor comments:

* On page 2, it is mentioned that UCB is considered "for brevity." Could the authors elaborate on whether other acquisition functions, such as expected improvement, could be used instead?
* On page 3, the statement "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is unclear. Could the authors provide more context or explanation?
* On page 4, it is mentioned that "At n=16, the far right...but no function evaluation occurs." Could the authors provide a brief explanation for this observation?

Overall, the paper presents a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian Global Optimization, but it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its limitations and potential extensions to higher-dimensional problems.
