This paper presents a Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence, addressing limitations of existing approaches. Most Bayesian optimization methods require auxiliary optimization – an additional, often time-consuming, non-convex global optimization problem. Furthermore, a recent method achieving exponential convergence [1] relies on δ-cover sampling, considered impractical [1, 2]. This work eliminates both these requirements and achieves an exponential convergence rate.

1 Introduction
The paper tackles a general global optimization problem: maximizing f(x) subject to x ∈ Ω ⊂ RD, where f : Ω → R is a non-convex black-box deterministic function. This problem arises in various applications, including parameter tuning in machine learning [3], engineering design [4], and biological model fitting [5]. A key performance metric is the simple regret, rn = supx∈Ω f(x) − f(x+), where x+ is the best input found by the algorithm. The authors use “regret” as shorthand for simple regret.

Global optimization is generally intractable without further assumptions [6]. A common simplifying assumption is a bound on the slope of f, such as Lipschitz continuity with a known constant. While algorithms under this assumption offer regret bounds [7, 8, 9], the need to know a tight Lipschitz constant is a practical concern. Researchers have addressed this by proposing methods to estimate the Lipschitz constant during optimization [10, 11, 12].

Bayesian optimization offers a way to relax the assumption of known Lipschitz continuity and has become a popular approach for non-convex black-box functions. Within the machine learning community, Gaussian processes (GPs) are frequently used for Bayesian optimization [13, 14, 15]. De Freitas et al. [1] recently proposed a theoretically sound procedure with exponential convergence, but it requires δ-cover sampling, which is computationally demanding. This paper aims to bridge this gap by presenting a novel GP-based algorithm with an exponential convergence rate *without* requiring δ-cover sampling.

2 Gaussian Process Optimization
The paper begins with a clear and concise overview of Gaussian Process optimization. It explains how GPs estimate the distribution over a function f and use this information to guide the selection of the next evaluation point. The discussion contrasts parametric approaches with the non-parametric GP approach, defining the GP prior f(∙) ∼ GP (m(∙), κ(∙, ∙)). The mathematical formulation of the GP model for a finite set of points is presented, followed by the derivation of the conditional distribution for predicting f at a new point. The paper highlights the advantages of GPs, notably the closed-form solution that simplifies analysis and implementation.

The discussion then details the importance of the mean and covariance functions (kernels), mentioning common choices like the Matern and Gaussian kernels. The process of estimating kernel hyperparameters using empirical Bayesian methods is also noted [16, 17]. Finally, the section explains how GPs are used in Bayesian optimization through acquisition functions like Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) and Expected Improvement (EI), focusing on UCB and its reliance on solving an additional non-convex optimization problem. The paper correctly points out that the cost of evaluating the objective function is assumed to dominate the cost of this additional optimization.

3 Infinite-Metric GP Optimization

3.1 Overview
The authors motivate their approach by identifying a limitation of GP-UCB: the tightness of the bound (UCB) determines its effectiveness. Obtaining a tight bound while maintaining correctness is difficult. They draw a parallel to A* search and PAC-MDP algorithms, emphasizing the trade-off between admissibility and tightness. Their solution strategy is to acknowledge that the GP-derived bound may not be sufficient and to leverage the existence of an unknown, potentially tighter, bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer, formalized in Assumption 1.

The core idea is to simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all possible candidates for this unknown bound, using the GP to reduce the number of candidates. This is a clever approach and potentially very effective. The code is publicly available at http://lis.csail.mit.edu/code/imgpo.html.

3.2 Description of Algorithm
The algorithm uses hierarchical partitioning to maintain hyperintervals, with each interval centered around an evaluation point. In each iteration, the algorithm selects intervals based on their center value and UCB, attempting to identify regions likely to contain the global optimizer.  The detailed procedure is well-explained, and the illustrative Figure 1 is helpful. The steps (i)-(v) are clearly outlined, and their purpose – selecting intervals potentially containing the global optimum based on both the known (GP) and unknown bounds – is well-articulated.

3.3 Technical Detail of Algorithm
This section provides the mathematical details of the algorithm. It defines key parameters like h (depth of the partitioning tree) and introduces notations for the exploration loss (ρt). The authors state Theorem 1, which demonstrates the exponential convergence rate, and discuss the factors influencing the convergence rate.

3.4 Relationship to Previous Algorithms
The authors position their work relative to BaMSOO [2] and DIRECT [19], highlighting the improvements in regret bounds achieved by their approach. They acknowledge the influence of Munos' work [18] on considering a set of potential bounds.

4 Analysis
The analysis section provides a theoretical justification for the algorithm's performance, presenting Theorem 1 and discussing the impact of various parameters on the regret bound. The authors also provide insightful remarks on the importance of the tightness of the GP-estimated bound and the benefit of considering infinitely many possible bounds. The discussion of scalability limitations and potential improvements is also valuable.

5 Experiments
The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO on eight different objective functions, comparing it with SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI. The authors acknowledge the limitations of using a fixed kernel and hyperparameters and explain their choice. The results consistently show IMGPO outperforming the other algorithms, and the discussion of the impact of Ξmax is helpful.

6 Conclusion
The conclusion summarizes the contributions of the paper, emphasizing the exponential convergence rate and the novel approach of considering all possible bounds. The authors suggest potential future directions, such as applying the same principle to other bound-based methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors clearly identify a practical limitation of existing methods and propose a novel solution with a theoretical guarantee of exponential convergence. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides a clear explanation of the algorithm and its analysis. The experimental results support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed algorithm addresses a significant limitation of existing Bayesian optimization methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The exponential convergence rate is rigorously proven.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its underlying principles.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability to very high dimensions may be limited, as discussed in the analysis. However, the authors acknowledge this and suggest potential avenues for improvement.
*   The experiments use fixed kernel hyperparameters, which may not fully reflect real-world scenarios. However, the authors explain their rationale for this choice.

**Final Recommendation:**

Strongly Accept. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is of high quality.
