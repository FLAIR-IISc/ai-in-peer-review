This paper proposes a global optimization algorithm that avoids delta-cover sampling while achieving exponentially decreasing regret, thereby generalizing the approach of Freitas et al. (2012), which relied on an impractical sampling scheme. The presentation is clear, the theoretical contribution is well motivated, and the connection to prior work on bound-based search methods is well articulated. Overall, the paper is technically solid and likely to be of interest to the community.

That said, my overall enthusiasm is tempered by the experimental section, which falls short of current standards for Bayesian optimization (BO) evaluation.

Strengths:
- Clear exposition and positioning within the literature on bound-based global optimization.
- Compelling theoretical guarantees suggesting exponential regret decay without resorting to delta-cover sampling.
- The work extends and improves upon a known line of research (Freitas et al., 2012) by removing a key practical limitation.

Main concerns:
- Lack of standardized BO evaluation protocol. The experiments do not use multiple random initializations or multiple initial evaluations of the objective, which are standard in BO. Without repeated runs and aggregated statistics (means, variances, confidence intervals), it is difficult to assess robustness or significance.
- Limited and incomplete baselines. Although the method is bound-based, it should still be compared against strong contemporary BO baselines, including information-theoretic approaches such as Entropy Search (and ideally Predictive Entropy Search), as well as commonly used acquisition strategies like Expected Improvement, GP-UCB, and Thompson sampling. Without these, it is hard to judge practical competitiveness.
- Synthetic-only evaluation and low dimensionality. All experiments are on synthetic test functions of up to six dimensions. There are no real-world benchmarks (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models, wet-lab optimization, robotics/control tasks) where BO is typically applied. This limits the practical relevance of the empirical claims.
- Missing analysis of computational overhead. For bound-based methods, runtime per iteration and wall-clock comparisons can be as important as sample efficiency. No timing results or scalability analysis are reported.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Adopt the standard BO experimental protocol: multiple random seeds, multiple initial design sizes (e.g., varying the number of initial evaluations), and report aggregate statistics with error bars or confidence intervals.
- Include stronger and more diverse baselines: Entropy Search (and/or Predictive Entropy Search), Expected Improvement, GP-UCB, Thompson sampling, and a strong random or quasi-random search baseline. Ensure fair hyperparameter tuning and consistent model assumptions across methods.
- Add real-world benchmarks (e.g., hyperparameter optimization for SVMs, neural networks, or gradient-boosted trees; robotics or control tasks; wet-lab or materials optimization if accessible). These will better demonstrate practical impact.
- Evaluate sensitivity to model misspecification (e.g., kernel choice, noise levels) and to key algorithmic parameters. Ablations that isolate the contribution of the bound-based component would be useful.
- Report computational cost per iteration and wall-clock time to convergence, especially in comparison to acquisition-based baselines.
- If feasible, explore slightly higher dimensions (e.g., 10–20) to probe the algorithm’s practical limits.

Overall assessment:
The paper is well written and offers an interesting and potentially impactful theoretical advance in global optimization by eliminating delta-cover sampling while maintaining exponential regret guarantees. However, the empirical evaluation is not yet commensurate with NIPS standards for BO papers. Strengthening the experiments along the lines above would substantially improve the paper’s credibility and impact.

Rating: 5 (promising theory and clear presentation, but empirics are currently too limited for a stronger recommendation)
