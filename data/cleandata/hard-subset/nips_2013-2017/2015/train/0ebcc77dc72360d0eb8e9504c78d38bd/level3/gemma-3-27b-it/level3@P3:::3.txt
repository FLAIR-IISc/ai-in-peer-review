This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), achieving exponential convergence without relying on δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization – limitations of prior approaches like de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea elegantly addresses the challenge of obtaining tight bounds in GP-based optimization by considering an infinite set of potential bounds encoded within an unknown semi-metric, effectively combining global and local search strategies. 

The work builds upon a strong foundation in Bayesian optimization, notably integrating ideas from both continuity estimation approaches (like those in Rasmussen & Williams [16]) and methods that aim to relax the need for strict Lipschitz continuity assumptions (e.g., [10, 11]). It’s positioned well within the NIPS community, drawing from and extending established techniques in GP modeling [13, 14, 15] and global optimization [6, 7, 8].  IMGPO can be viewed as a refinement of bound-based search methods, attempting to overcome the inherent tightness limitations often found in approaches like A* search or GP-UCB. The connection to BaMSOO [2] is clearly outlined, and the paper convincingly demonstrates a theoretical improvement in convergence rate.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes the exponential convergence rate.  The assumptions (Assumptions 1 & 2) are clearly stated, and the derivation appears well-justified.  The detailed analysis in Remarks 1, 2, and 3 provides valuable insight into the algorithm's behavior and sensitivity to various parameters.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivation.  Section 3.2, with its illustrative example (Figure 1), is particularly effective at conveying the high-level intuition behind the algorithm. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and the detailed technical explanation in 3.3 facilitate implementation and replication.
* **Originality:** The central idea of simultaneously exploring multiple potential bounds without requiring a specific Lipschitz constant or δ-cover sampling is novel.  While building on existing techniques, the combination and the theoretical justification represent a significant contribution.
* **Significance:** The removal of the δ-cover sampling requirement, identified as a practical bottleneck in [2], is a substantial advance.  The demonstrated empirical performance (Figure 2) and the improved theoretical regret bound suggest that IMGPO offers a valuable alternative to existing Bayesian optimization methods. The analysis framework provides a novel perspective on bounding optimization algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** While the analysis demonstrates an exponential convergence rate, the discussion acknowledges a potential scalability issue with increasing dimensionality (Section 4). The reliance on parameter λ approaching one as *D* increases raises questions about its practical applicability to very high-dimensional problems without further adaptations.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Although the experiments avoid hand-tuning hyperparameters, the performance does appear somewhat sensitive to the choice of Ξmax (discussed in Section 5). More extensive sensitivity analysis, perhaps including a discussion of methods for adaptively selecting Ξmax, would strengthen the work.
* **Computational Cost:**  Table 1 indicates that IMGPO is slower than SOO. Although the authors argue that it is faster than traditional GP optimization methods, the increased computational burden compared to SOO might be a limitation in certain applications.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of previous Bayesian optimization methods.
* The theoretical analysis is strong and supports the claimed exponential convergence rate.
* The algorithm is clearly explained and well-documented, facilitating implementation.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The work represents a novel and valuable contribution to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Scalability to very high dimensions remains a concern.
* The computational cost is higher than some competing methods.
* Further investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.



**Overall:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors have clearly articulated a novel and theoretically sound method that addresses a practical limitation of existing approaches. While scalability and computational cost are potential concerns, the demonstrated improvements in convergence rate and the removal of the δ-cover sampling requirement make this work a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the hyperparameter sensitivity and potentially expand the discussion of scalability limitations.
