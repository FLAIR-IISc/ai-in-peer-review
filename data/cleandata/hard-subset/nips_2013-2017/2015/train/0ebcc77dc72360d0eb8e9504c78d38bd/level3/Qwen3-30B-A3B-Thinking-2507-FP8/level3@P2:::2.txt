

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper proposes IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of existing methods: GP-UCB requires solving an additional non-convex optimization problem for selecting the next query point, and the exponential convergence method of de Freitas et al. [1] relies on impractical δ-cover sampling. The paper's core insight is to consider "infinitely many possible bounds" (semi-metrics) rather than relying on a single bound provided by the Gaussian process prior.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS, particularly the foundational GP-UCB work by Srinivas et al. [15] and the BaMSOO algorithm by Wang et al. [2]. It also builds on Munos' SOO [18] and Jones et al.'s DIRECT [19]. The authors correctly identify that the literature has diverged along two paths: methods with a specific bound (like GP-UCB) versus methods without (like SOO). IMGPO bridges these two approaches, providing a theoretically sound and practically oriented solution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous proof of exponential convergence (Theorem 1), which represents a significant improvement over BaMSOO [2] that only achieves polynomial regret. The analysis is thorough, with detailed discussion of how different parameters affect the regret bound (Remarks 1-3).

2. **Novel approach:** The idea of simultaneously considering all possible candidates of bounds rather than committing to a single bound is conceptually fresh and addresses a fundamental limitation in the Bayesian optimization literature.

3. **Clarity and presentation:** The paper is well-written with clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and a helpful illustrative figure (Figure 1). The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.

4. **Practical considerations:** The authors thoughtfully address the trade-offs between using GP information and considering multiple bounds, and they provide a clear explanation of when their approach is beneficial compared to relying on a single bound.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited experimental evaluation:** The most significant weakness is that the experiments appear to be limited to 1D test functions (as noted in the key points). While the theoretical results are strong, the practical relevance is questionable without evidence of performance in higher dimensions. The paper mentions "Sin2 embedded in 1000 dimension" in the experiments, but this appears to be using IMGPO as a subroutine within REMBO [14], which is a separate dimensionality reduction technique. The core algorithm's performance in high dimensions is not directly demonstrated.

2. **Scalability concerns:** The paper should discuss the scalability issues more thoroughly. The interval-partitioning approach used in IMGPO is related to DIRECT [19], which is known to perform poorly in high dimensions. The authors note that "λ can get close to one as input dimension D increases," but this critical limitation for practical applications needs more detailed discussion. The relationship between the algorithm's performance and dimensionality should be explicitly addressed.

3. **Minor clarifications needed:**
   - The sentence "simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is unclear and could benefit from more precise explanation.
   - The reason for no function evaluation at n=16 (Figure 1) is not sufficiently explained.
   - The paper states "for brevity, we consider Bayesian optimization with UCB" but doesn't clarify whether other acquisition functions (like expected improvement) could be used with the same theoretical guarantees.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature with a novel approach that achieves exponential convergence without the two practical limitations of previous methods. The theoretical analysis is sound, well-motivated, and provides valuable insights into why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial. However, the experimental evaluation is limited to 1D functions, which weakens the practical significance of the contribution. The paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of scalability concerns in higher dimensions and additional experiments demonstrating performance in moderate-dimensional problems.

The work is clearly original and addresses an important gap in the literature. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the limited experimental evaluation in higher dimensions is a significant weakness that needs to be addressed for practical adoption. The paper would be substantially stronger with additional experiments in higher dimensions (even if using dimensionality reduction techniques) and a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations.

**Recommendation:**

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. However, the limited experimental evaluation, particularly the lack of demonstration in higher dimensions, is a significant weakness. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors address the scalability concerns in the camera-ready version, including additional experiments in higher dimensions and a more thorough discussion of the dimensionality limitations.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical contribution with exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling
- Rigorous analysis with detailed discussion of parameter effects
- Clear presentation with helpful illustrations and pseudocode

*Con:*
- Experimental evaluation limited to 1D problems (with only one high-dimensional example using a separate dimensionality reduction technique)
- Insufficient discussion of scalability concerns in higher dimensions
- Minor clarity issues in the description of the algorithm

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, but the practical relevance would be significantly enhanced with more comprehensive experimental validation in higher dimensions.
