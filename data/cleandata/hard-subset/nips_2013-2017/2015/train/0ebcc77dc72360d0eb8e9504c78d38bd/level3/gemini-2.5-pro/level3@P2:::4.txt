This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to solve global optimization problems for non-convex black-box functions. The main contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate, a significant theoretical improvement over existing practical methods. The authors connect their work to two main lines of research. The first is Bayesian optimization with Gaussian Processes (e.g., GP-UCB), which typically requires solving a difficult auxiliary optimization problem at each step to select the next query point. The second is the family of hierarchical partitioning algorithms like SOO, which avoid auxiliary optimization but have previously only achieved polynomial regret bounds when combined with GPs (e.g., BaMSOO). The proposed IMGPO algorithm synthesizes ideas from both approaches. It uses a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, but leverages a GP-based upper confidence bound (UCB) to prune branches of the search tree, thereby avoiding the need for an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous theoretical work promising exponential convergence.

**Quality**

The paper appears to be technically sound. The authors provide a theoretical analysis culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential regret bound for IMGPO. This result is significant as it addresses what was previously identified as an open problem in the literature [2]. The analysis in the remarks following the theorem is particularly insightful, providing intuition on how the algorithm's performance is affected by the tightness of the GP's UCB and how the "infinite-metric" approach compares to methods that rely on a single, fixed bound.

The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on eight benchmark functions with varying characteristics and dimensionalities (from 1D up to 1000D via an embedding). The proposed IMGPO algorithm is compared against strong and relevant baselines, including SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods like GP-PI and GP-EI. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that IMGPO generally outperforms these competing methods, often converging to a better solution significantly faster. The authors are also careful to note a case where the GP prior was misleading (Rosenbrock2) and SOO performed competitively, which adds credibility to their evaluation.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and didactic. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the proposed work within the existing literature. The core idea of the algorithm is explained intuitively in Section 3.1 before diving into the technical details. A major strength of the paper is the use of Figure 1, which provides a clear, step-by-step visual illustration of the algorithm's mechanics. This figure is immensely helpful for building intuition about how the hierarchical partitioning, function evaluations, and UCB-based pruning interact. The pseudocode is detailed and appears to be sufficient for a knowledgeable reader to reproduce the work.

A couple of very minor suggestions for improvement:
- In the abstract (line 49 in the submitted PDF), the phrase "Our approach eliminates both requirements and achieves an exponential convergence rate" feels slightly redundant given the preceding sentence.
- In the description of the Matern kernel (line 72), there appears to be a misplaced parenthesis in the function `g(z) = σ2(1 + z + z2/3) exp(−z))`. It should likely be `...exp(-z))`.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. While it builds upon established concepts like hierarchical partitioning (from DIRECT and SOO) and GP-based upper confidence bounds, the synthesis of these ideas is novel. Specifically, using the GP to prune the search space of an "infinite-metric" optimization algorithm to achieve an exponential convergence rate without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling is a new and clever contribution. The paper clearly articulates how this approach differs from and improves upon closely related work, particularly BaMSOO [2] and the theoretical work of de Freitas et al. [1].

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are important and likely to be influential. Bayesian optimization is a widely used technique for hyperparameter tuning and other expensive black-box optimization problems. Providing a practical algorithm with stronger theoretical guarantees (exponential vs. polynomial convergence) is a significant advance. The fact that IMGPO also eliminates the need for an auxiliary global optimization step makes it more practical and potentially faster in terms of wall-clock time than standard GP-UCB/EI/PI methods, as supported by the timing results in Table 1. Researchers and practitioners in the field are likely to use these ideas and build upon them. The conceptual framework of combining a learned bound (from the GP) with a more general, assumption-free partitioning scheme could also inspire new algorithms in other areas of search and planning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses and solves a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization: achieving an exponential convergence rate in a practical algorithm without δ-cover sampling.
2.  The proposed algorithm, IMGPO, is novel in its synthesis of hierarchical partitioning and GP-based pruning.
3.  The work is supported by both strong theoretical analysis and convincing empirical results on a wide range of benchmark problems.
4.  The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
I have no major arguments against acceptance. The weaknesses are limited to minor typos that can be easily corrected. While I am not an expert in the nuances of global optimization theory, the paper's arguments, theory, and experiments are presented with a clarity and rigor that inspires confidence.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and well-executed contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
