

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling—two significant practical barriers in existing approaches. The authors address a key open problem identified by Wang et al. [2], who noted that while de Freitas et al. [1] achieved exponential convergence, their method relied on impractical δ-cover sampling. IMGPO eliminates both requirements, making it more practical for real-world applications while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it introduces the key insight that instead of relying on a single upper bound from the GP prior, one should consider all possible bounds encoded in an unknown semi-metric (Assumption 1). This "infinite-metric" approach allows the algorithm to be more flexible and effective when the GP-based bound is not tight. The algorithmic instantiation (IMGPO) cleverly combines hierarchical partitioning with GP-based upper confidence bounds to efficiently explore the search space while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. Section 3.2 provides an excellent illustrative example with Figure 1 that clearly conveys the high-level intuition behind the algorithm. Section 3.3 offers detailed programmatic descriptions and pseudocode (Algorithm 1) that make implementation straightforward, which is rare in theoretical papers. The authors carefully explain how the algorithm works through each step, with clear explanations of the purpose of different components.

The theoretical analysis in Section 4 is rigorous and well-motivated. Theorem 1 proves an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which improves upon the polynomial bounds achieved by BaMSOO [2]. The authors also provide insightful remarks (Remarks 1-3) that explain the effect of different parameters on the regret bound, demonstrating a deep understanding of the algorithm's behavior.

The experimental results in Section 5 are compelling. IMGPO consistently outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight benchmark functions with varying dimensions (1-1000). The authors conduct fair comparisons by using the same kernel and hyperparameter initialization for all methods, which is a significant improvement over prior work that hand-picked kernels for each function. The results demonstrate both theoretical and practical advantages of their approach.

The paper integrates ideas from Bayesian optimization literature both with and without continuity estimation, providing a bridge between two previously divergent research paths. It properly contextualizes its contributions relative to key prior work, including the DIRECT algorithm [19], SOO [18], and GP-UCB [1, 2].

Strengths:
- Solves a significant open problem in Bayesian optimization (exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling)
- Presents a theoretically sound method with clear practical advantages
- Well-organized with strong illustrative examples and implementation details
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions
- Provides deep theoretical insights into why not relying on a single bound is beneficial

Weaknesses:
- The dimensionality limitation noted in Remark 1 (λ approaching 1 as D increases) is a practical concern that deserves more attention
- The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how different kernel choices might affect performance
- The computational overhead of the infinite-metric exploration (ρ̄t) isn't fully analyzed in terms of scaling with problem size

This paper makes a strong contribution to the NIPS community. It advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization by providing a practically viable method with theoretical guarantees. The authors successfully bridge theoretical and practical aspects of optimization, making their work accessible to both theoretical and applied researchers. The clear presentation, strong theoretical foundation, and compelling experimental results make this paper a significant contribution to the field.

The paper addresses all four criteria effectively: it's technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with excellent organization, original in its approach to handling bounds in optimization, and significant in solving a long-standing open problem with practical implications. The authors have made a clear contribution that will likely be widely adopted in the Bayesian optimization community.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS. It represents a substantial advance in the field with practical benefits for real-world applications of Bayesian optimization.
