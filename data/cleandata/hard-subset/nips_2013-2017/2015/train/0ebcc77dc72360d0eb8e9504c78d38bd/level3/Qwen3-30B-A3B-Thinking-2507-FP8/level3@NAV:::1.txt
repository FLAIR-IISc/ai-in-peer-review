

This paper introduces IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential regret without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing a limitation in Freitas et al. (2012). The authors position their work within the bounded-based search literature, effectively contrasting with prior approaches like BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014) and GP-UCB. The theoretical contribution is significant, demonstrating that considering infinitely many possible bounds (rather than relying on a single estimated bound) leads to improved regret bounds compared to existing methods.

The paper excels in clarity and theoretical exposition. The algorithm description is well-structured with clear pseudocode and insightful remarks explaining the intuition behind the approach. The relationship to previous work is thoroughly discussed, and the mathematical analysis is sound, providing meaningful insights about the interplay between GP estimates and unknown bounds.

However, the experimental evaluation significantly undermines the paper's overall quality. The authors fail to follow standard Bayesian optimization practices: they do not use multiple initial evaluations (standard in BO literature), making statistical comparisons meaningless. More critically, they omit comparisons with state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods like Entropy Search (Hennig & Schuler, 2012) and GP-UCB variants. All experiments are conducted on synthetic functions with maximum dimension 6, lacking any real-world applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning or wet-lab experiments) that would validate the method's practical utility.

While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the experimental section falls below NIPS standards. The lack of statistical comparisons, absence of relevant baselines, and reliance on synthetic benchmarks alone prevent meaningful assessment of the algorithm's practical value. The paper would benefit from additional experiments on higher-dimensional synthetic functions, real-world parameter tuning tasks, and comparisons with information-theoretic BO methods.

Theoretical novelty (Originality: 4/5), technical soundness (Quality: 4/5), and clarity (Clarity: 5/5) are strong, but the experimental weaknesses significantly impact significance (Significance: 2/5). Given the conference's emphasis on both theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper's experimental shortcomings prevent a top acceptance. I recommend rejection, though the theoretical insights would be valuable in a more comprehensive study.
