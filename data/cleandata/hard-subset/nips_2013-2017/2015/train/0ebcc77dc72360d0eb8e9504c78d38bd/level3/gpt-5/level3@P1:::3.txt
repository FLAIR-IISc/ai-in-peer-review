This paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions that achieves exponential convergence without two impractical requirements that prior work relied on: δ-cover sampling and auxiliary global optimization of acquisition functions. IMGPO integrates a GP posterior for screening and uncertainty quantification with a hierarchical partitioning strategy that implicitly explores a family of potentially tighter unknown continuity bounds (“infinite metrics”). The algorithm proceeds by selecting and dividing hyperrectangles via a multi-criteria test: (i) center-value maximality at each depth (unknown semi-metric guidance), (ii) pruning by dominance across scales, and (iii) GP-UCB-based screening; it can use GP-derived UCB values as temporary “lazy” evaluations to avoid unnecessary function calls. Theoretical analysis shows an exponential regret bound of the form O(exp(−c(N+Ngp))) under a Hölder-type assumption on the function around the global optimum, with constants capturing dimension, division scheme, and an “infinite-metric exploration loss” that quantifies the price paid for not committing to a single bound. Empirically, IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP acquisition policies (PI/EI) across a range of test functions, with substantial practical improvements even when the GP prior is imperfect; code is provided.

Relation to prior work: The contribution sits at the intersection of optimistic partition-based methods (DIRECT, SOO/DOO) and GP-based Bayesian optimization (GP-UCB, EI/PI). de Freitas et al. established exponential rates for GP bandits under δ-cover sampling, which is impractical. BaMSOO combined GP-UCB with SOO to avoid δ-cover but achieved only polynomial rates. IMGPO’s novelty is to use the GP to reduce the candidate set of putative continuity bounds while maintaining a partition-based search that obviates auxiliary acquisition maximization. The analysis framework clarifies when a non-tight GP-UCB can remain useful and when not committing to a single bound can strictly improve rates, connecting to near-optimality dimension ideas and DIRECT’s multi-scale intuition.

Quality:
- Theory: The regret bound O(exp(−α[(N+Ngp)/(2CD ρ̄) − Ξ − 2] ln 3)) is clearly stated, improves on BaMSOO’s polynomial rates, and highlights the roles of dimension (D), division constants (C), and the exploration loss ρ̄. The remarks provide insightful comparisons: (1) benefit from GP (through Ngp and lower ρ̄), (2) downside if UCBs are too loose, and (3) advantages of infinite-metric exploration vs committing to an estimated bound. One potential concern is the rigor of the high-probability statement when GP hyperparameters are adaptively learned; a discussion of how the union bound over UCB calls interacts with hyperparameter updates would strengthen the guarantees.
- Algorithmic detail: Section 3.2’s walkthrough and the pseudocode are precise and implementation-ready. The role of parameters η and Ξmax is made explicit, and the computational steps are clear (including lazy GP-based labeling).
- Experiments: The setup is reasonable: fixed Matern-5/2 kernel with blind initialization, hyperparameter learning via empirical Bayes, and comparisons to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI/PI. The results are compelling across multiple benchmarks, including a REMBO-based high-dimensional illustration. Reporting of CPU times is helpful; more detailed runtime and memory scaling would be welcome.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The illustrative 1D example and figure are particularly effective. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., consistent use of n/N/t, and clearer definition of λ); and the semi-metric assumption could be linked more directly to near-optimality dimension to aid intuition.

Originality:
- The core idea—systematically leveraging an infinite family of candidate bounds while using GP-UCB to prune—is novel and bridges two lines of work. Achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover and without auxiliary acquisition maximization is a meaningful theoretical and algorithmic advance.

Significance:
- The work addresses a recognized gap: practical GP-based optimization with strong rates. The analysis provides a practical lens on how GP uncertainty helps even when imperfect, and the algorithm is likely to influence follow-up methods in partition-based BO and beyond (planning, UCT-style algorithms).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions: The unknown semi-metric with Hölder-type control is standard but could be better contextualized (e.g., near-optimality dimension) and discussed regarding real-world plausibility.
- High-dimensionality: The bound degrades with D as λ approaches 1; while the REMBO experiment suggests a path forward, additional ablations on dimensionality and subspace structure would help.
- Hyperparameter learning: Theoretical guarantees assume valid UCBs; empirical Bayes may invalidate strict coverage. Either add a robustness lemma or empirically quantify UCB calibration and its effect on pruning.
- Sensitivity: Provide a sensitivity study for Ξmax, η, and kernel misspecification; an ablation removing GP screening or infinite-metric screening would isolate their contributions.
- Noisy observations: The method is for deterministic objectives; a discussion or appendix sketch of extensions to noise would broaden applicability.

Pros:
- Eliminates δ-cover sampling and auxiliary acquisition optimization.
- Exponential convergence with clear, insightful analysis.
- Practical algorithm with code and strong empirical results.
- Clear exposition with implementable pseudocode and intuition.
- Bridges BO and optimistic partitioning in a principled way.

Cons:
- Theoretical coverage under adaptive hyperparameter learning is not fully justified.
- Performance and guarantees degrade with dimension; limited exploration of remedies beyond REMBO.
- New parameters (Ξmax, η) introduce tuning considerations not fully explored.
- Assumes deterministic evaluations; no noisy setting analysis.

Overall assessment: A strong, well-executed paper that advances the theory and practice of Bayesian optimization. With minor clarifications on theoretical assumptions under hyperparameter learning and additional empirical sensitivity analyses, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS.
