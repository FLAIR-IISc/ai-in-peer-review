### Summary

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed for global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The primary contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate without two significant practical drawbacks of prior art: the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization to select the next query point, and the reliance on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The method ingeniously combines a Gaussian Process (GP) prior with a hierarchical partitioning scheme. Unlike standard GP-UCB which relies solely on the GP-derived upper confidence bound, IMGPO implicitly considers an infinite family of possible semi-metrics that could define the function's smoothness. The GP bound is then used to efficiently prune the search space explored by the partitioning method. The authors provide a theoretical analysis proving an exponential regret bound, a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of related practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The paper is supported by compelling experiments on several benchmark functions, demonstrating superior performance against established methods like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based optimization with EI and PI acquisition functions.

### Strengths

The paper makes several strong contributions to the field of Bayesian optimization.

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The work successfully addresses an open problem explicitly stated in previous work [2]: developing a practical GP-based optimization algorithm with an exponential convergence rate. By eliminating the need for both auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, the proposed IMGPO algorithm represents a significant step forward in making theoretically powerful Bayesian optimization methods more practical and accessible. The core idea of integrating two distinct lines of global optimization research—one that assumes a known (or estimated) bound (e.g., GP-UCB) and one that cleverly searches across multiple potential smoothness assumptions (e.g., DIRECT, SOO)—is both novel and powerful. This synthesis provides a new and promising direction for algorithm design in this area.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
    *   **Theoretical Soundness:** It provides a theorem establishing an exponential regret bound, which is a major theoretical improvement over related work. The analysis presented in Section 4, particularly Remarks 1-3, offers valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior, explaining how the GP bound contributes to performance and why considering an "infinite-metric" approach can be superior even to using a known, but potentially loose, function bound.
    *   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is thorough and convincing. The authors compare IMGPO against a well-chosen set of relevant and state-of-the-art baselines. The results across a variety of benchmark functions, ranging in modality and dimensionality, robustly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The inclusion of an honest discussion about the Rosenbrock2 function, where a misleading GP prior led to weaker performance, adds to the credibility of the evaluation.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and situating their work within the existing literature.
    *   The high-level intuition behind the algorithm is conveyed effectively through the illustrative example in Section 3.2 and Figure 1. This makes a complex procedure much easier to understand.
    *   The detailed pseudocode in Algorithm 1, combined with the clear description of its steps, provides sufficient information for an expert to reproduce the work, which is a hallmark of a strong contribution.

### Weaknesses

The paper is very strong, and the weaknesses are minor and mostly related to limitations and potential avenues for future work.

1.  **Scalability with Dimensionality:** The authors correctly note in their analysis that the convergence rate constant λ approaches 1 as the input dimension D increases, implying that the performance benefit diminishes in higher dimensions. While the paper demonstrates a potential solution by embedding IMGPO within a random embedding framework (REMBO) for one high-dimensional problem, the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm remains a limitation. A more detailed discussion of the curse of dimensionality in the context of the hierarchical partitioning scheme would be beneficial.

2.  **Algorithmic Complexity and Hyperparameters:** The IMGPO algorithm, as presented in Algorithm 1, is considerably more complex than a standard GP-UCB loop (which offloads its complexity to a generic optimizer) or the simpler SOO algorithm. This complexity might pose a barrier to adoption. Furthermore, the algorithm introduces new hyperparameters, notably Ξmax and η. While η is standard for UCB methods, the role and sensitivity of Ξmax could be explored more fully. The experiments use a fixed value, but an ablation study showing the trade-off between computational cost and performance as Ξmax varies would strengthen the paper's practical claims.

3.  **The Nature of the GP "Bound":** The analysis and motivation rely on the property that `f(x) ≤ U(x|D)` holds with high probability. This is true when the GP prior is well-specified. However, as the Rosenbrock2 experiment shows, a poorly specified prior can violate this, causing the GP to be "misleading." While this is a fundamental challenge for all GP-based methods, the interplay between a misleading GP and the infinite-metric search component of IMGPO is intriguing and could warrant further discussion. For instance, does the algorithm degrade gracefully to something like SOO in this case, or can the misleading UCB values actively harm the partitioning search?

### Detailed Comments

- The update rule for Ξ in line 38 of Algorithm 1 seems somewhat heuristic. A brief sentence on the intuition behind this specific update rule (e.g., why powers of 2 are used) would be helpful for the reader.
- The theoretical analysis (Remark 3) provides an excellent intuitive justification for the "infinite-metric" approach. This is a key insight of the paper and is very well explained.
- The public release of the source code is a commendable step that will facilitate adoption and further research in this direction.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and well-supported contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. It presents a novel algorithm that is both theoretically sound and practically effective. I strongly recommend acceptance.
