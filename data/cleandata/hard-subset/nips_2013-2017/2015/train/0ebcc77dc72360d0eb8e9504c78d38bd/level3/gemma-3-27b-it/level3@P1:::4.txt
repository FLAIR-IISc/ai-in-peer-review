This paper introduces a Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence that avoids both auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, both limitations of existing approaches like that of de Freitas et al. [1]. The authors address a crucial challenge in Bayesian optimization – achieving exponential convergence without relying on potentially impractical techniques. They propose a novel Gaussian process (GP)-based algorithm that leverages an unknown semi-metric to guide the search, effectively combining global and local search strategies. 

The work builds upon a foundation of research in global optimization, acknowledging previous attempts to address the need for Lipschitz constant estimation [10, 11, 12] and the development of Bayesian optimization techniques using GPs [13, 14, 15]. It directly responds to the open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] – creating a GP-based method with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The paper contextualizes its approach within the broader class of bound-based search methods, drawing parallels to Lipschitz optimization and A* search [18].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm, demonstrating an exponential convergence rate. The analysis appears rigorous, and the authors carefully consider the impact of various parameters and assumptions. Theorem 1 provides a clear bound on the regret, and the discussion of its relation to the tightness of the GP-estimated bound (Remarks 1, 2, and 3) is particularly insightful.
* **Clarity and Didacticism:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a clear explanation of the underlying concepts. Even for readers less familiar with Gaussian Processes and global optimization (like myself), the methodology is presented in a comprehensible manner. The inclusion of Figure 1, illustrating the algorithm's operation with a 1-dimensional objective function, is exceptionally effective in conveying the core ideas.
* **Novelty and Significance:**  The elimination of both auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling is a significant contribution. It addresses a real-world limitation of existing Bayesian optimization methods, making the approach more practical. The combination of global and local search based on an unknown semi-metric appears to be a novel and potentially powerful technique.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results presented in Figure 2 demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm across a range of benchmark functions. The comparisons with established methods (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) consistently show that IMGPO performs well, often outperforming the other algorithms.
* **Thorough Analysis & Discussion:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results, offering a deep analysis of the method's strengths and limitations, as well as discussion on potential avenues for future research.  The exploration of how the algorithm performs with varying parameters like Ξmax provides a useful understanding of its sensitivity and potential for optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Abstract Formulation:** The statement in the abstract that the formulation is “redundant” (line 49) seems like a minor editorial issue but could be addressed for improved clarity.
* **Typographical Error:** A misplaced parenthesis in the function definition (line 72) should be corrected. 
* **Scalability Concerns:** While the experiments demonstrate strong performance, the authors acknowledge that scalability to very high dimensions may be a challenge, as indicated by the discussion surrounding λ. Further investigation into techniques for addressing this issue would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors use reasonable default hyperparameters, a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (e.g., the kernel parameters, η, and Ξmax) would strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Bayesian optimization methods.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and supports the claimed exponential convergence rate.
* The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the methodology.
* The novelty of the approach and its potential impact on the field are substantial.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* While scalability is acknowledged as a concern, it remains an open question for wider application.
* The minor typographical errors and abstract formulation issue, while not critical, indicate a need for careful polishing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors have developed a novel and theoretically sound algorithm that addresses a significant practical challenge. The clarity of the presentation and the convincing experimental results further strengthen the paper's case for acceptance. Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work significantly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the typographical error and abstract formulation issue. I also encourage the authors to consider further exploring scalability improvements in future work.
