This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel algorithm for global optimization of black-box functions. The method combines a Gaussian Process (GP) prior with a hierarchical partitioning scheme to select subsequent evaluation points. This approach cleverly circumvents two significant challenges in Bayesian optimization: 1) it eliminates the need for an auxiliary non-convex optimization step to maximize an acquisition function (like UCB or EI), which is a common bottleneck in standard GP-based methods; 2) it avoids the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure required by previous work [1] to achieve an exponential convergence rate. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that IMGPO achieves an exponential rate of convergence for the simple regret, an improvement over related methods like BaMSOO [2] which have polynomial regret bounds.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical result. The authors provide the first GP-based optimization algorithm that provably achieves an exponential convergence rate without impractical assumptions like access to a δ-cover sampler. This addresses an open problem explicitly mentioned in prior work [2] and represents a solid advance in the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization.

2.  **Novel and Practical Algorithm Design:** From a practical standpoint, the elimination of the auxiliary optimization problem is a major advantage. Maximizing the acquisition function can be computationally expensive and may have its own local optima issues. IMGPO replaces this with a deterministic partitioning and selection procedure, which is conceptually simpler and potentially more efficient. The algorithm is clearly described in the text, with pseudocode, and is well-illustrated with a 1D example that effectively conveys its mechanics.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The relationship to previous work (e.g., DIRECT, SOO, BaMSOO) is well-articulated, placing the contribution in proper context. The analysis in Section 4, particularly the "Remarks," provides valuable intuition about the trade-offs involved, such as the effect of GP bound tightness and the benefit of the "infinite-metric" approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability and Experimental Validation (Major Concern):** My primary reservation concerns the algorithm's scalability with input dimensionality. The experimental results, while positive, appear to be conducted almost exclusively on low-dimensional benchmark functions (1D to 3D, with one 2D function embedded in 1000D via REMBO). The hierarchical partitioning approach is fundamentally susceptible to the curse of dimensionality, as the number of regions to partition grows exponentially. This is a known weakness of related methods like DIRECT, which the paper cites but does not sufficiently discuss in this context. While the 1000D experiment shows IMGPO can be used as a component within a high-dimensional optimization framework, it does not demonstrate the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm itself. The theoretical regret bound also includes a dependency on the dimension D, which hints at this performance degradation. The practical significance of the algorithm is therefore unclear for problems with even moderate dimensionality (e.g., D > 5). The authors should include a more direct discussion of this limitation.

2.  **Ambiguity on Acquisition Criteria:** The paper states, "For brevity, we consider Bayesian optimization with UCB." This makes it unclear whether the UCB criterion is fundamental to the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees, or if other acquisition functions like Expected Improvement could be substituted. Clarifying this point would be valuable.

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The phrase "simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" in Section 3.1 is somewhat abstract. While this presumably refers to the hierarchical partitioning at different scales (depths), the intuition could be made more explicit for the reader.
    *   In the description of the 1D example, it is mentioned that at n=16, a function evaluation is skipped. The text explains this is due to step (v) of the algorithm. This is a nice feature for saving expensive function evaluations, and its effect could be highlighted more prominently as one of the benefits of the method.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for Bayesian optimization.
    *   Provides a significant theoretical result by proving an exponential convergence rate, solving a known open problem.
    *   Offers a practical advantage over standard GP-based methods by eliminating the need for an auxiliary optimization step.
    *   The paper is clear, well-written, and places its contributions appropriately within the existing literature.

*   **Arguments Against Acceptance:**
    *   The experimental evaluation is limited to low-dimensional problems and fails to address the critical issue of scalability, which is a known weakness for this class of partitioning algorithms. This limits the demonstrated practical impact of the work.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. The development of a practical algorithm with an exponential regret bound is a significant achievement. While the concerns about scalability are serious and limit the immediate practical applicability for higher-dimensional problems, the theoretical novelty and the elegance of the algorithmic approach are sufficient for acceptance at NIPS. I would strongly encourage the authors to incorporate a frank discussion of the limitations imposed by dimensionality in the final version of the paper.
