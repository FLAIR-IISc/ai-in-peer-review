This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which achieves an exponential convergence rate. The primary contributions are the elimination of two practical hurdles present in previous methods: the need to solve an auxiliary non-convex optimization problem to select the next query point, and the reliance on an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure required by prior work aiming for exponential convergence (de Freitas et al., 2012). The proposed method cleverly combines ideas from hierarchical partitioning methods like SOO (Munos, 2011) with the probabilistic bounds from Gaussian Processes. By simultaneously considering an infinite set of possible semi-metrics (or bounds on the function's smoothness) and using the GP-UCB to prune this set, IMGPO avoids committing to a single, potentially loose bound. This work directly addresses an open problem identified by Wang et al. (2014) and provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating its improved regret bounds.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance of Theoretical Contribution:** The core idea of combining the "infinite-metric" approach of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO with GP-based optimization is novel and elegant. It successfully bridges two distinct lines of research in global optimization. The main theoretical result—achieving an exponential regret bound without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization—is a significant step forward for the theory of Bayesian optimization. It directly answers an open question posed in the literature and provides a more practical path towards theoretically-grounded, high-performance optimization.

2.  **Clarity and Quality of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the broader context of bound-based search methods. The motivation is clearly laid out, the algorithm is described in detail with helpful illustrations, and the theoretical analysis is presented with insightful remarks that explain the practical implications of the results (e.g., the effects of GP tightness and the infinite-metric approach).

3.  **Potential Impact:** By removing the need for an inner optimization loop, the proposed method could be significantly faster in practice than traditional GP-UCB, GP-EI, or GP-PI methods, especially when the objective function is not prohibitively expensive. This makes it an attractive alternative for a wider range of problems.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

The primary weaknesses of this paper lie in the experimental evaluation, which is not thorough enough to fully support the paper's claims and falls below the standard expected at NIPS.

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:** A major concern is the experimental methodology. For the deterministic algorithms (SOO, BaMSOO, IMGPO), only a single run is performed. For the stochastic baselines (GP-PI, GP-EI), the authors report means and standard deviations over 50 runs but state they use a single random initialization for the first evaluation point. Standard practice in Bayesian optimization is to use multiple initial design points (e.g., 3-5) and/or run the entire optimization process from multiple different random seeds to ensure the results are robust and not an artifact of a lucky (or unlucky) starting point. Without this, it is difficult to make statistically meaningful comparisons or assess the algorithm's sensitivity to initialization.

2.  **Limited and Outdated Baselines:** The paper compares IMGPO to SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-EI/PI. While these are relevant, the comparison is incomplete. By 2015, the state-of-the-art in Bayesian optimization included more advanced, information-theoretic acquisition functions like Entropy Search (ES) and Predictive Entropy Search (PES). These methods often outperform UCB, EI, and PI. A convincing experimental section would need to demonstrate a clear advantage over these stronger, more contemporary baselines.

3.  **Lack of Real-World Application:** All experiments are conducted on synthetic benchmark functions. While these are useful for controlled comparisons, the abstract and introduction motivate the work with applications like parameter tuning and engineering design. The significance of the work would be substantially strengthened by demonstrating its effectiveness on at least one real-world problem, such as hyperparameter tuning for a standard machine learning model on a real dataset. The 1000-dimensional experiment is interesting, but it uses IMGPO as a subroutine within REMBO, so it doesn't demonstrate the scalability of IMGPO itself, but rather its utility as a component in a different high-dimensional strategy.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a theoretically strong and interesting contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and the writing is clear. However, the experimental validation is weak and does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of practical superiority. The lack of robust experimental methodology, the omission of state-of-the-art baselines, and the confinement to synthetic problems make it difficult to assess the true practical significance of the proposed algorithm. While the theoretical ideas are compelling, the empirical results are not currently convincing enough for a top-tier conference. I would be willing to reconsider my evaluation if the authors can provide a more rigorous and comprehensive experimental comparison in their rebuttal.
