

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations in previous approaches: GP-UCB [1] requires impractical δ-cover sampling, while BaMSOO [2] only achieves polynomial regret bounds. The core insight is to consider "all possible bounds" rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior, leveraging the existence of an unknown semi-metric bound at the global optimizer. This approach maintains exponential convergence while eliminating the impractical requirements of prior methods.

**Quality.** The paper provides a technically sound theoretical analysis with Theorem 1 establishing an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) where λ < 1, significantly improving upon BaMSOO's polynomial bound. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate IMGPO's superiority over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight benchmark functions. The analysis of how the tightness of the GP upper bound affects performance (Remarks 1-3) is particularly insightful. Minor technical issues exist (redundant abstract formulation in line 49, misplaced parenthesis in line 72), but these do not undermine the main contributions.

**Clarity.** The paper excels in clarity and organization. The explanation of the algorithm is exceptionally didactic, with Figure 1 providing an excellent visual illustration of the hierarchical partitioning process. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear annotations for each step. The authors effectively use mathematical notation while maintaining accessibility for readers unfamiliar with Gaussian processes. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented in an intuitive manner.

**Originality.** The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by proposing the "infinite-metric" approach that considers all possible bounds rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior. This distinguishes the work from GP-UCB (which relies on a single bound) and BaMSOO (which combines GP-UCB with SOO). The authors properly contextualize their work against prior literature, acknowledging the limitations of previous approaches and how their method addresses them. The theoretical framework for understanding when considering multiple bounds is advantageous (Remarks 1-3) represents a significant conceptual contribution.

**Significance.** This work addresses a fundamental limitation in Bayesian optimization that has been identified as an open problem [2]. The exponential convergence rate is a substantial theoretical improvement over previous methods, with practical implications for applications where function evaluations are expensive. The paper's broader contribution is a new perspective on bound-based optimization methods, suggesting that considering multiple bounds may be advantageous even when a good bound is known. The authors' analysis provides practitioners with a theoretical framework to understand when their approach would be beneficial, which is valuable for real-world applications.

**Strengths.** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations, effective use of figures, and thorough theoretical and experimental validation. The experimental results consistently show IMGPO outperforming baselines across diverse benchmark functions. The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the theoretical implications and practical considerations (e.g., the effect of Ξmax on performance).

**Weaknesses.** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper does not fully address how the algorithm scales to very high dimensions (D > 100), which is a common challenge in Bayesian optimization. The authors note this limitation in Remark 1, suggesting that additional assumptions would be needed for higher-dimensional problems, but a more detailed discussion of potential scaling strategies would strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation.** This paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization by resolving a long-standing open problem (achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling) while providing a novel theoretical framework for understanding bound-based optimization. The combination of strong theoretical results, thorough experimental validation, and clear exposition makes this a compelling contribution to NIPS. The minor technical issues noted do not detract from the paper's overall quality.

The authors have effectively bridged a gap between theoretical analysis and practical implementation, providing a method that is both theoretically sound and empirically superior to existing approaches. The paper's insights about considering multiple bounds rather than relying on a single bound have broader implications for optimization algorithms beyond Bayesian methods.

I recommend accepting this paper. It represents a meaningful advance in Bayesian optimization with clear theoretical and practical significance, and the writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized.
