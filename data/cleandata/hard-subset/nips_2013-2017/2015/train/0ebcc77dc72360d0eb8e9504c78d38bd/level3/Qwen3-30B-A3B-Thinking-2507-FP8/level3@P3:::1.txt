

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential regret without requiring auxiliary optimization or delta-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of prior work: de Freitas et al.'s (2012) method requires impractical delta-cover sampling, while standard GP-UCB methods require solving an additional non-convex optimization problem. The paper positions IMGPO within the broader bounded-based search literature, connecting it to Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and the SOO algorithm (Munos, 2011), while demonstrating how it overcomes limitations of prior approaches.

The theoretical contribution is strong: the authors prove an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, which improves upon BaMSOO's polynomial regret bound. The key insight is to consider "infinitely many possible bounds" rather than relying on a single upper bound from the GP prior, which the authors formalize through Assumption 1 (Unknown Bound). The proof techniques build on Munos' SOO framework while incorporating GP properties, and the analysis provides valuable theoretical insights about when relying on a single bound versus considering multiple bounds is advantageous (Remarks 1-3).

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization, precise mathematical formulations, and a helpful illustrative figure (Figure 1). The authors carefully distinguish their approach from related work, particularly BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2013), and provide a thorough comparison of their regret bound with previous results.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, providing the first GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or delta-cover sampling.
2. The paper clearly explains the intuition behind the algorithm's design and provides rigorous theoretical analysis.
3. The writing is exceptionally clear, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
4. The authors demonstrate thoughtful consideration of the relationship between their work and prior literature.

**Weaknesses:**
The experimental section falls significantly below NIPS standards. First, the paper does not use multiple initial evaluations of f (standard practice in Bayesian optimization), preventing meaningful statistical comparisons. The results are reported as single runs (Figure 2), with no error bars or statistical significance testing. This is particularly problematic since Bayesian optimization methods are typically evaluated over multiple random initializations.

Second, the paper lacks comparison with state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods. Notably absent are comparisons with information-theoretic approaches like Entropy Search (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014) and other modern BO methods that have become standard benchmarks in the field. The authors only compare against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, which are not the current state-of-the-art.

Third, all experiments are conducted on synthetic functions with maximum dimension 6. The paper includes a 1000-dimensional embedding of Sin2, but this is merely a demonstration of potential scalability with additional assumptions (REMBIO), not a real-world evaluation. There are no experiments on real-world applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models, wet-lab experiments, or engineering design problems) that would demonstrate practical utility.

**Quality:** The theoretical contribution is technically sound and well-supported, but the experimental evaluation is inadequate for a NIPS paper. The paper does not sufficiently address the weaknesses of its approach (e.g., the effect of Ξmax on performance).

**Clarity:** The paper excels in clarity, with excellent organization and exposition.

**Originality:** The approach of considering infinitely many bounds represents a novel and valuable theoretical insight that generalizes previous work.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is significant, but the lack of meaningful experimental validation limits the paper's impact. The absence of real-world evaluations and comparisons with modern BO methods prevents a strong assessment of the algorithm's practical significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Strong theoretical contribution with a novel perspective on bounded-based search
- Well-written paper that clearly positions the work within the literature
- Theoretical insights about when not relying on a single bound is beneficial are valuable

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Experimental section fails to meet NIPS standards (no multiple runs, no comparison with modern BO methods, no real-world experiments)
- Theoretical novelty is not adequately validated with meaningful experiments
- The paper's claims about "exponential convergence" are not substantiated with sufficient empirical evidence

The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to Bayesian optimization that addresses two important limitations of prior work. However, the experimental section is severely lacking and fails to demonstrate the practical value of the method. The authors would need to substantially improve the experimental evaluation to meet NIPS standards, including multiple initializations, comparisons with modern BO methods, and real-world applications.

Given the strong theoretical contribution, I would rate this paper a 5 if not for the experimental weaknesses. The experimental section is so inadequate that it significantly undermines the paper's scientific contribution. I therefore lower my score to a 4 (weak acceptance) due to the insufficient experimental validation, which is particularly concerning for a paper claiming exponential convergence results.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Add multiple random initializations for all experiments with proper statistical reporting
2. Include comparisons with modern Bayesian optimization methods (e.g., Entropy Search, Predictive Entropy Search)
3. Add at least one real-world application (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for a standard ML model) to demonstrate practical utility
4. Provide a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between Ξmax and performance

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is limited to theoretical novelty without sufficient empirical validation, which is insufficient for a top-tier machine learning conference like NIPS.
