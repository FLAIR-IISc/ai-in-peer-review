This paper introduces a new Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm, IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization – limitations of prior work like de Freitas et al. (2012). The authors position their work within the broader context of bound-based search methods, drawing connections to Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and PAC-MDP algorithms. The core idea is to leverage the existence of an unknown bound on the function’s smoothness, and to explore a wider range of potential bounds simultaneously, informed by the GP prior.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The motivation is well-articulated, the algorithm is described in a logical manner, and the theoretical analysis is presented with sufficient detail. The relationship to existing literature, particularly within the Bayesian optimization and bound-based search communities, is thoroughly discussed. The inclusion of a publicly available code repository is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility.

However, despite the theoretical appeal and clarity of presentation, I believe the experimental evaluation significantly weakens the paper, leading me to lower my score from a potential 5 to a 3. My primary concerns stem from the experimental setup and the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art BO methods.

Firstly, the experiments lack multiple initial evaluations of the objective function. Standard practice in Bayesian optimization is to perform several "warm-up" runs to mitigate the impact of random initialization and obtain more robust performance estimates. The absence of this is crucial; without it, observed performance differences might simply be due to luck rather than algorithmic superiority. This prevents meaningful statistical comparisons and casts doubt on the reported results.

Secondly, and perhaps more critically, the paper does not compare IMGPO against contemporary, state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization techniques. While comparisons to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI are included, the field has seen considerable advancement since those methods. The omission of information-theoretic approaches like Entropy Search (Hernandez-Lobato et al., 2014) and more recent developments in GP-based BO (e.g., methods incorporating deep Gaussian processes) is a significant oversight. Demonstrating superiority over established baselines is essential for establishing the contribution of a new algorithm.

Furthermore, the experiments are limited to synthetic functions, with a maximum dimensionality of 6. While these functions serve as useful testbeds for algorithm development, they lack the complexities and nuances of real-world optimization problems. The absence of evaluations on practical problems – such as hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models or parameter fitting in biological systems (as mentioned in the introduction) – hinders the assessment of IMGPO’s applicability and scalability. A demonstration of performance on a "wet-lab" or practical optimization task would greatly strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with an exponential convergence guarantee.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the algorithm and its underlying principles.
*   Novel approach to Bayesian optimization that avoids δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization.
*   Well-positioned within the literature of bound-based search methods.
*   Publicly available code.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak experimental evaluation lacking multiple initial evaluations.
*   Missing comparisons to state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods.
*   Limited to synthetic functions with low dimensionality, hindering assessment of real-world applicability.
*   The analysis, while thorough, doesn’t fully address scalability concerns in higher dimensions.



In conclusion, IMGPO presents a theoretically interesting contribution to Bayesian optimization. However, the lack of a comprehensive experimental evaluation prevents a strong endorsement. While the paper demonstrates a solid understanding of the theoretical landscape and offers a potentially valuable algorithm, it falls short of the experimental standards expected for a NIPS publication. Addressing the weaknesses in the experimental section – by including multiple initializations, comparisons to contemporary BO methods, and evaluations on real-world problems – would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
