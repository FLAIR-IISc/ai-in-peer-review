This paper proposes a novel Bayesian global optimization method, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves an exponential convergence rate without requiring auxiliary optimization or the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure. The work builds upon and synthesizes two distinct lines of research in global optimization: bound-based methods that estimate continuity (like GP-UCB) and those that do not assume a known smoothness constant (like DIRECT and SOO). By simultaneously considering an infinite set of potential semi-metrics and using the GP's upper confidence bound (UCB) to prune this set, the authors bridge a critical gap identified in previous work (e.g., Wang et al., 2014), which noted that a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling remained an open problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The core contribution is highly original and significant. It presents the first GP-based method to achieve an exponential convergence rate without the major practical limitations of prior art. This is a substantial theoretical and practical advance for the Bayesian optimization community.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with its claims well-supported by both theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) and compelling experimental results. The analysis in the remarks section, which thoughtfully discusses the trade-offs of the approach (e.g., the effect of a loose GP bound), is particularly insightful. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate superior performance against relevant baselines across several benchmark functions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide excellent intuition through a clear illustrative example in Section 3.2, and the programmatic details in Section 3.3 and the provided pseudocode are sufficient to facilitate replication.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The paper's main weakness, which the authors rightly acknowledge, is its scalability with respect to input dimensionality. The convergence rate's dependency on dimension D suggests performance may degrade in high-dimensional settings, a common challenge for methods relying on hierarchical partitioning. The high-dimensional experiment relies on a random embedding technique (REMBO), which confirms that IMGPO is best suited as a powerful subroutine for such problems rather than a direct high-dimensional solver.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that represents a strong contribution. The novel integration of ideas, strong theoretical guarantees, and solid empirical results far outweigh the noted limitation. It addresses a difficult open problem and is likely to be highly influential.
