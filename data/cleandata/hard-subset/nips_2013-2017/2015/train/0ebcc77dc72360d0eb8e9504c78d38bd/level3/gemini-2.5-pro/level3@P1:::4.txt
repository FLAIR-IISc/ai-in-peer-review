This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, named Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to solve non-convex black-box global optimization problems. The primary contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret without requiring two common but problematic components of previous methods: auxiliary non-convex optimization to select the next query point, and an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure.

The work is situated within the context of Gaussian Process (GP) based optimization, particularly building on the GP-UCB framework. The authors relate their work to prior art that either requires auxiliary optimization (e.g., standard GP-UCB), relies on the impractical δ-cover sampling to achieve exponential convergence (de Freitas et al., 2014), or achieves only polynomial regret bounds when avoiding these issues (Wang et al., 2015, with BaMSOO). The core idea of IMGPO is to combine the strengths of two distinct lines of global optimization research. It uses a hierarchical partitioning scheme similar to algorithms like DIRECT and SOO, which implicitly consider an infinite family of possible Lipschitz-like bounds (semi-metrics). It then intelligently uses the GP posterior, specifically the UCB, not to guide the search directly via an expensive optimization, but rather to prune the set of promising regions identified by the hierarchical partitioning. This novel combination allows the algorithm to exploit the best of both worlds: the robustness of considering all possible bounds and the informative guidance from the GP model.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clearly stated open problem in the literature: achieving exponential convergence in GP-based optimization without δ-cover sampling. The proposed solution is both novel and significant. By elegantly merging the "infinite-metric" approach of DIRECT/SOO with the probabilistic bounds from a GP, the authors have developed a method that is theoretically powerful and practically more appealing than its predecessors. This synthesis of two previously separate research paths is a key original contribution.

2.  **Quality:** The work is technically sound and well-supported.
    *   **Theory:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, formally establishes an exponential regret bound, improving upon the polynomial bounds of comparable practical algorithms like BaMSOO. The analysis provided in Remarks 1-3 is particularly insightful, offering a clear intuition for how the GP component helps (by reducing the "infinite-metric exploration loss"), why not relying on a single, potentially poor, bound is beneficial, and how the tightness of the GP's UCB affects performance.
    *   **Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is convincing. The authors compare IMGPO against a strong set of relevant baselines (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) across a diverse suite of eight benchmark functions, varying in dimensionality and modality. The results in Figure 2 clearly demonstrate that IMGPO generally outperforms these other methods, often by a significant margin. The inclusion of a high-dimensional experiment using REMBO also points towards the potential utility of IMGPO as a subroutine in more complex settings. The reporting of computation time in Table 1 adds practical context, showing the method is faster than traditional GP optimization methods that require auxiliary optimization.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and didactic. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction. The core concept is explained intuitively in Section 3.1 before the detailed algorithmic description. Figure 1 is an outstanding visual aid, making the complex dynamics of the algorithm's partitioning and selection steps easy to follow. The provision of detailed pseudocode in Algorithm 1 further enhances clarity and aids in reproducibility.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

The paper is of high quality, and the weaknesses are minor, pertaining mostly to presentation details that can be easily addressed.

1.  The formulation of the optimization problem in the abstract ("maximize f(x) subject to...") feels slightly redundant, as this is standard context for the field and could be streamlined to more quickly highlight the paper's specific contributions.

2.  There appears to be a small typo in the definition of the `fsin1` function in Section 5. The expression is given as `fsin1(x) = (sin(13x) sin+1)/2`. The authors should clarify the intended mathematical form.

3.  The authors rightly acknowledge the challenge of scalability with respect to input dimension D, as reflected in the convergence rate λ. While the 1000-dimensional experiment with REMBO is a good proof of concept, a brief discussion on the performance characteristics or limitations for moderately high-dimensional problems (e.g., D=10-50) without additional structural assumptions might add further practical context for the reader.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It solves a recognized open problem with a novel and elegant approach, supported by rigorous analysis and compelling experimental results. The clarity of the presentation makes the work accessible and the insights valuable. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the contribution.
