This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization method that achieves exponential convergence without the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling, generalizing the work of Freitas et al. (2012). The proposed algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), leverages the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer and simultaneously conducts global and local searches based on all possible candidates of the bounds.

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and clearly positions the algorithm within the bounded-based search literature. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's properties and prove an exponential convergence rate of O ( λN+Ngp ) with λ < 1. The theoretical contributions are interesting, and the authors provide a practically oriented analysis framework that enables understanding of why not relying on a particular bound is advantageous.

However, the experimental evaluation is below the conference standard. The experiments do not use multiple initial evaluations of f (standard in Bayesian optimization), preventing meaningful statistical comparisons. Moreover, there are no comparisons with state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods (e.g., information-theoretic approaches like Entropy Search). All experiments are on synthetic functions (maximum dimension 6), and there are no real-world (wet-lab or parameter tuning) evaluations.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper has several strengths. The algorithm is novel and has the potential to advance the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm's properties. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper scores high on originality, as it introduces a new algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling. The paper also scores high on quality, as the theoretical analysis is thorough and the algorithm is well-motivated. However, the paper scores low on significance, as the experimental evaluation is limited and does not provide a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

Overall, I would give this paper a score of 4. The algorithm is novel and has the potential to advance the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. However, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
