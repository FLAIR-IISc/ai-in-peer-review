Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a GP-based global optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box functions that (i) avoids the impractical δ-cover sampling used by de Freitas et al. (ICML’12) to obtain exponential regret, and (ii) does not require auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function at each step (as in GP-UCB/EI/PI). The key idea is to combine hierarchical partitioning (in the spirit of DIRECT and SOO) with upper confidence bounds from a GP. Instead of committing to a single, possibly loose, known bound, the method assumes the existence of some unknown, tighter semi-metric bound around a global optimizer and effectively hedges across infinitely many candidates, while using GP-UCB values to prune candidates that are looser than what the GP currently certifies. The algorithm adaptively divides hyperrectangles, evaluates either true function values or temporarily substitutes GP-UCB values, and updates GP hyperparameters by empirical Bayes.

Theoretically, under Assumption 1 (existence of an unknown semi-metric bound around x*) and a growth condition (Assumption 2), the authors establish an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 with probability ≥ 1 − η, improving over BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS’14), which achieved polynomial rates when combining SOO with GP-UCB. The analysis clarifies how the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρ̄t, the depth parameter Ξn, and GP usage (Ngp) influence the rate and constants, and it discusses dimensionality effects and connections to DIRECT/SOO/GP-UCB.

Empirically, IMGPO is compared to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on synthetic benchmarks (1–6D) and a 1000D synthetic task using REMBO with IMGPO/BaMSOO as subroutines. Using a Matern 5/2 kernel with empirical Bayes hyperparameter fitting, IMGPO generally outperforms baselines in regret versus evaluations, while being faster than standard GP-EI/PI but slower than SOO.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-organized presentation that positions the contribution within bound-based search (DIRECT, SOO, GP-UCB) and explains the intuition behind “infinite-metric” exploration.
- Interesting theoretical contribution: an exponential simple-regret bound without δ-cover sampling and without an auxiliary non-convex inner optimization, improving on BaMSOO’s polynomial rates. The role of ρ̄t, Ξn, and dimensionality is explicitly discussed.
- Algorithmic design is practical: deterministic tree-based partitioning, pruning via GP-UCB to avoid unnecessary function evaluations, and public code release.
- The analysis carefully articulates when and how GP information helps, and why not committing to a single bound can be beneficial.

Weaknesses:
- Experimental evaluation is below the conference standard for BO:
  • No comparison to information-theoretic state-of-the-art (Entropy Search; Predictive Entropy Search), Thompson sampling variants, or popular practical systems (e.g., SMAC, Spearmint) on standard BO tasks.
  • All experiments are synthetic; there are no real-world applications (hyperparameter tuning, engineering design, wet-lab).
  • The setup does not use standard BO practice of multiple initial evaluations/designs. Some methods are randomly initialized for one point, others are deterministic; this prevents robust statistical comparison and may bias outcomes.
  • Acquisition optimizers for EI/PI rely on SOO and a local method; fairness and computational budgets across methods are not fully controlled or reported.
- Assumptions for the theory (unknown semi-metric bound around x*) and the high-probability UCB guarantee can be sensitive to data-driven kernel hyperparameter updates; the paper acknowledges cases where the GP bound is violated (Rosenbrock). The theoretical implications of empirical Bayes (and hyperparameter misspecification) are not explored.
- The “exponential” rate can have a base λ close to 1 in higher dimensions; more quantitative guidance on the magnitude of constants and practical regimes would help.

Quality:
- Theoretical development appears sound and novel relative to prior BO theory without δ-cover sampling. The proof sketch is coherent, though the guarantees rely on nontrivial constants (ρ̄t, Ξn) and on the GP-UCB property under empirical Bayes.
- Empirical support is limited in scope and comparators. While synthetic results are promising, they do not convincingly establish practical superiority.

Clarity:
- Very well written. The algorithm, intuition, and connections to DIRECT/SOO/GP-UCB are clearly explained. Pseudocode is detailed. A few implementation choices (e.g., handling of GP-based labels, complexity of step (iii), tuning Ξmax) could be summarized more crisply with complexity/budget guidance.

Originality:
- The combination of infinite-metric exploration with GP pruning and the resulting exponential bound without δ-cover sampling is original and addresses an open question highlighted by Wang et al. (AISTATS’14). The analysis framing (ρ̄t, Ξn, Ngp) is insightful.

Significance:
- Potentially significant for deterministic BO: removes an impractical requirement while preserving fast rates, and removes auxiliary acquisition optimization. However, due to the limited empirical section and lack of real-world validations/comparisons to stronger baselines, the immediate practical impact is not yet demonstrated.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a clear theoretical gap (exponential regret without δ-cover) with a concrete, implementable algorithm.
- Insightful analysis clarifies when GP information is useful and how hedging over bounds helps.
- Public code and generally strong performance on synthetic benchmarks.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to established state-of-the-art BO methods (Entropy Search/PES, Thompson sampling) and practical systems on real tasks.
- Experimental protocol lacks standard BO practices (initial designs, multiple seeds/repeats) and does not provide statistically robust conclusions.
- Theoretical guarantees depend on assumptions that may be brittle in practice with empirical Bayes; no ablations to test sensitivity.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add experiments on real tasks (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for ML models, robotics/controllers, or engineering design) with wall-clock and evaluation budgets.
- Compare against Entropy Search (NIPS’12), Predictive Entropy Search (ICML’14), and Thompson sampling; include GP-UCB with competent acquisition optimization; use standard initial designs (e.g., 5–10 Latin hypercube points), multiple seeds, and report mean/variance.
- Provide ablations: effect of Ξmax, disabling GP pruning (to isolate Ngp and ρ̄t impacts), fixed vs. re-estimated hyperparameters, ARD vs. isotropic kernels.
- Clarify the theoretical status of updating hyperparameters and whether guarantees can be extended to data-dependent kernels.
- Report computational budgets and complexity per evaluation; ensure fairness in acquisition optimization across methods.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive is IMGPO to kernel/hyperparameter misspecification? Can the guarantees be adapted to empirical Bayes or priors over hyperparameters?
- How does performance scale with dimension beyond the REMBO setup? Any empirical characterization of λ or ρ̄t versus D?
- Could the method be extended to noisy observations?

Overall recommendation:
- Score: 5/10 (Weak reject). The theoretical contribution is interesting and the paper is clearly written, but the experimental evaluation is insufficient to support acceptance at NIPS. Strengthening the empirical section as suggested could raise this to a strong accept.
