## Review of “Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence”

This paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP)-based Bayesian optimization algorithm achieving exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing limitations of previous work by de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric bound alongside the GP prior, performing both global and local searches to refine optimization. The method, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), aims to avoid the impracticality of δ-cover sampling while maintaining a strong theoretical guarantee.

The paper is well-written and didactic, effectively explaining the motivation, algorithm, and analysis. The use of Figure 1 to illustrate the hierarchical partitioning procedure is particularly helpful in understanding the algorithm’s mechanics. The theoretical results, including Theorem 1, convincingly demonstrate the exponential convergence rate and are supported by experimental results on a variety of benchmark functions. The comparison to SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP-based methods (EI, PI) highlights the advantages of the proposed approach, especially in scenarios where a precise Lipschitz constant is unavailable. The authors thoughtfully acknowledge potential limitations concerning scalability and suggest future directions.

However, the abstract is somewhat redundant, stating the core contribution without providing specific details. A more concise abstract focusing on the key novelty (handling the unknown semi-metric) would be more effective (line 49). Additionally, there's a misplaced parenthesis in the function definition (line 72) which requires correction. 

My own familiarity with Gaussian Processes and global optimization is limited, so my evaluation is necessarily an educated guess. However, the paper provides sufficient context and clarity for a non-expert to grasp the core ideas and assess the technical soundness of the approach. The extensive referencing to relevant literature, including NIPS proceedings ([3, 13, 14, 15]), demonstrates a strong understanding of the field. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical guarantees: Exponential convergence rate without problematic requirements.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation:  The paper is easy to understand, even for those less familiar with the specific techniques.
*   Effective use of visualizations: Figure 1 significantly enhances understanding.
*   Convincing experimental validation: Demonstrates superior performance on various benchmark functions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Abstract is redundant and could be more focused.
*   Minor technical error (misplaced parenthesis) needs to be corrected.
*   Scalability limitations need further investigation.



**Overall:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The strengths of the work – its theoretical rigor, clarity of presentation, and strong experimental validation – outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with the request that the authors address the issues with the abstract and the parenthesis.
