

This paper presents Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization method that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of existing methods: GP-UCB's need for additional non-convex optimization and de Freitas et al.'s reliance on impractical δ-cover sampling. IMGPO innovatively considers all possible candidates of unknown bounds (via a semi-metric) rather than relying on a single bound from the GP prior, while using the GP's known bound to prune less promising candidates. The authors prove an exponential regret bound and demonstrate consistent empirical superiority over state-of-the-art methods including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight benchmark functions.

The paper excels in clarity and presentation. It is well-written with a clear didactic structure, effectively using graphs to explain the algorithm's operation (particularly Figure 1). The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Theorem 1 establishing the exponential convergence rate and Remark 1-3 providing insightful discussions about the algorithm's behavior under different conditions. The experimental section thoroughly compares IMGPO against relevant baselines, with results consistently showing its advantages.

Minor weaknesses include a redundant abstract formulation (line 49) and a misplaced parenthesis in a function definition (line 72). As a reviewer unfamiliar with Gaussian Processes and global optimization, my evaluation of the technical claims is an educated guess based on the paper's presentation, though the theoretical arguments appear sound. The paper effectively addresses a practical problem in Bayesian optimization, eliminating two significant implementation barriers of previous methods.

The work represents a meaningful contribution to the field, combining ideas from DIRECT, SOO, and GP-UCB in a novel way that achieves both theoretical and empirical advantages. The exponential convergence rate without impractical requirements is a significant theoretical advance that also has practical implications for real-world optimization problems. The paper is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in Bayesian optimization, with clear implications for both theoretical understanding and practical implementation.

Overall, this paper demonstrates high quality, originality, and significance. The strengths substantially outweigh the minor technical issues noted. I recommend acceptance.
