Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes IMGPO, a Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence for deterministic black-box global optimization without relying on δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization of an acquisition function. The key idea is to combine hierarchical partitioning with a “bound-based” search over an infinite family of potential upper bounds implied by an unknown semi-metric around the global optimum (Assumption 1), while using the GP posterior UCB only to prune overly loose candidates. The algorithm thereby avoids committing to a single, potentially loose bound and instead exploits whichever bound is tight in the region near the optimum. The authors provide a high-probability exponential regret bound O(λN+Ngp) (λ < 1), with explicit dependence on Hölder parameters (L, α, p) of the unknown semi-metric (Assumption 2), and analyze the roles of the infinite-metric exploration loss ρt, the number of GP “virtual” evaluations Ngp, and the pruning depth parameter Ξ.

Conceptually, this work unifies two lines of research: (i) bound-based partitioning methods without known smoothness (DIRECT; DOO/SOO), and (ii) GP-based Bayesian optimization (GP-UCB, EI/PI). It improves over BaMSOO (which couples GP-UCB with SOO) by removing the need to optimize an acquisition and by obtaining an exponential (rather than polynomial) regret bound under deterministic observations, while sidestepping the δ-cover requirement in de Freitas et al.’s exponential regret result. Empirically, IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on several multimodal benchmarks and a high-dimensional REMBO setting.

Quality
- Theory: The paper states a clear set of assumptions, provides a main theorem with exponential convergence under mild Hölder-type continuity near the optimum, and offers insightful remarks quantifying when leveraging GP UCB pruning helps, and how the infinite-metric approach compares to relying on a single, possibly misspecified bound. The dependence on dimension is transparent (λ approaches 1 as D grows), appropriately noting limitations in high dimensions. One possible gap is the use of empirical Bayes for hyperparameters; it would help to discuss conditions under which the UCB coverage property holds or degrades when hyperparameters are estimated online.
- Experiments: The comparisons are fair in that kernels/hyperparameters are not tuned per function, and code availability is a plus. IMGPO is consistently strong; SOO wins on Rosenbrock2 when GP priors are misleading, which is candidly discussed. It would strengthen the paper to include: (a) sensitivity analyses to Ξmax, kernel choice, and η; (b) an ablation showing IMGPO without GP-pruning (to isolate the gain from Ngp and ρ̄t reductions); and (c) a GP-UCB baseline with a competent global optimizer for the acquisition to quantify the practical benefit of avoiding auxiliary optimization.
- Reproducibility: The algorithm is fully specified with pseudocode and implementation details; figures are didactic and clarify the procedure.

Clarity
The paper is very well written and didactic. The figures (notably Figure 1) effectively illustrate interval selection, pruning, and deferred evaluations via GP UCB. The regret analysis highlights intuitions (effects of tight/loose UCB, infinite-metric loss) that aid understanding. Minor issues:
- Abstract: a phrasing around the removal of δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization is redundant (line 49); consider tightening.
- Notation/typos: there is a misplaced parenthesis in the kernel expression (line 72); also a minor “the the” duplication appears once in the algorithm description.
- Clarify constants: define C where first introduced and summarize its typical values inline.
- Complexity: add a brief time-complexity discussion of step (iii) in terms of Ξmax, dimension, and current tree size.

Originality
The central idea—searching over infinitely many candidate bounds while using GP UCB purely as a pruning signal—is novel in the GP optimization literature and provides a principled way to bridge DIRECT/SOO-style partitioning with GP posteriors. The theoretical result (exponential convergence without δ-cover or acquisition optimization) is a meaningful advance over prior GP-based methods for deterministic settings.

Significance
This work addresses a practical bottleneck in GP BO (auxiliary acquisition optimization) and improves theoretical guarantees beyond BaMSOO. Practitioners can benefit from a method that is competitive or superior empirically while being simpler to deploy (no global solver for the acquisition). The analysis may inspire analogous “infinite-metric” strategies in planning and RL algorithms based on optimistic bounds.

Pros
- Clear, original algorithmic idea that removes auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling.
- Strong, well-motivated theory with exponential regret and insightful remarks about when and why the approach helps.
- Convincing experiments across diverse benchmarks; code availability.
- Excellent exposition and effective figures.

Cons
- Dependence on dimension remains challenging; more discussion or experiments on scaling would help.
- UCB coverage under empirical Bayes is not fully justified; clarify robustness when the GP prior is misspecified (as in Rosenbrock2).
- Limited sensitivity/ablation studies; include analyses of Ξmax, η, kernel choice, and the role of Ngp/ρ̄t.
- Minor writing/notation issues (see below).

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify the impact of hyperparameter learning on the high-probability UCB guarantee used in Theorem 1; if the guarantee is heuristic in practice, state it and indicate any practical safeguards.
- Provide a short complexity bound for step (iii) and guidance on setting Ξmax versus computation budget.
- Report or plot empirical proxies for ρ̄t and Ngp to validate the theoretical narrative (e.g., how pruning reduces ρ̄t over time).
- Add a GP-UCB+multistart/DIRECT acquisition solver baseline to strengthen the empirical case for removing auxiliary optimization.
- Minor fixes:
  - Abstract redundancy at line 49.
  - Misplaced parenthesis in the Gaussian kernel definition at line 72.
  - Minor duplicate word in the algorithm description.

Reviewer expertise disclaimer
I am not a specialist in Gaussian processes or global optimization; my assessment of the theoretical contributions is an informed reading rather than an expert verification.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is well written and instructive, presents a novel and practically relevant idea with solid theoretical backing, and demonstrates compelling empirical performance. With minor clarifications and fixes, it will be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
