This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), for globally optimizing deterministic black-box functions. The core idea is to combine a hierarchical, interval-partitioning scheme with a Gaussian Process (GP) model. This approach cleverly avoids the need for a separate, often difficult, inner-loop optimization of the acquisition function, which is a standard step in many GP-based optimization methods like GP-UCB. The algorithm selects which interval to partition next based on a combination of the function values at the interval centers and the GP-derived Upper Confidence Bound (UCB).

The work relates to and aims to improve upon several lines of research. It builds on hierarchical partitioning methods like DIRECT and SOO, which do not require an explicit smoothness constant. It also advances the state of the art in provable GP optimization, improving on the polynomial regret bounds of BaMSOO and achieving an exponential convergence rate without the impractical δ-cover sampling assumption required by de Freitas et al. (2012).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is a novel algorithm that elegantly unifies two distinct approaches to global optimization: the partitioning strategy of DIRECT/SOO and the probabilistic modeling of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical result—proving an exponential convergence rate for simple regret without requiring an inner optimization loop or δ-cover sampling—is a significant advance for GP optimization theory.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes the exponential regret bound. The introduction of the "infinite-metric exploration loss" is an insightful concept for analyzing why considering a family of potential function smoothnesses (as in DIRECT) is beneficial. The analysis clearly demonstrates how the GP-UCB bound helps prune the search space and improve the convergence rate compared to a purely partitioning-based method.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, and the algorithm is clearly described through both pseudocode and a step-by-step illustrative example on a 1D function (Figure 1). This example is particularly effective at building intuition for the interplay between the partitioning rules and the UCB-based checks.

4.  **Practical Advantage:** By design, the algorithm circumvents the need to solve an auxiliary global optimization problem at each step to maximize the acquisition function. This is a well-known practical bottleneck for many Bayesian optimization methods, and its removal makes the proposed approach more efficient and easier to implement.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation and Scalability:** The paper's most significant weakness is the experimental evaluation. While the benchmark functions listed are standard, the paper is not explicit about the dimensionality used in the core experiments. Functions like Sin1, Branin, and Hartmann3 are low-dimensional benchmarks, and the plots in Figure 2 are characteristic of performance on 1D or 2D problems. If the core IMGPO algorithm was only evaluated on 1D problems, this is a major limitation that is not adequately acknowledged. Performance in one dimension is rarely predictive of performance in higher dimensions.

2.  **Unaddressed High-Dimensionality Concerns:** The interval-partitioning approach is fundamentally related to the DIRECT algorithm. It is well-known that such methods scale poorly with dimension due to the "curse of dimensionality"—the number of partitions required to achieve a certain resolution grows exponentially. While the authors briefly acknowledge this (end of Section 4), the issue deserves a much more thorough discussion. The connection to DIRECT and its known struggles in more than a few dimensions should be explicitly stated and discussed as a major limitation of the proposed method for practical applications. While this concern does not invalidate the theoretical contribution, it casts significant doubt on the broader practical utility of IMGPO as a standalone algorithm.

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The use of UCB is justified "for brevity." It is unclear if the theoretical framework is fundamentally tied to UCB or if other acquisition criteria (e.g., Expected Improvement) could be substituted. A brief comment on this would strengthen the paper.
    *   The sentence in Section 3.1, "we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is somewhat opaque. It would be clearer to explain this concept in terms of the algorithm's mechanics—i.e., by partitioning at different scales (depths `h`), the algorithm implicitly considers different potential Lipschitz constants or smoothness properties.
    *   In the Figure 1 walkthrough (t=9, n=16), it is stated that the far-right interval has no function evaluation because the UCB was below the best-found value `f+`. It is not immediately obvious why this would be the case for that specific region. A slightly more detailed explanation of the state of the GP model at that step would make the illustration clearer.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The derivation of an exponential regret bound without an inner optimization loop is impressive. However, the practical significance of the work is questionable due to major, unaddressed concerns about its scalability to higher dimensions. The experimental evaluation appears to be confined to very low-dimensional problems, which is insufficient for a general-purpose optimization algorithm. The authors should be more transparent about the limitations of the partitioning approach and the dimensionality of their experiments.

While the scalability concerns are serious for practical use, the novelty and strength of the theoretical contribution make this a valuable paper for the NIPS community. It successfully bridges two important sub-fields of global optimization and provides a new analytical framework.
