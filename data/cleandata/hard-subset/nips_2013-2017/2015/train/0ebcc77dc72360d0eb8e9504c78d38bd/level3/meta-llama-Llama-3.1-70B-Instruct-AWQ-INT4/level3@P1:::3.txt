This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that achieves exponential convergence without the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling. The authors' main contributions are clearly and coherently presented in the main section, making it easy to follow their line of reasoning.

The paper starts by introducing the problem of global optimization and the challenges associated with it. The authors then provide a brief overview of the existing Bayesian optimization methods, highlighting their limitations, particularly the requirement of auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling. They then present their proposed method, IMGPO, which leverages the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer.

The algorithmic instantiation of IMGPO is presented in Section 3.2, which provides a strong illustrative example that conveys high-level intuition. The programmatic details are presented in Section 3.3, which facilitates implementation and replication. The authors also provide a relationship to previous algorithms, highlighting the differences and advantages of their proposed method.

The experimental results presented in Section 5 are compelling and demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The authors compare IMGPO with other state-of-the-art algorithms, including SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, and show that IMGPO outperforms them in most cases.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to integrate ideas from the Bayesian optimization literature both with and without continuity estimation. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their method. The writing is clear and concise, making it easy to follow their line of reasoning. The experimental results are well-presented and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors could provide more details on the computational complexity of their method, particularly in terms of the number of function evaluations required. Secondly, they could provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters and how they affect the performance of the method.

Overall, the paper represents a strong contribution to the NIPS community, and the authors should be commended for their work.
