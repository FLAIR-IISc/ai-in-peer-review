## Review of “Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence”

This paper presents a novel Bayesian Optimization (BO) method, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to achieve exponential convergence without relying on auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling—both limitations of existing approaches like de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to leverage hierarchical partitioning and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) exploration, effectively considering a wider range of potential Lipschitz constants through interval division. The authors provide a clear algorithm description, accompanied by an illustrative example. 

The work builds upon the established field of BO, specifically addressing the challenges of practical implementation highlighted by Wang et al. [2].  IMGPO distinguishes itself by avoiding the need for potentially intractable δ-cover sampling, a significant contribution. The theoretical analysis, demonstrating exponential regret improvements, further strengthens the paper’s value. The connection to related methods like DIRECT [19] is acknowledged, although, as discussed below, not explored deeply enough.

The paper’s strengths lie in its novelty, clarity of presentation, and theoretical grounding. The algorithm is well-explained, and the example helps visualize the interval partitioning process. The promise of exponential convergence is a strong result, and the elimination of auxiliary optimization is a practical benefit.

However, the primary weakness is the limited experimental evaluation. The results appear to be presented solely for 1D test functions, raising concerns about the method’s scalability to higher dimensions—a crucial aspect for real-world applications. This limitation is particularly worrying given the inherent exponential growth in resolution required for interval partitioning, reminiscent of challenges faced by DIRECT [19]. The authors should discuss this scalability issue more thoroughly, potentially exploring dimensionality reduction techniques or adaptive partitioning strategies. 

Furthermore, while the use of UCB is justified for brevity, it’s unclear whether other acquisition functions (e.g., Expected Improvement) could be similarly integrated. The sentence regarding "simultaneously conduct global and local searches" is also somewhat ambiguous and could benefit from clarification. Finally, the explanation for the lack of function evaluation in the far-right region at n=16 requires further elaboration.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel approach to BO with a strong theoretical guarantee (exponential convergence). Clear presentation and a well-defined algorithm. Addresses a practical limitation of existing BO methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Limited experimental validation (only 1D). Scalability concerns not adequately addressed. Potential connection to known limitations of DIRECT not fully discussed.



**Overall Score:** 7/10. While the theoretical contribution is sound and the paper presents a genuinely novel idea, the lack of higher-dimensional experiments significantly limits its impact. Addressing the scalability concerns and providing more comprehensive empirical evidence would substantially strengthen the paper.
