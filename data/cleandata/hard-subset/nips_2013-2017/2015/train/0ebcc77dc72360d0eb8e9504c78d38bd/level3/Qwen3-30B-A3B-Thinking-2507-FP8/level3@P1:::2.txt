

This paper introduces IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing limitations in previous approaches. The authors build upon the theoretical framework of de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], who demonstrated exponential convergence but relied on impractical δ-cover sampling. IMGPO innovatively leverages the existence of an unknown semi-metric bound (Assumption 1) to simultaneously conduct global and local searches across infinitely many potential bounds, avoiding the need for a single tight bound.

The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, improving upon BaMSOO's polynomial regret bound. The analysis (Remark 1-3) provides valuable theoretical insights into how the GP prior and infinite-metric exploration interact, explaining why considering multiple bounds can outperform relying on a single (potentially loose) bound. The algorithm description is clear with well-structured pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and a helpful illustrative example in Figure 1. The authors effectively contextualize their work against DIRECT [19], SOO [18], and BaMSOO [2], demonstrating how IMGPO bridges two theoretical paths in global optimization.

Strengths include:
- A significant theoretical advancement with a clean exponential convergence rate
- Clear connection to prior work and well-motivated problem formulation
- Comprehensive analysis showing how GP and infinite-metric exploration interact
- Well-structured algorithm description with illustrative example
- Practical implementation details (kernel choices, hyperparameter initialization)

Weaknesses include:
- Experimental validation appears limited to 1D test functions (only Sin1, Sin2, Branin, Hartmann3 shown in Figure 2, with Sin2 embedded in 1000D as a special case). The paper does not explicitly state the dimensionality of most experiments, raising concerns about scalability. While the authors mention a 1000D experiment using REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine, this does not demonstrate IMGPO's standalone performance in high dimensions.
- The paper does not adequately address scalability concerns for higher dimensions. The interval-partitioning approach inherently faces the curse of dimensionality, as the number of required intervals grows exponentially with dimension. The authors cite DIRECT's poor performance in high dimensions but fail to discuss whether their method inherits this limitation or how it might be mitigated (e.g., through additional assumptions like those in [14, 20]).
- Minor clarity issues: the description of "simultaneously conducting global and local searches based on all candidates of bounds" is somewhat vague; the explanation for why the far-right interval at n=16 has no function evaluation (Figure 1) is insufficient; and the paper states UCB is used "for brevity" but doesn't clarify whether other acquisition functions could be used.

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and novel, making a meaningful advancement in the theoretical understanding of Bayesian optimization. The exponential regret bound represents a clear improvement over previous work, and the analysis provides valuable insights into the relationship between GP priors and bound-based search methods. However, the experimental evaluation's apparent limitation to 1D problems significantly weakens the practical relevance of the contribution. While the theoretical contribution stands on its own, the field of Bayesian optimization is increasingly focused on high-dimensional applications, making scalability a critical concern that the authors do not adequately address.

The authors make a good case that their method avoids the impractical δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization, which are major practical barriers to implementing exponential convergence methods. The algorithm's implementation is straightforward, and the provided source code (http://lis.csail.mit.edu/code/imgpo.html) should facilitate reproducibility.

For acceptance considerations:
Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with clear exponential convergence; novel approach to combining multiple bounds; well-motivated problem; comprehensive analysis; clear algorithm description.
Con: Experimental validation limited to 1D; insufficient discussion of scalability to higher dimensions; minor clarity issues in description.

The paper's theoretical contribution is significant and advances the field, but the limited experimental evaluation and lack of discussion about high-dimensional scalability are substantial concerns. The authors should acknowledge that while the theoretical results are strong, the practical applicability to high-dimensional problems remains unproven, and they should discuss potential approaches to address scalability (e.g., using dimensionality reduction or additional assumptions). The paper would benefit from additional experiments on higher-dimensional problems or a more thorough discussion of the scalability limitations.

Given the strong theoretical contribution and the paper's clear presentation, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the scalability concerns in the final version. The theoretical results are sound and represent a meaningful advancement over previous work, but the experimental evaluation needs to better reflect the practical relevance of the method for the Bayesian optimization community, which increasingly focuses on high-dimensional problems. The paper would be strengthened by explicitly discussing the dimensionality limitations and potential paths to address them.
