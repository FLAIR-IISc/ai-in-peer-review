This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which achieves exponential convergence without the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling. The authors propose a hierarchical partitioning approach to maintain hyperintervals, leveraging the existence of an unknown bound encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer. The algorithm uses the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) to select the next query point, avoiding the internal global optimization problem.

The paper provides a clear and well-illustrated description of the algorithm, making it easy to follow. The theoretical analysis shows exponential-regret improvements over previous work, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the relationship between their algorithm and previous work, including the DIRECT algorithm and GP-UCB.

However, the experimental results appear to be limited to 1D test functions, which raises concerns about the scalability of the method to higher dimensions. The required resolution grows exponentially with the dimensionality, which may make the interval-partitioning approach impractical for high-dimensional problems. The authors should discuss this issue and provide some insights into how to address it.

Additionally, the paper mentions that the method is related to DIRECT, which is known to perform poorly in high dimensions. The authors should discuss this issue and explain how their method differs from DIRECT and how it can overcome the scalability limitations.

Some minor points that need clarification include:

* Whether UCB is required or if other criteria (e.g., expected improvement) could be used.
* The sentence about "simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is unclear.
* The reason why at n=16 the far-right region has no function evaluation.

Overall, the paper presents sound and novel work, but the evaluation appears limited to 1D problems. The authors should address the scalability concerns and provide more insights into how to apply the method to higher-dimensional problems.
