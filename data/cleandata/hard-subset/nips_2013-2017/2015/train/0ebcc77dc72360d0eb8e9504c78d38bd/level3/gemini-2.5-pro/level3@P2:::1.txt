This paper introduces a new algorithm for Bayesian optimization, called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), which aims to solve the general problem of globally optimizing a deterministic black-box function. The primary contribution is a method that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret without requiring two common but problematic components of previous methods: (1) an auxiliary non-convex optimization step to maximize an acquisition function, and (2) an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure, which was a key requirement for the exponential regret bound in de Freitas et al. (2012).

The proposed method combines a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, similar to the SOO algorithm (Munos, 2011), with a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. The core idea is to operate as if there exists an unknown, true semi-metric that bounds the function's smoothness. Since this metric is unknown, the algorithm implicitly considers an infinite family of such bounds. This is balanced with the upper confidence bound (UCB) provided by the GP, which is used to prune branches of the search tree that are provably suboptimal. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this approach leads to an exponential regret bound, which is a significant improvement over the polynomial bounds of related methods like BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014).

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known open problem in Bayesian optimization theory: achieving exponential regret without impractical assumptions. The proposed method of combining the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle from bound-based methods with the "infinite-metric" idea from DIRECT/SOO is novel and elegant. It provides a new perspective on how to integrate GP priors into partitioning-based optimization schemes.
*   **Theoretical Quality:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) is strong, formally establishing an exponential convergence rate. The accompanying analysis and remarks provide valuable insights into the trade-offs involved, such as the role of the GP in reducing the "infinite-metric exploration loss" and why relying on a single, potentially loose, known bound can be suboptimal.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the algorithm is explained with a helpful illustrative example (Figure 1), and its relationship to prior work (GP-UCB, SOO, DIRECT, BaMSOO) is thoroughly discussed. This makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is not sufficiently rigorous or comprehensive to substantiate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithm.

*   **Lack of Statistical Rigor in Experiments:** The experiments compare IMGPO, SOO, and BaMSOO as deterministic procedures (i.e., from a single fixed starting point) against GP-PI and GP-EI, which were averaged over 50 random initializations. This is a flawed comparison. The performance of Bayesian optimization algorithms can be highly sensitive to the initial design points. The standard and required protocol is to evaluate *all* algorithms over multiple random initializations to produce meaningful statistical comparisons (e.g., plots with mean performance and confidence intervals for all methods). Without this, the reported results are anecdotal and conclusions about relative performance are unreliable.
*   **Missing Comparisons to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The set of baseline algorithms is limited. While comparing to SOO and BaMSOO is logical given the algorithmic similarities, the GP-based baselines (GP-PI, GP-EI) are classical but no longer represent the state of the art. A convincing evaluation should include comparisons to more modern, powerful Bayesian optimization methods, especially information-theoretic approaches like Entropy Search (ES) or Predictive Entropy Search (PES), which are known to be highly sample-efficient.
*   **Limited Scope of Empirical Problems:** The evaluation is performed exclusively on synthetic benchmark functions. While these are useful, a key motivation for Bayesian optimization is its application to real-world problems. The paper would be much more impactful if it demonstrated the algorithm's effectiveness on at least one practical application, such as tuning the hyperparameters of a machine learning model on a real dataset. Furthermore, most of the functions are of low dimensionality (up to 6D). The single high-dimensional experiment uses REMBO as a wrapper, which demonstrates IMGPO's utility as a subroutine but does not directly test its scalability.

### Summary of the Review

This paper presents a theoretically elegant and novel approach to Bayesian optimization that achieves an exponential convergence rate without the need for auxiliary optimization or impractical sampling schemes. The work is well-written and the theoretical contributions are significant. However, the empirical evaluation is substantially below the standards expected for NIPS. The experiments lack statistical rigor, omit crucial state-of-the-art baselines, and are confined to low-dimensional synthetic functions. While the theoretical ideas are interesting, the current experimental results are not sufficient to convince the reader of the algorithm's practical utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and theoretically sound solution to an important open problem in Bayesian optimization.
2.  The derived exponential regret bound is a significant theoretical improvement over related work.
3.  The paper is very clearly written and positions the work well within the existing literature.

**Arguments for rejection:**
1.  The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to a lack of proper statistical comparison across multiple initializations.
2.  The paper fails to compare against modern, state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization baselines.
3.  The experiments are limited to synthetic functions and do not demonstrate performance on any real-world problems.

Given the major shortcomings of the experimental section, I lean towards rejection. However, I believe the paper has a strong theoretical core and would be prepared to increase my score if the authors can convincingly address these experimental concerns in their rebuttal.
