This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, IMGPO, which uses a Gaussian Process (GP) model to guide a hierarchical space-partitioning search. The core idea is to bypass the standard, and often costly, auxiliary optimization problem of maximizing an acquisition function by instead selecting which interval to divide next. This approach connects ideas from partitioning-based methods like SOO and DIRECT with the probabilistic framework of Bayesian optimization. The work builds upon recent efforts to develop theoretically sound BO algorithms, notably improving on the polynomial regret bounds of BaMSOO and removing the impractical sampling requirements of other exponential-regret methods.

**Strengths and Contributions:**
- The paper's primary contribution is theoretical: it provides a proof that IMGPO achieves an exponential convergence rate. This is a significant improvement over related practical algorithms and advances our understanding of what is achievable in Bayesian optimization without an internal optimizer.
- The algorithm itself is a novel and elegant synthesis of ideas. Combining the UCB criterion with a DIRECT-style partitioning scheme provides a principled way to balance exploration and exploitation.
- The quality of the presentation is high. The paper is well-written, the algorithm is clearly described, and the inclusion of a detailed 1D illustrated example is very helpful for building intuition about the method's mechanics.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
- The most significant reservation is the method's scalability. The interval-partitioning approach is fundamentally susceptible to the curse of dimensionality. This issue is well-known for related algorithms like DIRECT, which this paper should cite and discuss in more detail. While the authors briefly mention that the convergence rate degrades with dimensionality, this point is understated. The practical significance of the work is severely limited by this concern.
- The experimental evaluation, while positive on low-dimensional benchmarks, does not adequately address the scalability issue. Most test functions are 1D-6D. The single high-dimensional experiment relies on a random embedding technique (REMBO), which is a wrapper that does not demonstrate the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm. A more direct evaluation and frank discussion of performance as dimensionality increases are needed.

**Minor Points:**
- It is unclear if the UCB acquisition function is required for the theoretical results or if others (e.g., Expected Improvement) could be used. The authors mention this is "for brevity," but clarification would be valuable.
- The phrase in Section 3.1, "simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is somewhat vague and could be clarified.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper with a novel theoretical contribution. However, the work's practical impact is questionable due to fundamental scalability limitations that are not sufficiently addressed in the discussion or experiments.
