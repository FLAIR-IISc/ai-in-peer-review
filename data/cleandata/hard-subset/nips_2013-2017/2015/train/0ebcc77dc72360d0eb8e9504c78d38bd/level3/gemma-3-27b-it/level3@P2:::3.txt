This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, dubbed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), achieving exponential convergence without relying on auxiliary optimization or the impractical δ-cover sampling technique used in prior work [1]. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric encoding the function’s continuity, effectively considering all possible bounds during optimization. This approach is well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by strong experimental results, making it a significant contribution to the field.

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional Bayesian optimization methods requiring Lipschitz constant estimation [7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12] and highlights the recent progress of de Freitas et al. [1] in achieving exponential convergence with δ-cover sampling, which was subsequently identified as impractical [2]. The authors clearly articulate how IMGPO overcomes these limitations. The connection to bound-based search methods like A* search and PAC-MDP algorithms [18, 19] is insightful, framing the work within a broader optimization context.  The discussion of related work is thorough and accurately reflects the state-of-the-art.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the technical approach is sound and innovative. The concept of simultaneously considering all possible bounds, guided by the GP prior to prune implausible candidates, is both elegant and effective. The detailed description of the algorithm in Section 3.2, accompanied by the illustrative example (Figure 1), significantly enhances understanding. The programmatic details (Algorithm 1) are comprehensive and will facilitate replication by other researchers.  Second, the theoretical analysis (Section 4) is rigorous, providing a clear convergence guarantee and insightful remarks on the interplay between the GP prior, the unknown semi-metric, and the resulting regret bound. The analysis convincingly demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach, even in cases where the GP prior provides a less-than-optimal bound. Finally, the experimental evaluation (Section 5) is well-designed and convincing. The comparison against established algorithms (SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, GP-EI) on a diverse set of benchmark functions consistently demonstrates the superior performance of IMGPO.  The authors also appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their experimental setup (fixed hyperparameters) and suggest avenues for further improvement.

The paper's clarity is exceptional. The writing is concise and well-organized. The motivation for each step of the algorithm is clearly explained, and the mathematical notation is consistently used.  The inclusion of the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is particularly helpful for readers interested in implementing the method. The discussion is accessible to a broad audience familiar with Bayesian optimization and global optimization techniques.

The originality of this work is significant. While building on existing concepts in Bayesian optimization and bound-based search, IMGPO introduces a novel combination of these ideas that addresses a key limitation of previous methods. The core contribution – the consideration of all possible bounds without requiring δ-cover sampling – is a genuinely original advance.

The significance of this work is substantial. IMGPO offers a practical and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem in global optimization. The elimination of the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling makes the method applicable to a wider range of real-world problems. The exponential convergence rate guarantees efficient optimization, and the clear theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.  This work is likely to be influential in the field, inspiring further research on Bayesian optimization and related techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to Bayesian optimization.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing methods (δ-cover sampling).
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with a clear convergence guarantee.
*   Compelling experimental results demonstrating superior performance.
*   Excellent clarity and well-organized presentation.
*   Strong potential impact on the field of global optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Scalability to very high-dimensional problems may be limited (addressed in the discussion).
*   Performance is sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (though the authors address this by using a standard approach).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The technical approach is novel and well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are compelling. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS 2015.
