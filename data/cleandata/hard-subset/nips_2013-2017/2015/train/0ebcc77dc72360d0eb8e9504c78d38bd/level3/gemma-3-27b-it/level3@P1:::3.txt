This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization method, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to achieve exponential convergence without requiring the computationally expensive δ-cover sampling procedure or auxiliary optimization—both limitations of existing approaches like de Freitas et al. [1].  The core idea is to leverage the assumption of an unknown, potentially tighter bound on the function’s smoothness, alongside the information provided by a Gaussian Process (GP) prior, to guide the search for the global optimum. This work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in Bayesian optimization [13, 14, 15], Lipschitz optimization [7, 8, 9], and methods attempting to relax the Lipschitz constant assumption [10, 11, 12].  It directly addresses the open problem highlighted by Wang et al. [2] of developing a GP-based optimization method with exponential convergence that avoids δ-cover sampling.

The authors present a technically sound and well-motivated approach. The introduction effectively frames the problem and clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods.  IMGPO operates by simultaneously considering multiple potential bounds, informed by the GP model, and using hierarchical partitioning to explore the search space. The algorithm balances exploration based on the GP’s uncertainty with exploitation guided by the potential for tighter bounds encoded in the function’s continuity.  A significant strength lies in the detailed explanation of the algorithm (Section 3.2), enhanced by the illustrative 1D example, which significantly aids in understanding the core mechanics. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and programmatic details solidify this clarity, making reproduction feasible.  

The theoretical analysis (Section 4) provides a compelling justification for IMGPO’s effectiveness. The authors demonstrate an exponential convergence rate and offer insightful remarks about the interplay between the GP prior, the unknown bound, and the algorithm's performance.  The discussion of how the tightness of the GP-derived bound affects the convergence rate (Remark 1) is particularly valuable.  The analysis effectively situates IMGPO within the broader landscape of bound-based search methods, drawing connections to DIRECT [19] and SOO [18].

The experimental evaluation (Section 5) provides strong evidence of IMGPO’s performance.  The comparisons to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight different objective functions demonstrate its superiority in many cases.  Importantly, the authors acknowledge the limitations of their experimental setup (using fixed hyperparameters) and suggest avenues for future improvement. The inclusion of a high-dimensional test case (1000 dimensions) using REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine demonstrates scalability potential.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of combining a GP prior with an infinite set of possible bounds is a genuinely novel contribution to the field.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, including a proven exponential convergence rate.
*   **Clarity & Reproducibility:**  The algorithm is exceptionally well-explained with a helpful illustrative example and detailed pseudocode.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of IMGPO.
*   **Connection to Prior Work:**  The paper effectively positions its contribution within the broader context of Bayesian optimization and related fields.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the high-dimensional experiment is promising, the authors acknowledge that scalability remains a challenge, particularly as the input dimension increases.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the experiments used fixed hyperparameters, the method's performance might be sensitive to their choice. A more thorough hyperparameter tuning analysis would strengthen the empirical results.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparisons with existing algorithms are strong, but a comparison to more recent and highly optimized Bayesian optimization packages (e.g., those using more sophisticated acquisition functions or ensemble methods) might be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   IMGPO represents a significant advancement in Bayesian optimization, addressing a key limitation of existing methods.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a clear and compelling explanation of the proposed approach.
*   The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method’s effectiveness.
*   The work addresses a problem of high relevance to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability concerns, while acknowledged, might be a limiting factor for certain applications.
*   Further investigation into hyperparameter sensitivity could strengthen the empirical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong theoretical and empirical results, makes it highly deserving of acceptance to NIPS.  The clarity of the presentation and the emphasis on reproducibility are particularly commendable.  I recommend acceptance with a minor suggestion to investigate hyperparameter sensitivity in future work.
