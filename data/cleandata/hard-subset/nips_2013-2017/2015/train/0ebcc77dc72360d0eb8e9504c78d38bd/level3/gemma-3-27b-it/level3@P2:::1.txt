This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, dubbed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), achieving exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric encoding function continuity, combined with the information provided by a Gaussian Process prior, to guide exploration and avoid relying on a potentially inaccurate GP-derived bound alone. The method is framed within the broader context of bound-based search, drawing connections to Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and previous work by Munos [18] and Wang et al. [2].

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, algorithm details, and theoretical analysis. The connection to existing literature is strong, and the authors carefully position their work relative to prior achievements, particularly addressing the limitations of δ-cover sampling highlighted by Wang et al. [2]. The theoretical results, specifically the proven exponential convergence rate, are significant and well-supported by a rigorous mathematical analysis (detailed in the supplementary material). The algorithm description is clear, and the inclusion of a pseudocode implementation (Algorithm 1) aids understanding. The authors also thoughtfully discuss the interplay between the GP component and the infinite-metric optimization strategy, and identify potential areas for future improvement, such as scalability to higher dimensions.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical novelty and clarity of presentation. Addressing the challenge of exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling is a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The analysis provides a nuanced understanding of the algorithm’s behavior and identifies the conditions under which it’s expected to perform well, offering insights into the benefits of considering a broader space of possible bounds.

However, the experimental evaluation represents a significant weakness. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of their experimental setup, the lack of robust statistical comparison hinders a full assessment of IMGPO's practical performance. Specifically, the experiments do not employ multiple initial random evaluations of the objective function – a standard practice in Bayesian optimization – making it difficult to draw definitive conclusions about the algorithm's efficiency. Furthermore, the absence of comparisons with state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization techniques, such as information-theoretic approaches like Entropy Search, limits the ability to gauge IMGPO’s competitive advantage. The focus solely on synthetic benchmark functions (with a maximum dimensionality of 6) is also a concern, as it doesn't demonstrate the algorithm's applicability to real-world problems where parameter spaces can be considerably higher-dimensional and more complex. The use of a fixed kernel and hyperparameter initialization, while justified by the desire to avoid problem-specific tuning, also introduces a potential bias in the results. While the authors note that increasing Ξmax *could* improve performance, this is presented as an aside rather than a thorough investigation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical contribution: Achieves exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling.
*   Strong theoretical analysis: Rigorous proof of convergence rate.
*   Clear and well-written presentation: Easily understandable algorithm description and connection to relevant literature.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak experimental evaluation: Lack of statistical rigor, limited comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, and absence of real-world applications.
*   Limited dimensionality of test functions.
*   Absence of multiple initial evaluations, a standard BO practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its compelling theoretical contributions, the limited experimental evaluation detracts from the overall quality of this paper. While the analysis is sound and the ideas are promising, the lack of robust empirical evidence makes it difficult to assess IMGPO's practical viability. The paper demonstrates significant potential, but further work is needed to validate its performance on more challenging problems and to compare it rigorously with existing state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization techniques. 

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. I believe the theoretical contributions warrant further consideration, but the experimental shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I encourage the authors to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation and address the concerns raised in this review before resubmitting.
