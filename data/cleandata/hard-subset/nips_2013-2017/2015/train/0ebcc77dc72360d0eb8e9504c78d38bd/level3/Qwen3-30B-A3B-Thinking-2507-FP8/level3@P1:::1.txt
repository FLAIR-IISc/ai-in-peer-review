

This paper introduces IMGPO, a novel Gaussian process-based global optimization algorithm that achieves exponential regret convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing a key limitation in prior work by de Freitas et al. (2012) and Wang et al. (2013). The authors frame their approach within bound-based search methods, proposing an "infinite-metric" strategy that simultaneously considers infinitely many candidate bounds encoded by an unknown semi-metric, rather than relying on a single GP-derived upper bound. The core theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential regret bound O(λ^{N+Ngp}) with λ<1 under Assumptions 1 and 2, generalizing prior results and demonstrating how GP information reduces regret compared to non-GP approaches. The algorithm leverages hierarchical partitioning with adaptive interval division, and the authors provide a clear pseudocode and analysis of the infinite-metric exploration loss.

The paper excels in clarity and theoretical positioning. It meticulously contextualizes IMGPO within the Bayesian optimization landscape, contrasting with GP-UCB (which requires auxiliary optimization) and BaMSOO (which achieves only polynomial regret), while connecting to foundational work like DIRECT and SOO. The writing is exceptionally well-organized, with Figure 1 and Algorithm 1 effectively illustrating the algorithm’s mechanics. The discussion of the trade-offs between bound tightness (Remarks 1–3) is particularly insightful, offering a principled rationale for the infinite-metric approach beyond mere theoretical novelty.

The originality is strong: the infinite-metric framework is a novel conceptual advance that reconciles two divergent paths in global optimization (fixed-bound vs. boundless methods), and the exponential regret guarantee without impractical sampling procedures represents a significant theoretical step forward. The connection to broader applications (e.g., A* search, PAC-MDP) in the conclusion further underscores its conceptual novelty.

However, the experimental evaluation severely undermines the paper’s significance. The authors claim IMGPO outperforms SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on eight synthetic functions (dimensions 1–6), but the experimental design violates standard Bayesian optimization practices. Crucially, no multiple initial evaluations of the objective function are performed—this is a fundamental requirement for fair BO comparisons (as established in Snoek et al., 2012; Shahriari et al., 2016), as it accounts for the stochasticity in GP hyperparameter estimation and initial conditions. Without this, the reported performance gains are statistically invalid, and the mean/std. deviation metrics for GP-PI/GP-EI (50 runs) are incomparable to the deterministic runs of other methods. 

Furthermore, the experiments lack critical comparisons with state-of-the-art BO methods. The omission of information-theoretic approaches (e.g., Entropy Search, MES; Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014) or recent high-dimensional BO techniques (e.g., REMBO; Wang et al., 2013) is particularly troubling, as these represent the current benchmark for BO performance. The paper’s claim of "outperforming" baselines is thus misleading without such comparisons. The experimental scope is also limited to synthetic functions (max dimension 6), with no real-world applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, robotics, or experimental design) to demonstrate practical utility. The 1000-dimensional Sin2 test case uses REMBO as a subroutine, but this does not constitute a meaningful real-world evaluation of IMGPO itself.

The theoretical quality is high, but the experimental weaknesses prevent the paper from meeting NIPS’s standard for significance. While the exponential regret bound is a valuable theoretical contribution, the absence of rigorous, reproducible experiments on standard BO benchmarks—coupled with the violation of basic BO evaluation protocols—diminishes its practical impact. The paper’s significance is further weakened by the lack of discussion on scalability beyond D=6, despite the theoretical bound suggesting challenges in higher dimensions (Remark 1). 

Pro: Strong theoretical novelty with a clear exponential regret bound; well-written and well-positioned in literature; insightful analysis of bound trade-offs.  
Con: Experimental design violates standard BO practices (no multiple initial evaluations); lacks comparisons with modern BO methods (e.g., Entropy Search); limited to synthetic functions (max D=6); no real-world validation.  

While the theoretical contribution is compelling, the experimental shortcomings prevent the paper from demonstrating the practical significance required for NIPS acceptance. The authors should address these gaps by: (1) conducting experiments with multiple initial evaluations; (2) comparing against state-of-the-art BO methods; and (3) including at least one real-world application. Without these, the paper’s claims about performance superiority cannot be substantiated. Given the high theoretical quality but deficient empirical validation, I recommend rejection.
