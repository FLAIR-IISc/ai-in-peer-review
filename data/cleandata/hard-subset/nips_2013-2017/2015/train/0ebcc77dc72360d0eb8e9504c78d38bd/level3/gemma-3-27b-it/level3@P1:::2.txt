This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization method, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to achieve exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure used in prior work (de Freitas et al. [1]). The core idea revolves around a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, guided by the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) criterion, to efficiently explore promising regions while implicitly considering a wider range of possible bounds on the objective function. This approach distinguishes itself from existing methods by eschewing the need for a separate global optimization step to determine the next evaluation point, simplifying the algorithm and potentially improving computational efficiency. 

The authors clearly position their work within the context of Bayesian optimization, acknowledging the challenges associated with estimating Lipschitz constants and the limitations of previous approaches. They effectively connect IMGPO to related concepts like bound-based search methods (including Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and PAC-MDP algorithms) and highlight its theoretical advantages over existing algorithms such as BaMSOO [2] and SOO [18], particularly regarding regret bounds.  The algorithm is described in detail, with a helpful illustrative example and pseudocode provided. A key assumption—the existence of an unknown semi-metric defining a bound on the function’s gradient—is explicitly stated, and its impact on the algorithm’s performance is analyzed. 

A significant strength of this work lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating an exponential convergence rate. The authors provide a detailed proof (in the supplementary material) and discuss the interplay between the tightness of the GP-estimated bound and the unknown semi-metric, offering valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior.  Furthermore, the analysis connects theoretical performance to practical considerations, acknowledging the potential impact of the algorithm’s parameters (e.g., Ξmax) and the characteristics of the objective function.

However, a major reservation concerns the scope of the experimental evaluation. While the paper presents results on eight test functions, the illustrations primarily focus on one-dimensional examples. The authors mention scaling the algorithm to 1000 dimensions with REMBO [14], but the primary results remain on lower-dimensional functions.  This limited experimental scope raises questions about the algorithm's scalability and performance in higher-dimensional spaces, which are common in many real-world applications. 

This concern is exacerbated by the algorithm’s reliance on hierarchical partitioning.  As noted by the authors, the resolution required for effective partitioning grows exponentially with the dimensionality of the problem.  This echoes a known limitation of methods like DIRECT [19], which also employ space partitioning and often struggle in high dimensions. The paper briefly acknowledges this connection but could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the potential scalability issues and possible mitigation strategies.  While the theoretical contribution remains valid regardless of scalability, the practical applicability of IMGPO is significantly impacted.

Several minor points could be clarified. The statement regarding the ability to use acquisition functions other than UCB (“for brevity”) is somewhat ambiguous. It would be helpful to explicitly state whether UCB is a necessary component of the algorithm or simply a convenient choice.  The sentence describing the simultaneous global and local searches could also be rephrased for clarity.  Finally, a brief explanation of why no function evaluation occurs in the far-right region at n=16 in Figure 1 would enhance understanding of the algorithm’s behavior. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to Bayesian optimization.
*   Achieves exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization.
*   Clear and detailed description of the algorithm with an illustrative example.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and insightful discussion of performance factors.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation, primarily on one-dimensional test functions.
*   Potential scalability issues due to the hierarchical partitioning approach, mirroring limitations of DIRECT.
*   Lack of a comprehensive discussion regarding the algorithm's performance in higher dimensions.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are strong and the proposed algorithm is well-motivated and clearly described. However, the limited experimental evaluation and potential scalability concerns are significant weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors expanding the experimental evaluation to include higher-dimensional problems and providing a more thorough discussion of the algorithm's scalability limitations and potential mitigation strategies.  A clearer explanation of the UCB requirement would also be beneficial.
