Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Infinite-Metric Gaussian Process Optimization (IMGPO), a GP-based Bayesian optimization algorithm for deterministic black-box global optimization that attains exponential simple-regret convergence without δ-cover sampling and without auxiliary nonconvex optimization of acquisition functions. The method blends hierarchical partitioning (as in DIRECT and SOO) with GP-based upper confidence bounds (UCB) to prune candidate regions. A key modeling assumption is the existence of an unknown semi-metric around the global maximizer that upper-bounds suboptimality; IMGPO implicitly considers infinitely many such bounds and uses the GP posterior to discard candidates that are looser than the GP-derived UCB. The algorithm interleaves (i) bound-based selection across depths of a partition tree and (ii) GP-based screening, and sometimes substitutes a GP-UCB value for a function evaluation to save queries.

On the theory side, the authors introduce the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt (the number of intervals divided at iteration t) and prove an exponential convergence rate O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1 (with probability at least 1 − η), where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp the number of GP-based substitutions, under standard near-optimality assumptions akin to those used in SOO/DOO. The analysis makes explicit how the tightness of GP-UCB affects ρ̄t and hence the rate, and clarifies the role of dimensionality and smoothness parameters.

Empirically, IMGPO is compared to SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI on standard benchmarks (Sin1/2, Branin, Hartmann3, Rosenbrock2, etc.) using a Matérn-5/2 kernel with empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates. Results show faster regret decay and favorable wall-clock time compared to GP-EI/PI, and better accuracy than SOO and BaMSOO in most tasks. The paper also demonstrates coupling with REMBO for a 1000-D embedded problem.

This work connects to: DIRECT (Jones et al.), SOO/DOO (Munos, NIPS 2011), BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS 2014), GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., ICML 2010), and practical BO (Snoek et al., NIPS 2012). It addresses an open gap highlighted by de Freitas et al. and Wang et al. regarding exponential regret without δ-cover.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The algorithmic contribution is clearly specified with pseudocode and a step-by-step illustration. The main theorem is stated precisely, with discussion of constants and assumptions; proofs are relegated to the supplement. The empirical evaluation covers diverse benchmarks and ablations (e.g., varying Ξmax) and uses standard baselines. One limitation is that the theory assumes noiseless evaluations and a semi-metric bound; impacts of hyperparameter learning and model misspecification on the probabilistic UCB validity are discussed informally but not analyzed. Overall, the technical claims appear sound and well supported by experiments.

- Clarity: The paper is well written and didactic. The figure explaining the algorithm’s steps is especially effective. The relationships to SOO/DOO/DIRECT and BaMSOO are clearly articulated. Minor presentation issues remain (see below).

- Originality: The combination of (i) infinite-metric selection with (ii) GP-based pruning to eliminate both auxiliary optimization and δ-cover, together with an exponential regret analysis, is novel relative to prior NIPS/AISTATS work (SOO/DOO, BaMSOO). The notion of infinite-metric exploration loss and the analysis that quantifies how GP tightness impacts convergence are original and insightful.

- Significance: Achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover and without acquisition optimization addresses a known practical and theoretical pain point. The approach is likely to influence practitioners who avoid auxiliary global optimizers, and researchers working on bound-based search, hierarchical partitioning, and BO. The dimensionality dependence and noiseless assumption limit immediate applicability to very high-D or noisy settings, but the ideas appear broadly reusable (e.g., within REMBO or additive models).

Strengths:
- Eliminates δ-cover and acquisition optimization while retaining exponential regret.
- Clear algorithmic design with a principled integration of GP information.
- Theoretical framework provides interpretable quantities (ρ̄t, Ξn) and connects tightness of UCB to performance.
- Strong empirical results under realistic kernel/hyperparameter settings; code availability.
- Didactic exposition with effective figures.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions and scope: The deterministic setting and the semi-metric bound (Assumptions 1–2) may not hold in noisy or highly irregular objectives. Discuss extensions to noisy evaluations and robustness to model misspecification.
- Dimensionality: λ approaches 1 as D grows; while REMBO results are promising, an explicit discussion or experiment on scalability trade-offs (beyond embeddings) would strengthen the paper.
- Hyperparameter learning: Empirical Bayes updates are used; clarify conditions under which the UCB validity (f ≤ U with prob ≥ 1 − η) still holds when hyperparameters are learned online. If relying on union bounds or empirical justifications, state this explicitly.
- Parameter sensitivity: Provide guidance for choosing Ξmax and η; include a brief sensitivity plot or table.
- Baselines: Consider including a strong GP-UCB baseline with multi-start/global optimization of the acquisition (even if slower) to contextualize accuracy gains.
- General domain shapes: The analysis assumes Ω is a hyperrectangle; the supplement mentions generalizations. A short main-text note on practical handling of non-rectangular domains would help.
- Minor presentation issues:
  - Abstract: a sentence around line 49 is redundant; consider tightening.
  - Typo: misplaced parenthesis in the kernel expression (around line 72); please fix.
  - A few long sentences could be shortened for readability (e.g., steps (i)–(v) narrative).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a recognized open problem: exponential BO without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization.
- Solid theory with clear insights; practically relevant algorithm; strong empirical gains.
- Well written and of broad interest to BO and global optimization communities.
- Public code increases impact and reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theory limited to noiseless case and relies on semi-metric bound; unclear robustness in noisy/misspecified settings.
- Scalability to high dimensions not fully addressed beyond embeddings; rate degrades with D.
- Limited sensitivity analysis and baseline coverage for acquisition-optimized GP methods.

Recommendation: Accept.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate. I am not a specialist in Gaussian processes or global optimization; my evaluation is an informed reading rather than an expert audit of technical lemmas.
