Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without auxiliary acquisition-function optimization and without δ-cover sampling. The method combines a GP posterior upper confidence bound with a hierarchical partitioning scheme that simultaneously considers an infinite family of putative semi-metric bounds, pruning candidates using GP-derived UCBs. Theoretical analysis yields an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) (λ < 1) under mild continuity assumptions and quantifies the roles of exploration loss and GP tightness. Experiments on standard benchmarks show superior or competitive performance versus SOO (Munos, NIPS’11), BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS’14), and GP-EI/PI (Snoek et al., NIPS’12), and a demonstration with REMBO (Wang et al., IJCAI’13) suggests potential for higher dimensions. The work is well grounded in and extends lines from DIRECT (Jones et al., JOTA’93), GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., ICML’10), and de Freitas et al.’s deterministic exponential-regret result that relied on δ-covers.

Quality: The theoretical claims are clearly stated, with a main theorem and insightful remarks connecting regret to GP tightness and exploration loss. While I am not a domain expert in Gaussian processes or global optimization, the analysis appears careful, and the experiments are sensible, using a fixed Matern kernel and blind hyperparameter updates. Some practical aspects could be expanded (e.g., per-iteration computational complexity of step (iii), sensitivity to Ξmax, and robustness to model misspecification/noise).

Clarity: The paper is well written and didactic; figures effectively illustrate the procedure. Minor issues: abstract wording feels redundant (line 49); there is a misplaced parenthesis in a function definition (line 72). Consider clarifying notation early (ρt, ρ̄t, Ξn) and giving a succinct algorithmic complexity summary.

Originality: The combination of infinite-metric selection with GP-based pruning to remove both δ-covers and auxiliary optimization is, to my knowledge, novel and distinguishes the work from BaMSOO and GP-UCB.

Significance: If the exponential bound holds in practice, removing auxiliary optimization makes BO more deployable. The analysis framework may influence other bound-based methods.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution (exponential regret without δ-covers/auxiliary optimization).
- Clear, pedagogical presentation; effective figures.
- Solid empirical evidence across benchmarks.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability; λ degrades with dimension.
- Deterministic setting; extensions to noisy observations are not analyzed.
- Minor writing/typo issues (lines 49, 72).

Overall: In scope for NeurIPS; I lean positive given theoretical novelty and clear exposition.
