Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a Bayesian global optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), that combines GP-based upper confidence bounds with a hierarchical space-partitioning strategy. The key idea is to avoid the standard auxiliary global optimization of an acquisition function by selecting and splitting intervals (hyperrectangles) according to a set of candidate bounds: the known (possibly loose) GP-UCB bound and an unknown but tighter bound implied by continuity at the global optimum. The algorithm screens candidates across scales and only evaluates when the UCB suggests potential improvement, otherwise postponing evaluation by placing UCB “dummy” values. The authors provide a clear algorithm description, pseudocode, and an illustrative 1D example.

Theoretically, the paper proves an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) (λ < 1) under assumptions that generalize Lipschitz-like smoothness, improving on the polynomial-rate bounds of BaMSOO (SOO + GP-UCB). This removes the impractical δ-cover sampling used in earlier exponential-rate analyses (de Freitas et al., ICML’12) while also avoiding auxiliary maximization typical of GP-UCB/EI/PI. The method is conceptually related to DIRECT (Jones et al.) and SOO (Munos), but leverages GP posteriors to prune candidates and obtain stronger rates.

Strengths
- Novelty and contribution: An original combination of infinite-metric partition-based search with GP-based screening yields exponential regret without δ-cover or auxiliary optimization. This is a substantive theoretical and algorithmic advance over BaMSOO and GP-UCB-style methods.
- Theory: Clear, carefully stated assumptions, probabilistic confidence control, and an explicit exponential regret bound that improves on prior work; the analysis exposes how GP tightness and exploration loss ρ̄t affect rates.
- Clarity: The algorithm is well specified (pseudocode, step-by-step example), with an intuitive explanation of when UCBs are used to defer evaluations.
- Practical relevance: Avoids solving a difficult nonconvex subproblem each iteration; provides a path to faster wall-clock BO when function evaluations are not overwhelmingly expensive.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scalability: The interval-partitioning scheme is subject to the curse of dimensionality; resolution and the number of cells grow exponentially with dimension. The bound’s constant λ worsens with D and may approach 1 as D increases (acknowledged by the authors), suggesting limited practical scalability. DIRECT and SOO-based methods are known to degrade in high-D; this should be explicitly discussed and empirically assessed.
- Experiments: As presented, the evaluation appears dominated by 1D (and very low-D) functions; it is not clearly stated which dimensions are covered in each plot, and I could not find convincing higher-dimensional benchmarks. If there are multi-D results, please make the dimensionality explicit in the figures and captions, and include standard 5–10D tests (e.g., Hartmann6, Ackley10, Rosenbrock10, powered Branin variants) and compare to strong BO baselines (EI/PI/UCB with multistart/DIRECT/CMA-ES inner solvers, TuRBO/REMBO if applicable). The current evidence is insufficient to assess practical performance beyond 1D/2D.
- Relation to DIRECT in high-D: Given the methodological similarity (tri-partitioning, center sampling), and the literature documenting DIRECT’s poor high-D performance, the paper should squarely address this and clarify whether IMGPO’s GP pruning materially changes the picture in D ≥ 6–10.
- Choice of UCB: The paper states “for brevity” it uses UCB. It is unclear whether UCB is required for the theory or whether EI/PI or other acquisition functions could be substituted while retaining the main guarantees. Please clarify the role of UCB in both algorithm and proof.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main claims are well supported by theory. Assumptions are reasonable for deterministic objectives, but the bounds’ D-dependence and semi-metric assumptions deserve more discussion. The experimental section needs clearer dimensional labeling and stronger high-D evidence to support practical claims.
- Reproducibility: Pseudocode and a code link are provided. Please document hyperparameters (η, Ξmax, ςM schedule) and kernel learning details to ease replication.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well written. Two places could be clearer:
  - “Simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds.” Please concretely define “global” vs “local” here and how steps (i)–(iii) instantiate this duality across depths.
  - In the example: why at n=16 the far-right region has no evaluation? As I understand it, step (v) places GP-UCB values as temporary center values if UCB < current best, deferring evaluation until the interval later survives steps (i)–(ii). Please restate this more explicitly next to the figure.
- Clearly state the dimensionality of each test function in the plots and captions.

Originality
- The combination of infinite-metric optimistic partitioning with GP-based bounds to obtain exponential regret is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The work is well positioned with respect to GP-UCB, BaMSOO, SOO, and DIRECT.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is high: first exponential-rate GP-BO without δ-cover and without auxiliary optimization. Practical significance will hinge on performance in moderate-to-high dimensions; current evidence is limited.

Questions and requested clarifications for the authors
- Is UCB essential for the analysis, or could EI/PI be used with analogous screening and still yield exponential regret under similar assumptions?
- Please discuss and, if possible, experimentally evaluate performance in D ≥ 6 using standard benchmarks; include a comparison to DIRECT, SOO, BaMSOO, and strong BO baselines with multistart inner solvers.
- Can IMGPO incorporate additive/low-dimensional structure (e.g., REMBO, additive GPs, TuRBO) to mitigate the exponential partitioning burden? Any theoretical statements in such settings?
- Clarify the sentence about “global and local searches” and the example detail at n=16 as noted above.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical contribution (exponential regret without δ-cover/auxiliary optimization).
- Clear algorithmic description and insight into the role of GP bounds.
- Potential practical impact when inner acquisition optimization is a bottleneck.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and ambiguously presented empirical evaluation; appears focused on 1D/low-D.
- Scalability concerns inherent to interval partitioning; unclear whether GP pruning suffices in higher dimensions.
- Unclear whether UCB is necessary or merely convenient; alternative criteria not discussed.

Overall assessment
A sound and novel contribution with a substantial theoretical advance and a clear algorithm. However, the empirical evaluation appears limited to very low-dimensional problems, and the scalability limitations of interval partitioning (and DIRECT-like methods) are not adequately addressed. I encourage acceptance contingent on stronger, clearly labeled multi-dimensional experiments and a more direct discussion of high-dimensional behavior and the necessity of UCB versus other acquisitions.
