This paper presents a novel Gaussian Process (GP) based Bayesian optimization algorithm, IMGPO, achieving exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling – limitations of prior work (de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]). The core idea is to leverage an unknown semi-metric, implicitly encoding function continuity, alongside the GP prior, to guide exploration. This contrasts with relying solely on the GP-estimated bound, which can be insufficiently tight.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and didactic. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature on Bayesian optimization, Lipschitz optimization, and global optimization. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, and the algorithm description is accompanied by a helpful illustrative figure (Figure 1). The connection to bound-based search methods and the discussion of the underlying intuition (Assumption 1) add significant clarity. The inclusion of a publicly available codebase is a valuable asset.

IMGPO builds upon the foundation laid by previous NIPS papers, particularly de Freitas et al. [1] which established exponential convergence for Bayesian optimization *with* δ-cover sampling, and Wang et al. [2], who addressed the impracticality of δ-cover sampling but achieved only polynomial convergence. This work directly tackles the open problem highlighted by Wang et al. [2]: achieving exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) convincingly demonstrates this result, and the experiments corroborate the performance gains over competing algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The exponential convergence rate is rigorously proven, and the analysis explores the impact of various parameters.
*   **Practical Significance:** Eliminating the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling makes the algorithm significantly more practical for real-world applications.
*   **Clear Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making the complex concepts accessible. The use of diagrams and a detailed algorithm description significantly aids understanding.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate IMGPO's superior performance across a range of benchmark functions.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of simultaneously considering a set of possible bounds encoded by an unknown semi-metric is a genuinely novel contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Minor Technical Issues:** The abstract formulation feels somewhat redundant; it essentially restates the problem being addressed. Additionally, there's a misplaced parenthesis in the function definition on line 72. These are minor issues and easily corrected.
*   **Reviewer Expertise:** I admit some unfamiliarity with the intricacies of Gaussian Processes and global optimization. Consequently, while I found the theoretical arguments compelling, my evaluation of their depth is somewhat educated guesswork. However, the clarity of the presentation helped mitigate this limitation.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper acknowledges a potential scalability issue with increasing dimensionality (discussed around Theorem 1), further investigation into methods for mitigating this – perhaps leveraging techniques from high-dimensional Bayesian optimization like those referenced in [14] – would strengthen the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in Bayesian optimization, resolving an open problem in the field.
*   The algorithm is demonstrably superior to existing methods in terms of convergence rate and practicality.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains a complex topic.
*   The publicly available code promotes reproducibility and wider adoption.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The minor technical issues (abstract redundancy, parenthesis error) should be addressed.
*   The potential scalability limitations warrant further investigation, although they are acknowledged by the authors.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, the algorithm is practical, and the presentation is excellent. The minor weaknesses are easily addressed, and the potential scalability concerns are acknowledged. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.
