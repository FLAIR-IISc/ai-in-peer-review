This paper introduces IMGPO, a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm using Gaussian Processes (GP) that achieves an exponential convergence rate. This work addresses a significant open problem in the field by removing the need for both the auxiliary non-convex optimization common to many GP methods and the impractical δ-cover sampling required by previous theoretical work aiming for exponential convergence (de Freitas et al., 2012). The approach skillfully synthesizes two distinct lines of research: the bound-based hierarchical partitioning of algorithms like SOO (Munos, 2011) and the probabilistic modeling of GP-UCB. In doing so, it demonstrably improves upon the polynomial regret bounds of closely related recent methods such as BaMSOO (Wang et al., 2014).

The paper's primary strengths are its exceptional quality and clarity. The authors have done an admirable job of presenting a complex algorithm and its analysis in a didactic and well-organized manner. The inclusion of Figure 1, which provides a step-by-step visual walkthrough of the procedure, is particularly effective and greatly enhances the reader's comprehension. The theoretical claims are convincingly supported by rigorous analysis, and the comprehensive experimental results clearly show the proposed method's advantageous performance over strong baselines on a diverse set of benchmark functions.

The manuscript is very polished, with only minor presentational weaknesses. The final sentence of the abstract (line 49) feels slightly redundant, as it restates the core contribution detailed in the introduction. There also appears to be a small typo (a misplaced parenthesis) in the function defining ςM in Section 3.3. It should be noted that while my own expertise is not centered on Gaussian Processes or global optimization, my educated assessment is that the work is technically sound and the contributions are solid.

The originality and significance of this work are high. It provides a practical and effective solution to an open problem explicitly identified in prior NIPS-related literature, thus advancing the state of the art. The core idea of simultaneously considering an infinite set of potential bounds while using the GP prior to efficiently prune the search space is novel and powerful. Arguments for acceptance are the paper's clear significance, high technical quality, and excellent presentation. The minor weaknesses are easily correctable. For these reasons, I strongly recommend acceptance.
