### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), designed to solve global optimization problems for black-box functions. The main contribution is an algorithm that achieves an exponential convergence rate for the simple regret without requiring an auxiliary non-convex optimization step to select the next evaluation point, a common and often costly component of standard GP-based optimization methods. Furthermore, it avoids the need for δ-cover sampling, an impractical requirement of previous work that also achieved exponential regret [1].

The proposed method, IMGPO, integrates a Gaussian Process (GP) prior with a hierarchical partitioning of the search space, akin to algorithms like DIRECT and SOO. At each iteration, it strategically selects which hyperrectangle (or interval) to divide based on a set of criteria that leverages both the function values at the centers of the intervals and the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) derived from the GP posterior. This allows the algorithm to implicitly consider an infinite set of possible semi-metrics for the function's smoothness, effectively balancing global and local search without committing to a single, potentially incorrect, smoothness assumption. The paper provides a theoretical analysis proving an exponential regret bound, improving upon the polynomial bounds of related practical algorithms like BaMSOO [2].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The primary strength lies in its theoretical contribution: the derivation of an exponential regret bound (Theorem 1) is a significant result that addresses a recognized open problem in the literature. The analysis clearly articulates how the regret bound depends on properties of the unknown function and the behavior of the algorithm. The algorithm itself is well-defined. The experiments, while limited in scope (see below), seem to be conducted carefully, and the results support the claim that IMGPO is competitive with or superior to existing methods on the chosen problems.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and positioning their work within the existing literature. The description of the algorithm in Section 3.2 is particularly effective, thanks to the step-by-step walkthrough of the 1D example illustrated in Figure 1. This illustration makes the mechanics of the partitioning, selection, and pruning strategy very intuitive. The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is detailed and appears sufficient for implementation.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While it draws upon established ideas from both GP-based optimization (GP-UCB) and hierarchical partitioning methods (SOO, DIRECT), the synthesis is novel. The core idea of using the GP-UCB to prune branches in a search tree that implicitly considers infinitely many smoothness assumptions is, to my knowledge, new. This approach successfully bridges two distinct lines of research in global optimization and resolves a challenging theoretical problem.

**Significance:**
The results presented are potentially of high significance to both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian optimization.
*   **Theoretical Significance:** The paper provides the first practical GP-based algorithm with a proven exponential convergence rate. This is a substantial theoretical advance for the field. The analysis in Remarks 1-3 also provides valuable insights into the interplay between known bounds (from the GP) and unknown true smoothness properties of the objective function.
*   **Practical Significance:** Eliminating the auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function is a major practical advantage, simplifying implementation and potentially saving significant computation time, as shown in Table 1.

However, the practical significance is tempered by a major concern:

*   **Scalability:** The primary weakness of the paper is the limited evaluation and discussion regarding the algorithm's scalability with input dimensionality. The hierarchical partitioning approach is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality, as the number of cells required to cover the space grows exponentially. This is a well-known issue for related methods like DIRECT [19], which perform poorly in even moderately high dimensions. The paper does not adequately address this critical limitation. The experiments are conducted on functions with very low dimensionality (mostly 1D-3D). While one 1000D experiment is included, it relies on REMBO [14] to perform random embedding, meaning IMGPO is only used as a low-dimensional subroutine. This does not provide evidence for the scalability of the core IMGPO algorithm itself. This concern does not invalidate the theoretical contribution but significantly limits the claims of practical utility for many real-world problems.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   **Discussion on Scalability:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a dedicated discussion on the scalability challenges of partitioning-based methods. Acknowledging this limitation and discussing potential avenues for mitigation or the specific types of problems where IMGPO would be most suitable would provide a more balanced perspective.
*   **Clarification on Acquisition Functions:** The authors state they use UCB "for brevity." Can this framework accommodate other acquisition functions like Expected Improvement (EI) or Probability of Improvement (PI)? A brief comment on this would be helpful for understanding the generality of the approach.
*   **Clarification on Figure 1 Example:** In the description of Figure 1 for iteration t=9, it is mentioned that the far-right interval at n=16 is divided without a new function evaluation. The text explains this is due to step (v) of the algorithm, where the UCB value is below the best-found value `f+`. It would improve clarity to explicitly state this condition (`U < f+`) in the description of the figure to help the reader immediately understand why a "dummy value" is used.
*   **Clarification on a Sentence:** The sentence in Section 3.1, "Accordingly, we simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is somewhat abstract. I believe it refers to how the algorithm considers partitions of all existing sizes in each iteration (lines 8-23 in Algorithm 1). Rephrasing this for greater clarity would be beneficial.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** Presents a novel algorithm that solves an open theoretical problem in Bayesian optimization.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** Provides the first proof of exponential convergence for a practical GP-based optimization algorithm.
3.  **Practical Advantage:** Eliminates the need for a costly auxiliary optimization of the acquisition function.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, with a clear explanation of the algorithm's mechanics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Major Scalability Concerns:** The hierarchical partitioning approach is unlikely to scale beyond low dimensions. This critical issue is neither addressed in the discussion nor convincingly refuted by the experiments, which are mostly on 1D-3D problems.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. The lack of a thorough investigation into its scalability is a significant drawback for its practical impact, but the theoretical novelty and the solution to a known open problem make it a solid contribution to the NIPS community.
