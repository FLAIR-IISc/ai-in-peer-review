- Proposes a novel GP-based Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling.  
- Leverages an unknown semi-metric to combine global and local search strategies.  
- Directly addresses an open problem of creating GP-based methods with exponential convergence (responding to Wang et al.).  
- Builds on prior work in Lipschitz estimation and GP-based Bayesian optimization, and relates to bound-based search methods.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and demonstrates an exponential convergence rate with a clear regret bound (Theorem 1).  
- Remarks on the tightness of the GP-estimated bound add useful theoretical insight.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible, even to readers less familiar with GPs and global optimization.  
- Figure 1 effectively illustrates the algorithm’s operation on a 1-D function.  
- Eliminates impractical techniques (auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling), increasing practical applicability.  
- Empirical results across benchmarks show strong performance, often outperforming SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI.  
- Analysis includes sensitivity exploration (e.g., effects of Ξmax) and discusses strengths, limitations, and future directions.  
- Minor editorial issues: an ambiguous phrase in the abstract (“redundant”) and a misplaced parenthesis in the function definition.  
- Scalability to very high-dimensional problems is a potential concern and remains an open issue.  
- Requires more thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (kernel parameters, η, Ξmax) to strengthen empirical claims.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant limitation addressed, rigorous theory, convincing experiments, clarity, and novel approach with substantial impact.  
- Arguments for rejection: unresolved scalability concerns and minor presentation errors needing polishing.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper worthy of acceptance with minor revisions to fix typos and clarify the abstract, and a suggestion to further explore scalability in future work.
