### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), for global optimization of deterministic black-box functions. The work is situated in the context of Gaussian Process (GP) based optimization and aims to solve a key open problem: achieving an exponential rate of convergence without relying on either an auxiliary non-convex optimization of an acquisition function (common in methods like GP-UCB) or an impractical δ-cover sampling procedure (a requirement of previous work with exponential guarantees). The core idea is to combine a GP-based upper confidence bound (UCB) with a hierarchical partitioning of the search space. The algorithm does not commit to a single bound given by the GP, but instead simultaneously considers an infinite family of potential bounds (semi-metrics), using the GP information to prune the search space efficiently. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an exponential regret bound and conduct experiments on several benchmark functions, showing that IMGPO outperforms other state-of-the-art methods like SOO, BaMSOO, and standard GP optimization with EI/PI acquisition functions.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important open problem in Bayesian optimization. The proposed method of integrating GP bounds with the "infinite-metric" optimization paradigm of algorithms like DIRECT and SOO is a novel and powerful combination. It elegantly bridges two distinct lines of research in global optimization, leading to a method that is both theoretically sound and practically effective.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper's contributions are very strong. The theoretical analysis provides the first exponential regret bound for a practical GP-based algorithm of this type, a clear improvement over the polynomial bounds of related methods like BaMSOO. The analysis in Remarks 1-3 is particularly insightful, offering clear intuition on how the GP-bound and the infinite-metric search interact and why their combination is advantageous. The experimental results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of IMGPO over several relevant baselines across a range of benchmark problems with varying dimensionalities.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and didactic. The motivation is well-established, and the authors build their case logically. The algorithm, despite its sophistication, is explained in an intuitive and accessible manner, aided significantly by the excellent use of graphics. Figure 1, in particular, is a superb visual aid that clarifies the complex mechanics of the interval selection and division process step-by-step. The overall high quality of the presentation makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Weaknesses

While this is a very strong paper, there are a few minor points that could be addressed to improve it further:

*   The phrasing in the abstract (around line 49) feels slightly redundant. The final sentence reads, "Our approach eliminates both requirements and achieves an exponential convergence rate." Since the preceding sentences establish that the goal is to match the exponential convergence rate of prior work *without* its impractical requirements, this could be phrased more concisely, for example: "Our approach eliminates both requirements while maintaining this exponential rate of convergence."
*   There appears to be a typo or error in the definition of λ in Theorem 1. The definition is given as `λ = 3 − α2CDρ̄t < 1`, which is a linear expression. This seems inconsistent with the exponential form of the regret bound, which can be written as `O(3^(-α[...]))`. It is likely that λ should be an exponential term itself, such as `3` raised to a negative power related to the constants in the bound. This is a crucial detail for the paper's main theoretical claim and should be carefully checked and corrected.
*   While the paper rightly acknowledges the challenge of high dimensionality and includes a promising experiment using REMBO, the discussion of scalability could be slightly expanded. The practical performance in moderately high dimensions (e.g., D = 20-50) without specialized embedding techniques remains an important question, and more commentary on the expected behavior of IMGPO in this regime would be valuable.

### Overall Assessment

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, well-motivated algorithm that makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The work is technically solid, empirically validated, and presented with outstanding clarity. It successfully solves a known open problem in the area. I am confident this paper will be of high interest to the NIPS community and will likely inspire future work at the intersection of hierarchical and Bayesian optimization methods. I strongly recommend acceptance.
