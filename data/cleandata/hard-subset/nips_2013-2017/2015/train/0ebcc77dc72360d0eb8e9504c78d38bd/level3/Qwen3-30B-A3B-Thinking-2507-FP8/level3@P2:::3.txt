

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization method that achieves exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization—two significant practical barriers in existing approaches. The authors address a well-known gap in the literature: de Freitas et al.'s [1] exponential convergence method relies on impractical δ-cover sampling, while Wang et al. [2] showed that GP-UCB requires auxiliary optimization. IMGPO resolves both issues by leveraging an "infinite-metric" framework that simultaneously considers all possible semi-metric bounds rather than committing to a single one.

**Quality.** The paper provides a technically sound theoretical contribution with a rigorous proof of exponential regret bounds (Theorem 1). The analysis carefully disentangles the effects of GP usage (Remark 2), bound tightness (Remark 1), and infinite-metric exploration (Remark 3), offering deeper theoretical insight than prior work. The experimental evaluation across eight diverse benchmarks (Figure 2) demonstrates consistent superiority over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, with compelling quantitative results. The authors appropriately address limitations (e.g., dimensionality scaling in Remark 1) and provide a clear ablation study of Ξmax. The supplementary material strengthens the theoretical claims. The paper is complete and thoroughly evaluated.

**Clarity.** The exposition is exceptionally clear. Section 3.2's illustrative example (Figure 1) effectively conveys the core intuition of IMGPO's hierarchical partitioning strategy. Algorithm 1 is well-structured and executable, with detailed line-by-line explanations in Section 3.3. The connection to related work (Section 3.4) precisely situates IMGPO within the DIRECT/SOO/GP-UCB lineage. The writing is precise without being overly technical, making the contribution accessible to the NIPS audience. Minor suggestions: The definition of ρ̄t (Definition 1) could be motivated earlier, and the supplementary material could include a brief comparison of computational complexity.

**Originality.** This work makes a significant conceptual advance by shifting from "relying on a single bound" (standard in GP-UCB) to "considering all possible bounds" (via the infinite-metric framework). This is not merely a combination of existing techniques but a novel theoretical perspective that explains *why* not relying on a single bound can be beneficial (Remarks 1-3). The paper explicitly connects to Jones et al.'s DIRECT [19] and Munos' SOO [18], but advances them with a theoretical guarantee and practical implementation. This is a meaningful step beyond the "two paths" (with/without bound) identified in the conclusion, addressing a longstanding open problem [2].

**Significance.** IMGPO removes two major practical barriers to applying Bayesian optimization: the need for δ-cover sampling (deemed "computationally too demanding" in [2]) and auxiliary optimization (which requires additional global solvers like CMA-ES). The experimental results confirm this advantage—IMGPO outperforms baselines in all but one benchmark (Rosenbrock2, where GP modeling failed), with substantial speedups over GP-UCB. The theoretical framework (Remarks 1-3) provides a new lens for analyzing bound-based methods beyond Bayesian optimization, potentially applicable to A* search or PAC-MDP algorithms. This is a substantial contribution to both theory and practice.

**Weaknesses.** The exponential convergence rate (λ < 1) becomes less favorable as dimensionality increases (Remark 1), though this is acknowledged. The paper does not fully resolve how to optimally select Ξmax in practice (though experiments show it is robust to moderate choices). The theoretical bound (Theorem 1) assumes Ω is a hyperrectangle, though the authors note this can be extended. These are manageable limitations given the paper's strong core contribution.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
- *Pro:* First method to achieve exponential convergence without δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization; compelling theory with practical insights; thorough experiments across diverse benchmarks; clear, well-structured writing.
- *Con:* Dimensionality scaling remains challenging (though acknowledged); Ξmax selection is empirical.

**Conclusion.** This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to Bayesian optimization by resolving two key practical limitations of existing exponential-convergence methods. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the implementation is well-documented, and the experimental results robustly demonstrate superiority. The infinite-metric framework offers a novel perspective with potential broader implications beyond Bayesian optimization. While high-dimensional scalability is a known challenge, this is not a flaw in the paper's contribution but a direction for future work. The paper aligns perfectly with NIPS' focus on high-impact theoretical and practical advances in machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
