

# Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary global optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of previous work: de Freitas et al.'s [1] method requires impractical δ-cover sampling, while Wang et al.'s [2] BaMSOO combines GP-UCB with hierarchical partitioning but achieves only polynomial regret. IMGPO overcomes these limitations by leveraging interval partitioning and using UCB to guide exploration without internal optimization.

The core innovation lies in considering "infinitely many possible bounds" rather than relying on a single upper bound. The authors formalize this as an "infinite-metric exploration" framework where they simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all candidate bounds, using the GP's UCB to prune less promising candidates. This approach is theoretically grounded in Assumption 1 (unknown bound) and leads to the key theoretical contribution: Theorem 1 proves an exponential regret bound O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1, representing a significant improvement over BaMSOO's polynomial regret.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and clearly demonstrates exponential regret improvement over prior work. The algorithm is well-described with a helpful illustrative example (Figure 1) showing the interval partitioning process. The relationship to previous algorithms (DIRECT, SOO, GP-UCB) is clearly articulated, and the paper makes a genuine novel contribution to the field. The writing is clear and well-organized, with the pseudocode (Algorithm 1) providing sufficient detail for reproducibility.

However, the paper has significant limitations that affect its overall contribution. The most serious issue is that all experimental results are limited to 1D test functions, despite the paper's claim of being applicable to general D-dimensional problems. This is a major concern because the authors themselves note in Remark 1 that "λ can get close to one as input dimension D increases," suggesting scalability challenges. The paper fails to address how the interval-partitioning approach would perform in higher dimensions, where the required resolution grows exponentially with dimensionality. The authors mention a relationship to DIRECT, which is known to perform poorly in high dimensions, but do not discuss this implication for their method.

The paper also has minor issues with clarity. The sentence about "simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds" is unclear. The reason why the far-right region has no function evaluation at n=16 is not explained, which would help readers understand the algorithm's behavior. Additionally, the paper states that UCB is used "for brevity" but doesn't clarify whether other acquisition functions like expected improvement could be used instead.

The significance of the work is mixed. The theoretical contribution is sound and represents a meaningful advance in the Bayesian optimization literature. However, the limited experimental validation to 1D functions significantly undermines the practical significance, as the primary challenge for Bayesian optimization methods is scaling to higher dimensions. While the theoretical contribution stands on its own, the practical relevance is questionable without evidence of performance in higher dimensions.

In summary, this is a technically sound and novel theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of Bayesian optimization. The paper makes a significant theoretical improvement with a clear proof of exponential regret. However, the lack of experimental validation beyond 1D problems and insufficient discussion of scalability concerns are serious limitations for a conference paper that should demonstrate both theoretical and practical relevance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contribution with clear exponential regret improvement
- Novel insight about not relying on a single upper bound
- Well-written with clear algorithm description and example
- Properly contextualizes relationship to previous work

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental results limited to 1D functions (not explicitly stated)
- Fails to address scalability concerns for higher dimensions
- Does not discuss relationship to DIRECT (known to perform poorly in high dimensions)
- Minor clarity issues with explanation of algorithm

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that would be suitable for publication at NIPS if the authors could address the scalability concerns with additional experiments in higher dimensions or a more thorough discussion of the limitations. As presented, the paper's practical relevance is limited due to the narrow experimental validation, which is a significant drawback for a conference that emphasizes both theoretical and practical contributions. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of scalability and additional experiments in higher dimensions.
