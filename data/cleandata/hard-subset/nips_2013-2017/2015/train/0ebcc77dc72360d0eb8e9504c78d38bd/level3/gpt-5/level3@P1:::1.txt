Summary and contributions
The paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian-process-based global optimization algorithm that avoids both (i) the auxiliary nonconvex acquisition-optimization step common in GP-BO and (ii) the impractical δ-cover sampling required by de Freitas et al. (ICML’12) to obtain exponential regret for deterministic objectives. IMGPO combines hierarchical partitioning with GP-UCB-style upper bounds to screen and expand hyperrectangles, while maintaining “infinite-metric” optimism over an unknown semi-metric that upper-bounds suboptimality near a global maximizer. The main theoretical result establishes an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) (λ<1), where Ngp counts “virtual” GP-based screenings that bypass objective evaluations, and the bound adapts to unknown smoothness parameters (L, α, p). The analysis introduces the infinite-metric exploration loss ρ̄t to capture the cost of not committing to a single bound, and shows how GP information reduces this loss relative to SOO/DOO-style methods. Empirical comparisons against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-EI and GP-PI on low-dimensional synthetic functions, plus a REMBO wrapper for a 1000-D embedded case, suggest favorable performance and reduced wall-clock time compared to standard GP-BO with acquisition optimization.

Relation to prior work
- GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., ICML’10) and practical BO (Snoek et al., NIPS’12) require solving an auxiliary global optimization at each iteration; IMGPO removes this step via hierarchical partitioning.
- de Freitas et al. (ICML’12) proved exponential regret for deterministic GP bandits with δ-cover sampling; IMGPO claims exponential rates without δ-covers, addressing a challenge highlighted in BaMSOO (Wang et al., AISTATS’14).
- The method leans on the SOO/DOO lineage (Munos, NIPS’11) and DIRECT (Jones et al., 1993) in using partitioning and optimism, but strengthens guarantees via GP-informed pruning.
- The paper does not compare against information-theoretic BO (Entropy Search, Hennig & Schuler; Predictive Entropy Search, Hernández-Lobato et al., NIPS’14), which are relevant strong baselines.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: elegant fusion of bound-based partitioning with GP posteriors to eliminate acquisition maximization and δ-covers, while retaining exponential convergence under reasonable deterministic assumptions.
- Theory: clear statement of assumptions; regret bound adapts to unknown smoothness and makes the roles of dimension D, ρ̄t, and GP screening explicit. The introduced analysis tools (ρ̄t, effect of Ngp, Ξn) are insightful for understanding tightness of bounds and exploration cost.
- Clarity and positioning: well written, with helpful intuition (Figure 1), explicit pseudocode, and a thoughtful discussion (Remarks 1–3) about when infinite-metric optimism helps.
- Practical implications: reduced reliance on hard-to-tune acquisition optimizers; code availability; empirical evidence that skipping many f-evaluations via GP screening can save time.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation below NIPS standards:
  - Baselines: missing strong information-theoretic methods (ES/PES) and modern Thompson sampling variants; GP-UCB is indirectly represented via BaMSOO but not directly compared.
  - Protocol: most BO studies use multiple random initial evaluations to stabilize GP hyperparameter estimation and allow fair statistical comparisons; here, deterministic starts are used for IMGPO/SOO/BaMSOO and single runs are reported for these, while EI/PI are averaged, making comparisons uneven.
  - Scope: only synthetic test functions up to D=6 (plus one REMBO case) with no real-world hyperparameter tuning or physical/wet-lab tasks (as in Snoek et al., NIPS’12), limiting claims about practical impact.
  - Ablations: sensitivity to Ξmax, kernel misspecification, and hyperparameter learning strategy are only lightly explored; wall-clock comparisons are limited.
- Assumption–implementation gap: the theoretical guarantee appears to assume a fixed GP prior, but the algorithm updates hyperparameters online via empirical Bayes; the impact of adaptive hyperparameters on the UCB validity (and union bound over calls) is not discussed or controlled.
- Scalability: the bound degrades with D (λ→1 as D grows), and the paper acknowledges remaining challenges in higher dimensions; more evidence beyond a single REMBO case would help.

Quality
- Technically sound overall with a meaningful advance over BaMSOO’s polynomial rates; however, the proof’s dependence on GP confidence bounds should explicitly state assumptions on kernel correctness and hyperparameter learning. Complexity per iteration (including GP updates O(n^3)) and the effect of Ξmax on computation could be summarized more concretely.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. Minor suggestions: define Ngp and ρ̄t earlier in the algorithm section; provide a concise complexity table (per iteration and cumulative); clarify T′h+ξ and the worst-case cost of step (iii).

Originality
- High. Achieving exponential deterministic regret without δ-covers or acquisition maximization, and the infinite-metric analysis framing, are novel and nontrivial.

Significance
- Potentially significant for deterministic black-box optimization, especially when acquisition maximization is costly or unreliable. Current empirical support is not yet sufficient to establish practical dominance across standard BO workloads.

Questions for the authors
1. How does the regret proof accommodate adaptive hyperparameter learning? Can you provide a variant with fixed hyperparameters to cleanly separate theory and practice?
2. What are typical values observed for ρ̄t and Ξn across problems, and how do they correlate with performance?
3. Can you report wall-clock and regret comparisons versus PES/ES and GP-UCB with robust acquisition maximization?
4. How sensitive is IMGPO to kernel misspecification relative to BaMSOO/GP-UCB?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comprehensive baselines (ES, PES, TS) with consistent multi-start initial designs and repeated runs; report mean±std and statistical tests.
- Include real-world BO tasks (hyperparameter tuning, robotics, chemical synthesis).
- Provide ablations on Ξmax, kernel choice, and hyperparameter learning; report time–regret trade-offs.
- Clarify theoretical assumptions regarding hyperparameters and kernel correctness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel algorithmic idea that removes two major practical hurdles (δ-covers, acquisition optimization).
- Strong theoretical contribution: exponential regret with an insightful analysis framework.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental section is limited and uneven, missing key baselines and real-world tasks.
- Some mismatch between theoretical assumptions and implemented procedure (adaptive hyperparameters).
- Scalability concerns not fully addressed empirically.

Recommendation: 5 (borderline reject). The theoretical contribution is interesting and well presented, but the empirical evaluation falls short of NIPS standards. With stronger experiments and clearer treatment of hyperparameter learning in the theory, this could become a solid paper.

Reviewer confidence: 0.7 (familiar with BO literature and optimistic partitioning methods).
