## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm, dubbed IMGPO, aiming to achieve exponential convergence without relying on auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling – limitations of existing methods like Freitas et al. (2012). The core idea revolves around leveraging an unknown semi-metric to simultaneously explore a wider range of potential bounds, effectively mitigating the need for precise Lipschitz constant estimation inherent in many BO approaches. The paper clearly positions IMGPO within the broader landscape of bound-based search methods, drawing connections to Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and PAC-MDP algorithms.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions. The authors successfully demonstrate an exponential convergence rate, addressing a significant open problem in the BO community. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow from problem definition to algorithm description and analysis. The detailed explanation of the algorithm's components and the rigorous mathematical analysis are commendable. The inclusion of a publicly available codebase is also a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility.

However, the experimental evaluation significantly weakens the paper’s impact. The most glaring issue is the absence of multiple initial evaluations of the objective function *f*, a standard practice in Bayesian optimization to account for randomness and obtain statistically meaningful results. Without this, the reported performance comparisons lack robustness. Furthermore, the paper completely omits comparisons to state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods such as Entropy Search or other information-theoretic approaches. All experiments are conducted on synthetic functions with a maximum dimension of 6, lacking any assessment of performance on real-world problems (e.g., parameter tuning or wet-lab optimization). While the theoretical results are promising, the lack of compelling empirical validation raises concerns about the practical applicability of IMGPO. 

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically sound and well-articulated algorithm with a novel approach to Bayesian optimization. However, the weak experimental section—specifically the lack of proper statistical comparisons and real-world evaluations—prevents it from reaching its full potential. I vote a 5, but lower the score due to the critical shortcomings in the experimental setup. Addressing these experimental deficiencies would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
