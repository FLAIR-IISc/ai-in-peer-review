This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), achieving exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing limitations of previous approaches like de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The core idea revolves around leveraging a hierarchical partitioning scheme coupled with Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) acquisition, while simultaneously considering a broad range of possible bounds on the objective function, rather than relying on a potentially inaccurate GP-derived bound alone.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, algorithm details, theoretical analysis, and experimental results. It effectively connects to the existing literature in Bayesian optimization, Lipschitz optimization, and related global optimization techniques like DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]. The inclusion of an illustrative example in Section 3.2 significantly aids understanding of the algorithm's mechanics. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, provides a solid foundation for the claimed exponential convergence rate. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** IMGPO presents a genuinely new approach to Bayesian optimization, circumventing the computational burden of δ-cover sampling and the need for auxiliary optimization. The concept of simultaneously considering multiple potential bounds is a clever way to mitigate the impact of inaccurate GP predictions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical justification for the algorithm, demonstrating exponential convergence under reasonable assumptions. The analysis considers the interplay between exploration loss (ρt) and the quality of the GP-estimated bound, providing valuable insights.
* **Clarity:** The paper is remarkably well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity. The algorithm is described precisely, and the rationale behind each step is clearly articulated.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the relevant literature, positioning IMGPO within the broader context of global optimization and Bayesian methods. The comparison to related algorithms like SOO, BaMSOO, and DIRECT is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The primary concern is the scope of the experimental evaluation. While the paper presents results on eight test functions (Figure 2), the visualization primarily focuses on 1D functions. While the authors state that one function is embedded in 1000 dimensions, it’s unclear how the partitioning scheme performs in truly high-dimensional spaces beyond this single case. A more comprehensive evaluation on higher-dimensional benchmark problems is crucial.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The hierarchical partitioning approach, while theoretically sound, raises significant scalability concerns. The resolution required to maintain accuracy grows exponentially with dimensionality, potentially rendering the method impractical for high-dimensional problems. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 4 but do not sufficiently discuss how it compares to the known scalability issues of DIRECT, which is a closely related algorithm. A more detailed discussion of potential mitigation strategies (e.g., adaptive partitioning, dimensionality reduction) would be beneficial.
* **Minor Clarifications Needed:** The sentence in Section 3.1, "We simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds," is somewhat vague and could benefit from further elaboration. The reasoning behind the lack of function evaluation at n=16 for the far-right interval (Figure 1) is also unclear. Finally, while the paper focuses on UCB for brevity, clarifying whether other acquisition functions are compatible would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound Bayesian optimization algorithm with compelling advantages over existing methods.
* The analysis is rigorous and provides a clear understanding of the algorithm's convergence properties.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.
* The work addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian optimization – achieving exponential convergence without computationally expensive auxiliary steps.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited and does not provide sufficient evidence of the algorithm's performance in high-dimensional spaces.
* Scalability concerns related to the hierarchical partitioning scheme are significant and require further discussion.
* The limited scope of experimental validation weakens the claim of practical applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in the empirical evaluation and the acknowledged scalability concerns, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical novelty and clarity of presentation are particularly commendable.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.  The authors should significantly expand the experimental evaluation to include higher-dimensional benchmark problems and provide a more in-depth discussion of the scalability challenges and potential mitigation strategies.  Addressing the minor clarification requests would also enhance the paper's overall quality. While the practical applicability remains somewhat uncertain, the theoretical contributions are strong enough to warrant acceptance.
