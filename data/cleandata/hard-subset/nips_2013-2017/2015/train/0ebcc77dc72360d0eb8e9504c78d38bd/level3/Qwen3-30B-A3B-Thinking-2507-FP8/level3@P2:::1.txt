

# Review for "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper introduces IMGPO (Infinite-Metric GP Optimization), a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address a significant limitation in existing approaches: de Freitas et al. (2012) established exponential regret bounds but required impractical δ-cover sampling, while Wang et al. (2014) proposed a practical method but only achieved polynomial regret. IMGPO generalizes the approach by considering all possible semi-metric bounds rather than relying on a single GP-based bound, thereby eliminating the need for δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization. The paper positions itself well within the bounded-based search literature, drawing connections to DIRECT, SOO, and GP-UCB.

Theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. The paper provides a clear proof of exponential regret bounds, with insightful remarks analyzing how the tightness of the GP upper bound affects performance. The regret bound O(λ^(N+Ng_p)) with λ < 1 represents a meaningful improvement over BaMSOO's polynomial bound, particularly for functions where the GP bound is not tight. The analysis of how the unknown semi-metric affects performance provides valuable theoretical insights into the inherent difficulty of optimization problems.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The algorithm is described with a helpful figure, pseudocode, and detailed explanation of the hierarchical partitioning procedure. The connection to previous work (DIRECT, SOO, GP-UCB) is thorough and well-motivated. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations, including the scalability challenge in higher dimensions and the potential trade-off between GP usage and exploration loss.

Regarding quality, the theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-supported. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations. The clarity is excellent, with the paper providing sufficient detail for reproduction. The originality is strong, as the approach of simultaneously considering infinitely many possible bounds is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature.

However, the experimental evaluation significantly undermines the paper's impact. The authors compare IMGPO with SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, but fail to address standard practices in Bayesian optimization. Specifically, the experiments do not use multiple initial evaluations of f (standard in BO to avoid bias), preventing meaningful statistical comparisons. The paper also omits comparisons with state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods like Entropy Search, which would have strengthened the experimental validation.

The experimental scope is limited to synthetic functions with maximum dimension 6, lacking any real-world applications (e.g., parameter tuning in machine learning or engineering design). The absence of real-world evaluations is particularly concerning given the paper's claim to provide a "practically oriented analysis framework." While the authors mention a 1000-dimensional test function using REMBO with IMGPO as a subroutine, this is merely a theoretical illustration rather than a substantial evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical contribution with clear proof of exponential regret
- Well-written, clearly positioned within literature
- Novel approach of considering infinitely many possible bounds
- Careful discussion of limitations and theoretical insights

**Weaknesses:**
- Experimental section lacks standard BO practices (multiple initial evaluations)
- Missing comparisons with state-of-the-art BO methods (e.g., Entropy Search)
- Limited to synthetic functions (max dimension 6), no real-world applications
- Insufficient statistical validation of results

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The theoretical contribution is significant and addresses a clear gap in the literature
- The paper provides valuable theoretical insights into why not relying on a single bound can be beneficial
- The algorithm eliminates two practical barriers in Bayesian optimization (δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization)

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The experimental evaluation is below NIPS standards, lacking proper statistical comparisons and real-world validation
- The absence of comparisons with leading BO methods weakens the claim of practical advantage
- The limited scope of experiments (only synthetic functions, low dimensionality) fails to demonstrate broader applicability

This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to Bayesian optimization with strong theoretical guarantees. However, the experimental evaluation is inadequate for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The authors should address the experimental weaknesses by adding multiple initial evaluations, comparing with state-of-the-art BO methods, and including at least one real-world application to demonstrate practical utility. Without these improvements, the paper's claim of "practical" relevance remains unsubstantiated. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental limitations prevent it from meeting NIPS' high bar for empirical validation. I recommend a borderline decision, but the experimental shortcomings make the paper difficult to recommend for acceptance at this stage.
