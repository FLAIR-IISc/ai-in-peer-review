This paper presents a Bayesian optimization method, termed Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), achieving exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or the impractical δ-cover sampling of de Freitas et al. (2012). The authors address a significant limitation of previous work, offering a more practical approach to exponential regret minimization in Bayesian optimization. The work is framed within the context of bound-based search methods and provides a clear connection to Lipschitz optimization, A* search, and PAC-MDP algorithms. The theoretical contribution is substantial, building on previous work by Munos (2012) and Wang et al. (2013), and offering a novel perspective on utilizing GP priors alongside unknown, potentially tighter bounds.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the contribution relative to existing literature. The algorithm description is lucid, aided by the illustrative figure, and the technical details are presented in a rigorous manner. The relationship to previous algorithms, particularly BaMSOO and SOO, is clearly articulated, and the analysis section provides a solid theoretical foundation for the claims made.  The authors demonstrate awareness of the limitations of their method, discussing potential scalability issues and future directions.

The originality of this work lies in the innovative approach of simultaneously considering all possible bounds, leveraging the GP prior to prune unlikely candidates, and the resulting exponential convergence guarantee without the problematic δ-cover sampling. The authors appropriately reference related work, including foundational papers in Bayesian optimization and global optimization (e.g., Rasmussen & Williams, 2006; Jones et al., 1993).  The theoretical analysis, demonstrating a clear improvement over existing polynomial regret bounds, further strengthens the originality of the contribution.

The significance of this work is potentially high. A practical Bayesian optimization method with provable exponential convergence could have a substantial impact on various fields, including machine learning hyperparameter tuning, engineering design, and biological modeling (as cited in the introduction). The theoretical insights regarding the interplay between known and unknown bounds are valuable and may inspire further research in bound-based search methods. However, the significance is currently tempered by the limitations in the experimental evaluation.

Despite the strong theoretical foundation, the experimental section is a notable weakness. While the results demonstrate IMGPO's effectiveness on several synthetic functions, the experimental setup lacks crucial elements for a robust comparison. Specifically, the absence of multiple initial random evaluations of the objective function is a significant oversight, as it's standard practice in Bayesian optimization to mitigate the impact of initial exploration. This makes statistical comparison with other methods difficult and potentially misleading. Moreover, the paper does not compare IMGPO against state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization methods beyond GP-UCB, GP-PI and GP-EI; information-theoretic approaches such as Entropy Search are notably absent.  Furthermore, all experiments are conducted on synthetic functions with a maximum dimensionality of 6. The lack of evaluation on real-world problems—such as optimizing parameters for a machine learning model or solving a practical engineering design problem—limits the demonstrable impact and applicability of the proposed method. The experiments primarily serve to validate the theoretical results on well-defined test cases rather than demonstrate practical utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical guarantees: The paper presents a novel algorithm with provable exponential convergence.
*   Clear and well-written presentation: The paper is easy to understand and logically structured.
*   Addresses a practical limitation: It overcomes the δ-cover sampling issue in previous work.
*   Novel approach: The concept of simultaneously considering all possible bounds is innovative.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak experimental evaluation: Lack of multiple initializations, comparisons with modern BO methods, and real-world validation.
*   Limited scalability demonstrated: Experiments only up to 6 dimensions.
*   Experimental setup does not follow standard Bayesian optimization practices.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. However, the limited and somewhat unorthodox experimental evaluation weakens its overall impact. While the theoretical results are compelling, the lack of robust experimental validation raises concerns about the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the shortcomings in the experimental section. Specifically, they should include multiple initial random evaluations, compare against a wider range of state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization algorithms, and ideally, include results on at least one real-world problem.  A revised version addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.

**Score:** 6 (Accept with Minor Revisions)
