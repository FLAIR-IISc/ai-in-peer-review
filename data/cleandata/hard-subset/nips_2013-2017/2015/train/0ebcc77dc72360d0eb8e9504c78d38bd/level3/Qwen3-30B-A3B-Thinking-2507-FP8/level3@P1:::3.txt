

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method with exponential convergence that eliminates two practical barriers in existing approaches: the need for auxiliary optimization and δ-cover sampling. The authors introduce Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a GP-based algorithm that maintains an exponential regret bound without relying on the impractical δ-cover sampling procedure or requiring additional global optimization steps for acquisition function maximization.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by addressing a key open problem identified by Wang et al. [2] regarding the development of a GP-based algorithm with exponential convergence rate that avoids δ-cover sampling. The authors' key insight is to consider infinitely many possible upper bounds encoded in the continuity at a global optimizer, rather than relying on a single bound provided by the GP prior. This approach is theoretically justified through Assumption 1 (Unknown Bound) and formalized in Theorem 1, which establishes an exponential regret bound of O(λ^(N+Ngp)) with λ < 1. The analysis in Section 4 is particularly strong, with Remark 1-3 providing valuable insights into how the tightness of GP's UCB, the use of GP, and the consideration of infinitely many bounds affect the regret bound.

The paper demonstrates excellent clarity through its structured presentation. Section 3.2 provides a well-executed illustrative example (Figure 1) that effectively conveys the algorithm's intuition, while Section 3.3 offers complete programmatic details in Algorithm 1 that facilitate implementation and replication. The pseudocode is well-commented and the description of the hierarchical partitioning procedure is precise. The authors effectively relate their work to prior literature, particularly clarifying the relationship with BaMSOO [2] and SOO [18] in Section 3.4.

The experimental evaluation (Section 5) provides compelling evidence of IMGPO's effectiveness. The authors compare their method against SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI across eight diverse multimodal benchmark functions, demonstrating consistent performance improvements. Notably, the experiments were conducted under realistic assumptions (using a single isotropic Matern kernel with blind initialization) that reflect practical implementation scenarios. The results in Figure 2 clearly show IMGPO's superiority, with the only exception being Rosenbrock2 where the GP prior was misleading. The ablation study in Table 1 further demonstrates that IMGPO is significantly faster than traditional GP optimization methods while maintaining competitive performance.

The paper's theoretical contribution is particularly noteworthy for its practical implications. The analysis reveals that even when a bound is known, it may be beneficial not to rely on it exclusively, which challenges conventional wisdom in bound-based search methods. This insight extends beyond Bayesian optimization to other domains like planning algorithms (A* search) and learning algorithms (PAC-MDP), as noted in the conclusion.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is complete and the experimental results corroborate the theoretical findings. The authors are careful to address both strengths and limitations, noting that the exponential convergence rate becomes less effective as dimensionality increases (Remark 1), suggesting a direction for future work.

For significance, this work advances Bayesian optimization by providing a theoretically grounded method that removes two practical barriers to implementation. The exponential convergence rate is a substantial improvement over the polynomial regret bounds of previous GP-based methods like BaMSOO [2]. The paper's practical orientation, demonstrated through the clear implementation details and realistic experimental setup, makes it highly relevant to the NIPS community and practitioners in machine learning.

The main limitation worth noting is the computational cost associated with considering infinitely many bounds, which is reflected in the need for the Ξmax parameter. However, the paper demonstrates that this cost is manageable and the authors provide guidance on selecting appropriate values for this parameter.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, addressing a fundamental practical limitation in the literature while advancing theoretical understanding. The clear presentation, strong theoretical foundation, and compelling experimental results make it a worthy contribution to NIPS. The paper successfully bridges theoretical advances with practical implementation considerations, offering both new theoretical insights and a practically useful algorithm for the Bayesian optimization community.
