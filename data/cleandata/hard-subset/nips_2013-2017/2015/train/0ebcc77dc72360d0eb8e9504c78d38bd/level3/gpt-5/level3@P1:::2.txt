This paper proposes IMGPO, a Gaussian process (GP)–based Bayesian optimization algorithm that avoids auxiliary nonconvex optimization for acquisition maximization and does not rely on δ-cover sampling. The method replaces the usual “optimize the acquisition” step with a hierarchical space-partitioning scheme (rectangular splits along the longest coordinate) and a bound-based selection strategy. The core idea is to combine two sources of optimism: (i) the probabilistic UCB bound from the GP posterior and (ii) an unknown but “tighter” semi-metric bound around the global optimizer, formalized by Assumption 1 and generalized by Assumption 2 (Hölder-type smoothness). Rather than committing to a single bound, IMGPO entertains infinitely many candidate bounds (“infinite-metric”), using the GP UCB to prune candidates that are looser than the known GP-derived bound. The algorithm keeps, for each depth, the most promising interval, filters them via UCB lookahead over a limited expansion horizon Ξ, and divides accepted intervals into three. It can also avoid function evaluations when the UCB at a candidate center is already below the incumbent. Theoretical analysis yields an exponential simple-regret bound O(λN+Ngp) with λ < 1, without δ-cover sampling and without acquisition maximization, improving on the polynomial regret of BaMSOO (which combines SOO with GP-UCB). The paper situates the method with respect to DIRECT and SOO, and explains when “not committing to a particular bound” is advantageous via the “infinite-metric exploration loss” ρt. Experiments on several benchmark functions (including Branin, Hartmann3, Rosenbrock2) and a 1000-D REMBO-embedded example show strong empirical performance and substantial wall-clock savings over GP methods that require auxiliary optimization.

Relation to prior work: Compared to GP-UCB (Srinivas et al.) and the exponential deterministic bound of de Freitas et al. (which assumes δ-cover sampling), IMGPO eliminates both the δ-cover requirement and acquisition maximization. Compared to SOO and BaMSOO (Wang et al.), IMGPO leverages GP information to prune and skip evaluations and achieves exponential rather than polynomial regret. The connection to DIRECT (Jones et al.) is acknowledged: IMGPO generalizes the “unknown metric” idea and provides finite-time exponential guarantees under the GP-UCB filtering.

Quality: The theory appears careful and technically sound. The assumptions are explicit; the confidence parameterization for UCB is chosen to ensure f(x) ≤ U(x|D) with high probability. The regret analysis is transparent about dimension dependence (λ approaches 1 as D increases) and decomposes the roles of ρ̄t, Ξ, and Ngp, with insightful remarks on when GP bounds help or hurt. One caveat is that the high-probability guarantee requires well-calibrated GP posteriors; empirical Bayes hyperparameter updates may violate the assumed confidence property in practice (as the authors note on Rosenbrock2). The experimental methodology is reasonable and includes comparisons to SOO, BaMSOO, and GP-PI/EI with common kernels and hyperparameter learning, though the breadth of higher-dimensional, non-embedded tests is limited. Overall, the work constitutes a complete contribution with theory, algorithm, and experiments.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear, with helpful pseudocode and a step-by-step figure. A few points could be clarified: (i) the statement “simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds” would benefit from a more operational description (the depth-wise selection and GP-pruned lookahead is the mechanism); (ii) explain more explicitly in text and figure captions why certain centers are not evaluated (step v: UCB below f+ implies a GP-based placeholder value); (iii) discuss the computational cost per iteration and the effect of Ξ and Ξmax on UCB computations; and (iv) be explicit that UCB (not EI/PI) is needed for the proofs because it provides valid upper bounds with high probability.

Originality: High. Combining hierarchical partitioning with GP-UCB–based pruning to avoid both δ-cover and auxiliary optimization, and proving exponential regret under reasonable smoothness, are novel. The “infinite-metric exploration loss” is an insightful analytical device that helps compare to methods that commit to a specific bound.

Significance: Strong theoretical significance for BO: exponential convergence in a practical algorithmic framework. Practically, removing auxiliary optimization is impactful. However, the partitioning approach likely faces scalability limits as D grows, and the theory acknowledges this; the practical significance would be strengthened by more direct evaluations beyond low-dimensional functions and the REMBO-embedded example, or by integrating structural assumptions (e.g., additive models, random embeddings) as part of the method.

Pros:
- Eliminates auxiliary acquisition optimization and δ-cover while achieving exponential regret.
- Clear algorithmic design with principled use of GP UCB to prune and skip evaluations.
- Insightful analysis (ρt, role of GP, comparison to committing to a bound).
- Strong empirical results on several benchmarks and wall-clock advantages.

Cons:
- Scalability concerns inherent to interval partitioning; λ worsens with D and pruning may not suffice in high dimensions.
- Empirical evaluation focuses mostly on low-dimensional problems; the single high-D example relies on REMBO.
- Theoretical guarantees assume deterministic noise-free evaluations and correctly calibrated UCB; empirical Bayes may break calibration.
- Limited discussion of DIRECT’s well-known degradation in higher D and how IMGPO differs practically.
- Minor clarity issues around step (v), the “simultaneous” search phrasing, and whether other acquisitions could be used.

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Is UCB essential for both practice and proofs, or can IMGPO use other acquisitions (e.g., EI) with modified screening? Please explain what breaks without valid upper bounds.
- Provide per-iteration complexity and empirical CPU-time breakdown for UCB computations vs. GP updates vs. evaluations across Ξ settings; discuss guidelines for Ξmax.
- Expand the discussion of DIRECT’s high-D behavior and explain why GP-guided pruning changes the practical picture (or not).
- Include additional mid- to high-dimensional (D ≥ 10–20) benchmarks without embeddings, or combine IMGPO with additive/random-embedding structures and report results.
- Discuss robustness to kernel mis-specification and the impact on the high-probability UCB property; if possible, add an experiment with deliberately mismatched kernels.
- Comment on extensions to noisy observations and whether exponential rates survive under sub-Gaussian noise.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a novel and theoretically strong contribution that removes two key practical obstacles for GP-based BO. While scalability and evaluation breadth remain concerns for practical impact, the ideas and analysis are valuable to the community, and the algorithm shows promising empirical performance.
