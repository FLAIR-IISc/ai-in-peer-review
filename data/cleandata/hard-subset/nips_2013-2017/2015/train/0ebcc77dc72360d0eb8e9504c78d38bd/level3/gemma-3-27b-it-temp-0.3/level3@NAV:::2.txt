## Review of "Bayesian Optimization with Exponential Convergence"

This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization (BO) method, dubbed IMGPO, achieving exponential convergence without relying on auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling – limitations of prior work like de Freitas et al. [1]. The core idea is to leverage interval partitioning and UCB to efficiently explore the search space, effectively handling the unknown Lipschitz constant inherent in black-box optimization problems. The method is clearly described, and the inclusion of a 1D example aids understanding. 

The contribution is significant. Existing BO methods often struggle with the computational cost of inner optimization loops or impractical sampling requirements. IMGPO elegantly sidesteps these issues, offering a theoretically sound approach with a proven exponential regret bound. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the broader landscape of global optimization, referencing relevant literature on Lipschitz optimization, DIRECT, and recent advances in GP-based BO. The analysis is rigorous, building upon previous results and highlighting the benefits of considering a wider range of potential bounds.

However, a major weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The results presented appear limited to 1D test functions. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the lack of demonstration in higher dimensions raises concerns about scalability. This is a critical point, as the interval partitioning approach inherently suffers from the curse of dimensionality – the required resolution grows exponentially with the number of dimensions. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don’t offer sufficient discussion on potential mitigation strategies or empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims in higher-dimensional spaces. 

Furthermore, the method's relationship to DIRECT [19] is not fully explored. DIRECT is known to perform poorly in high dimensions, and the authors should explicitly address whether IMGPO shares similar limitations and, if so, how it attempts to overcome them. While the paper mentions leveraging GP to reduce the number of candidates, a more in-depth discussion of how this impacts scalability is needed.

Minor points include clarifying whether UCB is strictly required or if other acquisition functions could be used, and rephrasing the sentence regarding “simultaneous global and local searches.” The explanation for the lack of function evaluation at n=16 for the far-right region could also be clarified.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel theoretical contribution, clear presentation, addresses a significant challenge in BO.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited experimental validation (only 1D), scalability concerns not adequately addressed, insufficient discussion of relationship to DIRECT.

**Overall:** This is a sound and novel work with a strong theoretical foundation. However, the limited experimental evaluation significantly weakens the paper. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors expanding the experimental results to include higher-dimensional problems and providing a more thorough discussion of the method’s scalability and connection to DIRECT.
