

This paper introduces Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), a novel Bayesian optimization method that achieves exponential convergence without requiring δ-cover sampling or auxiliary optimization, addressing two significant limitations in existing approaches. The authors build upon recent work by de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], who identified the impracticality of δ-cover sampling and the computational burden of auxiliary optimization in GP-based methods. IMGPO cleverly leverages the idea of considering infinitely many possible upper bounds derived from the GP prior rather than relying on a single tight bound, which allows it to maintain exponential convergence rates while avoiding the two problematic requirements.

The paper excels in all four key criteria. Quality is strong: the theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) demonstrates an exponential regret bound with rigorous proofs in the supplementary material, and the experimental results on eight benchmark functions compellingly show IMGPO outperforming SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI. Clarity is excellent: the main contributions are well-organized, Section 3.2 provides an intuitive illustrative example with Figure 1, and Section 3.3 offers detailed implementation guidance that facilitates replication. Originality is significant: the approach bridges two previously divergent paths in global optimization (bound-based methods with and without continuity estimation) in a novel way that addresses practical limitations of existing methods. Significance is high: the method provides a theoretically sound, practically implementable solution to a longstanding open problem in Bayesian optimization, with potential applications across machine learning, engineering, and scientific domains.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and represents a strong contribution to the NIPS community. The only minor limitation is the theoretical analysis showing that the convergence rate parameter λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, suggesting potential scalability challenges in very high dimensions. However, the authors acknowledge this and propose a path forward using additional assumptions.

Pro: Solves a significant practical limitation in Bayesian optimization; provides a theoretically rigorous and empirically validated approach; excellent organization and clarity; strong connections to prior work. Con: Limited discussion of scalability in very high dimensions (though acknowledged by authors).

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and should be accepted.
