- Proposes a Bayesian global optimization algorithm that replaces acquisition maximization with hierarchical space/interval partitioning guided by GP-UCB and proves exponential simple-regret under an unknown semi-metric smoothness assumption.  
- Method conceptually blends GP-UCB with DIRECT and SOO/DOO, using the GP to prune partitions without committing to a single smoothness bound.  
- Theoretical contribution: exponential-regret improvement over BaMSOO (which had polynomial rates), with analysis exposing roles of infinite-metric exploration loss and GP-driven pruning and noting λ can approach 1 as dimension D grows.  
- Algorithm is clearly described and supported by a helpful step-by-step example.  
- Empirical evaluation seems restricted to 1D (not explicitly stated), a major limitation given the partitioning strategy; paper should include ≥3D experiments or clearly state scope.  
- Scalability concerns: potential exponential blow-up of partitions in higher dimensions and need to discuss DIRECT’s known degradation in high-D with quantitative scaling expectations for IMGPO.  
- Clarity issues to fix: (i) whether UCB is essential or EI/PI can provide similar guarantees, (ii) formalize “candidates of the bounds” and how the GP eliminates them, (iii) explain example behavior at n=16 (region split without evaluation and resolution of GP-based placeholders).  
- Originality: novel combination of GP-driven bounds with infinite-metric partitioning and elimination of auxiliary optimization; advances theory beyond prior work.  
- Significance: could influence practical BO by removing inner-loop optimization and offering stronger guarantees if empirical results extend beyond 1D; scalability remains a substantial obstacle.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel algorithm, clear exposition, exponential regret bound, elimination of auxiliary acquisition optimization, and insightful analysis on bound tightness and exploration loss.  
- Cons for acceptance: experiments appear limited to 1D, scalability to higher dimensions untested, potential exponential partition growth, and a few minor clarity issues to address.
