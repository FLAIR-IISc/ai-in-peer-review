

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm called Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO) that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling, addressing limitations in prior work. The authors build on the theoretical framework of de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2], who identified that existing methods with exponential convergence either require impractical δ-cover sampling or suffer from auxiliary optimization costs. The key insight is to leverage a "semi-metric" assumption about the objective function's behavior near the global optimum, allowing the algorithm to simultaneously consider infinitely many possible upper bounds rather than relying on a single GP-estimated bound.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proving an exponential regret bound of O(λ^N+Ngp) with λ < 1, where N is the number of function evaluations and Ngp is the number of GP evaluations. This improves upon the polynomial regret bound of BaMSOO [2] and provides a more comprehensive theoretical framework for understanding when relying on a single bound versus considering multiple bounds is advantageous. The analysis carefully examines how the tightness of the GP-estimated bound affects performance, showing that even a "useless" bound can still be beneficial when combined with the infinite-metric approach.

In terms of quality, the paper presents technically sound theoretical results with a well-constructed proof (provided in supplementary material). The experimental evaluation on eight benchmark functions demonstrates consistent performance improvements over SOO, BaMSOO, GP-PI, and GP-EI, particularly for non-trivial multimodal functions. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach, noting that λ approaches 1 as dimensionality increases, suggesting scalability challenges for very high dimensions.

The paper excels in clarity, with an exceptionally well-structured exposition that carefully guides readers through the theoretical framework. The algorithm description is supported by effective visualizations (Figure 1) that clearly illustrate the hierarchical partitioning process. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is well-documented and logically organized. The paper is well-written and didactic, making complex concepts accessible to readers unfamiliar with the specific nuances of Bayesian optimization.

The originality is strong, as the paper addresses a clear gap in the literature identified by Wang et al. [2] regarding the impracticality of δ-cover sampling. The core idea of considering infinitely many possible bounds is novel, though it does draw inspiration from DIRECT [19] and SOO [18]. The authors effectively distinguish their work from prior art, particularly clarifying how IMGPO improves upon BaMSOO's polynomial regret bound.

The significance of this work is substantial. By removing the need for δ-cover sampling and auxiliary optimization, IMGPO makes exponential convergence practically achievable for a wider range of applications. The theoretical framework provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between using a single bound versus multiple bounds, which could influence future work in Bayesian optimization and related fields. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the approach across multiple benchmark functions.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution with exponential regret bound
- Practical algorithm that removes two major limitations in prior work
- Well-organized and well-written with excellent visual explanations
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on multiple benchmark functions
- Thoughtful analysis of the theoretical implications

Weaknesses:
- Minor technical errors (redundant abstract formulation at line 49, misplaced parenthesis in function definition at line 72)
- The paper assumes familiarity with Gaussian process theory, which may create a barrier for some readers
- Limited discussion of how the algorithm would perform on truly high-dimensional problems beyond the 1000-dimensional Sin2 test case
- The theoretical analysis could benefit from more intuitive explanations of why the infinite-metric approach provides advantages

The paper is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical contribution is significant, the experimental validation is strong, and the practical implications are clear. While the reviewer acknowledges some unfamiliarity with the specific nuances of Gaussian processes and global optimization, the paper's clear exposition and compelling results make the contribution evident. The minor technical errors are easily fixable and do not undermine the core contribution.

The authors have successfully addressed a meaningful open problem in Bayesian optimization, providing both theoretical advances and practical improvements. The work advances the state of the art by demonstrating that exponential convergence can be achieved without the impractical assumptions required by prior work, while also providing a valuable theoretical framework for understanding the trade-offs between different bound-based approaches.

The paper would benefit from a slightly more detailed discussion of the limitations in high-dimensional settings, but this does not detract from the overall strength of the contribution. The proposed algorithm is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, making it a valuable addition to the literature on Bayesian optimization.
