This paper proposes a novel Bayesian Global Optimization (BGO) variant that generates future samples via space/interval partitioning. The approach leverages the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) to avoid the internal global optimization problem of selecting the next sample. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, demonstrating exponential-regret improvements over previous work.

The paper is well-written, and the algorithm is clearly described and illustrated with an example. The authors make a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and the paper is overall clearly presented.

The main idea of the paper is to use a bound-based method but rely less on the estimated bound by considering all the possible bounds. This approach is different from traditional BGO methods, such as GP-UCB, which rely on a single bound given by the Gaussian process (GP) prior. The authors show that their approach can achieve an exponential convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over previous work.

The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the regret bound, which is the main theoretical contribution of the paper. The regret bound is analyzed in terms of the infinite-metric exploration loss, which is the cost incurred by not committing to any particular upper bound. The authors show that their approach can achieve a better regret bound than traditional BGO methods, even when the bound due to the GP prior is not tight.

The experimental results are also promising, showing that the proposed algorithm, Infinite-Metric GP Optimization (IMGPO), outperforms other state-of-the-art algorithms in most cases. However, it is worth noting that the experimental results are limited to 1D test functions, and it is unclear how the algorithm will perform in higher dimensions.

One major reservation is that the interval-partitioning approach may not scale well to higher dimensions, as the required resolution grows exponentially. This is a significant concern, as many real-world problems involve high-dimensional search spaces. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest that additional assumptions, such as those in [14, 20], may be needed to address this issue.

Another concern is that the method is related to DIRECT, which is known to perform poorly in high dimensions. This issue should be discussed in more detail, as it may be a significant limitation of the proposed approach.

Some minor issues include unclear sentences, such as the one about "simultaneously conduct global and local searches based on all the candidates of the bounds." Additionally, it is unclear why at n=16 the far-right region has no function evaluation. These issues do not significantly impact the overall quality of the paper but should be clarified in future revisions.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is clearly written, and the algorithm is well-described and illustrated. The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and the results are significant and relevant to the community.

However, the paper may not be suitable for NIPS, as it is primarily focused on theoretical contributions, and the experimental results are limited. The paper may be a better fit for a conference that focuses on theoretical aspects of machine learning or optimization.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
* The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-done.
* The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed algorithm outperforms other state-of-the-art algorithms in most cases.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experimental results are limited to 1D test functions, and it is unclear how the algorithm will perform in higher dimensions.
* The interval-partitioning approach may not scale well to higher dimensions.
* The method is related to DIRECT, which is known to perform poorly in high dimensions.

Overall, the paper is sound and novel, but the evaluation appears limited to 1D problems. The authors should consider addressing the concerns mentioned above and providing more experimental results in higher dimensions to demonstrate the scalability of their approach.
