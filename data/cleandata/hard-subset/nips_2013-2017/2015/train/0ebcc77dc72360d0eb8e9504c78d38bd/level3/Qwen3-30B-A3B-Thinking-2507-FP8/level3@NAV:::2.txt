

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization method, IMGPO, that achieves exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling. The authors address two key limitations of prior work: the need for an additional global optimization problem in GP-UCB (which is computationally expensive) and the impracticality of δ-cover sampling. Their approach leverages interval partitioning and considers infinitely many possible upper bounds through a novel "infinite-metric exploration" framework, which they theoretically analyze to demonstrate exponential regret improvements over BaMSOO (which achieves only polynomial regret).

The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical contribution. The authors provide a rigorous regret analysis showing exponential convergence under mild assumptions, and they effectively relate their method to prior work on DIRECT and SOO. The algorithm is well-described with a helpful illustrative example (Figure 1), and the pseudocode is detailed. The writing is clear and the presentation is generally strong.

However, the experimental evaluation presents a significant limitation. The authors only evaluate their method on 1D test functions, despite the theoretical analysis suggesting potential scalability challenges in higher dimensions. The paper acknowledges that the interval-partitioning approach may face exponential resolution requirements in higher dimensions, which directly conflicts with the known poor performance of DIRECT-like methods in high-dimensional spaces. The authors should explicitly discuss how their method compares to alternatives like REMBO for high-dimensional problems, rather than simply mentioning it as a possibility.

Several minor concerns exist: the necessity of UCB is unclear (the authors note they use it "for brevity" but don't justify why other acquisition functions wouldn't work), a sentence about "simultaneously conducting global and local searches" is confusingly phrased, and the reason for the lack of evaluation at n=16 is not explained.

While the theoretical contribution is strong and novel, the limited experimental evaluation to 1D problems significantly impacts the paper's significance for practical applications. The scalability concerns, though acknowledged in the theoretical analysis, remain unaddressed in the empirical results. This limitation prevents the authors from demonstrating the practical advantages of their method in real-world scenarios, which is essential for an NIPS paper. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution but would benefit from more comprehensive experimental validation across dimensions.
