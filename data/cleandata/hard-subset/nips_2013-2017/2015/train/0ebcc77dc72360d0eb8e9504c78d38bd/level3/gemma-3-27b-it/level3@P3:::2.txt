This paper presents a novel Bayesian optimization method, IMGPO, achieving exponential convergence without requiring auxiliary optimization or δ-cover sampling – limitations of prior work like de Freitas et al. [1] and Wang et al. [2]. The core idea is to leverage hierarchical partitioning of the search space and maintain multiple potential bounds, effectively conducting a global and local search simultaneously. The theoretical analysis demonstrates improved exponential regret bounds, and the algorithm is clearly described with a helpful illustrative example. Overall, the paper presents a sound and novel contribution, well-written and clearly presented.

However, a significant limitation is the exclusive evaluation on 1D test functions. While not explicitly stated, the example and discussion strongly suggest this restriction. This is a major concern, as the method’s scalability to higher dimensions is questionable. The interval-partitioning approach, reminiscent of DIRECT [19], inherently suffers from the curse of dimensionality – the required resolution grows exponentially with dimension. The authors acknowledge this in the analysis (regarding λ approaching one as D increases) and relate their method to DIRECT, but a more thorough discussion of this limitation, particularly given DIRECT’s known poor performance in high dimensions, is necessary. The paper doesn’t adequately address *how* IMGPO might overcome this issue, even with the proposed modifications or potential combination with techniques like those in [14]. 

The theoretical contribution remains valuable irrespective of scalability concerns. However, the practical utility of the method is significantly diminished if it’s limited to low-dimensional problems.

Several minor points could improve clarity. It's unclear whether the UCB acquisition function is strictly *required*, or if other criteria like Expected Improvement could be employed. The phrasing "for brevity" suggests the latter possibility, but doesn’t explicitly state it. The sentence describing simultaneously conducting global and local searches "based on all the candidates of the bounds" could benefit from further elaboration. What constitutes a “candidate bound” and how are these managed? Regarding Figure 1, clarification is needed for why no function evaluation occurs in the far-right region at n=16; is this due to the UCB criterion?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** IMGPO presents a genuinely new approach to Bayesian optimization with a demonstrably improved theoretical guarantee.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and supports the claimed exponential convergence.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and explains the algorithm clearly, aided by a helpful example.
*   **Significance:** Addressing the limitations of existing Bayesian optimization methods (auxiliary optimization, δ-cover sampling) is a significant contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The exclusive focus on 1D test functions severely limits the assessment of the method’s practical applicability.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The inherent scalability issues associated with interval partitioning raise doubts about the method's utility in real-world, high-dimensional problems.
*   **Missing Discussion:** Insufficient discussion regarding the comparison to DIRECT and potential mitigation strategies for the curse of dimensionality.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of existing methods and provides a novel algorithm with promising theoretical properties. However, the lack of evaluation in higher dimensions is a serious weakness. While the authors acknowledge scalability concerns, a more in-depth discussion and exploration of potential solutions are needed. 

I recommend acceptance with the strong caveat that the authors significantly expand the experimental evaluation to include higher-dimensional problems (even if with modifications or combined with dimensionality reduction techniques) or provide a compelling theoretical justification for why the method *can* scale despite the inherent limitations of the partitioning approach. Addressing the minor clarity points would also improve the paper’s overall quality.
