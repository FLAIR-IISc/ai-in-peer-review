

This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D views of objects from single images, with applications to human faces and chairs. The authors propose a framework that disentangles identity and pose factors through a recurrent architecture, enabling long-term prediction of rotation trajectories without explicit 3D model recovery. The approach builds upon related work in transforming autoencoders [12], predictive gating networks [19], and inverse graphics networks [17], but makes key contributions: (1) a recurrent structure with action units for modeling sequential rotations on the pose manifold, (2) weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors without class labels, and (3) curriculum learning to progressively increase rotation trajectory length for improved disentanglement.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through comprehensive experiments on Multi-PIE and chair datasets. The technical approach is sound, with thorough evaluation of both image synthesis quality (measured by MSE) and the discriminative power of the learned representations in cross-view recognition tasks. The authors effectively show that longer training sequences improve both image quality and feature disentanglement, with RNN16 achieving 42% better MSE than the best KNN baseline.

Clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition, clear network architecture diagrams, and detailed experimental setup. The motivation for each component is well-justified, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific network configurations and training protocols for both datasets.

Originality is strong, as the weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose emerges naturally from the rotation prediction objective rather than through explicit regularization. This differs from approaches like bilinear models [24] or disentangling Boltzmann machines [21], which require explicit factor separation mechanisms. The authors effectively demonstrate how their method differs from prior work through careful comparison in the related work section.

Significance is high, as the problem of synthesizing novel views from single images is fundamental to both graphics and vision. The ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories and learn view-invariant representations without class labels has clear implications for applications in photo editing, view-invariant recognition, and generative modeling. The disentangled representations achieve competitive results against supervised CNN baselines in cross-view recognition tasks, demonstrating practical utility.

Strengths: 
- Novel recurrent architecture for pose manifold traversal
- Effective weakly-supervised disentangling without class labels
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative and qualitative results
- Clear explanation of curriculum learning benefits
- Demonstrated generalization to novel object instances

Weaknesses:
- The method currently supports only discrete rotation angles based on training data, limiting its applicability to continuous rotation scenarios. The authors should address how to extend the approach to handle continuous rotations.
- The recurrent network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which contradicts the typical flexibility of RNNs for variable-length sequences. The authors should clarify why fixed-length training was chosen and whether this is a limitation for generalization.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to 3D view synthesis and representation learning. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are significant for the field. The minor weaknesses noted are addressable and do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The work represents a strong scientific contribution that advances the state of the art in weakly-supervised disentangling and 3D view synthesis.
