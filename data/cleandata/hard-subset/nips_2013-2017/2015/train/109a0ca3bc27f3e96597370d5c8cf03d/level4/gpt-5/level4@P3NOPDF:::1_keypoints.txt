- Clear problem statement and an intuitive recurrent formulation that models incremental rotations.  
- Architecture and training are described in sufficient detail and the paper is generally well written.  
- Curriculum learning experiment is appropriate and an interesting design choice for sequential rotation.  
- Cross-view recognition and class interpolation are thoughtful probes of representation quality and latent smoothness.  

- Evaluation is primarily qualitative and lacks robust quantitative metrics to assess progress or limitations.  
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines, particularly geometry-based methods cited in the paper.  
- Dataset scope is narrow (aligned faces and chairs), limiting claims of generality.  
- Rotation model seems tied to a fixed pivot/axis; unclear generalization to multi-axis motions, occlusions, or large out-of-plane rotations.  
- Evidence for disentanglement relies on a single cross-view recognition experiment and is insufficient.  
- Figures are small and hard to assess, weakening the qualitative case.  

- Add quantitative reconstruction metrics (per-pixel L2/PSNR, SSIM, IoU, keypoint/landmark error, identity-preservation accuracy).  
- Compare against diverse baselines: geometry-based methods, feed-forward encoder–decoder, nearest-neighbor retrieval, appearance-flow/warping, and RNNs without curriculum.  
- Provide ablations/generalization tests: curriculum vs. no curriculum, different schedules, train-short/test-long extrapolation, and open-loop error accumulation.  
- Test sensitivity to alignment, background, and lighting; report performance when alignment is relaxed.  
- Extend datasets to additional object categories (vehicles, household objects) to demonstrate breadth.  
- Evaluate more complex camera motions and non-static axes (combined yaw/pitch/roll, non-periodic trajectories).  
- Deepen disentanglement analysis: linear probes for identity vs. pose, compare to discriminative CNNs, visualize latent traversals, and report identity-consistency metrics.  
- Improve figure readability with higher-resolution, side-by-side ground truth and baseline comparisons annotated with error measures.  
- Discuss computational aspects: training time, memory for unrolled sequences, and inference cost per rotation step.  

- Paper is mostly clear; fix minor grammatical issues (e.g., “rotate” → “rotated”).  
- Clarify terminology like “pivoting viewpoints” and specify whether rotation axis is fixed or learned.  
- Back up or scope claims about “state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches” with experiments or careful language.  

- Overall: timely and promising work with a compelling recurrent formulation, but current evaluation is too limited and qualitative to support strong conclusions; promising but not yet ready for acceptance.
