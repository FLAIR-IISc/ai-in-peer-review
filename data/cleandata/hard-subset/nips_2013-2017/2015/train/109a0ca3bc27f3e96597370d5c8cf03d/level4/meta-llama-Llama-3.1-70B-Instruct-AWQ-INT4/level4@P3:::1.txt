This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single 2D image. The authors evaluate their model's performance on the Multi-PIE and Chairs datasets, demonstrating its ability to generate realistic images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the model's architecture and training procedure.

The main contributions of this work are the development of a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis and the demonstration of its effectiveness on two challenging datasets. The authors also explore the benefits of curriculum learning, showing that increasing the trajectory length of training sequences improves both image prediction performance and pose-invariant recognition.

However, the evaluation of the model's performance is somewhat limited. While the authors provide qualitative results in the form of visualizations, they could benefit from introducing quantitative measures of performance, such as error metrics, to compare their results to state-of-the-art methods. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from additional experiments on more diverse datasets to demonstrate the model's generalizability. Currently, the model is trained and evaluated on only two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible object classes. Demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for different types of objects would make the network architecture seem less ad hoc.

Additionally, it would be interesting to see how the proposed model could generalize to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis (non-repeating rotations). This would require the model to learn more complex and dynamic transformations, which could further demonstrate its potential for real-world applications.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough discussion of existing methods for 3D view synthesis and object recognition. However, they could benefit from a more detailed comparison of their approach to other state-of-the-art methods, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of their approach.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and interesting approach to 3D view synthesis. While it has some limitations in terms of evaluation and experimentation, it has the potential to contribute to the development of more sophisticated and generalizable models for this task.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to 3D view synthesis.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on two challenging datasets.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The evaluation of the model's performance is somewhat limited.
* The paper could benefit from additional experiments on more diverse datasets.
* The model's ability to generalize to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis is not demonstrated.

Recommendations for Improvement:

* Introduce quantitative measures of performance, such as error metrics, to compare results to state-of-the-art methods.
* Conduct additional experiments on more diverse datasets to demonstrate the model's generalizability.
* Explore the model's ability to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis.
* Provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed approach to other state-of-the-art methods.
