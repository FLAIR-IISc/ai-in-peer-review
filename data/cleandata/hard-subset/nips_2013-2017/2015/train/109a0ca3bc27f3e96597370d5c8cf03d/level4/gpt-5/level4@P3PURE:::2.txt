This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects such as human faces and 3D chair models. The base model uses an encoder to disentangle an input image into identity units and pose units. Action units are applied to the pose units to control the rotation direction. A decoder composed of convolution and upsampling layers then decodes the identity and pose into a rotated image and the corresponding object mask. To support longer rotation trajectories, the model is extended with a recurrent architecture: the encoded identity unit of the input image is held fixed, while the pose unit is updated by a sequence of action units; at each step, identity and pose units are fed into the decoder to generate the result image.

A key contribution is learning to disentangle identity/appearance and pose factors. The identity units are shown to be discriminative, view-invariant features in a cross-view object recognition task. Moreover, these disentangled representations benefit longer rotation trajectories and yield better renderings when the recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network is trained via curriculum training stages.

The paper is well written, easy to follow, and the motivation for each component of the method is clearly described. The qualitative renderings of rotated images and the quantitative cross-view recognition results provide solid support for the approach, especially for the disentangling of pose and identity.

Minor weaknesses and questions:
- The network appears to support only discrete rotation angles, determined by the set of angles present in the training data. Do the authors have initial ideas for extending the method to support continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which seems contradictory to the general flexibility expected of recurrent neural networks.

Overall, this work introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network trained end-to-end to render rotated objects from a single image. The main contribution—generative disentangling of identity and pose factors emerging from the recurrent rotation-prediction objective—is well demonstrated by both the qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
