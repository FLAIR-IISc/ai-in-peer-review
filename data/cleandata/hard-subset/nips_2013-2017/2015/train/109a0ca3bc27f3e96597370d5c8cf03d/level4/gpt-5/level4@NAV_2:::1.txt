This work introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture that, given a single 2D image, synthesizes novel views of an object under 3D rotations. The authors evaluate the method by generating rotated renderings for faces and chairs. They also investigate curriculum learning, assess disentanglement via cross-view object recognition, and explore class interpolation on chairs.

Although the model is clearly described and technically detailed, the empirical evaluation is relatively weak. The evidence is predominantly qualitative (and several visualizations would benefit from being larger for readability). Adding quantitative metrics and comparisons against state-of-the-art baselines with appropriate error measures would be more informative than qualitative examples alone.

The manuscript is generally well written and easy to follow. There are a few minor grammatical issues (e.g., line 124: “rotate” → “rotated”).

The paper’s significance is constrained by limited experimentation and analysis. Including additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs would strengthen the case that the approach is not overly tailored to those domains. It would also be valuable to examine generalization to rotation trajectories that do not adhere to a fixed axis (i.e., non-repeating or varying-axis rotations). In sum, the approach is novel and interesting, but the current experiments and analysis are insufficient to demonstrate its impact or competitiveness with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51).
