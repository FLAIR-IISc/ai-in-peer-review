In this paper, the authors present a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects such as human faces and 3D chair models. The approach begins with a base model whose encoder disentangles the input image into identity units and pose units; action units are applied to the pose units to control rotation direction, and a decoder composed of convolution and upsampling layers maps identity and pose to a rotated image and the corresponding object mask. To enable longer rotation trajectories, the model is extended with a recurrent architecture in which the encoded identity units of the input image are kept fixed while the pose units are updated by a sequence of action units; finally, both identity and pose units are fed to the decoder to generate the output image.

One of the main contributions of the paper is learning to disentangle the representations of identity/appearance and pose factors; the identity units are shown to be discriminative, view-invariant features for cross-view object recognition. Moreover, this disentangling improves rendering quality when using longer rotation trajectories within a curriculum training scheme for the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network.

The paper is well written, easy to follow, and the motivation for each component of the method is clearly articulated. The qualitative renderings of rotated images and the quantitative results on cross-view object recognition provide strong support for the approach, especially the disentanglement of pose and identity representations.

Some minor weaknesses are as follows, which the authors might address during the rebuttal:
- The network currently supports only discrete rotation angles, determined by the set of angles present in the training data. Do the authors have initial ideas for extending the method to handle continuous rotation angles?
- The proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder is trained with a fixed sequence length, which appears at odds with the general practice for recurrent neural networks.

Overall, this paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder trained end-to-end to render rotated objects from a single image. The primary contribution—generative disentanglement of identity and pose induced by the recurrent rotation prediction objective—is well demonstrated through both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
