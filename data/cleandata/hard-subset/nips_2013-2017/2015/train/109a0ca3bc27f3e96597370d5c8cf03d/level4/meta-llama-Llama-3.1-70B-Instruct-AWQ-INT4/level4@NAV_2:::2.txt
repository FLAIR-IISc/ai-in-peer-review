This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects such as human faces and 3D chair models. The proposed network starts from a basic model, where its encoder network disentangles the input image into identity units and pose units, then with the action units applied on pose units to control the rotation direction, its decoder network which consists of convolution and upsampling decodes the identity and pose into an image of rotated object and the corresponding object mask. To support longer rotation trajectories, the proposed network is then extended to have the recurrent architecture where the encoded identity unit of input image is fixed and the pose unit is changed by a sequence of action units, and finally both identity and pose units are fed into decoder to generate the result image.

One of the main contributions of this paper is learning to disentangle the representations for identity/appearance and pose factors, where the identity units are shown to be discriminative view-invariant features in the cross-view object recognition task. In addition, this disentangling property will benefit more and predict better rendering while using the longer rotation trajectories in the curriculum training stages for training the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network.

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the motivation for different parts of the proposed method is clearly described. Also, the qualitative results for predicted rendering of rotated images and quantitative evaluation on cross-view object recognition task provide good support for the method, especially the disentangled representations for pose and identity factors.

The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the previous work in the field, including the transforming autoencoder, Predictive Gating Pyramid, and Inverse Graphics Network, and how the proposed method differs from these approaches. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their method on two datasets, Multi-PIE and Chairs, and demonstrate its effectiveness in synthesizing 3D views of unseen object instances.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is a complete piece of work, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. The authors provide enough background information and context for the reader to understand the problem and the proposed solution. The paper is also well-structured, and the different sections are logically connected.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects. The proposed network is a new combination of familiar techniques, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in synthesizing 3D views of unseen object instances. The paper also provides a new perspective on the problem of 3D view synthesis, and the proposed method is significantly different from previous approaches.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in synthesizing 3D views of unseen object instances.
* The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on two datasets, Multi-PIE and Chairs.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The proposed network can only support discrete rotation angles, depending on the set of rotation angles shown in the training data.
* The proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length, which is actually contradictory to general recurrent neural networks.
* The paper does not provide a clear idea of how to extend the proposed method to support continuous rotation angles.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is novel and effective. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, and the paper is technically sound. However, the paper has some minor weaknesses, and the authors should address these in the rebuttal period.
