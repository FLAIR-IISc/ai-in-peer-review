Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder architecture that synthesizes novel views of an object given a single input image. The core idea is to factor latent variables into identity units (invariant to viewpoint) and pose units (updated by action inputs), and to traverse the pose manifold via a recurrent update conditioned on discrete “rotation” actions. The model is trained end-to-end on sequences of small rotations using curriculum learning to improve long-term prediction. Experiments on aligned face images (Multi-PIE) and rendered chairs demonstrate: (1) multi-step view synthesis; (2) emergent, weakly supervised disentangling of identity and pose, enabling cross-view recognition without labels; and (3) identity interpolation that yields plausible novel objects with consistent rotation trajectories.

The work is positioned among several relevant lines: transforming autoencoders and gating models for transformation learning [12,19], generative CNNs for image synthesis and graphics codes [8,17], and disentangling approaches (e.g., bilinear models and disBM) [24,21,29]. Compared to Dosovitskiy et al. [8], the paper adds an encoder to generalize to novel images, explicitly partitions latent codes into identity/pose, and uses a recurrent, action-conditioned update for long trajectories. Relative to IGN [17], it forgoes variational training in favor of a deterministic recurrent decoder with per-step control. The formulation is also inspired by mental rotation [22], implemented here as an action-driven pose manifold traversal.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent architecture that marries encoder–decoder CNNs with action-conditioned recurrence for long sequences.
- Training strategy (curriculum learning) is well motivated and empirically shown to improve both synthesis quality and identity invariance.
- Weakly supervised disentangling: identity units support cross-view recognition without using class labels, and longer curricula enhance this effect.
- Compelling qualitative results on faces and chairs, including identity interpolation with consistent rotation.
- The design choice to keep identity units constant across time and update only pose units is simple and effective.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Quantitative evaluation of view synthesis is limited. For chairs, the comparison is primarily to KNN averaging and MSE; for faces, the 3D morphable model baseline is only qualitative. Perceptual metrics (e.g., SSIM) and/or human preference studies would better capture fidelity than MSE alone.
- Baselines are weak or not fully aligned with the task. It would be useful to include stronger or more diverse baselines (e.g., IGN-style decoders, non-recurrent encoder–decoders with single-step application, or gating models [19]) and ablations (no curriculum, no action inputs, feeding previous outputs vs latent-only recurrence).
- The disentangling claim is mostly supported by recognition results; more systematic analysis (e.g., linear separability of identity/pose factors, pose estimation accuracy from pose units) would strengthen the evidence.
- The settings are controlled: aligned faces under neutral illumination and rendered chairs with uniform backgrounds. It is unclear how robust the model is to real-world variability (clutter, misalignment, illumination changes, occlusions).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow; figures are informative but some are small—please scale result visualizations so details are legible.
- Minor typos (e.g., “rotate” → “rotated” in the noted line).

Originality
- The combination of recurrent action-conditioned latent updates with explicit identity/pose separation and curriculum training is a novel and timely contribution in the NIPS 2015 context. However, the components relate closely to contemporary work (e.g., IGN [17], generative CNNs [8], gating/transforming models [12,19]), so careful empirical differentiation is important.

Significance
- The approach is promising for single-view novel view synthesis and for learning view-invariant representations. The demonstrated benefits of curriculum learning and identity/pose factorization are useful insights. However, significance is tempered by limited datasets and baselines; broader validation would better establish generality and impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen quantitative evaluation: add SSIM/PSNR on synthesis; report identity preservation under rotation (e.g., recognition accuracy between generated views and ground-truth target identities); and include a small-scale human study for perceptual quality.
- Expand baselines and ablations: compare to (i) a non-recurrent encoder–decoder repeatedly applied per step, (ii) variants without action inputs, (iii) models trained without curriculum, and (iv) gating-based or IGN-like decoders.
- Provide quantitative comparisons to 3D morphable models on Multi-PIE (e.g., landmark error, photometric error after pose normalization).
- Test additional categories and less constrained data (e.g., other rigid objects with diverse topology, faces with lighting/expression variation) to demonstrate generality beyond Multi-PIE and synthetic chairs.
- Analyze disentanglement more rigorously: mutual information between identity/pose units and labels; pose regression from pose units; failure cases where leakage occurs.
- Consider continuous action parameterization (degrees) instead of 1-of-3 coding, and report generalization to unseen step sizes and non-circular trajectories.
- Discuss multi-modality (self-occlusion/texture ambiguity) and whether probabilistic decoders (e.g., VAE/GAN) could handle one-to-many mappings.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether gradients flow only through latent recurrence (not via re-rendered images), and justify this choice versus using predicted images as inputs (exposure bias).
- Report parameter counts, training time, and memory constraints (noting the t=16 limit).
- Ensure all references are complete and consistently formatted.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, well-motivated architecture with clear contributions (action-conditioned recurrence + curriculum) and solid qualitative results.
- Demonstrates useful emergent disentangling and cross-view recognition without labels.
- Relevant and timely topic for the community; connects vision and graphics in a data-driven way.

Arguments against acceptance
- Quantitative evaluation and baselines are insufficient to firmly establish state-of-the-art performance or robustness.
- Validation is restricted to two controlled datasets; generality to more realistic settings remains unclear.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and disentanglement beyond recognition accuracy.

Overall, this is an interesting and thoughtfully executed paper with promising ideas. With stronger quantitative comparisons, broader datasets, and deeper analysis/ablations, it would make a compelling contribution.
