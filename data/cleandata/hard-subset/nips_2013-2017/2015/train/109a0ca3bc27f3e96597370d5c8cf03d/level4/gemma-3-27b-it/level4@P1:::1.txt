In this paper, the authors design a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that can render an object from different viewpoints after pivoting, given a single 2D image of the object. The authors evaluate the performance of their proposed model by demonstrating its ability to generate images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints. They also perform additional experiments to examine the benefits of curriculum learning, to evaluate the model's performance in learning disentangled representations through cross-view object recognition, and to explore its ability to perform class interpolation with chairs.

Although the proposed model is well presented and detailed, the evaluation of its performance is somewhat limited. The experiments used to demonstrate the effectiveness of the RNN model are largely qualitative (visualizations of results should be scaled to an appropriate size for the reader to easily discern details). Introducing quantitative measures of performance with appropriate error metrics—to allow comparison with state-of-the-art results—would be more insightful than qualitative assessments alone. 

In general, the paper is well written and easy to follow. There are a few minor grammatical corrections that could be made (e.g., line 124, change "rotate" to "rotated").

The significance of this paper is somewhat hindered by its limited experimentation and analysis. It would be beneficial to include experiments with more datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs. Demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for a broader range of objects would make the network architecture seem less specialized. Additionally, it would be interesting to investigate how the proposed model could generalize to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a fixed axis (i.e., non-repeating rotations). The paper develops a deep learning model for rendering an object from different rotational viewpoints given a single 2D image. Although the approach is novel and interesting, the paper lacks sufficient experimentation and analysis to fully establish the model's significance and potential for achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network offers a novel approach to 3D view synthesis from a single image, avoiding the limitations of traditional geometry-based methods.
*   The model demonstrates the ability to learn disentangled representations without explicit supervision, which is a valuable capability.
*   The curriculum learning strategy effectively improves performance, particularly for longer rotation sequences.
*   The model generates realistic and consistent rotations of both faces and chairs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The evaluation is largely qualitative, and lacks comprehensive quantitative comparisons against relevant baselines.
*   The experiments are limited to two datasets, and it is unclear how well the model would generalize to other object categories.
*   The model currently focuses on rotation around a single axis; extending it to more complex trajectories would enhance its practicality.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.
