This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that is trained end-to-end on the task of rendering rotated objects starting from a single image. The main contribution of the paper is the generative disentangling of the identity and pose factors, which emerged from the recurrent rotation prediction objective.

The authors tackle the challenging problem of 3D view synthesis from a single image, which is inherently ill-posed due to the loss of information in projecting a 3D object onto the image space. They propose a learning approach without explicit 3D model recovery, which relies on mappings between Euclidean image space and underlying nonlinear manifold. The authors draw inspiration from mental rotation experiments and propose a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network with action units to model the process of pose manifold traversal.

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the motivation for different parts of the proposed method is clearly described. The qualitative results for predicted rendering of rotated images and quantitative evaluation on cross-view object recognition tasks provide good support for the method, especially the disentangled representations for pose and identity factors.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the Multi-PIE dataset and a database of 3D chair CAD models, showing accurate renderings across trajectories of repeated rotations. The proposed curriculum training by gradually increasing the trajectory length of training sequences yields both better image appearance and more discriminative features for pose-invariant recognition.

One of the main contributions of this paper is learning to disentangle the representations for identity/appearance and pose factors, where the identity units are shown to be discriminative view-invariant features in the cross-view object recognition task. This disentangling property benefits more and predicts better rendering while using longer rotation trajectories in the curriculum training stages for training the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network.

However, there are a few minor weaknesses that the authors may want to address in the rebuttal period. Firstly, the proposed network can only support discrete rotation angles, depending on the set of rotation angles shown in the training data. It would be interesting to explore ways to extend the proposed method to support continuous rotation angles. Secondly, the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which is actually contradictory to general recurrent neural networks. It would be beneficial to investigate ways to relax this constraint and enable the network to handle variable-length sequences.

Overall, this paper proposes a novel and effective approach to 3D view synthesis and demonstrates the potential of generative disentangling for pose and identity factors. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, and the results show promise for various applications in computer vision and graphics.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to 3D view synthesis using a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network
* Effective disentangling of pose and identity factors
* Strong qualitative and quantitative results on Multi-PIE and 3D chair CAD models
* Well-written and easy to follow paper

Weaknesses:

* Limited to discrete rotation angles
* Trained with fixed-length sequences

Arguments for acceptance:

* Novel and effective approach to 3D view synthesis
* Strong evaluation on multiple datasets
* Potential applications in computer vision and graphics

Arguments against acceptance:

* Limited to discrete rotation angles
* Trained with fixed-length sequences
* May not generalize to more complex scenarios

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
