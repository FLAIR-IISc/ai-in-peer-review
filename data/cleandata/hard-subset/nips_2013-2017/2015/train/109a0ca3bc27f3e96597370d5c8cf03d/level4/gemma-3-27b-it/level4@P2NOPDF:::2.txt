This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, demonstrated on datasets of human faces and chair models. The network begins with a basic encoder that disentangles the input image into identity (appearance) and pose representations. Rotation is controlled by applying action units to the pose representation. A convolutional decoder then reconstructs the image and an object mask from the combined identity and rotated pose representations. To facilitate longer, more complex rotation trajectories, the authors extend this architecture with recurrence, holding the encoded identity representation constant while sequentially updating the pose representation via a series of action units. The final, rotated image is generated by decoding the fixed identity and modified pose.

A key contribution of this work is the learning of disentangled representations for identity/appearance and pose. The authors demonstrate that the learned identity units function as discriminative, view-invariant features in a cross-view object recognition task. This disentanglement also proves beneficial for improved rendering quality, particularly when employing longer rotation trajectories during curriculum training. 

The paper is clearly written and easy to understand, with a logical presentation of motivation for each component of the proposed method. Strong qualitative results, showcasing realistic renderings of rotated images, and quantitative evaluation on cross-view object recognition provide compelling support for the effectiveness of the approach, particularly the benefit of disentangled pose and identity representations.

I do have a few minor concerns that I hope the authors can address during the rebuttal period:

*   The current network is limited to discrete rotation angles determined by the training data. Have the authors considered how to extend the method to support continuous rotation angles, and do they have any preliminary ideas in this direction?
*   The recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which seems inconsistent with the typical behavior of recurrent neural networks designed for variable-length input. Further clarification on the rationale for this design choice would be helpful. 

Overall, this paper presents a novel and well-supported approach to rendering rotated objects from a single image via end-to-end training. The generative disentanglement of identity and pose factors, driven by the recurrent rotation prediction objective, is a significant contribution and is effectively demonstrated through both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
