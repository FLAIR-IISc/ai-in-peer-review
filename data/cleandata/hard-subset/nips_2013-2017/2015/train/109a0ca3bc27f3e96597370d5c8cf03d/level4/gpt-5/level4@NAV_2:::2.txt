The paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture for synthesizing out-of-plane rotations of 3D objects (faces and chairs) from a single image. The base model encodes an input into two latent partitions—identity units and pose units—then applies action units to the pose units to control rotation direction before decoding (via convolution and upsampling) back to an image, with an auxiliary mask prediction for objects like chairs. To handle long rotation trajectories, the model is extended with a recurrent structure: the identity units extracted from the input are held fixed while the pose units are updated step-by-step by a sequence of action inputs; the decoder then combines the fixed identity and updated pose codes to render the target views.

A key contribution is the learning of disentangled representations for identity/appearance and pose. The identity units act as view-invariant, discriminative features in cross-view recognition, while the recurrent training objective improves disentanglement and yields better renderings as the curriculum progresses to longer sequences.

The paper is clearly written, well organized, and the motivations behind each component of the approach are explicit. Qualitative results for multistep view synthesis and quantitative cross-view recognition provide solid evidence, particularly for the effectiveness of the identity/pose disentanglement.

Minor weaknesses and questions:
- The approach appears tied to discrete rotation steps defined by the training data. Do the authors have a plan for supporting continuous rotation angles (e.g., parameterizing actions continuously)?
- The recurrent encoder-decoder is trained with fixed-length sequences at each curriculum stage. Can the authors clarify how well the learned model generalizes to variable-length sequences at test time and whether training with variable lengths would offer benefits?

Overall, this work presents a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder trained end-to-end for view synthesis from a single image. The main contribution—emergent generative disentanglement of identity and pose from the recurrent rotation objective—is convincingly demonstrated by both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
