

This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for generating out-of-plane rotations of 3D objects, including human faces and chairs. The core architecture begins with a non-recurrent model where the encoder disentangles the input image into identity and pose representations. The pose representation is then modified by action units that control rotation direction, and the decoder (using convolution and upsampling) reconstructs the rotated object image and mask from the identity and modified pose. To handle longer rotation sequences, the authors extend this to a recurrent structure: the identity representation remains fixed across the sequence, while the pose representation is iteratively updated via a chain of action units before final decoding.

The primary contribution lies in learning disentangled representations for identity/appearance and pose. The authors demonstrate that the identity representations serve as discriminative, view-invariant features for cross-view object recognition. This disentanglement also enables more accurate rendering when leveraging longer rotation trajectories during curriculum learning for training the recurrent network.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, with strong qualitative results for rotated image synthesis and robust quantitative validation on cross-view recognition. The evaluations effectively support the claim that disentangling pose and identity enhances performance.

Two minor weaknesses warrant attention:
1. The model currently supports only discrete rotation angles defined by the training set. The authors should discuss potential approaches to extend the method for continuous rotation angles.
2. The recurrent network is trained on fixed-length sequences, which contrasts with the typical flexibility of recurrent architectures for variable-length inputs. Clarifying the rationale for this design choice would strengthen the method.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling approach to generative disentanglement through the recurrent rotation prediction objective, with the disentanglement properties clearly validated through both qualitative and quantitative results. The core contribution of disentangling identity and pose for improved rendering and recognition is well-established.
