This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network capable of rendering 3D objects, such as faces and chairs, from novel viewpoints after applying out-of-plane rotations. The model is trained end-to-end, learning from a single input image. The core of the proposed method is an encoder-decoder architecture. The encoder disentangles the input image into separate 'identity' and 'pose' representations. An 'action' unit, which controls the rotation, is then applied to the pose representation. The decoder, consisting of convolutional and upsampling layers, uses the identity and modified pose representations to generate a rendered image of the rotated object, along with a corresponding object mask.

To generate longer and more complex rotation trajectories, this base model is extended into a recurrent architecture. In this setup, the identity representation from the initial input image is held constant, while the pose representation is iteratively updated by a sequence of action units. At each step, the decoder uses the fixed identity and the current pose to generate the next image in the sequence.

One of the paper's main contributions is the learned disentanglement of identity (or appearance) and pose factors. This is a significant result, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness by showing that the learned identity features are discriminative and view-invariant, achieving strong performance on a cross-view object recognition task. Furthermore, the authors persuasively argue that this disentanglement is crucial for generating high-quality renderings, especially when using the longer rotation trajectories introduced via curriculum learning during training.

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the motivation for each component of the proposed method is clearly described. The qualitative results, which show convincing renderings of rotated objects, and the quantitative evaluation on the cross-view recognition task provide strong support for the method's effectiveness. The experiments successfully highlight the model's ability to learn disentangled representations for pose and identity, which is the central claim of the work.

While the paper is strong, I have a few minor concerns and questions, which I hope the authors can address in their rebuttal:

- The proposed network appears to be limited to discrete rotation angles, determined by the set of angles present in the training data. Could the authors comment on how this method might be extended to support continuous rotation angles?

- The recurrent network is trained using fixed-length action sequences. This seems to deviate from the typical application of RNNs, which are valued for their ability to handle variable-length sequences. Could the authors elaborate on this design choice and its implications?

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and effective method for rendering rotated objects from a single image. The main contribution—the generative disentanglement of identity and pose that emerges from the recurrent rotation prediction objective—is well-supported by compelling qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
