In this paper, the authors propose a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects such as human faces and chair models. The proposed network begins with a basic model where the encoder disentangles the input image into identity and pose units. Action units are then applied to the pose units to control the rotation direction, and the decoder—consisting of convolutional and upsampling layers—reconstructs the rotated object and corresponding object mask from the identity and pose representations. To support longer rotation trajectories, the network is extended with a recurrent architecture, where the encoded identity unit remains fixed while the pose unit evolves through a sequence of action units. Finally, both units are fed into the decoder to generate the output image. 

A key contribution of this work is learning to disentangle representations for identity/appearance and pose factors. The identity units are shown to provide discriminative, view-invariant features in a cross-view object recognition task. This disentangling property also improves rendering performance, particularly with longer rotation trajectories, through curriculum training of the recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network. 

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation for each component of the proposed method. The qualitative results demonstrating rotated image synthesis, and the quantitative evaluation of cross-view object recognition, provide strong support for the approach, especially the disentangled representations for pose and identity.

Some minor weaknesses are noted below, and addressing these in a rebuttal would be beneficial:

*   The proposed network currently supports only discrete rotation angles, limited by the set of angles present in the training data. Do the authors have preliminary ideas on extending the method to handle continuous rotation angles?
*   The recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which is somewhat contrary to the typical flexibility of recurrent neural networks. Could the authors elaborate on the reasoning behind this design choice?

This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network trained end-to-end for rendering rotated objects from a single image. The main contribution—generative disentangling of identity and pose factors emerging from the recurrent rotation prediction objective—is well demonstrated through qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
