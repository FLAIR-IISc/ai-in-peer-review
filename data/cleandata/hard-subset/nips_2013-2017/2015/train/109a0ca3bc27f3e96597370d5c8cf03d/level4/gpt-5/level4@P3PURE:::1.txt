This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that renders an object from different pivoting viewpoints given a single 2D image. The model is evaluated on generating novel views of faces and chairs, with additional experiments examining curriculum learning, disentangled representations via cross-view object recognition, and class interpolation on chairs.

The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The model and training setup are clearly described, and the design choices are presented in sufficient detail. There are a few minor grammatical issues (e.g., line 124: “rotate” → “rotated”).

However, the evaluation is inadequate for a definitive assessment of the model’s effectiveness. The experimental evidence is largely qualitative. Figures should be scaled so that visual results are legible, but more importantly, the paper needs quantitative metrics and comparisons. Introducing appropriate error measures and benchmarking against state-of-the-art baselines—particularly the “geometry-based approaches” referenced on line 51—would be substantially more informative than qualitative assessments alone. This applies both to view synthesis quality and to any claims about disentanglement (e.g., using cross-view recognition accuracy or other standard measures).

The significance of the work is further limited by the narrow scope of datasets. Results are presented only on Multi-PIE and Chairs. Demonstrating the method on a broader set of object categories would make the approach feel less ad hoc and better support claims of generality. It would also be valuable to explore how the model handles rotation trajectories that deviate from a single, static axis (i.e., non-repeating, off-axis, or compound rotations), to assess robustness beyond simple pivoting transformations.

In summary, the proposed approach is novel and interesting, and the paper is clear. Nonetheless, its impact is hampered by limited experimentation and analysis. Strengthening the work with:
- Quantitative evaluations and comparisons to relevant baselines (including geometry-based methods),
- Broader dataset coverage beyond faces and chairs,
- More thorough analysis of curriculum learning and disentanglement effects, and
- Tests on non-static-axis rotation trajectories

would substantially improve the paper and clarify its potential relative to the state of the art.
