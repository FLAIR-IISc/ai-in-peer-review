In this paper, the authors present a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network capable of rendering an object from novel pivoting viewpoints given a single 2D image. They evaluate their model by generating images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints, and include additional experiments to examine the benefits of curriculum learning, assess the quality of learned representations through cross-view object recognition, and explore class interpolation with chairs.

While the proposed model is well-presented and detailed, its evaluation feels somewhat limited. The experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of the RNN model are largely qualitative; the figures visualizing the results would benefit from being scaled to a size that allows for clearer inspection. Introducing a quantitative performance measure with an appropriate error metric – enabling comparison with state-of-the-art methods – would provide more insightful results than qualitative assessments alone. 

Overall, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. There are a few minor grammatical errors (e.g., line 124, “rotate” should be “rotated”).

The paper’s significance is currently hampered by a lack of extensive experimentation and analysis. Expanding the experiments to include datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs would be beneficial. Demonstrating the model’s ability to render rotated viewpoints for a wider variety of objects would help to establish the network architecture as more generalizable rather than specifically tailored to these choices. Furthermore, it would be interesting to investigate the model’s ability to generalize to object rotation trajectories that deviate from a fixed axis—in other words, to handle non-repeating rotations. The paper develops a deep learning model for rendering objects from different rotational viewpoints based on a single 2D image, and while the approach is novel and interesting, it lacks sufficient experimentation and analysis to fully establish the model’s significance and demonstrate results comparable to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51).
