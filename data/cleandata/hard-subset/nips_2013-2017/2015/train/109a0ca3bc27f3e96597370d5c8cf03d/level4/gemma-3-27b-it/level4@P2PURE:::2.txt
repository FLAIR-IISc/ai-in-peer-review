This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, demonstrated on datasets of human faces and 3D chairs. The network begins with a basic encoder that disentangles the input image into identity and pose representations. Action units are then applied to the pose representation to control the rotation direction. A decoder, composed of convolutional and unsampling layers, reconstructs an image of the rotated object and its corresponding mask from the combined identity and pose representations. To facilitate longer rotation trajectories, the network is extended with a recurrent architecture, where the encoded identity representation remains fixed while the pose representation is updated by a sequence of action units; both are then fed to the decoder to generate the output image.

A key contribution of this work is learning to disentangle representations for identity/appearance and pose factors. The authors demonstrate that the learned identity representations function as discriminative, view-invariant features in a cross-view object recognition task. Furthermore, this disentanglement improves rendering performance, particularly when utilizing longer rotation trajectories during curriculum training of the recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network. 

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation provided for each component of the proposed method. Qualitative results demonstrating predicted renderings of rotated images and quantitative evaluation on a cross-view object recognition task strongly support the method, particularly the effectiveness of the disentangled pose and identity representations. 

Several minor weaknesses warrant further consideration:
- The network currently supports only discrete rotation angles, determined by the training data. Do the authors have any preliminary ideas on how to extend the method to accommodate continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with a fixed sequence length, which contrasts with the typical variable-length input handling of recurrent neural networks.

Overall, this paper introduces a novel, end-to-end trained recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for rendering rotated objects from a single image. The generative disentanglement of identity and pose factors, driven by the recurrent rotation prediction objective, is convincingly demonstrated through qualitative and quantitative results.
