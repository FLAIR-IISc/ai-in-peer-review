This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that renders novel views of an object from a single 2D image by simulating rotations. The authors evaluate the model on faces and chairs, and include additional studies on curriculum learning, disentanglement via cross-view object recognition, and class interpolation (for chairs).

While the model is clearly presented and the paper is generally well written, the empirical evaluation is inadequate. The main evidence for effectiveness is qualitative; figures should also be scaled so that details are visible. A quantitative evaluation with an appropriate error metric, together with comparisons to strong baselines and state-of-the-art methods, would be far more informative than qualitative assessments alone.

The significance of the work is further limited by the narrow scope of the experiments. Results are reported only on Multi-PIE and Chairs. Including additional datasets and object categories would better substantiate claims of generality and make the architecture feel less ad hoc. It would also be valuable to examine how the model handles rotation trajectories that do not follow a single static axis and may be non-repeating.

The supplementary analyses are promising directions but need deeper treatment. For instance, the curriculum learning results would benefit from quantitative gains and ablations; the disentanglement claims should be supported by stronger cross-view recognition metrics; and the class interpolation experiments should be evaluated more rigorously to establish whether the model captures meaningful shape and appearance factors rather than overfitting to the training distribution.

Overall, the approach is novel and interesting, and the paper is easy to follow. However, the limited experimentation and lack of robust quantitative analysis make it difficult to assess the model’s significance and its competitiveness with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51). Minor comment: line 124, “rotate” should be “rotated.”
