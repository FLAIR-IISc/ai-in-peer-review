This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture designed to learn out-of-plane rotations of 3D objects, including human faces and 3D chair models. The proposed network begins with a basic model, where the encoder disentangles the input image into identity and pose components. The action units applied to the pose components control the rotation direction, and the decoder, comprising convolutional and upsampling layers, generates the rotated object image and corresponding mask. To accommodate longer rotation trajectories, the network is extended to incorporate a recurrent architecture, where the encoded identity component remains fixed, and the pose component is updated by a sequence of action units. The resulting identity and pose components are then fed into the decoder to produce the final image.

A primary contribution of this paper lies in learning to disentangle representations for identity/appearance and pose factors. The identity components are demonstrated to be discriminative view-invariant features in cross-view object recognition tasks. Furthermore, this disentangling property enables better rendering predictions when employing longer rotation trajectories during curriculum training stages for the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network.

The paper is well-structured, easy to follow, and provides clear motivation for each component of the proposed method. The qualitative results for predicted renderings of rotated images and quantitative evaluations on cross-view object recognition tasks offer strong support for the method, particularly the disentangled representations for pose and identity factors.

- The proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which contradicts the typical behavior of general recurrent neural networks.
