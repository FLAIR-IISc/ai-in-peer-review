

This paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views from single images through weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors. The authors address the challenging problem of 3D view synthesis by proposing a novel architecture that learns to traverse pose manifolds through sequences of small transformations, rather than modeling specific rotation sequences. Their approach builds on recent work in disentangled representations (e.g., IGN [17], disBM [21]) and sequence modeling (e.g., [27]), but makes significant contributions by incorporating recurrent structure with action units to handle long-term dependencies in rotation trajectories, and demonstrating weakly-supervised disentangling without explicit class labels.

The paper's main contribution is a recurrent architecture that disentangles identity (invariant to pose) and pose (transformable) factors through a curriculum learning approach that gradually increases rotation trajectory length. This design enables the model to generate realistic rotation sequences for faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs (CAD models), while simultaneously learning discriminative view-invariant features that outperform baseline methods in cross-view recognition tasks.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets and tasks. The curriculum learning strategy effectively addresses the long-term dependency problem in sequence prediction, with clear evidence of improved performance as trajectory length increases. The ablation studies comparing different curriculum stages (RNN1 to RNN16) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. The evaluation metrics (MSE, recognition accuracy) are appropriate for the task, and the qualitative results demonstrate high-quality image synthesis with preserved identity characteristics. The model's ability to disentangle identity and pose factors without explicit regularization is particularly impressive.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, methodology, and results are presented in a logical flow. Key concepts like the identity/pose units and action units are clearly explained with appropriate visualizations (Figures 1-2). The network architecture description is precise, and the training details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The authors effectively connect their approach to related work (Section 2) and demonstrate how their method advances beyond prior art.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by combining recurrent networks with weakly-supervised disentangling for 3D view synthesis. While related to IGN [17] and transforming autoencoders [12], the authors' recurrent architecture with action units for pose manifold traversal represents a significant departure from existing approaches. The demonstration that disentangling emerges naturally from the rotation prediction objective (rather than being explicitly regularized) is particularly novel. The authors effectively establish novelty by contrasting with relevant NIPS papers (e.g., IGN, transforming autoencoders, disBM).

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental problem in both computer vision and graphics with practical applications in photo editing, virtual reality, and vision-based reinforcement learning. The ability to synthesize novel views from single images without explicit 3D models is particularly valuable for domains where 3D scans are unavailable. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors has broader implications for representation learning, potentially enabling more robust object recognition systems. The paper's results on challenging datasets (Multi-PIE, chair CAD models) demonstrate clear advances over existing methods, particularly for large rotation angles.

**Strengths:** 
1. The recurrent architecture with curriculum learning effectively solves the long-term dependency problem in rotation trajectories.
2. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors is demonstrated convincingly through both qualitative and quantitative results.
3. The experiments on diverse datasets (faces, chairs) provide strong evidence of generalizability.
4. The ablation studies (Figures 6-7) clearly show the benefits of curriculum training.
5. The class interpolation results (Figure 9) demonstrate the model's ability to generate novel objects through manifold traversal.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The model currently supports only discrete rotation angles based on training data, which limits its applicability to continuous rotation scenarios. The authors should discuss how their approach might be extended to handle continuous rotations, perhaps through interpolation or more sophisticated action representations.
2. The fixed-length training sequences (despite the recurrent nature of the model) could be better justified. While the authors use curriculum learning to gradually increase sequence length, the approach still requires training separate models for different sequence lengths (RNN1, RNN2, etc.), which is inefficient compared to standard RNNs that can handle variable-length sequences.
3. The paper lacks analysis of computational efficiency compared to alternative approaches. How does the training time and memory usage scale with sequence length? This would be valuable for practitioners considering deployment.
4. The cross-view recognition results on chairs (Figure 8) show that VGG-16 outperforms the RNN model at large angle offsets (>120°). The authors should discuss this phenomenon in more depth, particularly their hypothesis about symmetric chair structures.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. While the limitations noted above are worth addressing in a revision, they do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors emerges naturally from the rotation prediction objective, which represents a significant advance over explicit regularization approaches. This work has clear potential for influencing future research in both graphics and vision.

Overall, I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, with the weaknesses being minor and addressable. The contribution is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant publication in this venue.
