

This paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views from single 2D images. The authors focus on two object classes (faces and chairs) and demonstrate how their model can generate realistic rotation trajectories through a sequence of small transformations. The key innovation lies in their recurrent structure that captures long-term dependencies during pose manifold traversal, along with a curriculum learning approach that gradually increases the complexity of rotation sequences. The model also achieves weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors without explicit class labels, as evidenced by cross-view recognition experiments.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough explanation of the model architecture and training procedure. The authors provide sufficient technical details for reproduction, including network configurations, training schedules, and implementation specifics. The related work section effectively contextualizes the contribution within the broader literature on 3D view synthesis, disentangled representations, and recurrent networks for sequence modeling.

The main strengths of the paper include the novel recurrent architecture that handles long-term dependencies in pose manifold traversal, the elegant weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors without explicit regularization, and the comprehensive evaluation across multiple aspects of the problem. The curriculum learning approach is particularly well-motivated and effectively demonstrates how longer training sequences improve both image quality and feature discriminability. The cross-view recognition experiments provide compelling evidence that the identity units learn pose-invariant representations, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the paper suffers from inadequate quantitative evaluation of the core view synthesis task. While the authors present qualitative results (Figures 3-5), they lack sufficient quantitative metrics to benchmark their approach against existing methods. For instance, the comparison with the KNN baseline in Figure 7 shows MSE values but fails to contextualize these results within the broader literature on 3D view synthesis. The paper would benefit from standard image quality metrics (e.g., PSNR, SSIM) and direct comparisons with geometry-based approaches such as 3D morphable models [28] or structure-from-motion techniques [5], which are mentioned but not properly evaluated.

Furthermore, the experimental scope is limited to two datasets (Multi-PIE and chairs), which restricts the generalizability of the findings. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not address it through additional experiments. Expanding the evaluation to more diverse object categories would strengthen the claim that the approach is not limited to specific object classes. The paper also lacks exploration of non-axis-aligned rotations, which would demonstrate the model's ability to handle more complex transformation trajectories beyond the static-axis rotations tested.

The significance of the work is further diminished by the absence of a thorough comparison with the state-of-the-art in 3D view synthesis. While the paper discusses related work comprehensively, it fails to position its quantitative results within the context of existing methods. For example, the MSE values reported (e.g., 179 for RNN16) should be compared against established baselines to demonstrate the model's relative performance.

Minor issues include a few grammatical inconsistencies (e.g., "rotate" should be "rotated" on line 124) and the lack of standardized image scaling in the figures, which makes visual comparisons difficult. The authors should ensure all visualizations are presented at a consistent scale for clear assessment.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1) Include quantitative metrics for view synthesis quality (PSNR, SSIM) and compare with relevant baselines
2) Expand experiments to additional object categories beyond faces and chairs
3) Evaluate the model on non-axis-aligned rotation trajectories
4) Provide more context for their quantitative results by comparing against established benchmarks

The paper presents an interesting approach to 3D view synthesis with a novel recurrent architecture that effectively handles long-term dependencies. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors is particularly noteworthy. However, the current evaluation does not adequately demonstrate the model's performance relative to existing methods, limiting the significance of the contribution. With more comprehensive quantitative evaluation and expanded experimental scope, this work could make a stronger contribution to the field.
