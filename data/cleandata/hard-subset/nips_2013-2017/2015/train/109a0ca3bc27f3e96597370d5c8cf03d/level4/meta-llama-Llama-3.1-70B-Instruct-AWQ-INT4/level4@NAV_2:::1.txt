This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for rendering an object from different rotational viewpoints given a single 2D image of the object. The authors evaluate the performance of their proposed model through its ability to generate images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints. They also perform additional experiments to examine the benefits of curriculum learning, to evaluate the model's performance of disentangled representations through cross-view object recognition, and to explore the model's ability to perform class interpolation with chairs.

The proposed model is well-presented and detailed, with a clear explanation of the network architecture and its components. However, the evaluation of its performance is somewhat limited. The experiments used to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed RNN model are largely qualitative, with visualizations of results that are not always easy to interpret. Introducing a quantitative measure of performance with an appropriate error metric would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise language. However, there are a few minor grammatical errors that should be corrected.

The significance of this paper is somewhat impaired by its lack of extensive experimentation and analysis. While the results on the Multi-PIE and Chairs datasets are promising, it would be relevant to include experiments with more datasets to demonstrate the model's ability to generalize to different types of objects. Additionally, it would be interesting to see how the proposed model could handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis (non-repeating rotations).

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest in evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches.

The clarity of the paper is good, with a clear and well-organized structure. The writing is concise, and the figures are well-labeled and easy to understand. However, some of the figures could be scaled to a more appropriate size for the reader to see.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to rendering an object from different rotational viewpoints given a single 2D image of the object. The use of a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is a new and interesting approach to this problem.

The significance of the paper is moderate, as it addresses an important problem in computer vision and provides a promising solution. However, the lack of extensive experimentation and analysis somewhat limits the paper's significance.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to rendering an object from different rotational viewpoints given a single 2D image of the object.
* The results on the Multi-PIE and Chairs datasets are promising, and the model's ability to generalize to different types of objects is demonstrated.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise language.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The evaluation of the model's performance is somewhat limited, with a lack of quantitative measures and comparisons with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches.
* The paper lacks extensive experimentation and analysis, which somewhat limits its significance.
* The model's ability to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis (non-repeating rotations) is not demonstrated.
