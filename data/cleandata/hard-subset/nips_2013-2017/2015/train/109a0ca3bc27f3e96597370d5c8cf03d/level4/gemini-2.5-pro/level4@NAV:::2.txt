This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture designed to learn out-of-plane rotations for 3D objects like human faces and chair models. The architecture is built upon a foundational model where an encoder separates an input image into distinct identity and pose representations. Action units are then applied to the pose representation to control the rotation, and a decoder, composed of convolutional and upsampling layers, synthesizes the rotated object image and its corresponding mask from the original identity and modified pose. To enable extended rotational sequences, this model is advanced into a recurrent framework. In this setup, the identity representation from the initial image is held constant while the pose representation is iteratively updated by a sequence of action units, with the resulting pairs fed to the decoder to generate the final image.

A key contribution of this work is its ability to learn disentangled representations for identity and pose. The effectiveness of this disentanglement is demonstrated by showing that the learned identity features are discriminative and view-invariant, as validated on a cross-view object recognition task. Furthermore, the authors show that this property improves rendering quality, particularly when longer rotational trajectories are employed during the curriculum training phase of the recurrent network.

The manuscript is clearly written and straightforward to understand, and the rationale behind each component of the proposed method is well-articulated. The presented qualitative results of rendered rotated images, along with the quantitative evaluation on the cross-view object recognition task, offer strong support for the proposed approach, particularly for the claims regarding the successful disentanglement of pose and identity factors.

Some minor points are listed below, which could be addressed by the authors in the rebuttal:
- The current framework appears limited to the discrete rotation angles present in the training data. Could the authors discuss potential strategies for extending the model to handle continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent network is trained using fixed-length sequences. This is a slight departure from the variable-length capabilities often associated with RNNs, and some justification for this design choice would be welcome.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture that is trained end-to-end for rendering rotated objects from a single image. Its primary contribution, the generative disentanglement of identity and pose factors that emerges from the recurrent rotation prediction objective, is convincingly supported by both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
