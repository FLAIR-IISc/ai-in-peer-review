This paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed to render an object from novel viewpoints, given only a single 2D image as input. The authors demonstrate the model’s capabilities by generating rotated views of faces and chairs. Supporting experiments investigate the benefits of curriculum learning, assess the quality of learned representations via cross-view object recognition, and explore class interpolation specifically for chair images.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented, with a detailed description of the proposed model. However, the evaluation feels insufficient to fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness. While the visualizations are helpful, the reliance on largely qualitative results is a significant weakness. Figures showcasing the generated images should be scaled for improved readability. Crucially, the inclusion of a quantitative evaluation using a suitable error metric would allow for a more rigorous assessment and comparison against existing state-of-the-art techniques.

The significance of this work is currently limited by a lack of comprehensive experimentation and analysis. Extending the evaluation beyond the Multi-PIE and Chairs datasets would strengthen the claims of generalizability. Demonstrating the model’s ability to render rotated views for a wider variety of object categories would reduce the perception that the architecture is specifically tailored to these chosen datasets. Furthermore, exploring the model’s performance with more complex rotation trajectories – those deviating from a fixed axis or involving non-repeating patterns – would be a valuable addition. 

The core contribution – a deep learning approach to single-image 3D viewpoint rendering – is novel and interesting. However, the paper lacks the substantial experimental validation needed to establish its significance relative to established ‘geometry-based approaches’ (as noted in line 51). A more thorough evaluation is required to convincingly demonstrate the potential of this method and position it within the broader field. A minor grammatical correction is suggested: on line 124, “rotate” should be changed to “rotated.”
