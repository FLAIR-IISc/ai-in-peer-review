This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that learns to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects (e.g., human faces and chairs) from a single image. The model begins with an encoder that disentangles the input into identity units and pose units. Action units are applied to the pose units to control the rotation direction. A decoder composed of convolution and upsampling layers then reconstructs the rotated image and its corresponding object mask from the identity and pose representations. To support longer rotation trajectories, the network is extended to a recurrent architecture in which the encoded identity units from the input image are held fixed while the pose units are updated by a sequence of action inputs; at each step, identity and pose are decoded to produce the next rendered view.

A key contribution is the learned disentanglement of identity/appearance and pose factors. The identity units are shown to be discriminative, view-invariant features in a cross-view object recognition task. Moreover, this disentanglement improves rendering quality, particularly when training with a curriculum that gradually increases the length of rotation sequences in the recurrent encoder–decoder.

The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, with well-motivated architectural choices. Both the qualitative renderings of rotated images and the quantitative cross-view recognition results support the approach, especially the emergence of disentangled pose and identity representations.

Minor weaknesses and questions:
- The network supports only discrete rotation angles, determined by those present in the training data. Do the authors have ideas for extending the method to handle continuous rotation angles (e.g., via continuous action parameterization or interpolation in pose space)?
- The recurrent encoder–decoder is trained with fixed-length sequences, which seems at odds with the general flexibility of recurrent neural networks. Can the authors clarify how variable-length sequences are handled at training and test time, and whether the model can generalize to longer or shorter trajectories than seen during training?

Overall, the paper presents a novel, end-to-end trained recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder for single-image novel-view synthesis. The emergent disentangling of identity and pose driven by the rotation-prediction objective is well demonstrated by both the qualitative renderings and the cross-view recognition evaluations.
