This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder that synthesizes novel views of an object from a single 2D image by applying a sequence of pivot rotations. The model is evaluated on faces and chairs, with additional studies on curriculum learning, disentanglement via cross-view recognition, and class interpolation for chairs.

Strengths:
- The architecture is clearly presented and technically well detailed.
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow.
- The ancillary experiments (curriculum learning, disentanglement, interpolation) are relevant and thoughtfully chosen.

Main concerns:
- The evaluation is largely qualitative. The paper relies heavily on visualizations, which, in several figures, are too small to inspect carefully. This limits the reader’s ability to assess performance.
- There is no rigorous quantitative comparison to strong baselines or state-of-the-art, including geometry-based approaches (as noted around line 51). Without appropriate error metrics, it is difficult to gauge the model’s true effectiveness.
- The experimental scope is narrow. Results are limited to Multi-PIE and Chairs, raising questions about generalization to other categories and whether the architecture is tailored to these domains.
- The paper does not address rotations that deviate from a fixed axis or non-repeating trajectories, which are common in real scenarios.
- Claims about disentanglement would be stronger with more direct quantitative evidence rather than qualitative impressions.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include quantitative metrics such as PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, and downstream recognition accuracy on rendered views. Report performance across rotation angles and degrees of occlusion/self-occlusion.
- Compare against robust baselines: geometry-based methods, standard encoder–decoder variants without recurrence, and recent view-synthesis models if applicable.
- Expand to additional datasets and object categories to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Evaluate the model’s ability to handle off-axis, non-periodic, and compound rotations, as well as longer trajectories.
- Provide ablations isolating the effects of recurrence, the latent representation, and curriculum learning.
- Improve figure readability by enlarging key visualizations.
- Minor editorial fix: line 124, “rotate” should be “rotated.”

Overall, the approach is novel and promising for single-image novel-view synthesis, and the paper is clearly written. However, its significance is undermined by limited experiments and a lack of rigorous quantitative analysis. Strengthening the evaluation and broadening the empirical scope would substantially improve the paper and clarify how it compares to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches.
