This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel views of 3D objects (faces and chairs) from a single image. The core idea is to learn a mapping between image space and a non-linear manifold representing object pose, enabling rotation through a sequence of transformations. The network disentangles image features into identity (viewpoint-invariant) and pose units, and utilizes recurrent connections to capture long-term dependencies in rotation sequences. The authors demonstrate the quality of generated images and the effectiveness of disentangled representations through extensive experiments.

This work builds upon a rich history of research at NIPS and elsewhere. Previous efforts in graphics have focused on rendering from 3D models and environmental variables [14], while vision algorithms have aimed to understand image content [10, 16, 18, 26]. This paper tackles the challenging problem of automatically generating novel views from a single image, bridging the gap between these fields. The authors correctly position their work against geometry-based approaches like multi-view stereo [5] and model-based registration, highlighting the limitations of these methods in terms of object class specificity and artifact generation. More recent work in generative modeling [8, 17, 25] provides relevant context, and the authors effectively differentiate their approach through the use of a recurrent architecture with action units, enabling controlled, sequential rotation. Related lines of work on disentangling latent factors [6, 21, 24, 29] are also appropriately cited.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of a recurrent convolutional architecture with disentangled identity and pose units for view synthesis is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound, with a clear and logical architecture. The use of curriculum learning to address the challenges of long-term dependencies is a sensible approach [4, 27].
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide compelling qualitative and quantitative results on both face and chair datasets. The cross-view recognition experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the learned disentangled representations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup.
*   **Significance:** The ability to synthesize novel views from a single image has significant potential applications in areas such as photo editing [14] and vision-based reinforcement learning [20].

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Discrete Rotation Angles:** The current implementation relies on discrete rotation angles defined during training. This limits the flexibility of the model. Investigating methods for continuous rotation control would be a valuable extension.
*   **Fixed-Length Sequences:** The use of fixed-length sequences in the recurrent network seems somewhat counterintuitive, given the inherent flexibility of RNNs. Further justification for this design choice is needed.
*   **Limited Action Space:** The action units only allow for clockwise, counter-clockwise, or no rotation. Expanding the action space to include more complex transformations or finer-grained control could improve performance.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to KNN is helpful, further comparison to other state-of-the-art image generation techniques (e.g., GANs, VAEs) would strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging and important problem in computer vision and graphics.
*   The proposed method is novel and technically sound.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limitation to discrete rotation angles restricts the practical applicability of the model.
*   The use of fixed-length sequences in the recurrent network is potentially suboptimal.
*   The comparison to other image generation techniques is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-executed approach to the problem of 3D view synthesis. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh them. I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. I encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in the rebuttal period, particularly regarding the limitations of discrete rotation angles and fixed-length sequences.
