Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses single-image 3D view synthesis: given one image of an object (faces and 3D chairs), generate novel views under out-of-plane rotations. The authors propose a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network with action units that control small rotation steps along a pose manifold. The encoder disentangles the input into identity units (appearance, view-invariant) and pose units (view), and the decoder reconstructs the image (and, for chairs, a foreground mask). For long trajectories, the pose units are updated recurrently with action inputs while identity units are held fixed, and curriculum learning increases trajectory length during training. Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and a large chair dataset show smooth, plausible renderings across significant pose changes, improved quantitative errors over a KNN baseline on chairs, and cross-view recognition accuracy using the identity units, indicating effective disentanglement.

This work is well positioned relative to prior image generation and disentangling efforts. Compared with chair generation via feed-forward decoders, the contribution here is a learned encoder that generalizes to novel instances and a recurrent, action-conditioned mechanism for long sequences of transformations. Relative to inverse graphics/graphics codes approaches, the method uses deterministic training and emphasizes weakly supervised disentangling that emerges from the multi-step rotation objective. It also relates to transforming autoencoders and time-series transformation models, but differs by explicitly separating identity and pose and by using action-controlled recurrent updates to traverse the pose manifold.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent model: a convolutional encoder–decoder with identity and pose units, and action units implementing local rotation steps. The recurrent extension is natural and addresses long-term dependency in pose changes.
- Curriculum learning is used effectively: increasing sequence length improves both rendering quality and the view-invariance of identity features.
- Empirical support: high-quality qualitative results on Multi-PIE and chairs, with smooth transitions and preservation of object characteristics; quantitative improvements over a KNN baseline on chair MSE; and cross-view recognition results showing that identity units are discriminative and increasingly pose-invariant as training length grows.
- The method demonstrates weakly supervised disentangling: without class labels, identity units support cross-view recognition while pose units carry little identity information.
- Additional capabilities such as interpolating identity codes to synthesize new chair instances are compelling and consistent with the intended disentanglement.

Weaknesses and questions
- Discrete actions/angles: As presented, the model supports a discrete set of rotation steps determined by training data. Do the authors have ideas for extending to continuous angles (e.g., continuous action parameterization, regression of rotation deltas, or learned interpolation between discrete transforms)?
- Fixed-length training: Although the architecture is recurrent, training uses fixed-length sequences. Could the model be trained with variable-length sequences and/or scheduled sampling to further improve robustness to long trajectories? Clarifying how well models trained at length t generalize to longer sequences at test time would also be helpful.

Quality
- The paper appears technically sound. The architecture and training objective are well specified, and the recurrent formulation addresses drift off the pose manifold. The experimental protocol is reasonable, with both qualitative and quantitative evaluations. The chair baseline (KNN over VGG features) is simple but sensible; the substantial gap to RNN16 is convincing.
- One suggestion: briefly discuss the effect of the mask branch (for chairs) on training stability and final image quality, and whether the same idea benefits faces.

Clarity
- The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow. The roles of identity, pose, and action units are well explained, and curriculum learning is motivated and demonstrated. Minor editorial notes: consistently use “upsampling” rather than “unsampling,” and explicitly state the rotation step size associated with the 1-of-3 action coding.

Originality
- The combination of a convolutional encoder–decoder with action-conditioned recurrent pose updates and curriculum learning for multi-step 3D rotation is novel and differentiates the work from prior feed-forward graphics-code decoders and earlier transformation models. The emergence of disentangled identity/pose representations under weak supervision is a noteworthy contribution.

Significance
- The results are practically and scientifically meaningful. The ability to synthesize plausible novel views from a single image within a class, while learning view-invariant identity features, is valuable for both graphics (editing, rendering) and vision (recognition under pose variation). The demonstrated improvements over a reasonable baseline and the cross-view recognition performance suggest the approach will be useful to others.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated model with strong qualitative and quantitative results.
- Effective curriculum learning for long transformation sequences.
- Convincing evidence of weakly supervised disentanglement and cross-view recognition.
- Interpolation results indicate learned identity/pose manifolds are meaningful.

Cons for acceptance
- Restriction to discrete rotation steps; unclear path to continuous control.
- Fixed-length training despite recurrent structure; unclear generalization to significantly longer sequences.

Overall assessment
This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder trained end-to-end to render rotated objects from a single image. The main contribution—generative disentangling of identity and pose emerging from the recurrent rotation objective—is well supported by qualitative renderings and cross-view recognition results. With clarification on continuous rotations and training sequence length, this would be a strong contribution.
