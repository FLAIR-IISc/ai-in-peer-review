This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for applying out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, such as human faces and chair models. The architecture begins with a basic encoder-decoder model. The encoder disentangles an input image into separate "identity" and "pose" representations. To control the rotation, "action units" are applied to the pose representation. A decoder, consisting of convolutional and upsampling layers, then uses the identity and modified pose representations to generate both an image of the rotated object and a corresponding object mask.

To support longer rotation trajectories, this model is extended into a recurrent architecture. In this setup, the identity representation from the initial input image is held constant while the pose representation is sequentially updated by a series of action units. The decoder then generates an output image at each step using the fixed identity and the current pose representation.

A key contribution of this work is the learned disentanglement of identity and pose. The authors demonstrate that the resulting identity representation serves as a discriminative, view-invariant feature, which is validated in a cross-view object recognition task. Furthermore, this disentanglement proves beneficial for generating high-quality renderings over long rotation trajectories, which are introduced via a curriculum learning strategy during training.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation for each component of the proposed method. The qualitative results of the rendered rotations and the quantitative evaluation on the cross-view object recognition task provide strong support for the method's effectiveness, particularly in its ability to learn disentangled representations of pose and identity.

A few minor weaknesses are listed below, which the authors can hopefully address in their rebuttal:
- The network is limited to discrete rotation angles determined by the training data. Could the authors comment on potential strategies for extending the model to support continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent network is trained using fixed-length sequences. As RNNs are generally capable of handling variable-length sequences, could the authors clarify the reasoning behind this design choice?

In summary, this paper presents a novel, end-to-end trainable recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for rendering rotated objects from a single image. The primary contribution is the generative disentanglement of identity and pose factors, an emergent property of the recurrent rotation prediction objective. This contribution is well-supported by both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
