

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of objects from single 2D images, with a focus on weakly-supervised disentanglement of identity and pose factors. The work builds upon recent NIPS contributions in view synthesis (e.g., IGN [17] for inverse graphics, and [8] for chair generation) but introduces a novel recurrent structure to model long-term dependencies in pose manifold traversal, avoiding explicit 3D reconstruction. The authors demonstrate effectiveness on Multi-PIE (faces) and CAD chair datasets, using curriculum learning to progressively increase rotation trajectory length and achieve disentangled identity features without class labels.  

**Strengths:**  
- *Originality:* The recurrent architecture for sequence-based pose traversal (vs. single-step transformations in [12, 19]) and the emergent weakly-supervised disentanglement (Section 4.4) represent significant advances over prior NIPS work on disentangling [6, 21, 29]. The curriculum learning approach (Section 3.1) is well-motivated and effectively improves disentanglement.  
- *Quality:* The technical formulation is sound, with clear ablation (Figures 6–7), rigorous cross-view recognition experiments (Figure 8), and quantitative MSE comparisons (Figure 7). The model’s ability to preserve object identity during rotations (Table 1) is a strong empirical contribution.  
- *Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with precise descriptions of the network architecture (Section 3) and training details (Section 4.2). The figures (e.g., Figure 2 for RNN unrolling) effectively illustrate the method.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- *Significance:* The scope is limited to faces and chairs (Section 4.1), with no analysis of generalization to other object classes (e.g., cars, animals) or complex scenes. This restricts the impact compared to geometry-based methods [5, 28] which handle broader domains.  
- *Comparison:* While Figure 4 compares to a 3D morphable model, the paper lacks quantitative comparisons to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (e.g., [5, 28]) or other learning-based view synthesis methods (e.g., [9]). The MSE improvement over KNN (Figure 7) is notable but insufficient to establish superiority over established baselines.  
- *Analysis:* The discussion of non-axis rotations (e.g., complex trajectories beyond simple pivoting) is absent (Section 5 mentions "future work" but lacks experimental exploration). The significance claim ("advances state of the art") is overstated without broader validation.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel recurrent pose traversal, strong weakly-supervised disentanglement (Table 1), rigorous curriculum learning, clear technical execution.  
*Con:* Limited dataset scope (faces/chairs only), insufficient comparison to geometry-based methods, no analysis of non-axis rotations.  

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to view synthesis but lacks sufficient evidence to justify its significance beyond the two tested domains. The quality and clarity are high, but the significance is constrained by the narrow experimental scope. A stronger case for impact would require generalization to diverse object classes or comparisons to geometry-based baselines.
