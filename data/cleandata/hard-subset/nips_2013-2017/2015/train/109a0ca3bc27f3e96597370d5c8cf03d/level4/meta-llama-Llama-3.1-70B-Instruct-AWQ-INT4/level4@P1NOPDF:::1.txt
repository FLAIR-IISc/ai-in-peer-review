In this paper, the authors propose a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that can generate an object's appearance from different viewpoints given a single 2D image. They evaluate their model's performance by generating images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints. Additional experiments investigate the benefits of curriculum learning, assess the model's ability to learn disentangled representations through cross-view object recognition, and explore its capacity for class interpolation with chairs.

Although the proposed model is well-presented and detailed, its evaluation is somewhat limited. The experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed RNN model are largely qualitative, relying on visualizations of results. To provide a more comprehensive assessment, the authors should consider introducing quantitative performance measures with appropriate error metrics, enabling comparison with state-of-the-art results.

In general, the paper is well-written and easy to follow, with only a few minor grammatical errors (e.g., line 124, "rotate" should be "rotated"). However, the paper's significance is impaired by the lack of extensive experimentation and analysis. To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider including experiments with additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs. Demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for various object types would make the network architecture seem less specialized. Furthermore, it would be interesting to investigate how the proposed model generalizes to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis (i.e., non-repeating rotations).

The paper develops a deep learning model for rendering an object from different rotational viewpoints given a single 2D image of that object. Although the approach is novel and interesting, the paper lacks sufficient experimentation and relevant analysis to fully establish the model's significance and potential for achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51).
