This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder that, given a single 2D image of an object, generates novel views by applying a sequence of pivoting rotations. The authors evaluate on faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs, and report additional analyses on curriculum learning, cross-view object recognition as a proxy for disentanglement, and class interpolation within chairs.

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and an intuitive formulation: the recurrent setup naturally models a sequence of incremental rotations.
- The architecture and training procedure are described in sufficient detail to follow, and the paper is generally well written.
- The curriculum learning experiment is conceptually appropriate for a sequential rotation task and is an interesting design choice.
- Cross-view recognition and class interpolation are thoughtful probes into representation quality and the latent space’s smoothness.

Weaknesses
- The empirical evaluation is primarily qualitative. Without robust quantitative metrics, it is difficult to assess progress, compare to prior art, or understand limitations.
- Comparisons to strong baselines are missing, especially relative to geometry-based methods explicitly cited in the paper (line 51). The current results do not establish whether the proposed approach meets or surpasses established techniques under comparable conditions.
- The scope of datasets is narrow (Multi-PIE and Chairs). This limits claims about generality beyond aligned faces and a single synthetic-like object category.
- The rotation model appears tied to a fixed pivot/axis; it is unclear how well the method generalizes to more complex, non-repeating or multi-axis trajectories, occlusions, or large out-of-plane rotations.
- Evidence for disentanglement rests on a single cross-view recognition experiment. Stronger, more direct tests of identity–pose separation are needed.
- Figures are difficult to assess due to small size; this further weakens the qualitative case.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Add quantitative reconstruction metrics on held-out views. Options include per-pixel L2/PSNR, SSIM, masked/silhouette IoU (for chairs), keypoint or landmark error (for faces), and identity preservation measures under rotation (recognition accuracy using a pretrained verifier).
- Compare against diverse baselines:
  - Geometry-based: e.g., morphable models or multiview reconstruction pipelines where applicable, using the same or closely matched data.
  - Learning-based: a feed-forward encoder–decoder without recurrence; nearest-neighbor view retrieval; simple appearance-flow or warping baselines; and an RNN without curriculum learning.
- Provide ablations and generalization tests:
  - With vs. without curriculum learning; different curriculum schedules; train on short sequences and test on longer sequences to measure extrapolation.
  - Open-loop generation over multiple steps to quantify error accumulation and drift.
  - Sensitivity to alignment, background variation, and lighting. If training relies on tight alignment (e.g., Multi-PIE), report performance when alignment is relaxed.
- Extend datasets and object types to demonstrate breadth. Include distinct categories with different structure (e.g., vehicles, household objects) to reduce the impression that the architecture is ad hoc to faces and chairs.
- Evaluate more complex camera motions and non-static axes. Demonstrating performance under combined yaw/pitch/roll or non-periodic trajectories would materially strengthen claims about generality.
- Deepen the disentanglement analysis:
  - Quantify pose–identity separation (e.g., train linear probes for identity vs. pose from the respective latent codes; report classification accuracy and mutual predictability).
  - Compare cross-view recognition to a strong discriminative CNN trained directly for recognition on the same data to contextualize gains or losses from the generative representation.
  - Visualize latent traversals that separately vary pose and identity while holding the other fixed; include metrics for identity consistency during pose changes.
- Improve figure readability by increasing resolution and providing side-by-side comparisons with ground truth and baselines, annotated with error measures.
- Discuss computational aspects: training time, memory footprint for unrolled sequences, and inference cost per additional rotation step.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is mostly clear and easy to follow. Please correct minor grammatical issues (e.g., line 124: “rotate” → “rotated”).
- Consider clarifying terminology such as “pivoting viewpoints” to avoid ambiguity (e.g., specify the rotation axis and whether it is fixed or learned).
- Where claims reference “state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches” (line 51), ensure that experimental comparisons or carefully scoped statements back this up.

Overall assessment
The model and task are timely and interesting, and the recurrent formulation is compelling. However, the current evaluation is too limited and largely qualitative to support strong conclusions about performance, generality, or advantages over established geometry-based methods. With rigorous quantitative metrics, stronger baselines, broader datasets, and a deeper analysis of disentanglement and generalization to non-static rotations, this work could make a meaningful contribution. In its current form, I view it as promising but not yet ready for acceptance.
