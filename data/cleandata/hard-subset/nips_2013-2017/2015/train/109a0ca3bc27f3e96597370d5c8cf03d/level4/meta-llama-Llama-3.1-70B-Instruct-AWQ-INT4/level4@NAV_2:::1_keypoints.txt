- Proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder to render objects from new rotational viewpoints given a single image.  
- Experiments demonstrate generated rotations for faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs, with additional tests on curriculum learning, disentangled representation via cross-view recognition, and class interpolation.  
- Model and architecture are well-presented with clear explanations of components.  
- Evaluation is largely qualitative; visualizations are useful but not always easy to interpret.  
- Lacks quantitative performance metrics and error measures to more comprehensively assess strengths and weaknesses.  
- Limited experimentation: results on two datasets are promising but more datasets are needed to demonstrate generalization.  
- Does not test non-axis or non-repeating rotation trajectories, leaving robustness to varied rotations unverified.  
- Technically sound; claims are supported by analysis and experiments, and authors fairly discuss limitations.  
- Needs more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches.  
- Writing is clear and concise, but contains minor grammatical errors to fix.  
- Figures are generally well-labeled and clear, though some should be scaled larger for readability.  
- Originality is high—the recurrent encoder–decoder approach to view synthesis is novel and interesting.  
- Overall significance is moderate due to methodological novelty but limited by sparse quantitative evaluation and limited experiments.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, promising results, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient quantitative evaluation, limited experiments/analysis, and untested rotation scenarios.
