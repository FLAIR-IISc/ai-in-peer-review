This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel viewpoints of an object from a single input image. The model's performance is demonstrated on face and chair datasets, where it generates images from rotated viewpoints. The authors also conduct experiments exploring the benefits of curriculum learning, the disentanglement of the learned representation (evaluated via cross-view object recognition), and the model's capacity for class interpolation.

The paper is well-written, and the proposed model is presented in a clear and detailed manner. However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its limited experimental evaluation and analysis, which significantly undermines its overall contribution. The evaluation relies heavily on qualitative results, which are insufficient for a thorough assessment. The inclusion of quantitative metrics and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, particularly the "geometry-based approaches" mentioned by the authors, would provide a much stronger validation of the proposed technique. On a related note, the figures presenting these qualitative results should be enlarged for better visibility. A few minor grammatical errors were also noted (e.g., on line 124, "rotate" should be "rotated").

Furthermore, the experiments are confined to faces and chairs. Expanding the evaluation to include more diverse object classes would be crucial to demonstrate the model's generality and to show that the architecture is not ad hoc for these specific datasets. It would also be interesting to see an analysis of the model's ability to generalize to more complex, non-repeating rotation trajectories beyond simple rotations around a static axis.

In summary, while the proposed deep learning approach for single-image viewpoint synthesis is novel and interesting, the paper in its current form lacks the rigorous experimentation and analysis necessary to substantiate its significance and demonstrate its potential to compete with established methods.
