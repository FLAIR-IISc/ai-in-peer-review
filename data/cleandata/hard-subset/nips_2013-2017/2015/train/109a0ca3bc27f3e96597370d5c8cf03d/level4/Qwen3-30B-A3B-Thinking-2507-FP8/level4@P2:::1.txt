

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views from single 2D images, with a focus on facial and chair objects. The authors address the challenging problem of 3D view synthesis by modeling pose manifold traversal through a sequence of small rotations, using a recurrent structure to capture long-term dependencies. The key innovation lies in the weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features without explicit class labels, alongside the ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories with user-controlled step-by-step transformations.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the technical contributions. The authors provide a thorough comparison with related work in image generation, 3D invariant recognition, and disentangling latent factors, demonstrating how their approach differs from previous methods. The model architecture is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the identity units, pose units, and action units that enable the recurrent processing of rotation sequences. The curriculum learning approach for training is appropriately justified and implemented.

The strengths of this work include the novel recurrent architecture that effectively handles long-term dependencies in rotation sequences, the demonstration of weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features, and the successful application to two distinct datasets (Multi-PIE for faces and a CAD chair dataset). The paper also provides valuable insights into how the curriculum training process enhances both image synthesis quality and the discriminative power of the learned representations for cross-view recognition.

However, the paper's quality is somewhat compromised by insufficient experimental evaluation. While the authors present qualitative results (Figures 3-6, 8-9), they lack comprehensive quantitative analysis. The authors compare against a KNN baseline for the chair dataset (Figure 7), but this is not a strong comparison to state-of-the-art methods for 3D view synthesis. A more rigorous quantitative evaluation should include metrics such as FID, LPIPS, or PSNR compared against relevant baselines from recent literature. The paper also fails to demonstrate how the model performs on more diverse object categories beyond faces and chairs, which would strengthen the claim of generalizability.

The significance of this work would be substantially enhanced by addressing these evaluation gaps. The paper currently lacks a clear demonstration of how the proposed method advances the state of the art in 3D view synthesis, particularly when compared to geometry-based approaches (mentioned in line 51). It would be valuable to include quantitative comparisons with recent methods such as Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) or other deep learning approaches for novel view synthesis. Additionally, the paper could better justify the necessity of the recurrent structure by comparing against non-recurrent variants on the same tasks.

The paper demonstrates originality in its approach to weakly-supervised disentangling through the recurrence structure, which is a novel contribution to the literature on latent factor disentanglement. The disentangling emerges naturally from the training objective rather than through explicit regularization, which is an interesting aspect of the work. The paper also makes a relevant contribution to the literature on pose manifold traversal, drawing inspiration from Shepard and Metzler's mental rotation experiments.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written with clear explanations of the model components. The figures are mostly well-designed, though some visualizations (particularly Figure 3) would benefit from being scaled to a more appropriate size for readers to assess the quality of the synthesized views. There are a few minor grammatical issues (e.g., "rotate" should be "rotated" on line 124), but these do not significantly impede understanding.

The paper's scope is appropriate for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in computer vision with a novel deep learning approach. The authors have properly referenced relevant work from NIPS and other conferences, demonstrating awareness of the field's recent developments.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding comprehensive quantitative metrics (e.g., PSNR, LPIPS, FID) for both datasets
2. Including comparisons against state-of-the-art view synthesis methods
3. Demonstrating the model on additional object categories beyond faces and chairs
4. Providing analysis of the model's performance on non-static axis rotations
5. Clarifying how the method advances beyond geometry-based approaches in terms of both quality and applicability

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis, but its current evaluation does not sufficiently demonstrate its significance relative to existing approaches. With the recommended improvements, this work would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. As it stands, the lack of comprehensive quantitative evaluation and limited scope of experiments prevent the paper from fully demonstrating its potential impact in the field.
