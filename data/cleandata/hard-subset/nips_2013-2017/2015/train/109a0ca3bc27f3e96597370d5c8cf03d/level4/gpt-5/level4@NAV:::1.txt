This paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that, given a single 2D image, can render an object from multiple pivoting viewpoints. The authors assess the model by generating images of faces and chairs under rotated views. They further run experiments to study the benefits of curriculum learning, evaluate disentangled representations via cross-view object recognition, and examine class interpolation on chairs.

Although the model is clearly described and well detailed, the performance evaluation is insufficient. The experiments used to showcase the proposed RNN are primarily qualitative (and the result figures should be scaled to a size that is easily readable). Adding quantitative evaluation with appropriate error metrics, along with comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, would be more informative than qualitative results alone.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and easy to follow, with a few minor grammatical issues (e.g., line 124: “rotate” -> “rotated”).

The paper’s significance is largely limited by the lack of comprehensive experimentation and analysis. Including additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs would strengthen the claims. Demonstrating rotated-view rendering for more object categories would make the architecture feel less ad hoc. It would also be valuable to test how the model generalizes to rotation trajectories that deviate from a fixed axis (non-repeating rotations). The paper presents a deep learning approach to rendering novel viewpoints from a single image; while the idea is novel and interesting, the work lacks sufficient experiments and analysis to establish its significance and potential competitiveness with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51).
