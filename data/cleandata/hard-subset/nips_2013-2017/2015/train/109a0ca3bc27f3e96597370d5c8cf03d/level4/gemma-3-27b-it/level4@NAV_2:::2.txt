This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, specifically human faces and chair models. The core idea is to disentangle an input image into identity and pose units within an encoder, then utilize action units to control pose transformations. A decoder network reconstructs the rotated image from these disentangled representations, employing a recurrent architecture to handle extended rotation sequences and maintain long-term dependencies. The network is trained end-to-end, and the authors demonstrate that the learned identity units exhibit discriminative, view-invariant properties.

The paper addresses the challenging problem of 3D view synthesis from a single image, a long-standing problem in both graphics and vision. It builds upon recent advances in convolutional networks for image generation [8, 25] and incorporates ideas from recurrent neural networks [19, 27] to model temporal dependencies in the rotation process. The use of action units to control pose transformations is inspired by human mental rotation experiments [22]. The approach differs from traditional geometry-based methods [5] by avoiding explicit 3D model recovery and instead learning a mapping between image space and a non-linear manifold. Related work like transforming autoencoders [12] and the Inverse Graphics Network [17] are appropriately discussed and differentiated.

The strengths of this work lie in its novel architecture, the successful application of recurrent networks to this problem, and the emergent disentangling of latent factors without explicit supervision. The qualitative results on both faces and chairs are compelling, demonstrating the ability to generate realistic rotations. Quantitative evaluation through cross-view object recognition further supports the claim of learning view-invariant identity features. The curriculum learning approach [4] is also well-justified and appears to be effective. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and technical details of the proposed method.

However, there are some weaknesses. The reliance on discrete rotation angles, dictated by the action units, is a limitation. While the authors provide strong results, exploring continuous rotation control would be a valuable extension. The fixed-length sequence training, while explained, is somewhat counterintuitive for a recurrent network, and the rationale could be further clarified. Although the results are good, a more comprehensive comparison to existing single-image 3D reconstruction or view synthesis methods (beyond the KNN baseline) would strengthen the evaluation. Additionally, while disentanglement is demonstrated, further analysis of the learned representations—perhaps through visualization or ablation studies—could provide deeper insights. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to 3D view synthesis.
*   Strong experimental results on both faces and chairs.
*   Emergent disentangling of latent factors is a significant contribution.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited to discrete rotation angles.
*   Fixed-length sequence training for a recurrent network is unconventional.
*   Limited comparison to alternative approaches.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I encourage the authors to address the reviewer's comments in the rebuttal period to further strengthen the paper.
