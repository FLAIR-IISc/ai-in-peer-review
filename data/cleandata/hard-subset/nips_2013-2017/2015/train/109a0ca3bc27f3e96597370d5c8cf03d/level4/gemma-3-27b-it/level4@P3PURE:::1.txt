In this paper, the authors design a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network capable of rendering an object from different pivoting viewpoints given a single 2D image. The model’s performance is evaluated by generating images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints. Additional experiments examine the benefits of curriculum learning, assess the quality of learned representations through cross-view object recognition, and explore the model’s ability to perform class interpolation with chairs.

Although the proposed model is well presented and detailed, its evaluation lacks sufficient rigor. The experiments used to demonstrate the effectiveness of the RNN model are largely qualitative; figures visualizing the results should be scaled appropriately to allow for clear assessment. Introducing a quantitative measure of performance, utilizing a relevant error metric to facilitate comparison with state-of-the-art results, would provide more insightful conclusions than qualitative assessments alone. 

The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. There are a few minor grammatical errors (e.g., line 124, “rotate” should be “rotated”).

The significance of this work is currently limited by a lack of extensive experimentation and analysis. Including experiments with datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs would be beneficial. Demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for a wider variety of objects would strengthen the argument for a generalizable, rather than ad hoc, network architecture. Furthermore, investigating the model’s ability to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis (i.e., non-repeating rotations) would be valuable. The paper develops a deep learning model for rendering objects from varying rotational viewpoints from a single 2D image. While the approach is novel and interesting, the paper lacks sufficient experimentation and analysis to fully establish the model’s significance or to demonstrate results comparable to those achieved by state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51).
