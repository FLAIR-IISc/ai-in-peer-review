Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of an object from a single 2D image. The model encodes an input image into two latent groups: identity units (view-invariant content) and pose units (view-dependent state). Action units, provided at each time step, drive small rotations, and a recurrent structure composes these into longer trajectories on the pose manifold. The network is trained end-to-end with curriculum learning, gradually increasing the sequence length to improve long-term predictions and encourage disentanglement. Experiments are presented on faces (Multi-PIE) and 3D chair renderings, along with cross-view recognition using identity features and class interpolation in the latent space.

The approach is related to transforming autoencoders, time-series models for image transformations (e.g., PGP), and recent inverse graphics and generative CNN work on view synthesis. The key differences highlighted here are the action-conditioned recurrent architecture for multi-step transformations, a convolutional encoder to generalize to unseen images (not only decode), and a weakly supervised disentangling that emerges from the training objective rather than explicit regularization.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation and a well-motivated architecture: action-conditioned recurrent updates to traverse a pose manifold, with identity and pose latent factors.
- Curriculum learning is used thoughtfully to stabilize training for long sequences and appears to improve both rendering quality and view-invariant recognition.
- Disentangled identity features demonstrate competitive cross-view recognition without class labels, indicating useful invariances learned by the model.
- Class interpolation results suggest a structured latent space and provide an intuitive sanity check of the representation.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Evaluation is predominantly qualitative. Figures should be scaled so that details are visible, but more importantly, standard quantitative metrics (e.g., MSE/PSNR/SSIM) and comparisons to strong baselines are limited. Quantitative comparisons to state-of-the-art methods would be more informative than qualitative assessments alone.
- Dataset scope is narrow (faces and chairs). Additional categories would strengthen claims of generality and reduce the impression that the architecture is tailored to these two domains.
- The experimental setup focuses on rotations around a fixed axis and regular step sizes. It would be valuable to test the model on trajectories that deviate from a static axis (e.g., changes in elevation, non-uniform or non-repeating rotations) to better assess robustness and applicability.
- The paper asserts advantages over “geometry-based approaches,” but the empirical evaluation does not provide head-to-head quantitative comparisons. For faces, a more thorough quantitative comparison with 3D morphable models would be informative; for chairs, comparisons to contemporary generative/inverse-graphics methods would clarify the performance gap and complement the KNN baseline.
- Minor issues: a few grammatical errors (e.g., line 124 “rotate” → “rotated”) and figures that are too small to assess.

Quality:
The model and training procedure are clearly described and technically sound. However, the experimental validation is not commensurate with the claims. The paper would benefit from:
- Quantitative evaluations across multiple sequence lengths and rotation magnitudes, including long-term rollouts, with confidence intervals.
- Stronger baselines: geometry-based methods for faces, and deep generative/inverse-graphics baselines for chairs, with identical train/test splits.
- Analysis of failure cases and sensitivity to factors such as step size, action noise, and sequence length.

Clarity:
Overall well written and easy to follow. The architectural choices and training scheme are explained clearly. Please fix minor grammatical issues (e.g., line 124) and increase figure sizes to make visual comparisons meaningful.

Originality:
The action-conditioned recurrent encoder–decoder for multi-step 3D view synthesis is a novel and interesting combination, distinct from single-shot generative decoders and prior capsule/gating approaches. The weakly supervised disentanglement arising from curriculum training is also noteworthy.

Significance:
The idea is promising, and the qualitative results are compelling, but the impact is limited by the scope and depth of the evaluation. Demonstrating strong, quantitative gains over state-of-the-art methods and broader applicability across categories and rotation patterns would substantially increase significance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add quantitative metrics for view synthesis (e.g., MSE/PSNR/SSIM) and, where appropriate, recognition-based metrics using generated views. Compare against geometry-based baselines (faces) and contemporary deep generative/inverse graphics models (chairs) on the same splits.
- Expand to additional object categories and more diverse camera trajectories (non-uniform steps, varying elevation, non-repeating paths).
- Include an ablation or analysis of the curriculum (e.g., impact of t on both synthesis and recognition), and discuss failure modes.
- Improve figure visibility; include side-by-side comparisons at sufficient resolution.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., line 124).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated recurrent architecture with action conditioning.
- Curriculum training yields improved long-term synthesis and disentanglement.
- Unsupervised identity features perform competitively for cross-view recognition.
- Latent interpolation indicates a meaningful structure in the learned space.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Predominantly qualitative evaluation; limited quantitative comparisons to strong baselines.
- Narrow dataset scope; unclear generalization beyond faces and chairs.
- No experiments on non-static-axis or non-uniform rotation trajectories.
- Limited evidence that performance approaches state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches.

Overall:
A creative and potentially impactful approach, but the current experimental evaluation is insufficient to substantiate the broader claims. With stronger quantitative comparisons, broader datasets, and more diverse rotation trajectories, this work could make a solid contribution.
