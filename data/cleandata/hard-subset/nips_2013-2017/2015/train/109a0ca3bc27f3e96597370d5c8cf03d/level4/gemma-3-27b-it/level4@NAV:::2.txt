This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, exemplified by human faces and chair models. The network begins with a foundational encoder that separates the input image into identity and pose components. Action units are then applied to manipulate the pose components, dictating rotation direction. The decoder, utilizing convolution and upsampling, reconstructs the image and corresponding object mask from the modified identity and pose representations. To facilitate handling extended rotation sequences, the network architecture incorporates recurrence: the identity representation remains fixed while the pose representation evolves through a series of action units, ultimately feeding into the decoder to generate the output image.

A key contribution of this work lies in the successful learning of disentangled representations for identity/appearance and pose. The resulting identity representations demonstrate strong view-invariance, proving effective in cross-view object recognition. Furthermore, this disentanglement enhances predictive rendering, particularly when utilizing longer rotation trajectories during curriculum training of the recurrent network.

The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and effectively motivates each component of the proposed method. Both the qualitative results, demonstrating realistic rendering of rotated images, and the quantitative evaluation on cross-view object recognition provide strong evidence supporting the approach, specifically highlighting the benefits of disentangled pose and identity factors. 

Several minor areas for improvement are identified:
- The current network is limited to discrete rotation angles determined by the training data. It would be valuable to understand the authors' initial thoughts on extending the method to accommodate continuous rotation angles.
- The use of a fixed sequence length during training appears inconsistent with the typical behavior of recurrent neural networks.

Overall, this paper presents a compelling novel approach—a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network trained end-to-end for single-image rotated object rendering—and effectively demonstrates the benefits of generatively disentangling identity and pose factors, as evidenced by both qualitative and quantitative results.
