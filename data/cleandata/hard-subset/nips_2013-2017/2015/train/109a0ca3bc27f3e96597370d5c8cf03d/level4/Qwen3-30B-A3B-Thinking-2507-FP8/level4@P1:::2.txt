

This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D views of objects from single images, specifically addressing the problem of generating rotated views of human faces and 3D chair models. The authors build upon recent advances in convolutional networks for image generation and disentangled representations, extending them with a recurrent structure to handle long-term dependencies in rotation trajectories. Their key insight is that by modeling pose manifold traversal as a sequence of small rotation steps, rather than attempting to predict large rotations directly, the network can better preserve identity information while generating realistic transformations.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a recurrent architecture that captures long-term dependencies in pose transformations; (2) a weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors without using class labels; and (3) curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation trajectory lengths, which improves both image synthesis quality and the discriminative power of identity representations. The authors demonstrate their approach on the Multi-PIE face dataset and a 3D chair model dataset, showing high-quality rotation trajectories and competitive performance in cross-view object recognition.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong connection between the proposed method and the problem it aims to solve. The motivation for using a recurrent structure to handle long-term dependencies in pose manifold traversal is particularly compelling, drawing a natural analogy to human mental rotation experiments. The authors effectively relate their work to related approaches such as the Transforming Autoencoder, Predictive Gating Pyramid, and Inverse Graphics Network, while clearly articulating how their approach differs.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate high-quality image synthesis for both faces and chairs across a wide range of rotation angles, and provide strong quantitative evidence for the disentangled representation learning through cross-view recognition experiments. The curriculum learning approach is particularly well-justified, with results showing that longer training sequences lead to better identity representations and more stable image synthesis. The comparison to KNN baselines on the chair dataset is especially convincing, with the RNN16 model achieving a 42% improvement over the best KNN baseline.

One notable strength of the paper is the demonstration of disentangled representations emerging from the training objective without explicit regularization. The authors show that identity units become increasingly view-invariant with longer training sequences, while pose units capture rotational information. The ablation studies in Figure 6 and Table 1 provide compelling evidence for this disentangling property, with identity units achieving near-perfect recognition on Multi-PIE while pose units perform at nearly random levels.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened. First, the model is limited to discrete rotation angles based on the training data, which restricts its applicability to continuous rotation scenarios. The authors should address how their approach might be extended to handle continuous rotations, perhaps by incorporating interpolation between discrete angles or using a different parameterization of the rotation manifold.

Second, the recurrent structure is trained with fixed-length sequences, which is somewhat atypical for RNNs that usually handle variable-length inputs. While the authors justify this with their curriculum learning approach, it would be helpful to discuss the implications of this design choice and whether it could be adapted to handle variable-length rotation trajectories in future work.

Third, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach. For instance, the model appears to struggle with highly complex chair shapes or large rotation angles beyond 180 degrees, as evidenced by the qualitative results. A more in-depth analysis of these failure cases would provide valuable insights for future work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of 3D view synthesis from single images. The proposed recurrent architecture effectively captures long-term dependencies in pose transformations, and the weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors is a particularly interesting finding. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides both qualitative and quantitative evidence for its claims. While there are some limitations to address, these do not detract significantly from the paper's overall contribution.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
