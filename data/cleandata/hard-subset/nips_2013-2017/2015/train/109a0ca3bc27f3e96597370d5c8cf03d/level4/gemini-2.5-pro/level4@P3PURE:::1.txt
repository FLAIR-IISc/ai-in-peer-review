This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed to render an object from various viewpoints, given only a single 2D input image. The authors evaluate their model's ability to generate images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints. They also conduct experiments to examine the benefits of curriculum learning, evaluate the model's disentangled representations via cross-view object recognition, and explore its capacity for class interpolation with chairs.

While the proposed model is well-presented and detailed, its evaluation is inadequate. The experiments are largely qualitative, relying on visual inspection of the generated images. (It should also be noted that the figures with these visualizations are too small for a clear assessment). To provide a more insightful analysis and enable comparison with state-of-the-art methods, the authors should introduce quantitative performance measures using appropriate error metrics.

The paper's significance is further limited by the narrow scope of the experiments. The model is only tested on faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs; demonstrating its performance on a wider variety of object classes would strengthen the claim that the architecture is not ad-hoc. Furthermore, it would be valuable to see how the model generalizes to more complex, non-repeating rotation trajectories that do not follow a static axis.

On the whole, the paper is well-written and easy to follow, with only a few minor grammatical errors (e.g., on line 124, "rotate" should be "rotated").

In summary, although the deep learning approach for this task is novel and interesting, the paper currently lacks the sufficient experimentation and rigorous analysis needed to demonstrate its full potential and competitiveness with state-of-the-art 'geometry-based approaches' (line 51).
