This paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network capable of rendering an object from novel pivoting viewpoints given a single 2D image. The authors demonstrate the model’s performance on face and chair datasets, rotating these objects to generate views from different angles. Supporting experiments explore the benefits of curriculum learning, assess the quality of learned representations via cross-view object recognition, and investigate class interpolation capabilities specifically for chairs.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents a novel approach. The model architecture is detailed and easy to understand. However, the evaluation feels incomplete and relies heavily on qualitative assessment. While the visualizations are helpful, figures should be scaled appropriately to allow for clearer visual inspection of the results. More importantly, the lack of quantitative evaluation significantly limits the assessment of the method’s effectiveness. Introducing a suitable error metric and comparing performance against state-of-the-art approaches – as alluded to in line 51, concerning geometry-based methods – would provide a more robust and insightful understanding of the proposed model’s strengths and weaknesses. 

The significance of this work is currently limited by the scope of the experimentation. Evaluating the model on additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs would broaden its demonstrated applicability and reduce the impression that the architecture is specifically tailored to these object categories. Demonstrating successful viewpoint rendering across a wider range of object types would enhance the generalizability and perceived robustness of the approach. Furthermore, exploring the model’s behavior with more complex rotation trajectories – specifically those deviating from a fixed axis of rotation (i.e., non-repeating or more dynamic rotations) – would be a valuable extension. This would investigate whether the model captures inherent 3D structure or is simply memorizing viewpoint correlations.

A minor point: there is a grammatical error on line 124 (“rotate” should be “rotated”).

In summary, this paper presents a promising and novel deep learning approach to the challenging problem of 3D viewpoint rendering from a single 2D image. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more comprehensive quantitative evaluation and expanded experimentation. Addressing these points would provide a more compelling demonstration of the model’s capabilities and better establish its position within the existing literature.
