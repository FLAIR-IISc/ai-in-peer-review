The authors present a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network capable of rendering an object from different pivoting viewpoints given a single 2D image. They evaluate the model's performance on generating images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints, as well as its benefits from curriculum learning, disentangled representations through cross-view object recognition, and class interpolation with chairs.

Although the proposed model is well-presented and detailed, its performance evaluation is somewhat limited. The experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed RNN model are largely qualitative, relying on visualizations of results. To provide more insightful comparisons with state-of-the-art results, introducing a quantitative performance measure with an appropriate error metric would be beneficial. Figures with visualizations should also be scaled to an appropriate size for better readability.

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a few minor grammatical errors, such as "rotate" instead of "rotated" on line 124.

However, the significance of this paper is impaired by the lack of extensive experimentation and analysis. To strengthen the paper, it would be relevant to include experiments with additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs, demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for diverse object types. This would make the network architecture seem less ad hoc. Furthermore, exploring the model's ability to generalize to non-repeating rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis would be interesting. While the paper develops a novel deep learning model for rendering an object from different rotational viewpoints given a single 2D image, it lacks sufficient experimentation and relevant analysis to fully establish the model's significance and potential for results comparable to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches.
