This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D views of objects from a single 2D image. The authors evaluate the performance of their model on the Multi-PIE dataset and a database of 3D chair CAD models, demonstrating its ability to predict accurate renderings across trajectories of repeated rotations.

The main contribution of this work is the development of a recurrent neural network architecture that can learn to apply out-of-plane rotations to objects, while also disentangling latent factors of variation without using class labels. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model through qualitative and quantitative experiments, including 3D view synthesis, cross-view object recognition, and class interpolation.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work and the significance of their contributions. However, the evaluation of the model's performance could be improved by including more quantitative measures and comparisons to state-of-the-art results.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model on two different datasets, including faces and chairs. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the model's performance, including its ability to disentangle latent factors of variation and to generalize to new objects and viewpoints.

However, the paper could benefit from more experimentation and analysis to flesh out the model's significance and potential. For example, it would be interesting to see how the proposed model generalizes to handle object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis (non-repeating rotations). Additionally, demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for different types of objects would make the network architecture seem less ad hoc.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on originality, as the proposed model is a novel contribution to the field. The paper also scores well on clarity, as the writing is clear and easy to follow. However, the paper could benefit from more quantitative measures of performance to support its claims of significance and impact.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to synthesizing 3D views of objects from a single 2D image.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on two different datasets, including faces and chairs.

Arguments against acceptance:
