This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to render 3D objects – specifically human faces and chair models – under out-of-plane rotations, starting from a single input image. The network initially employs an encoder to disentangle the input image into identity (appearance) and pose units. An action unit modulates the pose units, controlling the rotation direction. A decoder, composed of convolutional and upsampling layers, then reconstructs the rotated object and its corresponding mask from the combined identity and pose representations. To facilitate longer and more complex rotation trajectories, the authors extend this model with a recurrent architecture. In this recurrent version, the encoded identity unit remains fixed while the pose unit evolves through a sequence of action units; both are then fed into the decoder to generate the output image.

A key contribution of this work is the learning of disentangled representations for identity/appearance and pose. The authors demonstrate that the learned identity units act as discriminative, view-invariant features, performing well in a cross-view object recognition task. This disentanglement also proves beneficial during training, particularly when leveraging a curriculum learning approach with longer rotation trajectories to improve rendering quality. 

The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The motivation behind each component of the proposed method is clearly articulated. The qualitative results, showcasing rendered images of rotated objects, and the quantitative evaluation on cross-view object recognition, provide strong support for the effectiveness of the method, particularly regarding the successful disentanglement of pose and identity factors.

However, several minor weaknesses should be addressed:

*   **Discrete vs. Continuous Rotation:** The current network is limited to discrete rotation angles defined by the training data. Do the authors have any preliminary ideas on how to extend the method to accommodate continuous rotation angles? Exploring interpolation techniques or alternative representation schemes for pose could be beneficial.
*   **Fixed-Length Recurrence:** The recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with a fixed sequence length, which seems counterintuitive given the variable-length nature of typical recurrent neural network applications. The authors should clarify the rationale behind this design choice and discuss potential implications or alternative approaches for handling variable-length rotation sequences. 
*   **Limited Object Categories:** The experiments focus on faces and chairs. While demonstrating the method’s potential, the generalizability to more diverse and complex object categories remains unclear. Investigating performance on a broader range of objects would strengthen the conclusions.

Overall, this paper presents a compelling approach to rendering rotated 3D objects from single images. The generative disentangling of identity and pose, driven by the recurrent rotation prediction objective, is a significant contribution, effectively demonstrated through qualitative and quantitative evaluations. Addressing the points raised above would further enhance the paper’s impact.
