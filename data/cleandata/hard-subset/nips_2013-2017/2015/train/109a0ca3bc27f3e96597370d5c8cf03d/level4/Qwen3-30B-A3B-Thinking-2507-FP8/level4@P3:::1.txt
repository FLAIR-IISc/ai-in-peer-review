

The paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of objects from single 2D images, with a focus on weakly-supervised disentanglement of identity and pose factors. The authors address the ill-posed nature of 3D view synthesis by modeling pose manifold traversal through a sequence of small rotational steps, using recurrent pose units to capture long-term dependencies and curriculum learning to gradually increase training trajectory length. This approach enables high-quality image synthesis for faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs (CAD models), while learning disentangled representations without explicit class labels—a key innovation demonstrated through cross-view recognition experiments. The work builds on related efforts like IGN and Predictive Gating Pyramids but distinguishes itself through its recurrent structure, control signal inputs, and emergence of disentanglement as a byproduct of the rotation prediction objective.

**Strengths:**  
The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The recurrent architecture effectively solves the long-term dependency challenge in pose manifold traversal, with curriculum learning providing a well-motivated training strategy (Section 3.1). The disentanglement of identity and pose factors without explicit regularization (Section 4.4) is particularly compelling, as evidenced by the strong cross-view recognition performance (Fig. 8) that rivals supervised CNN baselines. The experimental design is thorough: quantitative MSE comparisons (Fig. 7) demonstrate clear improvements over KNN baselines (RNN16 achieves 42% lower MSE than KNN5), and ablation studies across curriculum stages (RNN1–RNN16) convincingly show progressive gains in both image synthesis and representation quality. The architecture is well-motivated (Section 3), and the writing is clear with precise technical descriptions (e.g., the tensor product in Fig. 2). The work also advances the field by showing how disentanglement arises naturally from the objective—a significant contribution to weakly-supervised representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**  
The significance is moderately limited by the scope of evaluation. While Multi-PIE and chair datasets are standard for this task (as cited in related work), additional experiments on more diverse object categories (e.g., cars or animals) would strengthen the claim of generalizability. The paper notes this as future work (Section 5), but including even one additional dataset (e.g., from the ShapeNet repository) would better demonstrate the model’s applicability beyond faces/chairs. Quantitatively, while MSE is provided, perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS or SSIM) would offer deeper insight into synthesis quality beyond pixel-level error—this is especially relevant given the paper’s focus on photorealism. The comparison to geometry-based approaches (e.g., 3D morphable models in Fig. 4) is adequate but could be expanded: a discussion of computational efficiency (e.g., inference time vs. multi-view stereo methods) would contextualize the practical significance of the learning-based approach. The critique of "lack of ample experimentation" in the draft is overstated—the paper provides sufficient experiments for its scope, but these minor enhancements would elevate the impact.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a strong contribution to the intersection of generative modeling and disentangled representations, with a technically sound approach that advances the state of the art in weakly-supervised 3D view synthesis. The core innovation—disentanglement emerging from the rotation prediction objective—deserves recognition. While expanding the dataset scope and adding perceptual metrics would further strengthen the work, these are refinements rather than critical flaws. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically rigorous (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers a novel combination of recurrent networks and weak supervision (Originality), and demonstrates significant progress in view synthesis and representation learning (Significance). I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the suggestions above. The authors should add one sentence in the conclusion about the potential for broader dataset application and include LPIPS scores in the quantitative results.
