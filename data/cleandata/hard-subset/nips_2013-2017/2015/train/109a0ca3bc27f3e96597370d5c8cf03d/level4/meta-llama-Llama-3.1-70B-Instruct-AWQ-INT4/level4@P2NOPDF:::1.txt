The authors present a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed to render an object from different viewpoints given a single 2D image. The proposed model is evaluated on its ability to generate images of faces and chairs from rotated viewpoints. Additional experiments investigate the benefits of curriculum learning, disentangled representations through cross-view object recognition, and class interpolation with chairs.

Although the model is well-presented and detailed, its performance evaluation is somewhat limited. The experiments primarily rely on qualitative assessments, with visualizations of results that could be scaled up for better readability. Introducing quantitative performance measures with suitable error metrics would provide more insight and allow for comparisons with state-of-the-art results.

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a few minor grammatical errors, such as the use of "rotate" instead of "rotated" on line 124. However, the significance of this paper is compromised by the lack of extensive experimentation and analysis. It would be beneficial to include experiments with additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs, demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for various object types. This would make the network architecture seem less ad hoc and more robust.

Furthermore, exploring the model's ability to generalize to non-repeating rotations or object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis would be valuable. The paper proposes a deep learning model for rendering an object from different rotational viewpoints given a single 2D image. While the approach is novel and interesting, the paper lacks sufficient experimentation and relevant analysis to fully establish the model's significance and potential for achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches.
