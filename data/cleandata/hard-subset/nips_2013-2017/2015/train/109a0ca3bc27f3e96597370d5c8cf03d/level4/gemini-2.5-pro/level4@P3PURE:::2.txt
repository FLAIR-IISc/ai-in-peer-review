This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that learns to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, such as human faces and chair models. The architecture begins with a basic encoder-decoder model. The encoder disentangles an input image into separate identity and pose representations. An action unit, which controls the direction of rotation, is then applied to the pose representation. A decoder network, consisting of convolution and upsampling layers, subsequently combines the identity and modified pose representations to generate an image of the rotated object and a corresponding segmentation mask. To support longer rotation trajectories, this model is extended into a recurrent architecture. In this setup, the identity representation from the input image is held constant while the pose representation is iteratively updated by a sequence of action units. The decoder then uses the fixed identity and the sequence of updated pose representations to generate the final rendered image.

A key contribution of this work is the learned disentanglement of representations for identity and pose. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this disentanglement by showing that the learned identity features are discriminative and view-invariant, achieving strong performance on a cross-view object recognition task. Furthermore, this disentanglement property improves rendering quality, particularly when using longer rotation trajectories within the curriculum learning framework employed for training.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivations for each component of the proposed method. The qualitative results of the rendered rotated images are compelling, and the quantitative evaluation on the cross-view object recognition task provides strong support for the method. These results effectively highlight the success of disentangling the pose and identity factors.

A few minor weaknesses are listed below, which I hope the authors can address in the rebuttal period:
- The network can only generate outputs at discrete rotation angles, dictated by the training data. Could the authors comment on potential extensions of their method to support continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent network is trained with fixed-length sequences. While practical, this is somewhat at odds with the general capability of recurrent neural networks to handle variable-length sequences. Could the authors elaborate on this design choice?

In summary, this paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that is trained end-to-end on the task of rendering rotated objects from a single image. The main contribution—generatively disentangling identity and pose factors as an emergent property of the rotation prediction objective—is well-supported by both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
