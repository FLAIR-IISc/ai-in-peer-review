

This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, including human faces and 3D chair models. The core architecture begins with a non-recurrent model: its encoder disentangles the input image into identity units (representing appearance) and pose units (representing rotation state). Action units are then applied to the pose units to control rotation direction, and the decoder—comprising convolutional and upsampling layers—reconstructs the rotated object image and its corresponding mask from the combined identity and pose representations. To support longer rotation trajectories, the model is extended to a recurrent architecture where the identity unit remains fixed across the sequence, while the pose unit is iteratively updated via a sequence of action units. The final output is generated by feeding both the fixed identity and evolving pose units into the decoder.

A primary contribution is the successful disentanglement of identity/appearance and pose factors, demonstrated by the identity units serving as discriminative, view-invariant features in cross-view object recognition. This disentanglement property further enables more robust and accurate rendering during curriculum training with extended rotation trajectories.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides a compelling motivation for each component. Qualitative results for rotated image rendering and quantitative evaluations on cross-view recognition offer strong empirical support for the method, particularly validating the disentangled representations of pose and identity.

Minor weaknesses require clarification in the rebuttal:
- The model currently supports only discrete rotation angles present in the training set. How might the authors extend the method to handle continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent network is trained on fixed-length sequences, contradicting the fundamental design principle of recurrent networks for variable-length inputs.

The main contribution lies in demonstrating how generative disentanglement of identity and pose factors emerges from the recurrent rotation prediction objective, effectively validated through both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
