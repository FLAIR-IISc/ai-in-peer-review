This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture for synthesizing novel views of an object under varying rotations, conditioned on a single input image. The model's capabilities are demonstrated by generating rotated images of faces and chairs. The authors also conduct further experiments to investigate the utility of curriculum learning, assess the quality of its disentangled representations via a cross-view object recognition task, and probe its capacity for class interpolation between different chairs.

While the proposed model is presented with clarity and detail, the experimental evaluation is insufficient. The validation of the proposed RNN relies heavily on qualitative results, and the visual examples provided in figures should be enlarged for clearer inspection. The analysis would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of quantitative metrics, which would enable a direct comparison with state-of-the-art methods and provide a more objective measure of performance than visual assessment alone.

Overall, the manuscript is well-written and generally easy to comprehend. A few minor typographical errors were noted (e.g., on line 124, "rotate" should be "rotated").

The primary weakness of this work, which impairs its potential impact, is the limited scope of its experimental validation and analysis. To bolster the paper's claims, it is crucial to include experiments on datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs. Showing that the model can successfully render novel views for a more diverse set of object categories would help demonstrate that the proposed architecture is not merely ad hoc. Furthermore, it would be insightful to evaluate how the model generalizes to more complex rotation trajectories that are not confined to a static axis. In summary, while the paper introduces a novel and interesting deep learning framework for single-image novel view synthesis, its contributions are not fully substantiated due to a lack of sufficient experimentation. This makes it difficult to assess the model's significance and its performance relative to established "geometry-based approaches" (line 51).
