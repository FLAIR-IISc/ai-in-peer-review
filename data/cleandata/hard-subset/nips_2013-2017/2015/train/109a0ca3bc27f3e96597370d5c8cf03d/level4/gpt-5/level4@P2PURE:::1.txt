This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that renders novel views of an object from a single 2D image by pivoting around the object. The authors evaluate the model on generating rotated views of faces and chairs, and include additional studies on the benefits of curriculum learning, on disentanglement via cross-view object recognition, and on class interpolation for chairs.

While the model is clearly described and technically detailed, the evaluation is insufficient. The experiments presented to demonstrate the effectiveness of the RNN are largely qualitative; figures should also be scaled appropriately so the visualizations are legible. Introducing quantitative performance metrics, with suitable error measures that enable comparison to state-of-the-art results, would be more informative than qualitative assessments alone.

In general, the paper is well written and easy to follow, with a few minor grammatical issues (e.g., line 124: “rotate” -> “rotated”).

The paper’s significance is limited by the scarcity of experiments and analysis. It would be valuable to include additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs. Demonstrating robust performance across a wider variety of object categories would make the architecture seem less ad hoc. It would also be interesting to evaluate how the model generalizes to object rotation trajectories that deviate from a fixed axis (non-repeating rotations). Overall, the paper develops a deep learning approach to rendering rotated viewpoints from a single image that is novel and interesting, but it lacks sufficient experimentation and analysis to substantiate its significance or its potential to achieve results comparable to state-of-the-art “geometry-based approaches” (line 51).
