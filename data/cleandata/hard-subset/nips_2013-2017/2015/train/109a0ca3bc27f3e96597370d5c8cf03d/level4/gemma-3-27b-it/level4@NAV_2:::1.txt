This paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed to render objects from novel viewpoints given a single 2D input image. The core idea is to learn a pose manifold traversal, enabling the generation of rotated views, and the authors demonstrate this on faces (Multi-PIE dataset) and chairs. They further investigate curriculum learning, disentangled representation learning through cross-view recognition, and class interpolation for chairs.

The approach is well-motivated, clearly presented, and technically sound. The recurrent architecture is a reasonable choice for capturing long-term dependencies inherent in view synthesis, and the curriculum learning strategy appears effective. The results on both faces and chairs demonstrate the model’s ability to generate plausible rotated views, and the disentangled representation learning is a notable, albeit weakly-supervised, achievement. The inclusion of related work is thorough, referencing relevant papers on view synthesis, disentangling representations, and recurrent neural networks.

However, while the qualitative results are promising, the quantitative evaluation is limited. The paper relies heavily on visual inspection, and the inclusion of standard quantitative metrics (e.g., PSNR, SSIM, or LPIPS) would strengthen the claims. Although a KNN baseline is provided for chair rotation, a comparison to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches, as mentioned in the introduction (line 51), is missing. A more rigorous comparison would contextualize the contributions more effectively. Additionally, the experiments are currently limited to rotation around a single axis. Exploring more complex transformations or non-cyclic rotations could reveal potential limitations and future research directions. While the grammatical writing is clear, there are a few minor editing errors (e.g., line 124 "rotate" should be "rotated").

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to view synthesis using a recurrent convolutional architecture.
*   Demonstrated ability to generate plausible rotated views of faces and chairs.
*   Weakly-supervised disentangling of latent factors (identity and pose).
*   Thorough related work section.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited quantitative evaluation relying heavily on visual inspection.
*   Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art geometry-based methods.
*   Experiments restricted to single-axis rotation.
*   Relatively narrow scope of datasets (Multi-PIE and chairs).
