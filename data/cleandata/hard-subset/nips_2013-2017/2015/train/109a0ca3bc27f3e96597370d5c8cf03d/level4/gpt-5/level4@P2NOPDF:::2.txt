This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that learns to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects such as human faces and chairs. The base model uses an encoder to disentangle an input image into identity units and pose units. Action units are applied to the pose units to control the rotation direction. A decoder composed of convolution and upsampling layers then reconstructs the rotated image and its object mask from the identity and pose representations. To support longer rotation trajectories, the model is extended with a recurrent architecture: the identity code from the input is held fixed, while the pose code is updated by a sequence of action units, and the decoder generates the rotated result at each step.

A key contribution is the learned disentanglement of identity/appearance and pose factors. The identity units are shown to be view-invariant and discriminative for cross-view object recognition. Moreover, this disentanglement improves rendering quality, particularly for longer rotation sequences trained via curriculum learning in the recurrent setup.

The paper is well written and easy to follow, with clear motivation for each component of the method. Both the qualitative renderings of rotated images and the quantitative results on cross-view recognition strongly support the approach, especially the effectiveness of the disentangled identity and pose representations.

Minor weaknesses and questions:
- The network currently handles only discrete rotation angles determined by the training set. Do the authors have plans or preliminary ideas for extending the method to support continuous rotations?
- The recurrent encoder–decoder is trained with fixed-length sequences, which is somewhat at odds with the typical flexibility of RNNs. Can the authors discuss the implications and potential strategies to allow variable-length training and inference?

In summary, this work introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder trained end-to-end to render rotated objects from a single image. The emergence of disentangled identity and pose factors driven by the recurrent rotation prediction objective is convincingly demonstrated by both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
