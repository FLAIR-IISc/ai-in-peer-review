This paper introduces a new recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed to learn the application of out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects, exemplified by human faces and chair models. The network begins with a foundational encoder that separates the input image into identity and pose components. Rotation is then controlled by applying action units to the pose components, with a decoder – comprising convolutions and upsampling – reconstructing the rotated object and its corresponding mask from both identity and pose information. To facilitate longer, more complex rotation sequences, the architecture is extended with a recurrent structure; the identity component remains fixed while the pose component evolves through a series of action units, ultimately feeding into the decoder for image generation.

A key contribution of this work is the learning of disentangled representations for identity/appearance and pose. The resulting identity units are demonstrated to function as discriminative, view-invariant features in cross-view object recognition. Furthermore, this disentanglement proves beneficial for improved rendering performance, particularly when utilizing longer rotation trajectories within the curriculum learning framework employed for training the recurrent network.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow and transparent motivation for each component of the proposed method. Both qualitative results, showcasing rendered rotated images, and quantitative evaluations on cross-view object recognition provide strong support for the approach, particularly highlighting the effectiveness of the disentangled pose and identity representations. 

A few minor points could be addressed during the rebuttal period:
- The current network is limited to discrete rotation angles dictated by the training data. Do the authors have preliminary thoughts on extending the method to handle continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with a fixed sequence length, which contrasts with the typical behavior of recurrent neural networks.

In summary, this paper presents a novel end-to-end trained recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for single-image based rotated object rendering. The core contribution of generatively disentangling identity and pose factors, driven by the recurrent rotation prediction objective, is convincingly demonstrated through both qualitative and quantitative results.
