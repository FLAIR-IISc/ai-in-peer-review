This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects such as human faces and 3D chair models. The authors' approach is closely related to previous work on 3D view synthesis and image generation, but it introduces several key innovations, including the use of a recurrent neural network architecture to model long-term dependencies in rotation sequences and the ability to disentangle identity and pose factors without explicit regularization.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the motivation behind each component of the proposed method. The qualitative results for predicted renderings of rotated images and quantitative evaluations on cross-view object recognition tasks provide strong support for the method. In particular, the disentangled representations for pose and identity factors are a significant contribution, as they enable the model to generate realistic rotation trajectories and achieve competitive performance on cross-view object recognition tasks.

The authors' use of curriculum training to gradually increase the difficulty of the training data is also a notable aspect of the paper. By starting with short rotation sequences and progressively increasing the length, the authors are able to train a model that can handle longer-term dependencies and generate more realistic renderings.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the proposed network can only support discrete rotation angles, depending on the set of rotation angles shown in the training data. It is unclear how the authors plan to extend the proposed method to support continuous rotation angles, which would be a more general and useful capability.

Another minor weakness is that the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which is actually contradictory to the general definition of recurrent neural networks. However, the authors' use of curriculum training helps to mitigate this issue by allowing the model to learn to handle longer-term dependencies.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis and image generation, and the authors' use of a recurrent neural network architecture and disentangled representations for pose and identity factors is a notable innovation.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to 3D view synthesis and image generation.
* The use of a recurrent neural network architecture and disentangled representations for pose and identity factors is a significant contribution to the field.
* The qualitative and quantitative results provide strong support for the method.
* The authors' use of curriculum training is a notable aspect of the paper.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The proposed network can only support discrete rotation angles, which may limit its generality and usefulness.
* The proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which is actually contradictory to the general definition of recurrent neural networks.
