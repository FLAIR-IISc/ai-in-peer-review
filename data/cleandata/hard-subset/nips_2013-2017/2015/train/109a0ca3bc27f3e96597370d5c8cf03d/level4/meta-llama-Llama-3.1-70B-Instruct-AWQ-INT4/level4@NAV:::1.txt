This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network capable of generating an object's appearance from diverse viewpoints given a single 2D image. The authors assess their model's performance by rendering faces and chairs from rotated perspectives, and conduct supplementary experiments to investigate the benefits of curriculum learning, evaluate disentangled representations via cross-view object recognition, and explore class interpolation with chairs.

While the proposed model is thoroughly presented, its performance evaluation is somewhat limited. The experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of the RNN model are predominantly qualitative, with visualizations that could be scaled up for better readability. Incorporating quantitative performance metrics to compare with state-of-the-art results would provide more valuable insights than qualitative assessments alone.

Overall, the paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a few minor grammatical errors, such as "rotate" instead of "rotated" on line 124.

However, the paper's significance is hindered by inadequate experimentation and analysis. It would be beneficial to include experiments with additional datasets beyond Multi-PIE and Chairs, demonstrating the model's ability to render rotated viewpoints for diverse object types. This would make the network architecture less ad hoc. Furthermore, exploring the model's generalization to handle non-repeating, non-static axis object rotation trajectories would be intriguing. Although the approach is novel and interesting, the paper lacks sufficient experimentation and relevant analysis to substantiate the model's significance and potential for comparable results with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (line 51).
