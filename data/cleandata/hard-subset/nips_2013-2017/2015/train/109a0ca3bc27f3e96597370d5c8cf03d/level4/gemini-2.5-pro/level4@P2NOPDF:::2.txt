This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for rendering 3D objects, such as human faces and chairs, with out-of-plane rotations from a single input image. The core of the proposed method is an encoder-decoder architecture that learns to disentangle the input image into two distinct representations: an 'identity' vector capturing the object's intrinsic appearance, and a 'pose' vector representing its orientation. Rotations are controlled by applying discrete 'action units' that modify the pose vector. The decoder, composed of convolutional and upsampling layers, then synthesizes a new image of the object in its target pose, along with a corresponding segmentation mask, by combining the identity vector with the modified pose vector.

To generate longer and more complex rotation sequences, this base model is extended into a recurrent architecture. In this configuration, the identity vector from the initial input is held constant while the pose vector is iteratively updated by a sequence of action units. The decoder renders an output at each step, conditioned on the fixed identity and the evolving pose.

**Strengths and Contributions:**

A key contribution of this work is the successful disentanglement of identity and pose factors within a generative framework. The model learns these separate representations as an emergent property of the view-synthesis objective, without requiring direct supervision on identity or pose labels. The effectiveness of this disentanglement is convincingly demonstrated through quantitative experiments. The learned identity features prove to be highly discriminative and view-invariant, achieving strong performance on a cross-view object recognition task. This result provides compelling evidence that the encoder has successfully isolated the core appearance of the object from its spatial orientation.

The qualitative results are also impressive, showcasing the model's ability to generate realistic and coherent rotated views of objects. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation provided for each component of the proposed method. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that this factorization is crucial for the success of the recurrent model, particularly when using curriculum learning with longer rotation trajectories to improve rendering quality.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper is strong, but there are a few minor weaknesses that the authors could potentially address:

-   The current model is limited to discrete rotation angles, determined by the actions available in the training data. An interesting direction for future work would be to extend the framework to support continuous rotation angles, perhaps by interpolating in the pose space or conditioning on a continuous rotation vector. It would be valuable if the authors could comment on the feasibility of such an extension.

-   The recurrent network is trained using fixed-length action sequences. While this approach is effective, it does not fully leverage the flexibility of RNNs, which can typically handle variable-length sequences. Could the authors elaborate on the reasons for this design choice and whether training with variable-length trajectories was explored?

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a novel and effective method for synthesizing rotated views of an object from a single image. The primary contribution—learning a generative model that disentangles identity and pose—is well-supported by both strong quantitative performance on a downstream recognition task and impressive qualitative rendering results. The work represents a significant step towards building models that can understand and manipulate 3D structure from 2D images.
