Summary
The paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that learns to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects (e.g., human faces and chairs) from a single input image. The encoder decomposes the image into identity units and pose units. Action units then modify the pose representation to control rotation direction and magnitude, while the decoder—composed of convolution and upsampling layers—reconstructs the rotated image and its corresponding object mask. To support longer rotation trajectories, the model is extended with a recurrent architecture in which the identity representation is held fixed and the pose is updated step-by-step by a sequence of action inputs; the decoder uses the current pose and fixed identity to synthesize each output. Training uses a curriculum over trajectory length to stabilize learning and encourage disentanglement.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that combines disentangled representations with recurrent transformation control.
- The identity/pose decomposition is compelling: identity units are shown to be discriminative and view-invariant in cross-view recognition, while pose units capture rotation.
- The action-unit mechanism provides an intuitive and effective interface for controlling transformations.
- The recurrent extension enables longer rotation sequences by keeping identity fixed and updating pose incrementally.
- Both qualitative results (rendered rotated images) and quantitative evaluations (cross-view object recognition) substantiate the claims, especially regarding disentanglement.
- The paper is well written and easy to follow, with clear motivation for each component and training strategy (including curriculum learning).

Weaknesses and Questions
- Discrete rotations: The current model appears limited to the set of discrete rotation angles observed during training. Can the authors discuss how to extend the approach to continuous rotations? For example, could action units be parameterized as continuous variables, or could the pose be modeled on a continuous manifold so that arbitrary angles can be interpolated or extrapolated at test time?
- Fixed sequence length: The recurrent model is trained with fixed-length trajectories, which is at odds with typical RNN usage. How well does the model generalize to variable-length sequences at test time? Can the training be adjusted (e.g., variable-length curriculum, randomized unroll lengths, or scheduled sampling) to reduce potential mismatch between training and inference?

Suggestions
- Provide more detail on how disentanglement is encouraged beyond the task objective and curriculum (e.g., architectural constraints, loss terms, or regularization). An ablation showing the effect of curriculum length and any disentanglement-specific design choices would strengthen the causal link to the observed invariance.
- Include analysis of interpolation in the identity space and in the pose space to further validate the factorization (e.g., identity interpolation at fixed pose and vice versa).
- If feasible, demonstrate continuous control over rotation by varying action inputs smoothly, even when trained on discrete angles, to indicate the model’s potential for continuous transformations.
- Discuss failure cases, such as extreme rotations or occlusions, and how identity preservation behaves under long sequences.

Overall Assessment
This paper introduces a novel and well-executed recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder framework for learning to rotate objects from a single image while disentangling identity and pose. The design is elegant, the empirical evidence is convincing, and the work addresses a meaningful problem with practical implications for view synthesis and cross-view recognition. The main limitations—discrete angle support and fixed-length training—seem addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
