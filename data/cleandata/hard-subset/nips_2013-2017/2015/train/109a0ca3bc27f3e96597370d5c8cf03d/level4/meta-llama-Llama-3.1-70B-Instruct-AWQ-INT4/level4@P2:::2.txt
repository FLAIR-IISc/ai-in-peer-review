This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects such as human faces and 3D chair models. The proposed network starts from a basic model, where its encoder network disentangles the input image into identity units and pose units, then with the action units applied on pose units to control the rotation direction, its decoder network which consists of convolution and upsampling decodes the identity and pose into an image of rotated object and the corresponding object mask. To support longer rotation trajectories, the proposed network is then extended to have the recurrent architecture where the encoded identity unit of input image is fixed and the pose unit is changed by a sequence of action units, and finally both identity and pose units are fed into decoder to generate the result image.

The proposed method is closely related to several previous works, including transforming autoencoders, Predictive Gating Pyramid (PGP), and Inverse Graphics Network (IGN). However, the proposed method differs from these works in several key aspects. For example, the proposed method uses a convolutional network architecture instead of capsules, and incorporates action inputs and recurrent structure to handle repeated rotation steps. Additionally, the proposed method uses a deterministic feed-forward training rather than the variational auto-encoder (VAE) framework.

One of the main contributions of this paper is learning to disentangle the representations for identity/appearance and pose factors, where the identity units are shown to be discriminative view-invariant features in the cross-view object recognition task. This disentangling property is beneficial for predicting better rendering while using longer rotation trajectories in the curriculum training stages for training the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network.

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the motivation for different parts of the proposed method is clearly described. The qualitative results for predicted rendering of rotated images and quantitative evaluation on cross-view object recognition task provide good support for the method, especially the disentangled representations for pose and identity factors.

However, there are some minor weaknesses in the paper. For example, the proposed network can only support discrete rotation angles, depending on the set of rotation angles shown in the training data. It would be beneficial to extend the proposed method to support continuous rotation angles. Additionally, the proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length, which is actually contradictory to general recurrent neural networks. It would be interesting to explore the possibility of training the network with variable-length sequences.

Overall, this paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that is trained end-to-end on the task of rendering rotated objects starting from a single image. The main contribution of generative disentangling the identity and pose factors which emerged from the recurrent rotation prediction objective is well demonstrated by the qualitative and quantitative evaluations.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.
* The qualitative results for predicted rendering of rotated images and quantitative evaluation on cross-view object recognition task provide good support for the method.
* The disentangled representations for pose and identity factors are beneficial for predicting better rendering while using longer rotation trajectories in the curriculum training stages.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The proposed network can only support discrete rotation angles, depending on the set of rotation angles shown in the training data.
* The proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is trained with fixed-length, which is actually contradictory to general recurrent neural networks.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the minor weaknesses mentioned above, such as extending the proposed method to support continuous rotation angles and exploring the possibility of training the network with variable-length sequences.
