The paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder network that learns to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects (e.g., human faces and 3D chair models) from a single input image. The basic model uses an encoder to disentangle the image into identity units and pose units. Action units are applied to the pose units to control the rotation direction. A decoder composed of convolution and upsampling layers then decodes the identity and pose into a rotated image and the corresponding object mask.

To support longer rotation trajectories, the network is extended to a recurrent architecture: the encoded identity units from the input image are kept fixed, while the pose units are updated by a sequence of action units. At each step, identity and (updated) pose units are fed into the decoder to generate the output image.

A main contribution is learning to disentangle identity/appearance from pose. The identity units serve as discriminative, view-invariant features for cross-view object recognition. Moreover, this disentanglement improves rendering quality on longer rotation trajectories, as demonstrated via curriculum training of the recurrent model.

The paper is well written and easy to follow, with clear motivation for each component. Qualitative renderings of rotated images and quantitative results on cross-view object recognition provide strong support for the approach, particularly the disentangled pose and identity representations.

Minor weaknesses (for the authors to address in rebuttal):
- The network currently supports only discrete rotation angles determined by the training set. Do the authors have ideas for extending the method to continuous rotation angles?
- The recurrent encoder–decoder is trained with fixed-length sequences, which seems at odds with the general flexibility of recurrent neural networks.

In summary, the paper presents a novel, end-to-end recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder for rendering rotated objects from a single image. The primary contribution—generative disentanglement of identity and pose emerging from the recurrent rotation-prediction objective—is convincingly supported by both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
