This paper introduces a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed to render an object from novel viewpoints, given only a single 2D input image. The authors evaluate the model's performance on the task of generating rotated views of faces and chairs. They conduct further experiments to investigate the benefits of curriculum learning, assess the quality of the learned disentangled representations via a cross-view object recognition task, and explore the model's capacity for class interpolation between different chairs.

Although the proposed model is well-presented and detailed, the evaluation of its performance is inadequate. The experiments are largely qualitative, relying on visual inspection of the generated images. While these visualizations are helpful, they are not a substitute for a rigorous quantitative assessment. Introducing appropriate error metrics would allow for an objective measure of performance and a direct comparison to state-of-the-art results, which is essential for validating the contributions of this work.

The significance of the paper is also limited by its narrow experimental scope. The model is only tested on the Multi-PIE and Chairs datasets; it would be beneficial to include experiments on more diverse datasets. Demonstrating the ability to render rotated viewpoints for different types of objects would strengthen the claims of generality and make the network architecture seem less ad hoc. Additionally, it would be interesting to see an analysis of how the proposed model generalizes to handle more complex object rotation trajectories that deviate from a static axis (e.g., non-repeating rotations).

On the whole, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. There are a few minor issues: the figures with result visualizations should be scaled larger for better readability, and there are occasional grammatical errors (e.g., in line 124, "rotate" should be "rotated").

In summary, the paper develops a novel and interesting deep learning model for rendering an object from different viewpoints given a single 2D image. However, while the approach is promising, the paper lacks the sufficient experimentation and analysis needed to fully demonstrate the model's significance and its ability to achieve results comparable to state-of-the-art 'geometry-based approaches' (line 51).
