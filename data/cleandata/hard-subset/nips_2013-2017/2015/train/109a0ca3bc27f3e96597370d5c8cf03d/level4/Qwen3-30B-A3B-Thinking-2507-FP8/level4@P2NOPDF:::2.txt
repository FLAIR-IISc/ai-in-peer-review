

This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing out-of-plane rotations of 3D objects (e.g., human faces, chairs) from a single input image. The core architecture disentangles input representations into identity (appearance) and pose (rotation) factors. The encoder decomposes the input image into these units; pose units are then modified by action units specifying rotation direction, and the decoder (using convolutional and upsampling layers) reconstructs the rotated object image and mask. To enable longer rotation trajectories, the model extends to a recurrent structure where the identity representation remains fixed, while pose evolves through sequential action units, culminating in the decoder generating the final rotated output.

A primary contribution is the successful learning of disentangled identity and pose representations. The authors demonstrate that identity units serve as discriminative, view-invariant features, significantly enhancing cross-view object recognition performance. This disentanglement also enables more robust rendering during curriculum learning, where progressively longer rotation sequences are used to train the recurrent model.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The methodological rationale for each component is logically presented, and the evaluation provides strong empirical support: qualitative results showcase high-fidelity rotated image synthesis, while quantitative results on cross-view recognition validate the disentanglement’s utility.

Two minor points warrant clarification in the rebuttal:

1.  **Continuous Rotation Support:** The current model operates with discrete rotation angles defined by the training data. The authors should discuss potential extensions to handle continuous rotation angles (e.g., via regression heads or continuous action spaces).
2.  **Recurrent Architecture Specification:** The recurrent network is trained on fixed-length sequences of action units. While this aligns with the specific task (generating a predefined sequence of rotations), the phrasing "trained with fixed-length, which is actually contradictory to general recurrent neural networks" may mislead. The authors should clarify that the recurrence is designed for fixed-length trajectory generation (a common setup for such tasks), distinguishing it from architectures requiring variable-length input processing.

The paper makes a solid contribution by demonstrating that a recurrent encoder-decoder trained end-to-end for rotation synthesis inherently learns disentangled identity and pose representations. This disentanglement is effectively validated through both qualitative rendering results and quantitative cross-view recognition performance, providing a compelling case for the method’s efficacy. The core innovation—generative disentanglement emerging from the rotation prediction objective—is well-supported and clearly articulated.
