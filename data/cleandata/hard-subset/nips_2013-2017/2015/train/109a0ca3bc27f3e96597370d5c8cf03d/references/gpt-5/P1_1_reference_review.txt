Summary
The paper tackles single-image 3D view synthesis by learning to apply sequences of small out-of-plane rotations using a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder. The model encodes an input image into two latent groups: identity units (intended to be view-invariant) and recurrent pose units (updated by action inputs indicating clockwise/no-op/counterclockwise steps). Predictions are decoded from a fixed identity code and the pose code at each step; training uses pixel-level supervision along full rotation trajectories, optimized with BPTT and ADAM. A curriculum (increasing sequence length) is used to stabilize and improve long-horizon predictions and to encourage disentanglement. Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and a chairs CAD dataset show: (a) progressively rotating novel instances yields smooth, plausible trajectories; (b) identity units support cross-view recognition without using class labels; (c) longer curricula improve both synthesis MSE and view-invariant recognition; (d) chair identity interpolation plus pose traversal qualitatively produces coherent novel instances.

Relation to prior work
The work is related to transforming autoencoders, predictive gating pyramids, and control-conditioned generative models, as well as to recent CNN-based image generators for chairs and inverse graphics networks. The key differences claimed are: an action-conditioned recurrent latent update modeling pose manifold traversal, explicit identity/pose partition (without explicit disentangling regularization), and curriculum training for long sequences. Compared to prior chair generators that decode from labels only, this model learns an encoder for generalization to novel images and handles multi-step rotation. Compared to IGN (contemporaneous), the approach eschews VAE training and emphasizes recurrent control and curriculum.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of action-conditioned recurrent latent dynamics with a simple decoding pipeline; training is straightforward and practical.
- Curriculum learning is well-motivated and ablated across lengths, showing consistent gains for long-horizon synthesis and recognition.
- Emergent disentanglement is convincingly demonstrated via cross-view recognition: identity units improve with curriculum while corresponding pose units carry little identity information.
- Qualitative synthesis on faces and chairs is compelling; identity interpolation plus pose traversal shows smooth, coherent changes.
- The method generalizes to novel instances and supports user-specified rotation direction/length.
- Architecture and training details are mostly specified, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Baselines for view synthesis are weak. For chairs, KNN averages of retrieved views is not competitive; for faces, the 3DMM comparison is qualitative and not apples-to-apples. No comparison to non-recurrent or feedforward multi-angle decoders, nor to single-step models rolled out at test time, nor to VAE/GAN-based decoders.
- Evaluation metrics are limited. MSE is reported (which is poorly correlated with perceptual quality); no PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS or user studies. Recognition is reported, but no quantitative image-quality comparisons to other methods.
- The “weakly-supervised disentangling” claim relies on the training setup (paired adjacent frames with known action) and curriculum; no explicit disentanglement metrics or mutual information analyses are provided.
- Limited scope: images are aligned or rendered on plain backgrounds; robustness to clutter, occlusions, and real-world pose/illumination variation is not addressed.
- Architectural specifics of the action–pose interaction are under-specified (e.g., dimensionality and parameterization of the “selected matrix,” parameter sharing across steps), and there are few ablations (size of identity vs pose units, action encoding choices, effect of mask loss).
- The choice not to feed predicted images to subsequent steps is interesting but untested; it would be informative to compare to teacher-forcing/closed-loop variants.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the recurrent latent update and curriculum are appropriate, and experiments support the main claims. However, the lack of strong baselines and broader metrics weakens the empirical case. The disentanglement evidence is promising but could be more rigorously quantified. Training details are given, but some implementation specifics and ablations are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The problem, architecture, and curriculum are well explained. Figures (referenced) appear to illustrate key behaviors. Suggestions: clarify the action–pose transformation (exact parameterization, sharing), specify batch size/regularization, and add a schematic for the mask branch and loss.

Originality
The combination of action-conditioned recurrent latent traversal with identity/pose partition and curriculum is a novel and coherent synthesis of ideas present in prior work. While each component has precedents, the specific formulation and demonstration of emergent disentanglement without labels in a recurrent control setting is original for the time.

Significance
The approach addresses an important problem (controllable view synthesis) and demonstrates practical gains in long-horizon prediction and view-invariant recognition. Given the simplicity and generality of the framework, it is likely to influence follow-up work on action-conditioned generation and representation learning. The current scope (aligned/clean datasets) limits immediate applicability, but the methodological contribution is valuable.

Questions for the authors
- How exactly is the action unit realized? Is it a set of learned matrices multiplying pose units (one per action), shared across time? What is the pose dimensionality and sensitivity to this choice?
- How does a non-recurrent baseline (single-step encoder–decoder applied iteratively to images, or a feedforward decoder conditioned on desired absolute angle) compare?
- What is the effect of feeding previous predictions to the next step (closed-loop), with or without teacher forcing?
- Can you report perceptual metrics (SSIM/LPIPS) and/or user studies? For faces, could you include identity preservation scores?
- How crucial is the mask loss for chairs? Any failure cases without it?
- Does curriculum length saturate beyond t=16? Any signs of drift over >180° on chairs?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines and ablations as above, including a non-recurrent variant and a repeated single-step rollout.
- Report perceptual quality metrics and structure/edge preservation measures; consider human preference studies.
- Provide more rigorous disentanglement analysis (e.g., linear probe for pose on identity units and vice versa, mutual information estimates).
- Discuss limitations and potential extensions to cluttered scenes, illumination changes, and multi-modal futures (e.g., via stochastic decoders).
- Release code/models to aid reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline accept.

Pros:
- Simple, well-motivated model with recurrent control and curriculum.
- Strong qualitative results and improved long-horizon synthesis.
- Emergent, label-free disentanglement with measurable recognition gains.
- Clear exposition and sensible experiments on two domains.

Cons:
- Weak baselines and limited metrics.
- Narrow scope (aligned/clean data); limited ablations.
- Some architectural details under-specified.
