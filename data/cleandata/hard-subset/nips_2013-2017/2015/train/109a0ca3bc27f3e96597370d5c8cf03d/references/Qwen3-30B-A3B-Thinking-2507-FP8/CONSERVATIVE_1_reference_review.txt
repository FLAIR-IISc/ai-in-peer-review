

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of objects through a sequence of small rotations. The authors frame the problem as pose manifold traversal, using action units to control local rotations at each step and employing curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation trajectory length. The key claims are that their approach can synthesize realistic rotations without explicit 3D model recovery, and that it learns disentangled identity and pose features without explicit supervision.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a well-structured methodology with clear architecture and training procedure.
- The experiments are thorough for the problem domain, with appropriate metrics (MSE) and comparisons against relevant baselines (KNN for chairs, 3D morphable model for faces).
- The curriculum learning approach is well-justified and implemented correctly, with clear evidence of performance improvements as training sequences lengthen.
- The disentangling claim is interesting, with evidence that identity units capture pose-invariant features (Figure 8) and pose units correlate with identity (Table 1).

**Weaknesses:**
The primary concern is the lack of sufficient evidence to justify the novelty and superiority of the proposed approach. The authors present a combination of established techniques (recurrent networks, convolutional encoders, curriculum learning) rather than a fundamentally new method. The paper doesn't adequately differentiate their approach from closely related work, particularly the Predictive Gating Pyramid (PGP) [19] they cite, which also handles sequential image transformations with a control signal. The recurrent structure seems to be primarily motivated by the need to handle longer trajectories, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that a non-recurrent approach would fail for longer sequences (e.g., a single-step rotation model with appropriate architecture could potentially handle longer sequences without recurrence).

The weakly-supervised disentangling claim is overstated. The paper shows that identity units don't correlate with pose, but this is a direct consequence of the training objective (the model must preserve identity across rotations), not a novel disentanglement mechanism. Previous work has explicitly trained for disentanglement using regularizers [6, 21, 24], and the paper doesn't compare against these methods to demonstrate that their implicit approach is better. The results in Table 1 showing identity units outperform pose units in recognition tasks is expected given the training objective, not evidence of superior disentanglement.

The experimental results show modest gains over baselines (42% MSE improvement for RNN16 over KNN5 on chairs), but these gains don't translate to significant practical advantages. The KNN baseline with 5 neighbors already achieves comparable performance to RNN4, and the authors don't demonstrate why their approach would be preferable in real-world applications where KNN might be simpler and more efficient.

The paper also lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the recurrent structure. While they note that "long-term dependency" is a challenge, they don't provide evidence that this dependency is actually a problem for standard approaches, nor do they analyze why their specific recurrent formulation solves this problem better than alternatives.

**Significance Assessment:**
The problem of 3D view synthesis is important, but the paper doesn't advance the state-of-the-art sufficiently. The method is incremental rather than transformative, and the gains over existing approaches are modest. The weakly-supervised disentangling is presented as a key contribution, but it's a natural byproduct of the training objective rather than a novel methodological contribution. The paper would be stronger if it had compared against explicitly disentangled representations (e.g., using the disBM [21] or bilinear models [24]), rather than simply showing that their approach happens to disentangle features.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-executed, it presents an incremental combination of existing techniques without sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty or significant advancement. The authors overstate the significance of their weakly-supervised disentangling, which is a consequence of their training objective rather than a novel contribution. The modest performance gains over baselines don't justify the methodological complexity introduced by the recurrent structure.

The paper would be much stronger if it had:
1) More rigorous comparison against explicit disentanglement methods
2) A clearer demonstration of why recurrence is necessary rather than just convenient
3) Better justification for the novelty of the approach relative to closely related work
4) More substantial evidence that the method solves a significant problem beyond what existing approaches can handle

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The work is interesting but not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS.
