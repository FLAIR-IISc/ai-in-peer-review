

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D views of objects from a single image. The authors frame 3D view synthesis as pose manifold traversal, where a desired rotation is decomposed into small sequential steps. The key innovation is using a recurrent structure with curriculum learning to handle long-term dependencies in rotation trajectories. The model learns disentangled representations of identity (what doesn't change during rotation) and pose (the rotating part) without explicit supervision. The approach is evaluated on face images (Multi-PIE dataset) and 3D chair models (CAD dataset), demonstrating both high-quality view synthesis and the emergence of view-invariant features.

The paper relates to several previous NIPS works: it extends the transforming autoencoder [12] by adding recurrent structure and action inputs, differs from Predictive Gating Pyramid [19] through disentangled latent features, and contrasts with IGN [17] by using deterministic feed-forward training rather than VAE. It also connects to disentangling literature [6, 21, 24] but focuses on emergent disentanglement through the view synthesis task rather than explicit regularization.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The technical approach is sound with a clear formulation of the pose manifold traversal problem. The curriculum learning strategy effectively addresses long-term dependencies, as evidenced by the progressive improvement from RNN1 to RNN16. The experiments are comprehensive, with appropriate baselines (KNN for chairs, 3D morphable model for faces) and quantitative metrics (MSE, recognition accuracy). The paper carefully evaluates both strengths (e.g., smooth transformations, preservation of identity features) and weaknesses (e.g., sensitivity to facial landmark localization, limitations with symmetric chair structures).

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The model architecture is thoroughly explained with precise mathematical formulation (Equation 1) and visualizations (Figures 1-2). Training details for both datasets are sufficiently detailed to allow reproduction. The ablation studies (Figure 6) effectively demonstrate the impact of curriculum learning.

*Originality:* The paper presents a novel approach to 3D view synthesis that avoids explicit 3D model recovery. The key innovation is using recurrent structure with curriculum learning to model pose manifold traversal, which differs from previous work that either uses explicit 3D models or simpler transformation approaches. The emergent disentanglement without explicit regularization is particularly noteworthy and distinguishes this work from related disentangling literature.

*Significance:* The work addresses an important problem in computer vision with applications in graphics, augmented reality, and view-invariant recognition. The ability to synthesize novel views from a single image without explicit 3D models is valuable for practical applications. The demonstration that disentangled representations emerge naturally from the view synthesis task is significant for representation learning, as it shows that complex disentanglement can occur without explicit regularization.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* While the model demonstrates good performance on the tasks, the paper doesn't thoroughly address the limitations of the action unit representation (1-of-3 encoding). The model's performance on symmetric chairs (Figure 8 right) shows that it doesn't fully capture symmetric structures, which could be improved with data augmentation. The paper also doesn't explore the impact of varying the action unit encoding scheme.

*Clarity:* The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the pose units are initialized for the first time step in the recurrent network. The relationship between the pose units and the underlying pose manifold could be more explicitly described to help readers understand how the model navigates the manifold.

*Originality:* While the approach is novel, the paper could better emphasize how it differs from concurrent work like IGN [17], which also uses an encoder-decoder architecture for view synthesis. The claim that "our model can learn discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels" might be overstated since the model is trained on sequences of views for the same object, which implicitly contains class information.

*Significance:* The significance would be enhanced by discussing broader applications beyond faces and chairs, such as how the approach might scale to more complex objects or scenes with occlusions. The paper also doesn't explore how the learned representations could be transferred to other vision tasks beyond view-invariant recognition.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel approach to 3D view synthesis using recurrent pose manifold traversal with curriculum learning
- Demonstrates emergent disentanglement without explicit regularization, a significant contribution to representation learning
- Strong experimental results on both faces and chairs with clear ablation studies
- Well-structured paper with clear methodology and sufficient detail for reproducibility

*Con:*
- Limited discussion of the model's limitations with symmetric objects and complex scenes
- Some claims about the novelty could be better substantiated against concurrent work
- The action unit representation (1-of-3 encoding) is somewhat restrictive and not thoroughly explored

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field by presenting a novel approach to 3D view synthesis that achieves both high-quality image synthesis and emergent disentangled representation learning. The technical soundness, clarity, and significance of the work meet NIPS standards, with minor limitations in addressing all model constraints and contextualizing the novelty against concurrent work. The paper is a valuable addition to the literature on view synthesis and representation learning.
