

# Review: Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image, using curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation trajectory length. The key innovation is modeling pose manifold traversal through small step rotations with recurrent structure, rather than predicting large rotations directly. The model learns disentangled identity and pose representations without explicit supervision, demonstrated on face images (Multi-PIE) and chair CAD models.

**Strengths:**
- The approach of using recurrent networks with action units for pose manifold traversal is well-motivated and technically sound.
- The curriculum learning strategy effectively addresses the long-term dependency challenge in rotation sequences.
- The disentanglement of identity and pose features without explicit regularization is a notable contribution.
- The experiments on both faces and chairs are comprehensive, with appropriate baselines (KNN for chairs, 3D morphable model for faces).
- The cross-view recognition experiments provide strong evidence for the disentangled representations.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper contains several significant technical inconsistencies and omissions that undermine its scientific rigor:

1. **Dataset size inconsistency:** The paper states "we use a subset of 809 chair models" but later claims "we use the images of the first 500 models as the training set and the remaining 409 models as the test set" (500+409=909, not 809). This is a critical data discrepancy that affects reproducibility.

2. **Missing mathematical details:** The description "the triangle indicates a tensor product taking as input the pose units and action units" lacks mathematical definition. Without the precise formulation of how action units transform pose units, the core mechanism remains unclear.

3. **Inconsistent notation:** The paper uses "RNN1," "RNN2," etc., to denote curriculum stages, but the "1," "2," etc. inconsistently represent both sequence length and training stage (e.g., "RNN1" for single-step training, "RNN2" for two-step training, but "RNN16" for 16-step training).

4. **Incomplete quantitative results:** The paper reports MSE values without standard deviations or confidence intervals, making it impossible to assess statistical significance of results (e.g., "RNN16 model (∼179 MSE)").

5. **Unclear training procedure:** The paper states "we prepare training samples by pairing face images of the same person captured in the same session with adjacent camera viewpoints" but doesn't specify how many training samples were created or how edge cases (−45° and 45° viewpoints) were handled.

6. **Figure reference inconsistencies:** The paper references "Figure 6" and "Figure 7" but these figures aren't included in the submission, making it impossible to verify claims about model performance comparisons.

7. **Overstated claims:** The claim "our model learns discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels" is not sufficiently supported beyond the cross-view recognition results, without ablation studies showing the necessity of the recurrent structure.

8. **Formatting issues:** The references contain inconsistent formatting (e.g., "CVPR, , , " with extra commas), and the paper uses inconsistent terminology (e.g., "pose units" vs. "identity units" without clear definitions).

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a technically sound approach to 3D view synthesis with some promising results, the numerous technical inconsistencies and omissions significantly undermine its scientific contribution. The dataset size discrepancy alone is sufficient to cast doubt on the experimental validity. The missing mathematical details prevent proper understanding of the core innovation, and the inconsistent notation creates confusion about the model's operation.

The paper's claims about "weakly-supervised disentangling" are overstated given the lack of ablation studies showing the necessity of the recurrent structure for disentanglement. The comparison to IGN [17] is also insufficiently detailed, failing to clarify how this work differs from prior approaches.

Although the paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, the cumulative effect of these technical shortcomings means it does not meet the quality standards expected at NIPS. The issues identified are not merely minor formatting errors but rather fundamental gaps that prevent proper evaluation of the method's validity and contribution.

I recommend rejection due to the significant technical inconsistencies that compromise the paper's scientific validity, despite the generally sound approach and promising results. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
