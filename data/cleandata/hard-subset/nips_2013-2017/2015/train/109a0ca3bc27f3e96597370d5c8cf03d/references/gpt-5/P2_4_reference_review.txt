Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a recurrent, action-conditioned convolutional encoder–decoder to synthesize novel 3D views (out-of-plane rotations) of an object from a single input image. The core idea is to disentangle identity (pose-invariant content) and pose (view) in a latent space: the encoder produces shared identity units and pose units; action units (1-of-3: clockwise/noop/counterclockwise) modulate a recurrent transformation on the pose units to traverse the pose manifold over multiple steps; the decoder renders images from identity and current pose. The model is trained end-to-end by pixel reconstruction loss over sequences, using curriculum learning (increasing trajectory length) to stabilize and improve long-horizon predictions. Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and the Chairs CAD dataset show: (a) visually plausible long trajectories; (b) quantitative MSE improvements over a KNN baseline for chair view synthesis; (c) emergent disentanglement evidenced by improved cross-view recognition from identity units and poor recognition from pose units; and (d) identity interpolation producing novel objects whose views can be rotated.

The work relates to transforming autoencoders and gating models (Hinton et al.; Memisevic/PGP) by explicitly modeling transformations, but differs in using deep conv encoder/decoder with action-conditioned recurrent pose updates and shared identity. It is complementary to Dosovitskiy et al. (chair generation from codes) and IGN (inverse graphics) by adding recurrence for long sequences, control at each step, and demonstrating weakly supervised disentanglement without labels. Compared to classical 3D morphable models, this avoids explicit 3D reconstruction and can generalize across object instances.

Evaluation by criteria:

Quality:
- Methodology is technically sound. The architecture is well-specified (layer types/sizes; action gating; recurrent update; loss). The training protocol (ADAM, curriculum learning) is clear. The choice to encode only once and propagate in latent space is sensible for stability.
- Empirical support: For faces, qualitative results look robust; a comparison to a 3D morphable baseline highlights strengths (robustness to landmark errors) and weaknesses (texture preservation). For chairs, quantitative MSE over rotation steps and a stage-wise curriculum study convincingly show that longer training sequences improve long-horizon synthesis. Cross-view recognition experiments demonstrate that identity features learned without labels are discriminative and increasingly view-invariant with longer curricula; pose features perform poorly at recognition, consistent with disentanglement.
- However, some evaluations are limited. MSE is a weak image quality metric; no SSIM/perceptual metrics or user studies are reported. The KNN baseline (averaging retrieved images) is weak; stronger baselines (e.g., retrieval-and-copy, warping, or feature-space nearest neighbor decoding) would strengthen claims. For faces, more quantitative measurements (e.g., identity preservation using a face recognizer) would be persuasive. Ablations on the action mechanism, recurrent vs. non-recurrent, and the auxiliary mask loss for chairs are minimal. Runtime/parameter counts and generalization across illumination/elevation are not reported.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The model components (encoder, identity/pose units, action units, recurrent structure) and loss are described precisely enough for reproduction. Training details, datasets, and curriculum schedules are provided.
- Some details could be improved: clarify the exact form of the action–pose interaction (tensor product vs. matrix selection) and whether the recurrent pose update shares parameters across time; specify regularization (weight decay, dropout) and data augmentation; quantify failure cases noted in figures; define evaluation protocols (e.g., how MSE is aggregated) more explicitly.

Originality:
- The combination of a deep conv encoder–decoder with action-conditioned recurrent pose traversal and shared identity units, trained via curriculum to produce long sequences, is a novel and coherent design. The weakly supervised disentanglement emerging from the training objective (without explicit disentangling losses) is an interesting finding.
- Prior work on capsules/transforming autoencoders and PGP addressed transformations, but with different architectures and without the same decomposition or long-horizon controlled traversal in latent space. Dosovitskiy/IGN generate/encode images but do not focus on recurrent traversal with control. The related work section is comprehensive and correctly positions the contributions.

Significance:
- Synthesizing novel 3D views from a single image is a challenging and impactful problem. The paper advances the state of the art for two widely used datasets and shows that a generation objective can yield useful view-invariant identity features without labels. The curriculum insight (longer sequence training improves both synthesis and disentanglement) is practically valuable.
- Limitations constrain immediate impact: evaluations are on constrained settings (aligned faces with neutral illumination, rendered chairs); comparisons to stronger baselines are lacking; generalization to real-world scenes, occlusions, and lighting remains open. Nonetheless, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work on action-conditioned generative models and disentanglement.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled architecture with action-conditioned recurrence and shared identity.
- Effective curriculum training strategy with convincing empirical benefits.
- Demonstrated weakly supervised disentanglement (identity vs. pose) with cross-view recognition.
- Qualitative and quantitative improvements on long-horizon view synthesis.
- Identity interpolation producing coherent novel objects and rotations.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Metrics/baselines: add SSIM (or other perceptual metrics), human preference tests, and stronger baselines (e.g., nearest-neighbor selection without averaging, warping-based methods, or comparison to IGN-style decoders where applicable).
- Quantify identity preservation (e.g., accuracy with a pre-trained recognizer across synthesized views) and pose accuracy (pose regression from decoded images).
- Ablations: (i) remove recurrence to test multi-step by iterated one-step feedforward; (ii) remove action gating or vary action granularity; (iii) assess the effect of the mask branch; (iv) disentanglement metrics (e.g., linearly predict pose from identity units and vice versa).
- Scope: include varying illumination/elevation for chairs and faces; report robustness to misalignment; provide runtime and parameter counts.
- Comparisons: where possible, include quantitative comparisons against contemporaneous models (e.g., IGN on overlapping tasks), or clarify why comparisons aren’t feasible.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and novel recurrent, action-conditioned architecture with solid evidence that curriculum-trained long-horizon traversal improves both synthesis and disentanglement. Despite some evaluation shortcomings and limited baselines, the contribution is of interest to the NeurIPS community and likely to spur follow-up work.
