## Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel views of 3D objects (faces and chairs) from a single input image. The key idea is to learn a pose manifold traversal, driven by user-provided action units representing small rotations. The network disentangles pose and identity information, allowing for manipulation of one factor while preserving the other. The authors demonstrate that this approach can generate realistic rotations and, surprisingly, learn view-invariant features without explicit supervision. They employ curriculum learning to facilitate training with longer rotation sequences.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon several lines of research, including: 3D morphable models, transforming autoencoders, inverse graphics networks, and disentangled representation learning. It differs from previous work by combining a recurrent architecture with explicit control signals for rotation, relying on a purely learning-based approach without explicit 3D mesh recovery, and demonstrating the emergent property of learning view-invariant features as a byproduct of the rotation synthesis task. The paper correctly positions itself within the existing landscape, acknowledging related work like [12], [17], [8], and [24].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The combination of recurrent networks, explicit control signals for transformations, and implicit disentanglement is a novel contribution.
*   **Strong Results:** The qualitative results (Figures 3, 5, 9) demonstrate the network's ability to generate realistic rotations and interpolations.  The quantitative results (Figure 7) show competitive performance on chair rendering compared to a KNN baseline.
*   **Weakly-Supervised Disentanglement:** The emergence of view-invariant features without explicit supervision is a particularly interesting result, hinting at the potential for learning useful representations in an unsupervised manner.
*   **Curriculum Learning:** The use of curriculum learning is well-motivated and appears effective in tackling the challenges of long-term dependency in the recurrent model.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the model architecture and training procedure.  The diagrams (Figures 1 & 2) are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Transformations:** The method currently only addresses rotations. Extending it to other transformations (e.g., scaling, translation, deformation) would significantly broaden its applicability.
*   **Qualitative Focus:** While quantitative results are presented for chair rendering, the evaluation is largely qualitative. More robust metrics for evaluating the quality of generated images and the degree of disentanglement would strengthen the claims.  Specifically, a quantitative analysis of the view-invariant feature space would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison to the 3D morphable model (Figure 4) is somewhat limited. More comprehensive comparisons to other view synthesis methods (e.g., neural texture transfer) would provide more context.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the curriculum learning aspect is mentioned, more detailed ablation studies investigating the impact of different architectural choices (e.g., the size of the identity and pose units, the recurrent cell type) would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the effort required to tune the model for different datasets.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to 3D view synthesis.
*   The results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the method.
*   The weakly-supervised disentanglement aspect is particularly noteworthy.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The evaluation is somewhat limited and relies heavily on qualitative results.
*   The scope of the method is currently restricted to rotations.
*   The lack of detailed ablation studies makes it difficult to assess the importance of different design choices.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and promising results. While the evaluation could be more comprehensive and the scope of the method is currently limited, the authors have presented a compelling argument for their approach. The emergent property of learning view-invariant features is particularly exciting.  I believe this paper is a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS community and recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the weaknesses outlined above in a future iteration of the work.



**Score:** 7.5 / 10
