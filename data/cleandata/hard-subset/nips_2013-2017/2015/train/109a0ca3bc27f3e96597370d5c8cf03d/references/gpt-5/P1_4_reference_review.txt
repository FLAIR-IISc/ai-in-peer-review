Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder model for 3D view synthesis from a single image. The architecture disentangles identity (view-invariant) and pose (view-dependent) latent factors: identity units are shared across time, while pose units are updated recurrently via action inputs that encode clockwise/no-op/counter-clockwise steps. The model traverses the pose manifold through sequences of small rotations, trained with backpropagation through time and curriculum learning that gradually increases trajectory length. The method is evaluated on Multi-PIE faces and a synthetic Chairs dataset, showing long-term rotation synthesis, improved cross-view recognition using the learned identity features (without class supervision), and class interpolation in the identity space.

This line of work sits between image generation and disentanglement. It builds on: transforming autoencoders and gating models (capsules/PGP) for learning transformations; Dosovitskiy et al.’s chair generator (decoder with graphics codes) by adding a deep encoder, distributed identity/pose codes, and recurrent control; and contemporary Inverse Graphics Networks (IGN) by replacing VAE training with deterministic feed-forward training, adding action-conditioned recurrence, and focusing on long sequences. It also relates to bilinear/disBM disentangling models and to sequence prediction/curriculum learning.

Strengths:
- Clear and compelling formulation: recurrent pose units with action control, identity units shared over time, and curriculum training to enable long rotations.
- Demonstrates weakly supervised disentanglement: identity features learned solely from synthesis loss transfer to cross-view recognition on faces and chairs, with improved invariance as trajectory length increases.
- Strong qualitative results: smooth, identity-preserving rotations on faces and chairs; interpolation of identities with consistent style across poses.
- Sensible training design: action inputs, latent-space transitions (not feeding generated images), and mask branch for chairs to improve decoding.
- Quantitative evaluation includes trajectory MSE and recognition accuracy as a proxy for disentanglement; curriculum benefit is shown empirically.

Weaknesses:
- Baselines are limited. For chairs, KNN with VGG features is weak compared to modern controllable generators; no direct comparison to Dosovitskiy et al. (condition on pose) using an encoder; faces are compared qualitatively to 3DMM without quantitative metrics.
- Metrics are narrow: primarily L2/MSE. No perceptual metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) or human studies; visual quality/fidelity to input identity is not quantified beyond MSE and recognition proxy.
- Limited scope of data: Multi-PIE is controlled (aligned, neutral lighting); chairs have white backgrounds. Generalization to in-the-wild backgrounds, occlusions, varying illumination is untested.
- Details/ablations are missing: no ablation of curriculum, action units, shared identity vs not shared, pose/identity dimensionality, or mask loss. It’s unclear how much each component contributes.
- Disentanglement is emergent but not measured directly (e.g., mutual information tests, linear separability of pose in identity units). Pose discretization to 3 actions may limit generality and step size control.
- Citations and presentation: some references are incomplete; some architecture/optimization details could be crisper for reproducibility.

Quality:
Technically sound overall: the architecture and training objective are standard; using recurrent latent transitions with action gating is reasonable and supported by experiments. The curriculum improves long-horizon predictions and identity invariance. However, evaluation could be stronger with better baselines and perceptual metrics. The claims about disentanglement are mostly supported by cross-view recognition and poor recognition from pose units, but more rigorous disentanglement analyses would help. Limitations (controlled data, discretized actions) are not deeply analyzed.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The model is described with enough detail to implement (conv specs, latent sizes, training schedules, ADAM settings). Figures (not included here) are referenced coherently. Some typos and incomplete references should be fixed. Explicitly specifying the action-to-pose transformation parameterization (tensor product details, weight sharing across time) and decoder upsampling choices would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
The combination of: (i) convolutional encoder-decoder, (ii) action-controlled recurrent pose traversal, (iii) shared identity units over time, and (iv) curriculum training for long sequences, is a novel and useful composition relative to prior work on chair generation, IGN, and gating/transforming models. The focus on weakly supervised disentanglement emerging from a synthesis objective and its empirical validation is a valuable contribution.

Significance:
View synthesis from a single image and learning disentangled, controllable representations are important problems. The paper advances the state of the art for long-horizon, stepwise 3D rotations and shows transfer to cross-view recognition without labels. While limited to controlled datasets, the ideas are likely to influence subsequent controllable generative models and representation learning.

Questions and suggestions:
- Provide stronger baselines: an encoder+decoder conditioned directly on target pose (non-recurrent), and a version without shared identity units; compare to IGN-like VAE or to Dosovitskiy decoder with learned encoder.
- Add perceptual metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) and identity similarity metrics for faces; consider a small user study.
- Ablate curriculum length, latent dimensionalities, action design (continuous vs 1-of-3), and mask loss. What fails without curriculum?
- Test robustness to illumination changes and mild background clutter; show results on non-neutral Multi-PIE lighting or composited backgrounds for chairs.
- Discuss generalization to variable step sizes and continuous control; could you regress rotation angle and apply a learned continuous transform?
- Analyze disentanglement quantitatively (e.g., predict pose from identity units and vice versa; CCA/MI measures).

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean, well-motivated model with action-controlled recurrence.
- Demonstrated long-horizon 3D view synthesis and smooth identity-preserving rotations.
- Emergent, useful disentanglement validated by cross-view recognition without labels.
- Curriculum learning shown to materially improve both synthesis and invariance.

Cons for acceptance:
- Weak baselines and limited metrics; reliance on MSE and KNN comparisons.
- Evaluations on controlled datasets; unclear performance in more realistic settings.
- Missing ablations make it hard to isolate which components matter most.
- Discrete action design and lack of continuous or multi-factor controls.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a novel and influential approach to controllable, recurrent view synthesis and weakly supervised disentangling with solid qualitative and informative quantitative results. Strengthening baselines and metrics would further solidify the contribution.
