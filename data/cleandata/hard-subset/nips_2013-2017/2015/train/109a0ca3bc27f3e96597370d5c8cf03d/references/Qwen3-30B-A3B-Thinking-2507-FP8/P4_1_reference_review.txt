

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image, with the key innovation being a weakly-supervised approach to disentangling identity and pose representations. The authors address the challenging problem of synthesizing novel views of objects undergoing 3D rotations without explicit 3D model recovery. The model learns to traverse the pose manifold through a sequence of small rotation steps, using a recurrent structure to handle long-term dependencies and curriculum learning to gradually increase the difficulty of rotation sequences. The paper demonstrates the approach on face images (Multi-PIE dataset) and chair CAD models, showing both high-quality view synthesis and the emergence of disentangled representations.

The paper relates well to prior work on view synthesis, including the transforming autoencoder [12], Predictive Gating Pyramid [19], and Inverse Graphics Network (IGN) [17]. It distinguishes itself from IGN by using a recurrent structure for trajectory prediction rather than a VAE framework, and from other view synthesis approaches by focusing on weakly-supervised disentangling without explicit class labels. The paper also connects to mental rotation experiments [22] and disentangling techniques like bilinear models [24] and disBM [21].

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured approach with clear implementation details. The curriculum learning strategy effectively addresses the long-term dependency problem in rotation trajectories, as evidenced by the progressive improvement in synthesis quality and recognition performance across training stages. The quantitative results (Figure 7) demonstrate consistent improvement with longer training sequences, and the qualitative results (Figures 3-6) show high-quality synthesized views with preserved identity characteristics.

2. **Novelty:** The key novelty lies in the weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose through the recurrent rotation prediction objective. The paper demonstrates that disentangled representations emerge without explicit regularization, which is a significant contribution to the literature on disentangled representations. The approach also extends beyond previous work by enabling continuous view synthesis through recurrent transformations rather than just single-step predictions.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The model architecture is effectively explained with Figure 1 and described in Section 3. The experimental setup is detailed and reproducible, with specific network architectures, training parameters, and dataset details provided in Section 4. The ablation studies (Figures 6-8) effectively demonstrate the impact of curriculum training on both synthesis quality and representation learning.

4. **Significance:** The ability to synthesize novel views from single images has broad applications in computer vision and graphics. The disentangled representations learned by the model could benefit view-invariant recognition tasks, as demonstrated in Section 4.4 where RNN6 achieves competitive results against supervised CNN classifiers for face recognition. The chair interpolation experiments (Figure 9) further show the practical utility of the learned representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Application:** The paper focuses exclusively on rigid rotations of objects with well-defined 3D structure (faces and chairs). It does not address more complex scenarios such as articulated objects, non-rigid deformations, or objects embedded in complex scenes, which would significantly broaden the applicability of the approach.

2. **Vague Technical Description:** The "triangle" operation (Figure 1) that combines pose and action units is described as "equivalently, the action unit selects the matrix that transforms the input pose units to the output pose units" but lacks mathematical detail. This ambiguity makes it difficult to fully understand the model's inner workings.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** While the paper references IGN [17] as a related work, it could provide a more detailed comparison of how the proposed recurrent approach specifically improves upon IGN's VAE framework for the specific task of long trajectory prediction.

4. **Lack of Discussion on Limitations:** The paper does not thoroughly address the limitations of the approach, such as performance degradation at very large rotation angles (beyond 180 degrees) or challenges with objects that have symmetries (e.g., chairs with symmetric designs). The observation that VGG-16 outperforms the RNN model for very large angle offsets (Figure 8) is noted but not thoroughly analyzed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. The technical approach is sound, the results are convincing, and the weakly-supervised disentangling is a noteworthy advance. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance, with strong experimental validation across two challenging datasets.

The main limitations relate to the scope of application and some technical vagueness in the model description. However, these do not detract significantly from the paper's core contributions. The work is novel in its approach to weakly-supervised disentangling through recurrent rotation prediction and has clear potential for future applications.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS and would be a valuable contribution to the conference proceedings. It advances the state of the art in view synthesis by demonstrating how disentangled representations can emerge from a simple rotation prediction objective without explicit regularization. The technical soundness, clear presentation, and meaningful results make this a strong candidate for acceptance.
