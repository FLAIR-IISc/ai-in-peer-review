

# NIPS Peer Review: Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the challenging problem of synthesizing novel 3D views of objects from a single image, without explicit 3D model recovery. The authors propose a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that models pose manifold traversal as a sequence of small rotation steps. The key innovation is the use of recurrent structure to handle long-term dependencies in rotation trajectories, with identity units (representing invariant features) and pose units (representing transformable features). The model uses curriculum learning to gradually increase the complexity of training sequences from single-step rotations to longer trajectories. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves high-quality view synthesis for faces and chairs, and importantly, learns disentangled identity and pose features without explicit supervision.

This work builds on related approaches such as transforming autoencoders [12], Predictive Gating Pyramid [19], and Inverse Graphics Network [17], but makes several key contributions: (1) a recurrent structure for handling sequences of transformations, (2) curriculum learning for training the recurrent network, and (3) weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly articulates the importance of 3D view synthesis as a fundamental challenge in computer vision, and effectively connects it to human mental rotation experiments [22]. The problem statement is well-motivated and addresses a significant gap in the literature.

2. **Novel Architectural Approach**: The recurrent architecture with identity and pose units is well-justified and addresses a key challenge in view synthesis (long-term dependencies in rotation sequences). The use of curriculum learning to gradually increase trajectory length is a practical and effective training strategy.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper conducts thorough experiments on two distinct datasets (Multi-PIE for faces and chair CAD models) with appropriate baselines (KNN for chair synthesis). The evaluation metrics (MSE, cross-view recognition accuracy) are well-chosen and meaningful.

4. **Notable Weakly-Supervised Disentangling**: The demonstration that the model learns disentangled features without explicit supervision is particularly impressive. The cross-view recognition experiments (Figure 8) convincingly show that the identity units become increasingly viewpoint-invariant with longer training sequences.

5. **Clear Technical Contributions**: The paper provides sufficient detail on network architecture, training procedures, and experimental setup to allow for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty and Overlap with Existing Work**: While the recurrent structure is a reasonable extension, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work. The Inverse Graphics Network (IGN) [17] is cited but not sufficiently contrasted - IGN also uses a deep encoder to learn graphics codes and a decoder, though without the recurrent structure. The paper should more clearly articulate how their approach differs from IGN, particularly regarding the recurrent aspect.

2. **Unclear Disentanglement Mechanism**: The paper claims "weakly-supervised disentangling" but doesn't provide a clear explanation of *how* the disentanglement emerges. The paper states "the identity units can do invariant identity recognition but are not informative of pose, and vice-versa" but doesn't provide evidence or theoretical justification for how this happens. A deeper analysis of the latent space would strengthen this claim.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper doesn't provide any theoretical analysis to support the use of recurrent networks for pose manifold traversal. While the curriculum learning approach is practical, it's not clear why the recurrent structure is necessary rather than a non-recurrent model with a longer sequence input.

4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Modes**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the model fails. For instance, in Figure 5, the chair synthesis shows some artifacts at the edges of chairs, particularly for more complex rotations. A failure analysis would help understand the limitations of the approach.

5. **Ambiguous "Weakly-Supervised" Claim**: The paper claims "weakly-supervised disentangling" but doesn't clarify what "weakly-supervised" means in this context. Since the model is trained with rotation sequences (not class labels), it's essentially unsupervised disentanglement. The term "weakly-supervised" is misleading and should be clarified.

6. **Limited Generalization to Other Transformations**: The paper only addresses rotation, but the approach could potentially be extended to other transformations (e.g., deformation). The authors don't discuss how the approach would generalize to other types of transformations, which limits the broader significance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a reasonable contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are promising. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental compared to recent work, particularly the Inverse Graphics Network [17], which shares many similarities.

The paper's most significant contribution is the demonstration of weakly-supervised disentangling through the recurrent architecture, which is an interesting finding. However, the paper would be stronger with a clearer explanation of how the disentanglement emerges and a more rigorous comparison to the closest related work.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, particularly with its demonstration of weakly-supervised disentangling. The reviewers should focus on clarifying the "weakly-supervised" claim, providing a better explanation of the disentanglement mechanism, and more clearly differentiating the approach from related work (particularly IGN).

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the "Weakly-Supervised" Claim**: Either revise the claim to be more accurate (e.g., "unsupervised disentangling") or provide a clear explanation of what "weakly-supervised" means in this context.

2. **Provide More Detail on Disentanglement Mechanism**: Add an analysis of the latent space (e.g., t-SNE visualizations of identity and pose units) to better explain how the disentanglement emerges.

3. **Strengthen Comparison to Related Work**: Add a more detailed comparison to IGN and other similar approaches, particularly highlighting the key differences in architecture and training methodology.

4. **Add Failure Analysis**: Include a section analyzing cases where the model fails, particularly for complex chair rotations, to provide a more balanced evaluation.

5. **Clarify Curriculum Learning Justification**: Provide more theoretical or empirical justification for the curriculum learning schedule rather than just stating it's beneficial.

## Final Thoughts

This is a solid paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis. The recurrent architecture with curriculum learning is a practical and effective approach, and the weakly-supervised disentangling is an interesting finding. With the suggested clarifications and improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The results on cross-view recognition (Figure 8) are particularly compelling and demonstrate the value of the learned representations.

The paper addresses an important problem in computer vision with a well-executed approach that advances the state of the art in view synthesis and representation learning. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the quality of the work and the significance of the results make this a strong candidate for acceptance.
