## Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of 3D view synthesis from a single image, circumventing the need for explicit 3D model recovery. The authors propose a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network with disentangled pose and identity units to model pose manifold traversal via a sequence of small rotations. The network is trained with a control signal at each step, effectively guiding the rotation process.  The approach is evaluated on both faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs, demonstrating the ability to generate realistic rotations and, importantly, learn discriminative view-invariant features without explicit supervision.  A curriculum learning strategy is used to facilitate training.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors position their work within the broader context of 3D reconstruction and view synthesis, contrasting it with traditional geometry-based methods and recent deep learning approaches.  They appropriately cite relevant works like transforming autoencoders, PGP, IGN, and disentangled representation learning methods (e.g., bilinear models, disBM). The paper effectively highlights how their approach differs: using a recurrent structure *with* control signals for iterative transformations, and achieving weak supervision via the rotation prediction task itself. The connection to Shepard and Metzler’s mental rotation experiments provides compelling motivation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:**  The combination of a recurrent CNN with disentangled representations and action units for iterative 3D rotation is a novel and compelling approach. This allows for view synthesis *without* needing a 3D mesh or explicit pose estimation.
* **Weakly-Supervised Learning:** Learning view-invariant features *without* labels is a significant achievement and opens doors for broader applicability.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both faces and chairs are impressive, demonstrating the ability to generate realistic rotations and the quality of the learned disentangled representations (as shown by cross-view recognition performance). The quantitative comparison against KNN on the chair dataset is particularly strong.
* **Curriculum Learning:** The effective use of curriculum learning is crucial for training the recurrent network and highlights an understanding of the challenges involved.
* **Clear Motivation:**  The connection to human mental rotation is well-articulated and provides a strong intuitive basis for the proposed method.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are thorough, including ablation studies (via curriculum training stages) and comparisons to baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Transformations:** The method is currently limited to rotations. While a sensible starting point, extending to other transformations (e.g., scaling, translation, deformations) would be a significant step. The discussion in the conclusion hints at this, but it remains a limitation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper doesn't delve deeply into the sensitivity of the results to various hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, network architectures, curriculum schedule).  More analysis on this front would strengthen the claims.
* **Qualitative Results – Limited Scope:** While the qualitative results are visually appealing, they are relatively limited in scope (few examples shown).  A broader range of test cases would bolster the visual argument.
* **Potential for Mode Collapse:** Recurrent models can sometimes suffer from mode collapse. The paper doesn't explicitly address or demonstrate the robustness of the model against this issue.
* **Computational Cost:** Recurrent networks, especially deep convolutional ones, can be computationally expensive. The paper doesn't discuss the computational resources required for training or inference.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are generally well-supported by the experimental results.  The use of MSE as a quantitative metric, combined with the visual results, provides a strong case for the effectiveness of the approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and organized. The description of the network architecture and training procedure is clear.  However, some sections (e.g., the mathematical formulation of the training objective) could benefit from further simplification and explanation.
* **Originality:** The approach is original, representing a novel combination of recurrent networks, disentangled representations, and control signals for 3D view synthesis.  The authors adequately reference related work.
* **Significance:** The results are significant.  The ability to synthesize realistic rotations from a single image, coupled with the learning of view-invariant features, is a valuable contribution.  The potential applications in areas like photo editing and vision-based reinforcement learning are promising.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to 3D view synthesis.
* Strong empirical results on two diverse datasets.
* Successful learning of weakly-supervised disentangled representations.
* Clear writing and thorough experimental evaluation.

**Against:**

* Limited scope of transformations (only rotations).
* Lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Limited scope of qualitative results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning.  The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent upon the authors addressing the following minor points:

*   Expand the qualitative results with a wider range of test cases.
*   Provide a brief discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method.
*   Consider including a brief analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   Clarify the mathematical formulation of the training objective in the paper.
