Summary and contributions
The paper tackles 3D view synthesis from a single image by learning to apply sequences of small out-of-plane rotations. The core model is a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder with two disentangled latent groups: identity units (constant across a trajectory) and recurrent pose units (updated by an action input). A simple 1-of-3 action encoding (cw/noop/ccw) selects a linear transform on pose units at each step; decoding combines identity and pose units to produce the image. The network is trained end-to-end with pixel-wise reconstruction loss over multiple timesteps using backpropagation through time. To stabilize long-horizon prediction, they employ curriculum learning by gradually increasing sequence length. Despite only using reconstruction supervision, the learned identity units perform competitively on cross-view recognition (faces and chairs), and the method produces plausible interpolations in identity space.

The work is positioned relative to transforming autoencoders, gating models for transformations, the chair-generating CNN, and the Inverse Graphics Network (IGN). Compared to these, the paper emphasizes (i) action-conditioned recurrent pose traversal, (ii) no explicit 3D model recovery, (iii) weakly supervised disentangling emerging from the training objective, and (iv) curriculum learning for long-term rotations.

Empirical results are provided on Multi-PIE (7 yaw views) and synthetic chair renders (31 azimuths, 2 elevations). The method outperforms a KNN baseline for chair view synthesis (MSE), yields smooth long trajectories (up to 16 steps), compares favorably to a 3D morphable face model in robustness to landmark noise, and achieves improved cross-view recognition as the curriculum length increases. Interpolations between identity codes produce coherent intermediate chairs across views.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent formulation: action-conditioned recurrent latent updates for pose with identity shared across time. Encoding once and traversing in latent space is elegant and practical.
- Curriculum learning is well-motivated for long-horizon image prediction and is shown to materially improve both synthesis and representation quality.
- Weakly supervised disentangling: identity units become view-invariant and useful for recognition without labels; pose units degrade in identity discrimination as desired.
- Qualitative results are convincing for both faces and complex chair shapes; rotations are smooth and style-preserving; interpolation demos are compelling.
- Solid set of baselines/controls for the time (KNN for chairs; 3DMM for faces; supervised CNN/VGG recognition).
- Methodologically relevant to NIPS topics in representation learning, sequence modeling, and generative modeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation of perceptual quality. MSE is known to correlate poorly with human perception; no SSIM/LPIPS or human studies. Comparisons to stronger baselines for view synthesis are missing (e.g., appearance flow or later view synthesis methods; though some are contemporary, a discussion would help).
- Ablations are sparse. It would be useful to quantify the contribution of:
  - Recurrence vs. non-recurrent multi-step decoder,
  - Action units vs. directly regressing target pose code,
  - Curriculum length, latent dimensionalities,
  - Mask branch for chairs,
  - Feeding predicted images vs. latent-only recurrence.
- Disentanglement is not quantified beyond recognition. E.g., predict pose from pose units and identity from identity units; measure mutual information or linear separability to substantiate disentanglement.
- Generalization limits: training and evaluation assume clean backgrounds and alignment (faces) or synthetic renders (chairs). Robustness to real scenes, occlusions, or background clutter is not examined.
- The action encoding uses fixed small steps; the model’s ability to handle variable step sizes or different actions (e.g., illumination, elevation, deformation) is not empirically verified.
- Architectural/training details could be clearer: precise definition of the “tensor product”/gating (parameterization and parameter count), nonlinearity choices in FC layers, batch sizes, regularization, data augmentation, and runtime/memory.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and the claims are supported by qualitative results and some quantitative measures (MSE curves, cross-view recognition). The curriculum learning finding is convincing and consistent across datasets. However, the experimental evaluation would be stronger with more comprehensive ablations, perceptual metrics, and additional baselines. The paper is careful in positioning and does not overclaim 3D fidelity, but a brief error analysis (e.g., failure modes, accumulative drift) would help.

Clarity
Overall well-organized and readable. The motivation, architecture overview, and training scheme are clear. Some notational and implementation specifics are underspecified (exact gating formulation; mask compositing; optimizer hyperparameters beyond LR; step size and action mapping for faces vs. chairs). Figures (as described) seem informative.

Originality
The combination of action-conditioned recurrent latent traversal with shared identity, applied to multi-step 3D rotations and trained with a curriculum, is a novel and interesting synthesis of ideas from transforming/gating models and encoder-decoders. The emergence of disentangled representations without explicit regularizers is noteworthy. The relation to contemporary IGN and chair-generation work is discussed.

Significance
Addresses a meaningful problem (view synthesis and view-invariant recognition) and provides a reusable architectural pattern. Likely to inspire follow-up work on action-conditioned generation and representation learning. Practical significance is somewhat limited by dataset constraints and lack of comparisons to stronger perceptual baselines, but the ideas are valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add perceptual metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) and, if feasible, a small user study.
- Provide ablations on recurrence, curriculum, mask branch, and latent sizes; examine closed-loop decoding (feeding generated images) vs. latent-only recurrence.
- Quantitatively probe disentanglement by predicting identity from pose units and vice versa; report mutual information or linear classification accuracy.
- Analyze symmetry: can the model exploit bilateral symmetry (e.g., faces, chairs) to improve large-angle generalization?
- Explore variable step sizes or continuous control in action units.
- Clarify gating/tensor product parameterization and training details; report compute and inference time; consider releasing code/models.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and compelling modeling idea with action-conditioned recurrent pose traversal.
- Strong qualitative results and improved recognition from weak supervision.
- Curriculum learning insight that improves long-horizon prediction and disentanglement.
- Well-situated within relevant literature.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited quantitative/perceptual evaluation and sparse ablations.
- Constrained datasets and relatively weak baselines for synthesis.
- Some architectural/training details missing for full reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a neat, general approach to action-conditioned view synthesis with emergent disentanglement and provides solid evidence of its effectiveness. Strengthening the quantitative evaluation and ablations would make it a clear accept.
