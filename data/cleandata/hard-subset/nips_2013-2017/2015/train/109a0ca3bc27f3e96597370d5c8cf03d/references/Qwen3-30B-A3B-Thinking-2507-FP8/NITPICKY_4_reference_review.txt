

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D view rotations from single images, with a focus on learning disentangled representations of identity and pose. The authors frame the problem as pose manifold traversal using a recurrent structure, with curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation sequence length. The approach demonstrates promising results on face and chair datasets, with claimed advantages over existing methods in generating realistic rotations and learning disentangled representations.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in 3D view synthesis with clear applications in computer graphics and vision.
2. The recurrent network architecture with action units provides a novel way to handle long-term dependencies in rotation sequences.
3. The curriculum learning approach is well-motivated and implemented with reasonable detail.
4. The experiments are comprehensive, comparing with relevant baselines and demonstrating both qualitative and quantitative results.
5. The weakly-supervised disentangling claim is interesting and well-supported by the cross-view recognition experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological inconsistencies and unclear terminology:** The paper repeatedly uses inconsistent naming conventions (e.g., RNN1, RNN2, RNN4, RNN6 for faces vs. RNN2, RNN4, RNN8, RNN16 for chairs), which creates confusion about what these models actually represent. The authors should clarify whether the number refers to the maximum sequence length or something else.

2. **Misuse of "weakly-supervised":** The paper claims "weakly-supervised disentangling" but the training is fully supervised with explicit rotation actions. The supervision is not weak in the standard sense of the term (e.g., using only image-level labels), making this misnomer confusing.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The claim that "the pose units will fall off the manifold resulting in bad predictions" when accumulating linear increments is presented as fact without any mathematical justification. The paper doesn't explain why a linear accumulation would cause manifold drift, nor how the recurrent structure specifically prevents this.

4. **Inadequate disentanglement evidence:** While the paper claims the identity units are pose-invariant, the cross-view recognition experiments don't conclusively prove this. The best RNN6 achieves only ~75% recognition at 90° offset (Figure 8), which is far from perfect invariance. The paper should provide more concrete evidence of disentanglement beyond recognition rates.

5. **Experimental design issues:** The chair dataset selection is vague ("selected out of 1393 by Dosovitskiy et al. [8] in order to remove near-duplicate models..."). The paper should clarify the exact selection criteria and provide statistics on the remaining dataset. Additionally, the KNN baseline uses the entire training set for retrieval, which may be unfair compared to the RNN that's trained on the same data.

6. **Formatting and presentation errors:** The paper contains several minor but distracting formatting issues, including inconsistent use of "RNN" naming conventions, a reference with a stray comma ("CVPR,, ,"), and unclear descriptions of the action unit encoding scheme (1-of-3 encoding with no explanation of how it's implemented in the tensor product).

7. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims "our model learns discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels," but the cross-view recognition performance is only competitive with a CNN classifier that uses class labels (Figure 8). The paper should temper this claim to reflect the actual performance comparison.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents an interesting approach to 3D view synthesis with some promising results, the methodological inconsistencies, misused terminology, lack of theoretical justification, and overstatement of claims significantly undermine the contribution. The paper's claims about disentanglement are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the weakly-supervised terminology is misleading.

The paper would benefit from clarifying the naming conventions, providing mathematical justification for the manifold drift claim, providing more concrete evidence of disentanglement, and correcting the terminology. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution appears to be a slight variation of existing sequence modeling approaches for view synthesis, rather than a significant advance.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial methodological and presentation issues that prevent the paper from making a clear contribution to the field. While the overall problem is important, the execution does not meet NIPS standards for technical rigor and clarity.
