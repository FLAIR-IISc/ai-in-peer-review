

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis through pose manifold traversal. The authors address the problem of generating novel views of 3D objects from a single image by modeling rotation as a sequence of small transformations, using a recurrent network with action units and curriculum learning. The main contributions include a novel architecture for pose manifold traversal, a weakly-supervised disentangling approach that learns view-invariant features without class labels, and demonstrations on face and chair datasets.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in computer vision. The recurrent architecture with action units effectively models sequential transformations, and the curriculum learning strategy is well-justified. The weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly impressive, as the model learns view-invariant features without explicit supervision. The experimental results on both face and chair datasets are convincing, with quantitative comparisons showing clear advantages over baselines. The paper is generally well-written and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising results, I identified several minor but significant issues that prevent me from recommending acceptance:

1. **Methodological inconsistencies:** The paper claims "we found that fixed stride-2 convolution and upsampling worked better than max-pooling and unpooling with switches" without providing any ablation study or quantitative comparison. This is a substantial claim that should be supported with evidence.

2. **Inconsistent notation:** The loss function in Equation (1) uses inconsistent indexing (N∑ i=1 T∑ t=1) and mathematical notation (||...||22 instead of ||...||^2_2). This is a basic formatting error that undermines the paper's technical precision.

3. **Incorrect citation:** The paper states "a subset of 809 chair models... selected out of 1393 by Dosovitskiy et al. [8]" but [8] ("Learning to generate chairs with convolutional neural networks") does not describe this selection process. This is a factual error in the related work.

4. **Missing experimental details:** The paper mentions "We did not find it necessary to use gradient clipping" without providing any supporting evidence (e.g., maximum gradient norm observed). This is a significant claim that requires justification.

5. **Inconsistent terminology:** The paper inconsistently uses "3D" (title) vs "3-D" (Section 2), "manifold" vs "manifolds", and "RNN" vs "recurrent neural network" (sometimes abbreviated, sometimes spelled out).

6. **Technical errors:** The statement "We carry out experiments using Caffe [13] on Nvidia k40c and Titan X GPUs" is factually incorrect - Caffe is a deep learning framework, not a GPU. This is a minor but noticeable error that suggests carelessness.

7. **Unsubstantiated claims:** The paper claims "characteristics of faces, such as gender, expression, eyes, nose and glasses are also preserved during rotation" without providing any evaluation of these characteristics (e.g., through a gender classifier). This is an important claim that lacks supporting evidence.

8. **Formatting issues:** The paper has inconsistent spacing around mathematical symbols (e.g., "5× 5" vs "5×5") and inconsistent use of hyphens in "3D" vs "3-D".

While the paper presents a novel approach with promising results, these minor but persistent issues undermine the paper's technical credibility and reproducibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these details are important for a high-quality NIPS submission. The paper would benefit from a careful proofreading to address these inconsistencies, and providing the missing experimental details to support key claims.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's significant contributions are overshadowed by the numerous minor but important technical inconsistencies that compromise its quality as a scientific contribution. While the core idea is promising, the lack of attention to these details prevents me from recommending acceptance. The authors should address these issues in a future revision before resubmission.
