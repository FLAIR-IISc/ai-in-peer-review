This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for weakly-supervised 3D view synthesis, specifically focusing on rotating objects in 3D from a single image. The core idea—modeling pose manifold traversal with a recurrent network guided by action units—is compelling and a refreshing departure from traditional 3D reconstruction or reliance on explicit 3D priors. The curriculum learning approach to train this recurrent network is also a sensible and well-motivated choice. The authors demonstrate results on both faces and chairs, showcasing the method's ability to generate plausible rotated views and learn disentangled representations without explicit supervision.

The strengths of this work lie primarily in its *novelty* and *boldness*. The approach avoids the pitfalls of classic geometry-based methods (mesh recovery, alignment issues) and tackles a difficult problem with a creative solution. The observation that humans mentally rotate objects inspired by a recurrent model is a nice connection. The disentangling of pose and identity without explicit labels is a significant, and interesting, emergent property. The experimental results, while not quantitatively overwhelming, are visually convincing, particularly for the chair rotation. The inclusion of class interpolation adds another layer of interest, demonstrating the learned latent space's potential.

However, the paper isn't without its weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge limitations, a more thorough quantitative evaluation against strong baselines would strengthen the claims. The comparison to KNN is a good start, but more sophisticated methods (e.g., recent generative models, or even simpler interpolation techniques within a learned latent space) should be considered.  The ablation studies are limited. For example, the impact of the action unit encoding (1-of-3) isn't explored, and it's not entirely clear why this specific representation was chosen. The theoretical justification for *why* this recurrent approach is particularly suited for pose manifold traversal beyond the mental rotation analogy is somewhat thin. While curriculum learning is employed, the details of the curriculum—the exact schedule of increasing sequence length—could be more clearly articulated. The discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the types of objects it will generalize to, could be more nuanced. Finally, the writing, while generally clear, could benefit from tighter editing for conciseness.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The approach is a significant departure from existing methods for 3D view synthesis.
*   **Promising results:** The visual results are encouraging, demonstrating the feasibility of the approach.
*   **Emergent properties:** The disentangled representation learning is an interesting and valuable side effect.
*   **Bold approach:** Tackling this problem without explicit 3D supervision is ambitious and worthwhile.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited quantitative evaluation:** The lack of comprehensive comparisons to strong baselines weakens the claims of superiority.
*   **Ablation studies are lacking:** Understanding the contribution of individual components (e.g., action units) would be beneficial.
*   **Theoretical justification could be stronger:** More formal reasoning behind the suitability of the recurrent architecture would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some methodological limitations and a lack of exhaustive quantitative analysis, I believe this paper presents a novel and promising approach to 3D view synthesis. The core idea is compelling, the results are visually convincing, and the emergent disentangled representation learning is particularly interesting. I am willing to overlook the shortcomings in light of the originality and potential impact of the work. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the points raised above in a future version. This work opens up an interesting new direction for research in 3D vision and generative modeling.
