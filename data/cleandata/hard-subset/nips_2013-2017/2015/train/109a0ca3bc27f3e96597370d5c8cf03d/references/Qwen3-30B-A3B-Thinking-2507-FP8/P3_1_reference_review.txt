

# Peer Review: Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image, without explicit 3D model recovery. The key innovation is modeling pose manifold traversal as a sequence of small rotation steps using a recurrent network with action inputs, trained with curriculum learning to handle long-term dependencies. The model learns to disentangle identity and pose features in a weakly-supervised manner, without requiring class labels. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets: Multi-PIE for face rotation and a CAD chair dataset, demonstrating high-quality view synthesis and pose-invariant recognition capabilities.

The work builds upon related efforts in 3D view synthesis (e.g., [8, 17]), disentanglement (e.g., [24, 29]), and sequence modeling (e.g., [19, 27]). It differs from prior work by using a recurrent structure with action inputs to model pose manifold traversal, rather than direct prediction of final rotations, and by achieving weakly-supervised disentanglement through the training objective itself.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
1. The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The use of recurrent networks with curriculum learning to handle long sequences of rotations addresses a key challenge in view synthesis - maintaining identity information throughout the trajectory.
2. The experiments are comprehensive, evaluating both view synthesis quality (via visual inspection and MSE) and disentanglement (via cross-view recognition).
3. The paper demonstrates a clear progression of results with curriculum training (RNN1 → RNN16), showing improved performance with longer sequences.
4. The weakly-supervised disentanglement is a significant contribution - identity features become pose-invariant without explicit regularization, which is a notable achievement.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The comparison to state-of-the-art 3D view synthesis methods is inadequate. The KNN baseline for chairs is not a strong comparison, as it's a simple nearest-neighbor approach rather than a dedicated 3D view synthesis method. The authors should compare against recent methods like Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) or other 3D-aware generative models.
2. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach. For instance, the failure to handle symmetric structures (evident in Figure 8 right where VGG-16 outperforms the model for large angle offsets) is mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed.
3. The paper claims "weakly-supervised disentangling" but doesn't clearly connect to the broader disentanglement literature (e.g., β-VAE, FactorVAE, DIP-VAE). It would strengthen the paper to discuss how this approach relates to explicit disentanglement methods.
4. The paper lacks a discussion of computational cost. How does the training and inference time compare to other approaches?

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
1. The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear sections describing the problem, methodology, and evaluation.
2. The model architecture is well-described with a clear figure (Figure 1) and sufficient detail in Section 3.1.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The description of the action units (Section 3) is somewhat confusing. The 1-of-3 encoding is not fully explained, and it's unclear why this specific encoding was chosen over alternatives (e.g., continuous rotation values).
2. The connection between the pose manifold traversal concept and the actual network architecture could be clearer. A more detailed explanation of how the pose units and action units interact would improve understanding.
3. The evaluation metrics for cross-view recognition (Section 4.4) could be better justified. Why use cosine distance with identity features, and how does this compare to other metrics?

## Originality

**Strengths:**
1. The approach of using a recurrent network with curriculum learning for pose manifold traversal is novel. Most prior work either predicts final rotations directly or uses explicit 3D models.
2. The weakly-supervised disentanglement emerging from the training objective is a significant contribution that distinguishes this work from other disentanglement approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from the Inverse Graphics Network (IGN) [17], which also uses a deep encoder to extract graphics codes and a decoder to re-render. The key differences (recurrent structure, control signals at each step) are mentioned but could be more thoroughly contrasted.
2. The relationship to the disentanglement literature (e.g., [24, 29, 6]) is not as clear as it could be. The paper mentions disentanglement but doesn't position itself within the broader context of unsupervised disentanglement research.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
1. The work addresses an important problem in computer vision - 3D view synthesis from a single image - with a novel approach that avoids explicit 3D model recovery.
2. The weakly-supervised disentanglement of identity and pose features has significant potential applications in representation learning and pose-invariant recognition.
3. The ability to interpolate between object identities (Figure 9) demonstrates the practical utility of the learned representations.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently argue why this approach is better than existing 3D view synthesis methods for practical applications. The comparison to KNN is not convincing.
2. The limitations of the approach (e.g., difficulty with symmetric objects) aren't fully addressed, which limits the perceived significance of the work.

## Specific Comments

1. **Methodology**: The use of curriculum learning for training the recurrent network is well-motivated and effective. However, the paper doesn't explore alternative curriculum strategies (e.g., random sequence lengths) that might improve generalization.

2. **Experiments**: The chair dataset experiments (Section 4.3) would be stronger with visual comparisons to recent 3D view synthesis methods. The KNN baseline is not a meaningful comparison for a task that requires modeling 3D structure.

3. **Related Work**: The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to recent disentanglement methods. For example, how does the weakly-supervised disentanglement here compare to methods like DIP-VAE or β-VAE?

4. **Figure 8**: The performance drop for large angle offsets (e.g., 90° for faces) is expected, but the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why the RNN6 model still outperforms RNN1 at these angles. This would be a valuable insight.

5. **Table 1**: The performance of pose units is described as "close to random guess," but the paper doesn't provide a statistical test to confirm this is significantly different from random.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of 3D view synthesis and representation learning. The approach is novel, the experiments are well-designed, and the weakly-supervised disentanglement is a particularly compelling contribution. However, the paper has some significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before acceptance:

1. The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is inadequate - the authors should compare against recent 3D view synthesis approaches (e.g., NeRF variants, other recent methods).
2. The limitations of the approach (particularly regarding symmetric objects) need to be more thoroughly discussed.
3. The connection to the broader disentanglement literature needs to be strengthened.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel approach to 3D view synthesis without explicit 3D model recovery
- Effective use of curriculum learning for recurrent networks
- Weakly-supervised disentanglement of identity and pose features
- Comprehensive evaluation on two different datasets
- Demonstrated ability to interpolate between object identities

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art 3D view synthesis methods
- Insufficient discussion of limitations (e.g., symmetric objects)
- Limited connection to disentanglement literature
- Weak baseline comparison for chair synthesis (KNN)

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, but needs to strengthen the comparison to recent methods and provide a more thorough discussion of limitations. The core idea is strong and well-executed, and with the requested revisions, it would be a valuable contribution to NeurIPS.

The authors should address the specific weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by:
1. Adding a comparison to at least one recent 3D view synthesis method (e.g., NeRF, 3D-GANs)
2. Expanding the discussion of limitations, especially regarding symmetric objects
3. Strengthening the connection to the disentanglement literature
4. Clarifying the description of action units and the transformation process

With these revisions, this would be a strong NeurIPS paper that advances the state of the art in 3D view synthesis and representation learning.
