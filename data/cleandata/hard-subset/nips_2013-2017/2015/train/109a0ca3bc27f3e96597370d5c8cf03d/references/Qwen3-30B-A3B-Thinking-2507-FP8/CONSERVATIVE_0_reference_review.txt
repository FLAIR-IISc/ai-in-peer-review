

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from single images, framing the problem as pose manifold traversal with local rotation steps. The authors claim to achieve weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose without explicit regularization. While the problem of 3D view synthesis is well-motivated, the paper's contribution appears to be a combination of established techniques rather than a genuinely novel approach.

The paper builds upon several existing works: the Inverse Graphics Network (IGN) [17] for image encoding and decoding, Predictive Gating Pyramid (PGP) [19] for sequence modeling, and disentangling techniques like bilinear models [24]. The proposed recurrent architecture with curriculum learning for trajectory prediction is an interesting application of known methods to a specific problem, but the novelty is limited. The authors do not adequately distinguish their approach from IGN, which also uses a deep convolutional encoder to learn disentangled representations without explicit regularization. The paper's claim that "despite only trained to synthesize images, our model learns discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels" is not sufficiently substantiated by their experiments.

The quality of the paper is mixed. The experimental design is generally sound, with appropriate datasets (Multi-PIE for faces, chairs from CAD models) and reasonable evaluation metrics (MSE, recognition accuracy). However, the baseline comparisons are problematic. Using KNN with VGG-16 features as a baseline for chair view synthesis is not a fair comparison to the proposed method, as KNN is not designed for this task and lacks the ability to model transformations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing against established 3D view synthesis methods, such as those using explicit 3D reconstruction (e.g., [5], [8]) or neural rendering techniques (e.g., [9]).

The disentanglement claim is particularly concerning. The authors state that "our model can learn disentangled features (i.e. identity units can do invariant identity recognition but are not informative of pose, and vice-versa) without explicitly regularizing to achieve this effect." However, they provide no ablation studies to verify that the disentanglement is due to their specific architecture rather than the curriculum learning approach or the nature of the training data. The evidence presented in Figure 8 and Table 1 is suggestive but not conclusive - the success rates improve with longer curriculum stages, which could simply reflect better generalization rather than true disentanglement.

The paper's most significant weakness is its lack of thorough evaluation. The authors do not address failure cases, nor do they provide a detailed analysis of when and why the model fails. For instance, they don't analyze the performance on chairs with complex textures or non-symmetric structures, which would be critical for real-world applications. The paper also doesn't discuss computational efficiency or scalability to more complex objects.

While the paper is well-written and the experiments are generally well-executed, the contribution does not advance the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way. The results on Multi-PIE (Figure 4) show comparable performance to a 3D morphable model, but the paper fails to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. On the chair dataset, the improvement over KNN is marginal (42% MSE reduction with RNN16 vs KNN5), and the comparison is not against a strong baseline.

The paper makes a plausible claim about the value of curriculum learning for sequence prediction, but this is not novel (see [27] for similar applications). The authors could have strengthened their contribution by demonstrating how their approach outperforms existing sequence modeling techniques for view synthesis.

Strengths:
- Clear problem statement and motivation
- Well-organized presentation with appropriate figures
- Reasonable experimental design with two distinct datasets
- Demonstrates the ability to synthesize realistic view rotations

Weaknesses:
- Overstated novelty claim; the approach is a combination of established techniques
- Insufficient evidence for the disentanglement claim
- Weak baseline comparisons (KNN is not a fair baseline for view synthesis)
- Lack of thorough evaluation of limitations and failure cases
- Does not clearly demonstrate how this work advances the state-of-the-art

I find the paper to be a competent application of existing techniques to a relevant problem, but it lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The claims about weakly-supervised disentanglement require stronger evidence, and the paper does not adequately differentiate itself from related work like IGN. Without more compelling evidence of significant advancement, this paper does not meet NIPS' high standards for originality and significance.

The paper would be significantly improved by: 1) providing stronger evidence for disentanglement (e.g., ablation studies, more detailed analysis of feature representations), 2) comparing against more relevant baselines (e.g., explicit 3D reconstruction methods, neural rendering approaches), and 3) thoroughly analyzing the model's limitations and failure cases.
