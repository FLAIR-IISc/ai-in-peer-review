This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image. The core idea is to model pose manifold traversal as a sequence of small rotations, employing recurrent connections to handle long-term dependencies and action units to control the local transformations. The authors also highlight a weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features, emerging as a byproduct of this rotation prediction task. While the concept is interesting and the experimental setup reasonably comprehensive, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that detract from its overall quality.

The introduction is rather verbose and contains several instances of imprecise language. For example, the statement that classic geometry-based approaches are “vulnerable to image alignment mistakes” is a broad generalization. Moreover, the connection drawn between mental rotation experiments and the proposed approach feels tenuous and lacks deeper discussion of psychological insights. The claim of "weakly-supervised disentangling" is overstated; disentanglement is not demonstrated rigorously, but rather observed as a beneficial side effect.

The related work section is extensive but suffers from a lack of critical analysis. The authors simply list relevant papers without clearly articulating *how* their work differs beyond incremental modifications. For instance, the comparison to the Transforming Autoencoder [12] mentions the use of convolutions instead of capsules, but doesn't address potential downsides of this choice or delve into the theoretical implications. Similarly, the discussion of Inverse Graphics Network [17] glosses over the crucial distinction of the VAE framework, which introduces regularization and uncertainty modeling absent in the current work. References are not consistently formatted (e.g., some include commas after the year, others do not).

The network architecture description is adequate, but lacks sufficient detail. The motivation for using stride-2 convolutions instead of max-pooling is weakly justified. The 1-of-3 encoding for action units feels arbitrary and lacks a clear rationale; why not a continuous representation? The explanation of the tensor product operation is confusingly worded. The training objective (Equation 1) contains redundant notation (e.g., x(i) is already the i-th image, no need to repeat 'i' in the indices of y). It’s unclear why the identity features are shared across all timesteps – is this empirically validated, or just an assumption?

The experimental section is the most substantial part of the paper, but still riddled with issues. The dataset descriptions are brief and lack key details (e.g., resolution of Multi-PIE images *after* alignment and cropping). The network architectures for Multi-PIE and Chairs differ significantly—there’s no discussion of why these design choices were made. The curriculum learning schedule is described vaguely; what specific criteria were used to determine when to transition to the next stage? The quantitative results presented in Figure 7 are difficult to interpret; the y-axis label simply says “MSE” without specifying the scale or normalization. The claim of a “42% improvement” feels misleading without a clear baseline. The discussion of the VGG-16 results for chair recognition is speculative and lacks strong evidence. Figure 6’s visual comparison is poor; the generated images are small and lack sufficient detail for effective evaluation.

There are numerous minor inconsistencies throughout the paper: inconsistent use of terminology (e.g., "pose units" vs. "pose features"), grammatical errors, and awkward phrasing. The figure captions are often incomplete or lack context.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies throughout the paper, indicating a lack of meticulousness.
*   Overstated claims regarding disentanglement and novelty.
*   Insufficiently detailed explanations of key architectural choices and experimental setups.
*   Weak analysis of related work.
*   Poorly presented quantitative results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea of modeling pose manifold traversal with recurrent networks is potentially interesting.
*   The experimental evaluation is reasonably comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and tasks.
*   The generated images demonstrate qualitatively promising results, particularly for face rotation.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the multitude of flaws and inconsistencies make this paper unsuitable for acceptance at NIPS. The lack of rigor and attention to detail undermines the credibility of the research. While the authors demonstrate some promising results, these are overshadowed by the numerous shortcomings in the presentation and analysis. A major revision addressing these issues would be necessary for reconsideration.
