

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D views of objects from a single image by modeling pose manifold traversal. The authors frame 3D view synthesis as a sequence of small rotation steps, using curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation trajectory length. The model claims to learn disentangled representations (identity and pose) without explicit regularization, with experiments on faces and chairs demonstrating view synthesis and cross-view recognition.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in computer vision with a novel approach to 3D view synthesis. The concept of using a recurrent network to model pose manifold traversal with control signals at each step is well-motivated. The curriculum learning approach is appropriately applied to handle long-term dependencies in rotation trajectories. The disentangling property observed in the identity units is an interesting byproduct of the training process, and the experiments on both faces and chairs provide reasonable evidence of the model's capabilities.

## Weaknesses

### Minor but Cumulative Technical Issues
1. **Formatting and Notation Errors**: The paper contains multiple minor formatting inconsistencies that undermine its technical credibility. The training objective equation (1) has incorrect summation notation: "N∑ i=1 T∑ t=1" should be "∑_{i=1}^N ∑_{t=1}^T". The 1-of-3 encoding notation uses inconsistent brackets ([100] vs. [1,0,0]), which is confusing and unprofessional.

2. **Methodological Ambiguities**: The paper states "the model predictions at time step t are not fed into the next time step input," which contradicts standard recurrent network practice. This needs clarification - is the model actually non-recurrent in the pose units despite being called a "recurrent network"? The explanation of how the pose units are transformed through the action units (via "triangle indicates a tensor product") is insufficiently detailed.

3. **Experimental Design Concerns**: The Multi-PIE dataset split uses "first 200 people for training and remaining 137 for testing" without explaining the selection criteria. This could introduce bias if the first 200 people have different characteristics. The KNN baseline tuning process ("tune K value in [1,3,5,7]") lacks explanation of how the optimal K was selected (validation set?).

4. **Missing Figure References**: The paper references "Figure 3" and "Figure 4" but provides no figures, making it impossible to verify the claims about visual quality. The paper also references Figure 1 and Figure 2 in section 3 without including them.

5. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims "weakly-supervised disentangling" as a main contribution, but the disentanglement is a byproduct of the curriculum learning approach rather than a novel method. The paper should acknowledge that the disentanglement is implicitly encouraged by the training procedure rather than being a primary goal.

### Significance Concerns
The paper's contribution to the field is modest. The authors compare against KNN baselines, but this is a weak comparison for a task that has been previously addressed with more sophisticated methods (e.g., 3D morphable models for faces). The paper does not clearly articulate how their approach differs from the Inverse Graphics Network (IGN) [17], which also uses a deep encoder-decoder with a similar goal of learning disentangled representations for view synthesis.

The paper's claim that their model "learns discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels" is somewhat misleading. While they don't use explicit class labels for training, their model is still trained on a dataset where the identity is implicitly known (same person across views), making it a form of weak supervision. This should be acknowledged more clearly.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a reasonable approach to 3D view synthesis with a novel training strategy (curriculum learning for rotation trajectories). However, the cumulative effect of minor methodological inconsistencies, formatting errors, ambiguous explanations, and overclaimed contributions significantly undermines the paper's quality. While the approach is sound and the results are reasonable, the paper fails to meet the high standards of technical precision expected at NeurIPS.

The paper's technical writing is inconsistent (e.g., inconsistent notation, missing figure references), and the claims about "weakly-supervised disentangling" are overstated. The experimental design has several unexplained aspects that would make replication difficult. While the problem addressed is important, the paper does not make a sufficiently novel or rigorous contribution to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.

I recommend rejection due to the accumulation of minor but significant technical flaws that collectively reduce the paper's credibility and impact. The authors would benefit from addressing these issues before resubmitting to a venue with less stringent standards.

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Well-motivated problem and approach
- Reasonable experimental results on two datasets
- Curriculum learning is appropriately applied to handle long sequences

**Con:**
- Multiple minor but persistent technical errors and inconsistencies
- Overstated claims about disentanglement and weak supervision
- Ambiguous methodology descriptions
- Missing figure references
- Weak comparison baseline for view synthesis task
- Insufficient differentiation from related work (e.g., IGN)
