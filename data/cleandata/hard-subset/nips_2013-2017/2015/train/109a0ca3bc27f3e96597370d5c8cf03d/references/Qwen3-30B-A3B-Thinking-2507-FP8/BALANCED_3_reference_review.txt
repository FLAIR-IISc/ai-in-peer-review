

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image, focusing on generating realistic rotations of objects (faces and chairs) through a pose manifold traversal approach. The authors frame 3D view synthesis as a sequence of small rotation steps, using a recurrent structure to maintain identity information while transforming pose. The key innovation is the use of curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation sequence length, which enables the model to learn disentangled identity and pose representations without explicit supervision. This work builds upon related approaches like transforming autoencoders [12], Predictive Gating Pyramid [19], and Inverse Graphics Network [17], but distinguishes itself through its recurrent structure for long-term prediction and weakly-supervised disentangling.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-justified design choices. The recurrent structure with identity and pose units is well-motivated to address long-term dependencies in rotation sequences. The curriculum learning strategy is effectively implemented and demonstrates clear benefits in both image synthesis quality and disentangled representation learning. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with quantitative metrics (MSE) and qualitative comparisons across multiple datasets and baselines (KNN, CNN, 3D morphable model). The ablation studies showing improved performance with longer training sequences are particularly convincing.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by combining recurrent networks with pose manifold traversal for view synthesis, particularly through the curriculum learning approach that enables long-term prediction. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose without explicit regularization is a significant advance over prior work. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work in Section 2, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.

**Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in computer vision with clear applications in photo editing, VR, and robotics. The ability to generate novel object classes through interpolation (Figure 9) and the view-invariant recognition performance (Figure 8) demonstrate practical value. The model's success in generating realistic rotations for complex objects like chairs (where previous methods were not tested) represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with effective use of figures to illustrate the model architecture and results. The methodology section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though some implementation specifics (e.g., exact training times, GPU memory usage) could be elaborated.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** The comparison with the 3D morphable model for face synthesis (Figure 4) is qualitative only, which weakens the claims about performance advantages. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach for highly complex objects with intricate details (e.g., chairs with complex textures or patterns). The ablation study for curriculum learning is somewhat limited - the paper doesn't show how the model would perform without curriculum learning, which would help validate this key design choice.

**Originality:** While the paper presents a novel combination of techniques, the core idea of using recurrent networks for pose manifold traversal is conceptually similar to other sequential modeling approaches. The paper could better emphasize how their specific implementation (with the identity/pose unit separation and curriculum training) differs from more general sequence modeling approaches.

**Significance:** The paper doesn't fully explore the practical implications of their weakly-supervised disentangling. How does this compare to explicitly supervised disentanglement methods? What are the limitations of the disentangled representations for downstream tasks beyond recognition? These questions would strengthen the significance claim.

**Clarity:** The paper could benefit from more detailed explanation of the action unit encoding (e.g., why 1-of-3 encoding was chosen) and how the model handles the circular nature of rotation (e.g., 360° rotation). The description of the chair dataset preparation (Section 4.1) could be clearer about how they handled the 31 viewpoints.

## Recommendation and Suggestions

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. It makes a solid contribution to the field with a well-executed approach that advances the state of the art in 3D view synthesis and weakly-supervised disentangling. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear improvements over baselines.

To strengthen the paper further, I suggest:

1. Adding a quantitative comparison with the 3D morphable model for face synthesis (e.g., using MSE or perceptual metrics).

2. Including an ablation study showing performance without curriculum learning to validate its necessity.

3. Discussing the limitations of the approach for objects with highly complex textures or fine details.

4. Providing more details about the training process for the chair dataset, particularly how they handled the circular rotation space.

5. Adding a brief discussion comparing the weakly-supervised disentangling approach to explicitly supervised methods (e.g., [21, 24, 29]) to clarify the advantages of their approach.

The paper's core contribution - using curriculum learning with recurrent transformations to enable weakly-supervised disentangling for 3D view synthesis - is significant and well-executed. With the suggested minor clarifications, this would be a strong addition to NIPS.
