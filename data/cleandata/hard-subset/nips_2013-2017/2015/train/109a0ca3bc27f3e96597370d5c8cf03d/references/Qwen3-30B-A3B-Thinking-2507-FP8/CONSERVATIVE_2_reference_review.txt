

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D views of objects from a single image by modeling pose manifold traversal. The authors claim to learn disentangled identity and pose representations through a weakly-supervised approach without explicit regularization. The model uses curriculum learning to gradually increase the length of rotation sequences, with the hypothesis that longer training sequences improve disentanglement.

The paper builds upon prior work in transforming autoencoders [12], predictive gating networks [19], and inverse graphics networks [17], but adds a recurrent structure with action units for pose traversal. The authors demonstrate their approach on face rotation (Multi-PIE) and chair rotation (CAD models), comparing against KNN baselines and showing reasonable image synthesis quality and improved cross-view recognition performance with longer training sequences.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The experimental setup is reasonable, and the authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The curriculum learning approach is a sensible strategy for training recurrent networks for sequence prediction. The results on cross-view recognition for faces are promising, showing improved performance with longer training sequences, which aligns with the authors' claims about disentanglement.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main weakness is its lack of sufficient validation against established baselines. The comparison to KNN is superficial and does not include modern neural network-based approaches for view synthesis, such as the work by Dosovitskiy et al. [8] on chair generation or recent 3D-aware image generation methods. The paper fails to adequately differentiate itself from the Inverse Graphics Network (IGN) [17], which shares many similarities (deep convolutional encoder, decoder, and graphics codes) but doesn't use recurrence for trajectory generation. The "weakly-supervised disentangling" claim is overstated - the paper doesn't provide evidence that this disentanglement is better than explicit regularization methods like β-VAE [21] or the disentangling Boltzmann machine [21]. The ablation studies are insufficient, with no experiments showing the necessity of the recurrent structure or the curriculum learning approach. The paper also fails to address the limitations of their approach, such as the sensitivity to initial pose alignment or the difficulty of handling complex 3D structures beyond chairs and faces.

The paper's claims about "disentangling" are particularly problematic. The authors note that identity units perform well at recognition while pose units perform poorly (1.4% success rate vs. 200 classes), which is expected behavior for a disentangled representation. However, they don't compare against methods that explicitly optimize for disentanglement, making it impossible to assess whether their approach achieves better disentanglement than established techniques. The paper's main contribution appears to be a variation of existing architectures with curriculum learning, rather than a fundamentally new approach to view synthesis or disentanglement.

**Quality Assessment:** The technical approach is sound but not rigorously validated. The claims about disentanglement are not well-supported by evidence, and the experiments lack sufficient comparison to relevant baselines. The paper doesn't provide theoretical analysis of why their approach should work better than existing methods, and the empirical results, while promising, don't demonstrate a significant advance over the state of the art.

**Originality Assessment:** The paper's core idea is not novel. The recurrent structure with action units is a straightforward extension of prior work on sequence prediction [19, 27]. The "weakly-supervised disentangling" is not a new concept, as disentanglement has been extensively studied in the context of representation learning [21, 24, 29]. The paper fails to clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon these existing methods.

**Significance Assessment:** The problem of 3D view synthesis is important, but the paper doesn't demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods. The results on chair rotation are promising but don't show a clear advantage over more direct approaches like the chair generation network [8] or recent 3D-aware generative models. The weakly-supervised disentangling claim is overstated, as the paper doesn't provide evidence that this is better than explicitly regularized disentanglement methods.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper is well-written and presents a reasonable approach to view synthesis, it lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and significance. The paper builds upon existing work without providing compelling evidence that it advances the state of the art. The weakly-supervised disentangling claim is particularly problematic, as the paper doesn't provide adequate validation against established disentanglement methods. The experiments are limited in scope and comparison, and the paper fails to adequately differentiate itself from related work like IGN [17].

The authors should substantially revise the paper to include more thorough comparisons with relevant baselines, provide stronger evidence for their disentanglement claims, and better articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods before reconsideration.
