

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis, where the model learns to generate rotated views of objects from a single image by traversing the pose manifold through a sequence of small rotation steps. The authors introduce identity units (representing invariant features) and pose units (representing transformation features), with action units controlling the rotation direction at each step. The key innovation is the use of curriculum learning to gradually increase the length of rotation sequences during training, enabling the model to learn stable representations for longer rotation trajectories. The work builds on related approaches like transforming autoencoders [12], Predictive Gating Pyramid [19], and Inverse Graphics Network [17], but differs in its recurrent structure with action inputs and its ability to learn disentangled representations without explicit regularization.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with appropriate experimental evaluation. The model architecture is well-defined, and the authors provide thorough ablation studies (Figure 6) demonstrating the benefits of curriculum learning. The quantitative results on both Multi-PIE (face) and chair datasets (Figures 7-9) show significant improvements over baselines, particularly for longer rotation sequences. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features (Table 1) is particularly compelling, as the model learns discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels. However, the paper lacks detailed analysis of the computational complexity and training time compared to baselines, and the comparison with the 3D morphable model (Figure 4) is qualitative without quantitative metrics.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate figures that effectively illustrate the model architecture (Figure 1) and results (Figures 3-9). The explanation of the curriculum learning approach (Section 3.1) is particularly clear. However, the description of the action units (the "triangle" in Figure 1) is insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to fully understand how the local transformations are computed. The paper could benefit from a more explicit mathematical description of the transformation operation.

**Originality:** The paper makes several novel contributions. The recurrent architecture with action units for modeling pose manifold traversal represents a significant departure from previous approaches that typically model entire rotations at once. The curriculum learning approach for training the recurrent network is a practical and effective technique not previously applied to this problem. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features without explicit regularization is particularly noteworthy, as it builds on recent work in disentangling representations [21, 24, 29] but achieves this through the learning objective rather than explicit regularization. While related to Inverse Graphics Network [17], the authors' approach differs significantly in its recurrent structure and control signal input.

**Significance:** The problem of 3D view synthesis from a single image is an important one with applications in computer graphics, augmented reality, and vision-based reinforcement learning. The paper demonstrates strong results on both face and chair datasets, showing the approach's generality. The ability to learn disentangled representations without class labels is particularly valuable, as it could be applied to other tasks requiring disentangled representations. The model's ability to generate smooth rotation trajectories and interpolate between object classes (Figure 9) further demonstrates its practical utility.

**Strengths:**
- Novel recurrent architecture with action units for pose manifold traversal
- Effective curriculum learning approach for training long sequences
- Strong quantitative results showing improvements over baselines
- Demonstrated ability to learn disentangled representations without explicit regularization
- Good experimental evaluation on two diverse datasets (faces and chairs)

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient detail about the action unit transformation operation
- Limited quantitative comparison with 3D morphable model (Figure 4)
- No analysis of computational complexity or training time
- Limited discussion of limitations (e.g., handling occlusions or complex scenes)
- The disentangling analysis (Table 1) could be expanded with more feature visualization

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a technically sound approach to a significant problem
- The recurrence with action units and curriculum learning is novel and well-justified
- The weakly-supervised disentangling is a particularly strong contribution
- The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate clear improvements
- The work builds on and extends relevant prior work while making distinct contributions

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The description of the action units is insufficiently detailed
- The comparison with the 3D morphable model lacks quantitative metrics
- The paper doesn't fully address the computational cost of the approach
- The limitations section is too brief

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis by introducing a novel recurrent approach that effectively handles long sequences of rotations through curriculum learning. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features is particularly noteworthy. While the paper could benefit from more detailed explanation of the action units and additional quantitative comparisons, the technical soundness, novelty, and significance of the work make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The results are well-supported by thorough experiments, and the approach has clear potential for broader application in computer vision and graphics.
