This paper presents a novel approach to 3D view synthesis using a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network. The core idea – framing 3D rotation as a pose manifold traversal and using recurrent networks with action units to control movement along this manifold – is quite compelling and represents a fresh perspective on a long-standing problem. It cleverly sidesteps the need for explicit 3D reconstruction, a common bottleneck in traditional methods. The weakly-supervised disentangling of pose and identity is particularly noteworthy, demonstrating an emergent property of the system rather than a specifically engineered one. The curriculum learning strategy also seems crucial for stabilizing training.

The strengths of the paper lie heavily in its novelty and the potentially significant shift in perspective it offers. The ability to generate rotation trajectories with a control signal is a strong practical element, and the disentangled feature learning, even without explicit supervision, is a promising outcome. The results on both faces and chairs, while not quantitatively groundbreaking compared to specialized methods, demonstrate a reasonable level of performance and generalization. The related work section is comprehensive and adequately positions the work within the broader landscape of 3D vision and generative modeling.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the experimental results are encouraging, they lack a more rigorous quantitative comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods *specifically* for view synthesis. The KNN baseline provided is helpful, but more direct comparisons to techniques like IGN or methods leveraging 3D morphable models are needed to truly assess the contribution. The ablation studies are limited; a more detailed analysis of the impact of different components (e.g., the recurrent structure vs. a feedforward network) would strengthen the argument. Furthermore, the justification for architectural choices (e.g., stride-2 convolutions vs. max-pooling) feels somewhat superficial. The discussion of limitations is also relatively brief. For example, the observation that VGG-16 outperforms the RNN on chair recognition for large angle offsets, due to symmetry awareness, warrants further exploration. The statement that "longer training sequences force the identity units to better disentangle themselves" feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks strong empirical backing beyond the observed performance improvements.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The recurrent pose manifold traversal approach is a genuinely new way to tackle 3D view synthesis.
*   **Weakly-supervised disentangling:** The emergent disentangling of pose and identity is a valuable outcome and could be influential.
*   **Potential impact:** The method offers a promising alternative to traditional 3D reconstruction-based approaches.
*   **Solid experimental validation:** Although not exhaustive, the experiments demonstrate reasonable performance on challenging datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited quantitative comparison:** Lack of direct comparison to SOTA view synthesis methods.
*   **Limited ablation studies:** Insufficient analysis of component contributions.
*   **Superficial architectural justification:** Limited rationale for specific design choices.
*   **Somewhat weak theoretical grounding:** The concept of the pose manifold traversal, while intuitive, could benefit from a more formal treatment.



Overall, I believe the paper's strengths – particularly its novelty and potential impact – outweigh its weaknesses. The approach is bold and represents a significant departure from conventional methods. While further refinement and more rigorous evaluation are needed, the work presents a compelling contribution to the field and deserves to be accepted. I would encourage the authors to address the concerns raised regarding quantitative comparisons and ablation studies in a future revision. The current work is a promising starting point for exploring a novel and potentially fruitful research direction.
