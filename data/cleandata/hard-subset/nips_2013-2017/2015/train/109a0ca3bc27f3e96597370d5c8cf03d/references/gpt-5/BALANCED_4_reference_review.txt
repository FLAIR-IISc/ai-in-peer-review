Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles single-image 3D view synthesis via a recurrent, action-conditioned convolutional encoder-decoder that disentangles “identity” (pose-invariant) and “pose” (transformable) latent units. Given an input image and a sequence of action codes (clockwise/noop/counter-clockwise), the model traverses a pose manifold recurrently in latent space while keeping identity fixed, and decodes the sequence of predicted views. Training uses pixel-wise losses over rotation sequences with curriculum learning (increasing sequence length). Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and rendered chairs show (i) smooth, long-horizon rotation sequences, (ii) improved long-horizon fidelity with longer curriculum, (iii) identity features that are increasingly pose-invariant (cross-view recognition), and (iv) identity interpolation leading to plausible novel chairs.

The work builds on and relates to: transforming autoencoders and gated models for transformations and mental rotation (Hinton et al.; Memisevic; PGP), encoder-decoders for image synthesis (Dosovitskiy et al.), and inverse graphics approaches (Kulkarni et al., IGN). Compared to these, the contributions are the explicit action-conditioned recurrent traversal on the pose manifold, the separation into recurrent pose and shared identity units, and curriculum training to stabilize long sequences. It also relates to sequential generative and transformation models (e.g., DRAW; video prediction LSTMs) and Spatial Transformer Networks (STN) for controlled transformations; explicit comparisons to such methods would further situate the contribution.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent model that marries action-conditioned recurrence with encoder-decoder architectures; simple but effective interface (one-hot actions).
- Compelling qualitative demonstrations on two domains with nontrivial rotations; identity preserved and smooth pose transitions.
- Curriculum learning is well-motivated and empirically important; longer curricula improve both synthesis and disentanglement.
- Disentangling emerges without explicit regularizers; identity units support cross-view recognition competitively with a supervised CNN baseline on faces, and improve with longer curricula.
- Interpolation experiments show a continuous, meaningful identity manifold.
- Training/inference occur purely in latent space (no feeding predictions forward), avoiding pixel accumulation of errors.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is limited. Image quality is assessed mainly via MSE; no perceptual metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) or human study. KNN baseline for chairs is weak; no comparisons against stronger learned baselines (e.g., IGN, DRAW-like sequential decoders, STN-based methods, or a non-recurrent encoder-decoder trained for multi-angle prediction).
- The “weakly-supervised” claim hinges on having access to ordered views and action labels; this is strong supervision for pose and only weak for identity. Discussion on how the approach fares without known actions or with noisy pose would be useful.
- The recurrent pose update is described abstractly (“tensor product”/matrix selection). Precise parameterization, stability properties, and whether the noop is constrained to identity are not fully specified.
- Datasets are either aligned (faces) or rendered with clean backgrounds (chairs). Generalization to in-the-wild scenes, occlusions, clutter, and illumination changes is untested.
- Deterministic L2 training yields some blurriness and cannot model multi-modality (self-occlusion disocclusions).
- No explicit cycle-consistency or group-structure constraints; error accumulation is reduced via curriculum, but formal guarantees of staying on-manifold are absent.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with reasonable architectural choices and training procedures. The empirical evidence supports the main claims (trajectory synthesis and emergent disentanglement).
- However, the baseline comparisons are not as strong as they could be, and metrics could better capture perceptual quality and identity preservation. Some implementation details (pose update matrices, capacity vs. CNN baseline) could be clarified.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; datasets, architectures, and training schedules are described, and figures (referred to) illustrate key phenomena.
- Suggested clarifications: exact recurrent update equation; whether noop weights are initialized/regularized to identity; parameter counts; how action-conditioned matrices are shared across time and classes; effect of the mask branch on chairs.

Originality
- Moderate. The components exist in the literature, but the combination—action-conditioned recurrent traversal with identity/pose partition and curriculum for 3D rotations—feels novel and is executed convincingly.

Significance
- Moderate. The paper advances controllable view synthesis without explicit 3D and provides evidence that such training yields pose-invariant identity features. At the time, this was a useful step toward learned inverse graphics. Broader impact would be strengthened by more challenging datasets and stronger baselines.

Constructive suggestions
- Add stronger baselines: (i) IGN encoder-decoder, (ii) a non-recurrent model trained to output multiple target views jointly, (iii) STN-based models for 3D-aware transformations, (iv) sequential decoders (e.g., DRAW/video LSTMs).
- Report PSNR/SSIM and LPIPS; consider identity preservation metrics (face recognition accuracy across synthesized views).
- Incorporate cycle-consistency (e.g., rotate + inverse-rotate ≈ identity) and/or group constraints to encourage staying on the rotation manifold.
- Explore probabilistic or adversarial/perceptual losses to reduce blur and handle multimodality at disocclusions.
- Stress test on in-the-wild objects with clutter/occlusion; ablate the mask branch to quantify its contribution.
- Provide ablations: no curriculum; feed-forward of predicted images vs. latent-only; action/noop matrix constraints; GRU/LSTM vs. simple linear updates for pose units.
- Discuss symmetry explicitly (e.g., left-right ambiguity in chairs) and whether mirroring augmentation or symmetric constraints help large-angle recognition.
- Analyze failure cases.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, actionable idea with a simple architecture that works in practice.
- Strong qualitative results and nice demonstration of emergent disentanglement.
- Curriculum learning insight for long-horizon transformation prediction.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and somewhat weak baselines; evaluation relies on MSE and curated datasets.
- Missing comparisons to closely related contemporary methods.
- Deterministic L2 training leads to blur; no handling of multimodality.
- Some technical details of the recurrent update are under-specified.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper presents a coherent and timely contribution with solid empirical evidence and interesting insights about curriculum and disentangling. Strengthening baselines and metrics and clarifying the recurrent parameterization would make the case unequivocal, but in its current form it is still a valuable addition.
