## Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel views of 3D objects (faces and chairs) from a single image, achieved through simulating rotations. The approach avoids explicit 3D model recovery, instead learning to traverse a "pose manifold" using recurrent connections and action units that control local rotations. A key contribution is the weak supervision – the model learns view-invariant features and disentangles pose and identity *without* explicit labels for these factors. The authors also employ curriculum learning to facilitate training on longer rotation sequences.  The paper demonstrates promising results in both image synthesis and downstream pose-invariant recognition tasks.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly situate their work within the broader context of 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. They acknowledge and differentiate themselves from methods relying on explicit 3D reconstruction (multi-view stereo, structure-from-motion), approaches requiring prior 3D models (e.g., face alignment), and recent deep learning methods such as transforming autoencoders, PGP, and IGN. They effectively highlight how their recurrent architecture with action units addresses limitations of these earlier works, particularly in handling long-term dependencies and avoiding explicit 3D model recovery. The comparison to disentangling methods like bilinear models and disBM is also relevant, showcasing the novel learning paradigm offered by their sequential rotation prediction task.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The recurrent formulation for view synthesis is a strong contribution.  It provides a conceptually appealing way to handle the ill-posed nature of the problem by modeling rotations as a sequence of small steps, implicitly capturing the underlying pose manifold.
* **Weakly-Supervised Disentanglement:** Learning disentangled representations without explicit labels is a significant achievement.  The fact that the model learns view-invariant features as a byproduct of the rotation prediction task is notable.
* **Curriculum Learning:**  The use of curriculum learning is well-justified and demonstrably improves performance, indicating the difficulty of training recurrent models on long sequences.
* **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments on two datasets (Multi-PIE and Chairs) with appropriate baselines (including a KNN method) and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in both image synthesis and cross-view recognition.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the network architecture is clearly explained with the accompanying figure. The inclusion of ablation studies and a discussion of limitations are positive.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors detail network architectures and training parameters sufficiently, allowing for potential reproducibility.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Generalization Discussion:** While the model performs well on faces and chairs, the paper lacks a broader discussion on the potential for generalizing to more complex objects or scenes. The reliance on relatively simple object categories might limit the perceived impact.
* **Lack of Qualitative Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While KNN is used as a baseline, there's a lack of qualitative comparison to more advanced image-to-image translation methods (e.g., GANs) that might offer even more realistic renderings.  A direct visual comparison would strengthen the claims about the quality of the synthesized images.
* **Action Unit Limitations:** The 1-of-3 encoding for rotation actions feels somewhat arbitrary and potentially limiting. Exploring more nuanced control mechanisms or continuous action spaces could be a fruitful direction.
* **Pose Unit Interpretation:**  The nature of the "pose units" remains somewhat opaque. More analysis into what information these units encode would be helpful. What kind of pose information is being captured and how does it relate to actual 3D pose parameters?
* **Overstated Disentanglement:** While the authors claim disentanglement, the experiments showing pose unit performance are less convincing.  The pose units perform poorly, and the conclusion that this demonstrates disentanglement feels somewhat weak. It might be more accurate to say they *attempt* to disentangle, and the success is primarily reflected in the identity units.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to 3D view synthesis.
* The weakly-supervised disentanglement aspect is a significant contribution.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The generalization capabilities of the approach are not sufficiently explored.
* The qualitative comparison to state-of-the-art rendering methods is missing.
* The claim of disentanglement may be overstated.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis. The recurrent formulation and weakly-supervised disentanglement are particularly compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** 

**Confidence:** 8/10
