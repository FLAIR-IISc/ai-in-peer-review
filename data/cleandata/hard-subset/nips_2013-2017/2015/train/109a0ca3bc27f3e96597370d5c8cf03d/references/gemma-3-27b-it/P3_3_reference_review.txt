## Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling approach to 3D view synthesis, leveraging a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for pose manifold traversal. The work addresses a challenging problem – generating novel views from a single image without explicit 3D reconstruction – and demonstrates promising results on both faces and chairs. The method’s ability to learn disentangled representations *without* supervised labels is a notable strength. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough ablation study and a deeper discussion of limitations, particularly regarding generalization to significantly different objects.  I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of 3D view synthesis from a single image, a long-standing challenge in both computer graphics and computer vision. It proposes a recurrent convolutional network that learns to apply rotations to an object by traversing a learned pose manifold. The core idea – using recurrent transformations with action units to control local movements on the manifold – is well-motivated and draws inspiration from human mental rotation experiments. 

The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the contribution within the existing literature. It appropriately highlights connections to transforming autoencoders, predictive gating pyramids, and disentangled representation learning.  The authors effectively distinguish their approach from existing methods (e.g., IGN, chair rendering networks) by emphasizing the recurrent structure, control signals, and weakly-supervised nature of the disentangling process. However, the mention of DeepStereo feels a bit tangential and could be trimmed.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The combination of recurrent networks, disentangled representations, and action units for controlling pose manifold traversal is a novel and interesting approach. It avoids the pitfalls of explicit 3D reconstruction.
* **Weakly-Supervised Disentangling:**  The ability to learn disentangled identity and pose representations without explicit supervision is a significant strength. This allows the model to generalize to new instances and perform view-invariant recognition.
* **Curriculum Learning:**  The use of curriculum learning to train the recurrent network is well-justified and demonstrably effective. The staged approach to increasing trajectory length makes the learning problem more tractable.
* **Promising Results:** The experimental results on both faces and chairs are encouraging. The synthesized images appear realistic, and the cross-view recognition experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the disentangled representations.  The quantitative comparisons with KNN are also strong.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology.  The figures are helpful in visualizing the network architecture and results.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the curriculum learning experiments are insightful, a more thorough ablation study would be beneficial. For example, how does performance change with different numbers of identity/pose units? What is the impact of the action unit encoding scheme (1-of-3)?  
* **Generalization Concerns:** The experiments are limited to faces and chairs.  While these are good test cases, it’s unclear how well the approach would generalize to other object categories with more complex shapes or textures. The paper doesn't adequately address this limitation.  Showing results on a more diverse set of objects (even if qualitatively) would strengthen the paper.
* **Pose Manifold Assumption:** The approach implicitly assumes a well-behaved pose manifold. This may not be true for all objects, particularly those with articulated parts or complex deformations. This assumption should be discussed more explicitly.
* **Computational Cost:** Recurrent networks can be computationally expensive to train and run. The paper does not discuss the computational resources required for the experiments, which could be a barrier to adoption.
* **Lack of User Study:** While the visual results are compelling, a user study to assess the perceptual quality of the synthesized images would provide more convincing evidence of the method’s effectiveness.



**4.  Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro-Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging and important problem.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates promising results on benchmark datasets.
* Learns disentangled representations without explicit supervision.
* Well-written and clear presentation.

**Con-Acceptance:**

* Limited ablation study.
* Generalization to other object categories is unclear.
* Implicit assumptions about the pose manifold.
* Computational cost is not discussed.
* Lack of user study.

**5. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Page 3:** "Equivalently, the action unit selects the matrix that transforms the input pose units to the output pose units." This sentence is a bit unclear. A more concrete explanation would be helpful.
* **Equation 1:** It would be beneficial to clarify the dimensions of the terms in the equation.
* **Figure 3 & 5:**  Adding a baseline (e.g., nearest neighbor) alongside the generated images would provide a clearer visual comparison.
* **Figure 8:** The error bars are quite large.  Providing more context about the data distribution would be helpful.
* **Section 4.5:** The interpolated chairs look very smooth. It would be interesting to see examples of more extreme interpolations and discuss their quality.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to 3D view synthesis. While some areas could be strengthened with additional experiments and discussion, the core contribution is significant and well-executed. I believe this work is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
