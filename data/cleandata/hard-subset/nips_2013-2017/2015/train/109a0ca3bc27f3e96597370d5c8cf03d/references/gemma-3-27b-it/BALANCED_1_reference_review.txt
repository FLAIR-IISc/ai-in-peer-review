This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image. The core idea is to model pose manifold traversal with recurrent units, allowing the network to synthesize views corresponding to rotations. The network is trained in a weakly-supervised manner, learning disentangled representations for identity and pose without explicit supervision. The authors demonstrate results on both faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs, showing both good synthesis quality and the ability to learn view-invariant features.

This work builds upon a rich body of literature spanning computer graphics and computer vision. The paper appropriately references works on 3D morphable models [Blanz & Vetter 2003], transforming autoencoders [Hinton et al. 2016], predictive gating pyramids [PGP 2016], and generative models for chairs [Dosovitskiy et al. 2015]. It also positions itself well within the broader context of disentangled representation learning [Reed et al. 2015, Cheung et al. 2017]. The authors do a good job of differentiating their approach from existing methods, highlighting the benefits of their recurrent architecture and weakly-supervised learning strategy. The connection to the human mental rotation experiments [Shepard & Metzler 1971] provides a useful psychological motivation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of recurrent neural networks with action units to model pose manifold traversal is a novel approach to 3D view synthesis. The idea of controlling the rotation via a sequence of small steps is well-motivated and allows for a more stable and controllable synthesis process.
* **Weakly-Supervised Learning:** Learning disentangled representations without explicit supervision is a significant achievement. The results suggest that the network is indeed learning to separate identity and pose information.
* **Good Empirical Results:** The qualitative and quantitative results on both faces and chairs are promising. The synthesized images look realistic, and the cross-view recognition experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the learned representations. The improvement over KNN baselines is significant.
* **Curriculum Learning:** The use of curriculum learning is well-justified and seems to be crucial for the success of the model.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experiments.  The figures are helpful and illustrate the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Transformations:** The method is currently limited to rotations. Extending it to other types of transformations (e.g., scaling, translation, deformation) would be a valuable extension.
* **Computational Cost:** Recurrent networks can be computationally expensive to train, especially with long sequences. The authors do not provide detailed information about the training time and computational resources required.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to KNN is useful, it would be more informative to compare the method to other state-of-the-art 3D view synthesis techniques, such as those based on differentiable rendering or neural radiance fields.  The IGN [Kulkarni et al. 2017] comparison is present, but a more thorough quantitative comparison would be beneficial.
* **Ablation Studies:** More ablation studies would be helpful to understand the contribution of different components of the model, such as the identity units, recurrent units, and action units.  For example, what happens if you remove the recurrent structure and attempt to predict the rotated image directly?
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successful examples. A more critical discussion of failure cases and limitations would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to 3D view synthesis.
* The results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas and experiments.
* The weakly-supervised learning aspect is a significant contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The scope of transformations is limited.
* The comparison to stronger baselines is lacking.
* The computational cost of training is not adequately addressed.
* The discussion of failure cases is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. However, the limited scope of transformations and the lack of comparison to stronger baselines are concerns.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept**. The novelty and potential of the approach outweigh the weaknesses, but the authors should consider addressing the concerns raised in the review in future work. Specifically, extending the method to other types of transformations and comparing it to more recent and competitive baselines would significantly strengthen the paper.  Adding more ablation studies and discussing failure cases would also improve the presentation.
