

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D view rotations from single images without explicit 3D model recovery. The authors frame the problem as pose manifold traversal, where the model learns to apply small sequential rotations to generate novel views. Their key innovations include a recurrent structure with action units for pose transformations and a curriculum learning approach to handle long-term dependencies. The model learns disentangled representations of identity and pose without explicit supervision, which the authors demonstrate through cross-view recognition experiments.

The paper builds on related work in view synthesis (e.g., [8, 17]), disentangled representations (e.g., [24, 21]), and mental rotation modeling (e.g., [7]). It distinguishes itself by using a recurrent architecture with action inputs for sequential pose transformations rather than predicting a single rotated view, and by demonstrating weakly-supervised disentangling through the learning process itself.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel approach to view synthesis:** The recurrent transformation framework with action units provides a clean solution to the sequential nature of pose manifold traversal, addressing the long-term dependency challenge that would be problematic for standard RNNs.

2. **Effective curriculum learning:** The staged training approach (RNN1, RNN2, etc.) is well-motivated and clearly demonstrates how longer training sequences improve both image synthesis quality and disentangled feature learning.

3. **Strong empirical results:** The paper provides comprehensive experiments on two datasets (faces and chairs) showing consistent improvements over baselines (KNN, CNN, 3D morphable model), with particularly strong results for longer rotation sequences (RNN16 vs. RNN1).

4. **Compelling disentanglement demonstration:** The cross-view recognition experiments (Figure 8) convincingly show that identity units become increasingly pose-invariant with curriculum training, while pose units become increasingly identity-agnostic (Table 1).

5. **Clear architecture and implementation details:** The network design is well-explained with sufficient technical details for reproducibility, and the ablation studies (Figure 6, 7) effectively demonstrate the value of curriculum learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited comparison with related work:** While the paper cites IGN as a related approach, it doesn't thoroughly compare with it. The authors claim their model differs in "training a recurrent network to perform trajectories of multiple transformations" and "using deterministic feed-forward training," but don't demonstrate how this translates to better performance or why their approach is superior.

2. **Insufficient explanation of key design choices:** The paper doesn't clearly explain why their sequential prediction approach (predicting intermediate views) is better than simply predicting the final rotated view with a standard RNN. This is a critical design decision that needs stronger justification.

3. **Underdeveloped analysis of limitations:** The paper notes that VGG-16 outperforms their model for large angle offsets on chairs due to chair symmetry, but doesn't explore how their approach might be modified to better handle symmetric objects. This is an important limitation for practical applications.

4. **Incomplete disentanglement analysis:** While the paper demonstrates that identity units become pose-invariant, it doesn't provide a quantitative measure of disentanglement quality compared to methods specifically designed for disentanglement (e.g., [6, 21]).

5. **Lack of qualitative analysis for failure cases:** The paper shows successful results but doesn't discuss common failure modes (e.g., when the model produces artifacts during rotation) or analyze why these occur.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of view synthesis with a novel approach to pose manifold traversal. The methodology is technically sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines. The weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly noteworthy.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the following issues before acceptance:
1. Provide a more detailed comparison with IGN, including a discussion of why their approach is superior.
2. Justify the sequential prediction framework more thoroughly, potentially by showing ablation studies comparing it to a single-step prediction approach.
3. Expand the analysis of limitations, particularly regarding symmetric objects, and suggest potential extensions to handle these cases.
4. Include a quantitative measure of disentanglement quality to better contextualize the weakly-supervised aspect.

The paper is of high quality and addresses an important problem in computer vision. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
