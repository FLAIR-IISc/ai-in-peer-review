

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis that learns to apply rotations to objects from a single image without explicit 3D reconstruction. The authors frame the problem as pose manifold traversal, using a recurrent network with action units to model incremental rotations, and employ curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation sequence length. The key contributions include a novel network architecture, the ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories, and weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision with potential applications in photo editing and reinforcement learning. The recurrent architecture with action units is a reasonable approach for modeling sequential transformations. The curriculum learning strategy is well-motivated and shows clear benefits in experiments. The weakly-supervised disentangling claim is interesting, and the cross-view recognition results for faces are promising. The experimental setup is generally sound, with appropriate datasets and evaluation metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological inconsistencies and unclear notation:** Equation (1) contains a critical typo: "fpose(x(i), a(i), t)" should be "f_pose(x(i), a(i), t)" to match the description, but the paper inconsistently uses both notations. The action unit encoding is confusingly described: "[100] encoded a clockwise rotation, [010] encoded a noop, and [001] encoded a counter-clockwise rotation." This is counterintuitive as [100] would typically represent the first action, not clockwise rotation. The "triangle" tensor product notation (Figure 1) is non-standard and not explained.

2. **Overstated claims about disentanglement:** The paper claims "weakly-supervised disentangling" and that "the model learns discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels." However, the paper provides no quantitative disentanglement metrics (e.g., DCI score) or ablation studies demonstrating that the features are truly disentangled. The cross-view recognition results are presented as evidence of disentanglement, but this is insufficient as recognition performance can be achieved with entangled representations.

3. **Inadequate baselines and evaluation:** The KNN baseline for chair dataset is inappropriate for generative modeling tasks. KNN retrieves similar images but cannot synthesize novel views, making the comparison unfair. The paper also fails to compare against recent 3D view synthesis methods like Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) or 3D-GANs, which have become standard baselines in the field. The cross-view recognition for chairs uses angle offsets up to 174°, which is extremely challenging and likely not representative of practical applications.

4. **Insufficient experimental details:** The paper claims "we found that fixed stride-2 convolution and upsampling worked better than max-pooling" but provides no ablation study to support this claim. The training details for chair dataset are incomplete—no mention of batch size, optimizer momentum, or weight decay. The claim "we did not find it necessary to use gradient clipping" for recurrent networks is surprising and requires justification, especially given the long sequences.

5. **Formatting and writing issues:** The paper contains inconsistent notation (e.g., "fpose" vs. "f_pose"), grammatical errors ("the resulting mesh can be used to re-render the scene from novel viewpoints" should be "re-render the scene from novel viewpoints"), and formatting inconsistencies in references (e.g., "SIGGRAPH,," with two commas). The paper also fails to clearly differentiate its approach from the Inverse Graphics Network (IGN), which has similar architecture and goals.

6. **Lack of theoretical grounding:** While the paper is primarily empirical, it lacks any theoretical analysis of why the recurrent structure with curriculum learning works better than alternatives. The connection to Shepard and Metzler's mental rotation experiments is superficial and not leveraged to inform the model design.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents a reasonable approach to 3D view synthesis with some promising results, the technical inconsistencies, overstated claims, and insufficient evaluation significantly undermine its contribution. The weakly-supervised disentangling claim is particularly problematic without proper disentanglement metrics. The KNN baseline comparison for chair synthesis is inappropriate, and the paper fails to compare against more recent 3D view synthesis methods.

The paper has a clear problem statement and reasonable methodology, but the execution contains multiple technical flaws that prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. The minor methodological details and formatting inconsistencies, while seemingly small, collectively indicate a lack of rigor that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial methodological flaws and the paper's failure to properly validate its key claims. The authors should address these issues through additional experiments, theoretical analysis, and clearer presentation before resubmission.
