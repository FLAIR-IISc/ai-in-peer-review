Summary
The paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture for 3D view synthesis from a single image. The key idea is to disentangle latent identity units (shared across time) and pose units (recurrently updated) and to control pose traversal via simple “action units” that select a learned linear transformation of the pose state at each step. The model encodes the input once, then performs all subsequent rotations in the latent space, decoding at each step. Training uses pixel-wise losses, and curriculum learning is employed by gradually increasing sequence length, enabling longer and more stable rotations. Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and synthetic Chairs show: (a) realistic multi-step rotations; (b) cross-view recognition using identity units without class supervision (“weakly-supervised disentangling”); and (c) identity interpolation combined with pose traversal.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on: convolutional encoder-decoders for image generation (Dosovitskiy et al., generative chairs), transforming autoencoders/capsules (Hinton et al.), gating models (PGP, transforming distance), and contemporaneous inverse graphics networks (Kulkarni et al.). The contributions relative to these are the recurrent control of pose traversal, action-conditioned latent updates, curriculum training for long trajectories, and empirical evidence of disentanglement without explicit regularizers. The paper situates itself well, though it does not provide experimental comparisons to some closely related controllable transformation models (e.g., PGP, IGN), and omits Spatial Transformer Networks (Jaderberg et al.) in the related work.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea: sharing identity units across time and recurrently updating pose units with action-conditioned linear maps is elegant and scalable.
- Curriculum learning is well-motivated and empirically improves both synthesis fidelity and identity disentanglement.
- Solid qualitative results on faces and chairs; trajectories are smooth and maintain identity over many steps.
- Quantitative improvements over KNN on chairs (MSE reduction ~42%), and competitive cross-view recognition with identity units despite no class supervision.
- Interpolation experiments suggest a smooth identity manifold coupled with controllable pose traversal.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited baselines. On chairs, the main quantitative comparison is a simple KNN; there is no head-to-head with contemporaneous controllable generative models (e.g., IGN) or time-series transformation models (PGP), nor with a non-recurrent encoder-decoder that directly predicts a target view given a relative rotation. For faces, the 3DMM qualitative comparison is informative but not comprehensive.
- Evaluation metrics. MSE is a weak perceptual metric; no SSIM/LPIPS, user studies, or FID-like measures are reported. Cross-view recognition is encouraging, but disentanglement claims would benefit from more direct analyses (e.g., mutual information, linear separability of pose from identity, or interventions).
- Scope and generalization. Faces are aligned, neutral illumination, and limited yaw (±45°). Chairs are synthetic and have strong symmetries. Generalization to in-the-wild images, to other transformations (e.g., elevation, scale, lighting), and to occlusions is not evaluated.
- Ambiguity and multimodality. The method is deterministic with an L2 loss; hallucination of unseen surfaces and multimodal futures (self-occlusions, textures) are not addressed, which likely biases results toward blur in harder settings.
- Method details. The action “tensor product” is described qualitatively; more precise specification (dimensions, parameterization, whether maps are shared across instances, and capacity of the pose-state transition) would improve reproducibility. The effect of the mask loss on chairs is not ablated.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with clear training protocol and reasonable experimental design for the chosen datasets. Claims about curriculum-driven disentanglement are supported by cross-view recognition trends and pose/identity unit analysis, albeit indirectly. Missing ablations (no recurrence, feeding previous images, action granularity, mask-loss impact) and stronger baselines limit the strength of the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. Architecture and training schedules are described with sufficient detail to implement, including layer sizes and optimization. Some notational ambiguity remains around the action-conditioned transition (“tensor product”), and a few minor typos are present. Figures (as described) appear to support the claims.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of a recurrent latent pose controller with shared identity units and curriculum learning for long rotations is novel relative to referenced work, and the demonstration of weakly supervised disentanglement emerging from the training objective is interesting. However, components (encoder-decoders, gating, action-conditioning) are known, and concurrent work (IGN) tackles related inverse graphics tasks.

Significance
View synthesis from single images and learning view-invariant representations are important problems. The paper advances the state of practice for multi-step view control with a simple, effective architecture and shows that useful identity features can emerge without labels. Impact is somewhat tempered by limited baselines, datasets, and metrics, but the approach is likely to inspire follow-on work (e.g., broader actions, probabilistic decoders, better metrics).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines: IGN-style encoder-decoder with direct target-view conditioning; PGP-like transformation models; a non-recurrent model trained to predict T-step rotations in one shot; STN-based baselines.
- Report perceptual metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) and, if possible, a small user study.
- Provide ablations: without curriculum; without recurrence (single-step repeated); feeding predicted images as inputs; varying action step size; with/without mask loss for chairs.
- Clarify the action-transition parameterization and provide full implementation details; release code.
- Expand to elevation/lighting changes and demonstrate on less controlled, real images.
- Include an explicit disentanglement analysis (e.g., probing classifiers, MI estimates, linear subspace tests).

Minor comments
- Cite Spatial Transformer Networks (Jaderberg et al., NIPS 2015) as related controllable transformation work.
- Clarify how end-of-range views are handled in Multi-PIE sequence sampling.
- Discuss runtime and memory; the curriculum is limited by GPU memory at T=16.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated recurrent architecture with action control and curriculum learning.
- Strong qualitative results and clear improvement with longer curricula.
- Evidence that identity-pose disentanglement emerges without labels.
- Addresses an important problem with potential impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines and metrics; comparisons do not include the most relevant contemporary models.
- Restricted datasets and transformation types; uncertain generalization to real-world scenarios.
- Deterministic L2 training may not handle multimodality or fine texture synthesis.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a clear and useful architectural idea with solid empirical support on two benchmarks and makes a plausible case for weakly supervised disentanglement. Strengthening baselines and evaluation would raise confidence, but the contribution is likely of interest to the NeurIPS community.
