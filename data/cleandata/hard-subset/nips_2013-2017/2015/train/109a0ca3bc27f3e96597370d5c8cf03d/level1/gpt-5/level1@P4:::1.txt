Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses single-image 3D view synthesis by learning to render rotated views of an object (faces, chairs) using a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network. The key idea is to traverse a pose manifold via a sequence of small rotations, controlled by discrete “action units” (clockwise, noop, counterclockwise). The model splits a latent code into identity units (shared across timesteps) and recurrent pose units (updated by actions), and is trained end-to-end with an L2 image reconstruction loss over multiple steps. Curriculum learning increases the rotation sequence length during training to improve long-horizon predictions and promote disentanglement of pose and identity. The learned identity units are then evaluated for cross-view recognition without class labels; the model also demonstrates identity interpolation.

This work sits among prior generative/transformational models: transforming autoencoders (Hinton et al.), Predictive Gating Pyramid and other gated models for learning transformations, Dosovitskiy et al.’s chair generator (decoder-only with labeled factors), and the contemporaneous Inverse Graphics Network (Kulkarni et al.) which uses a VAE encoder-decoder with graphics codes. Compared to these, this paper emphasizes: (1) recurrent composition of small actions to realize long rotations; (2) weakly-supervised disentangling emerging from the training objective without identity labels; and (3) curriculum training for stability and performance. It also relates to disentangling approaches (bilinear models, disBM, multi-view perceptron) and to view synthesis from multiple images (DeepStereo), though the input setting differs.

Strengths:
- Technical formulation: A clear latent factorization into identity and pose with a simple, trainable, action-conditioned recurrent update. The choice to keep identity units constant across the sequence and to operate transformations only in latent space is well-motivated.
- Curriculum learning: Systematic curriculum on sequence length improves both synthesis quality and identity invariance; the ablation across RNN1/2/4/8/16 is informative.
- Empirical evidence of disentanglement: Cross-view recognition using identity units (without labels) improves with longer curricula and is competitive with a supervised CNN on Multi-PIE; pose units become non-discriminative of identity when identity units improve, indicating factor separation.
- Generalization and interpolation: Successful synthesis on unseen faces/chairs and smooth identity interpolation with consistent multi-view rendering.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Architectures, dataset splits, action encoding, and training schedules are described with sufficient detail for reimplementation.

Weaknesses:
- Baselines and metrics: Quantitative evaluation relies heavily on per-pixel MSE and a relatively weak KNN baseline for chairs; no comparisons to closely related learned baselines (e.g., IGN/variational encoder-decoders, PGP), nor perceptual metrics (SSIM) or user studies, which would better capture visual quality than L2.
- Limited scope of transformations: Only rotation is considered; actions are discrete and limited to ±1 step per time, with no demonstration of continuous control or other factors (illumination, scale, deformation).
- Dataset constraints and resolution: Experiments are limited to aligned Multi-PIE faces and synthetic chair renderings at low resolution; generalization to in-the-wild images or cluttered scenes is not evaluated.
- Under-specified recurrence: The exact parameterization of the action-conditioned pose update is only briefly described (“tensor product”, action-selects-matrix). A more explicit formulation and ablations (e.g., non-recurrent, feed-forward multi-step; teacher forcing vs. closed-loop) would strengthen technical clarity.
- Fairness of recognition comparisons: On chairs, VGG-16 fc7 features serve as the baseline, while for faces a supervised CNN is trained on the same data; a more consistent comparison across domains would be preferable.

Quality:
Overall technically sound. The training objective, architecture, and curriculum are appropriate, and the experiments demonstrate clear trends (longer curricula improve long-horizon synthesis and disentanglement). Claims are supported by qualitative sequences and quantitative MSE/recognition results. Some experimental choices limit conclusiveness (lack of strong generative baselines; reliance on MSE).

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized, with explicit architectural details, training schedules, and dataset preparation. Figures (rotation trajectories, curriculum ablation) convey results effectively. The recurrence/action mechanism could be more formally specified, and training stability details (e.g., parameter counts, convergence diagnostics) could be expanded, but overall the manuscript is clear.

Originality:
Moderate. The combination of an action-conditioned recurrent encoder-decoder with weakly supervised disentangling and curriculum for long rotations is a meaningful step beyond single-step transformation networks and decoder-only generators. Contemporary work (IGN) targets similar goals with different training (VAE) and without recurrent composition; prior gated models consider transformations but with different architectures and datasets.

Significance:
Moderate. The paper advances learned view synthesis from single images within constrained domains and shows emergent disentanglement useful for view-invariant recognition. The approach is likely to influence subsequent work on controllable generation and learned graphics pipelines. Impact is tempered by dataset simplicity and limited baselines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add stronger baselines: a non-recurrent encoder-decoder trained to predict multi-step rotations, a VAE-based model (e.g., IGN-style), or a gated transformation model; evaluate with SSIM or perceptual distances.
- Provide a precise mathematical description of the action-conditioned pose update and test continuous action parametrizations.
- Explore additional transformations (e.g., larger angle steps, illumination) or demonstrate transfer to less aligned, real-world imagery.
- Include ablations on identity/pose unit sizes and mask branch contribution.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-executed idea with a novel recurrent, action-conditioned architecture for multi-step view synthesis.
- Demonstrated weakly supervised disentanglement with measurable benefits for cross-view recognition.
- Strong qualitative results and informative curriculum ablations.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited and partially mismatched baselines; evaluation relies on MSE and lacks perceptual metrics or stronger comparators.
- Experiments restricted to aligned faces and synthetic chairs at low resolution; unclear robustness to real-world conditions.
- Some architectural details (pose update) under-specified; limited analysis of design choices.

Overall, this is a solid contribution with clear methodology and evidence, offering incremental but meaningful advances in learned 3D view synthesis and disentanglement.
