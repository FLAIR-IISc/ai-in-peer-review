## Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of 3D view synthesis from a single image, focusing on faces and chairs. The authors propose a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network trained end-to-end to render rotated objects. The recurrent structure aims to capture long-term dependencies during transformations, and a key contribution is the demonstration of disentangling latent factors without explicit class labels.

The work builds upon a rich history of approaches to 3D reconstruction and view synthesis. It acknowledges geometry-based methods (multi-view stereo, fitting to prior 3D models like [5]) and their limitations, positioning itself within the growing trend of learning-based approaches ([8, 17]). The use of recurrent networks for sequential transformations is inspired by work on time series prediction ([19]) and mental rotation phenomena ([22]). The authors appropriately situate their work relative to recent advances like transforming autoencoders ([12]) and inverse graphics networks ([17]), highlighting their differences in architecture (convolutional vs. capsules), control signals, and training paradigms.

**Strengths:** The proposed approach is conceptually sound and addresses a difficult problem. The recurrent structure is a sensible choice for handling sequential transformations, and the results on both faces and chairs are visually compelling. The claim of weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly interesting and supported by the cross-view recognition experiments. The curriculum learning strategy appears effective, as demonstrated by the performance improvements with increasing sequence length. The ablation studies and comparisons to KNN baselines are valuable.

**Weaknesses:** While the results are promising, the paper lacks a detailed quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for 3D view synthesis. The comparison to the 3D morphable model [5] is limited. The explanation of *why* the recurrent structure aids in disentangling is somewhat hand-wavy; a more thorough analysis of the latent space would be beneficial. The discussion of the VGG-16 performance on chairs suggests a potential limitation related to symmetry, but this isn’t fully explored. The paper could benefit from a discussion of computational cost and scalability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of recurrent networks to 3D view synthesis.
*   Demonstrated ability to learn disentangled representations without explicit supervision.
*   Visually appealing and quantitatively supported results on two datasets.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited quantitative comparison to strong baselines in 3D view synthesis.
*   Lack of in-depth analysis of the latent space and disentangling mechanism.
*   Potential scalability concerns not addressed.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with a clear presentation and promising results. While not a breakthrough, the work offers a novel and effective approach to 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. I recommend **acceptance**, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in a future revision.
