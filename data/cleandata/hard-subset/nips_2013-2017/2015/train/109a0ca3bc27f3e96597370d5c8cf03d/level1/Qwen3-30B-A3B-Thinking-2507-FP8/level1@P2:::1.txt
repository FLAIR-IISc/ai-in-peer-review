

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing 3D views from a single image, specifically addressing the challenge of learning to apply multiple sequential rotations. The authors build upon previous work in view synthesis (e.g., IGN [17], transforming autoencoders [12], and chair generation [8]) by introducing a recurrent structure to handle long-term dependencies in pose manifold traversal. The key innovation is the use of curriculum learning to progressively train the model on longer rotation trajectories, which enables both high-quality view synthesis and the emergence of disentangled identity and pose features without explicit regularization.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The model is well-motivated to address the long-term dependency problem in sequential rotation prediction, and the authors provide thorough experimental validation across two datasets (faces on Multi-PIE and chairs from CAD models). The curriculum learning approach is particularly well-executed, with the authors systematically increasing training sequence length (from RNN1 to RNN16) and showing clear improvements in both image synthesis quality (Figures 3-7) and disentangled representation learning (Figures 8-9). The quantitative results (MSE comparisons in Figure 7) are convincing, showing RNN16 outperforms KNN baselines by 42%. However, the paper could strengthen its theoretical grounding by more explicitly discussing why the recurrent structure is necessary for long sequences, and how the model avoids the "manifold drift" problem that plagues sequential prediction (the authors mention this issue but don't provide a detailed analysis).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The model description (Section 3) is precise, with helpful figures (Figures 1-2) that clarify the architecture. The experimental setup (Section 4) is meticulously detailed, including dataset specifics, network architectures, and training procedures. The qualitative results (Figures 3-9) are well-chosen to demonstrate key capabilities: view synthesis quality, disentangling properties, and interpolation capabilities. The paper would benefit from slightly more discussion of failure cases (e.g., where the model struggles with highly complex chair shapes), but overall it meets the standard of being reproducible for an expert reader.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While previous work has explored view synthesis (IGN [17], [8]) and disentanglement (e.g., bilinear models [24], disBM [21]), the specific combination of recurrent structure for sequential rotation prediction, curriculum learning, and the resulting weakly-supervised disentanglement is novel. The authors properly distinguish their work from related approaches (Section 2), particularly noting how their model differs from IGN in using deterministic feed-forward training rather than VAEs, and how their recurrent structure handles long sequences better than previous methods. The "weakly-supervised" disentanglement claim is particularly noteworthy, as it emerges from the training objective rather than explicit regularization.

**Significance:** This work addresses an important problem with clear applications in computer graphics and vision. The ability to synthesize novel views from single images has implications for photo editing, virtual try-ons, and vision-based reinforcement learning. The demonstrated disentanglement of identity and pose features without class labels is a significant contribution to representation learning, with potential applications in view-invariant recognition. The results on both faces and chairs (two very different object classes) demonstrate the approach's generality, and the interpolation results (Figure 9) show the model's ability to generate novel objects. The paper advances the state of the art in view synthesis by providing a more robust method that handles longer rotation sequences than previous approaches.

**Strengths:**
- Clear technical contribution with a novel recurrent approach for sequential view synthesis
- Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets and metrics
- Effective use of curriculum learning to handle long-term dependencies
- Demonstrated emergence of disentangled representations without explicit regularization
- Well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of failure cases and limitations (e.g., complex chair shapes)
- The "weakly-supervised disentangling" claim could be more precisely qualified
- Comparison to IGN is limited, despite IGN being the most directly related work
- The paper doesn't explore the computational efficiency of their approach compared to alternatives

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of view synthesis and representation learning. It addresses a significant problem with a technically sound approach that demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The results are thorough, the writing is clear, and the contribution is sufficiently novel compared to previous NIPS work. The weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly noteworthy and could inspire future work on unsupervised representation learning. While there are minor weaknesses in the discussion of limitations, these don't undermine the paper's substantial contribution to the field.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS, as it makes a scientifically rigorous contribution to a problem of broad interest, with clear significance for both vision and graphics research. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides unique insights into how disentangled representations can emerge from a simple training objective.
