This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image. The core idea is to model pose manifold traversal via a recurrent network, with action units controlling local rotations at each time step. The model is trained end-to-end on faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs, demonstrating the ability to generate rotated views and, surprisingly, learn disentangled representations without explicit supervision.

The work builds upon several existing lines of research. It connects to traditional graphics rendering techniques, but aims to bypass explicit 3D model recovery. It leverages recent advances in convolutional networks for image generation (Dosovitskiy et al., 2015; Kulkarni et al., 2015) and incorporates recurrent structures inspired by sequence modeling (e.g., Michalski et al., 2014) and the observation of mental rotation in humans (Shepard & Metzler, 1971). The paper appropriately cites related work, including transforming autoencoders (Hinton et al., 2011), predictive gating pyramids (van den Oord et al., 2016) and disentangled representation learning (Reed et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of recurrent networks with convolutional encoders/decoders for continuous 3D view synthesis is a reasonable and interesting approach. The use of action units to control pose manifold traversal is a clever idea.
* **Technical Soundness:** The network architecture appears well-motivated, and the curriculum learning strategy is a sensible way to address the difficulty of learning long-term dependencies. The experiments are reasonably thorough, using standard datasets (Multi-PIE, chairs) and comparing against baselines (KNN, CNN).
* **Results:** The qualitative results (figures 3, 5, 9) look promising, demonstrating the ability to generate plausible rotated views. The quantitative results (figure 7, table 1) support the claim that the model learns disentangled representations and achieves competitive performance.
* **Disentanglement:** The emergence of disentangled features without explicit supervision is a noteworthy finding, suggesting that the recurrent structure encourages the network to separate pose and identity information.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the method is explained in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** While the paper includes some baseline comparisons, they are not exhaustive. For example, comparing against more sophisticated 3D morphable models (Blanz & Vetter, 1999) for face rotation would strengthen the evaluation. A more thorough comparison to recent generative models like GANs would also be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions curriculum learning, but provides limited details on how the curriculum was designed and the sensitivity of the results to different curriculum schedules.
* **Computational Cost:** Training recurrent networks can be computationally expensive. The paper doesn’t explicitly address the computational resources required for training.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It would be helpful to include ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the model, such as the recurrent structure, action units, and curriculum learning. For example, what happens if you remove the recurrent connections? Or if you replace the action units with a different mechanism for controlling pose?
* **Qualitative Evaluation:** The qualitative assessment primarily focuses on visualization. While the renderings look plausible, a more rigorous evaluation of image quality (e.g., using metrics like SSIM or PSNR) would be beneficial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to 3D view synthesis.
* The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and the emergence of disentangled representations.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
* The work addresses a challenging problem with potential applications in graphics and vision.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The baseline comparisons are limited.
* The paper lacks detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.
* The absence of ablation studies makes it difficult to assess the contribution of individual components.
* The evaluation relies heavily on qualitative assessment.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. The proposed approach is novel and well-motivated, and the results are promising. While there are some weaknesses, they are not major enough to warrant rejection. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider adding more comprehensive baseline comparisons, providing more details on the curriculum learning schedule, and including ablation studies to better understand the contribution of different model components. They should also include quantitative image quality metrics.
