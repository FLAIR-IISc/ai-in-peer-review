## Review of “Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of 3D view synthesis from a single image, focusing on faces and chairs. The authors propose a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network designed to learn pose manifold traversal through a series of small rotations. The key idea is to use a recurrent structure to capture long-term dependencies during these transformations, enabling the model to synthesize plausible novel views. Importantly, the model achieves this without explicit 3D supervision or object class labels, performing weakly-supervised disentangling of latent factors.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in 3D vision, generative modeling, and recurrent neural networks. It acknowledges relevant approaches like transforming autoencoders [12], Predictive Gating Pyramids [19], and generative CNNs for chairs [8]. It positions itself as an alternative to geometry-based methods [5] and recent inverse graphics networks [17], offering a purely learning-based approach.  The connection to mental rotation experiments [22] is also cleverly used to motivate the recurrent structure and action-based control. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of recurrent networks with action units for controlled 3D view synthesis is a promising approach. The weakly-supervised disentangling aspect is particularly interesting.
* **Technical Soundness:** The network architecture seems well-designed and the use of curriculum learning is a sensible choice for training recurrent models. The experimental setup with Multi-PIE and the chairs dataset is appropriate.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates visually compelling results for both faces and chairs, and the quantitative comparisons (MSE, cross-view recognition) support the claims of improved performance, especially with the full RNN16 model.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the method and experimental details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** While KNN is used for chairs, the comparison could be strengthened by including more state-of-the-art generative models or view synthesis techniques for a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Disentangling Evaluation:** While the authors show improved recognition performance, a more rigorous evaluation of disentangling could be beneficial. Metrics like mutual information could quantify the independence of identity and pose representations.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The report lacks details on the sensitivity of hyperparameters like learning rate and the size of latent spaces to help understand the robustness of the findings. 
* **Computational Cost**: The training of the recurrent model, especially RNN16, seems computationally expensive, which could limit its scalability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.
* The weakly-supervised disentangling aspect is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to existing methods could be more thorough.
* The evaluation of disentangling is somewhat limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While some aspects could be strengthened, the novelty, technical soundness, and promising empirical results make it worthy of acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on strengthening the baselines and providing a more in-depth analysis of the disentangling properties of the learned representations.
