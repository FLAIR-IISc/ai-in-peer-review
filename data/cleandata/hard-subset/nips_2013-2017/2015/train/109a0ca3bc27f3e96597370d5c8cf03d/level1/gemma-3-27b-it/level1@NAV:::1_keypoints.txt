- Problem: 3D view synthesis from a single image for faces and chairs using a recurrent conv encoder-decoder with action units for small rotations, trained weakly without 3D labels.
- Key idea: recurrent structure to traverse pose manifold via sequential small rotations, capturing long-term dependencies for plausible novel-view synthesis.
- Relation to prior work: builds on transforming autoencoders, predictive gating pyramids, generative CNNs, and connects to mental rotation experiments.
- Novelty: combining recurrent networks with action-based control for controlled 3D view synthesis and weakly-supervised disentangling.
- Technical soundness: well-designed architecture and sensible use of curriculum learning.
- Datasets/experimental setup: appropriate evaluation on Multi-PIE (faces) and chairs dataset.
- Empirical results: visually compelling outputs and improved quantitative metrics (MSE, cross-view recognition), especially for the full RNN16 model.
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written and organized with clear method and experimental descriptions.
- Weakness — limited baselines: comparisons are weak (only KNN for chairs); needs more state-of-the-art generative/view-synthesis baselines.
- Weakness — disentangling evaluation: lacks rigorous metrics (e.g., mutual information) to quantify identity vs. pose independence.
- Weakness — hyperparameter sensitivity: missing analysis of sensitivity to learning rate, latent size, etc., to assess robustness.
- Weakness — computational cost: training, particularly RNN16, appears computationally expensive and may limit scalability.
- For acceptance: addresses a significant problem with a novel, well-motivated approach and promising empirical evidence for weakly-supervised disentangling.
- Against acceptance: insufficient comparative baselines and limited evaluation of disentangling.
- Overall recommendation: solid contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen baselines and provide deeper disentangling analysis.
