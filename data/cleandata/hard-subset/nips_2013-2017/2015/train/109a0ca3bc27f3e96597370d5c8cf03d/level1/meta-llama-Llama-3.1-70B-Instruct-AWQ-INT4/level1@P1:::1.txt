The paper "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis" proposes a novel approach to synthesizing novel views of 3D objects from a single image. The authors introduce a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network that can capture long-term dependencies along a sequence of transformations, allowing it to generate realistic rotation trajectories with a control signal supplied at each step. The network is trained end-to-end on the task of rendering rotated objects starting from a single image, without requiring explicit 3D model recovery.

The main contributions of this work are the development of a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network, the ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories with a control signal, and the learning of discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the Multi-PIE dataset and a database of 3D chair CAD models, showing that their model can predict accurate renderings across trajectories of repeated rotations.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' argument and understand the proposed approach. The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the current state of the art in 3D view synthesis and disentangling.

One of the strengths of the paper is the use of a recurrent neural network to model the process of pose manifold traversal. This allows the model to capture long-term dependencies along a sequence of transformations, which is essential for generating realistic rotation trajectories. The authors also propose a novel curriculum training procedure, which gradually increases the difficulty of training by increasing the trajectory length. This approach is shown to be effective in improving the model's performance on both image prediction and pose-invariant recognition.

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the ability of the model to generate high-quality renderings of novel objects and to learn discriminative view-invariant features. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods, such as the 3D morphable model for face pose normalization, shows that the proposed approach is competitive and often outperforms existing methods.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the assumption that the object is isolated and can be rendered against a white background. This assumption may not hold in real-world scenarios, where objects are often embedded in complex scenes. Additionally, the model is only trained on a limited number of object classes (faces and chairs), and it is unclear how well it would generalize to other object classes.

Another limitation is the lack of explicit regularization to achieve disentangling. While the authors argue that the recurrent rotation prediction objective is sufficient to encourage disentangling, it is unclear how robust this approach is and whether it would work in other scenarios.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and effective approach to 3D view synthesis and disentangling. The use of a recurrent neural network and curriculum training procedure is a significant contribution to the field, and the experimental results demonstrate the potential of this approach.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to 3D view synthesis and disentangling.
