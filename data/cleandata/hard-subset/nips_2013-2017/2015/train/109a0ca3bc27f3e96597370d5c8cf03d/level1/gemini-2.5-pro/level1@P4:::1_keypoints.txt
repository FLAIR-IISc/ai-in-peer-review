- Problem & approach: Recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder models large 3D rotations as sequences of small incremental transformations using separate identity and pose latent units plus action inputs.  
- Training: Model trained end-to-end to predict image sequences with curriculum learning (short→long sequences).  
- Datasets: Evaluated on Multi-PIE faces and rendered 3D chairs.  
- Key contribution: Weakly-supervised disentanglement of identity and pose emerges from the sequential prediction objective without explicit labels or regularization.  
- Originality/significance: Novel, well-motivated combination of encoder-decoder and recurrent structure inspired by mental rotation.  
- Technical quality: Sound methodology with thorough qualitative and quantitative experiments and sensible curriculum training.  
- Ablation/results: Curriculum and longer sequence training (RNN1→RNN16) improve recognition and generation quality.  
- Disentanglement evidence: Identity units perform well on cross-view recognition; pose units perform near chance, supporting disentanglement claims.  
- Clarity issue: The action-to-pose transformation (described as a "tensor product") lacks a clear mathematical definition and implementation detail.  
- Scope limitation: Experiments are limited to centered, aligned single-object images with simple backgrounds; generalization to in-the-wild scenes is untested.  
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited (k-NN for chairs, 3DMM for faces); missing a non-recurrent single-step baseline to isolate recurrence benefits.  
- Texture fidelity: Generated faces sometimes lose texture detail compared to 3DMM reconstructions.  
- Reproducibility concern: Insufficient detail on the action mechanism and transformation matrices could hinder replication.  
- Overall verdict: High-quality, novel, and empirically strong work whose strengths outweigh weaknesses and is likely to inspire future research.
