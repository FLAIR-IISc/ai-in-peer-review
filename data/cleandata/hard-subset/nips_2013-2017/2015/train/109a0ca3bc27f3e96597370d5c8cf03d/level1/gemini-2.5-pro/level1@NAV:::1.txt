This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of an object from a single input image. The core idea is to disentangle an object's representation into view-invariant "identity" features and view-dependent "pose" features. A recurrent network then operates on the pose features, taking discrete actions (e.g., rotate clockwise) to predict a sequence of transformed views. The model is trained end-to-end using a curriculum learning strategy that gradually increases the length of the rotation trajectories. This work builds upon existing encoder-decoder architectures for image generation, such as the Inverse Graphics Network (Kulkarni et al., 2015), but makes a key contribution by introducing a recurrent structure to model long-term transformation sequences, a problem not addressed by single-step transformation models. The emergent, weakly-supervised disentangling of factors also connects to prior work in representation learning, but achieves it through the synthesis task itself rather than explicit regularization.

The paper has several significant strengths.
*   **Originality and Significance:** The use of an RNN to model pose manifold traversal by composing small, controlled transformations is an elegant and novel approach to the view synthesis problem. This allows for the generation of long, coherent sequences of views, which is a clear advance. The most compelling result is the weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose. The experiments convincingly show that by training on longer rotation sequences, the identity features become more view-invariant and suitable for recognition, achieving performance competitive with a fully-supervised network without using any identity labels.
*   **Quality:** The experimental validation is thorough and well-executed on two distinct datasets (Multi-PIE faces and synthetic chairs). The authors provide strong qualitative results, quantitative comparisons against a k-NN baseline, and insightful ablation-like studies demonstrating the benefit of their curriculum learning strategy on both synthesis quality and recognition performance. The claims are well-supported by the evidence provided.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, and the model architecture is well-explained with helpful diagrams.

The paper also has some minor weaknesses.
*   **Scope of Evaluation:** The method is demonstrated exclusively on single, centered objects against clean backgrounds. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the model's limitations and potential failure modes when applied to more complex, "in-the-wild" scenes with clutter or occlusions.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to the 3D Morphable Model on faces seems to be hindered by landmark localization errors, which may not reflect the best-case performance of that baseline approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated recurrent architecture for multi-view synthesis.
*   Demonstrates impressive results in generating long, coherent rotation sequences.
*   The emergent disentangling of identity and pose is a significant finding for weakly-supervised representation learning.
*   The experimental analysis is comprehensive and strongly supports the paper's claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The evaluation is limited to single-object datasets, leaving generalization to complex scenes as an open question.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novel formulation and strong results on weakly-supervised disentangling, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a high-quality submission that is likely to be impactful.
