Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles single-image 3D view synthesis by learning to predict sequences of out-of-plane rotations for object classes with ample training data (faces, chairs). The core contribution is a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder with disentangled latent variables: “identity” units shared across time and “pose” units updated by action-conditioned recurrent transitions. The model is trained end-to-end with an L2 image loss over trajectories, and curriculum learning increases sequence length to improve stability and disentanglement. Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and rendered chairs show plausible long-range rotations, improved quantitative reconstruction error over KNN baselines on chairs, and emergent view-invariant identity features competitive with supervised CNNs for cross-view recognition. The model also supports identity interpolation with consistent pose traversal.

Relative to prior work: it builds on transforming autoencoders and multiplicative/gating models (Hinton et al.; Memisevic), but adopts a convolutional encoder/decoder and explicit action inputs. It differs from Dosovitskiy et al. (chair generator) by learning an encoder for novel instances and performing recurrent multi-step transformations rather than decoding from labels. Compared to IGN (Kulkarni et al.), it uses a deterministic recurrent trajectory with action control and curriculum instead of a VAE formulation; experiments target longer sequences and demonstrate emergent disentangling without explicit regularizers. It also relates to multi-view perceptron and disBM but uses weak supervision via the transformation task rather than labels.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is well-specified, with clear latent partitioning and action-conditioned recurrent updates. The choice to update pose units recurrently while sharing identity units is justified by the task. Curriculum training is a reasonable strategy for long-term stability and is empirically validated.
- Empirical support: Results include qualitative sequences (faces, chairs), quantitative MSE on chairs against KNN baselines, and cross-view recognition using learned identity units (faces vs. CNN trained on labels; chairs vs. VGG fc7). The chair experiments show substantial gains with longer curricula (RNN16), supporting the hypothesis that longer training sequences improve both synthesis and disentanglement.
- Missing/weak comparisons: For generation, the KNN baseline is fairly weak; comparisons to stronger neural baselines are absent, e.g., (i) a non-recurrent encoder-decoder trained to predict multi-step rotations directly, (ii) a recurrent model that feeds back rendered images (teacher forcing vs. latent-only recurrence), (iii) IGN-like encoder-decoder trained to apply single or multiple rotations without recurrence. For faces, a quantitative comparison to the 3D morphable model is not provided (only visuals). Metrics are limited to MSE; PSNR/SSIM or human preference studies would strengthen the case given L2’s tendency to blur.
- Scope/limitations: Faces are tightly aligned and chairs are synthetic; generalization to in-the-wild scenes, complex backgrounds, or non-rigid classes is untested. Rotations are discretized (1-of-3 actions, 15° steps), and handling of continuous control or variable step sizes is not evaluated. Long-horizon behavior is capped by memory (t=16 on chairs); robustness beyond the training horizon is partially shown but not systematically measured.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. Architecture details (layer types/sizes, latent dimensionalities), training protocol (ADAM settings, curricula), data preprocessing, and evaluation setups are described sufficiently to reproduce experiments.
- Some clarifications would help: exact form of the action-conditioned transformation (e.g., dimensions and sharing of matrices for the three actions), how the mask branch is combined with RGB in the chair decoder, and whether any regularization or normalization is applied to pose/identity units. A brief ablation on identity/pose dimensionality would add clarity to design choices.

Originality
- The combination of action-conditioned recurrent latent transformations with a convolutional encoder-decoder, trained via curriculum for long sequences, is a meaningful and nontrivial extension of prior inverse-graphics and gating ideas. The weakly supervised disentangling that emerges from sequence training (without explicit disentangling regularizers) is a notable observation.
- While elements (capsules/transforming autoencoders, gating, encoder-decoder synthesis) are known, their integration and the demonstrated multi-step view synthesis with identity/pose partitioning is novel for NIPS 2015.

Significance
- The problem is important for both graphics and vision. The method shows stateable improvements in long-horizon view synthesis on chairs and yields useful view-invariant features without labels, which is valuable for representation learning.
- The impact is somewhat bounded by the restricted domains (aligned faces, synthetic chairs) and the lack of comparison to stronger learned baselines; nevertheless, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work on action-conditioned generative models and disentangling.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated architecture with action-conditioned recurrent pose updates and shared identity.
- Strong qualitative sequences and quantitative gains with curriculum length; compelling evidence that curriculum improves disentanglement and recognition.
- Emergent, label-free disentangling validated via cross-view recognition and identity/pose swap experiments; identity interpolation demonstrates smooth, meaningful latent manifolds.
- Reproducible details and careful experimental setups on two distinct domains.

Cons
- Limited baselines for synthesis; no comparison to non-recurrent or IGN-style encoder-decoders, nor to feedback-based recurrence.
- Metrics limited to MSE; lack of PSNR/SSIM or perceptual/human evaluation. Visuals suggest some blurriness consistent with L2 training.
- Datasets and preprocessing simplify the problem (aligned faces, synthetic chairs); missing tests on real-world backgrounds or nonrigid/deforming objects.
- Discrete action coding and fixed step size; no study of continuous control or robustness beyond trained horizons. Memory constraints cap the demonstrated sequence length.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: (i) feedforward multi-step predictor, (ii) recurrent model with image feedback, (iii) single-step encoder-decoder applied iteratively, (iv) IGN-like model; report PSNR/SSIM and, if possible, a small-scale user study.
- Quantify disentanglement more directly (e.g., correlation/predictability of pose from identity units and vice versa) and include ablations on latent dimensionalities.
- Evaluate continuous rotation control (e.g., interpolate actions) and generalize beyond 15° steps; report performance beyond trained sequence lengths.
- Provide quantitative comparisons to 3D morphable models on faces (landmark sensitivity vs. photometric fidelity).
- Explore less constrained settings: backgrounds, mild nonrigid motion, and real photographs of chairs.

Overall assessment
A solid, technically sound contribution with a clear architectural idea, good empirical support, and interesting emergent disentangling. Despite limited baselines and constrained datasets, the paper meets the NIPS criteria well: clear (high), original (moderate-to-high), quality (good), and significant (moderate). I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthened comparisons and metrics.
