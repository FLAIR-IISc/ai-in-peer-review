This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis, which is a challenging problem in both graphics and vision. The network is trained end-to-end to render rotated objects starting from a single image, and it demonstrates the quality of its predictions for human faces on the Multi-PIE dataset and for a dataset of 3D chair models. The main contributions of this work are the development of a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network, the ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories with a control signal supplied at each step by the user, and the learning of discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear motivation and explanation of the components of their network. The experimental results are also impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method for 3D view synthesis and cross-view object recognition.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is a complete piece of work, and it provides a significant contribution to the field of computer vision.

The clarity of the paper is also excellent, with a well-organized structure and clear explanations of the network architecture and the experimental results. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to 3D view synthesis and demonstrates its effectiveness on challenging datasets. The related work section is also well-written, providing a clear overview of the existing methods and highlighting the contributions of the proposed method.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it addresses a challenging problem in computer vision and provides a novel solution that can be used in various applications, such as image editing and object recognition.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One of the main limitations is that the model only supports discrete rotation angles determined by the training data, and there is no method for continuous-angle support. Additionally, the network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which conflicts with the typical flexibility of recurrent networks.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, as it provides a significant contribution to the field of computer vision and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on challenging datasets. The strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and it has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
