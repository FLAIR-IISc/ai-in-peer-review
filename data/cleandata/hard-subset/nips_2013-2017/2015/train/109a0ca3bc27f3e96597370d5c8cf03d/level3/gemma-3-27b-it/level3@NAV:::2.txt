## Review of “Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis”

This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing rotated views of 3D objects, specifically faces and chairs, from a single image. The core idea is to disentangle identity and pose within the latent space, controlling pose through a sequence of action units. The work extends previous approaches by employing a recurrent architecture to handle longer rotation trajectories, enabling the learning of view-invariant features for cross-view recognition without explicit supervision. This builds on the growing body of work leveraging deep learning for 3D understanding and generation, drawing connections to techniques like transforming autoencoders [12], predictive gating pyramids [19], and disentangled representation learning [21, 24].  The authors address a challenging problem in computer vision and graphics, where inferring 3D structure from 2D images is inherently ill-posed. 

The strengths of this work are considerable. The proposed recurrent structure effectively addresses the long-term dependency problem in pose manifold traversal, demonstrated through qualitative results showing smooth rotations. The disentangled representation learned appears to be genuinely useful, as evidenced by the cross-view recognition performance. The end-to-end training and curriculum learning strategy further contribute to the model's efficacy. The paper is exceptionally well-written, providing clear motivation, a comprehensive explanation of the network components, and a thorough experimental evaluation. The inclusion of both face and chair datasets broadens the applicability and demonstrates generalizability.

However, the paper also has limitations. A key weakness is the restricted support for discrete rotation angles dictated by the training data. The lack of a mechanism for continuous angle support limits the model’s flexibility and real-world applicability. The fixed-length sequence requirement also contrasts with the typical flexibility of recurrent networks and could potentially hinder performance with highly variable rotation trajectories. While curriculum learning aids training, the justification for the specific length increases would benefit from more analysis. Additionally, while the qualitative results are compelling, a more rigorous quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art methods beyond the cross-view recognition task would strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Novel recurrent architecture for 3D view synthesis.
* Effective disentangling of identity and pose without explicit supervision.
* Strong quantitative results in cross-view recognition.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited to discrete rotation angles.
* Fixed-length sequence constraint for the recurrent network.
* Limited comparison to existing 3D reconstruction/rendering methods.



Overall, despite the limitations, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed approach is novel, the experimental results are encouraging, and the paper is of high quality. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the continuous angle limitation and providing a more comprehensive comparison to related work.
