The paper "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis" proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to human faces and 3D chair models. The network is trained end-to-end on the task of rendering rotated objects starting from a single image, and it demonstrates the ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories with a control signal supplied at each step by the user.

The main contributions of this work are the development of a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis, the ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories, and the learning of discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels. The paper also demonstrates the effectiveness of curriculum training in improving the model's performance.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner. However, there are some limitations to the paper. The evaluation is largely qualitative, and the paper could benefit from more quantitative metrics and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. Additionally, the experiments are limited to the Multi-PIE and Chairs datasets, and more diverse datasets are needed to demonstrate the generality of the approach.

The paper also lacks sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from testing the generalization of the model to non-static-axis or non-repeating rotation trajectories, which would provide a more robust evaluation of the model's performance.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the proposed method is novel and interesting. However, the significance of the paper is undermined by the insufficient experimentation and analysis. The paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of the results, including error analysis and ablation studies, to provide a deeper understanding of the proposed method.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but there are some minor grammatical errors that could be corrected. The figures and visualizations are clear, but they could be scaled larger for readability.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to 3D view synthesis, and the use of recurrent transformations is an interesting contribution to the field. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the proposed method.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of computer vision, particularly in the area of 3D view synthesis. However, the paper needs to address the limitations mentioned above to fully realize its potential.
