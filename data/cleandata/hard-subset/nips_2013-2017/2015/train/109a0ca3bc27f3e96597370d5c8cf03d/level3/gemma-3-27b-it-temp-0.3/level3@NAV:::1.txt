## Review of “Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis”

This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel views of 3D objects (faces and chairs) from a single image. The core idea of leveraging a recurrent structure to capture long-term dependencies during rotations is interesting and builds upon recent work in image generation and disentangled representations. The approach aims to address the ill-posed nature of 3D reconstruction by learning a mapping between image space and a non-linear pose manifold, controlled by action units representing incremental rotations. The inclusion of curriculum learning and demonstration of disentangled representations through cross-view recognition and interpolation are also positive aspects.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of quantitative evaluation. While visual examples are provided, they are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. The authors need to include standard metrics for image quality (e.g., PSNR, SSIM) and object recognition accuracy to support their claims. Crucially, a comparison to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches (mentioned in the related work, such as [5, 17]) is missing.  Simply stating the limitations of these approaches isn’t enough; a direct comparison is essential. The figures are also quite small and difficult to assess, hindering a proper evaluation of the generated images.

The experiments are limited to the Multi-PIE and Chairs datasets. While these datasets are reasonable starting points, the generality of the approach remains unclear. Testing on more diverse object categories and exploring non-static-axis or non-repeating rotation trajectories (as suggested by the supervisor) would strengthen the paper.  The reliance on relatively simple rotation sequences also limits the scope of the work.

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, although minor grammatical errors (e.g., “rotate” instead of “rotated”) are present. The related work section is comprehensive, but could benefit from more detailed discussion of the differences between the proposed method and existing techniques. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel approach to view synthesis leveraging recurrence and disentanglement. Well-written and clearly presented.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Insufficient quantitative evaluation. Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods. Limited dataset diversity and experimental scope. Small and difficult to assess figures.

**Overall:** While the proposed approach is interesting and shows promise, the lack of rigorous evaluation significantly undermines its significance. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the aforementioned weaknesses through more comprehensive experimentation and analysis. The authors should prioritize quantitative metrics, comparisons to existing methods, and broader dataset evaluation before resubmission.
