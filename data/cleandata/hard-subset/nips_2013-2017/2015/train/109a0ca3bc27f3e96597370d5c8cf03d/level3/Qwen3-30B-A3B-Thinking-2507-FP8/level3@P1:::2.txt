

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of objects from single images, with a key innovation in weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors. The authors address the ill-posed nature of 3D view synthesis by modeling pose manifold traversal as a sequence of small rotations, using action units to control local transformations and a recurrent structure to maintain long-term dependencies. Crucially, the identity units (representing invariant object features) and pose units (representing view-dependent features) emerge naturally from the training objective without explicit disentanglement regularization, yielding view-invariant representations that enable strong cross-view recognition performance. The model is trained end-to-end on faces (Multi-PIE) and chairs (CAD-rendered dataset), with curriculum learning progressively increasing rotation trajectory length to improve both image synthesis quality and feature discriminativeness.

The paper makes significant contributions relative to prior work. Unlike geometry-based methods (e.g., [5]) that require explicit 3D models or structure-from-motion, this learning-based approach avoids intermediate mesh representations and associated artifacts. It differs from IGN [17] by using a recurrent architecture with action inputs for trajectory synthesis (rather than VAE-based generation) and from disentangling methods like disBM [21] by achieving disentanglement unsupervised through the rotation task itself. The weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly compelling: identity units achieve 78% cross-view recognition accuracy on Multi-PIE at 90° offsets (vs. 58% for RNN1), approaching supervised CNN performance without class labels. The curriculum training strategy (gradually increasing sequence length from t=1 to t=16) effectively solves the long-term dependency problem, as evidenced by the 42% MSE improvement (179 vs. 310) of RNN16 over KNN baselines on chairs.

Strengths include strong technical execution, clear exposition of the model components (Figures 1-2), and comprehensive evaluation across multiple dimensions: qualitative synthesis (Figures 3-5, 9), quantitative MSE (Figure 7), and cross-view recognition (Figure 8). The ablation of curriculum learning’s impact on disentanglement (Section 4.4) is particularly well-justified. The paper is well-written with precise terminology and logical flow, making it accessible to the NIPS audience.

Key weaknesses warrant consideration. The model supports only discrete rotation angles determined by training data (e.g., 15° steps), lacking continuous-angle interpolation—a limitation acknowledged in the conclusion but not addressed. While curriculum learning mitigates the fixed-sequence constraint, the model cannot handle arbitrary-length sequences during inference, as seen in RNN1’s failure beyond t=1 (Figure 6). The chair experiments, though challenging, could benefit from more nuanced analysis of failure modes (e.g., why VGG-16 outperforms RNN16 at >120° offsets due to symmetry). Additionally, the weakly-supervised disentangling claim is slightly overstated; while identity units are view-invariant, pose units are not explicitly disentangled from other factors (e.g., lighting), though the paper correctly notes this limitation.

The significance is high. The work advances view synthesis beyond single-view rendering by enabling plausible long-trajectory rotations without 3D supervision. More importantly, the emergent disentanglement provides a practical unsupervised method for learning view-invariant features—a critical step for robust recognition systems. The approach is broadly applicable to other transformation tasks (e.g., lighting changes), and the chair interpolation results (Figure 9) demonstrate generative potential beyond synthesis.

In summary, this is a technically sound, well-executed paper that makes a clear contribution to view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. The weakly-supervised disentangling via recurrent rotation modeling is novel and well-supported by experiments. While the discrete-angle limitation is a minor drawback, the paper’s strengths—robust disentanglement, strong quantitative results, and clear methodology—far outweigh this. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and will interest both vision and graphics researchers. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: high quality (sound theory, comprehensive experiments), clarity (well-organized, reproducible), originality (novel architecture and emergent disentanglement), and significance (practical view-invariant features for recognition). I recommend acceptance.
