Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles single-image 3D view synthesis by learning to apply out-of-plane rotations to object images via a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder. The encoder maps an input image into two sets of latent variables: identity units (pose-invariant appearance) and pose units (view-dependent factors). “Action” units specify a local rotation (clockwise/no-op/counter-clockwise), which updates the pose units; the decoder then renders the rotated image (and, for chairs, an auxiliary mask). A recurrent formulation allows the model to traverse long rotation trajectories while keeping identity fixed. Training uses curriculum learning: the network is first trained for one-step rotations, then gradually fine-tuned for longer sequences, which empirically improves both image quality and disentanglement.

The work is well-situated with respect to transforming autoencoders, predictive gating pyramids, bilinear/disentangling approaches, and contemporaneous inverse graphics networks. Compared to Dosovitskiy et al. (chair generation), this paper adds an encoder for generalization to novel instances, recurrent control for multi-step transformations, and weakly supervised disentangling without explicit labels or regularizers. Relative to IGN, the main differences are the recurrent control over trajectories and deterministic training (rather than a VAE). The method also relates to mental rotation studies by conditioning on stepwise actions.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea: recurrently controlled pose units with shared identity units; practical and scalable encoder/decoder design.
- Strong qualitative results on Multi-PIE faces and 3D chairs, including long trajectories with smooth appearance changes and preserved structure; mask prediction improves chair synthesis.
- Disentangling emerges from the rotation objective and curriculum: identity units are discriminative and view-invariant, enabling cross-view recognition without labels.
- Solid experimental methodology showing the effect of curriculum (RNN1→RNN16), with both qualitative and quantitative analyses (MSE, recognition accuracy).
- Simple but effective control interface (action units) and demonstration of identity interpolation with consistent pose traversal.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Rotations are discretized to the training step size; the model does not provide a mechanism for continuous-angle control or calibration to arbitrary angles.
- Although the recurrent formulation suggests variable-length control, training is staged at fixed sequence lengths, and generalization outside trained horizons is limited; no explicit variable-length training/evaluation protocol is provided.
- Baselines are limited: KNN for chairs is weak; no comparisons to recent neural view-synthesis/inverse graphics methods on chairs, and only a qualitative comparison to a 3DMM for faces.
- Evaluation relies heavily on MSE, which is known to be poorly aligned with perceptual quality; no SSIM/LPIPS or user study.
- Scope is constrained to category-specific settings with curated/registered data (aligned faces; rendered chairs), no illumination changes or real-scene backgrounds, and only rotation as the transformation.

Quality
The approach is technically sound, with a well-motivated recurrent latent update, action gating, and curriculum training. Architectural/training details are sufficient to reproduce (optimizers, learning rates, curricula, latent sizes). The disentangling claim is supported by cross-view recognition using identity vs. pose units (identity strong, pose near-chance). However, stronger baselines and perceptual metrics would strengthen the empirical claims, and continuous control would better validate the manifold-traversal narrative.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to follow. Model components, training regimes, datasets, and evaluation setups are described in sufficient detail, with informative figures. Minor suggestions: explicitly describe the action-to-pose transformation (e.g., matrix selection) and provide a concise algorithmic summary for training curricula.

Originality
The combination of a convolutional encoder/decoder with action-conditioned recurrent pose updates and weakly supervised disentangling is novel for this setting circa NIPS 2015. The curriculum-driven emergence of disentanglement is a notable insight. The work builds on known ideas (transforming autoencoders, inverse graphics) but contributes a distinct recurrent control formulation and empirical demonstration.

Significance
The method advances 3D-aware view synthesis for category-specific objects and suggests a practical route to learn view-invariant, discriminative features without labels. Given broad interest in learned graphics codes and controllable generation, this is a meaningful step with likely impact, though current scope (discrete rotations; curated data) tempers immediate applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enable continuous-angle control: e.g., replace 1-of-3 actions with a continuous rotation embedding (sin/cos of angle) and train with random angles; or learn a small continuous generator in pose space.
- Train and evaluate with variable-length sequences (randomized lengths) to encourage better generalization, and report performance beyond the trained horizon.
- Strengthen baselines: compare to contemporaneous inverse graphics/view-synthesis methods on chairs; add a learned feed-forward multi-angle decoder to isolate recurrence gains.
- Report perceptual metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) and, if feasible, a small user study; include ablations (no mask branch; no curriculum; feed-forward vs. recurrent).
- Discuss robustness to misalignment and to real-world images (backgrounds, illumination).

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, well-executed model with clear technical novelty in recurrent action-controlled latent traversal.
- Strong qualitative synthesis and quantitative improvements with curriculum; convincing disentangling evidence without labels.
- Clear writing, thorough experiments on two domains, and practical insights (mask branch, curriculum).

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited and sometimes weak baselines; lack of continuous-angle control undermines generality.
- Evaluation depends on MSE; no perceptual metrics or user study.
- Scope restricted to aligned/ rendered datasets and single transformation type; unclear robustness to real-world variability.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution to learned 3D-aware generation and representation learning. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the control generality and strengthening evaluation.
