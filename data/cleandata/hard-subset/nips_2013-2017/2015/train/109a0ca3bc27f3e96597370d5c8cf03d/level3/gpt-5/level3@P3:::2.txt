Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles single-image 3D view synthesis by learning to apply out-of-plane rotations using a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder. The encoder disentangles latent identity (appearance) from pose; “action units” specify local rotation steps; a decoder reconstructs the rotated image (and, for chairs, a foreground mask). The recurrent extension holds identity fixed and updates pose over a sequence of action inputs, enabling long rotation trajectories. Trained end-to-end with curriculum learning (increasing trajectory length), the model yields both higher-fidelity renderings and stronger view-invariant identity features useful for cross-view recognition.

The work builds on several strands. Compared to transforming autoencoders and Predictive Gating Pyramid, it adopts modern convolutional encoder/decoder networks and explicit action control with a recurrent pose state. Relative to Dosovitskiy et al. (chair image generation from labels), this model adds an encoder to generalize to unseen images, a recurrent formulation for multi-step transformations, and weakly supervised disentangling that emerges from the training objective. Versus IGN, it forgoes the VAE formulation in favor of deterministic training and focuses on long, action-controlled trajectories. The disentangling aspect is related to bilinear models, disBM, and the multi-view perceptron, but here emerges without class labels via the recurrent multi-step rotation objective and curriculum.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea: an encoder that factorizes identity vs. pose, action units that drive pose updates, and a decoder that synthesizes images (and masks for chairs).
- Recurrent design for long trajectories: keeping identity fixed while recurrently updating pose is sensible and effective.
- Weakly supervised disentangling: identity units become view-invariant and discriminative without class labels; demonstrated by cross-view recognition on faces and fine-grained chairs.
- Curriculum training: convincingly improves both rendering quality and recognition performance as trajectory length increases.
- End-to-end training on real and rendered data; thorough qualitative results and quantitative evaluations (MSE, cross-view recognition) support claims.
- Paper is well-written and easy to follow; components, training regime, and datasets are described clearly.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Discrete rotations only: the action units encode a small fixed step (1-of-3), so the model supports discrete angles dictated by the training grid; no mechanism for continuous-angle control or arbitrary step sizes is provided.
- Fixed-length training sequences: training uses fixed T and BPTT truncation, which conflicts with the usual flexibility of RNNs to variable-length control; generalization beyond trained lengths is empirical rather than principled.
- Objective and outputs: pixelwise L2 loss tends to blur fine details; no probabilistic or adversarial component to model multi-modality in occlusion/disocclusion during large rotations.
- Scope: experiments focus on centered faces and rendered chairs with relatively clean backgrounds; generalization to in-the-wild scenes, lighting changes, and occlusions is not evaluated.
- Action/state semantics: the pose state is unconstrained and learned implicitly; while effective, interpretability and compositionality of actions beyond rotation are not explored.

Quality
The methodology is sound: a well-motivated architecture, careful ablations via curriculum stages (RNN1/2/4/8/16), and consistent training details. Results substantiate claims:
- View synthesis: long rotation sequences on Multi-PIE and chairs, with RNN16 substantially reducing MSE vs. KNN baselines.
- Disentangling: identity features improve with longer curricula and achieve competitive face recognition vs. a supervised CNN; pose features become non-discriminative for identity as desired.
- Interpolations: identity interpolation produces plausible novel classes with consistent multi-view renderings.
The paper is primarily empirical; there is no theoretical analysis, but experiments are convincing and honest about limitations (e.g., memory limiting T, sensitivity of 3DMM baseline to landmarks).

Clarity
Exposition is clear and organized. The roles of identity/pose/action units and the recurrent update are well explained. Dataset preparation and curricula are explicit. Figures (progressive rotations, curricula comparisons) effectively illustrate behavior. Minor suggestions: explicitly define the action-to-pose transformation (e.g., gating/tensor product) with a compact equation; clarify how mask loss is weighted and how masks guide image synthesis during decoding.

Originality
The combination of (i) convolutional encoder-decoder with (ii) action-conditioned recurrent pose updates and (iii) emergent disentangling without labels is novel at the time, beyond contemporaneous decoder-only generators and single-step inverse graphics encoders. Curriculum training for longer transformations is a thoughtful and effective addition.

Significance
High practical and scientific impact. View synthesis from a single image is challenging; demonstrating long rotation trajectories and cross-view recognition from the same model is valuable. The weakly supervised disentangling result should interest both graphics and recognition communities, and the action-conditioned recurrence is a reusable pattern for learned transformations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Continuous control: replace 1-of-3 actions with a continuous angle input (e.g., sin/cos embedding or Lie algebra parameterization) and train on a mixture of step sizes; this would enable arbitrary rotation magnitudes and interpolation.
- Variable-length training: randomize sequence lengths and directions during training; consider scheduled sampling or training with teacher forcing vs. free-running to improve stability beyond trained T.
- Better losses/uncertainty: perceptual or adversarial losses, or a probabilistic decoder, could reduce blur and better handle multi-modality from occlusions.
- Broader actions and scenes: extend to elevation changes, zoom, and illumination; test on cluttered backgrounds and in-the-wild faces to assess robustness.
- Interpretability: regularize or supervise pose with weak angle annotations where available to align pose units with physical semantics.

Pros
- Elegant, well-motivated architecture with clear modular roles.
- Strong empirical evidence for both synthesis and disentangling; identity features are useful without labels.
- Curriculum yields substantial gains and is carefully studied.
- Clear writing and reproducible details.

Cons
- Discrete-angle control; no principled continuous-angle mechanism.
- Fixed-length sequence training and limited analysis of generalization to longer trajectories.
- Pixel L2 loss leads to some blurring; uncertainty not modeled.
- Limited evaluation in complex, real-world settings.

Overall
A solid, well-executed paper with a novel and useful architecture for action-conditioned 3D view synthesis and emergent disentangling. Despite limitations around discrete control and fixed-length training, the contributions are clear, the experiments are convincing, and the work is likely to influence subsequent research on learned transformations and view-invariant representation learning. I lean toward acceptance.
