

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper introduces a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder architecture for synthesizing 3D view rotations from single images without explicit 3D model recovery. The authors propose a model that disentangles identity and pose factors, using action units to control incremental rotations along a pose manifold. The key innovation lies in using a recurrent structure to handle long rotation trajectories while maintaining identity invariance, with curriculum learning to progressively increase sequence length during training.

The work builds upon and extends several recent NIPS contributions. It relates to the transforming autoencoder [12] and Predictive Gating Pyramid [19] for sequence prediction, but differs by incorporating disentangled latent representations and a recurrent structure for pose manifold traversal. It also connects to disentangling approaches like bilinear models [24] and multi-view perceptron [29], but uniquely achieves unsupervised disentangling without class labels, a significant advancement over prior work.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The proposed architecture effectively addresses the challenge of long-term dependencies in rotation sequences through a recurrent pose unit mechanism. The curriculum training strategy is well-motivated and successfully implemented, as evidenced by the progressive improvement in rendering quality (from RNN1 to RNN16) and cross-view recognition performance. The ablation studies (Figure 6, Figure 7) provide compelling evidence that longer training sequences improve both image synthesis quality and disentanglement. The model achieves competitive results on both face and chair datasets, with the RNN16 model showing a 42% improvement in MSE over the KNN baseline on chairs.

Clarity is excellent throughout the paper. The architecture is well-explained with clear figures (Figures 1-2) and the curriculum training concept is intuitively presented. The experimental setup is thorough, with detailed datasets (Multi-PIE and chairs), network architectures, and evaluation metrics. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific training parameters and implementation choices (e.g., fixed upsampling instead of max-pooling).

The originality is strong. While disentangling identity and pose has been explored before, this paper uniquely demonstrates that it can emerge naturally from the recurrent rotation prediction objective without explicit regularization. The ability to learn view-invariant features unsupervised is particularly novel and significant. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work like IGN [17] by focusing on trajectory-based rotation rather than single-view synthesis.

The significance is high for both computer vision and graphics. The ability to generate plausible novel views from single images without 3D models addresses a fundamental challenge in visual understanding. The disentangled identity features also demonstrate practical utility for cross-view recognition, achieving competitive results against supervised CNNs on both face and chair datasets. The interpolation experiments (Figure 9) further showcase the model's generative capabilities.

Strengths:
1. Novel recurrent architecture that effectively handles long rotation sequences
2. Demonstrated unsupervised disentangling of identity and pose yielding view-invariant features
3. Curriculum training strategy that significantly improves performance
4. Strong quantitative results with clear ablation studies
5. Well-written and clearly organized presentation

Weaknesses:
1. Limited to discrete rotation angles determined by training data; no method for continuous-angle rotation
2. Trained with fixed-length sequences, which contradicts the typical flexibility of recurrent networks
3. The discrete rotation limitation (e.g., 15° increments) may limit practical applicability where continuous rotation is needed

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised representation learning for 3D view synthesis. While the discrete rotation limitation is notable, it's a reasonable constraint given the training data, and the authors acknowledge this limitation. The fixed-length sequence training is a minor issue that could be addressed in future work, but doesn't significantly undermine the main contribution.

This paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The results demonstrate that disentangling identity and pose can emerge naturally from the rotation prediction task, with practical benefits for both view synthesis and cross-view recognition. The authors have provided strong evidence through both qualitative examples and quantitative metrics.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel approach to unsupervised disentangling, strong empirical results, well-motivated architecture, clear writing, and significant contribution to 3D view synthesis.

Con arguments against acceptance: Discrete rotation limitation and fixed-length sequence training are notable weaknesses, but these are minor compared to the paper's substantial contributions.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in unsupervised disentangling for 3D view synthesis and provides valuable insights about how recurrent structures can help maintain identity across transformations. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
