

# Review for "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image, demonstrating its application on faces (Multi-PIE dataset) and chairs (CAD model dataset). The authors develop a novel approach where the model learns to perform sequential rotations by receiving control signals at each step, using a recurrent structure to capture long-term dependencies. The key innovation lies in the curriculum learning approach that gradually increases rotation trajectory length, enabling the model to learn disentangled representations without explicit supervision. This work relates to recent NIPS papers on image generation (e.g., [8, 17]) and disentanglement (e.g., [6, 21]), but introduces a novel recurrent structure for sequential transformation rather than one-shot prediction.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with a clear formulation of the recurrent network architecture and curriculum learning strategy. The model demonstrates reasonable performance on the two datasets tested, and the ablation studies on curriculum stages (RNN1 to RNN16) provide useful insights into how training sequence length affects performance. However, the evaluation lacks sufficient quantitative metrics and meaningful comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The paper compares against a KNN baseline for chairs, but this is not a strong comparison to the current SOTA in 3D view synthesis. Crucially, the paper omits comparisons to geometry-based approaches (e.g., [5, 28]) that are standard for view synthesis tasks, which undermines the claimed significance of the learning approach. The paper also lacks proper analysis of failure cases or limitations of the model.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with clear explanations of the model architecture and training procedure. The figures are mostly informative, though they would benefit from being larger for better readability (as noted in the key points). The paper contains minor grammatical errors (e.g., "rotate" instead of "rotated" in some places), but these do not significantly impede understanding. The organization is logical, moving from problem statement to related work to model description, experiments, and conclusions.

**Originality:** The recurrent structure for sequential view synthesis is novel within the context of NIPS 2013-2017, though it builds on the mental rotation concept from Shepard and Metzler [22] and recent work on sequence modeling [27]. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors is particularly interesting, as it emerges from the training objective rather than explicit regularization. However, the paper could better clarify how this differs from related work like IGN [17] and the disentangling Boltzmann Machine [21].

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in computer vision (3D view synthesis from single images), but its significance is limited by the narrow scope of evaluation. The experiments are confined to two datasets (faces and chairs) with limited generalization testing, and the paper does not demonstrate how the approach could scale to more complex scenes or objects. The claim that the model "learns discriminative view-invariant features without using class labels" is interesting, but the cross-view recognition experiments are not sufficiently rigorous (e.g., no comparison to established view-invariant feature extractors like those in [29]). The chair interpolation experiments are compelling but represent a limited demonstration of the method's capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- Novel recurrent architecture for sequential 3D view synthesis with curriculum learning
- Demonstrates weakly-supervised disentanglement of identity and pose factors
- Interesting visual results showing smooth rotation trajectories
- Clear explanation of the model architecture and training procedure
- Good experimental design for analyzing the impact of curriculum training

**Weaknesses:**
- Lacks quantitative comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (both learning-based and geometry-based)
- Limited to two datasets (faces and chairs) without demonstration of broader applicability
- Insufficient analysis of failure cases and limitations
- Minimal discussion of how the approach handles complex scenes or non-static-axis rotations
- Figure quality could be improved for better readability

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The recurrent approach for sequential view synthesis is novel and well-motivated
- The weakly-supervised disentanglement is a compelling contribution
- The paper is well-written with clear technical explanations

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The evaluation lacks sufficient quantitative metrics and comparisons to SOTA
- The limited scope of experiments (only two datasets) undermines the claimed generality
- The significance of the contribution is diminished by the lack of meaningful comparisons
- The paper does not adequately address how the approach compares to geometry-based methods

Overall, this is an interesting paper with a novel approach to 3D view synthesis. However, the evaluation is insufficiently rigorous to support the significance claims, and the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons to existing methods, broader experimental validation, and more thorough analysis of limitations. The authors should address these concerns before the paper can be considered for acceptance at NIPS.
