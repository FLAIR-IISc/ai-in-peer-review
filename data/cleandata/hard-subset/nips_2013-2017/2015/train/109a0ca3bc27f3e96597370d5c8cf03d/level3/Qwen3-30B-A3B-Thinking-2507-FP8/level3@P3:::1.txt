

# Review of "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis"

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of objects from a single 2D image, specifically targeting faces and chairs. The authors address the challenging problem of 3D view synthesis without explicit 3D model recovery, which is typically handled by geometry-based approaches. The core innovation is a recurrent structure that enables the model to capture long-term dependencies along rotation trajectories, using action units to control local transformations at each step. The approach is trained end-to-end with curriculum learning, gradually increasing the complexity of rotation sequences. The authors demonstrate the model's ability to synthesize realistic rotations, disentangle identity and pose features, and interpolate between object classes.

The work relates to several lines of previous research. It builds on the transforming autoencoder [12] and Predictive Gating Pyramid [19] for image transformations, but introduces a recurrent structure with action inputs to handle repeated rotation steps. It also connects to recent work on disentangled representations [21, 24, 29] and the Inverse Graphics Network [17], though the authors' approach differs in its focus on trajectory-based prediction rather than single-image transformations.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates a technically sound approach to view synthesis, with a well-structured recurrent architecture that addresses the long-term dependency challenge in rotation trajectories. However, the evaluation is largely qualitative and lacks sufficient quantitative metrics for comparison. The paper compares against a KNN baseline for chairs but fails to provide comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art geometry-based methods (e.g., 3D morphable models [5, 28]) or other learning-based approaches (e.g., [8, 17]). The quantitative results for chair synthesis (MSE of ~179 for RNN16) are presented, but without comparison to other methods on the same dataset. The lack of standard metrics like LPIPS or FID for visual quality assessment weakens the evaluation.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the model architecture and training procedures. The figures generally illustrate the main concepts, though they would benefit from larger scaling for better readability. The authors provide sufficient technical details for reproduction, though the grammatical errors (e.g., "rotate" instead of "rotated" in Figure 3 caption) should be corrected.

**Originality**: The recurrent structure for handling long-term rotation trajectories represents a novel approach to view synthesis, particularly in its weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features. The curriculum learning strategy for training the recurrent network is also a reasonable contribution. However, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of experiments (only faces and chairs) and the lack of comparison to more recent methods.

**Significance**: The problem of 3D view synthesis from single images is important for both graphics and vision, and the approach has potential applications in photo editing and vision-based reinforcement learning. However, the significance is undermined by the limited evaluation scope and lack of comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The paper demonstrates promising results on the tested datasets but fails to establish how this approach advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The weakly-supervised disentangling is an interesting finding, but without quantitative evidence of its superiority over explicitly regularized approaches [6, 21], its significance is limited.

**Strengths**:
- Novel recurrent architecture that effectively handles long-term rotation dependencies
- Demonstrates convincing qualitative results for face and chair rotation
- Weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose features is an interesting finding
- Clear explanation of curriculum learning strategy
- Good theoretical foundation in pose manifold traversal

**Weaknesses**:
- Lacks quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art methods
- Limited to two datasets (Multi-PIE and Chairs) without demonstration of broader generalizability
- No comparison to geometry-based approaches that directly address the problem
- Insufficient analysis of failure modes and limitations
- Figures are too small for proper readability
- Fails to test generalization to non-static-axis or non-repeating rotation trajectories
- Minor grammatical errors throughout

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
*Pro*: The paper presents a technically sound approach with an interesting application of recurrent networks to view synthesis. The weakly-supervised disentangling of features is a notable finding that could be valuable for future work on representation learning.

*Con*: The evaluation is inadequate and fails to demonstrate the method's superiority over existing approaches. The limited scope of experiments and lack of quantitative comparisons significantly undermine the claimed contributions. Without stronger evidence of performance gains, the paper does not sufficiently advance the state of the art.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting approach to 3D view synthesis with a novel recurrent structure, the insufficient evaluation and lack of comparative analysis prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. The authors should substantially expand their evaluation to include quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, test on more diverse datasets, and analyze the model's limitations and failure cases. The current results are promising but not sufficient to demonstrate the method's significance for the NIPS community.
