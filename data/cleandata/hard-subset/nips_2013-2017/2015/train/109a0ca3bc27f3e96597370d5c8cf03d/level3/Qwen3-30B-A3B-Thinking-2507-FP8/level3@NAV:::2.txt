

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for unsupervised disentanglement of identity and pose in 3D object rotation tasks. The authors introduce a novel architecture where a shared identity unit remains fixed while recurrent pose units are updated via action units controlling small rotation increments. This approach enables long rotation trajectories by learning pose manifold traversal through curriculum training, which progressively increases sequence length during training. The key innovation lies in the unsupervised disentanglement of identity and pose factors, yielding view-invariant identity features that significantly improve cross-view recognition performance without using class labels.

The paper builds upon related work in disentangling representations (e.g., multi-view perceptron, IGN) and recurrent networks for sequence prediction, but makes a clear contribution by applying these concepts to 3D view synthesis with a focus on unsupervised disentanglement. The authors demonstrate strong results on Multi-PIE face dataset and chair CAD models, showing high-quality rendering and improved cross-view recognition accuracy compared to baselines like KNN and VGG-16 features. The curriculum training strategy is particularly effective for learning long rotation sequences.

Strengths include the well-structured methodology, clear experimental validation across multiple datasets, and the important demonstration of unsupervised feature learning. The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of components and thorough ablation studies. The disentangled identity features provide a significant contribution to the field, as they enable view-invariant recognition without explicit supervision.

Two main weaknesses limit the paper's impact. First, the model only supports discrete rotation angles determined by training data, lacking a method for continuous-angle interpolation. Second, the fixed-length sequence training conflicts with the flexibility of typical recurrent networks, potentially limiting generalization to variable-length transformations. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, they don't provide solutions.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field with high technical quality, clear significance for 3D view synthesis and disentanglement, and excellent clarity. The weaknesses are notable but do not undermine the substantial technical contribution. I recommend acceptance.
