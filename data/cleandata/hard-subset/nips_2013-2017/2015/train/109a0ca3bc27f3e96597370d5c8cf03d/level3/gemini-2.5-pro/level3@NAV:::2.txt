This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network to synthesize novel 3D views of an object from a single input image. The core idea is to disentangle the object's identity from its pose. The encoder maps an image to separate identity and pose representations. A sequence of "action units," representing small rotations, recurrently updates the pose representation while the identity remains fixed. The decoder then reconstructs the object's image and mask at each new pose. This work builds on prior research in generative models like Transforming Auto-encoders and Inverse Graphics Networks but introduces a novel recurrent formulation to handle long transformation trajectories, using curriculum learning to stabilize training.

The paper's primary contribution and main strength is its elegant method for achieving weakly-supervised disentanglement. By training the network to re-render an object across long rotation sequences, the model is forced to learn a view-invariant representation for identity. The quality of this disentanglement is convincingly demonstrated through quantitative cross-view object recognition experiments. The resulting identity features are shown to be highly discriminative and competitive with supervised methods, which is a significant finding. The use of curriculum learning is well-motivated and empirically shown to improve both rendering quality and the discriminative power of the learned features. The paper is clearly written, the model is well-explained, and the claims are well-supported by strong qualitative and quantitative results on both face and chair datasets.

Despite these strengths, the work has a couple of limitations. First, the model is constrained to discrete rotation angles defined by the training data. The paper does not propose a method to generalize to continuous or arbitrary-angle rotations, which limits its practical applicability for fine-grained view synthesis. Second, the network is trained with fixed-length sequences. While this is a practical choice for the curriculum strategy, it underutilizes the inherent flexibility of recurrent networks to handle variable-length sequences.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting an original and significant contribution. The strengths in demonstrating effective unsupervised disentanglement for view-invariant feature learning far outweigh the limitations, which point toward clear directions for future research. The work advances the state-of-the-art in generative modeling for view synthesis and representation learning.
