This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder that, given a single image, synthesizes novel views by traversing a pose manifold with action-conditioned recurrent pose units and shared identity units. The curriculum training strategy (increasing sequence length) is used to stabilize long-horizon predictions. The approach is positioned relative to transforming autoencoders, gating/predictive models for transformations, and contemporaneous “inverse graphics” work such as IGN and generative chair renderers, and it further emphasizes weakly supervised disentangling evidenced by cross-view recognition and chair identity interpolation. The problem is timely and aligns with NIPS work on representation learning and neural rendering.

Quality: The architecture and training scheme are technically sound and clearly motivated; the ablations across curriculum length are informative. However, the evaluation is predominantly qualitative. Quantitative analysis is limited (MSE for chairs; cross-view recognition), lacks perceptual metrics (PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS, FID/KID), pose error, mask IoU, or calibration of uncertainty, and omits systematic comparisons to strong baselines. Comparisons to geometry-based pipelines (e.g., 3D morphable models, SfM/MVS or single-view 3D fit-and-render) are insufficient beyond a small qualitative face example.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and easy to follow. Figures are too small to assess fine details; scaling up visualizations and adding zoomed crops would help. Minor grammatical issues occur (e.g., “rotate” should be “rotated”).

Originality: The recurrent, action-conditioned disentangling within a convolutional encoder–decoder is a novel combination that extends prior chair rendering and IGN-style coding to long sequences with curriculum learning. Related work is reasonably covered.

Significance: The idea is promising, but the current scope limits impact. Experiments are confined to Multi-PIE faces and rendered Chairs, both with constrained, static rotation axes. Generalization to other categories, real-world imagery, and non-repeating or non-fixed-axis rotations is untested, undermining claims of broad applicability.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated model with elegant recurrent/action design and curriculum training.
- Compelling qualitative results; evidence of emergent disentangling (cross-view recognition, interpolation).
- Relevance to view synthesis and invariant representation learning.

Cons for acceptance:
- Inadequate quantitative evaluation and lack of comparisons to state of the art (geometry-based and learned baselines).
- Narrow datasets; no tests on diverse categories or natural scenes.
- No evaluation under non-static-axis or non-uniform rotation trajectories.
- Small figures, minor language issues.

Suggestions: Add rigorous metrics (PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS/FID), pose accuracy, and user studies; compare against 3D morphable models, single-view 3D fit-and-render, IGN, and KNN/flow-based or warping baselines; test SE(3) control beyond yaw and non-repeating trajectories; include broader datasets (e.g., cars, other ShapeNet classes, in-the-wild images). Overall: interesting and potentially impactful, but currently below the bar due to limited experimentation. Borderline reject.
