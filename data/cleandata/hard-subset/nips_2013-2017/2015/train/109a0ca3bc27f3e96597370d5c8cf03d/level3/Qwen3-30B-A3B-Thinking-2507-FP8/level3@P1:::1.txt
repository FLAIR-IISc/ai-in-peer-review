

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views from single 2D images, with a focus on learning disentangled representations of identity and pose. The authors address the challenging problem of view synthesis by modeling pose manifold traversal through sequential transformations, using a recurrent structure with action units to handle long-term dependencies. The approach is evaluated on Multi-PIE (faces) and chair CAD models, demonstrating the ability to generate realistic rotation trajectories, learn disentangled features without class labels, and interpolate between object classes.

The paper builds on relevant work in image generation (e.g., [8, 25]) and disentangled representations (e.g., [21, 24]), but introduces a novel recurrent architecture with curriculum learning to handle multi-step rotations. The key innovation lies in using action units to control local transformations along the pose manifold, allowing the model to learn from short trajectories before progressing to longer sequences. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose is particularly noteworthy, as it emerges naturally from the training objective without explicit regularization.

The paper demonstrates promising qualitative results on both datasets, with smooth transitions between views and preservation of identity characteristics during rotation. The cross-view recognition experiments provide some evidence for the disentangled representations, showing improved performance with curriculum training (e.g., RNN6 achieves 10% performance drop between 15° and 90° angle offsets on Multi-PIE compared to 20% for RNN1). The chair interpolation results also demonstrate the model's ability to generate novel objects by interpolating between identity units.

However, the evaluation suffers from significant limitations that undermine the claimed contributions. The paper lacks comprehensive quantitative metrics (e.g., PSNR, SSIM, FID) to properly assess image quality, relying instead on qualitative observations. The KNN baseline comparison is insufficient, as it only evaluates at specific points rather than providing a thorough comparison across different rotation angles. Crucially, the paper does not compare with state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches like 3D morphable models [28] or multi-view stereo methods, which would provide meaningful context for evaluating the proposed learning-based approach.

The experimental scope is limited to two datasets (faces and chairs), with no demonstration of broader applicability to other object classes or more complex scenes. The paper claims to handle "complex 3D objects" but the chair dataset is still relatively simple compared to real-world objects with intricate textures and structures. The authors should test generalization to non-static-axis rotations or non-repeating trajectories to demonstrate the model's robustness.

The figures are too small to properly evaluate the visual results, and minor grammatical errors (e.g., "rotate" instead of "rotated") should be corrected. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of limitations, such as the model's inability to handle complex scenes with multiple objects or occlusions.

While the recurrent architecture with curriculum learning is a technically sound approach, the significance of the work is diminished by the inadequate evaluation. The weakly-supervised disentangling claim is interesting but not sufficiently supported by quantitative evidence. The paper should provide more rigorous metrics for disentanglement (e.g., mutual information between latent factors) and compare more thoroughly with relevant baselines.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making it easy to follow the technical contributions. The authors have made a solid effort to demonstrate the model's capabilities, but the lack of comprehensive evaluation prevents a strong claim of advancing the state-of-the-art. The work has potential but requires more substantial experimental validation to demonstrate its significance.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to 3D view synthesis with a novel recurrent architecture, but the insufficient evaluation and limited scope prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. The authors should address these limitations by providing quantitative metrics, comparing with relevant baselines, and expanding the experimental scope to demonstrate broader applicability.
