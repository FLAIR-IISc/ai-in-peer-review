- Proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder that receives stepwise control signals to perform sequential 3D view synthesis from a single image.  
- Uses curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation trajectory length, enabling weakly‑supervised disentanglement of identity and pose.  
- Novelty: a recurrent sequential-transformation framework versus prior one‑shot prediction methods.  
- Technical formulation and training procedure are sound and clearly explained.  
- Demonstrates reasonable visual results on Multi‑PIE faces and CAD chairs; chair interpolation results are compelling.  
- Ablation study across curriculum stages (RNN1–RNN16) provides useful insight into effects of sequence length.  
- Lacks sufficient quantitative metrics and rigorous comparisons to state‑of‑the‑art learning‑based methods.  
- Omits comparisons to standard geometry‑based view synthesis approaches, weakening claimed significance.  
- Evaluation is narrow (only two datasets), limiting evidence of generalization to more complex scenes or objects.  
- Cross‑view recognition experiments are not rigorous and lack comparison to established view‑invariant feature methods.  
- Insufficient analysis of failure cases, limitations, and handling of non‑static‑axis or complex rotations.  
- Figures are informative but should be larger/higher quality for readability.  
- Paper is generally well‑written and logically organized, with only minor grammatical errors.  
- Strengths summary: novel recurrent architecture, emergent weakly‑supervised disentanglement, clear exposition.  
- Weaknesses summary: limited evaluation scope, missing SOTA and geometry comparisons, and inadequate failure analysis.  
- Acceptance arguments: well‑motivated, novel approach and interesting disentanglement; rejection arguments: weak empirical evaluation and limited generality.
