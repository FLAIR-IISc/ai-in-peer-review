## Review of “Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis”

This paper presents a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single 2D image. The approach aims to address the ill-posed nature of the problem by learning to apply rotations via a recurrent structure that captures long-term dependencies. Experiments are conducted on face (Multi-PIE) and chair datasets, exploring curriculum learning, disentangled representations for cross-view recognition, and interpolation between chair classes. The core idea of using a recurrent network to model pose manifold traversal with action units is interesting and builds upon recent work in generative modeling and disentangled representations. It relates to the broader field of learning 3D representations from 2D images, moving beyond traditional geometry-based methods like multi-view stereo and 3D morphable models. 

The strength of this work lies in its novel approach to integrating recurrence with convolutional networks for view synthesis, and the initial demonstration of disentangled representations emerging from this framework without explicit supervision. The writing is generally clear and easy to follow, and the problem statement is well-motivated. However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its evaluation. While qualitative results are presented, they are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. There is a conspicuous lack of quantitative metrics—standard measures like PSNR, SSIM, or learned perceptual image patch similarity (LPIPS) are absent, hindering a rigorous assessment of rendering quality. Crucially, a comparison to state-of-the-art geometry-based methods (as acknowledged in the related work) and other recent learning-based approaches is missing. The figures are also quite small, making it difficult to assess the visual quality of the generated images.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is limited. The choice of datasets (Multi-PIE and Chairs) feels somewhat narrow, and the lack of experiments with more diverse objects restricts the demonstration of generality. The paper also does not investigate generalization to more complex rotation trajectories beyond simple repeated rotations, as suggested by the reviewer guidelines. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel approach to view synthesis using recurrent convolutional networks. Emergence of disentangled representations without explicit supervision.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Insufficient quantitative evaluation. Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods. Limited datasets and experimental scope. Small figures hinder visual assessment.

**Overall:** While the proposed approach is conceptually interesting, the insufficient experimentation and analysis significantly undermine its significance. I recommend **rejection** in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the evaluation and experimentation concerns before resubmission.  A more thorough and quantitative evaluation, including comparisons to relevant baselines and broader datasets, is essential to establish the contribution of this work.
