This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis, specifically designed to apply out-of-plane rotations to 3D objects. The network consists of an encoder that disentangles identity and pose, action units that control pose, and a decoder that reconstructs the rotated image and mask. The architecture is extended to a recurrent structure that keeps the identity fixed and updates the pose via a sequence of action units, enabling the support of longer rotation trajectories.

The main contribution of this work is the unsupervised disentangling of identity/appearance and pose, resulting in identity units that act as discriminative, view-invariant features for cross-view recognition. The authors demonstrate that disentangling and curriculum training improve rendering quality and prediction accuracy for longer rotations. The network is trained end-to-end, and both qualitative renderings and quantitative cross-view recognition results support the method.

The paper is well-written, with clear motivation and explanation of the components. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work and position their contribution within the context of existing research.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address a challenging problem in computer vision, namely 3D view synthesis, using a novel and effective approach. The use of a recurrent architecture and curriculum training enables the network to learn long-term dependencies and improve its performance on longer rotation trajectories. The disentangling of identity and pose is a significant contribution, as it allows for the generation of discriminative, view-invariant features for cross-view recognition.

However, there are also some weaknesses to the paper. One of the main limitations is that the model supports only discrete rotation angles determined by the training data, and no method is provided for continuous-angle support. Additionally, the network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which conflicts with the typical flexibility of recurrent networks. These limitations may restrict the applicability of the method to certain scenarios.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to 3D view synthesis, combining elements of recurrent neural networks and disentangled representations. While the individual components are not entirely new, their combination and application to 3D view synthesis are innovative and contribute to the state of the art.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to advance the field of computer vision, particularly in areas such as 3D view synthesis, cross-view recognition, and generative modeling. The method's ability to disentangle identity and pose, and to generate discriminative, view-invariant features, has implications for a wide range of applications, from face recognition to object manipulation.

In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of computer vision, with a novel approach to 3D view synthesis that combines recurrent neural networks and disentangled representations. While there are some limitations to the method, its strengths and potential impact make it a valuable addition to the literature.
