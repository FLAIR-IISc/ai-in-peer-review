- Proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder that disentangles identity and pose to synthesize novel 3D views from a single image.  
- Encoder separates identity (fixed) and pose (recurrently transformed) latent features; decoder generates images from identity + transformed pose.  
- "Action" inputs drive discrete rotation steps (clockwise/counter-clockwise) applied recurrently to the pose features.  
- Key finding: training end-to-end for sequential view synthesis yields weakly-supervised, view-invariant identity features without class labels.  
- Uses curriculum learning (increasing sequence lengths) to improve image quality and disentangling.  
- Extends transforming auto-encoders and generative conv nets by adding recurrence for long-term sequential transformations.  
- Positions itself within disentangled representation literature, offering a novel sequential-prediction-based weak supervision approach.  
- Strength — originality/significance: novel recurrent traversal of pose manifold and demonstration of emergent disentangling.  
- Strength — technical quality: solid, complete method with extensive experiments on Multi-PIE and rendered chairs.  
- Strength — empirical results: strong qualitative long-rotation synthesis and quantitative gains over a KNN baseline.  
- Strength — learned features: competitive cross-view recognition performance versus supervised CNNs despite no identity labels.  
- Strength — ablation: curriculum stages (RNN1, RNN2, etc.) clearly show benefits of recurrent long-sequence training.  
- Strength — clarity: paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly presented with helpful diagrams.  
- Weakness — discrete transformations: trained on dataset-specific discrete rotation steps; unclear extension to continuous arbitrary angles.  
- Weakness — fixed-length recurrence: curriculum uses fixed sequence lengths, raising questions about generalization to much longer variable-length sequences.  
- Overall verdict: high-quality, significant contribution with strong results; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance despite the noted limitations.
