Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles single-image novel view synthesis by learning to apply 3D rotations to objects using a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder with explicit “action” inputs. The encoder decomposes an image into identity units (view-invariant) and pose units (view-dependent). At each time step, an action vector (clockwise/noop/counterclockwise) linearly transforms the pose units; the identity units are shared across steps. The decoder renders the image from identity and pose units. Training uses pixel-wise loss over sequences and curriculum learning to progressively increase trajectory length, encouraging both long-horizon consistency and disentanglement. Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and a chairs CAD dataset show qualitative sequences of rotations, a KNN baseline comparison on chairs, cross-view recognition using identity units, and identity interpolation that yields smooth class morphs with consistent pose traversal.

This work connects to transforming autoencoders and gating architectures (Hinton et al. 2011; Michalski et al. 2014), generative CNNs for chairs (Dosovitskiy et al. 2015), and inverse graphics (Kulkarni et al. 2015). Unlike prior feedforward image generators, the model explicitly controls transformations via action units and uses a recurrent latent transition to traverse the pose manifold. Compared to IGN, it avoids a VAE objective and emphasizes multi-step control and emergent disentanglement. A qualitative comparison with a 3D morphable model (Blanz & Vetter) is provided; DeepStereo (Flynn et al.) is acknowledged but addresses multi-view scenes. A stronger discussion of spatial transformer networks (Jaderberg et al., NIPS 2015) as an alternative learned transformation module would be relevant.

Quality:
- Soundness: The recurrent latent transition with action-gated updates is a coherent design for long-range transformations, and curriculum training is appropriate. Keeping decoding independent at each step (i.e., not feeding rendered frames forward) is a reasonable choice that can reduce error accumulation in pixel space.
- Empirics: The evaluation is largely qualitative. Quantitative measures are limited (MSE on chairs; cross-view recognition using identity units). There is no perceptual metric (e.g., SSIM/PSNR) or human evaluation. Comparisons are limited to KNN (chairs) and a qualitative morphable model baseline (faces). Important baselines such as IGN, the chair generator (as a decode-only “oracle”), or a geometry-based pipeline with pose-aligned texture mapping are missing. Ablations (e.g., non-recurrent variant, without curriculum, without action units, with/without mask branch) would help establish where gains come from.
- Analysis: Disentanglement is demonstrated via cross-view recognition and via a negative result using pose units, but a more systematic analysis (e.g., linear probes predicting pose from identity units and vice versa; mutual information estimates) would strengthen the claim. Generalization beyond fixed, repeated small rotations (non-static axes, variable step sizes, random action sequences) is not evaluated.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-structured, with sufficient architectural and training detail to largely reproduce results (filter sizes, strides, learning rates, curriculum schedule). Figures are too small; scaling them up would improve readability. Minor grammatical issues occur (e.g., “rendering rotated objects” instead of “rendering rotate objects”; missing “3D” in ref. [14] title). Please also standardize notation for time indices and actions and briefly justify the 1-of-3 action coding.

Originality:
The combination of a convolutional encoder–decoder with action-conditioned recurrent pose units and curriculum learning for long-horizon transformations is novel and interesting for this problem. The emergence of disentangled identity/pose without explicit disentangling losses is a nice finding. While related to prior work on transforming autoencoders, gating pyramids, and inverse graphics, the proposed formulation and control interface are distinct.

Significance:
View synthesis from a single image is important for graphics, recognition, and model-based reasoning. The approach is promising, but the current scope (faces and chairs only) and limited quantitative comparisons undercut the broader impact. Demonstrating generality (e.g., cars, airplanes, other ShapeNet classes; objects in natural scenes; larger pose ranges and non-circular trajectories) and stronger baselines would increase significance. The identity features’ cross-view recognition results are encouraging but not yet competitive with state-of-the-art recognition pipelines, and the KNN baseline on chairs is weak for view synthesis.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated model with an intuitive control signal and recurrent latent transition.
- Curriculum learning thoughtfully applied; measurable gains in both synthesis and recognition as trajectories lengthen.
- Emergent disentanglement with weak supervision demonstrated via cross-view recognition and interpolation.
- Qualitative results show smooth, identity-preserving trajectories.

Cons:
- Evaluation is mainly qualitative; limited and sometimes weak baselines (KNN) and metrics (MSE only).
- Narrow domains (Multi-PIE faces, synthetic chairs); unclear generality to diverse categories or real-world scenes.
- Missing comparisons to strong geometry-based pipelines and contemporaneous inverse graphics approaches on shared metrics.
- No ablation studies to isolate contributions (recurrence, action units, curriculum, mask branch).
- Generalization to non-repeating, non-fixed-axis transformations not tested.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add quantitative perceptual metrics (PSNR/SSIM) and, if feasible, a small user study for realism.
- Include stronger baselines: IGN-style encoder–decoder (single-step and iterative), a spatial transformer-based model, and a geometry-based pipeline (e.g., morphable model or pose-estimated mesh with texture mapping) with quantitative error vs. ground truth.
- Provide ablations: (i) single-step non-recurrent model rolled out at test time, (ii) without curriculum, (iii) without action gating, (iv) without mask branch, and (v) with/without feeding previous predictions to test compounding error.
- Test variable and non-circular trajectories (random action sequences, varying step sizes, rotations about non-principal axes if available).
- Strengthen disentanglement analysis with linear probes and leakage metrics; report recognition vs. pose predictability trade-offs across curriculum stages.
- Broaden datasets (e.g., cars, airplanes, other rigid ShapeNet objects; objects in clutter) and report cross-dataset generalization.
- Improve figure sizes and fix minor grammar/typos; consider releasing code to enhance reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper presents an appealing and potentially impactful idea with solid modeling choices and clear writing, but the experimental evaluation is not yet sufficient in breadth and depth to support strong claims of state-of-the-art view synthesis or generalizable disentanglement.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
