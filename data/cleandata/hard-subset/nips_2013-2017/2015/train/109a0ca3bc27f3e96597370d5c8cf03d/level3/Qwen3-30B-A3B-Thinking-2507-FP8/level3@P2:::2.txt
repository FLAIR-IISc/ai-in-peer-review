

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from single images, with a focus on weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors. The authors address the challenging problem of synthesizing novel views of 3D objects (faces and chairs) from a single input image by modeling pose manifold traversal through a sequence of small rotational steps. The key innovation is using a recurrent architecture with action units that control local transformations, allowing the model to learn long-term dependencies without explicit 3D reconstruction. Crucially, the model learns to disentangle identity (what remains constant during rotation) and pose (what changes) without using class labels, enabling view-invariant recognition from the identity units.

The work builds on several recent NIPS contributions. It extends the Inverse Graphics Network (IGN) [17] by adding a recurrent structure to handle sequences of transformations rather than single transformations, and incorporates action units for controllable pose traversal. It also relates to disentangling work like bilinear models [24] and disBM [21], but differs by achieving disentanglement through the learning objective rather than explicit regularization. The paper effectively contrasts with related work on transforming autoencoders [12] and Predictive Gating Pyramid [19] by emphasizing the recurrent structure and weakly-supervised disentangling.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The model is well-motivated and the architecture is clearly explained with appropriate components (encoder, identity units, pose units, action units, decoder). The curriculum learning approach effectively addresses the challenge of training for longer rotation sequences, and the ablation studies (Figure 6) convincingly show how incremental training improves both rendering quality and disentanglement. The experiments on Multi-PIE and chair datasets provide compelling qualitative results (Figures 3-5, 9) and quantitative validation through cross-view recognition (Figure 8) and MSE comparisons (Figure 7). The disentangling property is particularly notable: as shown in Table 1, the identity units achieve near-perfect recognition while pose units perform at random levels, demonstrating effective separation of factors.

The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear motivation, logical organization, and effective use of figures to illustrate concepts. The technical explanations of the recurrent structure and curriculum learning are particularly accessible. The authors thoroughly address the problem's challenges and provide detailed implementation details.

The contributions are significant. The method advances 3D view synthesis beyond previous approaches by handling longer rotation trajectories without explicit 3D models. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose is a valuable contribution to representation learning that has direct applications for view-invariant recognition. The ability to interpolate between object identities at fixed pose (Figure 9) demonstrates the practical utility of the learned representations.

The paper has two main weaknesses. First, the model supports only discrete rotation angles determined by the training data, with no method for continuous-angle rotation. This limits practical applicability for scenarios requiring smooth rotation. Second, the network is trained with fixed-length sequences, which conflicts with the flexibility of typical recurrent networks that should handle variable-length inputs. While the curriculum learning approach mitigates this to some extent, the fixed-length constraint remains a notable limitation.

In terms of the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: Strong technical approach with thorough evaluation. The weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly impressive.
- Clarity: Exceptionally clear writing with well-structured explanations.
- Originality: Novel use of recurrent structure for pose manifold traversal with weakly-supervised disentangling.
- Significance: Advances view synthesis and representation learning with practical applications.

Arguments for acceptance:
1. Novel approach to 3D view synthesis using recurrent structure for pose manifold traversal
2. Effective weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose without class labels
3. Strong experimental validation on multiple datasets with both qualitative and quantitative results
4. Clear, well-written paper with effective figures and explanations
5. Demonstrated utility through cross-view recognition and interpolation

Arguments against acceptance:
1. Limited to discrete rotation angles with no method for continuous rotation
2. Fixed-length sequence training contradicts typical recurrent network flexibility
3. Limited to specific object classes (faces, chairs) without broader generalization claims

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. The weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly noteworthy and could inspire future work in unsupervised representation learning. While the discrete rotation limitation is a drawback, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh this weakness. The work is well-suited for NIPS and addresses an important problem in computer vision with a technically sound and clearly presented solution. I recommend acceptance.
