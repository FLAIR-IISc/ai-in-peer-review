The paper "Weakly-supervised Disentangling with Recurrent Transformations for 3D View Synthesis" proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel views of 3D objects from a single image. The authors demonstrate the quality of their predictions for human faces on the Multi-PIE dataset and for a dataset of 3D chair models. They also show the ability of their model to disentangle latent data factors without using object class labels.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed model and its components, including the encoder network, pose units, and decoder network. They also provide a thorough description of their experiments and results.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to generate high-quality images of 3D objects under various rotations. The authors demonstrate this ability through qualitative experiments on the Multi-PIE and chairs datasets. They also show that their model can learn disentangled features without explicitly regularizing to achieve this effect.

However, the paper has some limitations. The evaluation is largely qualitative, and the authors do not provide sufficient quantitative metrics and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The figures and visualizations could be scaled larger for better readability. Additionally, the experiments are limited to the Multi-PIE and chairs datasets, and more diverse datasets are needed to demonstrate the generality of the proposed model.

The authors also do not test the generalization of their model to non-static-axis or non-repeating rotation trajectories. Furthermore, they do not provide sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art geometry-based approaches. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model.

Despite these limitations, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to 3D view synthesis. The authors' use of a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is a unique contribution to the field. The paper also provides a good starting point for future research in this area.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, and originality. However, it falls short in terms of significance, as the evaluation is not comprehensive enough to demonstrate the impact of the proposed model.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to 3D view synthesis.
* The authors demonstrate the quality of their predictions for human faces and 3D chair models.
* The paper provides a good starting point for future research in this area.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The evaluation is largely qualitative and does not provide sufficient quantitative metrics and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
* The experiments are limited to the Multi-PIE and chairs datasets, and more diverse datasets are needed to demonstrate the generality of the proposed model.
* The paper does not test the generalization of the model to non-static-axis or non-repeating rotation trajectories.

Recommendation:

I recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their proposed model, including quantitative metrics and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The authors should also consider testing the generalization of their model to non-static-axis or non-repeating rotation trajectories and providing more diverse datasets to demonstrate the generality of their approach.
