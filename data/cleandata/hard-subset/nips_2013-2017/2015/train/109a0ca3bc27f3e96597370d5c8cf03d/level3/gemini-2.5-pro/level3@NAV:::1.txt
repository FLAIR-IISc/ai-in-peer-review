This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of an object from a single input image. The core idea is to disentangle an object's representation into an invariant 'identity' component and a 'pose' component, which is then sequentially updated by a recurrent unit that takes transformation actions as input. This approach builds upon prior work in transforming autoencoders and generative convolutional networks, but introduces a recurrent mechanism to handle long-range transformations. The model is trained using a curriculum learning strategy and evaluated on the Multi-PIE (faces) and ShapeNet (chairs) datasets.

The main strength of the paper lies in its novel and elegant formulation. Using a recurrent network to model view synthesis as a sequential manifold traversal is an interesting and promising direction. A significant result is the model's ability to learn disentangled identity and pose representations in a weakly-supervised manner, which is convincingly demonstrated through experiments on cross-view recognition and class interpolation. The paper is also generally well-written and the proposed model is explained clearly.

However, the paper's significance is undermined by several major weaknesses, primarily in its evaluation and experimental scope.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Originality:** The proposed recurrent architecture for sequential view synthesis is a novel and intuitive approach to the problem.
*   **Significance:** The model demonstrates an impressive ability to learn disentangled representations for identity and pose without explicit supervision, a valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the core concepts easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Quality:** The evaluation is largely qualitative and inadequate. Quantitative analysis is limited to a comparison against a simple k-NN baseline. The paper critically lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art deep learning and geometry-based view synthesis methods, making it impossible to assess its performance in the context of existing work.
*   **Significance:** The experiments are limited to faces and chairs, two object classes with relatively constrained poses. To demonstrate generality, evaluation on more diverse datasets is needed. Furthermore, the model is only tested on simple, static-axis rotation trajectories, leaving its ability to generalize to more complex transformations unevaluated.
*   **Clarity:** Many figures are too small, hindering a detailed assessment of the visual results. Minor grammatical errors (e.g., "rotate" vs. "rotated") should also be corrected.

In conclusion, while the proposed method is interesting and the disentangling results are promising, the insufficient experimental validation and lack of comparison to relevant baselines significantly weaken the paper's contribution.
