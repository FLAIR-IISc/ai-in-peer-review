- Proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder that disentangles view-invariant identity features and view-dependent pose features to synthesize novel object views from a single image.  
- Encoder maps input to identity and pose latents; recurrent updates pose using discrete action inputs while identity remains fixed; decoder reconstructs images at each step.  
- Weakly-supervised disentanglement achieved by training to predict long rotation sequences, forcing identity units to retain only view-invariant information.  
- Uses curriculum learning (progressively longer rotation sequences) to improve rendering quality and view-invariance of learned features.  
- Demonstrated on Multi-PIE faces and 3D chairs with strong qualitative renderings and quantitative cross-view recognition results.  
- Relates to prior work (transforming autoencoders, Dosovitskiy et al., Inverse Graphics Networks) but adds an encoder and recurrent trajectory modeling.  

Strengths:
- Original idea: using long transformation sequences as a supervisory signal to learn unsupervised, view-invariant identity representations.  
- Technical quality: sound model and thorough experiments showing high-quality synthesis and strong cross-view recognition; curriculum ablation (RNN1 vs RNN16) supports claims.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly explains architecture and experiments.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited to discrete, categorical rotation actions; no treatment of continuous-angle transformations.  
- Training uses fixed-length sequences per curriculum stage, reducing apparent flexibility of the recurrent model and lacking discussion of variable-length training.  
- Core components are standard (conv encoder-decoder and RNN); novelty mainly in their combination and training objective.  

Overall:
- High-quality, significant contribution with well-supported claims; despite limitations, recommended as an accept and likely to inspire follow-up work.
