

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D object views from a single 2D image, focusing on faces and chairs. The authors develop a novel architecture that uses recurrent pose units with action inputs to model sequential rotations, incorporating curriculum learning to gradually increase rotation trajectory length. The model demonstrates disentangled representations where identity units capture view-invariant features without explicit supervision.

Strengths include a well-structured approach inspired by mental rotation phenomena, clear exposition of the architecture, and interesting qualitative results showing smooth view transitions. The weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose is particularly noteworthy, as evidenced by cross-view recognition experiments where the model achieves competitive performance against supervised CNN baselines.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses that undermine its scientific contribution. The evaluation is largely qualitative with no quantitative metrics or comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., 3D morphable models, deep view synthesis approaches). The authors compare only to KNN baselines for chairs, which are not appropriate for this task. The paper also fails to demonstrate generalization beyond the limited datasets (Multi-PIE and Chairs) and doesn't test on more diverse object categories or non-static rotation trajectories. Figures are too small for proper assessment, and minor grammatical errors detract from professionalism.

While the recurrent architecture is novel and the concept of weakly-supervised disentangling is interesting, the insufficient experimentation and lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation significantly diminish the paper's scientific value. The authors fail to adequately address how their approach advances the state of the art compared to geometry-based methods or recent neural view synthesis work. Without proper quantitative benchmarks and broader experimental validation, the claimed significance is unsubstantiated.

The paper would benefit from adding quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, LPIPS), comparisons to SOTA methods, experiments on more diverse datasets, and clearer demonstration of generalization capabilities. The current version does not meet NIPS standards for quality and significance as a scientific contribution.
