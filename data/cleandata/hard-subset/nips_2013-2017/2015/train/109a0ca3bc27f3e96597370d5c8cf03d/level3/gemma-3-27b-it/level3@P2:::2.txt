This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel views of 3D objects from a single image, specifically focusing on rotating objects in 3D space. The core idea is to learn a mapping between image space and an underlying non-linear manifold, using recurrence to capture long-term dependencies along a sequence of transformations. A key contribution lies in the unsupervised disentangling of latent factors – identity and pose – achieved during training, resulting in view-invariant features useful for object recognition. The approach is evaluated on both face (Multi-PIE) and chair datasets, demonstrating compelling results in both image synthesis and cross-view recognition.

The problem addressed is a long-standing challenge in both computer graphics and computer vision. Previous approaches often rely on explicit 3D model recovery (multi-view stereo, structure-from-motion) or registration to prior 3D models (e.g., morphable models for faces). These methods can be limited by object class specificity, alignment errors, and artifacts in texture synthesis. The authors rightly position their work as a learning-based alternative that avoids explicit 3D reconstruction. Related work is well covered, acknowledging connections to transforming autoencoders [12], predictive gating pyramids [19], and generative CNNs [8], and clearly differentiating the proposed method from these prior efforts. The discussion of the Inverse Graphics Network [17] and disentangling latent factors [21, 24, 6] demonstrates awareness of current research directions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The recurrent architecture combined with disentangled latent representations for 3D view synthesis is a novel approach. The use of action units to control pose manifold traversal is a clever mechanism.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed network architecture appears well-motivated and technically sound, combining established components (CNNs, RNNs) in a novel configuration. The curriculum learning strategy is also a sensible approach to training recurrent networks for sequential tasks.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both Multi-PIE and chair datasets are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Qualitative results (rendered images) are visually appealing, and quantitative results (cross-view recognition) show competitive performance. The ablation study regarding curriculum training further strengthens the analysis.
*   **Unsupervised Disentangling:**  The emergence of view-invariant identity features without explicit supervision is a significant outcome, showcasing the potential for learning useful representations during the view synthesis task.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanation of the model components and the training process is clear and concise. The use of figures aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Discrete Rotation:**  The model is currently limited to discrete rotation angles dictated by the training data. The paper does not address how to extend the approach to support continuous-angle rotations, which would be a practical limitation in many applications.
*   **Fixed-Length Sequences:** The use of fixed-length sequences in the recurrent network somewhat contradicts the typical flexibility of RNNs, which are generally designed to handle variable-length inputs. While curriculum learning mitigates this, it's a design choice that warrants further discussion.
*   **Limited Scope of Transformations:** The method currently focuses solely on 3D rotations. Exploring other transformations (e.g., scaling, translation, deformations) would broaden the applicability of the approach.
*   **Comparison with Strong Baselines:** While the comparison with KNN is reasonable, a comparison to more sophisticated view synthesis or 3D reconstruction techniques, even if more complex, would further strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to 3D view synthesis.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The unsupervised disentangling of latent factors is a significant contribution.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
*   The approach addresses a challenging and important problem in computer vision and graphics.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limitation to discrete rotation angles may restrict the practical applicability of the method.
*   The use of fixed-length sequences in the recurrent network is a potentially suboptimal design choice.
*   The lack of comparison with stronger baseline methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis. The proposed recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network is novel, technically sound, and well-supported by experimental results. While the limitations regarding discrete rotations and fixed-length sequences are valid concerns, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The unsupervised disentangling of latent factors is a particularly noteworthy achievement. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. I suggest the authors briefly discuss the limitations related to continuous-angle rotations and fixed-length sequences in the conclusion and potentially outline future work to address these issues. Adding a comparison with a stronger baseline method (if feasible) would also be beneficial.
