This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image, specifically focusing on faces and chairs. The core idea is to model pose manifold traversal using a recurrent structure, allowing the network to capture long-term dependencies across rotation steps. The approach is trained end-to-end and demonstrates the ability to disentangle latent factors without explicit class labels.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a challenging problem at the intersection of graphics and vision. The proposed approach, leveraging recurrence to model pose changes, is a reasonable and potentially effective strategy. The experimental results on both Multi-PIE and a chair dataset are promising, showing qualitative improvements in rendering quality and quantitative gains in cross-view recognition. The curriculum learning approach is also a sensible addition. The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant areas such as transforming autoencoders, predictive gating pyramids, and disentangled representation learning. The inclusion of comparisons to a KNN baseline, and a 3D morphable model (for faces) strengthens the evaluation.

However, several areas could benefit from improvement. While the network architecture is described, the details of the encoder and decoder (number of layers, filter sizes, etc.) feel somewhat buried and could be presented more clearly – perhaps a table summarizing the architecture. The choice of a 1-of-3 encoding for the action units seems arbitrary; a justification for this choice, or an exploration of alternative encodings, would be helpful.  The ablation studies are limited.  For example, what is the impact of removing the recurrent component?  How sensitive is performance to the curriculum learning schedule?  The disentanglement claim, while interesting, is demonstrated primarily through the observation that identity units perform well on recognition. A more direct evaluation of disentanglement (e.g., measuring the independence of identity and pose representations) would strengthen this claim.  The discussion of why the VGG-16 outperforms the RNN for chair recognition at large angle offsets is insightful but feels somewhat incomplete. Further investigation of the symmetries in chair data and how they relate to network performance would be valuable. The paper lacks error analysis. Showing examples of failure cases and discussing the limitations of the approach would be beneficial. The notation could be improved for clarity, particularly within equation (1). 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a challenging and important problem in vision and graphics.
*   The proposed recurrent architecture is a novel and promising approach.
*   Strong experimental results on two datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The disentanglement property, even without explicit supervision, is noteworthy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The architectural details could be presented more clearly.
*   Limited ablation studies prevent a thorough understanding of the contribution of each component.
*   The disentanglement claim could be supported with more rigorous analysis.
*   Lack of error analysis and discussion of limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis. While some aspects could be improved with further experimentation and analysis, the novelty of the approach, the quality of the results, and the comprehensive related work suggest that this paper is suitable for acceptance.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper. A major revision is recommended, prioritizing the addition of ablation studies and a more detailed analysis of the disentanglement property.
