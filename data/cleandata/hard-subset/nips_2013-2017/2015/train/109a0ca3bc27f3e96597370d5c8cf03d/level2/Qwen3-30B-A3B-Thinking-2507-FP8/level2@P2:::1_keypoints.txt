- Proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder that models pose traversal as sequential small rotations with action units to synthesize novel 3D views from single images.  
- Uses curriculum learning to train the recurrent pose traversal, improving long-term synthesis and disentanglement.  
- Evaluated on face rotation (Multi-PIE) and chair view synthesis (CAD models), with qualitative results showing smooth transformations and preserved identity.  
- Interpolation experiments indicate generative disentangling of identity and pose in latent space.  
- Architecture choices (no pooling/unpooling) and relation to prior work are well-motivated and contextualized.  
- Main flaw: overstates "weakly-supervised disentangling" without direct factor-manipulation experiments to prove true disentanglement.  
- Evidence for disentanglement is limited to improved cross-view recognition, which shows pose invariance but not necessarily disentangled factors.  
- Baseline comparisons are inadequate: KNN for chairs is weak, and no direct comparisons to relevant prior view-synthesis methods (e.g., [8], [9], [28]).  
- Dataset limitations: CAD chair dataset is highly controlled and does not demonstrate robustness to real-world occlusion or clutter.  
- Terminology issues: "weakly-supervised" is misleading since rotations/actions are provided during training, and invariance is conflated with disentanglement.  
- Some quantitative results (e.g., RNN16 MSE) lack comparison context to existing methods.  
- Overall: technically sound and promising for controlled data, but the central disentanglement claim is unsubstantiated; recommendation: Weak Reject pending stronger disentanglement experiments, better baselines, and clearer claims.
