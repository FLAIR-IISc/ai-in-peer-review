- Paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder that applies sequences of 3D rotations via disentangled identity and pose latent units plus explicit action inputs.  
- Training is end-to-end with pixel loss over sequences and curriculum learning to increase sequence length for stable long-term predictions.  
- Differs from prior work by using convolutional encoder–decoder, explicit action inputs, learned encoder for novel images, and recurrent multi-step control (no VAE).  
- Strength: coherent architecture linking representation learning, control signals, and multi-step latent-space dynamics to avoid pixel-space error accumulation.  
- Strength: curriculum learning improves synthesis quality and emergence of disentangled identity features across longer horizons.  
- Strength: strong qualitative results on Multi-PIE faces and rendered chairs, including smooth rotations and class interpolation.  
- Strength: cross-view recognition shows identity units are view-invariant while pose units are not, supporting weakly-supervised disentangling.  
- Strength: sufficient technical details (architectures, training schedules) to facilitate reproduction.  
- Weakness: synthesis evaluation relies mainly on MSE, lacking SSIM/L1/perceptual metrics or human studies; MSE biases toward blur.  
- Weakness: baselines are weak (3DMM for faces; KNN for chairs); stronger baselines and ablations (non-recurrent, no action units, closed-loop) are missing.  
- Weakness: action input is discrete 1-of-3; continuous or finer-grained control and extrapolation to unseen magnitudes are not tested.  
- Weakness: datasets are highly controlled (aligned faces, rendered chairs); generalization to in-the-wild images with clutter/occlusion is unclear.  
- Weakness: disentangling evidence is indirect (recognition and swap test); more direct quantitative measures would strengthen the claim.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of failure cases and long-horizon stability; no cyclic consistency reported and degradation beyond trained horizon observed.  
- Weakness: unclear role of chair foreground mask branch and lack of ablation on auxiliary mask loss and weighting.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-organized with informative figures, but could clarify recurrence details (gating, weight sharing) and exact angular steps.  
- Originality: novel combination of recurrent pose updates, explicit action inputs, and encoder–decoder for long rotations; curriculum insight is useful.  
- Significance: meaningful within category-specific view synthesis and disentangling; ideas may transfer but applicability is limited by class-specific, controlled training data.  
- Suggested experiments: report SSIM/L1/human studies, compare to IGN/encoder+decoder baselines, test continuous actions, linear probes for angle predictability, cyclic consistency, closed-loop encoding, and ablations (mask, dimensionality, LSTM/GRU, curriculum).  
- Overall: technically sound, convincing qualitative and recognition evidence, but needs stronger quantitative metrics, baselines, and ablations; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
