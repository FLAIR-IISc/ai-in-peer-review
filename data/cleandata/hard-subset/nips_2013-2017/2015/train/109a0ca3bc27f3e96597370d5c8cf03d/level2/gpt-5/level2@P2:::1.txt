Summary
This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder that performs controlled 3D view synthesis from a single image by traversing a pose manifold with small, action-conditioned steps. The model splits a bottleneck representation into “identity” units (shared across time) and “pose” units (updated recurrently given an action input indicating clockwise/noop/counterclockwise). Training uses backpropagation through time with a curriculum on sequence length, encouraging long-term consistency and emergent disentangling of identity and pose. Experiments on Multi-PIE faces and a rendered chairs dataset show: (1) smooth long-range rotations with preserved identity, (2) improved reconstruction error over a KNN baseline on chairs, (3) cross-view recognition using identity units that improves with curriculum length and is competitive with a supervised CNN on faces, and (4) interpolation in identity space producing plausible novel chairs. The work is positioned with respect to transforming autoencoders, gating networks/PGP, Dosovitskiy et al. (chair generator), and the Inverse Graphics Network.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and a simple, effective architecture that supports controlled multi-step transformations without explicit 3D reconstruction.
- The recurrent design with action inputs is a natural, scalable way to realize “mental rotation” and avoids feeding back pixels; identity is shared and pose is updated in latent space.
- Curriculum training is well-motivated for long-term prediction; the paper clearly demonstrates its effect on both synthesis quality and recognition invariance.
- Disentangling emerges from the objective without labels; identity units yield strong cross-view recognition, while pose units do not carry identity, confirming separation.
- Convincing qualitative results for long sequences (up to 16 steps on chairs) and an informative quantitative evaluation against a KNN baseline.
- Interpolation experiments are compelling and demonstrate smooth control over identity and pose.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Supervision strength: while called “weakly supervised,” training relies on paired views with known relative rotations (action labels) and, for faces, alignment; discuss the extent to which pose supervision is required and whether approximate/unknown pose could be handled.
- Missing or underspecified details: the exact recurrent update for pose units (e.g., explicit equation, parameterization of action-conditioned transformation matrices, weight sharing across time) is not fully specified, which may hinder reproducibility.
- Baselines: on chairs, comparison is only to KNN; more direct comparisons to contemporaneous image-generation approaches (e.g., Dosovitskiy et al. for chairs, IGN) would strengthen claims, even if adapted (e.g., encode-then-decode vs. recurrent rollout).
- Metrics: reconstruction is evaluated with MSE only; perceptual metrics (e.g., SSIM) or user studies would better reflect visual quality. Recognition results are informative but could be complemented with standard identification/verification metrics.
- Scope: results are limited to two categories with constrained backgrounds and known camera rigs; generalization to more diverse objects/scenes is not tested.
- Handling ambiguity: the deterministic decoder produces a single completion of unseen content; the paper acknowledges multi-modality but does not explore it. This limits realism for severe occlusions/large rotations.
- Control granularity: actions are discrete (± one step, noop). It is unclear whether the model can handle arbitrary step sizes or continuous control.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a coherent architecture and training procedure. The empirical evaluation is thorough within the chosen domains and ablations across curriculum length are insightful. However, the lack of stronger baselines and limited metrics modestly weaken the empirical case. Some architectural details (precise recurrence/action operator) should be clarified for reproducibility.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The motivation for recurrence and curriculum is clear, and datasets/training schedules are described. Please add:
- A precise recurrence equation for pose update, dimensionalities of action-conditioned matrices, and whether they are shared across time and objects.
- Clarify how the mask branch is combined with the image output and provide an ablation showing its effect.
- More details on sequence sampling during curriculum (distribution over start poses/directions) and on how the noop action is used during training.
- For faces, discuss the impact of landmark alignment accuracy and any pre-processing variability.

Originality
The combination of a recurrent, action-conditioned encoder–decoder with shared identity and recurrent pose units for long-horizon 3D view synthesis is a novel and timely contribution distinct from prior transforming/gating models and from contemporaneous inverse graphics work. The emergent disentangling via curriculum, without explicit disentanglement losses, is interesting.

Significance
Synthesis of novel views from a single image is important for graphics and vision. The approach is simple, computationally practical, and yields useful representations for cross-view recognition. While demonstrated on limited domains, the ideas (action-conditioned recurrence, curriculum for disentangling) are likely to influence subsequent work on controllable generation and representation learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines: adapt IGN and Dosovitskiy et al. (encode-then-decode) to the same chair protocol; include a non-recurrent multi-step baseline (e.g., repeated application of a single-step feedforward transform with shared weights) to isolate the benefit of recurrence.
- Report SSIM (and possibly perceptual distances) alongside MSE; consider a small user study to assess realism for large rotations.
- Provide an ablation on: (i) identity/pose dimensionalities, (ii) presence/absence of the mask branch, and (iii) curriculum vs. direct long-horizon training.
- Explore data augmentation and symmetry priors (e.g., mirroring for chairs) to mitigate the observed performance drop at large offsets and leverage object symmetries.
- Discuss or demonstrate continuous control (fractional steps) and generalization to other actions (e.g., elevation, scaling, lighting).
- Consider a stochastic or variational extension to capture one-to-many mappings for severe occlusions.
- Release code/models to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated architecture for controlled, long-horizon view synthesis.
- Clear empirical evidence that curriculum enables better synthesis and disentangling.
- Strong qualitative results and reasonable quantitative gains over baselines.
- Useful unsupervised identity features for cross-view recognition.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited baselines and metrics; missing direct comparisons to closest contemporaries.
- Some implementation details are under-specified.
- Demonstrations restricted to two curated categories; limited evidence of broader applicability.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper advances controllable image synthesis with a simple, effective recurrent formulation and presents solid evidence of emergent disentangling and practical benefits. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation gaps would further strengthen the contribution.
