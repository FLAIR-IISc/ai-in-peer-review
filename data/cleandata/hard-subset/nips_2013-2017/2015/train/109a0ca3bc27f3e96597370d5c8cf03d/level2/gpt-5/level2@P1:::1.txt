Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles single-image novel view synthesis by learning to apply sequences of 3D rotations to objects (faces and chairs). The proposed model is a recurrent convolutional encoder–decoder with three key ingredients: (i) an encoder producing disentangled latent “identity” and “pose” units, (ii) action units that specify local rotation direction, and (iii) a recurrent update on pose units, while identity units are shared across time. The decoder renders an image at each step. Training is end-to-end with pixel-level loss over sequences, and curriculum learning increases sequence length progressively to stabilize long-term predictions. Beyond synthesis, the authors show that identity units become view-invariant enough to support cross-view recognition without class labels, i.e., weakly supervised disentangling.

Relative to prior work: compared to transforming autoencoders and PGP, this work uses a convolutional encoder–decoder, explicit action inputs, and emphasizes long rotation trajectories. It differs from Dosovitskiy et al.’s chair generator by learning an encoder that generalizes to novel images and using recurrent transformations, and from the contemporaneous IGN by not using a VAE and by focusing on recurrent multi-step control. It relates to disentangling (bilinear models, disBM, multi-view perceptron) but does not impose explicit disentangling regularizers.

Strengths:
- Clear and coherent architecture that links representation learning, control signals, and multi-step prediction. The choice to keep the dynamics in latent space avoids compounding pixel-space errors.
- Curriculum learning yields demonstrable improvements in both synthesis quality and emergence of disentangled identity features, with sensible analyses across increasing horizons.
- Compelling qualitative results on Multi-PIE faces and rendered chairs; identity preservation and smooth rotational trajectories are evident. Class interpolation experiments are a nice demonstration of the learned manifolds.
- Cross-view recognition results substantiate the claim of weakly-supervised disentangling: identity units are view-invariant; pose units are poor for identity, supporting factor separation.
- Technical details (architectures, training schedules) are provided to a level that facilitates reproduction.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation for synthesis relies mainly on MSE, which is known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality and can bias toward blur. No SSIM/L1/perceptual metrics or human studies are reported. For faces, comparison is only to a 3DMM-based method; for chairs, the KNN baseline is weak relative to existing image generators (e.g., Dosovitskiy et al.). A stronger baseline or ablations (e.g., non-recurrent variant; no action units; feeding predicted images back into encoder) would better support the design choices.
- The action input is a discrete 1-of-3 signal. A continuous control (e.g., angle scalar or one-hot over multiple step sizes) could allow arbitrary step sizes and off-grid rotations. The paper does not test interpolation/extrapolation to unseen rotation magnitudes.
- Evaluation domains are highly controlled: aligned faces and rendered chairs with clean backgrounds. Generalization to in-the-wild images with clutter, lighting changes, and occlusions is unclear.
- “Disentangling” is demonstrated via recognition accuracy and a simple pose/identity swap test. A more direct, quantitative measure (e.g., linear predictability of angle from identity units, mutual information estimates, or adversarial classifiers) would strengthen the claim.
- Limited analysis of failure cases and stability beyond the trained horizon; the model appears to degrade when rolling out much longer than trained (e.g., RNN1). Cyclic consistency (back-and-forth rotations) is not reported.
- Chairs include a foreground mask branch; it’s unclear whether this branch is essential. The effect of the auxiliary mask loss and λ is not ablated.

Quality:
The method is technically sound and the experiments support the main claims, especially the utility of curriculum training and the emergence of view-invariant identity features. Some evaluation choices could be strengthened with better baselines and metrics. The paper is careful to distinguish from closely related work and to control for data splits.

Clarity:
Generally well-organized and readable. The architecture and training schedule are described with sufficient detail. Figures (as described) seem informative, though it would help to include more explicit failure analyses and a schematic of the recurrent pose update. Minor suggestions: clarify whether the recurrent update is linear-gated or has nonlinearity; specify if weights are shared across time; and state the exact angular step used for faces and chairs.

Originality:
Combining a recurrent pose update with explicit action inputs and an encoder–decoder for long rotation sequences is a novel and interesting contribution. The curriculum training for improved disentangling is a useful insight. While related components exist, the overall formulation and demonstrations feel original within the 2015 landscape.

Significance:
Within category-specific view synthesis, this is a meaningful step that connects sequence prediction and representation disentangling, with potential impact on controllable generation and recognition. Applicability may be limited by the need for class-specific training and controlled data, but the ideas (action-driven latent recurrence, curriculum) could transfer to broader settings.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Could you report SSIM/L1 and/or a small human preference study for synthesis quality? For faces, can you compare to IGN or a learned encoder+decoder baseline without recurrence?
- How does performance change with continuous action inputs (e.g., angle scalar) or larger step vocabularies?
- Do identity units linearly encode identity? Can you show a linear probe predicting angle from identity units (should be low R^2) and from pose units (high R^2)?
- Can you report cyclic consistency (rotate +k then −k) and long-horizon stability beyond training length?
- What happens if you feed the predicted image back through the encoder at each step (“closed-loop”)? Any advantage/disadvantage vs your latent-only recurrence?
- Ablations: mask head for chairs; dimensionality of identity/pose; using LSTM/GRU for pose units; removing curriculum.

Pros:
- Elegant model that unifies action-driven recurrent transformations with disentangled representations.
- Strong qualitative results and convincing recognition-based evidence of disentangling.
- Curriculum training demonstrably improves long-term synthesis and invariance.

Cons:
- Weak quantitative baselines and metrics for image quality.
- Evaluations limited to controlled datasets; unknown robustness in the wild.
- Disentangling evidence could be more direct and comprehensive.

Overall: A solid and timely contribution with clear ideas and promising results. Strengthening quantitative evaluation and adding ablations would further solidify the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
