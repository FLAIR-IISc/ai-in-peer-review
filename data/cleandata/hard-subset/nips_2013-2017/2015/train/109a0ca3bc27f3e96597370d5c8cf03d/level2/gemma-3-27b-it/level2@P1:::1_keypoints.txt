- Proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder that models 3D rotation as a sequence of small steps guided by a control signal.  
- Tackles an important problem (3D view synthesis) with motivation tied to rendering and vision tasks.  
- Related work is comprehensive and the paper is well-contextualized.  
- Novelty: using recurrence plus control signal for transformations and showing weakly-supervised disentangling.  
- Empirical results on Multi-PIE and chairs are visually convincing and supported by quantitative metrics (MSE, cross-view recognition).  
- Curriculum learning (longer training sequences) improves performance, as demonstrated.  
- Paper is clearly written with helpful diagrams explaining architecture and training.  
- Experiments explore curriculum effects, disentangling, and class interpolation.  
- Authors acknowledge some limitations (e.g., chair symmetry affecting recognition).  
- Weakness: comparisons are limited to a simple KNN baseline; lacks stronger recent baselines (e.g., differentiable rendering methods).  
- Weakness: disentangling evaluation is indirect (cross-view recognition); more direct quantitative measures are missing.  
- Weakness: pose units perform poorly and the analysis of what they encode is incomplete.  
- Weakness: computational cost for training/inference is not reported, harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness: limited discussion and analysis of failure cases where the model struggles.  
- Weakness: the description of action units (1-of-3 encoding) is vague and needs clarification.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound approach with strong empirical evidence and clear exposition.  
- Arguments for rejection: insufficient baseline comparisons, limited disentangling evaluation, and missing compute/failure analyses.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution leaning toward acceptance with minor revisions; recommend adding a differentiable-rendering comparison and more quantitative disentangling evaluation.
