

This paper proposes a recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for 3D view synthesis from a single image, addressing the challenging problem of predicting rotated views of objects. The key innovation lies in using a recurrent structure to handle long-term dependencies during rotation sequences, where the model learns to apply transformations incrementally through a sequence of small rotations. The model features identity units that remain invariant to pose changes and pose units that capture rotational information, with the recurrent structure allowing the network to maintain identity information across multiple transformation steps. The authors demonstrate that this approach enables weakly-supervised disentangling of identity and pose factors without explicit class labels.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a novel recurrent architecture specifically designed for sequential 3D transformations, which differs from prior work using capsules [12], gating networks [7, 19], or inverse graphics [17]. Second, the model demonstrates impressive performance in synthesizing high-quality rotated views for both faces (Multi-PIE dataset) and chairs (CAD model dataset), with smooth transitions between views and preservation of key object characteristics. Third, the paper shows that the model learns disentangled representations through curriculum training, where longer rotation sequences force the identity units to better separate from pose information. This weakly-supervised disentangling is particularly notable as it emerges naturally from the training objective rather than requiring explicit regularization.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors provide detailed results on both datasets, with quantitative comparisons against a KNN baseline for chair synthesis. The RNN models consistently outperform the KNN baseline, with the RNN16 model achieving a 42% improvement in MSE over the best KNN variant (KNN5). The cross-view recognition experiments effectively demonstrate the quality of the disentangled representations, with RNN6 achieving competitive performance against a CNN classifier trained with class labels. The ablation studies on curriculum training stages clearly show the benefits of gradually increasing sequence length, with longer training sequences improving both image synthesis quality and recognition performance. The chair interpolation experiments further validate the model's ability to generate novel object instances by interpolating identity units.

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The model architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is detailed enough to allow reproduction. The authors adequately address related work, particularly differentiating their approach from previous methods for 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. The paper's contributions are clearly positioned within the context of NIPS research, with appropriate citations to relevant prior work.

The main limitations concern the scope of evaluation and potential generalizability. The chair dataset is limited to 809 models out of 1393 available, and the paper does not explore more complex scenes or occluded objects. While the disentangling property is impressive, the paper does not thoroughly investigate the limits of this disentanglement (e.g., how the model handles objects with significant appearance changes across views). Additionally, the weakly-supervised disentangling claim could be clarified - the disentanglement emerges from the self-supervised rotation prediction task rather than from weak supervision in the traditional sense.

The significance of this work is high. The ability to synthesize novel views from single images has practical applications in photo editing, virtual reality, and robotics. The weakly-supervised disentangling approach could benefit other vision tasks requiring identity-pose separation. The model's performance on challenging datasets like Multi-PIE and chairs demonstrates clear advancement over previous methods, particularly the significant improvement over the KNN baseline. The paper effectively shows that the recurrent structure enables better long-term prediction and more discriminative representations than single-step approaches.

In terms of originality, the recurrent architecture for handling rotation sequences represents a meaningful contribution to the literature on 3D view synthesis. While some concepts like disentangled representations have appeared in other works [21, 24, 29], the specific way they emerge from the rotation prediction task is novel. The paper's technical approach is distinct from previous methods that either require explicit 3D models [5, 8] or use different architectures for transformation tasks [7, 12, 19].

Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution to the field of 3D view synthesis and disentangled representation learning. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. The only major limitation is the restricted scope of the evaluation to controlled datasets without complex scenes. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential applications to more complex scenarios and limitations of the current approach. Given the quality of the work and its significance, this paper represents a strong contribution to the field and deserves consideration for acceptance.
