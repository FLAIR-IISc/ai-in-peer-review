This paper proposes a novel recurrent convolutional encoder-decoder network for synthesizing novel 3D views of an object from a single input image. The core idea is to model large 3D rotations as a sequence of smaller, controlled transformations within a disentangled latent space. The latent representation is split into view-invariant "identity" units and recurrent "pose" units, which are updated at each step by an action input. The authors demonstrate that by training the network on progressively longer transformation sequences (curriculum learning), it not only generates high-quality images but also learns to disentangle identity from pose without explicit supervision. This work builds upon prior efforts in generative modeling and disentangled representation learning, such as Transforming Autoencoders and Inverse Graphics Networks, but distinguishes itself through its use of a recurrent architecture to handle long-term dependencies in transformation sequences.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper's main contribution—achieving weakly-supervised disentanglement of identity and pose simply by training a recurrent model to predict transformation sequences—is a significant and elegant idea. It provides a novel and effective mechanism for learning view-invariant representations.
*   **Quality:** The claims are well-supported by thorough experiments on two distinct datasets (Multi-PIE faces and rendered chairs). The qualitative results for view synthesis are convincing, showing smooth and identity-preserving rotations for unseen objects. Quantitatively, the model significantly outperforms a k-NN baseline on reconstruction error. The cross-view recognition experiments provide strong evidence for the disentanglement claim, with the model achieving performance competitive with a fully supervised CNN without using any class labels.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The model architecture and training procedure, including the curriculum learning strategy, are explained in sufficient detail to suggest good reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of its limitations. For instance, the model currently handles single, centered objects on clean backgrounds and is only demonstrated for out-of-plane rotation. It is unclear how it would perform on more complex scenes or with other transformations like lighting changes or non-rigid deformations.
*   The observation that a pre-trained VGG network outperforms the model's identity features for chair recognition at very large angles is interesting. While the authors hypothesize this is due to learned symmetries, this point could be strengthened by further analysis or discussion on how such invariances could be incorporated into their model.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel approach with strong empirical validation. The core idea is insightful and the results are impressive, making a valuable contribution to generative modeling and representation learning.
