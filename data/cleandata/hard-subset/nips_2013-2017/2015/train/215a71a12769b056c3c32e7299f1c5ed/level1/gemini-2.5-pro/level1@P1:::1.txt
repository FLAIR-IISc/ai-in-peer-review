### Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the critical problem of training very deep feedforward neural networks. While empirical and theoretical evidence suggests that network depth is a key factor for model performance, training becomes progressively harder as depth increases, primarily due to difficulties in propagating information and gradients through many non-linear layers (the vanishing/exploding gradients problem).

The authors propose a novel architecture, called "highway networks," designed to alleviate this issue. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanism of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. In a highway network, each layer's output is a gated combination of a non-linear transformation of its input, H(x), and the input itself, x. The output is computed as `y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 - T(x))`, where T(x) is a "transform gate" and (1 - T(x)) is a "carry gate." These gates are implemented as sigmoid functions of an affine transformation of the input.

This architecture allows the network to dynamically regulate the flow of information. If a transform gate T(x) outputs a value close to 0, the layer acts as an identity function, allowing information to pass unimpeded along an "information highway." If T(x) is close to 1, the layer behaves like a conventional non-linear transformation. By initializing the biases of the transform gates to negative values, the network starts training in a state where information flows easily, which the authors show is sufficient to enable stable training via SGD even for networks with hundreds of layers.

The paper provides strong empirical validation. Experiments on MNIST show that while plain networks degrade significantly as depth increases from 20 to 100 layers, highway networks maintain strong performance. On CIFAR-10, the authors demonstrate that their method can directly train deep, thin networks that match or outperform "FitNets," which required a complex, two-stage hint-based training procedure for similar architectures. Finally, an insightful analysis section visualizes the learned gate behavior, showing that the gates implement a form of data-dependent information routing and that the network learns to use an "effective depth" appropriate for the complexity of the task. This work directly builds on the long line of research into training deep networks, offering a more flexible alternative to fixed skip-connections (e.g., Szegedy et al., 2014) and a simpler training paradigm than teacher-student approaches (Romero et al., 2015).

---

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The proposed method is sound, well-motivated, and directly addresses a recognized and important problem. The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed set of experiments. The optimization experiments on MNIST provide a clear, controlled comparison that convincingly demonstrates the advantage of highway networks over plain networks in extreme depth settings. The comparison to FitNets is particularly powerful, as it shows that the proposed architecture simplifies the training of previously hard-to-train models. The analysis in Section 4 is a major strength, going beyond simple performance metrics to provide insight into *how* the networks function. The lesion study (Figure 4) offers a novel and compelling visualization of the network learning to utilize depth according to task complexity. This is a complete and carefully executed piece of research.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the proposed architecture is explained with a simple and precise mathematical formulation, and the experimental setup and results are described in sufficient detail. Figures are clear, informative, and effectively support the paper's narrative. The authors have provided a link to their source code, which greatly enhances reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the gating mechanism is borrowed from the LSTM literature (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), its application to the *depth* dimension of a feedforward network is a novel and powerful conceptual leap. This reframing of a recurrent network mechanism for feedforward architectures provides a more flexible and adaptive solution than the fixed skip-connections that were being explored in concurrent work. The idea of learning an "effective depth" and the method for analyzing it via lesioning are also significant original contributions. The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself relative to prior art, including initialization strategies, optimizers, and other architectural modifications.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is profound. It provides one of the first practical and elegant solutions for training feedforward networks that are orders of magnitude deeper than what was previously feasible. This opens up entirely new avenues for architectural exploration and has the potential to impact a wide range of applications. The core idea—creating paths for unimpeded information flow through identity-like mappings—is a fundamental insight that is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by the community. The paper doesn't just present a method; it provides a new tool for understanding the role of depth in neural networks. This work is a clear and significant advance for the field.

---

**Arguments for and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Solves a Major Problem:** Addresses the critical and long-standing challenge of training very deep neural networks.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Solution:** The highway network architecture is a simple, intuitive, and highly effective idea, cleverly adapting concepts from RNNs to feedforward models.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The paper provides compelling results across multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100) that rigorously validate the method's effectiveness and superiority over standard deep networks.
4.  **Excellent Analysis:** The analysis of gate activations and the layer lesioning study provide deep insights into the model's behavior, adding significant scientific value beyond just a performance improvement.
5.  **High Potential Impact:** The work is likely to be highly influential, enabling the community to explore much deeper architectures and inspiring further research into dynamic network structures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   It is difficult to find significant weaknesses in this paper. One could argue that the core gating mechanism is not entirely novel as it is directly inspired by LSTMs. However, the application and framing in this new context are so innovative and impactful that this does not detract from the paper's contribution.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that presents a foundational contribution to the field of deep learning. It is technically sound, clearly presented, highly original, and of major significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
