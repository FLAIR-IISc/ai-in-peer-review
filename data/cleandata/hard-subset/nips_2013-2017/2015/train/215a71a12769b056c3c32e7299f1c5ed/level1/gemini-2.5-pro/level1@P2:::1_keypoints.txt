- Introduces highway networks that enable training of extremely deep feedforward networks using LSTM-style gating to control information flow.  
- Highway layers use transform (T) and carry (C) gates with y = H(x)·T(x) + x·C(x); with C=1−T they interpolate between identity and nonlinear mapping.  
- Gating creates “information highways” that mitigate vanishing gradients and allow unimpeded gradient flow across many layers.  
- Empirically demonstrates direct training of 50, 100, and even 1000-layer networks using standard SGD, unlike prior complex methods.  
- High technical quality: experiments (e.g., MNIST optimization) convincingly show superiority of highway over plain networks as depth increases.  
- Comparison with FitNets shows comparable or better performance with a simpler, single-stage training process.  
- Analysis (gate visualizations and lesion study) provides insight that gates learn data-dependent routing and the network uses depth effectively.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written; notation, experimental setup, and figures are informative and reproducible.  
- Highly original: applies learnable, data-dependent gating from RNNs to control feedforward network depth, distinct from fixed skip connections.  
- Potentially high significance: opens the door to exploring much deeper feedforward architectures and shows networks can learn an “effective depth.”  
- Strength: provides a practical solution to a major, long-standing problem in deep learning optimization.  
- Strength: the idea is elegant and intuitively connects RNN gating to feedforward depth control.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation across increasing depths supports the central claim.  
- Strength: simplicity — can be trained directly with SGD and a simple initialization trick.  
- Strength: insightful analyses go beyond performance to explain learned behavior.  
- Weakness: primarily empirical; lacks deeper theoretical analysis of gradient dynamics and layer Jacobians.  
- Weakness: performance sensitive to transform gate bias initialization; requires more thorough sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: CIFAR-10/100 results are good but not state-of-the-art, leaving open how far this scales on harder benchmarks.  
- Overall conclusion: significant, original, and well-supported work with minor weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
