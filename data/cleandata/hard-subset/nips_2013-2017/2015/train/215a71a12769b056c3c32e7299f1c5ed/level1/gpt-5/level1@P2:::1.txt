Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that uses learned, LSTM-inspired gates to regulate information flow across depth: each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), with T a sigmoid “transform gate” and the “carry” gate set to 1−T. A negative bias initialization on T biases layers initially toward carrying (identity), facilitating gradient flow over many layers. The authors provide optimization studies on fully connected networks (up to 100 layers) and convolutional experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, along with analyses of gate activity and layer ablation (“lesioning”).

This work is closely related to ideas in LSTM gating for temporal credit assignment (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Gers et al., 2000) and to skip/shortcut connections used historically (e.g., “deeply supervised” nets and injection of error to intermediate layers; skip connections in conv nets). It addresses limitations of initialization strategies (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.) and optimization methods (Martens; Sutskever et al.; Dauphin et al.) that improve, but do not fully solve, training at extreme depth. It is also germane to thin/deep training strategies like FitNets (Romero et al.), and locally competitive units/maxout (Goodfellow et al.; Srivastava et al.), by offering an architectural route to train deep, thin models with standard SGD in a single stage.

Quality:
- Soundness: The architectural formulation is clear and mathematically straightforward; the gating mechanism directly interpolates between identity and a learned transform, ensuring a well-conditioned path for gradient propagation. The negative-bias initialization rationale is sensible and consistent with LSTM practice. While no formal convergence guarantees are offered, the empirical optimization results are strong: plain MLPs degrade sharply with depth, whereas highway MLPs train well (10–100 layers) and converge faster.
- Empirical support: The MNIST optimization study is thorough (100 random hyperparameter trials per setting, parameter-matched plain vs. highway). Convolutional experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 demonstrate that highway nets reach competitive accuracy with relatively small parameter budgets and without multi-stage distillation. The lesioning and gate-activity analyses are insightful and substantiate claims about data-dependent routing and layer utilization.
- Limitations: Experimental scope is modest on large-scale tasks (no ImageNet); CIFAR results are not state-of-the-art under strong augmentation, and comparisons to non-gated skip connections are absent. Compute/FLOP and wall-clock analyses are not reported; gating adds parameters and multiplications that deserve quantification. The choice C=1−T is a design decision that may restrict flexibility (no simultaneous strong carry and transform); an ablation with independent C would strengthen the case. A quantitative analysis of gradient norms across depth would further support the optimization claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and easy to follow. The notation is clear, the motivation is well tied to LSTM gating, and the training recipe (negative T-bias) is explicit. Figures analyzing bias, gate activity, and lesioning are informative.
- Some details could be expanded: exact architectures per CIFAR experiment (filter sizes, channels per layer), computational cost comparisons to FitNets, and hyperparameter schedules for all datasets. A concise pseudocode block or layer diagram would help practitioners.

Originality:
- The core idea—applying LSTM-style gating to deep feedforward paths to produce input-dependent, differentiable highways—is novel and distinct from prior fixed skip/shortcut connections or deep supervision. The interpolation between identity and transform governed by learned gates is an original contribution to deep feedforward architecture design in 2015.
- The analysis of data-dependent routing and per-layer lesioning in very deep feedforward nets is also novel and valuable.

Significance:
- The contribution is important: it presents a practical architectural mechanism that enables training of very deep networks with standard SGD, potentially unlocking depth as a resource without complicated optimization tricks or multi-stage procedures. The method is general (applicable to fully connected, convolutional, possibly recurrent transforms) and likely to be adopted and extended.
- Even though absolute CIFAR accuracy is not pushed to the limit, the ability to train deep thin networks directly is a substantial advance. The diagnostic tools (gate activity, lesioning) can inform future architecture design.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled architecture with simple training recipe (negative gate bias).
- Strong optimization evidence across depths; faster convergence than plain nets.
- Single-stage training of deep, thin conv nets competitive with FitNets without teacher hints.
- Insightful analyses demonstrating data-dependent routing and depth utilization.
- Public code and hyperparameter search results aid reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- No comparison to ungated identity skip connections to isolate the value of gating.
- Limited large-scale experiments (no ImageNet), and CIFAR results not SOTA under strong augmentation.
- Computational overhead and parameter/multiplication breakdowns are not reported.
- Constraint C=1−T may limit expressiveness; ablations (independent C, different nonlinearities, batch normalization) are missing.
- Claims of training >1000 layers are anecdotal; more systematic evidence would help.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a controlled comparison to fixed shortcut/identity-mapping architectures with similar parameter/FLOP budgets to quantify the incremental benefit of learned gates.
- Report FLOPs, parameter counts, and training time for all CIFAR models; clarify how gating affects efficiency.
- Include ablations: C free vs. 1−T, effect of T-bias, with/without batch normalization, and gradient norm profiling across depth.
- If feasible, include at least a preliminary ImageNet experiment to validate scalability.

Pro/con arguments for acceptance:
Pros:
- Conceptually elegant and practically impactful mechanism to train very deep nets.
- Solid empirical evidence on optimization and generalization for moderate-scale vision tasks.
- Broad applicability and good clarity; analysis provides interpretability benefits.
- Reproducible with released code.

Cons:
- Missing direct baselines to simple skip connections; limited large-scale benchmarks.
- Incomplete reporting of computational costs and ablations.
- Some design choices (C=1−T) not justified by experiments.

Overall recommendation:
I lean toward acceptance. The paper presents a novel and influential architectural idea, convincingly demonstrates its optimization advantages, and provides useful analyses. While broader comparisons and larger-scale experiments would strengthen the case, the contribution meets NIPS criteria on quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
