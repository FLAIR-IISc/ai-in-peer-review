Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that incorporates LSTM-inspired gating to facilitate training of very deep networks. Each highway layer computes y = H(x, WH)·T(x, WT) + x·(1 − T(x, WT)), where T is a sigmoid “transform gate” and the residual path x·(1 − T) acts as a “carry gate.” With a negative bias initialization for T, layers are initially biased to pass information forward, allowing gradients and activations to propagate across many layers. This aims to mitigate optimization difficulties as depth increases.

The work is grounded in literature on the benefits of depth and the difficulties of training deep nets (e.g., activation/initialization choices [Glorot & Bengio], rectifier nets, and optimizers), skip/shortcut connections, and teacher–student distillation (FitNets). The gating concept is explicitly inspired by LSTM’s gating for long-range temporal credit assignment. Related ideas include skip connections and deep supervision, but highway networks differ by using learned, input-dependent gates rather than fixed shortcuts. The paper acknowledges contemporaneous LSTM variants across spatial dimensions (e.g., Grid LSTM).

Strengths:
- Quality: The formulation is simple, clearly specified, and differentiable. The initialization strategy for transform gates is justified by analogy to LSTMs and supported empirically. The optimization study on MNIST demonstrates depth-robust training: highway networks of 50–100 layers train effectively and converge faster than capacity-matched plain networks with normalized initialization. The CIFAR-10/100 experiments show that thin, deep highway networks can be trained in a single stage, matching or exceeding FitNets trained with hints. The analytical section (gate biases/activities, class-conditional gate patterns, lesion studies) provides insight into learned routing and effective depth utilization.
- Clarity: The core idea is clearly explained, with straightforward equations, intuitive interpretation (interpolating between H(x) and identity), and concrete guidance on gate bias initialization. The paper reports multiple datasets, parameter budgets, and includes public code and scripts, strengthening reproducibility.
- Originality: Applying LSTM-like gating to the depth dimension of feedforward networks and coupling it with a simple initialization is novel relative to standard skip connections and prior deep training aids (distillation, layerwise pretraining). The elementwise, input-dependent gating sets highway networks apart from hard-wired shortcuts.
- Significance: The method directly addresses the long-standing optimization barrier in very deep feedforward nets, enabling hundreds of layers with vanilla SGD. The ability to train thin, deep models within limited parameter/compute budgets without multi-stage procedures is practically valuable. The gating analysis and lesion experiments provide a useful framework to assess how depth is utilized.

Weaknesses:
- Quality: While the Jacobian intuition is clear, there is no formal theoretical analysis quantifying gradient preservation (e.g., bounds on gradient norms through depth as a function of gate statistics). Claims of successful training beyond 1000 layers are not supported with quantitative experiments beyond pilot notes; the main empirical demonstrations top out at 100 layers (fully connected) and ~30–32 layers (convolutional). Comparisons to plain networks could be augmented with alternative modern aids (e.g., batch normalization, alternate initializations), to ensure the improvement is attributable to gating rather than baseline choices. Computational overhead (extra parameters and multiplications from gates) and wall-clock training time are not reported.
- Clarity: Some implementation details are deferred or brief: precise convolutional architectures, exact parameter counts per layer, and how dimensionality changes are handled in deep conv stacks are only sketched. Minor typos and repetitions occur. A concise algorithmic summary or pseudocode for a highway layer would help.
- Originality: The paper notes a similar LSTM-inspired model (Grid LSTM), but does not delineate in detail how highway networks differ functionally and empirically from such gated depth connections. A direct comparison to ungated skip/shortcut connections under matched budgets would clarify the unique contribution of learned gating.
- Significance: Results on CIFAR are competitive but not state-of-the-art under strong augmentation; the paper’s focus is on trainability rather than pushing top accuracy. Demonstrations on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) are absent.

Detailed evaluation:
- Quality: Empirical evidence for improved optimization with depth is convincing within the presented scope. The MNIST optimization study uses careful capacity matching (~5k parameters/layer) and hyperparameter random search for both baselines. The CIFAR experiments reuse hyperparameters chosen on CIFAR-10 for CIFAR-100, which favors parsimony but may understate achievable accuracy. The analysis of gate behaviors and layer lesioning is a strong addition, indicating data-dependent routing and selective depth usage. Missing pieces include runtime/memory profiling and ablations: (i) fixing T to a constant vs. learning; (ii) C ≠ 1 − T; (iii) sensitivity to gate bias initialization; (iv) comparisons with batch normalization or other skip strategies.
- Clarity: The presentation is generally clear, with intuitive motivation and figures (training curves; gate activities; lesion study). Suggestions: fix minor typos; provide detailed architecture tables in an appendix; report exact hyperparameters/schedules; include gradient norm statistics through depth to directly support “unimpeded flow.”
- Originality: The gating formulation in depth is a novel and clean generalization of LSTM ideas to feedforward nets, distinct from prior skip/destination layers and from distillation-based training. The paper fairly cites relevant contemporaneous works.
- Significance: The method lowers the barrier to training deep networks under standard SGD without auxiliary supervision, which is impactful. The explicit, learnable routing has interpretability advantages and practical implications for building thin, deep models under parameter budgets.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of gating: (a) remove gates (T fixed), (b) hardwired skip connections vs. learned gates, (c) C learned independently vs. 1 − T.
- Include comparisons with batch normalization and other strong baselines.
- Report wall-clock times, memory, and multiply-add counts attributable to gates.
- Extend empirical validation to deeper conv stacks and a larger dataset.
- Add gradient norm/conditioning analyses across depth.
- Clarify architectural specifics and initialization ranges per depth more systematically.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, simple, and novel architectural idea enabling very deep training.
- Strong empirical evidence that optimization difficulty with depth is alleviated.
- Competitive results under constrained budgets without multi-stage training.
- Insightful analyses (gating activity, class-dependent routing, lesion studies).
- Reproducibility supported by public code and multiple runs.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical backing beyond intuition; no gradient preservation guarantees.
- Comparisons omit some contemporary strong baselines (e.g., batch normalization, alternative skip connections).
- No large-scale dataset demonstration; depth beyond 100 layers not extensively validated.
- Computational overhead and runtime impact not quantified.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and original contribution with meaningful empirical validation and analysis, though it would benefit from stronger baselines, ablations, and broader-scale experiments.
