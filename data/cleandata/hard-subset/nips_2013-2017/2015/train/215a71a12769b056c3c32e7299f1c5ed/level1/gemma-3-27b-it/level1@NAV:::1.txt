## Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces Highway Networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea, inspired by LSTM recurrent networks, is to incorporate “information highways” – adaptive gating mechanisms – that allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers, mitigating the vanishing/exploding gradient problems typically encountered in deep networks. The authors demonstrate through experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD to significantly greater depths than traditional networks, achieving competitive or superior performance.

This work builds upon a rich history of techniques addressing the challenges of deep network training. Previous approaches, such as better optimizers (e.g., [13, 14, 15]), careful initialization strategies ([16, 17]), and skip connections ([22, 23, 24]), are effectively addressed by the proposed highway architecture, which provides a more general and adaptive mechanism for gradient flow. The paper also acknowledges related work on “teacher-student” training [25, 26] and highlights the advantage of direct, single-stage training afforded by highway networks. 

**Strengths:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its elegant and effective solution to a significant problem in deep learning – training very deep networks. The highway network architecture is conceptually simple yet powerful, enabling the successful training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard SGD.  The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of this approach, particularly on more complex datasets like CIFAR-10/100. The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figure 2 & 4) provides valuable insights into the network's inner workings and confirms the utilization of multiple layers. The provided code and hyperparameter search details are a commendable practice.

**Weaknesses:** While the paper demonstrates success, a more thorough ablation study would strengthen the findings. Specifically, analyzing the impact of different gating functions or the precise initialization scheme of the transform gates would be valuable. The comparison to state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10/100, while positive, doesn’t explore more recent and advanced architectures.  The explanation around the gradient flow is a bit hand-wavy and could benefit from more theoretical justification. Furthermore, the analysis of layer importance (Figure 4) relies on lesioning which can be a less reliable indicator of true importance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective architecture for training deep networks.
*   Strong experimental results on multiple datasets.
*   Clear explanation and insightful analysis of the gating mechanism.
*   Publicly available code.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited ablation study.
*   Comparison to state-of-the-art could be more comprehensive.
*   Theoretical justification for improved gradient flow could be enhanced.




**Overall:** I believe this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The highway network architecture is a clever and effective solution to the problem of training very deep networks, and the results are compelling. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of this work outweigh its limitations, and I recommend **acceptance**.
