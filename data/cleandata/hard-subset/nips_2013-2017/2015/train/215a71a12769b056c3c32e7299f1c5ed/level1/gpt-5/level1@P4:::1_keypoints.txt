- Introduces highway networks: feedforward layers with LSTM-like transform gate T and carry 1−T enabling y = H(x)·T + x·(1−T); negative transform-bias init biases layers to pass information forward.  
- Grounded in prior work on depth, initialization/activation choices, skip connections, and LSTM gating; related to Grid LSTM and distillation/FitNets.

Strengths:
- Simple, differentiable formulation with justified gate-bias initialization and public code for reproducibility.  
- Empirically trains very deep fully connected networks (50–100 layers) and converges faster than capacity-matched plain nets.  
- CIFAR-10/100 results show thin, deep highway nets can match/exceed FitNets without multi-stage training.  
- Analytical studies (gate biases/activities, class-conditional patterns, lesioning) provide insight into learned routing and effective depth.  
- Clear exposition, intuitive interpretation, and practical guidance on gate initialization.  
- Novelty in applying LSTM-like, input-dependent elementwise gating across depth, distinct from fixed shortcuts.  
- Significant practical impact: enables training of deep networks with vanilla SGD and building thin deep models under parameter/compute constraints.

Weaknesses:
- No formal theoretical analysis or quantitative bounds on gradient preservation through depth.  
- Claims about training beyond 1000 layers lack quantitative experiments; main demos stop at ~100 (FC) and ~30 conv layers.  
- Missing comparisons to modern training aids (e.g., batch normalization), alternative initializations, and matched ungated skip connections.  
- Computational overhead (extra parameters, multiplies) and wall-clock training time/memory not reported.  
- Some implementation details (precise conv architectures, per-layer parameter counts, dimensionality changes) are only sketched; minor typos.  
- CIFAR results are competitive but not state-of-the-art; no large-scale dataset (ImageNet) validation.

Detailed evaluation:
- MNIST optimization study is well controlled with capacity matching and hyperparameter search, supporting improved optimization with depth.  
- CIFAR experiments reuse hyperparameters across tasks, trading possible peak accuracy for parsimony.  
- Gate behavior and lesion analyses convincingly show data-dependent routing and selective use of depth.  
- Missing ablations: fixed vs learned T, independent carry gate C, sensitivity to gate bias; missing gradient-norm statistics and runtime profiling.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablations isolating gating effects (fixed T, hardwired skips, independent C) and sensitivity to bias init.  
- Compare directly with batch normalization and other strong baselines; report wall-clock times, memory, and FLOPs.  
- Extend empirical validation to deeper convolutional stacks and larger datasets.  
- Provide gradient norm/conditioning analyses, detailed architecture tables, and pseudocode for highway layers.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, simple, and novel architectural idea that alleviates depth-related optimization issues, backed by strong empirical evidence and insightful analyses.  
- Reproducible experiments and practical utility for training thin, deep models without multi-stage procedures.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical justification, incomplete comparisons to contemporary strong baselines, lack of large-scale demonstrations, and unquantified computational cost.

Overall:
- Technically sound and original contribution with meaningful empirical validation and analysis, but would benefit from stronger baselines, ablations, and broader-scale experiments.
