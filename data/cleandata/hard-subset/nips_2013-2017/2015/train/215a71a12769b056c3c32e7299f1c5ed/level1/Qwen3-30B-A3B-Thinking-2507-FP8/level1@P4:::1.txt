

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep neural networks through an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism. The authors argue that conventional "plain" networks suffer from increasing training difficulties as depth increases due to poor propagation of activations and gradients. Highway networks address this by allowing information to flow across layers via "information highways" through transform and carry gates. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD for hundreds of layers, outperforming plain networks on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets. The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on deep learning (e.g., Krizhevsky et al. 2012, Szegedy et al. 2014), skip connections (Srivastava et al. 2013), and LSTM networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997).

## Quality
The paper presents strong empirical evidence supporting the main claim that highway networks can be trained directly even at extreme depths. The experimental design is rigorous, with careful comparison to plain networks using normalized initialization and thorough hyperparameter search. The authors provide detailed results across multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) and architectures, with statistical significance assessed through multiple runs. The lesioning experiments (Figure 4) effectively demonstrate that highway networks can utilize all layers for complex tasks, unlike plain networks. However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis explaining *why* highway networks are easier to train than plain networks. The empirical evidence is strong, but the absence of theoretical justification for the observed phenomena is a notable gap.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent and clearly defined. The mathematical formulation of highway networks (Equations 2-5) is precise and accessible. Figures 1-4 effectively illustrate key findings: training curves (Figure 1), generalization capability (Table 1), comparison to state-of-the-art (Table 2), and analysis of information flow (Figures 2-4). The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics (e.g., negative bias initialization for transform gates, use of ReLU activation). The writing is clear and concise, making complex concepts accessible to the NIPS audience.

## Originality
The highway network architecture represents a significant novel contribution to the field. While the paper acknowledges related work (skip connections, distillation, LSTM), it clearly differentiates highway networks as a distinct approach that enables direct training of very deep networks through adaptive gating. The authors explicitly state that their work is inspired by LSTMs but adapted for feedforward networks, creating a new architecture rather than a simple combination of existing techniques. The concept of information highways and dynamic routing of information (Figure 3) provides a fresh perspective on deep network architecture. The paper's novelty is well-established through comparison to recent NIPS work (e.g., FitNets [25], Maxout networks [20]).

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. The ability to train extremely deep networks directly with SGD (rather than requiring two-stage training or specialized initialization) opens new possibilities for exploring deep architectures. The paper demonstrates that highway networks achieve competitive results with fewer parameters than state-of-the-art methods (Table 2), and provides insights into layer utilization (Figure 4) that were previously difficult to obtain with plain networks. The practical impact is evident in the authors' demonstration that highway networks can match or exceed the performance of wider, shallower networks while being deeper. This work directly addresses a fundamental limitation in deep learning and enables new research directions.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear, compelling problem statement addressing a fundamental limitation in deep learning
- Rigorous experimental validation across multiple datasets and architectures
- Insightful analysis of information routing and layer importance
- Practical contribution enabling direct training of extremely deep networks
- Well-organized, clearly written with excellent figures and supplementary materials

**Weaknesses:**
- Lack of theoretical justification for the observed training advantages
- Limited demonstration of extremely deep networks (max 100 layers shown, though claimed to work for hundreds)
- Does not address computational overhead of the gating mechanism compared to plain networks
- The paper's claim that highway networks "allow the study of extremely deep and efficient architectures" could be better substantiated with additional examples

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of deep learning by introducing highway networks, a novel architecture that enables direct training of extremely deep networks. The work is technically sound, well-validated, and addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning. While the lack of theoretical analysis is a minor limitation, the empirical evidence is compelling and the practical significance is clear. The paper is clearly written and makes a significant contribution to the literature on deep network architectures. I recommend acceptance.
