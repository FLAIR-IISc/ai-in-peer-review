Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture with per-unit gating that enables information to flow across many layers via “information highways.” Inspired by LSTM cells, each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine + nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate” with negative bias initialization to encourage carry behavior early in training. The authors show that (i) very deep fully connected networks (50–100 layers) train reliably on MNIST using SGD, whereas equally parameterized “plain” networks degrade with depth; (ii) convolutional highway networks train in one stage on CIFAR-10/100 with budgets comparable to FitNets, obviating the need for teacher hints; and (iii) the gating learns meaningful data-dependent routing, supported by gate activity visualizations and lesion studies that quantify layer importance. This work builds on and contrasts with: LSTM gating for recurrent nets [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997; Gers et al.], normalized initializations and rectifiers [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision [Raiko et al.; Graves; Lee et al.], and hint-based distillation for thin deep nets [Romero et al.]. The novelty here is bringing LSTM-style gates to deep feedforward networks to create trainable identity paths.

Quality
Technical soundness: The layer formulation is simple and well-motivated. The identity-carry path provides a near-identity Jacobian when T≈0, addressing gradient attenuation with depth. The training protocol (SGD with momentum, ReLU for H, biasing T negative) is reasonable. The optimization experiments on MNIST are carefully controlled in depth/parameters and use random hyperparameter search. The CIFAR-10/100 experiments compare fairly to FitNets under similar parameter/compute budgets and standard augmentation.

Empirical support: The findings convincingly demonstrate that (a) increasing depth harms optimization in plain nets but not in highway nets; (b) highway nets learn meaningful, input-dependent routing; and (c) thin and deep highway nets match or exceed FitNets trained with hints, using simple one-stage training. The lesion and gate-activity analyses are insightful and go beyond accuracy numbers.

However, several experimental gaps limit the strength of the claims:
- No ablation comparing against simple skip connections without gates (e.g., additive identity shortcuts with fixed weights); thus, the unique contribution of adaptive gating vs. any shortcut remains unclear.
- No comparison to batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy 2015), a strong contemporary baseline that eases optimization of deep nets.
- The carry gate is tied as C=1−T; effects of independent C are not studied.
- The overhead in parameters/compute from gates is not quantified, nor is training/inference speed.
- Optimization results emphasize “best” runs rather than distributions across the random search; reporting medians/interquartile ranges would strengthen robustness claims.
- Claims of training >1000 layers are anecdotal (no quantitative results shown).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with succinct equations, appropriate notation, and helpful intuition via Jacobian analysis. The MNIST/CIFAR architectural choices are described sufficiently for reproduction, and the analysis figures (gate biases/activities, lesion curves) are illuminating. Minor issues: explicitly state per-layer parameter counts and FLOPs for H and T; clarify how dimensions change while preserving highways (projection vs. plain layers); and report full training details (weight decay, dropout usage, exact schedules) in an appendix. Some references are incomplete in formatting.

Originality
The core idea—LSTM-like gating in feedforward layers to create learnable identity paths—is novel in this form and distinct from prior work on fixed skip connections, local competition/maxout, deep supervision, and distillation. The initialization heuristic for gates is a direct and sensible transfer from LSTM practice. The analysis of routing and layer importance is also original and valuable.

Significance
Enabling reliable training of very deep (50–100+) networks with simple SGD is important, and the method is general (applies to FC and convolutional layers). The paper will likely stimulate follow-up on shortcut architectures and gating in feedforward nets, and the empirical results suggest practical utility on standard benchmarks under modest budgets. The broader impact is high given the difficulties of optimizing very deep models.

Strengths
- Simple, well-motivated architecture that provides identity paths and mitigates vanishing gradients.
- Strong optimization evidence across depths with controlled parameter budgets.
- Competitive CIFAR-10/100 results vs. FitNets without hints; thin-and-deep models train in one stage.
- Insightful analyses (gate activity visualizations, class-conditional routing, lesion studies).
- Public code and hyperparameter search details.

Weaknesses
- Missing ablation vs. fixed skip connections; unclear how much adaptivity is needed.
- No comparison to batch normalization or other strong contemporary stabilization techniques.
- Limited exploration of design variants (independent carry gate; different nonlinearities for T; scalar vs. vector gates; per-layer vs. per-unit gating).
- No quantification of parameter/compute overhead and training/inference speed.
- Some results emphasize best-case runs; distributional reporting would increase credibility.
- Only small/medium-scale datasets; no ImageNet evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) fixed identity shortcuts without gates; (ii) scalar T per layer; (iii) independent carry gate C; (iv) highway + batch normalization.
- Report distributions (median/variance) over random search runs and gradient-norm diagnostics across depth.
- Quantify overhead in parameters/FLOPs and wall-clock times; discuss test-time gating sparsity and potential efficiency gains (e.g., L1 on T).
- Explore projection shortcuts when changing dimensionality to preserve highways.
- Provide results with gate bias bT=0 and bT>0 to demonstrate the importance of initialization.
- If possible, add a preliminary large-scale result or stronger CIFAR setting (heavier augmentation) to position against state-of-the-art.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a central challenge (training very deep nets) with a principled, general, and simple mechanism.
- Empirically demonstrates clear optimization benefits and competitive accuracy under fair budgets.
- Provides novel analyses that enhance understanding of depth utilization and routing.
- Likely high impact and broad adoption.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lacks critical ablations against non-gated skip connections and batch normalization, leaving the unique value of gating underexplored.
- Evaluation limited to MNIST/CIFAR; no large-scale confirmation.
- Incomplete reporting of computational overhead and robustness across hyperparameters.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that, with a few additional comparisons, would be a clear accept. I lean toward acceptance.
