### Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the well-known and significant challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, termed "highway networks," which facilitates optimization by allowing unimpeded information flow across many layers. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. Each layer in a highway network is augmented with a "transform gate" and a "carry gate." These gates dynamically regulate the layer's behavior, allowing it to smoothly interpolate between a standard non-linear transformation of its input and a simple identity mapping that passes the input through unchanged.

This architecture ensures that, even in a randomly initialized network, there exists a straightforward path for gradients to propagate through the entire depth of the network. The authors propose a simple and effective initialization scheme where the transform gate biases are initialized with negative values, encouraging the network to initially behave like a series of identity mappings, which makes learning tractable from the start.

The authors validate their approach with a series of compelling experiments. First, on MNIST, they demonstrate that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained effectively with SGD, while standard "plain" networks of similar depth fail to optimize. Second, on CIFAR-10, they show that their method can directly train deep, thin networks that achieve performance comparable or superior to the recent "FitNets" [25], but without requiring the complex, multi-stage, hint-based training procedure proposed there. Finally, the paper includes a detailed analysis of the learned gate activities, suggesting that the network learns a form of data-dependent information routing and automatically adjusts its effective depth based on task complexity.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper presents a simple, elegant, and powerful solution to a major problem in deep learning. While the difficulty of training deep networks is well-established (e.g., [16, 28]), and skip connections have been used before, the introduction of a learnable, dynamic gating mechanism inspired by LSTMs [29] to control information flow in a feedforward architecture is a highly novel and insightful contribution. This work effectively unlocks the ability to explore network depths that were previously intractable, which is a significant step forward for the field.

2.  **Technical Quality and Experimental Rigor:** The claims are strongly supported by well-designed experiments. The direct comparison of optimization performance between plain and highway networks at increasing depths (Sec 3.1) provides unequivocal evidence of the proposed method's effectiveness. The ablation-like analysis in Section 4, where the authors probe layer importance by lesioning transform gates, is particularly insightful. It demonstrates that the network can learn to utilize its depth according to the complexity of the task, a highly desirable property. The results on CIFAR, especially the comparison to FitNets, are very convincing and highlight the practical utility of the method.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The motivation is clearly laid out, the mathematical formulation is precise, and the connection to LSTMs is explained well. The figures, particularly the analysis of gate activities and the lesion study, are informative and provide strong support for the authors' interpretations.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Missing Baseline: Non-Gated Skip Connections:** The central mechanism is a gated skip connection: `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x))`. The paper argues this is superior to fixed shortcuts, but a crucial baseline is missing: a simple, non-gated, additive skip connection (`y = H(x) + x`). This architecture would also provide an unimpeded information "highway" for the gradient and seems like a natural, simpler alternative. Comparing highway networks to this baseline would help isolate the specific benefit of the adaptive gating mechanism. Is the added complexity and parameterization of the gates essential, or does most of the benefit come from simply having an identity path available?

2.  **Justification for `C = 1-T`:** The authors simplify the gating mechanism by setting the carry gate `C` to be `1-T`, departing from the independent gates in an LSTM. While this is pragmatic, the implications of this design choice are not discussed. Did the authors experiment with two independent gates? It seems plausible that decoupling the "transform" and "carry" decisions could offer additional flexibility. Some justification for this simplification would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Sensitivity to Gate Bias Initialization:** The paper proposes initializing the transform gate bias `b_T` to a negative value as a key enabler for training. This introduces a new, important hyperparameter. While general guidelines are provided, the paper would benefit from a more thorough sensitivity analysis. For instance, what happens if `b_T` is initialized to 0 or a positive value? Does training fail catastrophically at great depths, or does it merely slow down? Understanding the robustness to this initialization is important for practitioners.

4.  **Interpretation of Gate Dynamics:** The analysis shows that for CIFAR-100, the learned biases `b_T` often become even more negative during training, which the authors interpret as the gates becoming more "selective." An alternative view is that the network learns that the identity mapping is the optimal default behavior for most layers and only learns to apply a transformation `H(x)` in specific, necessary circumstances. This would imply that the network is learning a sparse set of active transformations. Further discussion on this interpretation would be welcome.

**Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the field. The proposed highway network architecture is novel, well-motivated, and empirically shown to be highly effective at overcoming the optimization barriers associated with training very deep networks. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and is likely to have a substantial impact on future research in deep learning architectures. Despite the minor weaknesses noted above—primarily the lack of a simple additive skip-connection baseline—the strengths of the paper are overwhelming. This work is a clear accept for NIPS and has the potential to become a foundational paper in the study of deep architectures.
