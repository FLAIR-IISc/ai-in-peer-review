

# Peer Review: Training Very Deep Networks

## Summary

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep neural networks through adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. The authors argue that conventional deep networks suffer from poor gradient propagation as depth increases, making training difficult. Highway networks address this by incorporating transform and carry gates that regulate information flow across layers, creating "information highways" that allow unimpeded information flow. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that highway networks can be trained directly with simple gradient descent even with hundreds of layers, outperforming plain networks on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets.

This work builds upon previous efforts to improve deep network training, including skip connections [22, 23, 24], knowledge distillation [25], and specialized activation functions [20, 21]. It extends the LSTM-inspired architecture proposed in [32] by providing a more general framework applicable to feedforward networks. The paper relates to NIPS work on deep learning challenges, such as the analysis of training difficulties in deep networks [17, 36], and the development of better initialization strategies [16, 17].

## Strengths

1. **Technical Quality**: The paper presents a well-motivated and carefully designed architecture with strong empirical validation. The experimental section is comprehensive, including detailed comparisons with plain networks and Fitnets [25] across multiple datasets and network depths. The results consistently demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly.

2. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The highway network architecture is clearly explained with precise mathematical notation (Equations 1-5), and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The figures (particularly Figures 1-4) effectively illustrate key findings about information flow and network behavior.

3. **Originality**: The adaptive gating mechanism represents a significant departure from previous approaches to deep network training. While skip connections and knowledge distillation have been used before [22-25], the highway network's dynamic routing capability (where gates adapt to input) is novel. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from related work, particularly noting that highway networks can be trained in a single stage without the need for teacher-student training.

4. **Significance**: The problem of training very deep networks is fundamental to deep learning. The authors demonstrate that highway networks enable training of networks with 100+ layers directly with SGD, which has practical implications for model design and performance. The analysis of information routing (Figures 2-4) provides valuable insights into how deep networks process information.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: While the experimental evidence is strong, the paper lacks a formal theoretical analysis explaining *why* highway networks train better than plain networks. The authors mention that the Jacobian properties (Equations 4-5) suggest easier gradient flow, but they don't provide rigorous analysis of how this affects optimization dynamics or convergence rates.

2. **Comparison to Residual Networks**: The paper was submitted before the introduction of residual networks (ResNets) [37], which use similar skip connections and were published in 2015. The authors acknowledge similar LSTM-inspired work [32] but don't compare their approach to ResNets, which would have strengthened the significance argument. In hindsight, this is a notable omission for a 2015 conference.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper notes that the transform gate bias initialization (e.g., -1, -2, -3) is a hyperparameter that requires tuning. While they provide guidelines, this adds complexity that wasn't present in plain networks. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive highway networks are to this hyperparameter across different datasets and architectures.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Demonstrates a practical solution to a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training very deep networks
- Provides strong experimental evidence across multiple benchmarks
- Clear, well-organized writing with thorough experimental validation
- Introduces a novel architectural element (adaptive gating) with significant potential impact
- Analysis of information flow (Figures 2-4) provides valuable theoretical insights

**Con:**
- Lacks rigorous theoretical analysis of why highway networks train better
- Does not compare with residual networks (which were being developed concurrently)
- Requires additional hyperparameter (transform gate bias) that must be tuned
- The "information highway" concept is somewhat vague without more formal analysis

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The highway network architecture addresses a fundamental problem in training very deep networks with clear empirical benefits. While the paper could have been strengthened by more theoretical analysis and comparison to residual networks, its experimental evidence is robust and its insights valuable.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in training deep networks and provides a practical solution to a major challenge. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained with hundreds of layers directly through simple gradient descent, which is a significant practical contribution.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is of high quality and significance, and its contributions are clearly differentiated from previous work. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the empirical evidence is sufficiently compelling to support the claims. The insights about information routing through highway networks provide valuable understanding of how deep networks process information, which will likely inspire further research in the field.
