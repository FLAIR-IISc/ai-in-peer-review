Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that augments each layer with adaptive, input-dependent gates that regulate the mixture between a transformed signal H(x) and a shortcut (carry) of the input x. Specifically, each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), with T(x) a sigmoid “transform gate,” and (1 − T) serving as a “carry gate.” The design is inspired by LSTM gating for recurrent networks and aims to create information “highways” that allow gradients and activations to propagate across hundreds of layers. A simple but effective initialization strategy biases the transform gates to be initially closed (negative gate biases), encouraging identity flow early in learning. The authors show that very deep highway networks (50–100 layers) train reliably with vanilla SGD and achieve competitive performance on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, including single-stage training where prior work (FitNets) required teacher hints. They also present analyses of gate activities, data-dependent routing, and lesion studies to assess layer importance.

Relative to previous work, this sits between earlier skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision (e.g., inject error at intermediate layers), normalization/initialization methods for deep nets, and the LSTM literature on gating. Unlike fixed skip connections or purely structural deepening (e.g., VGG, Inception), Highway Networks provide per-unit, input-dependent gating that interpolates between identity and transformation, reminiscent of LSTM input/forget gates but applied depth-wise in feedforward nets. The paper is roughly contemporaneous with residual learning; at the time, stronger comparisons to fixed residual connections were not yet standard, though skip ideas existed.

Strengths:
- Clear architectural idea, principled from LSTM gating, implemented simply and generally (fully connected and convolutional).
- Convincing optimization results: as depth increases, plain nets degrade while highway nets remain trainable and converge faster, even with thin layers and matched parameter budgets.
- Empirical validation on MNIST, CIFAR-10/100, with single-stage training outperforming or matching FitNets trained with hints under similar compute/parameter budgets.
- Insightful analyses: visualization of gate biases/activities, evidence for data-dependent routing, and lesion studies showing which depth is functionally used per dataset.
- Practical guidance: gate bias initialization heuristics; code and hyperparameters made available, supporting reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- Comparisons focus on relatively simple baselines; no head-to-head against equally deep networks with fixed skip connections (e.g., additive residual shortcuts) or batch normalization baselines, which were becoming common.
- Limited exploration of design choices: C = 1 − T is assumed throughout; the effect of allowing independent carry gates, or of removing gating (pure skip), is not ablated.
- Claims of training “even with hundreds of layers” are stronger than the reported experiments (up to 100 layers quantitatively; >1000 only in pilot remarks without results).
- Generalization results are competitive but not state-of-the-art under strong augmentation; compute/time overhead and memory footprint of gates are not quantified.
- Sensitivity to gate bias, width/depth trade-offs, and interaction with regularization (dropout, weight decay) and normalization (e.g., batch norm) are not systematically analyzed.

Quality:
The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The optimization study is carefully controlled with parameter-matched thin networks and random search over hyperparameters. The CIFAR experiments reuse hyperparameters from CIFAR-10 to CIFAR-100, which is conservative. The analyses (gate activity, class-conditional routing, lesioning) substantiate the intended behavior. However, broader baselines (e.g., fixed residual connections, batch norm) and ablations would strengthen causal claims about the source of the optimization gains. Reporting beyond 100 layers and wall-clock/memory metrics would further support the practical claims.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core layer equation and its implications for identity/progressive transformation and Jacobian behavior are explained succinctly. Experimental setups are generally described, though some architectural details (exact layer widths and receptive fields in all configurations) and training schedules are only summarized or relegated to supplementary code/scripts. The analysis figures are informative.

Originality:
The key novelty is the application of LSTM-like gating to depth-wise information flow in feedforward nets, with per-unit, input-dependent gates and a simple carry/transform decomposition. While related to prior skip connections and deep supervision, this specific gated formulation and initialization constitute a novel contribution. The lesion and routing analyses are also a nice addition.

Significance:
Enabling direct training of very deep networks is an important problem. The proposed mechanism demonstrably improves optimization and maintains generalization across standard benchmarks under constrained budgets. The idea is likely to influence architectures and be adopted or adapted by practitioners. Even if state-of-the-art accuracy is not the target, the paper advances the understanding and practice of training deep networks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablations: (a) independent carry gate vs. C = 1 − T, (b) remove gating but keep identity skip (residual-like), (c) vary gate bias systematically, (d) parameter-match when adding gates to confirm gains are not due to capacity alone.
- Compare to contemporary strong baselines: fixed residual shortcuts without gates; batch normalization with deep plain nets.
- Report training/inference cost and memory overhead due to gates; analyze gradient norms across depth to empirically support “unimpeded flow.”
- Explore broader domains (e.g., SVHN, sequence tasks) to highlight generality, given the LSTM inspiration.
- Clarify maximum depth with quantitative results and provide more details on the very deep (>100) pilot experiments if claimed.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, general mechanism with clear motivation and demonstrated benefits for training very deep nets.
- Solid empirical evidence and insightful analyses of how and when depth is used.
- Likely to have high impact and spur follow-on work on deep architectures and gating.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to strong, concurrent alternatives (fixed residual shortcuts, batch norm).
- Limited ablations to isolate which components are necessary.
- Modest SOTA performance; practical overheads not quantified.

Overall recommendation:
Lean accept. This is a timely, well-argued contribution that improves the trainability of very deep networks with a simple architectural change and offers valuable analysis, despite some missing comparisons and ablations.
