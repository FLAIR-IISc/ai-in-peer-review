This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces data-dependent gating to enable “information highways” across many layers. Each layer outputs y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where T is a sigmoid transform gate and the carry gate is 1−T. Inspired by LSTM gating in RNNs [29,30], the authors bias T with negative initial offsets to favor carry paths early in training. Empirically, they demonstrate that very deep fully connected and convolutional networks (50–100 layers, with pilot results beyond) can be trained by vanilla SGD without special layerwise pretraining or teacher hints. On MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, highway nets match or exceed comparably budgeted plain nets and FitNets [25], and converge faster as depth grows. Analyses of gate activity and “lesioning” layers suggest input-dependent routing and that deeper problems exploit more layers. The work situates itself among efforts on initialization [16,17], alternative optimizers [13–15], locally competitive activations [20,21], skip/deep supervision [22–24], and distillation [25], clarifying that the gates provide adaptive, multiplicative routing rather than fixed shortcuts.

Quality: The method is technically sound and clearly specified; the initialization rationale is plausible and consistent with LSTM practice. Optimization studies are careful (matched per-layer parameter counts) and the gate analyses are insightful. However, comparisons are limited to small/medium-scale datasets; there is no ImageNet result, no quantification of computational overhead from gates, and limited ablations (e.g., sensitivity to gate bias, effect of setting C independent from 1−T, role of activation choices or batch normalization). Claims of training “>1000 layers” are anecdotal.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized, with explicit equations and training details, and public code. Minor typos and duplicated words could be fixed; reporting of FLOPs and exact parameter counts per model would improve reproducibility.

Originality: Introducing learnable gates in feedforward layers is a novel and natural extension of LSTM ideas to depth. The adaptive routing distinguishes this from fixed skip connections and deep supervision.

Significance: If robust, the approach could materially ease training of very deep nets and stimulate further architectural work. The absence of large-scale benchmarks tempers immediate impact but the idea is promising.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general architectural idea with strong motivation from LSTMs
- Clear optimization wins as depth increases; informative analyses
- Competitive accuracy under comparable budgets; open-source release

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited benchmarks; no large-scale results
- Sparse ablations and overhead analysis
- Some design choices (C=1−T, bias schedules) not thoroughly justified experimentally
