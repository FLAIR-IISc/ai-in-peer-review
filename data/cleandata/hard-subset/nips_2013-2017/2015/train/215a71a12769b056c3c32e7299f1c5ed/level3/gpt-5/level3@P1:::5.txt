This paper proposes highway networks, an LSTM-inspired gated architecture for very deep feedforward networks that enables training with plain SGD. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where T is a data-dependent transform gate. With a negative bias initialization, T starts near zero so information and gradients can propagate along “information highways,” while the network learns when and where to transform. The authors provide optimization studies showing that highway networks avoid the degradation plain nets suffer as depth grows, pilot classification results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with competitive accuracy at modest parameter budgets, and insightful analyses of gate activity, data-dependent routing, and layer lesioning.

Relation to prior work: The work is positioned against difficulties in training deep nets and the role of depth in expressivity. It connects to LSTM gating [29,30], skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision [22,23,24], teacher-student hint-based training [25], and initialization strategies that stabilize signal propagation [16,17]. In contrast to layerwise pretraining or staged distillation, highway networks train end-to-end in one stage. Compared to fixed skip connections, the gates provide adaptive, input-dependent routing. The empirical studies align with concurrent directions that use narrow receptive fields (VGG [3]) or multi-branch modules (Inception [2]) to build depth while preserving trainability, but here the mechanism is multiplicative gating at each layer.

Quality: The core claim—that gating enables optimization of very deep feedforward networks—is supported by clear experiments. On MNIST, highway networks with 50–100 layers train faster and to significantly lower loss than matched plain networks; on CIFAR, highway models reach or exceed fitnets without multi-stage distillation. Analyses of gate biases, per-sample activity, class-conditional routing, and lesioning provide convincing evidence that (1) gradients and activations propagate effectively, (2) routing is input-dependent, and (3) depth is utilized when the task demands it (CIFAR-100) and gracefully bypassed when it does not (MNIST). The methodology is careful (random search, matched parameter counts per layer, reporting mean ± std across runs). Some ablations could be expanded (see suggestions), but overall the paper is technically sound and complete.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. The formulation of the highway layer is simple and easy to implement. The training recipe (negative transform-gate bias) is clearly described, with practical heuristics by depth. Figures analyzing gate activity and lesioning are informative. A few clarifications could help practitioners: explicit accounting of parameter/compute overhead introduced by gates, and a consolidated architectural diagram for convolutional highway blocks.

Originality: Introducing LSTM-like gating into feedforward depth is a simple, elegant idea that—despite the long history of skip connections—appears novel in this specific form for deep feedforward networks at the time of submission. The “carry vs. transform” view and the biasing-to-carry initialization are clean conceptual contributions. The analyses of data-dependent routing and layer importance in very deep feedforward nets are also novel and insightful.

Significance: Enabling straightforward training of hundreds of layers is impactful and likely to influence subsequent architectures. The approach is generic (applicable to fully connected and convolutional layers), introduces a reusable building block, and offers diagnostic tools (lesioning, gate analysis) to reason about effective depth. Results on CIFAR are solid; demonstrating scalability to larger datasets would further increase significance.

Alternative perspective and interpretation: The paper makes a compelling case that very deep models can be trained, but the gating shows many layers acting as near-identity in parts of the depth/space. This invites the interpretation that highway networks are “deep compositions of richer sub-layers,” akin to Inception’s multi-branch modules or VGG’s stacked 3×3 blocks, where the effective transformation is sparse and distributed. From this angle, highway layers can be viewed as composite modules that select, per input, whether to apply H or pass through, yielding an adaptive effective depth.

Suggestions and questions:
- Larger-scale validation: Include ImageNet experiments (even with modest data augmentation) to assess scalability and training dynamics at scale.
- Stronger baselines: Compare to plain deep nets using contemporary best practices (e.g., He initialization, batch normalization if compatible), and to fixed skip-connection variants to isolate the benefit of adaptive gates versus identity shortcuts.
- Ablations:
  - Independent carry gate vs. C = 1−T: Does decoupling improve accuracy or stability?
  - Gate-bias sensitivity: Provide curves for different initial biases and depths; report gradient norms across depth early in training.
  - Parameter/compute overhead: Quantify extra parameters, FLOPs, and wall-clock time due to gates; report accuracy-per-compute trade-offs.
- Effective depth: Report distributions of T across layers (e.g., entropy, fraction of “open” gates) and their correlation with lesion sensitivity, to better quantify utilization.
- Multi-task sharing experiment (proposed): Train a single thin-and-tall trunk jointly on MNIST and CIFAR, branching only at the top. Compare gate activity maps, lesion profiles, and parameter importance between the jointly trained model and separately trained models. Does the network repurpose layers underutilized by MNIST for CIFAR? How efficiently can deep hierarchies share parameters across tasks?
- Architectural clarity: Provide a schematic of a convolutional highway block, including where padding/stride are applied for H and T.

Pros:
- Simple, principled architectural change with strong intuition (LSTM gating) and clear practical recipe.
- Compelling optimization evidence that depth ceases to hurt; faster convergence than plain nets.
- Solid results on CIFAR without distillation; competitive with fitnets at similar budgets.
- Insightful analyses revealing data-dependent routing and differential layer importance.
- Broad applicability (FC and conv) and ease of implementation.

Cons:
- Limited large-scale validation; no ImageNet results.
- Incomplete comparison to the strongest contemporary training tricks (e.g., batch norm) and fixed skip connections.
- Some ablations missing (independent carry gate, compute overhead, bias sensitivity).

Overall recommendation: Strong accept. The paper presents an elegant, practically useful mechanism that makes very deep networks trainable, with convincing evidence and thoughtful analysis. Adding a larger-scale experiment and a few ablations would further strengthen the camera-ready version.
