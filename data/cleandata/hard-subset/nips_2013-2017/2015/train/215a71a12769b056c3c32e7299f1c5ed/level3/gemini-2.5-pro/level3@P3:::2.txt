This paper introduces highway networks, a novel neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep feed-forward models. The core innovation is a gating mechanism, inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, that adaptively controls the flow of information through layers. Each layer can dynamically choose between transforming its input and carrying it through unchanged via "information highways." This mechanism allows for unimpeded gradient flow, overcoming the optimization difficulties that typically plague very deep "plain" networks. The authors demonstrate that this architecture allows for the direct training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent.

The work is well-situated within the context of existing research on training deep networks, acknowledging prior work on improved initialization strategies, advanced optimizers, and architectural modifications like skip connections. The authors explicitly draw a parallel to LSTMs, effectively translating their temporal gating mechanism to the spatial dimension of network depth. They also provide a direct and favorable comparison to the concurrent work on FitNets, showing that highway networks can train deep, thin models in a single stage where FitNets required a more complex teacher-student approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of using input-dependent gates to control information flow across layers is both novel and highly significant. It provides a principled and elegant solution to the well-known problem of training very deep networks. By enabling the exploration of architectures an order of magnitude deeper than was previously practical with standard feed-forward nets, this work opens up new avenues for research and has the potential for major impact on the field.
*   **Technical Quality and Empirical Results:** The paper's claims are substantiated by a comprehensive set of experiments. The optimization experiments on MNIST (Figure 1) provide a clear and compelling demonstration that highway networks do not suffer performance degradation with increasing depth, in stark contrast to plain networks. The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are very promising, showing that highway networks can achieve competitive performance, even outperforming the more complex, two-stage training procedure of FitNets on comparable architectures.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, the highway network architecture is explained precisely, and the experimental setup and results are presented with clarity. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing literature, explaining both similarities and key differences from related models.
*   **In-Depth Analysis:** A standout feature of this paper is the thorough analysis of the trained networks' internal dynamics in Section 4. The visualizations of the gate activities (Figure 2) and the investigation into data-dependent information routing (Figure 3) provide fascinating insights into how these networks operate. The layer lesioning study (Figure 4) is particularly insightful, demonstrating that the network learns to utilize only the necessary depth for a given problem's complexity (using fewer layers for MNIST than for CIFAR-100). This level of analysis goes far beyond typical results-focused papers and adds significant value.

**Weaknesses:**

I could not identify any significant weaknesses in this work. The methodology is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the conclusions are well-supported.

**Minor Comments:**

A minor typo was noticed on page 5, which refers to "Tables 3 and 3."

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a very solid and high-quality paper. It introduces a novel, well-motivated, and effective technique for a fundamental problem in deep learning. The combination of a simple yet powerful idea, strong empirical validation, and insightful analysis makes this a standout contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
