Summary and relation to prior work: This paper proposes Highway Networks, deep feedforward architectures with transform and carry gates (T and C=1−T) that enable input-dependent routing and near-identity paths across many layers, inspired by LSTM gating. The authors argue this alleviates optimization pathologies that impede very deep plain nets. Empirically, they show stable training with tens to hundreds of layers on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, competitive accuracy under modest data augmentation, and insightful analyses of gate activity, routing, and lesioning. The approach relates to LSTM gating for long-term credit assignment, skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, normalized initialization, maxout, and FitNet teacher-student training, but differs by learning multiplicative gates that can implement conditional identities in very deep feedforward nets.

Strengths (quality, clarity, originality, significance):
- The idea is clearly described, simple to implement, and well-motivated by LSTM principles.
- Experiments are fairly convincing that optimization remains stable with depth; analyses of gate outputs and layer lesioning are particularly strong and insightful.
- The work is original in bringing adaptive gating to deep feedforward stacks and shows potential impact for training extremely deep models without multi-stage distillation.
- The paper is generally well organized and readable.

Weaknesses and requested revisions:
- Technical correctness: Because T uses a sigmoid, outputs are never exactly 0/1; Eq. 5 as stated is misleading. Please provide the exact Jacobian used in backprop, including the T′(x) terms, and clarify how gradients flow through the multiplicative gates in practice (per-dimension vs. shared gates).
- Initialization: Constant negative transform-gate biases “by depth” act like a regularizer favoring longer highways. Provide a plot of performance vs. initial bias (and depth), and examine whether coupling C=1−T is necessary vs. learning an independent carry gate.
- Comparative claims: Clearly state whether recent baselines (e.g., FitNets, maxout) are trained under identical data augmentation, parameter/compute budgets, and optimization settings; otherwise detail the differences and avoid direct numerical comparisons.
- Reporting practice: Do not report only best accuracies. For every method and dataset you ran, report mean ± std over multiple seeds and the number of runs; for numbers taken from prior work, indicate source and setting.
- Broader analysis: Consider adding a learning-theory perspective on gates as conditional identities enabling selective deep paths (e.g., path-wise capacity/regularization or sample-dependent routing).

Arguments:
Pros: clear, simple, effective; strong analyses; likely to influence deep architecture design.
Cons: gradient derivation issue; incomplete ablations on initialization; comparability and reporting need tightening.

Overall: I lean toward acceptance after addressing the technical and reporting clarifications above.
