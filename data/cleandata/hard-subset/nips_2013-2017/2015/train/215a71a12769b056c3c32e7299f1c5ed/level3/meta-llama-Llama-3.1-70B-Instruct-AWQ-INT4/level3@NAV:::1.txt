This paper introduces a novel architecture for training very deep neural networks, called highway networks, which allows for unimpeded information flow across many layers. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and use adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow. The proposed architecture is shown to be effective in training extremely deep networks directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), without the need for complex initialization schemes or layer-wise training.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and its relation to previous work. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks in training deep networks on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The analysis of the learned networks' gate-output patterns is also insightful, providing a better understanding of how the highway networks work.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address the problem of training very deep networks, which has been a long-standing challenge in the field. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed architecture and its advantages over previous approaches. The experimental results are also comprehensive, covering various aspects of the proposed architecture, including its ability to train deep networks, its generalization performance, and its robustness to different hyperparameters.

However, there are a few concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the authors use a sigmoid function for the gate unit, which means that the outputs are never exactly 0 or 1. This raises questions about the correctness of the gradients in Equation 5, and the authors need to provide a clear explanation of how backpropagation is implemented in this case.

Secondly, the initialization scheme used in the paper is not entirely satisfying. The authors use a constant negative bias for the transform gates, which is not a principled way of initializing the network. A more thorough analysis of the initialization scheme and its effect on the network's performance is needed.

Thirdly, the authors make comparative claims about the performance of highway networks versus other approaches, but these claims are not entirely clear. The authors need to provide a more detailed comparison of the experimental settings used in different studies and clarify the differences between them.

Finally, the authors only report the maximum accuracy achieved by each method, but it would be more informative to provide the mean and standard deviation of the accuracy across multiple runs.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. With some minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, this paper has the potential to make a major impact in the field.
