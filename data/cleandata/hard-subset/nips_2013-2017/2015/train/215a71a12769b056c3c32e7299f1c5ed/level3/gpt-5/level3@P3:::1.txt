Summary
This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that augments each layer with LSTM-inspired gates to regulate information flow: y = H(x) ⊙ T(x) + x ⊙ (1 − T(x)). With negative bias initialization for T, the network is initially biased toward carry behavior, creating “information highways” that facilitate gradient flow and enable training of very deep networks with SGD. The authors present optimization studies showing stable training up to 100 layers (and pilot >1000), competitive results on MNIST/CIFAR-10/100 with relatively small models, and insightful analyses of gate activity, routing, and layer importance. The approach is related to prior work on skip connections and deep supervision but is distinguished by adaptive, input-dependent gating, drawing explicitly on LSTM ideas.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear formulation of highway layers and a simple recipe (negative gate bias) that reliably enables training very deep nets.
- Experiments convincingly show optimization advantages over plain networks and competitive accuracy under modest data augmentation.
- Particularly strong analysis section: gate bias dynamics, sample-conditional routing, and lesion studies provide rare visibility into the computation of deep nets.

Weaknesses and required clarifications:
- Gradients through gates. Equation (5) is only valid in the limiting/extreme cases T∈{0,1}, but with a sigmoid gate T(x)∈(0,1) the Jacobian is not as stated. Please provide the exact forward- and backpropagation equations you implemented. For y = H(x) ⊙ T(x) + x ⊙ (1 − T(x)), the general Jacobian is:
  dy/dx = J_H(x) diag(T) + diag(1 − T) + diag(H − x) J_T(x)
  which implies the backpropagation form g_x = diag(1 − T) g_y + J_H(x)^T [diag(T) g_y] + J_T(x)^T [diag(H − x) g_y].
  If any approximation is used (e.g., stopping gradients through T or H in some branches), please state it explicitly.
- Initialization as a hyperparameterized regularizer. The constant negative bias schedule by depth is ad hoc. Since b_T controls the expected effective depth, it plays the role of a structural regularizer favoring “longer highways.” Please add a plot of performance vs. initial b_T (per depth and dataset), and report how sensitive optimization and generalization are to b_T. Also clarify whether b_T was tuned on validation only and whether it differs across runs.
- Comparative claims. Much of the positioning (e.g., vs. FitNets/hint-based training) hinges on whether the experimental setting matches prior work. Please state precisely what differs for each comparison: architecture width/depth, parameter count, FLOPs, data augmentation, learning-rate schedules, and model selection protocol. If settings differ, do not claim direct superiority; instead present them as complementary results.
- Reporting practice. Avoid reporting only best runs. For every configuration and dataset, report mean ± standard deviation over multiple seeds and the number of trials. For the random search experiments, provide distributions (or at least mean/median/best) for both plains and highways to avoid cherry-picking “best curves.”
- Ablations that would strengthen the paper:
  - Independent carry gate C(x) vs. C = 1 − T: effect on optimization, accuracy, and gate interpretability.
  - Gate nonlinearity (sigmoid vs. hard-sigmoid) and saturation statistics over depth/epochs.
  - Cost analysis: parameter overhead, training time per epoch, and memory vs. matched plain nets.
  - Where dimensionality changes are needed, compare “plain resize layers” vs. gated resizing blocks.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. To improve reproducibility, please:
- Include explicit forward/backward equations for a highway block (as above), and summarize tensor shapes and parameter counts per block.
- Add details of the learning-rate schedules used per dataset, and the precise selection criterion for “best” models.
- Ensure all reported numbers have accompanying standard deviations, and that plots include confidence intervals.

Originality
Applying LSTM-style adaptive gating to feedforward depth is a novel and well-motivated idea distinct from fixed skip connections. The analysis of gate behavior (conditional routing and lesion studies) is original and valuable. Related work is largely well covered.

Significance
If robust, the method lowers the optimization barrier for very deep networks and suggests a principled path-composition view of depth. While the datasets are modest-scale, the demonstrated stability and the interpretability of gating are likely to influence future architectures.

Suggestions for additional theory
A light learning-theory treatment would add depth: e.g., analyze how gates implement conditional identities and reduce effective depth; path-based complexity measures where T gates select active computation; generalization bounds that depend on the expected fraction of open gates; and conditions under which carry paths ensure non-vanishing gradients regardless of H.

Pros
- Clear, compelling idea with simple implementation.
- Strong evidence that very deep nets can be trained with vanilla SGD.
- Insightful empirical analysis of gate-output patterns, routing, and layer importance.

Cons
- Missing/incorrect gradient expressions for the gated layer; backprop implementation is underspecified.
- Gate-bias initialization plays a pivotal role but is insufficiently analyzed.
- Comparisons are not fully normalized; some results reported as best-only.

Recommendation
Lean accept, contingent on (1) fixing and fully specifying the gradient/backprop equations and (2) strengthening reporting and comparisons (mean ± std, sensitivity to b_T, and explicit setting differences). These changes would make an already promising and influential paper both sound and reproducible.
