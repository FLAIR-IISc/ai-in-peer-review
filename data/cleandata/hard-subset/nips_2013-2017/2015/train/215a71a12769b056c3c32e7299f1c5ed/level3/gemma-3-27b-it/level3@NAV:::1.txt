## Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward neural networks. Inspired by LSTM recurrent networks, highway networks employ adaptive gating units to allow for direct information flow across many layers, mitigating the vanishing gradient problem that plagues deep networks. The core idea is well-described and builds logically upon prior work addressing the difficulties of training deep architectures, such as improved initialization techniques ([16, 17]), skip connections ([22, 24]), and fitnets [25]. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that highway networks can be trained effectively with hundreds of layers using standard SGD, a significant achievement given the challenges with training plain networks of comparable depth. The analysis of learned gate output patterns is particularly strong, revealing interesting insights into information routing within the network (Figures 2 & 3).

However, there are several points requiring attention. The paper states that the sigmoid gate unit *never* outputs exactly 0 or 1. This is true, but the implication that equation 5's gradients are therefore *incorrect* is misleading. The gradients are still well-defined; the issue is saturation, and it's unclear if this is the primary concern or simply a nuance. The authors *must* explicitly detail their backpropagation implementation to clarify this point. Furthermore, the initial negative bias scheme, while effective, feels somewhat arbitrary. A plot of performance versus initial bias value, as suggested by the guidelines, would be highly valuable, demonstrating the regularization effect of the bias and justifying the chosen values.

The comparative claims to Fitnets [25] need clarification. It is crucial to explicitly state whether the experimental settings (network architecture, datasets, training procedure, etc.) are identical, allowing for a direct comparison, or if differences exist. If differences exist, they need to be clearly articulated. Additionally, reporting only *maximum* accuracies is insufficient. Providing the mean and standard deviation for each method is essential for evaluating the statistical significance and reproducibility of the results.

Finally, while the empirical results are compelling, a learning-theory analysis of these gate units would significantly strengthen the contribution. Exploring why these gates facilitate the propagation of gradients and how they allow layers to act as conditional identities would provide deeper theoretical insight. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper tackles an important problem in deep learning – training very deep networks – with a novel and effective architectural solution. The idea is well-motivated, the experiments are convincing, and the analysis of learned networks is insightful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The lack of clarity regarding the backpropagation implementation, the arbitrary nature of the bias initialization without supporting analysis, and the insufficient reporting of results (mean/std dev) are significant weaknesses. 

Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. Addressing the points above would substantially improve the manuscript and further strengthen its case for acceptance.
