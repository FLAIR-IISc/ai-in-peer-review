This paper introduces Highway Networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea is to allow information to flow unimpeded across many layers via “information highways” achieved through adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTMs. This addresses the well-known problem of vanishing/exploding gradients that hinders the training of deep networks. The authors convincingly demonstrate that highway networks can be trained effectively with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent, something that proves challenging for traditional feedforward architectures. 

The paper builds upon previous work addressing the depth problem in neural networks, referencing techniques like better optimizers, initialization strategies, skip connections (FitNets), and the use of soft targets (neural history compression). It clearly distinguishes itself from these approaches by offering a mechanism for dynamic information routing rather than relying on fixed connections or pre-training schemes. The authors acknowledge similarities to LSTMs but highlight the key difference: highway networks operate on a layer-by-layer basis in feedforward networks, whereas LSTMs are recurrent. 

A major strength of this work is the thorough empirical analysis. The authors present comprehensive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, comparing highway networks to plain networks and existing state-of-the-art methods like FitNets. They demonstrate that highway networks not only train more easily but also achieve comparable or superior performance. The detailed analysis of gate behavior (Figures 2 & 3) and layer importance (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how these networks function, revealing a dynamic routing mechanism and showcasing that many layers contribute to the final result, especially for complex problems.

Arguments for acceptance are strong: the technique is novel, well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. It tackles a fundamental challenge in deep learning – training very deep networks – and offers a promising solution. The clarity of writing and organization is also commendable. A minor editorial point: “Tables 3 and 3” on page 5 should be corrected. Overall, this is a very solid contribution with significant potential impact. I have no suggested improvements.
