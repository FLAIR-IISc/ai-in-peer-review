

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture that enables training of extremely deep feedforward networks (hundreds of layers) through an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly with simple gradient descent, unlike plain networks that suffer from increasing depth. The work builds on previous efforts to address the challenges of training deep networks, including normalized initialization [16, 17], skip connections [22, 23], and knowledge distillation [25], but makes a significant contribution by enabling direct training of very deep architectures without two-stage procedures.

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant solution to a fundamental problem in deep learning. The highway network's gating mechanism (Equation 3) allows information to flow unimpeded through layers, effectively creating "information highways" that prevent gradient vanishing. This is a simple yet powerful idea that requires minimal architectural modification to existing feedforward networks. The authors provide strong empirical evidence through rigorous experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, showing that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly. The 100-layer highway network outperforms similarly-sized plain networks by over two orders of magnitude on MNIST, and highway networks achieve competitive results with state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10 with fewer parameters.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The theoretical analysis (Equations 4-5) clearly explains how the highway architecture enables information flow, and the experimental design is thorough and well-justified. The authors address potential weaknesses by analyzing transform gate behavior (Figure 2), demonstrating that the gating mechanism learns to route information dynamically based on input (Figure 3), and showing that layers contribute meaningfully to performance (Figure 4). The ablation study on layer importance for MNIST versus CIFAR-100 reveals an important insight: highway networks can learn to utilize all layers for complex problems while keeping unnecessary layers idle for simpler tasks, a behavior difficult to achieve with plain networks.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The notation is consistent, the architecture is well-explained, and the experiments are thoroughly documented. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproduction, including initialization strategies (negative bias for transform gates), implementation details, and publicly available code. The clarity of presentation makes the technical contributions accessible to the NIPS audience.

Regarding originality, this work represents a novel application of LSTM-inspired gating to feedforward networks, distinct from previous approaches. While skip connections [22, 23] and residual networks (which emerged shortly after this paper) share conceptual similarities, highway networks introduce a continuous, adaptive gating mechanism rather than fixed skip connections. The paper properly contextualizes this innovation against related work, noting that highway networks differ from "neural history compressors" [26] and "Fitnets" [25] by enabling single-stage training of very deep networks.

The significance of this work is substantial. It enables the study of extremely deep architectures, which was previously infeasible due to training difficulties. This advances the field by demonstrating that network depth can be scaled without compromising training efficiency or generalization. The authors' analysis of layer importance (Figure 4) provides new insights into how deep networks actually utilize their layers, which has implications for network design and pruning. While the current experiments are limited to MNIST and CIFAR, the approach has clear potential for larger-scale applications like ImageNet, which the authors rightly note as an important next step.

The paper has one notable limitation: it does not explore how highway networks relate to other deep network architectures that use layered substructures (e.g., Inception [36] or VGG's stacked 3x3 filters). An interesting perspective would be to view highway networks as layers composed of richer substructures, where each highway layer itself contains multiple computational paths. The authors might consider an experiment where a single thin-and-tall highway network is trained jointly on MNIST and CIFAR-10 (with a fork at the top), allowing analysis of how parameters underutilized by MNIST are repurposed by CIFAR-10. This would provide valuable insights into parameter sharing efficiency across different tasks within a deep hierarchy.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to deep learning. It successfully demonstrates that highway networks overcome the fundamental limitation of training very deep networks, opening the door to studying architectures with hundreds of layers. The authors provide strong theoretical and empirical support for their claims, and their analyses of gate behavior and layer importance are particularly compelling.

I recommend acceptance. The work is original, significant, and of high quality, addressing a fundamental challenge in deep learning. The authors have clearly demonstrated that highway networks enable training of extremely deep networks without requiring complex initialization schemes or two-stage training procedures. While testing on ImageNet would further validate the approach, the current results on standard benchmarks are sufficient to establish the paper's contribution. The simplicity and elegance of the highway network architecture make it a valuable addition to the deep learning toolbox, with clear potential for future applications.
