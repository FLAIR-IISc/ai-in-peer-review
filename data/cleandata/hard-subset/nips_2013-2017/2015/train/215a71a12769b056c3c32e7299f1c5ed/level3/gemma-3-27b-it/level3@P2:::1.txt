## Review of Training Very Deep Networks

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea, inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to incorporate adaptive gating mechanisms that allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers. The authors demonstrate, through a series of experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, that these networks can be trained effectively with standard stochastic gradient descent, surpassing the performance of plain networks of comparable size and complexity. The analysis of the learned gate outputs provides valuable insights into the network’s internal workings. Overall, this is a strong submission with a compelling idea, well-executed experiments, and insightful analysis.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the difficulty of training very deep networks. The authors correctly highlight that depth is crucial for achieving state-of-the-art results in various machine learning tasks, but increasing depth often leads to optimization difficulties. Previous work has explored solutions ranging from improved optimizers (e.g., [13, 14, 15]) and initialization strategies ([16, 17, 18, 19]) to novel activation functions ([20, 21]) and skip connections ([22, 23, 24, 25]).  The proposed highway networks build upon this line of research by explicitly designing layers that facilitate information flow, drawing inspiration from the gating mechanisms employed in recurrent neural networks like LSTMs [29, 30]. The work differentiates itself from previous approaches like FitNets [25] by demonstrating that deep networks can be trained *directly* in a single stage, without the need for teacher-student training or specialized architectures like Maxout.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The highway network architecture is a clever and intuitive solution to the vanishing gradient problem in deep networks.  The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism provides a principled way to enable long-range information flow.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained to significantly greater depths than plain networks, achieving competitive or superior performance on standard benchmarks (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100).
* **Analysis:**  The analysis of the learned gate biases and activity patterns (Figure 2 & 4) is particularly insightful. The observation that the network learns to selectively route information through different layers, and that the importance of layers varies depending on the task, is a valuable contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, architecture, and experimental setup. The notation is consistent and the mathematical formulation of the highway layer is straightforward.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Backpropagation Implementation:** The authors state that the Jacobian of the layer transform can be expressed as in equation (5), but this is only valid if T(x) is *exactly* 0 or 1. Since the transform gate uses a sigmoid function, T(x) will never be precisely 0 or 1. This raises a crucial question: how is backpropagation actually implemented through these layers? The authors *must* clarify this point and provide a more accurate description of the gradient computation.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** While the authors suggest a negative bias initialization for the transform gates, the justification for choosing specific values (e.g., -1, -2, -3 based on depth) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A plot illustrating the performance of the network as a function of the initial bias would be highly informative, revealing whether this bias acts as a regularizer and how it affects the learning dynamics.
* **Comparative Claims:** The comparisons to previous work, particularly FitNets [25], are not always clear. The authors state that they achieve comparable performance with a simpler training procedure, but it’s unclear whether the experiments were conducted using the *exact same* experimental setup (hyperparameters, data augmentation, etc.). The authors must explicitly state the differences and similarities in the experimental configurations to allow for a fair comparison.
* **Reporting of Results:** Reporting only the best accuracy achieved across multiple runs is insufficient.  The authors should provide the mean and standard deviation for each method (as they do in some sections, but not others) to allow for a more rigorous assessment of the results’ statistical significance.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound overall, but the ambiguity regarding backpropagation implementation and the lack of detailed analysis of the initialization scheme raise concerns. The experiments are well-designed and support the claims made.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the proposed method. However, clarifying the backpropagation details and providing more comprehensive results reporting would further improve clarity.

**Originality:** The highway network architecture is a novel and significant contribution to the field of deep learning. While inspired by LSTMs, the application of this gating mechanism to feedforward networks and the resulting benefits in terms of trainability are novel.

**Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of deep learning. Enabling the training of very deep networks opens up new possibilities for designing more powerful and efficient models. The analysis of the learned gate outputs provides valuable insights into the internal workings of these networks, which could inform the development of even more effective architectures.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
* Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks.
* Insightful analysis of learned gate outputs.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Ambiguity in the backpropagation implementation.
* Ad-hoc justification for the initial bias initialization.
* Lack of clarity in comparative claims.
* Incomplete reporting of results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. While there are some weaknesses that need to be addressed, the novelty, empirical results, and insightful analysis outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the backpropagation implementation, provide a more thorough analysis of the initialization scheme, and ensure consistent reporting of results.  I encourage the authors to address the comments regarding comparative claims to strengthen the paper's impact. A learning-theory analysis of the gate units could also add significant value.
