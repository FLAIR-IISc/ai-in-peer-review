This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel feed-forward architecture designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep neural networks. Inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, this architecture incorporates learnable, input-dependent gates that regulate information flow. These gates dynamically determine whether to transform the input at a given layer or carry it through unchanged, effectively creating "information highways." This elegantly addresses the optimization difficulties and degraded information propagation that have historically plagued standard "plain" networks as depth increases. The authors effectively situate their work, acknowledging conceptual similarities to LSTMs and more recent work like FitNets, while clearly differentiating their approach and providing direct empirical comparisons.

The paper's primary strengths are its originality, technical quality, and the depth of its analysis.
**Pros for acceptance:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The proposed architecture is a significant and novel contribution, offering a practical and effective solution to the long-standing challenge of training very deep networks. The ability to train networks hundreds of layers deep directly with SGD is a major step forward.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound and the claims are convincingly supported by a thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments on MNIST and CIFAR datasets are well-designed, demonstrating the clear superiority of highway networks over plain networks as depth increases, and showing competitive performance against contemporary models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making the core concepts and experimental results easy to follow.
*   **Analysis:** A particularly impressive aspect is the in-depth analysis of the trained networks' internal dynamics. The visualizations of gate activity and the lesion studies provide valuable insights into the learned data-dependent information routing and how the network learns to utilize its depth based on task complexity.

The paper presents a complete and polished piece of research. I have no major criticisms or suggestions for improvement, as the authors have been careful in their evaluation, thorough in their analysis, and clear in their exposition. The work is self-contained and convincing.

A minor editorial note: on page 5, the text refers to "Tables 3 and 3."

Overall, this is a very solid paper presenting an important, well-validated, and novel technique. It directly addresses a difficult and highly relevant problem in the field. The work is likely to be influential and widely used by both researchers and practitioners. I strongly recommend acceptance.
