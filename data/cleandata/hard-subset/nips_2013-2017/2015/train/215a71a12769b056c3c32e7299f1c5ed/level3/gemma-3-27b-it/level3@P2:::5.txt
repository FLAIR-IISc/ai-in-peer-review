This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. The core idea – inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) – is to incorporate adaptive gating mechanisms that allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers, effectively creating “information highways.” The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained directly via stochastic gradient descent to depths previously unattainable with conventional architectures, and offer strong experimental results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research addressing the difficulties of training deep networks. The introduction accurately contextualizes the problem, referencing advancements in optimizers ([13, 14, 15]), initialization strategies ([16, 17]), activation functions ([20, 21]), and skip connections ([22, 23, 2, 24]). It also appropriately acknowledges related work on knowledge distillation ([25, 26]) and layer-wise training ([26, 27]). The authors convincingly argue that despite these advances, training very deep networks remains challenging, motivating their proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Originality:** The application of an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism to deep feedforward networks is a genuinely novel contribution. While the individual components (gating, skip connections) are not new, their combination in this specific architecture is a significant advancement.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed highway network architecture is clearly defined and mathematically sound. The equations presented are concise and accurate, and the explanation of the transform and carry gates is easy to follow.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of highway networks. The comparison to plain networks on MNIST (Figure 1) is particularly striking, showcasing the ability of highway networks to train successfully at depths where plain networks fail.  Results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 further validate the approach.
* **Analysis and Insights:** The ablation studies and visualizations (Figures 2 & 4) provide valuable insights into the inner workings of highway networks.  The analysis of gate biases and layer importance contributes to a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive. The authors make the paper accessible even to readers unfamiliar with the specifics of LSTM networks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Relationship to Existing Architectures:** While the authors acknowledge related work, the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how highway networks relate to other deep learning architectures that incorporate complex internal structures.  Specifically, the architecture bears a resemblance to more recent developments such as Inception networks and the stacked 3x3 convolutions used in VGG.  A deeper exploration of whether highway layers can be interpreted as layers composed of richer sub-structures would be valuable.
* **Limited Dataset Scale:** Although the results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are encouraging, the ultimate test of a deep learning architecture is its performance on large-scale datasets like ImageNet.  The authors acknowledge this in their discussion, but extending the experiments to ImageNet would significantly strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a significant and original contribution to the field of deep learning.
* The proposed highway network architecture overcomes a key limitation in training very deep feedforward networks.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
* The analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of highway networks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of results on a large-scale dataset like ImageNet weakens the claims of practical significance.
* The connection to other architectures with complex internal layers could be more thoroughly discussed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism is a simple yet elegant solution to the problem of training very deep feedforward networks. The authors provide compelling experimental evidence to support their claims, and the analysis offers insightful observations into the behavior of the model. 

I would be particularly interested to see an experiment that further explores the dynamic routing of information within the network. Specifically, I propose training a single “thin-and-tall” highway network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR, forking the network at the top to produce separate classification outputs. This would allow investigation of whether parameters underutilized by MNIST are effectively repurposed by CIFAR, and how efficiently parameter sharing is achieved across the deep hierarchy. This experiment could reveal whether the network learns a generalized representation or maintains separate feature extractors for each dataset.

Despite the minor weaknesses mentioned above, I **strongly recommend acceptance** of this paper. The novelty, technical soundness, and empirical validation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.  I encourage the authors to consider extending the experiments to ImageNet and further exploring the relationship to existing architectures in future work.
