This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, dubbed "highway networks," which allows for the training of very deep networks using simple gradient descent. The key idea is to modify the architecture of feedforward networks by incorporating an adaptive gating mechanism, inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, to regulate the information flow across layers. This enables the creation of "information highways" that facilitate the training of networks with hundreds of layers.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the benefits and limitations of their approach, including experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets. They demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent, without the need for specialized initialization schemes or optimization techniques. The results show that highway networks can achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods, while also providing insights into the inner workings of the network.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to tackle the challenging problem of training very deep networks. The authors provide a simple and elegant solution that is grounded in theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. The experiments are well-designed and provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach.

A potential weakness of this paper is that it may be seen as a specialized solution to a specific problem. However, the authors provide a compelling argument that their approach can be applied to a wide range of problems, and that the benefits of highway networks can be extended to other areas of deep learning.

An alternative perspective on this work is that very deep networks may not be as "deep" as they seem, especially when layers contain sub-layers or more complex nonlinearities. For example, the Inception architecture and the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG can be seen as layers composed of richer substructures. It is possible that highway networks can be viewed similarly, with each layer composed of a combination of simpler transformations. This perspective suggests that the benefits of highway networks may be more related to the composition of layers rather than their sheer depth.

One potential experiment that could provide further insights into the workings of highway networks is to train a single thin-and-tall network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR, forking at the top. This would allow us to see if the inference patterns match those of separately trained networks, and whether the parameters underutilized by MNIST are repurposed by CIFAR. This experiment could also provide insights into the efficiency of parameter sharing across a deep hierarchy.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, and the authors should be commended for their innovative approach. The paper is well-written, and the analyses are solid. While there are some potential weaknesses, they are largely outweighed by the strengths of the paper. I recommend testing the approach on larger datasets, such as ImageNet, to further demonstrate its efficacy.
