Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces LSTM-inspired gating (transform and carry gates) to enable unimpeded information flow across many layers. Each layer outputs H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where T is a learned transform gate and 1−T acts as a carry gate. With an initialization that biases T toward small values, the network initially behaves close to identity and progressively learns to open gates where transformation is beneficial. The authors demonstrate successful optimization with tens to hundreds of layers via standard SGD, show competitive generalization on MNIST/CIFAR without elaborate augmentation, and analyze learned gating patterns, layer importance via lesioning, and data-dependent routing.

The work builds on: (i) LSTM gating for long-range credit assignment [29,30], (ii) normalized initialization and rectifiers that stabilize deep nets [16,17], (iii) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision [22,23,24], (iv) FitNets/teacher hints for training thin/deep nets [25], and (v) maxout/locally competitive mechanisms that help credit assignment [20,38]. The paper’s contribution is a simple, general architectural mechanism that makes deeply stacked feedforward networks trainable with direct backprop, without layerwise pretraining or teacher hints, and with dynamic, input-dependent routing.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and elegance: a minimal LSTM-like gating mechanism adapted to deep feedforward stacks; C=1−T simplifies parameterization and preserves dimensionality.
- Strong optimization evidence: thin but very deep MLPs (up to 100 layers) train readily; highway nets converge faster and achieve far lower training loss than equally parameterized plain nets.
- Generalization: competitive MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 performance in a restrained training regime; ability to train deep/parameter-limited models comparable to FitNets in a single stage.
- Analysis: informative gate bias/activity visualizations, class-conditional routing patterns, and lesion experiments revealing which depths contribute on simple vs complex datasets.
- Practicality: hyperparameter-insensitive gate-bias heuristic; applicability to fully connected and convolutional layers; publicly released code.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scale and scope: results stop at CIFAR; demonstrating the method on a large-scale dataset (e.g., ImageNet) would make the significance more conclusive and probe very deep regimes in realistic settings.
- Comparisons: more direct baselines against other skip/shortcut mechanisms (fixed identity skips, deep supervision) would quantify the added value of adaptive gating versus hard-wired residual paths.
- Ablations: the choice C=1−T is reasonable but untested; evaluating explicit carry gates (C learned independently), or tying versus untied gate parameters across depth, would clarify design trade-offs.
- Overhead and efficiency: report parameter/FLOP overhead attributable to gates, wall-clock training time, and memory footprint; quantify whether gains persist at equal compute.
- Stability diagnostics: provide gradient norm statistics across depth, sensitivity to gate bias initialization, and robustness to activation choices, to strengthen the optimization claims.
- Depth claims: pilot notes mention >1000-layer training; including at least a brief quantitative result (even on MNIST) would substantiate the headline.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and careful. Claims are supported by controlled optimization studies, multiple datasets, and analyses that reveal mechanism rather than mere accuracy gains. Some additional ablations and compute accounting would further bolster the empirical case.

Clarity
Well-written and organized; the architectural description is clear and the training recipe is easy to follow. Minor suggestions: (i) consolidate notation early (block vs unit), (ii) detail the hyperparameter search ranges and selected schedules in an appendix, (iii) clarify dimensionality-changing transitions (where plain layers are interleaved).

Originality
Adapting LSTM-style gating to deep feedforward networks is novel and insightful. While related to skip connections and competitive nonlinearities, the adaptive, multiplicative gating that yields input-dependent routing and identity paths distinguishes the approach. The related Grid LSTM connection is appropriately cited.

Significance
High. The method directly targets a central obstacle in deep learning (training very deep networks) with a simple, general mechanism likely to influence architectures and practice. If validated at ImageNet scale and compared against alternative shortcut strategies, it could meaningfully advance the state of the art.

Suggested additional experiments/analyses
- Large-scale test: train a 50–100+ layer convolutional highway net on ImageNet with standard augmentation; compare to similarly sized plain/skip-based baselines.
- Gate design ablations: independent C vs C=1−T; gated identity with learned scaling; per-channel vs per-unit gates; impact on accuracy/compute.
- Compute budget accounting: parameters, MACs, and training time with/without gates; scalability with width/depth.
- Path-depth perspective: analyze effective path length distribution (fraction of open gates) per example/class; gradient flow statistics across depth to connect to vanishing/exploding narratives.
- Multitask sharing experiment (new): train a thin-and-tall highway backbone jointly on MNIST and CIFAR, forking only at the classifier heads. Evaluate (a) whether layers underutilized by MNIST become functional for CIFAR, (b) alignment of gate activity and lesion profiles with separately trained single-task models, and (c) the efficiency of parameter sharing across the hierarchy.
- Alternative perspective: connect to “rich sublayer” views from Inception and VGG (stacked 3×3) by interpreting highway layers as composite modules; compare representational capacity per depth under matched compute.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general mechanism that materially eases optimization of very deep networks.
- Solid empirical evidence and insightful analyses of gating behavior and layer utility.
- Competitive results without heavy engineering; code availability aids reproducibility.
- Clear relation to and extension of influential ideas (LSTM gating, skip connections).

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of large-scale validation; unclear compute/parameter overhead versus fixed-skip baselines.
- Limited ablations on gate parameterization and sensitivity to initialization/hyperparameters.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The idea is elegant and impactful, the empirical and analytical evidence is compelling for the paper’s stated goals, and the work is likely to spur follow-up on deep architectures. Strengthening with the suggested comparisons and a large-scale experiment would make the case unequivocal.

Reviewer confidence
High on methodology and positioning; moderate on large-scale performance implications pending ImageNet-scale results.
