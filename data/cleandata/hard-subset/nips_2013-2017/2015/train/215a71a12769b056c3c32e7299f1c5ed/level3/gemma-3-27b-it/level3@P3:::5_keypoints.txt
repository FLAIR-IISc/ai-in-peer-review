- Introduces highway networks, an architecture using LSTM-like gating to enable training of very deep feedforward networks.
- Core idea: adaptive transform and carry gates create “information highways” that regulate layer-to-layer information flow.
- Demonstrates direct training with standard SGD to depths previously inaccessible to plain networks.
- Shows competitive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.
- Situates work within prior literature on vanishing gradients, initializations, activations, skip connections, distillation, and LSTMs.
- Key strength: simple, effective LSTM-inspired gating trick that scales to exceptional depth.
- Strong experimental validation showing highway networks outperform plain networks as depth increases.
- Analyses of information routing and layer importance provide useful insights into network behavior.
- Code release enhances reproducibility and practical impact.
- Weakness: comparison to other modern deep architectures (e.g., residual networks) is limited.
- Weakness: limited investigation of pruning or regularization for potentially unused layers.
- Weakness: information routing analysis is intriguing but not sufficiently quantitative across classes.
- Raises concern that achieved “depth” might be illusory, akin to richer per-layer substructures in other architectures.
- Proposes a shared “thin-and-tall” multitask experiment (MNIST + CIFAR-10 heads) to test representational reuse and parameter efficiency.
- Recommendation: overall positive — strong contribution with minor weaknesses; recommends acceptance and suggests testing on larger datasets like ImageNet.
