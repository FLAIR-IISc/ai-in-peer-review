This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea is to allow information to flow directly across many layers via “information highways” inspired by the gating mechanisms of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained effectively using standard stochastic gradient descent, even with hundreds of layers, overcoming the difficulties typically encountered when training very deep “plain” networks.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research addressing the challenges of training deep neural networks. Previous approaches have focused on improved optimization algorithms (e.g., [13, 14, 15]), initialization strategies ([16, 17, 18, 19]), specialized activation functions ([20, 21]), and skip connections ([22, 23, 24, 25]). The authors appropriately position their contribution within this context, highlighting how highway networks differ from and potentially complement these existing techniques. The connection to LSTM networks is also well-established and provides a strong motivation for the proposed architecture. The problem addressed – the difficulty of training very deep networks – is a central challenge in the field and has been recognized in prior NIPS proceedings and related literature.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The core idea of highway networks is conceptually simple yet surprisingly effective. The authors convincingly demonstrate, through a series of well-designed experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, that these networks can be trained to depths previously inaccessible to standard architectures. The experimental results are compelling, showing that highway networks not only train effectively but also generalize well to unseen data. The analysis section, while perhaps expandable (see weaknesses below), provides valuable insights into the inner workings of these networks, showing how the transform gates regulate information flow and which layers are most crucial for performance. The clarity of the paper is also commendable. The authors present their ideas in a well-organized and accessible manner, with clear notation and explanations. The availability of code and hyperparameters further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the analysis section includes visualizations of gate biases and activity patterns, it could benefit from more extensive investigation. For example, exploring the learned gate values for different input classes in more detail, beyond the initial glimpses provided in Figure 3, could offer deeper insights into the routing mechanism. Furthermore, while the ablation studies relating to the transform gate bias are solid, a more comprehensive ablation study regarding the impact of different activation functions or layer configurations would be beneficial. The comparison to Fitnets [25] is valuable, but a more nuanced discussion of the computational trade-offs between the two approaches would be useful. Finally, the discussion of related work, while thorough, could perhaps be expanded to include recent advancements in residual networks [36] and dense connections [37], which share similarities with the concept of highway connections.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network architecture offers a novel approach to addressing the challenges of training very deep networks. It introduces a unique mechanism for regulating information flow that distinguishes it from existing techniques.
*   **Significance:** The ability to train very deep networks opens up new possibilities for tackling complex machine learning problems and potentially achieving state-of-the-art performance on a wider range of tasks.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The analysis section provides supporting evidence for the underlying mechanisms.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The analysis section, while insightful, could be more extensive and provide deeper insights into the learned behavior of the networks.
*   The connection to more recent advances in residual networks and dense connections could be more explicitly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed highway network architecture is a novel and effective approach to training very deep networks, and the experimental results are compelling. While the analysis section could be further expanded, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** to NIPS 2015.
