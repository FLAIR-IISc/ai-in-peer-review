- Proposes "Highway Networks," a novel feed-forward architecture with learned transform and carry gates (inspired by LSTMs) to enable very deep networks.  
- Gates create information highways that let gradients flow, mitigating vanishing/exploding gradient problems.  
- Highway networks can be trained with standard SGD at extreme depths (demonstrated up to 100 layers).  
- Achieves strong performance on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, translating training ease into competitive generalization.  
- Highly original: adapts LSTM-style, data-dependent gating to feed-forward depth problems, a significant insight beyond simple skip connections.  
- Technically sound with compelling experiments; optimization comparisons clearly show plain networks fail at large depths while highway networks succeed.  
- Exceptionally clear and well-written presentation with intuitive explanation and straightforward notation.  
- Insightful analysis: gate activity visualizations and lesioning experiments show adaptive, task-dependent use of depth and data-dependent routing.  
- Minor textual inconsistency: statement that "most transform gates are active on average" for CIFAR-100 conflicts with figure showing low average gate activity and needs clarification.  
- Suggestion: compare to simpler baselines (e.g., near-identity-initialized plain networks) to empirically isolate the benefit of dynamic, per-example gating.  
- Overall: excellent, impactful paper with strong empirical and theoretical contributions; minor clarifications needed but clearly a top-tier accept.
