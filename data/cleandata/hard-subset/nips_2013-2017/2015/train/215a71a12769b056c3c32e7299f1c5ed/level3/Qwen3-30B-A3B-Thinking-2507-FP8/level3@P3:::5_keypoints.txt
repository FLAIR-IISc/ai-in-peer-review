- Introduces highway networks that enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward nets (100+ layers) using LSTM-inspired adaptive gating.  
- Core formulation: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where sigmoid gate T(x) controls information flow.  
- Empirical claim: highway networks overcome plain-deep training difficulties—100-layer highway nets match 10-layer plain nets on MNIST and generalize to CIFAR-10/100.  
- Gate-activity analyses (Figs.2–4) reveal dynamic, input-dependent routing and layer-specific utilization.  
- Lesioning experiments show deep layers contribute to complex tasks (CIFAR-100) but remain largely idle for simple tasks (MNIST).  
- Originality: first application of LSTM-style gating to feedforward architectures, bridging recurrent and feedforward paradigms.  
- Experimental quality: rigorous validation across datasets, effective negative-bias initialization, and reproducible code.  
- Clarity: clear notation, intuitive figures, and an accessible highway-layer definition for non-recurrent experts.  
- Significance: addresses a fundamental bottleneck—direct training of very deep networks—and provides a new tool (layer-importance analysis) for architecture design.  
- Weakness: limited scale—no ImageNet experiments to confirm scalability and real-world effectiveness at 100+ layers.  
- Weakness: needs better contextualization of “depth” vs. layer complexity—highway layers are richer substructures, blurring traditional depth definitions.  
- Suggested experiment: jointly train a thin-and-tall network on MNIST and CIFAR (forking at the top) to test cross-task parameter reuse across depth.  
- Recommendation: accept—novel, elegant, and impactful—request addition of ImageNet experiments in the camera-ready version.  
- Final insight: reframe the contribution as increasing hierarchical complexity within layers (akin to Inception/VGG) rather than solely increasing layer count.
