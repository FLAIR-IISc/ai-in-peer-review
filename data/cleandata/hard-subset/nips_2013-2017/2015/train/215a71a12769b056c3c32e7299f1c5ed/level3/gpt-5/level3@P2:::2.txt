Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes highway networks, a feed-forward architecture that introduces input-dependent gating (transform and carry gates) to regulate information flow across many layers. The key idea is to allow information to pass unchanged through layers when appropriate, thereby enabling effective gradient propagation and alleviating optimization difficulties associated with depth. The design is inspired by LSTM gating mechanisms, but applied to deep feed-forward (and convolutional) networks without recurrence. A practical initialization (negative bias for the transform gate) encourages early carry behavior, easing optimization.

The work is well situated in the literature: it relates to LSTM gating [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber], skip connections/deep supervision, normalized initialization for deep nets [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], local competition/maxout [Goodfellow et al.], and teacher-student “hints” training [Romero et al.]. Relative to FitNets, which use a two-stage distillation procedure to train thin, deeper nets under budget, highway networks train directly via SGD in one stage and achieve similar or better accuracy with comparable parameter and compute budgets. Compared to classic skip connections, the gating makes shortcuts input-adaptive rather than static. The paper includes thorough analyses of gating behavior (gate statistics across layers, data-dependent routing) and layer lesioning, shedding light on when depth is utilized.

Quality
The paper is technically sound. The architectural formulation is clear: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), with T(x) = σ(WT x + bT), and C = 1 − T for simplicity. The initialization strategy (negative bT) is justified by analogy to LSTM forget-gate biasing and validated empirically. Optimization studies on MNIST carefully control capacity across depths, showing that plain deep networks degrade while highway networks of 50–100 layers train effectively and converge faster. Generalization is demonstrated on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 with convolutional highway networks, including apples-to-apples comparisons against FitNets at fixed parameter/compute budgets. The analyses (gate activity distributions, class-conditional routing, and layer lesioning) provide compelling evidence that gating both eases optimization and contributes meaningfully to computation, with deeper layers activated more for harder datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100). Claims are supported by experiments across multiple datasets, and code is publicly available.

A reasonable limitation is that results are not pushed to absolute state-of-the-art with heavy augmentation; instead the focus is on demonstrating trainability and generalization under standard settings. This is appropriate for the stated goals.

Clarity
The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized. The architectural description is easy to follow, and figures/analyses are informative and well-explained. Training details and hyperparameters are described sufficiently to reproduce results, including the practical guidance for gate bias initialization by depth. Minor editorial nit: “Tables 3 and 3” on p.5 appears to be a typo.

Originality
The work is original in adapting LSTM-style gating to deep feed-forward networks, yielding input-dependent, learnable “highways” that differ from fixed skip connections and prior deep supervision strategies. The combination of simple gating, practical initialization, and extensive depth is novel for feed-forward networks at the time of submission. The differentiation from FitNets (no teacher, direct training, comparable budgets) and from skip-connection-only approaches is clearly articulated.

Significance
The contribution is significant. Training very deep networks reliably is central to modern deep learning; providing a simple architectural mechanism that scales to hundreds (and pilot experiments suggest thousands) of layers with straightforward SGD is impactful. The method is broadly applicable (fully connected and convolutional layers), does not depend on specialized optimizers, and empirically enables deeper, thinner models under compute constraints. The careful analyses (data-dependent routing, layer importance) provide insights likely to influence future architecture design and diagnostics.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated architecture enabling direct training of very deep networks via input-dependent gating.
- Strong optimization results demonstrating robustness to depth and faster convergence than plain nets.
- Competitive generalization on MNIST/CIFAR-10/100, including fair comparisons to FitNets under comparable budgets.
- Thorough analyses of gate behavior, dynamic routing, and layer importance via lesioning.
- Practical, simple initialization guidance; code and scripts released.
- Well-written and carefully related to prior work; similarities to LSTM and FitNets are acknowledged and differences clarified.

Cons
- Results are not driven to SOTA under heavy data augmentation; focus is on trainability rather than ultimate accuracy.
- Empirical scope is limited to MNIST/CIFAR; larger-scale experiments would further strengthen the case (though not strictly necessary for this contribution).
- The choice C = 1 − T is reasonable but other parameterizations or ablations are not explored in depth (not a major concern).

Decision rationale
The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective architectural idea with convincing evidence that it addresses a core challenge in deep learning (training very deep networks). The empirical results are sound and fairly presented, the analysis is insightful, and the writing is excellent. Limitations are minor and consistent with the paper’s stated goals. Overall, this is a very solid submission that merits acceptance.

Minor comments
- Editorial: fix “Tables 3 and 3” on p.5.
