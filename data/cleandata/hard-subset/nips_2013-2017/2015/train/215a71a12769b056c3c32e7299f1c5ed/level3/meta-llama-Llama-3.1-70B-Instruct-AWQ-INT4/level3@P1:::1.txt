The paper "Training Very Deep Networks" introduces a novel architecture called highway networks, designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep neural networks. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), even with hundreds of layers, and achieve competitive performance on various tasks.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the problem of training deep networks, highlighting the importance of depth in achieving state-of-the-art performance in various machine learning tasks. The authors also provide a thorough review of previous work on deep networks, including the challenges of training deep networks and various approaches that have been proposed to address these challenges.

The proposed highway network architecture is inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and uses adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow between layers. The authors provide a detailed description of the architecture, including the notation and mathematical formulation of the layer transformations.

The experimental results presented in the paper are fairly convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep networks. The authors provide a series of optimization experiments, comparing highway networks to plain networks with normalized initialization, and show that highway networks do not suffer from increasing depth. They also provide results on MNIST digit classification, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 object recognition tasks, demonstrating competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

The analysis of learned networks' gate-output patterns is also strong, providing insights into how the highway networks work. The authors show that the transform gate biases decrease during training, and that the mean activity of the transform gates is inversely correlated with the average activity of the gates. They also demonstrate that the network can learn to dynamically adjust the routing of information based on the current input.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, the authors use a sigmoid function for the gate unit T, which means that the outputs are never exactly 0/1. The gradients in Equation 5 are therefore incorrect, and the authors must explain exactly how backpropagation is implemented. Secondly, the initialization of the gate biases with constant negative values by depth is unsatisfying, and the authors should provide a plot of performance vs initial bias to demonstrate the effect of the bias on the performance of the network.

Additionally, the comparative claims made in the paper should be clarified. The authors should state whether recent studies use the same experimental setting, allowing for direct comparison, or not. Furthermore, the authors should provide mean and standard deviation for every method, rather than just reporting max accuracies.

Finally, the authors could benefit from including a learning-theory analysis of gate units, which allow layers to act as conditional identities and enable inputs to propagate through many selective layers. This would provide a deeper understanding of how the highway networks work and why they are effective.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel architecture that addresses the challenges of training very deep neural networks.
* The experimental results are fairly convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep networks.
* The analysis of learned networks' gate-output patterns is strong, providing insights into how the highway networks work.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The implementation of backpropagation is not clearly explained, and the gradients in Equation 5 are incorrect.
* The initialization of the gate biases is unsatisfying, and the authors should provide a plot of performance vs initial bias.
* The comparative claims made in the paper are not clearly stated, and the authors should provide more details on the experimental settings used in recent studies.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the idea is well-described. The experiments are fairly convincing, and the analysis of learned networks' gate-output patterns is strong. However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement that need to be addressed.
