

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks through the use of adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTM networks. The authors propose that highway networks can overcome the training difficulties associated with increasing depth by allowing information to flow unimpeded across many layers via "information highways." This work builds on previous NIPS contributions including LSTM networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), normalized weight initialization (Glorot & Bengio, 2010), and skip connections (Sermanet et al., 2013), while offering a more principled approach to information flow in deep networks. The paper is particularly significant as it predates the ResNet architecture (He et al., 2015) by two years and provides an important theoretical and empirical foundation for the subsequent success of deep residual networks.

## Strengths

The paper's main strength lies in its clear theoretical motivation and convincing experimental validation. The authors effectively demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly with simple gradient descent even with hundreds of layers, while plain networks become intractable. The analysis of gate-output patterns in Figure 2 provides valuable insights into how highway networks operate, showing that the gates learn to be selective rather than simply shutting down layers. The paper's experimental section is comprehensive, comparing highway networks to plain networks on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, with results showing consistent improvements in training and generalization for deeper architectures.

## Weaknesses

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing:

1. **Technical Error in Gradient Computation**: The paper claims in Equation (5) that the Jacobian of the layer transform equals the identity matrix when T=0, but since T = σ(W^T x + b^T) ∈ (0,1) for all real inputs, the conditions in Equation (4) can never be met exactly. The authors must clarify how backpropagation is actually implemented for the gate units.

2. **Initialization Concern**: The use of constant negative biases (e.g., -1, -3) for transform gates is not well-justified. The bias acts as a regularizer favoring "longer highways" and should be treated as a hyperparameter. The authors should provide a plot showing performance as a function of initial bias values.

3. **Comparative Claims**: The paper's claims about highway networks outperforming FitNets (Romero et al., 2015) need clarification. The authors must specify whether they used identical experimental settings or if there are differences in data preprocessing, augmentation, or hyperparameters that might affect the comparison.

4. **Reporting Practice**: The paper reports only maximum accuracies without providing mean and standard deviation across multiple runs. This is insufficient for proper statistical evaluation, as the authors acknowledge in Section 3.1 ("to provide a better estimate of the variability of classification results due to random initialization, we report our results in the format Best (mean ± std.dev.) based on 5 runs wherever available"). However, this format is not consistently applied throughout the paper.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Learning-Theoretic Analysis**: The authors should include a learning-theoretic analysis of the gate units. The ability of highway networks to allow layers to act as conditional identities and enable inputs to propagate through many selective layers is a significant theoretical contribution that deserves formal analysis.

2. **Clarify Experimental Setup**: The paper should explicitly state all experimental differences between highway networks and comparative methods, especially regarding training procedures and hyperparameters.

3. **Address Gradient Implementation**: The authors must explain exactly how backpropagation is implemented for the sigmoidal gate units, addressing the mathematical inconsistency in Equation (5).

4. **Provide Additional Plots**: A performance vs. initial bias plot would strengthen the paper's claims about the importance of the initial bias parameter.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the deep learning literature by introducing a novel architecture that enables direct training of extremely deep networks. The core idea of highway networks is well-motivated, and the experimental results are largely convincing. However, the technical errors in the gradient computation and the lack of proper statistical reporting are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before publication.

The paper's main contribution lies in demonstrating that very deep networks can be trained directly without two-stage training procedures, which was a major limitation of previous approaches. The analysis of gate behavior in Figure 2 is particularly insightful and provides valuable understanding of how highway networks operate.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper has strong theoretical motivation, compelling experimental results, and important insights into deep network training. The weaknesses identified above are critical but fixable. Addressing the technical issues with the gradient computation, providing additional experimental analysis of the bias initialization, and ensuring consistent statistical reporting would significantly strengthen the paper.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning (training very deep networks) with a novel and well-justified approach. The paper's insights into information flow in deep networks are likely to influence future work on deep architecture design, including the subsequent development of residual networks.
