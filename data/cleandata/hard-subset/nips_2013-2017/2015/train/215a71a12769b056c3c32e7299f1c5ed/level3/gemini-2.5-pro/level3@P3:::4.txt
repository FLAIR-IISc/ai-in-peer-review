### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, termed "Highway Networks," which facilitates gradient-based training of networks with hundreds of layers. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The proposed highway layers introduce adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates that learn to regulate the flow of information. For a given input `x`, a layer's output is a dynamic combination of a non-linear transformation of `x` (as in a standard network layer) and `x` itself. This creates "information highways" where information can pass through many layers without attenuation, effectively mitigating the vanishing gradient problem that plagues traditional deep architectures.

The authors relate their work to previous attempts to train deep networks, such as improved initialization strategies (e.g., Glorot & Bengio, 2010; He et al., 2015), advanced optimizers, and methods involving skip connections or deep supervision (e.g., FitNets, Romero et al., 2015). They position their method as a more direct and flexible architectural solution, generalizing fixed skip connections into a learnable, data-dependent routing mechanism. The paper provides extensive empirical evidence on MNIST and CIFAR datasets, demonstrating that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD to depths of 100 layers or more, where conventional "plain" networks of similar depth fail to train.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly relevant problem in deep learning. By enabling the direct training of extremely deep networks, this work opened up new avenues for architectural exploration. The ability to successfully train networks with tens or hundreds of layers using simple SGD was a major advance at the time and has had a lasting impact on the field, influencing subsequent work on deep architectures.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The core contribution—adapting the LSTM gating concept to feedforward networks—is both clever and highly effective. While the idea of shortcut connections was not new, the introduction of a learnable, dynamic gating mechanism to control the information flow is a significant and original contribution. This provides a principled framework that generalizes prior, more ad-hoc approaches.

3.  **Quality of Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evidence supporting the authors' claims is strong and convincing.
    *   The controlled experiments in Section 3.1, comparing plain and highway networks of increasing depth on MNIST, provide a clear and compelling demonstration of the method's effectiveness at overcoming optimization difficulties. The results show that while plain networks degrade rapidly after ~20 layers, highway networks maintain stable performance up to 100 layers.
    *   The comparison with FitNets on CIFAR is particularly strong. The authors show that highway networks can achieve similar or better performance than the more complex, two-stage "hint-based" training procedure required for FitNets, highlighting the practical advantages and simplicity of their approach.
    *   The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are competitive with state-of-the-art methods of the time, demonstrating that the architecture not only trains well but also generalizes effectively.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core idea is introduced with clear motivation and simple, intuitive equations. The connection to LSTMs is explained well, and the experimental setup and results are presented clearly. The main messages of the paper are communicated effectively, making it easy for the reader to grasp the key contributions.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4, while insightful, could have been more comprehensive. The visualizations of gate activities and the lesion studies provide a fascinating glimpse into the network's inner workings. However, these analyses are based on single "best" models for MNIST and CIFAR-100. The conclusions would be stronger if they were supported by aggregated statistics over multiple runs or if they explored a wider range of architectures. For instance, further investigation into how routing patterns differ across various tasks or network depths could have added more depth to the analysis.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper's claims are supported almost exclusively by empirical results. While the intuition inherited from LSTMs is strong, a more formal analysis of the gradient dynamics in highway networks would have been a valuable addition. A brief theoretical discussion on why the gating mechanism prevents gradients from vanishing (by ensuring the Jacobian can be close to the identity matrix) would have further solidified the paper's foundations.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   In the analysis of transform gate biases (Section 4), it's noted that biases often decrease during training, making the gates more selective. This is an interesting finding. Does this suggest that the initialization with a negative bias is not just a "trick" to get training started, but rather a good prior for the network's final configuration?
*   The layer lesioning study (Figure 4) is a great way to probe layer importance. It would be interesting to see the complementary experiment: what is the effect of forcing a single layer's transform gates to be fully *open* (`T=1`)? This might help disentangle the importance of the highway path versus the transformation itself at each layer.
*   The paper mentions that a more complex block design "closely resembling an LSTM block" was explored in pilot experiments. While the simplified form presented is elegant, a brief comment on the performance trade-offs of the more complex design (e.g., in an appendix) would have been informative.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a novel, significant, and well-supported contribution to the field. It presents an elegant and effective solution to the crucial problem of training very deep neural networks. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. While the analysis section could be expanded, this is a minor point that does not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. The paper is a clear accept.
