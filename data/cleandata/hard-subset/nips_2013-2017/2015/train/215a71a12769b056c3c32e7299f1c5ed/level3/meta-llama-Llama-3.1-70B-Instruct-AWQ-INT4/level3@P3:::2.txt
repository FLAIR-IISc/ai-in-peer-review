This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture, called highway networks, that enables the training of very deep networks. The key idea is to introduce input-dependent gates that allow information to flow unchanged through layers, thereby alleviating the problem of vanishing gradients. This approach is inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and uses adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of how the gates select inputs across different layers and demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), even with hundreds of layers. The paper also presents promising empirical results on several benchmark datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

One of the strengths of this paper is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of training deep networks and motivate the need for a new architecture. The presentation of the highway network architecture is also well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the gate behavior and its implications for training deep networks.

The paper also acknowledges similarities to previous work, such as FitNets and LSTMs, but clearly explains the differences and includes comparisons. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained more easily and efficiently than FitNets, especially for deeper networks.

The empirical results presented in the paper are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep networks. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the gate behavior and its implications for training deep networks.

One minor editorial note is that there is a typo in the paper, where "Tables 3 and 3" is mentioned on page 5.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the gate behavior and its implications for training deep networks, and the empirical results are promising. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and original.
