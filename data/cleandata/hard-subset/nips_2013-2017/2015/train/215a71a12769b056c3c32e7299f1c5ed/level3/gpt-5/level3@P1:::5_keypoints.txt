- Proposes highway networks: LSTM-inspired gated layers y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)) enabling very deep feedforward nets to train with plain SGD.
- Uses negative transform-gate bias initialization so gates start near carry (identity) and let gradients/information flow along "information highways."
- Empirical optimization studies show highway nets avoid degradation with depth and train faster/to lower loss than matched plain nets (50–100 layers on MNIST).
- Classification results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 are competitive at modest parameter budgets, matching or exceeding fitnets without distillation.
- Provides analyses of gate bias behavior, per-sample gate activity, class-conditional routing, and layer lesioning showing input-dependent routing and effective/useful depth.
- Positions work relative to prior art: connects to LSTM gating, skip/shortcut connections, deep supervision, hint-based distillation, and initialization strategies while offering end-to-end trainability and adaptive routing.
- Methodology is careful (random search, matched parameter counts, reporting mean±std); technical approach is sound and experiments convincing.
- Paper is well written and organized; highway layer formulation and biasing recipe are simple and practical.
- Requests clearer accounting of parameter/compute overhead introduced by gates and a consolidated schematic for convolutional highway blocks.
- Novelty: applying LSTM-like gating to feedforward depth and the carry-vs-transform view with biasing-to-carry initialization is a clean, original contribution.
- Significance: enables straightforward training of hundreds of layers, is generic to FC and conv layers, and provides diagnostic tools; scalability to larger datasets would increase impact.
- Alternative interpretation: many layers act near-identity, so highway nets can be seen as deep compositions of richer sub-modules selectively applied per input.
- Suggested larger-scale validation: include ImageNet experiments to assess scalability and training dynamics.
- Suggested stronger baselines: compare to deep nets with modern tricks (He init, batch norm) and to fixed skip connections to isolate gate benefit.
- Recommended ablations: test independent carry gate vs C=1−T, gate-bias sensitivity across depths, and report gradient norms early in training.
- Measure parameter/compute overhead: quantify extra params, FLOPs, and wall-clock time and report accuracy-per-compute trade-offs.
- Quantify effective depth: report distributions/entropy of T across layers and correlation with lesion sensitivity.
- Proposed multi-task experiment: train a single thin-and-tall trunk jointly on MNIST and CIFAR to study layer repurposing and shared gating behavior.
- Provide architectural clarity: schematic of convolutional highway block showing where padding/stride are applied for H and T.
- Pros: simple principled change with strong intuition, compelling optimization evidence, solid CIFAR results without distillation, insightful analyses, broadly applicable and easy to implement.
- Cons: lacks large-scale validation (ImageNet), incomplete comparisons to strongest contemporary tricks and fixed skip connections, missing several ablations (independent carry, compute overhead, bias sensitivity).
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept — elegant, practical mechanism that makes very deep networks trainable, with convincing evidence; add large-scale experiment and ablations for camera-ready.
