This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate training of very deep feedforward networks by allowing for unimpeded information flow across layers through adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTMs. The core idea is sound, building upon prior work demonstrating the benefits of depth in neural networks and addressing the challenges of training such deep architectures. The authors effectively relate their work to existing approaches like improved optimizers, initialization strategies, and skip connections. The paper is well-written and presents its contributions clearly.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the experimental results are fairly convincing, demonstrating that highway networks can be trained to greater depths than plain networks without significant degradation in performance. The analysis of learned gate-output patterns is a strong point, providing insight into how the network utilizes the highway mechanism. However, a crucial technical detail is missing (discussed below). The work feels complete in terms of experimental validation, but theoretical backing beyond the LSTM inspiration would strengthen it. The authors are reasonably careful in presenting their results, but could be more rigorous in their reporting (see below).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The notation is standard and the core concepts are explained in a straightforward manner. The inclusion of equations helps to formalize the architecture, although equation (5) is problematic (see below). The analysis section provides valuable visualizations and insights into the network's behavior.

**Originality:** The core idea of using adaptive gating mechanisms to create "information highways" is novel and represents a significant contribution. While inspired by LSTMs, applying this concept to feedforward networks in this specific way is a new approach. The analysis of the learned gating patterns and layer importance adds to the originality of the work. The authors acknowledge related work, including a similar LSTMinspired model proposed concurrently.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. Enabling the training of very deep networks opens up opportunities for exploring more powerful and efficient architectures. The insights gained from analyzing the gating mechanisms could inform the design of future network architectures. The ability to investigate the contribution of individual layers is also a valuable contribution. This paper addresses a difficult problem – training very deep networks – and offers a promising solution.

**Specific Concerns & Suggestions:**

1. **Gradient Calculation (Critical):** The authors state in Equation 5 that “dy/dx = {I, if T(x,WT) = 0, H’(x,WH), if T(x,WT) = 1.” This is incorrect. Since T(x,WT) outputs a value *between* 0 and 1 due to the sigmoid function, the Jacobian will *always* be a weighted average of I and H’(x,WH). The authors *must* explain exactly how backpropagation is implemented through these gates. Simply stating the Jacobian as a discrete choice is misleading and incorrect. This significantly impacts the confidence in the experimental results.

2. **Initialization Bias Exploration:** The authors acknowledge that the initial bias for the transform gates acts as a regularizer. However, simply stating that -1, -2, and -3 are "good" values is insufficient. A plot of performance (e.g., test accuracy) versus initial bias value would be extremely informative. This would demonstrate the sensitivity of the network to this hyperparameter and provide practical guidance for future users.

3. **Comparative Claims:** The paper compares to FitNets and other methods. However, the authors need to be precise about the experimental settings. Do recent studies use *exactly* the same CIFAR-10/100 datasets, data augmentation techniques, and network architectures? If so, a direct comparison is valid. If not, the comparison is weakened. The authors should explicitly state the differences in experimental setup when comparing to prior work.

4. **Reporting Statistics:** Reporting only the "best" accuracy is insufficient. The field is increasingly moving towards reporting mean and standard deviation across multiple runs to provide a more reliable estimate of performance. This should be done for *every* method reported in the tables. This is especially important for the MNIST experiments.

5. **Learning-Theory Analysis:** The authors highlight the ability of highway networks to act as conditional identities. A learning-theory analysis exploring the advantages of this property (e.g., how it relates to the expressiveness of the network, the ability to avoid vanishing gradients, or the generalization capabilities) would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work. Consider discussing how these gates facilitate training in the regime where the network approaches an identity mapping.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
*   Convincing experimental results demonstrating the benefits of highway networks.
*   Insightful analysis of learned gate-output patterns and layer importance.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The incorrect statement regarding gradient calculation in Equation 5 is a significant flaw.
*   Lack of rigorous exploration of the initial bias hyperparameter.
*   Insufficient detail regarding experimental comparisons to prior work.
*   Limited theoretical analysis.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The core idea of highway networks is promising, and the paper presents compelling evidence of its effectiveness. However, the authors *must* address the issues related to gradient calculation and provide a more thorough analysis of the initialization bias. Addressing the other concerns (comparative claims, reporting statistics, and learning-theory analysis) would further strengthen the paper. Resolving the gradient calculation error is critical for the validity of the results.
