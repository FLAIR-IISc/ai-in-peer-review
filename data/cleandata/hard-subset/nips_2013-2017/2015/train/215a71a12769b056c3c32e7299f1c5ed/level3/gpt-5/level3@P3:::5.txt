Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that inserts adaptive, LSTM-inspired gates into each layer: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)). The transform gate T controls how much of the transformed signal H(x) passes versus how much of the input is carried forward. With a simple initialization that biases gates toward carry (negative gate bias), the authors show that extremely deep networks (hundreds of layers) can be trained directly with SGD. Empirically, the paper presents: (i) controlled optimization studies on thin fully connected networks of varying depth on MNIST showing that highway networks optimize well where plain networks stall; (ii) convolutional highway networks on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, including comparisons with FitNets, trained in a single stage without teacher hints; and (iii) analyses of gate statistics and lesioning experiments that illuminate learned routing and layer contributions (many layers “idle” on MNIST but are utilized on CIFAR-100). The work builds on a long line of ideas to ease credit assignment in deep nets: initialization schemes, local competition/maxout, skip connections, deep supervision, and distillation/hints. Its key novelty is to bring an LSTM-like gating trick to depth along the spatial/feature hierarchy, offering dynamic, input-dependent routing paths that include identity paths for unimpeded signal and gradient flow.

Strengths
- Originality: The LSTM-inspired gating applied to feedforward depth is simple and elegant. To my knowledge, this is the first to explicitly use such gating to successfully train very deep feedforward nets end-to-end with plain SGD.
- Quality: The optimization study is carefully controlled (matched parameter counts per layer), the training is stable across depths, and the analyses of gate activity and lesioning provide insight into what depth buys and when. Code release and clear training details increase credibility and reproducibility.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written, with a concise definition of the architecture, training strategy, and analysis. The figures (gate biases, activities, lesioning curves) are particularly informative.
- Significance: If adopted, the method lowers the barrier to training very deep networks and offers a principled alternative to fixed skip connections and teacher-student pipelines, likely to influence both research and practice.

Alternative perspective and questions
- “Not-so-deep” view: Modern “deep” architectures often treat a layer as a rich substructure (e.g., Inception modules or stacked 3×3 in VGG). Highway layers can similarly be viewed as micro-architectures comprising an identity subpath and a transform subpath with input-dependent mixing. It would help to discuss “effective depth” or typical path length through the network (fraction of open gates) to contextualize depth claims relative to Inception/VGG-style sublayers.
- Can highway networks be understood as an ensemble of shallow paths with input-dependent selection? Reporting statistics such as average T(x) per layer, per class, and estimated expected transform depth per example would sharpen this perspective.

Requested experiment
- Multitask sharing across depth: Train a single thin-and-tall convolutional highway network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR (shared trunk, forked heads). Compare (a) gate activation patterns per task/class versus those from separately trained single-task networks; (b) whether parameters/layers underutilized by MNIST are repurposed by CIFAR (e.g., via increased T activity and lesioning sensitivity for CIFAR while remaining idle for MNIST); and (c) the efficiency of parameter sharing along depth (e.g., performance vs. shared-depth tradeoff). This would directly test the hypothesis that highways enable flexible, input/task-dependent reuse of deep capacity.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scale of evaluation: Results on MNIST and CIFAR are encouraging but limited. An ImageNet-scale experiment (even with modest depth) would better assess training stability, accuracy, and computational overhead at scale.
- Baselines and ablations: A direct comparison to fixed skip/identity shortcuts with matched topology and parameter budgets would isolate the value of learned gating versus hard-wired residual paths. Ablate C = 1 − T versus independent carry/transform gates, and quantify the added parameter/computation overhead of T.
- Sensitivity: Report sensitivity to the transform-gate bias, activation functions, and width; and whether batch normalization or alternative normalizations interact beneficially or adversely with gates.
- Expressivity vs. sparsity: Many layers appear idle on MNIST. While desirable, it raises questions about the effective capacity used. Measuring effective path length, transform sparsity, and per-layer mutual information with targets could illuminate how capacity is deployed.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: Technically sound with well-supported claims. The optimization evidence is compelling, and the analysis sections are thoughtful. Some broader baselines and larger-scale tests would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: Well-organized and readable. Minor editorial issues (e.g., “varying varying” duplication) can be fixed easily. Consider adding computational cost breakdowns for gating.
- Originality: High. Adapting LSTM-style gating to feedforward depth is a novel and impactful idea distinct from prior fixed skip connections, hint training, or initialization heuristics.
- Significance: High. The approach is likely to be adopted and extended; the analysis tools (gate activity, lesioning) offer a useful lens on depth utilization.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, elegant mechanism that demonstrably enables training of very deep feedforward networks.
- Solid empirical optimization results and insightful analyses of routing and layer importance.
- Competitive CIFAR performance without teacher hints; faster convergence than plain nets.
- Clear exposition and released code.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited evaluation on large-scale datasets; unclear computational overhead vs. benefits at scale.
- Missing direct comparisons to fixed skip-connection baselines with matched capacity.
- Some design choices (C = 1 − T) and hyperparameter sensitivities not thoroughly ablated.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The LSTM-inspired gating insight is timely and important, the experiments and analyses are convincing, and the work will likely stimulate follow-up research. I encourage the authors to add the proposed multitask experiment, broaden baselines to include fixed skip connections, and include a preliminary ImageNet result to demonstrate scalability. Congratulations on being the first to successfully bring LSTM-like gating to the training of very deep feedforward networks.
