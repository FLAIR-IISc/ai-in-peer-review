This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate training of very deep feedforward networks. Inspired by the gating mechanisms in LSTMs, highway networks employ “transform” and “carry” gates to enable unimpeded information flow across many layers, addressing the vanishing gradient problem that plagues traditional deep networks. The core idea – allowing layers to adaptively choose between transforming input or directly passing it through – is presented clearly and elegantly. The work builds upon prior research exploring the benefits of depth in neural networks (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) and addresses challenges in training deep architectures through improved optimization [13, 14, 15] and initialization [16, 17]. The proposed gating mechanism resonates with existing techniques like skip connections [22, 23] and the concept of “hint-based” training [25], but offers a more flexible and dynamically adjustable approach.

The strengths of this paper lie in its simplicity and effectiveness. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that highway networks can be trained to significant depths (hundreds of layers) using standard SGD, a feat previously difficult to achieve with plain networks. The analyses presented (Figures 2 & 4) provide valuable insights into the inner workings of these networks, revealing how information is routed and which layers contribute most to the final result. The comparison to FitNets [25] and other state-of-the-art methods (Table 2 & 3) highlights the competitive performance of highway networks. 

However, the paper could benefit from further exploration of a crucial point: the relationship between highway networks and other existing deep architectures. The authors briefly acknowledge the similarity to LSTMs, but I wonder if the "highway" concept isn’t a generalization of techniques already present in more complex layers. For instance, very deep networks employing Inception modules [2] or stacked 3x3 convolutions [3] can be seen as having implicitly richer substructures within each layer, potentially mitigating the vanishing gradient problem. Similarly, can highway networks be viewed as a more explicit instantiation of the principles behind highway connections? 

I suggest an experiment to probe this connection. Train a single, relatively thin and tall network on both MNIST and CIFAR-10, “forking” the network architecture at the top layers to specialize for each dataset. Compare the learned inference patterns (e.g., transform gate activations) to those of separately trained networks on each dataset. This could reveal whether parameters underutilized by MNIST are repurposed by CIFAR, and how efficiently parameter sharing occurs across the deep hierarchy. 

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field. While further investigation into the broader context of deep network design would be beneficial, the core idea is novel, well-executed, and supported by solid experimental results. I recommend extending the experiments to larger datasets like ImageNet to further demonstrate the scalability of this approach.
