## Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces Highway Networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea, inspired by LSTM networks, is to incorporate adaptive gating mechanisms that allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers, addressing the vanishing gradient problem that plagues traditional deep networks. The authors demonstrate through experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 that highway networks can be trained effectively with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent, outperforming or matching the performance of existing methods, particularly in scenarios with limited computational resources. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and mechanics of highway networks. The connection to LSTM networks is effectively drawn, and the mathematical formulation is concise and understandable. The experimental results are fairly convincing, showcasing the ability of highway networks to train successfully at depths where traditional networks struggle. The analysis of learned gate outputs, visualizing information flow and layer importance, is a significant strength, providing valuable insights into the network’s behavior. This work builds upon previous efforts to improve deep network training, such as improved optimizers [13, 14, 15], initialization strategies [16, 17], and skip connections [22, 23, 24], offering a distinct and promising approach.

However, there are several points that require attention. Firstly, the paper states the sigmoid output of the gate unit *T* is never exactly 0 or 1, yet equation (5) implies a discrete Jacobian. The authors *must* clarify how backpropagation is implemented with this continuous gate output. Secondly, the constant negative bias initialization is somewhat unsatisfying. A plot of performance versus initial bias would be beneficial, as the bias acts as a regularizer favoring “longer highways”. Thirdly, comparative claims need clarification. The authors should explicitly state whether recent studies used the *exact* same experimental settings, allowing for direct comparison, or if there are differences. Finally, reporting only maximum accuracies is insufficient; mean and standard deviation should be provided for all methods to allow for a proper statistical assessment. 

I also suggest the authors consider including a learning-theory analysis of these gate units. The ability of these layers to act as conditional identities, enabling inputs to propagate through many selective layers, is a unique property that warrants further theoretical investigation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated architecture addressing a key challenge in deep learning.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating successful training of very deep networks.
*   Insightful analysis of learned gate outputs.
*   Clear and concise writing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential inaccuracy in the gradient calculation due to the sigmoid gate.
*   Lack of justification for the chosen bias initialization.
*   Ambiguity in comparative claims.
*   Insufficient reporting of experimental results (lack of standard deviation).
