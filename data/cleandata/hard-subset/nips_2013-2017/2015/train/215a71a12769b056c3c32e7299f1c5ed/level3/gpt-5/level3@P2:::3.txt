Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces LSTM-inspired gating (transform gate T and carry gate C with C=1−T) within each layer to regulate the flow of information. With T near zero, a layer behaves like an identity mapping; with T near one, it applies its standard transform H. By initializing the transform gates with negative biases, the network is initially biased toward carrying information forward, allowing gradients and activations to propagate across depth. The authors show that very deep (50–100+ layer) networks can be trained directly with SGD, without special multi-stage training or auxiliary losses. Experiments compare highway networks to plain networks with variance-preserving initialization, demonstrate competitive results on MNIST/CIFAR-10/100 with relatively thin models, and provide insightful analyses of information routing and layer importance via gating activity and lesioning.

This work is well-grounded in, and clearly differentiated from, prior literature: gating and credit assignment in LSTM [29,30]; initialization strategies and rectifiers [16,17]; locally competitive units and maxout [20,21]; skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision [22–24]; and hint-based training (FitNets) [25]. Unlike fixed skip connections or deep supervision, highway networks learn input-dependent routing through multiplicative gates, and unlike FitNets, they train deep networks end-to-end in a single stage without a teacher.

Quality:
- Empirical support is strong for the optimization claims: controlled comparisons on MNIST across depths show that plain nets degrade rapidly while highway nets maintain performance and converge faster; pilots on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 indicate competitive generalization with modest capacity; and the analysis of gate activity, data-dependent routing, and lesioning is particularly compelling and goes beyond typical benchmarking.
- The training protocol is carefully described (random search over hyperparameters, multiple runs with mean±std, code released), enhancing reproducibility.
- Some comparisons are missing that would further strengthen the case: (i) identity/orthogonal-initialized deep plain nets with small noise or identity “bypass” layers (e.g., linear layers initialized to identity) to disentangle the effect of gating versus near-identity initialization [18,19,22]; and (ii) fixed skip/shortcut connections as a baseline distinct from learned gates.
- Additional ablations would be helpful: effect of the transform-gate bias choice; freeing C rather than tying C=1−T; computational and parameter overhead of the gates; and gradient-norm/activation-statistics across depth to directly substantiate “unimpeded” flow.
- The study does not include large-scale datasets, but the focus is on optimization behavior rather than absolute state of the art.

Clarity:
- The paper is clear, well-organized, and a pleasure to read. Figures effectively illuminate gating behavior and routing.
- Specific correction: In Section 4.1, the sentence “Especially for the CIFAR-100 case, most transform gates are active on average” appears inconsistent with the reported negative biases and sparse per-sample activity; it should read “most transform blocks are not active on average.”
- Minor suggestions: quantify the parameter/computation overhead of the gates; explicitly describe how dimensionality changes are handled (plain layers vs. padding) in all experiments; and clarify the practical impact of tying C=1−T versus learning a separate carry gate.

Originality:
- Introducing LSTM-style gating into deep feedforward networks to create learnable, input-dependent information highways is novel and elegant. It differs from prior skip connections by making routing conditional and learnable, and from previous initialization-only approaches by building the identity path into the architecture.

Significance:
- The ability to reliably train very deep networks with vanilla SGD is important. The method is simple, broadly applicable (fully connected and convolutional layers), and likely to be adopted and extended by the community. The analyses (routing and lesioning) provide rare insight into how very deep models utilize depth.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, original architectural idea that is easy to implement.
- Strong evidence that optimization remains stable as depth increases; converges faster than plain nets.
- Competitive results on standard benchmarks with thin/deep models and single-stage training; surpasses FitNet-like setups without hints in comparable regimes.
- Insightful analysis of gate activity, data-dependent routing, and layer importance; code availability aids reproducibility.
- High-quality writing and presentation.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to identity-initialized deep plain nets and to fixed skip/shortcut connections under matched capacity/compute.
- Limited ablations on gate design (C free vs. 1−T), gate bias sensitivity, and computational overhead.
- No experiments on larger-scale datasets; state-of-the-art claims are not the focus.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines with identity/orthogonal initialization plus small noise and with fixed identity bypass paths to delineate gating benefits beyond near-identity initialization [18,19,22].
- Report gradient norms and activation statistics across depth to directly support the “unimpeded flow” claim.
- Provide ablations on the transform-gate bias and on learning a separate carry gate C; quantify parameter and FLOP overhead of gates.
- Correct Section 4.1 as noted above; add more precise descriptions of the gate activity statistics shown in Figure 2.

Overall assessment:
An excellent, original paper that addresses a central obstacle in training very deep networks with a simple, effective idea, supported by careful experiments and insightful analysis. I strongly recommend acceptance.
