This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. The core idea, inspired by LSTM gating mechanisms, is to allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers via “information highways” – adaptive gating units that regulate information passage. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained directly using standard stochastic gradient descent to depths previously inaccessible to plain networks, and achieve competitive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

This work builds upon a significant body of research addressing the challenges of training deep neural networks. The paper correctly identifies the difficulties arising from vanishing gradients and poor propagation of activations in very deep architectures, referencing prior work on improved optimizers (e.g., [13, 14, 15]), initialization strategies ([16, 17, 18, 19]), activation functions ([20, 21]), and skip connections ([22, 23, 24]). The connection to “distillation” techniques ([25, 26]) and layer-wise training ([26, 27]) provides useful context. The explicit link to LSTM networks ([28, 29, 30]) is well-motivated, drawing a clear parallel between the gating mechanisms used for handling long-term dependencies in recurrent networks and the proposed highway architecture.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The LSTM-inspired gating trick is a simple yet remarkably effective idea, enabling the training of exceptionally deep feedforward networks. The experimental validation, particularly the demonstration that highway networks outperform plain networks at increasing depths (Section 3.1), is compelling. The analyses presented in Sections 4.1 and 4.2, investigating information routing and layer importance, offer valuable insights into the inner workings of these networks. The results on CIFAR-10/100 (Section 3.3) demonstrate that the method generalizes beyond MNIST and is competitive with state-of-the-art approaches. The code availability is commendable and enhances reproducibility. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses, though not major. While the comparison to FitNets [25] is relevant, a more comprehensive comparison to other recent advances in deep network training, like residual networks [36] (which weren’t available at NIPS 2015), would strengthen the analysis. The authors mention the potential for unused layers, but a more detailed investigation into strategies for pruning or regularizing these layers could be beneficial. Additionally, the analysis of information routing (Figure 3) is intriguing but somewhat limited; quantifying the difference in activity patterns between classes would provide a more rigorous assessment.

I find myself wondering if the "depth" achieved by highway networks is somewhat illusory. Many successful deep learning architectures, such as Inception [37] and the VGG networks utilizing stacked 3x3 convolutions, achieve effective depth through internal substructures and complex nonlinearities within each layer. In this light, highway networks might be more accurately viewed as layers composed of richer, more complex substructures – similar to how one might interpret the "highways" themselves. It would be fascinating to explore this perspective further.

I propose an experiment to test a hypothesis related to this idea. Train one "thin-and-tall" network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR-10, forking the network into two separate classification heads at the top layer – one for each dataset. Compare the resulting inference patterns (e.g., activation maps, transform gate activations) to those obtained by training two separate networks (one on MNIST, one on CIFAR-10) with identical architectures. If the parameters underutilized by MNIST are effectively repurposed by CIFAR-10, and if the parameter sharing across the deep hierarchy is efficient, we might see similar inference patterns in the combined network as in the separately trained networks. This would suggest a degree of representational flexibility and parameter efficiency.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating improved performance compared to plain networks.
*   Insightful analysis of information routing and layer importance.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.
*   Code availability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Comparison to other recent deep network architectures could be more comprehensive.
*   Analysis of information routing could be more rigorous.
*   The claim of “depth” could be further nuanced in relation to existing architectures with complex internal structures.

**Overall:**

Despite these minor weaknesses, I believe this is a strong and significant contribution to the field. The highway network architecture represents a clever and effective solution to a fundamental problem in deep learning – training very deep networks. I recommend acceptance, and encourage the authors to consider testing the architecture on larger datasets like ImageNet as suggested in the paper and further exploring the nature of the "depth" it achieves.
