This paper introduces highway networks, a novel feedforward architecture designed to ease the training of networks with extreme depth. The core idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to introduce learnable gates that regulate information flow. This allows gradients to propagate unimpeded through many layers, overcoming the optimization challenges that plague traditional 'plain' deep networks. The authors effectively position this work as an elegant solution to a well-known problem, contrasting it with previous approaches like careful initialization or fixed skip connections.

The paper's primary strength is its originality and simplicity. The authors deserve congratulations for being the first to successfully adapt an LSTM-like gating mechanism to train very deep feedforward networks. The idea is elegant, the implementation is straightforward, and the results are compelling. The work is of high quality, providing clear evidence that highway networks do not suffer from performance degradation with increased depth. The analyses of gate activations and layer lesioning are thorough and provide valuable insights into the network's behavior.

I would like to propose an alternative perspective. Recent work has shown that very deep networks may be functionally 'not-so-deep' if individual layers contain richer substructures, such as VGG's stacked convolutions or Inception modules. Can highway networks be viewed through a similar lens—as a sequence of layers with a more powerful, gated computational block, rather than simply a mechanism for skipping layers? This framing might offer new insights.

To further explore the dynamic routing capabilities, I suggest an experiment: train a single, thin-and-tall highway network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR, with a shared trunk that forks near the output. It would be fascinating to see if the learned gating patterns for each task's inputs resemble those from the separately trained networks. This could reveal how efficiently parameters underutilized by a simple task (MNIST) are repurposed for a more complex one (CIFAR) within a deep hierarchy.

In summary, this is an excellent paper with no major weaknesses. The central idea is a significant contribution, enabling the direct training of previously intractable architectures. The work is technically sound and clearly presented. My only recommendation is to validate the approach on a larger benchmark like ImageNet to fully demonstrate its potential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
