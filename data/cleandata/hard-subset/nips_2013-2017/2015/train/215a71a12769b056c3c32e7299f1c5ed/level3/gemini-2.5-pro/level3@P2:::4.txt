This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. The core problem addressed is that as traditional "plain" networks become deeper, they become increasingly difficult to train with gradient-based methods due to issues with information and gradient propagation. The proposed solution is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. Highway networks introduce adaptive gates at each layer that regulate the flow of information, dynamically choosing between transforming the input and carrying it through to the next layer. This creates "information highways" that allow gradients to flow unimpeded across many layers. This work builds upon a long history of using skip connections to ease training but offers a significant generalization by making these pathways dynamic and data-dependent, much like the cell state updates in LSTMs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a central and highly important problem in deep learning: how to effectively train networks with a large number of layers. The ability to train networks with hundreds or even thousands of layers directly with stochastic gradient descent is a major breakthrough. This work unlocks the potential to explore architectural depth in a way that was previously impractical, significantly advancing the state of the art in network design and optimization. The ideas presented are likely to be widely used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.

2.  **Originality:** The concept of using adaptive, LSTM-inspired gating to control information flow in a feedforward architecture is highly novel and elegant. While skip connections are not new, the introduction of a learned, data-dependent gating mechanism that can smoothly interpolate between transforming and carrying information is a clever and powerful generalization. This provides a principled framework for managing information flow through depth, which is a more flexible approach than fixed, hard-wired shortcuts.

3.  **Quality:** The technical claims are well-supported by a solid and convincing experimental evaluation.
    *   The optimization experiments on MNIST (Section 3.1) provide a clear and compelling demonstration that highway networks do not suffer from the degradation in performance with increasing depth that plagues plain networks.
    *   The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this ease of training translates to strong generalization performance on more complex tasks. The ability to train deep, thin networks that outperform the wider, shallower "FitNets" (which required a complex two-stage training procedure) in a single stage is a particularly strong result.
    *   The analysis in Section 4, including the visualization of gate activities and the lesion study, provides valuable initial insights into the learned behavior of these networks, demonstrating that they can learn data-dependent routing and that deeper layers are indeed utilized for more complex problems.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The core idea is presented with clear motivation, simple notation (Equation 3), and an intuitive explanation. The experiments are well-designed and the results are presented effectively, making the paper's main messages clear and impactful.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary area for potential improvement is in the depth of the analysis provided in Section 4. While the analysis is insightful and supports the paper's claims, it could be made more comprehensive.
*   The visualizations of transform gate activity for a single random sample (Figure 2) are illustrative, but it would be beneficial to see how this behavior varies across different samples or to see statistics aggregated over the entire test set.
*   The lesion study (Figure 4) is an excellent way to probe layer importance. This analysis could be extended to investigate layer redundancy, for instance, by studying the effect of lesioning pairs or small groups of layers.
*   It would also be interesting to see an analysis of how the gating behavior evolves during training. Does the network initially rely heavily on the carry connections and then gradually open the transform gates as it learns? Visualizing the evolution of the gate biases could shed light on this.
These are suggestions for strengthening what is already a very strong paper, rather than pointing out a fundamental flaw.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel, significant, and practical solution to the important problem of training very deep networks.
*   **Pro:** The proposed highway network architecture is an elegant and principled generalization of existing ideas.
*   **Pro:** The claims are backed by strong, well-executed experiments that clearly demonstrate the method's effectiveness.
*   **Pro:** The work opens up new possibilities for research into deep architectures and is likely to have a high impact on the field.
*   **Con:** The analysis section, while good, could be more thorough to provide even deeper insights into the network's learned behavior.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear, novel, and significant contribution to the field. The proposed highway network architecture is a powerful tool that effectively addresses a major obstacle in deep learning. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. This work is of high quality and will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
