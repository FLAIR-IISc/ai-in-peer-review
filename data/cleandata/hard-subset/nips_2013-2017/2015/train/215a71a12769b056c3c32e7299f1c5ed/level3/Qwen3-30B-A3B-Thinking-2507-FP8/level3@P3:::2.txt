

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture enabling direct training of extremely deep feed-forward networks via adaptive input-dependent gates. The authors draw inspiration from LSTMs but propose a distinct mechanism: each layer computes an output as $y = H(x, W_H) \cdot T(x, W_T) + x \cdot (1 - T(x, W_T))$, where $T$ is a transform gate (initialized with negative bias to favor information "carrying" initially). This design creates dynamic "information highways" that allow gradients and activations to flow unimpeded across layers, contrasting with static skip connections (e.g., ResNets) and teacher-student distillation (FitNets). The work directly addresses a key bottleneck in deep learning—training very deep networks—by providing a simple, architecture-based solution rather than relying on complex initialization or multi-stage training.

**Quality** is exceptionally strong. The paper delivers a complete technical contribution: a clear mathematical formulation (Section 2), rigorous empirical validation (Section 3), and insightful analysis (Section 4). The optimization experiments (Figure 1) conclusively demonstrate that highway networks train robustly up to 100+ layers, while plain networks fail catastrophically beyond 20 layers on MNIST. The analysis of gate behavior (Figures 2–4) is particularly compelling: gates dynamically route information per input (Figure 3), layers become selectively active depending on class (Figure 4), and deeper networks *utilize* all layers for complex tasks (CIFAR-100), unlike shallow networks. The authors thoroughly address limitations (e.g., layer lesioning shows highway networks avoid "unused layers" in complex problems) and provide reproducible code. The empirical results on CIFAR-10/100 (Table 3) match or exceed state-of-the-art with single-stage training, contrasting sharply with FitNets’ need for two-stage distillation (Section 3.3.1).

**Clarity** is exemplary. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with intuitive notation (e.g., $T$ and $C$ gates), concise explanations, and effective figures. The highway concept is motivated clearly via LSTM inspiration (Section 1) and distinguished from prior work: the authors explicitly acknowledge FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) and LSTMs but clarify key differences (highway gates are input-dependent and adaptive, unlike FitNets’ static soft targets; highways are feed-forward, unlike LSTMs’ recurrent structure). The experimental setup is transparent, with hyperparameters tuned on CIFAR-10 and fixed across tasks. Minor editorial note: "Tables 3 and 3" (p.5) should be corrected to "Tables 2 and 3."

**Originality** is high. While inspired by LSTMs and skip connections, the highway architecture is a novel *combination* of adaptive gating with feed-forward networks. Crucially, it avoids the limitations of prior work: it does not require careful initialization (unlike normalized initialization for plain nets), eliminates the need for teacher networks (unlike FitNets), and enables direct training of deeper architectures than previously feasible. The analysis of input-dependent gate routing (Section 4.1) and layer importance (Section 4.2) provides unique insights into deep network behavior, distinguishing this from purely empirical "deep is better" approaches.

**Significance** is substantial. The paper resolves a fundamental barrier to deeper networks, enabling the study of architectures with hundreds of layers—previously infeasible. This advances the field by: (1) demonstrating that depth *can* be scaled without sacrificing ease of training (a major step beyond ResNets’ static skips); (2) providing a framework to analyze *how* deep networks compute (via gate activity); and (3) achieving competitive results on CIFAR with single-stage training, avoiding FitNets’ cumbersome distillation. The methodology is broadly applicable beyond vision (e.g., to recurrent or convolutional networks), and the code availability accelerates adoption.

**Strengths vs. Weaknesses**:  
*Pro*: Novel architecture; rigorous depth experiments (100+ layers); insightful gate analysis; clear differentiation from FitNets/LSTMs; strong empirical results; excellent writing.  
*Con*: Minor typo (Tables 3 and 3); no discussion of computational cost (though the paper focuses on training feasibility, not efficiency).  

This paper is a significant, high-quality contribution to deep learning. It provides a simple yet powerful solution to a long-standing problem, backed by thorough analysis and experiments. The clarity and completeness make it immediately useful to researchers. The minor typo does not detract from the work’s merits. **I recommend acceptance.**
