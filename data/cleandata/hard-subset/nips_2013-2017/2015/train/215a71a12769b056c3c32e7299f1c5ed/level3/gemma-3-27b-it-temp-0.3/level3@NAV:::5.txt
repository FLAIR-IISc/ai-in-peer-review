This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. Inspired by the gating mechanisms of LSTMs, highway networks employ “transform” and “carry” gates to allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers, mitigating the vanishing gradient problem that plagues traditional deep networks. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained effectively with standard stochastic gradient descent, even with hundreds of layers, and achieve competitive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets.

The work builds upon a rich history of attempts to improve deep network training, referencing techniques like better optimizers, initialization strategies, skip connections (e.g., FitNets), and the use of soft targets. The core contribution lies in the elegant application of an LSTM-like gating mechanism to the feedforward setting, offering a simple yet effective solution to the depth problem. The analysis of gate behavior and layer importance provides valuable insights into how these networks function.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The idea is conceptually simple, well-motivated, and empirically supported. The experiments are thorough, demonstrating the ability to train significantly deeper networks than previously possible with standard techniques. The ablation studies and visualizations (Figures 2 & 4) offer a compelling understanding of the learned representations and information flow. The clarity of writing is also commendable. 

However, I wonder if the notion of “depth” is somewhat relative here. Modern architectures like Inception and VGG, while deep in terms of layer count, often contain sub-layers or complex nonlinearities (e.g., stacked 3x3 convolutions) within each layer. In this light, a 100-layer highway network might not be “so deep” if each highway layer is relatively simple. Similarly, highway networks share some conceptual similarities with earlier work on highway connections, and it would be interesting to consider if they can be viewed as layers composed of richer substructures. 

I propose an experiment to explore this further: train one thin-and-tall network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR-10, forking the network at the top into separate classification heads. This would allow a comparison of inference patterns and parameter utilization between networks trained independently on each dataset. Specifically, it would be insightful to see if parameters underutilized by MNIST are repurposed by CIFAR, and how efficiently parameter sharing occurs across the deep hierarchy.

Despite this suggestion, the paper is a strong contribution. I recommend testing the approach on larger datasets like ImageNet to further validate its scalability. Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound, and original work with significant potential impact.
