This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, called highway networks, designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep networks. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and propose an adaptive gating mechanism that allows for unimpeded information flow across many layers. This mechanism enables the training of extremely deep networks using simple gradient descent.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of their proposed architecture, including a detailed description of the highway network's components and its training procedure. They also present experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep networks, including a comparison to plain networks and state-of-the-art methods on several benchmark datasets.

One of the key contributions of this paper is the introduction of the adaptive gating mechanism, which allows highway networks to dynamically adjust the routing of information based on the current input. This mechanism is shown to be effective in training very deep networks, and the authors provide an analysis of its behavior on several datasets.

The authors also discuss the relationship between highway networks and other deep network architectures, such as Inception and VGG. They propose an alternative perspective on very deep networks, suggesting that they may be viewed as "not-so-deep" when layers contain sub-layers or more complex nonlinearities.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their proposed architecture and its benefits. The experimental results are thorough and well-presented, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of their findings.
