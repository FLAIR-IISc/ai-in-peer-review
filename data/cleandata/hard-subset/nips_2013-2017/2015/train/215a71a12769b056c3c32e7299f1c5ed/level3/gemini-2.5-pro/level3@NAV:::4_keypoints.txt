- Addresses the problem of training very deep networks where performance degrades due to poor gradient propagation.  
- Proposes "highway networks" with adaptive gating units (inspired by LSTMs) to regulate information flow.  
- Gates create "information highways" that allow gradients and information to travel unimpeded across many layers.  
- Extends skip-connection ideas and improved initialization by providing flexible, data-dependent control rather than fixed shortcuts.  
- Quality: technically sound with convincing experiments; MNIST results show no performance degradation up to 100 layers.  
- Practical utility: CIFAR-10/100 experiments enable single-stage training of deep, thin networks that previously required multi-stage procedures.  
- Originality: novel learned-gating mechanism for cross-layer connections, an elegant generalization of existing architectures.  
- Significance: offers a practical solution to a major deep-learning bottleneck, likely to be widely adopted and to inspire future work.  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written and clearly motivated, with easy-to-follow experimental setup and results.  
- Weakness: analysis is limited—would benefit from more diverse visualizations of gate activities across different classes or tasks.
