This paper introduces a novel architecture, called highway networks, designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep neural networks. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and propose the use of adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow across layers. This approach enables the training of extremely deep networks using simple gradient descent, allowing for the study of efficient architectures with hundreds of layers.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of the related work in the field. They discuss the importance of network depth in achieving state-of-the-art results in various machine learning tasks and highlight the challenges associated with training deep networks, such as the vanishing gradient problem.

The authors propose a new architecture, highway networks, which consists of multiple blocks with adaptive gating units. These units allow for the regulation of information flow across layers, enabling the training of very deep networks. The authors provide a detailed description of the architecture and the training procedure, including the initialization of the transform gate biases.

The experimental results presented in the paper are impressive, demonstrating the ability to train highway networks with up to 100 layers. The authors show that these networks can achieve state-of-the-art results on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. They also provide an analysis of the inner workings of the highway networks, including the routing of information and the importance of individual layers.

The strengths of the paper include the originality of the idea, the quality of the writing, and the compelling experimental results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work and clearly explain the contributions of their paper. The experiments on information routing and layer importance are particularly interesting, providing insights into the behavior of the highway networks.

One minor correction is suggested: in Section 4.1, the text should read "not active on average" instead of "most transform blocks are active on average." Additionally, it would be interesting to see a comparison with identity-initialized layers (with noise) and an explanation of how the transform gates differ from this simple scheme.

Overall, this is a great paper that presents a novel idea and demonstrates its effectiveness through compelling experimental results. The writing is clear and concise, making it a pleasure to read.
