

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks through adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. The authors propose a transformation where each layer's output is a weighted combination of a transformed input and the original input, controlled by a sigmoidal transform gate. This allows information to flow through "information highways" across many layers without attenuation, addressing the training difficulties of very deep networks.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results showing highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, unlike plain networks which degrade significantly. On MNIST, 100-layer highway networks outperform similarly-sized plain networks by over two orders of magnitude. On CIFAR-10, highway networks achieve comparable or better results than Fitnets (which require two-stage training) while being trained in a single stage. The analysis of gate outputs (Figure 2) provides compelling evidence of information routing mechanisms, showing how different layers become active for different inputs, and the layer importance analysis (Figure 4) demonstrates that highway networks can effectively utilize all layers for complex tasks like CIFAR-100.

The paper's main strengths include its clear description of the highway network architecture, convincing experimental results across multiple datasets, and insightful analysis of how the gating mechanism enables information flow. The comparison to previous work (particularly Fitnets and plain networks) is well-motivated, and the paper successfully demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD without the need for two-stage training procedures.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. First, Equation 5 claims the Jacobian of the highway layer is either the identity matrix or the Jacobian of H depending on the gate value, but since the sigmoidal gate never outputs exactly 0 or 1, this gradient calculation is technically incorrect. The authors must clarify how backpropagation is actually implemented in practice, as this is critical for understanding the optimization dynamics.

Second, the initialization strategy using constant negative biases for transform gates (e.g., -1, -2, -3 for different depths) is unsatisfying. The authors should provide a plot showing performance as a function of initial bias value, as this bias acts as a regularizer favoring "longer highways" and should be properly characterized. A more principled initialization scheme would strengthen the paper.

Third, the paper makes comparative claims about recent work (particularly Fitnets) without fully clarifying the experimental settings. The authors must explicitly state whether their experiments use the same settings as previous work or explain the differences, as this affects the validity of the comparisons.

Fourth, the reporting practices need improvement. The paper reports only maximum accuracies, but the authors should provide mean and standard deviation for all methods to better assess the variability of results across random initializations.

Finally, while not required, a learning-theory analysis of the gate units would be valuable. The paper could explore how the adaptive gating mechanism allows layers to act as conditional identities, enabling inputs to propagate through many selective layers while maintaining the ability to learn useful transformations.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by enabling direct training of extremely deep networks, which has been a persistent challenge in deep learning. The highway network architecture represents a novel and effective approach to addressing the depth-related training difficulties that have plagued deep learning. The empirical results are convincing, and the analysis of learned gate patterns provides valuable insights into how information flows through these networks.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong connection to previous work on skip connections and LSTM networks. The main technical contribution—adaptive gating for information flow—represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches to training deep networks. While the paper has some technical issues that need addressing (particularly regarding gradient calculation and initialization), the core idea is sound and the experimental results are compelling.

In terms of significance, highway networks enable the study of extremely deep architectures that were previously intractable to train directly. This opens up new possibilities for exploring the benefits of depth in neural network architectures without the need for complex training procedures. The paper's insights into information routing and layer importance also provide valuable theoretical understanding that can inform future deep learning research.

The paper meets all four key criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with strong experimental results, original in its approach to information flow in deep networks, and significant for enabling direct training of very deep networks. The minor weaknesses noted above are fixable and do not detract from the paper's core contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the technical points raised.
