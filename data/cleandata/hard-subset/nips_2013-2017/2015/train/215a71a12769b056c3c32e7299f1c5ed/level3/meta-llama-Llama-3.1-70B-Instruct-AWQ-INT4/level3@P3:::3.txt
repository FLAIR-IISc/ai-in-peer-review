This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture, called highway networks, designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep networks. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and introduce an adaptive gating mechanism that allows for unimpeded information flow across many layers. This enables the training of extremely deep networks using simple gradient descent.

The paper is well-written, and the idea is original and interesting. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problems associated with training deep networks and how their proposed architecture addresses these issues. The experiments on information routing and layer importance are particularly compelling, demonstrating the ability of highway networks to dynamically adjust the routing of information based on the current input and to utilize all of their layers for complex problems.

The authors provide a comprehensive review of previous work on deep neural networks, including the successes and limitations of various approaches. They also discuss the theoretical and empirical evidence indicating that the depth of neural networks is crucial for their success.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a new architecture that allows for the training of extremely deep networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through a series of experiments on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. They show that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent, in contrast to plain networks which become hard to optimize as depth increases.

The authors also provide an analysis of the inner workings of their proposed architecture, including the behavior of the transform gates and the block outputs. They demonstrate that the network can learn to dynamically adjust the routing of information based on the current input and that the gating system acts not just as a mechanism to ease training but also as an important part of the computation in a trained network.

The paper has several strengths, including its original idea, well-written text, and compelling experiments. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problems associated with training deep networks and demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed architecture. The paper is also well-organized, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of comparison to other approaches that use identity-initialized layers with noise. The authors could benefit from discussing how their proposed architecture differs from these approaches and providing experimental results that compare the performance of highway networks to these alternative methods.

Another potential weakness is the need for a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed architecture. The authors could benefit from providing a more detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of their approach and comparing it to other approaches.

Overall, this is a great paper that proposes a novel neural network architecture designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep networks. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problems associated with training deep networks and demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed architecture through a series of compelling experiments.
