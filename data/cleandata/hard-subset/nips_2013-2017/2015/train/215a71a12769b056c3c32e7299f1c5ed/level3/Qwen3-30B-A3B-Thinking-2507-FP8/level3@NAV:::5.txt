

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture that enables direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks using simple gradient descent. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, introducing adaptive gating mechanisms that allow information to flow unimpeded across many layers through "information highways." This approach fundamentally differs from previous work on skip connections [22, 23] and two-stage training methods [25], as it enables single-stage training of networks with hundreds of layers without requiring specialized initialization schemes.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 datasets. The highway networks consistently outperform plain networks of similar depth, with 100-layer highway networks achieving results more than two orders of magnitude better than comparable plain networks. The analyses are particularly compelling, including the insightful layer importance study showing how highway networks dynamically utilize different layers for different inputs, and the observation that for complex tasks like CIFAR-100, nearly all layers contribute meaningfully to performance.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured explanations of the highway architecture and effective visualization of information routing. The originality is significant - while skip connections have been explored before, the authors' LSTM-inspired gating mechanism represents a novel application that fundamentally changes how we think about deep network training. The perspective that "very deep networks may be not-so-deep" when layers contain richer substructures offers a valuable conceptual shift beyond previous work on Inception modules and stacked 3x3 convolutions.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it opens the door to systematically studying extremely deep architectures without the training difficulties that have previously limited exploration. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that simpler datasets like MNIST may not fully utilize all layers, while more complex tasks benefit from the full depth.

The only suggestion for improvement would be to address the proposed experiment of jointly training on MNIST and CIFAR to examine parameter sharing efficiency, as this could further illuminate how highway networks dynamically allocate resources across different tasks. Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed contribution that advances the state of the art in deep learning architecture design. I recommend acceptance.
