- Problem: addresses training very deep feedforward networks and mitigating vanishing gradients.  
- Contribution: introduces "highway networks," an architecture with adaptive gating inspired by LSTM.  
- Mechanism: each layer has transform and carry gates to dynamically route information and enable gradient flow across hundreds of layers.  
- MNIST result: plain networks degrade past ~20 layers, while highway networks train reliably to ~100 layers with better optimization.  
- CIFAR result: matches or exceeds FitNets using single-stage SGD, avoiding complex two-stage training.  
- Analysis: layer lesioning and gate-activity visualizations show data-dependent routing and genuine depth utilization.  
- Quality: technically sound with strong empirical and analytical support for claims.  
- Clarity: clearly written, well-organized, with an intuitive connection to LSTMs and effective figures/tables.  
- Originality: novel application of LSTM-like dynamic gating to feedforward depth; dynamic skip connections are a conceptual advance.  
- Significance: high impact potential by enabling exploration of extreme depths and informing questions about model capacity.  
- Strength: elegant, simple, and effective architectural solution to a critical training problem.  
- Strength: thorough empirical validation demonstrating optimization and practical benefits.  
- Strength: insightful internal analyses that go beyond reporting performance metrics.  
- Strength: opens avenues for future research on deep architectures and efficient depth use.  
- Alternative view: highway layers could be seen as richer computational blocks rather than merely increasing depth.  
- Suggested experiment: jointly train a thin-and-tall highway network on MNIST and CIFAR-100 to study routing and parameter reuse.  
- Suggested future work: test scalability and benefits on large-scale datasets like ImageNet.  
- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
