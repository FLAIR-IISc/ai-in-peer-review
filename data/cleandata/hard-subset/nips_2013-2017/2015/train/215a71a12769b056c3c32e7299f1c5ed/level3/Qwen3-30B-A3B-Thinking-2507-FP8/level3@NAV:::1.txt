

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep networks through adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTM units. The authors present theoretical motivation showing highway networks can maintain information flow across many layers, and provide empirical evidence that they can be trained directly with SGD even for very deep architectures (up to 100+ layers), in contrast to traditional "plain" networks which become increasingly difficult to train as depth increases. The work builds on previous research on deep networks, skip connections, and LSTM units, but offers a novel approach to information flow that enables direct training of very deep architectures.

The paper's main strengths include a clear description of the highway network architecture, convincing experimental results demonstrating superior performance of highway networks compared to plain networks at extreme depths, and particularly strong analysis of the learned gate-output patterns (Section 4). The authors demonstrate that highway networks can effectively learn to route information based on input data, and provide compelling evidence through lesioning experiments that the networks do utilize multiple layers for complex tasks.

However, several critical points need addressing. First, the gate unit T uses a sigmoid, so outputs are never exactly 0/1, yet Equation 5 claims gradients for the case when T=0 or T=1. The authors must clarify how backpropagation is implemented in practice. Second, the constant negative bias initialization scheme (e.g., -1, -3) is unsatisfying; the authors should provide a plot showing performance as a function of initial bias value, as this parameter acts as a regularizer favoring "longer highways." Third, comparative claims need clarification - the authors should explicitly state whether they're using the same experimental settings as recent studies for direct comparison. Finally, the paper reports only maximum accuracies; the authors should provide mean and standard deviation for all methods to properly assess statistical significance.

The suggestion to include a learning-theory analysis of the gate units would further strengthen the paper, as it would provide theoretical grounding for how the gate units allow layers to act as conditional identities.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with significant potential impact on deep learning research. With the suggested revisions, the paper would be suitable for publication at NIPS.
