## Review of Training Very Deep Networks

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. The core idea, inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to introduce “information highways” allowing information to flow across many layers without attenuation via adaptive gating mechanisms. The paper provides compelling experimental results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrating that these networks can be trained effectively with standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD), outperforming or matching the performance of existing methods, especially as depth increases.  The thorough analysis of the learned gate outputs is a particularly strong point.

The motivation for this work is well-established. The authors accurately summarize the recent successes achieved through increased network depth in areas like image classification and the challenges associated with training these deeper architectures, citing relevant work like [1, 2, 3] and the difficulties described in [16, 17].  The paper appropriately positions highway networks within the context of previous attempts to address these challenges, including better optimizers [13, 14, 15], initialization strategies [16, 17, 18, 19], alternative activation functions [20, 21], and skip connections [22, 23, 24, 25, 26]. The connection to LSTM networks [29, 30] is also clearly explained.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network architecture presents a conceptually simple, yet effective approach to address the vanishing gradient problem in deep networks. The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism is a clever adaptation for feedforward architectures.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is strong. The authors demonstrate the ability to train significantly deeper networks than previously possible with standard techniques, achieving competitive results on benchmark datasets. The comparison with Fitnets [25] is particularly insightful.
*   **Analysis:** The analysis of the learned gate biases and activity patterns (Figure 2 and 4) is excellent and provides valuable insights into how the highway networks function.  The observation regarding layer importance and the dynamic routing of information is compelling.
*   **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly, with a good balance between mathematical notation and intuitive explanations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Mathematical Error:** The claim regarding the Jacobian in Equation 5 is incorrect. The transform gate *T* outputs a value between 0 and 1 due to the sigmoid function; therefore, the Jacobian can never exactly equal *I* or *H’(x,WH)*. The authors must clarify how backpropagation is implemented with this continuous gate value and correct the equation.
*   **Initialization Strategy:** While the use of negative biases for the transform gates is effective, the justification for choosing specific values based on depth is somewhat ad-hoc. The paper would benefit from a more systematic investigation of this hyperparameter. A plot showing performance versus initial bias value, highlighting the sensitivity of the network to this parameter, would be valuable, as the bias acts as a regularizer favoring "longer highways”.
*   **Comparative Claims:**  The paper makes comparisons to other methods (e.g., Fitnets).  However, it isn't always clear whether these comparisons are conducted under identical experimental conditions. The authors need to explicitly state whether previous studies used the same datasets, hyperparameters, and training procedures, or if the comparison is based on replicating results. This clarification is critical for assessing the significance of the improvements.
*   **Reporting:** The reporting of results is inconsistent. While some experiments include mean and standard deviation, others only report the best accuracy. Providing the mean and standard deviation for *all* methods used in the experiments is crucial for a fair assessment of performance and reproducibility.
*   **Theoretical Considerations:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis.  Exploring the theoretical implications of these gate units, specifically how they allow layers to act as conditional identities and enable inputs to propagate through many selective layers, would strengthen the contribution. A brief learning-theory discussion of this aspect would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The analysis of learned gate behavior provides valuable insights into the functioning of these networks.
*   The code and hyperparameters are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical error regarding the Jacobian (Eq. 5) needs to be corrected.
*   The explanation for the initial bias selection is somewhat unsatisfactory and lacks a thorough investigation.
*   The clarity regarding experimental comparisons needs improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the identified weaknesses, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed highway networks provide a practical and effective solution to the challenges of training very deep networks, and the accompanying analysis offers valuable insights.  Addressing the mathematical error and clarifying the experimental comparisons are crucial. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address these points.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Correct the mathematical error in Equation 5 and explain the backpropagation implementation.
2.  Provide a more thorough investigation of the initial bias parameter, including a plot of performance versus bias value.
3.  Clarify the experimental setup for comparative claims, stating explicitly whether studies were replicated under identical conditions.
4.  Report mean and standard deviation for all experiments.
5.  Consider adding a brief discussion of the theoretical implications of the gate units.
