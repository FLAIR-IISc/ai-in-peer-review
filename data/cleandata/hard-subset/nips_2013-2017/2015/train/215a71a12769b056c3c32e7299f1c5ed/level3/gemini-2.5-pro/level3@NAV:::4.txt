This paper addresses the critical and well-known problem of training very deep neural networks, where performance often degrades as depth increases due to difficulties in gradient propagation. The authors propose a novel architecture, "highway networks," which introduces adaptive gating units inspired by LSTMs. These gates regulate information flow through the network, creating "information highways" that allow unimpeded gradient and information travel across many layers. This approach builds upon prior work on skip connections and improved initialization but offers a more flexible, data-dependent mechanism for controlling information flow, distinguishing it from methods that use fixed shortcut paths.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and its claims are convincingly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The optimization experiments on MNIST clearly demonstrate that highway networks do not suffer from performance degradation at depths of up to 100 layers, in stark contrast to conventional "plain" networks. Furthermore, the experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show the practical utility of the method, enabling single-stage training for deep, thin networks that previously required complex multi-stage procedures like FitNets.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using a learned gating mechanism to control cross-layer connections is highly original. While drawing inspiration from LSTMs, its application to deep feedforward networks provides a novel and elegant generalization of both standard architectures and those with fixed skip connections.
*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant as it provides a practical and effective solution to a major bottleneck in deep learning research. By enabling the direct training of extremely deep networks, this paper opens up new possibilities for exploring the relationship between depth and performance on complex tasks. The method is likely to be widely adopted and to inspire future architectural innovations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivating the problem and presenting the proposed solution with precision. The main messages are communicated effectively, and the experimental setup and results are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The analysis section provides valuable initial insights into the network's behavior, particularly the lesion study demonstrating how layer importance varies with task complexity. However, this analysis could be expanded. For instance, providing more diverse visualizations of the gate activities across different data classes or tasks would offer a more comprehensive understanding of the learned dynamic routing behavior.

Overall, the paper's strengths far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, significant, and well-validated contribution to the field.
