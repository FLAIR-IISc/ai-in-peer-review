Summary
This paper introduces Highway Networks, a deep feed-forward architecture that augments each layer with input-dependent gates enabling the layer to either transform its input or carry it forward unchanged. Concretely, the layer output is y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where H is the usual transform and T is a learned sigmoid gate (with C:=1−T). This LSTM-inspired gating along depth creates “information highways” that preserve both activations and gradients across many layers, allowing straightforward training of networks with tens to hundreds of layers via standard SGD.

The authors relate their proposal to prior work on deep feed-forward nets, skip connections, initialization schemes for deep nets, and knowledge transfer methods such as FitNets. They emphasize the conceptual link to LSTMs (gated additive paths) while clarifying differences: the gates modulate depth-wise information flow in feed-forward architectures rather than time, and they enable direct single-stage training rather than relying on teacher hints or deep supervision.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates:
- Optimization robustness: highway MLPs with up to 100 layers train easily on MNIST, whereas comparable plain MLPs degrade sharply with depth.
- Competitive generalization: convolutional highway networks achieve strong results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 within modest parameter/compute budgets, matching or exceeding FitNet-style baselines in a single training stage.
- In-depth analysis: visualizations and lesion studies reveal how gates adapt across layers, implement data-dependent routing, and selectively allocate depth based on task complexity.

Relation to prior work
- LSTMs: The transform/carry gating is directly inspired by LSTM’s input/forget gates, but applied spatially along depth; the paper clearly delineates this difference and uses LSTM-style negative gate bias initialization to encourage early carry behavior.
- FitNets/distillation: Unlike multi-stage hint-based training, highway networks train deep thin models end-to-end from scratch. The authors provide empirical comparisons on CIFAR that show similar or better accuracy without a teacher.
- Skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision: Prior skip connections are typically fixed and input-independent; here, multiplicative gates learn input- and layer-specific routing, which the authors analyze extensively.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is simple and well-justified. Claims about ease of optimization with depth are supported by controlled MNIST studies across multiple depths and extensive hyperparameter search. The proposed biasing scheme for T is sensible and effective.
- Empirical support: Results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 are promising, especially given conservative data augmentation and compute, and they directly address FitNet comparisons. The detailed gate analyses (bias dynamics, mean activity, per-sample selectivity), “information highway” visualizations, and lesion experiments are particularly persuasive in showing that gates are functionally used, not merely facilitating optimization.
- Completeness and honesty: Limitations are acknowledged (e.g., modest data augmentation and budgets), and related work is appropriately cited.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical setup is concise, with an intuitive interpretation of the gates and their Jacobians. Figures analyzing gate behavior and layer importance are informative. Code and hyperparameters are made available, supporting reproducibility.

Originality
- The architectural idea—per-layer gated additive pathways that carry inputs unchanged through depth—is novel in the feed-forward setting, going beyond fixed shortcuts and distinct from hint-based training. The analytical treatment of gating behavior across depth and inputs is unusually thorough for an architecture paper.

Significance
- Addressing the fragility of very deep feed-forward training is important. Highway networks offer a practical and general recipe that integrates seamlessly with convolutional layers and standard optimizers. The input-dependent gating mechanism provides a new knob—dynamic routing across depth—that others are likely to build upon, both architecturally and analytically.

Strengths
- Clear, elegant architecture that materially eases optimization of very deep nets.
- Input-dependent gates enabling learned carry paths; principled initialization.
- Strong empirical evidence on optimization and competitive performance with modest resources; direct comparisons to FitNets.
- Thorough analyses (gate activity, per-class routing, lesioning) that elucidate how and where depth is used.
- High-quality writing and positioning with respect to LSTMs, FitNets, and skip connections.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope focuses on MNIST and CIFAR with relatively conservative augmentation and budgets; large-scale evaluations are not included. This is a minor limitation given the paper’s architectural and analytical contributions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated architecture with clear advantages over plain feed-forward nets.
- Demonstrated ability to train very deep models directly with SGD.
- Careful comparisons to closely related methods (FitNets) and clear explanation of differences from LSTMs.
- Rich analysis providing insight into gating, routing, and layer utility.
- Clean, reproducible presentation with released code/hyperparameters.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evaluation on larger-scale datasets or broader domains (minor).

Recommendation
Strong accept. This is a very solid paper presenting a novel technique with compelling empirical results and unusually thorough analysis. It advances our understanding and practice of training very deep networks.
