

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture enabling direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanisms. The core innovation replaces standard layer transformations with a gated combination of transformed inputs and direct carry paths: $y = H(x) \cdot T(x) + x \cdot (1 - T(x))$, where $T$ is a transform gate (sigmoid) regulating information flow. The authors demonstrate that highway networks overcome the training difficulties of plain deep networks—achieving near-identical performance on MNIST with 100 layers versus 10 layers in plain networks—while generalizing well to CIFAR-10/100. Key analyses include gate activity patterns revealing dynamic input-dependent routing (Fig. 2–4), and lesioning experiments confirming that deeper layers contribute meaningfully to complex tasks (e.g., CIFAR-100) but remain idle for simpler problems (MNIST).

**Strengths:**  
*Originality:* The paper is the first to apply LSTM-style gating to *feedforward* networks for depth scalability, distinct from skip connections (e.g., ResNet) or teacher-student distillation (FitNets). It bridges recurrent and feedforward learning paradigms in a simple, elegant manner, with clear theoretical grounding (Eqs. 4–5).  
*Quality:* Rigorous experiments validate claims across MNIST (thinner networks), CIFAR-10/100 (comparable to FitNets), and depth (100 layers). The analysis of gate dynamics (Fig. 2–4) provides novel insights into layer utilization, and the negative bias initialization scheme is well-motivated and effective.  
*Clarity:* Well-structured, with precise notation, intuitive figures (e.g., information highways in Fig. 2), and reproducible code. The highway layer definition (Eq. 3) is accessible to non-recurrent experts.  
*Significance:* Solves a fundamental bottleneck in deep learning—direct training of very deep networks—enabling exploration of depth-accuracy tradeoffs. The layer importance analysis (Fig. 4) offers a new lens for understanding network depth, which is critical for efficient architecture design.

**Weaknesses & Suggestions:**  
*Limited Scale:* While experiments on MNIST/CIFAR are compelling, the absence of ImageNet results (as suggested in the review) is notable. The paper’s claim that highway networks "enable the study of extremely deep architectures" would be stronger with ImageNet validation (e.g., 100+ layers on a large-scale task).  
*Contextualization:* The paper could better position its "highway" perspective relative to *intra-layer* depth (e.g., Inception’s stacked 3x3 convs or VGG’s repeated blocks). Highway layers themselves are *richer substructures*—each highway layer could be viewed as a "mini-network" (e.g., with stacked transforms), blurring the line between "depth" and "layer complexity." This ties to the reviewer’s point: highway networks may not be "deep" in the traditional sense but rather composed of *complex, hierarchical layers*.  
*Parameter Sharing:* The suggested experiment—jointly training a thin-and-tall network on MNIST and CIFAR (forking at the top)—would directly test whether CIFAR repurposes MNIST-optimized parameters. This would reveal if highway networks enable efficient cross-domain parameter sharing across depth hierarchies. The authors could explore this in future work.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* First to solve depth training via adaptive gating; simple, elegant architecture; solid theory/experiments; provides new tools (e.g., layer importance analysis) for depth studies.  
*Con:* Limited to smaller datasets (ImageNet missing); minor ambiguity in "depth" definition (layers vs. substructures).  

**Recommendation:**  
This is a high-quality, significant contribution to deep learning. The highway network concept is novel, well-executed, and addresses a critical problem. The minor weaknesses (lack of ImageNet results, contextualization of depth) are easily addressable in revision. The paper’s elegance and impact warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with a note to include ImageNet experiments in the camera-ready version.  

**Final Note:** The reviewer’s insight about highway networks as "layers with richer substructures" (e.g., analogous to Inception’s stacked convs) is particularly valuable—it reframes the paper’s contribution as part of a broader trend toward *hierarchical complexity within layers* rather than merely increasing layer count. This perspective should be acknowledged in the paper’s discussion to strengthen its connection to prior work.
