

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to enable training of extremely deep neural networks through adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTM networks. The authors propose a simple yet effective modification to standard feedforward networks where each layer incorporates transform and carry gates that regulate information flow, creating "information highways" that allow unimpeded gradient and activation propagation across layers. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly using standard SGD without the need for complex initialization or two-stage training procedures, even with hundreds of layers.

The paper excels in several key areas. The proposed method is clearly original, building upon but significantly generalizing prior work on skip connections and recurrent networks. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, showing consistent improvements in classification accuracy across multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) compared to plain networks and state-of-the-art alternatives like Fitnets. The authors provide compelling evidence that highway networks can achieve comparable or better performance than shallower networks with significantly fewer parameters, while maintaining training simplicity.

The paper's strengths are its technical soundness, clear communication, and significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The architecture is well-motivated, the implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility, and the results strongly support the claims. The analysis section effectively demonstrates how information flows through the network and validates the dynamic routing capability of the transform gates.

The primary weakness is that the analysis section could benefit from more varied details or additional illustrations to further elucidate how the gating mechanisms operate across different layers and datasets. While the current analysis is strong, more visualizations of gate behavior across diverse input types would strengthen the theoretical understanding.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by enabling the training of extremely deep networks with minimal architectural modification. The method is both practical and theoretically grounded, with clear potential for broad application. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards, demonstrates strong originality, and addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning with meaningful significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
