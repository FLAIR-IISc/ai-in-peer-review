- Core idea: Introduces "highway networks" that use LSTM-style adaptive gates to let layers choose between transforming or carrying inputs, enabling training of very deep (hundreds-layer) feedforward nets with SGD.
- Empirical claim: Highway networks train up to 100 layers directly, outperform plain networks on MNIST and achieve competitive results on CIFAR-10/100 with single-stage training.
- Originality/significance: Adapting data-dependent gating to feedforward architectures is novel, elegantly generalizes skip connections, and opens important new architectural directions.
- Experimental quality: Controlled depth comparisons (MNIST) and CIFAR experiments convincingly support the main claim and show practical performance advantages over methods like FitNets.
- Clarity and analysis: Paper is well-written with strong analyses and visualizations of gate behaviors, data-dependent routing, and layer lesioning that demonstrate meaningful learned use of depth.
- Weakness — gradient formulation: Equation (5) misleadingly treats gates as binary; the authors should state the correct smooth Jacobian used in backprop and clarify the simplification.
- Weakness — initialization heuristic: The transform gate bias initialization (e.g., -1, -3) is ad-hoc and needs systematic analysis (e.g., performance vs. bias plots) to justify robustness.
- Weakness — comparative rigor: Comparisons to other methods lack full apples-to-apples details (augmentation, width, compute); differences should be explicitly stated to validate claims.
- Weakness — result reporting: Some tables omit mean±std.dev.; results (especially Tables 2/3) should report variance across runs for completeness.
- Suggested addition: A short theoretical/discussion section on how gating yields conditional identity mappings and implicitly represents ensembles of shallower networks would add valuable context.
- Summary — pro: Simple, novel, effective architectural innovation with strong empirical support and deep analysis, likely to spur follow-up work.
- Summary — con: Minor issues in technical clarity, a key hyperparameter analysis, and comparative rigor; these are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution.
- Recommendation: Overall acceptance recommended.
