Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feed-forward architecture that augments each layer with input-dependent gates inspired by LSTM, enabling information to be “carried” across many layers with minimal attenuation. A highway layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where T is a sigmoid “transform gate” and 1−T is the “carry gate.” With negative gate bias initialization, the network initially favors carrying (near-identity mappings), facilitating gradient flow and optimization at very large depths. The paper provides optimization studies on MNIST demonstrating robust training at 50–100 layers (and pilot stability beyond 1000 layers), competitive MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 results with thin but deep architectures, and insightful analyses of learned gating patterns, dynamic routing, and layer importance. Prior work on training deep nets has focused on improved initialization, activation functions, skip connections, layerwise training, and teacher–student distillation (FitNets); this work builds on that line by introducing adaptive, input-dependent skip pathways analogous to LSTM’s constant-error carousel but in feed-forward settings.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea with strong intuition (LSTM-inspired gates) and simple implementation.
- Convincing optimization evidence: depth does not degrade trainability; 50–100 layer networks train faster and better than plain networks of similar capacity.
- Empirical results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 competitive with prior thin/deep settings without multi-stage distillation and with modest augmentation.
- Careful, novel analyses: visualization of gate biases/activities, class-conditional routing, and lesion studies showing depth utilization on harder tasks.
- Practical guidance (negative bias initialization) and public code.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Gradient/Jacobian statements: Eq. 5 asserts dy/dx = I when T=0 and H′ when T=1. Since T=σ(·) ∈ (0,1), these cases are never exact; moreover, y = H(x)T(x) + x(1−T(x)) implies additional terms from ∂T/∂x, proportional to (H(x)−x). Please (a) provide the exact Jacobian used for backprop, and (b) empirically report gate saturation (|∂T/∂x|) across depth to justify negligible cross terms when gates are near 0/1.
- Initialization sensitivity: The constant negative bias bT by depth is central. Please include a quantitative sensitivity plot of training/test performance vs initial bT (and depth), and discuss the regularization effect (favoring “longer highways”) and how you select bT in practice.
- Comparative claims: For CIFAR comparisons (FitNets, maxout), explicitly state if training protocols match (data augmentation, parameter/FLOP budgets, depth/width, optimizer schedules, epochs). If not identical, detail the differences and adjust claims accordingly.
- Reporting practice: The paper sometimes reports “Best (mean ± std)” and sometimes best only. Please report mean ± std over multiple seeds for all methods and settings, including baselines, and include the number of runs.
- Theoretical grounding: A learning-theoretic or optimization analysis of gated layers would strengthen the contribution. For example, conditions under which gradient norms remain bounded with depth; how negative gate bias induces an approximate identity map and mitigates vanishing/exploding gradients; and when input-dependent routing affords advantages over fixed skip connections.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture and training procedure are sound and align with LSTM gating principles. The optimization study is systematic, and the analysis sections are exemplary. However, the Jacobian/backprop discussion needs to be made precise (see above), and more rigorous ablations would help (e.g., learn C separately vs setting C=1−T; remove gating on subsets; vary gate parameterization; parameter/FLOP overhead quantification).
- Empirical support: Results convincingly show ease of optimization with depth and solid generalization. Please add: (i) fairness-controlled CIFAR comparisons (same augmentation and compute), (ii) gradient norm vs depth plots for plain vs highway nets, and (iii) training curves variability across seeds.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well organized. Figures illustrating gate behavior and lesion studies are insightful.
- Please correct minor typos (“varying varying,” “backpropogation”), and ensure consistent notation for elementwise products and Jacobians.
- Add a brief pseudo-code block for forward/backward passes through a highway layer, including gradients w.r.t. H and T paths.

Originality
- While skip connections have a long history, the specific LSTM-inspired, input-dependent gating in deep feed-forward nets is a novel and well-motivated combination. The diagnostic analyses (dynamic routing, layer lesioning) are also original and valuable.

Significance
- Training very deep nets directly with SGD without multi-stage tricks is important. The method is simple, widely applicable (fully-connected and convolutional), and could influence how deep architectures are designed.
- The analyses provide rare insight into how depth is utilized and how data-dependent routing emerges, likely to inspire further work.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact form of the Jacobian and the backprop equations, including terms involving ∂T/∂x? Do you observe gate saturation that justifies the simplified view in Eq. 5?
- How sensitive is performance to bT, weight initialization scale, and depth? Please provide performance vs bT plots and gradient norm vs depth plots.
- What is the parameter and FLOP overhead of adding T for common conv settings (e.g., 3×3 convs), and how does it trade off with depth/width?
- Have you tried learning C independently (not constrained to 1−T)? Does it help/hurt optimization and generalization?
- Do highway networks still train at 200–1000 layers on CIFAR-100 with your settings? Any degradation?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and effective architectural idea enabling very deep training.
- Strong empirical evidence and compelling analyses.
- Clear relation to prior work, with practical guidance and released code.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete reporting (best vs mean±std) and fairness details in comparisons.
- Lack of precise gradient/Jacobian treatment and theoretical guarantees.
- Limited ablation on design choices (C=1−T, gate parameterization).

Minor comments
- Unify experimental reporting: seeds, epochs, augmentation, compute budgets.
- Include per-layer gate activity histograms and saturation metrics.
- Provide CIFAR-10/100 results with and without global average pooling for completeness.

Overall evaluation
A strong submission with a clear, impactful idea, convincing optimization results, and unusually thoughtful analyses. Addressing the gradient/Jacobian precision, reporting consistency, and initialization sensitivity would significantly strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
