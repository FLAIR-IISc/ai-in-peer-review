This paper introduces highway networks, a novel feedforward architecture designed to ease the training of very deep networks. Drawing inspiration from the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, the authors propose adaptive transform and carry gates that regulate information flow between layers. This allows gradients to propagate unimpeded through many layers, overcoming the optimization challenges that plague traditional 'plain' deep networks. The work builds upon extensive prior research into training deep models, including improved initialization schemes and skip-connection architectures.

The paper's primary strength is its clear presentation of an elegant and effective idea. The experiments are fairly convincing, demonstrating that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained effectively with SGD, whereas plain networks of similar depth fail to converge. A particularly strong contribution is the detailed analysis of the learned networks' gate-output patterns, which provides compelling evidence of dynamic, data-dependent information routing and allows for an interesting 'lesion study' of layer importance.

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. A critical technical issue lies in Equation 5. Since the transform gate T uses a sigmoid function, its output is never exactly 0 or 1, rendering the presented Jacobian incorrect. The authors must clarify the exact implementation of backpropagation. Furthermore, the initialization strategy of using constant negative biases feels ad-hoc; a plot showing performance as a function of the initial bias would provide valuable insight into its role as a regularizer. The paper’s comparative claims, particularly against FitNets, also require clarification—the authors should explicitly state whether the experimental settings are identical or detail the differences. Finally, reporting only maximum accuracies is insufficient; mean and standard deviation across multiple runs should be provided for all methods to allow for robust comparison.

Overall, this is a significant and timely contribution with potential for high impact. If the authors can address the points above, particularly the technical clarification on gradients and the rigor of the experimental comparisons, the paper will be a strong candidate for acceptance. As a suggestion, the work could be further strengthened by a learning-theory analysis of how the gating units, which allow layers to function as conditional identities, impact optimization and representation capacity.
