This paper proposes "Highway Networks," a novel neural network architecture that enables the training of extremely deep feed-forward networks, with demonstrations of up to 100 layers. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. Each layer includes a "transform gate" and a "carry gate" which learn to adaptively control how much of the input signal is transformed by the layer's non-linearity versus how much is passed through unchanged. This creates unimpeded "information highways" that allow gradients to flow smoothly through many layers, effectively mitigating the vanishing and exploding gradient problems that have historically been a major obstacle to training very deep networks. The authors demonstrate that, unlike conventional "plain" networks, highway networks can be trained directly with standard SGD, even at extreme depths, and achieve strong performance on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution is highly original and significant. While skip connections existed previously, the application of a learnable, data-dependent gating mechanism from the recurrent network literature (specifically LSTMs) to solve the deep feed-forward training problem is a novel and powerful insight. This work directly tackles one of the most fundamental challenges in deep learning at the time and provides an elegant and effective solution. The ability to successfully train networks with hundreds of layers using simple SGD was a major breakthrough.

2.  **Technical Quality and Experimental Support:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by a strong set of experiments. The optimization comparison in Section 3.1 is particularly clear and compelling, starkly illustrating the failure of plain networks at depths of 50-100 layers while highway networks train successfully. The results on CIFAR benchmarks demonstrate that this ease of training translates to competitive generalization performance.

3.  **Clarity and Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and well-organized. The core concept is explained intuitively, and the notation is straightforward. It is a pleasure to read.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is a major strength. The visualizations of gate activity and the layer lesioning experiments provide fascinating insights into the network's learned behavior. The demonstration that the network learns to use more layers for a complex task (CIFAR-100) than for a simple one (MNIST) is particularly illuminating. This shows that the highway architecture doesn't just enable training, but allows the model to learn an effective depth appropriate for the problem. The analysis of data-dependent information routing further reinforces that the gates are not just a static training trick but a dynamic part of the network's computation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Minor Correction in Text:** There appears to be a small inconsistency in Section 4.1. The text states, "...most transform gates are active on average," when describing the CIFAR-100 case. However, looking at Figure 2 (column 2, bottom row), the average gate activity appears to be quite low (dark colors dominate). The subsequent discussion of sparse, selective activity for single examples seems to support the idea that the gates are *not* active on average. Perhaps the authors meant that different gates are active for different inputs, but on average across the entire dataset, any given gate is mostly closed. Clarifying this point would strengthen the analysis.

2.  **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** To further emphasize the benefit of the learnable gating mechanism, it would be valuable to compare it against a simpler, non-adaptive baseline. For instance, a plain network with layers initialized to be near-identity transformations (e.g., weights as an identity matrix plus small noise, biases set to zero) could also facilitate gradient flow at the beginning of training. The key advantage of the highway network is that its gates are dynamic and data-dependent, allowing the network to learn a flexible routing policy. A highway layer can decide *per-example* whether to transform or carry its input, a capability a statically-initialized identity layer lacks. A direct empirical comparison would powerfully highlight the importance of this adaptive routing.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of deep learning. It introduces a novel, well-motivated architecture that elegantly solves the critical problem of training very deep networks. The claims are backed by strong empirical results and insightful analysis. The work is highly original and has significant practical and theoretical implications for designing and understanding deep models. Despite the minor points for clarification, this is a top-tier paper. It is well-written, impactful, and a clear accept for NIPS.
