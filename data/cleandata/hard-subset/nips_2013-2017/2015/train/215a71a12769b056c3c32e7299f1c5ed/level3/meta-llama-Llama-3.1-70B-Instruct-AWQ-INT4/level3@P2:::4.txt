This paper proposes a novel architecture for training very deep neural networks, which the authors term "highway networks". The key idea is to introduce direct cross-layer connections, referred to as "information highways", that allow unimpeded information flow across many layers. This approach is inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and uses adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow.

The authors provide a clear and concise background on the importance of network depth in achieving state-of-the-art results in various machine learning tasks. They also discuss the challenges associated with training very deep networks, including the vanishing gradient problem and the difficulty of propagating activations and gradients. The proposed highway network architecture is designed to overcome these challenges by allowing information to flow across layers without attenuation.

The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with plain networks and other state-of-the-art methods. The results demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and achieve improved classification accuracy on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The authors also provide an analysis of the learned representations and the routing of information through the network, which provides insights into the workings of the proposed architecture.

The paper is well-written, and the main messages are clearly and effectively communicated. The method is sufficiently original while generalizing prior cross-layer techniques, and the contributions are novel and well-supported by experimental evidence. The work is significant for enabling the training of increasingly deeper networks in applications, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the implications of their results.
