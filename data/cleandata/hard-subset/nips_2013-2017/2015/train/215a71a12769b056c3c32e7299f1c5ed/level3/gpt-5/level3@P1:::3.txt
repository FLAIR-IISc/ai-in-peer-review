This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces LSTM-inspired gating across layers to enable unimpeded information and gradient flow through very deep networks. Concretely, each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is the usual nonlinear transform and T is a transform gate (with carry gate 1 − T). Initializing gate biases negatively biases layers to “carry” their inputs at the start of training, creating effective identity paths (“information highways”) analogous to LSTM’s constant error carousel. The authors demonstrate that, with this mechanism, very deep networks (50–100+ layers, and pilot runs >1000 layers) can be trained via plain SGD without specialized optimization tricks. Experiments cover fully connected and convolutional settings (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100), with additional analyses on gate activity and layer importance via lesioning. The work relates to skip connections and deep supervision, teacher–student distillation (FitNets), normalized initialization, and locally competitive networks, but differs in providing data-dependent, multiplicative routing akin to LSTM gating in a feedforward setting.

Quality: The technical formulation is sound and well-motivated by gradient flow considerations. The optimization study on MNIST is careful: comparable parameter counts per layer, random search for hyperparameters, and depth sweeps for both plain and highway nets. Results convincingly show depth-robust optimization (100-layer highway nets converge faster and to lower loss than plain controls). Generalization experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 are competitive given modest model sizes and limited augmentation, and the models can be trained in a single stage where FitNets required hint-based training. The analyses (gate activity profiles, data-dependent routing, and layer lesioning) are insightful and support the intended mechanism: early layers transform more, many mid layers can remain near-carry on simple tasks, and difficult tasks (CIFAR-100) exploit more depth. Weaknesses: stronger baselines are missing (e.g., identity-initialized or residual-style skip connections with learned scalars), and ablations that isolate the benefit of input-dependent gating vs fixed/learned static skips would strengthen causal claims. Runtime/parameter overhead of gating is not discussed quantitatively. Some results use “best run” without full variance reporting.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, equations are precise, and the architectural description is concrete enough to reproduce (and code is promised). The training protocol is mostly complete; however, a concise table of hyperparameters and data pre-processing would aid reproducibility across datasets. Specific correction: in Section 4.1, the sentence “most transform gates are active on average” appears inconsistent with the figures and interpretation; it should read “most transform gates are not active on average,” with high selectivity at the single-example level.

Originality: The idea to bring LSTM-style gating (transform/carry) to deep feedforward architectures is novel and timely. While skip connections and distillation have been explored, data-dependent multiplicative gating that interpolates between identity and transformation provides a distinct mechanism for depth-robust credit assignment. The connection to LSTM is explicit and well-executed.

Significance: This contribution meaningfully advances our ability to train very deep networks with standard optimization, likely to influence subsequent architectures and analyses of depth. The empirical results are strong enough to demonstrate feasibility and competitive accuracy under fair budgets; the diagnostic analyses provide new tools to reason about depth utilization.

Suggestions for improvement and additional experiments:
- Compare to identity-initialized plain networks with small noise (e.g., linear or ReLU layers initialized near identity as in deep linear analyses), and to simple residual/skip baselines such as y = H(x) + x with:
  - fixed skip (no gates),
  - learned per-layer scalar α (y = αH(x) + x),
  - learned per-channel α.
  This will help disentangle the value of input-dependent gating from simpler schemes.
- Ablate gating to assess its necessity:
  - freeze T to a constant per layer (learned scalar) vs input-dependent T(x);
  - test C ≠ 1 − T (independent carry gate) to evaluate flexibility vs parameter cost;
  - restrict T to lower-dimensional parametrizations (per-channel vs per-unit) and report accuracy/compute trade-offs.
- Provide quantitative overhead (parameters and multiplications) from the gating paths and timing vs plain and residual counterparts.
- Expand reporting of variance across runs in all tables (the paper already does this in places).
- Clarify sensitivity to the initial gate bias bT and whether a schedule (depth-dependent or learned initial bias) improves stability.
- Discuss relation to contemporaneous identity/skip methods (e.g., linear transformations in perceptrons; normalized/MSRA init) and articulate theoretical differences (constant vs data-dependent routing, Jacobian control through T(x)).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, elegant architectural idea with strong theoretical motivation (Jacobian control, gradient flow).
- Convincing optimization study showing depth-robust training with standard SGD.
- Competitive results on CIFAR-10/100 without heavy augmentation; surpasses FitNet baselines with single-stage training.
- Insightful analyses (routing selectivity, layer lesioning) that deepen understanding of depth utilization.
- Well-written and likely to be influential.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing direct comparisons to identity/residual baselines and identity-initialized deep nets.
- Limited reporting of computational overhead and comprehensive variance across all experiments.
- Some results rely on “best-of” runs; broader statistical characterization would strengthen claims.
- Minor clarity issue in Section 4.1 wording.

Overall evaluation: This is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that makes training very deep networks practical via LSTM-inspired gating. The paper is a pleasure to read and should be of broad interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
