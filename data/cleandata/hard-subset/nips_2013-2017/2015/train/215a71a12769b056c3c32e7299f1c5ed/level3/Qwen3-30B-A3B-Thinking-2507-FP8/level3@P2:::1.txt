

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture for deep feedforward networks that enables training of extremely deep models (100+ layers) through adaptive gating units inspired by LSTM recurrent networks. The authors propose modifying standard layers with transform and carry gates (T and C=1-T), allowing information to flow unimpeded across layers via "information highways." This approach contrasts with previous methods like layer-wise pretraining, distillation, or skip connections, and addresses the core challenge of vanishing gradients in very deep networks. The work builds on foundational NIPS contributions (e.g., LSTMs [29,30], normalized initialization [16,17]) while offering a direct training solution without two-stage procedures.

**Strengths**  
The paper’s primary strength lies in its clear, well-motivated architecture and compelling empirical validation. The highway network design is rigorously evaluated across MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, demonstrating that 100-layer networks train effectively with SGD—achieving results comparable to shallow networks (e.g., 100-layer highway networks outperform similarly-sized plain networks by >100× on MNIST). The analysis of gate behavior (Figures 2–4) is exceptionally insightful, revealing how networks dynamically route information (e.g., sparse gate activity per sample, class-dependent routing, and layer importance analysis via lesioning). This provides novel empirical evidence on *how* deep networks utilize layers, a critical gap in the literature. The experimental setup is thorough, with ablation studies (e.g., bias initialization sensitivity) and comparisons to relevant baselines (FitNets [25]).

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Technical clarity on gradients**: Equation 5 claims the Jacobian is I (when T=0) or H' (when T=1), but T is a sigmoid output (never exactly 0/1). The authors must clarify how backpropagation handles this continuous gate, as the gradient derivation appears incorrect. A brief explanation or equation correction is essential for reproducibility.  
2. **Initialization justification**: The use of a constant negative bias (e.g., -1 for depth ~10) is ad hoc. A plot showing performance vs. initial bias (e.g., -10 to 0) would demonstrate its role as a regularizer and justify the heuristic. Without this, the initialization strategy lacks theoretical grounding.  
3. **Comparative clarity**: Table 2 compares to FitNets but omits key experimental differences (e.g., hyperparameters, data augmentation). The authors should explicitly state whether settings align with prior work (e.g., [25]) or differ, to enable fair comparison.  
4. **Reporting practices**: Results report only max accuracy (e.g., Table 2), not mean ± std.dev. as standard in NIPS (e.g., [16,20]). This obscures variability; the authors should include these metrics for all methods.  
5. **Missing theoretical analysis**: A brief learning-theory perspective (e.g., how gates enable selective identity mappings) would strengthen the contribution, as the paper’s core innovation is a practical solution without theoretical justification.

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality contribution that significantly advances the practical training of deep networks. The highway architecture is original (not merely a combination of prior techniques), and the experiments convincingly demonstrate its utility for very deep models. The analysis of gate behavior is a standout feature, offering new insights into deep network dynamics. While the weaknesses are technical and resolvable, they do not undermine the paper’s core claim. The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on impactful, well-executed research.  

**Recommendation**  
Accept. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a critical problem in deep learning. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in revision. The significance is high: highway networks enable direct training of 100+ layer networks, removing a major barrier to deeper architectures. This work has already inspired later developments (e.g., ResNets [36]), underscoring its importance.  

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*: Strong architecture; convincing experiments; insightful gate analysis; enables training of previously intractable depths.  
*Con*: Gradient derivation requires clarification; initialization needs justification; comparative claims need explicit setting details; reporting lacks variability metrics.  

The authors should revise the gradient explanation, add a bias-sensitivity plot, clarify experimental comparisons, and report mean ± std.dev. These changes will solidify an already excellent paper.
