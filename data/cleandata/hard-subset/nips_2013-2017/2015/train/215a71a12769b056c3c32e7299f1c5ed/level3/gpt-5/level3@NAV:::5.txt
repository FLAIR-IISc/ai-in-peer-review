This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that borrows LSTM-style gating (transform and carry gates) to enable information to flow across hundreds of layers without attenuation. The authors demonstrate that simple SGD suffices to train very deep networks (100+ layers) and support the claims with optimization curves, CIFAR/MNIST results, and insightful analyses of gate activity and layer lesioning. The work relates well to prior efforts on depth (VGG’s stacked 3x3, Inception’s richer modules), initialization for rectifiers, skip/shortcut connections, and FitNets/distillation, while providing a distinct, LSTM-inspired mechanism for depth.

Quality: Technically solid and carefully evaluated. The optimization study is convincing; the analyses of gate selectivity and layer importance are particularly illuminating. Comparisons to FitNets under matched budgets are fair. Minor gaps: a more systematic accounting of parameter/computation overhead from gates, ablations on C=1−T, and sensitivity to gate-bias initialization would strengthen the case.

Clarity: Clear, well-organized, and reproducible (code and hyperparameters provided). Figures effectively reveal dynamics of gating and utilization across depth.

Originality: The key contribution—importing LSTM-like gating to deep feedforward nets—is simple and elegant. To my knowledge, this is the first thorough application of such gating to grow/train very deep feedforward networks.

Significance: High. If widely adopted, the method could make very deep nets routine without specialized training tricks. Results on CIFAR are competitive; scaling to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would cement impact.

Alternative perspective: Some “very deep” nets can be viewed as layers composed of richer substructures (e.g., Inception modules, VGG’s stacked 3x3). It would be interesting to position highway networks similarly—as layers that internally choose between identity and transformation—clarifying the notion of effective depth.

Suggested experiment: Train one thin-and-tall network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR with a shared trunk that forks only near the top. Examine whether the shared highway trunk learns inference patterns similar to separately trained networks; whether parameters underused for MNIST are repurposed by CIFAR; and how efficiently information and capacity are shared across the depth.

Pros:
- Conceptually simple, novel, and effective; strong analyses.
- Enables training of very deep nets with plain SGD.
- Clear presentation and released code.

Cons:
- Limited large-scale validation; modest discussion of compute/parameter overhead.
- Missing ablations on gate design choices.

Recommendation: Strong accept; also encourage testing on ImageNet and deeper analyses of parameter sharing and gate design.
