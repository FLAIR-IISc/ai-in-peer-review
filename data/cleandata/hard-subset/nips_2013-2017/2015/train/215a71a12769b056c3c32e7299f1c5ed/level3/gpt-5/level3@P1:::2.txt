This paper introduces Highway Networks, a gated feed-forward architecture that enables information and gradients to flow across many layers via input-dependent “transform” and “carry” pathways inspired by LSTM. Each layer outputs y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where T is a sigmoid gate and H is any transformation (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution). By initializing the transform-gate bias negatively, the network starts close to identity mappings and adaptively learns when to transform vs. carry. The authors demonstrate that such networks can be trained directly with SGD at depths that cause plain networks to stall, and they analyze the learned gating behavior to show data-dependent routing and the effective use of depth.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well-positioned relative to (i) LSTM’s gating mechanisms for long-term credit assignment, (ii) initialization strategies and rectifier networks enabling moderately deep models, (iii) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision used to improve gradient flow, and (iv) FitNets, which require teacher hints to train thin deep nets. Unlike fixed skip connections, highways provide input-dependent, multiplicative routing; unlike FitNets, they allow single-stage training without hints; and unlike pure initialization tricks, the architecture itself preserves gradient flow regardless of H.

Quality: The technical formulation is clear and sound. The gating design is simple yet expressive, and the negative-bias initialization is justified by analogy to LSTM. Optimization experiments convincingly show that thin plain networks degrade with depth while similarly sized highway networks do not, across 10–100 layers on MNIST. On CIFAR-10/100, the method matches or exceeds FitNets under comparable parameter/multiply budgets without multi-stage training. The analysis of gate biases/activities and lesion studies provides rare insight into depth utilization and data-dependent routing. The empirical methodology is careful: multiple runs with mean/std, random hyperparameter search, re-use of settings across datasets, and public code.

Clarity: The paper is very clearly written and well organized. The architectural description is concise, and figures on gate activities and lesioning are informative. The assumptions (e.g., C = 1−T) and initialization are stated explicitly, and implementation details for convolutional variants are provided. Minor editorial issue: on p.5 there is a typo “Tables 3 and 3.”

Originality: Bringing LSTM-style gating to deep feed-forward networks in this simple form is novel and distinct from earlier fixed skip connections and deep supervision. The combination of a principled initialization, a minimal gating rule, and thorough analysis of routing is an original contribution.

Significance: Training very deep models with simple SGD, without auxiliary losses or teacher hints, is an important capability. The method is practical (small overhead, compatible with common H) and comes with actionable initialization guidance. The detailed analyses (activity patterns, class-conditional routing, lesioning) are likely to inform future architecture design. Results on CIFAR-10/100 are competitive and demonstrate the approach’s utility; the optimization evidence suggests potential broader impact across domains where depth is beneficial.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea that directly targets the depth optimization problem.
- Strong optimization evidence: highway nets scale in depth while plain nets with careful init do not.
- Competitive CIFAR-10/100 accuracy under comparable resource budgets; no need for teacher hints.
- Thorough analyses of gating behavior, routing, and layer importance; code availability.
- Well-positioned relative to LSTM gating, skip connections, FitNets, and initialization literature.

Weaknesses (minor)
- Evaluation focuses on MNIST and CIFAR; large-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) are not included.
- Limited ablations on design choices (e.g., learning C independently vs. tying C = 1−T, gate placement variants, overhead vs. benefit).
- Comparisons to alternative skip-connection designs are mostly conceptual; more head-to-head ablations would strengthen claims.
- Practical computational cost (extra parameters and multiplies for T) is not quantified in detail.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a central and timely challenge (training very deep nets) with a novel, principled mechanism.
- Demonstrated empirical benefits in depth scaling and competitiveness on standard vision benchmarks.
- Insightful analyses that improve understanding of deep computations and data-dependent routing.
- Clear writing, reproducibility (code), and careful methodology.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of large-scale experiments and limited ablations leave some questions about generality and cost-benefit trade-offs.
- Overlap in spirit with skip connections; a broader empirical comparison could further delineate advantages.

Questions for authors (optional)
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of T bias beyond the suggested range? Does adaptive schedule for bT help?
- Have you tried learning C independently of T, or adding gate regularizers (e.g., sparsity) to encourage parsimony?
- What is the relative compute/parameter overhead of the gates, and how does it trade off with depth?

Minor comments
- Typo: “Tables 3 and 3” (p.5). Please correct.
- Consider reporting gate-induced compute (fraction of active transforms) as a proxy for dynamic depth.

Overall assessment: A very solid paper introducing a novel and effective technique for training very deep networks, with strong evidence and insightful analysis. I recommend acceptance.
