This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of training very deep feed-forward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, termed "highway networks," which facilitates the flow of information and gradients through many layers, enabling stable training with simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD) even for networks with hundreds of layers.

The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. Each layer in a highway network is augmented with a "transform gate" and a "carry gate." These gates dynamically control the proportion of the output that is produced by the standard non-linear transformation of the layer versus the proportion that is simply a copy of the input. This mechanism creates unimpeded "information highways" where data can flow across layers without modification, effectively allowing the network to learn to regulate its own depth based on the input data. This contrasts with previous approaches like fixed skip-connections or specialized initialization schemes, offering a more flexible, data-dependent solution.

The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation of their proposed architecture. First, they conduct a rigorous optimization experiment on MNIST, demonstrating that while standard "plain" networks degrade in performance as depth increases beyond 20 layers, highway networks can be trained effectively up to 100 layers and beyond, converging faster and to a much better solution. Second, they show on CIFAR-10 that their method can directly train deep, thin networks in a single stage, achieving comparable or better performance than the recently proposed FitNets, which required a complex two-stage training procedure with hints from a pre-trained teacher network. Finally, they demonstrate strong performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 with very deep convolutional highway networks, establishing the practical utility of the approach.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of very high quality. The claims are technically sound and are exceptionally well-supported by a comprehensive and carefully designed set of experiments. The work is not merely a proposal of a new architecture; it includes an in-depth analysis section that provides significant insight into the model's behavior. The visualizations of gate activities and the lesioning study (Figure 4) are particularly illuminating. They convincingly demonstrate that the network learns data-dependent information routing and that for complex tasks like CIFAR-100, a large number of layers actively contribute to the final result, whereas for simpler tasks like MNIST, many layers can remain dormant without harming performance. This is a very desirable property and a strong piece of evidence for the method's effectiveness.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation is well-established, and the proposed highway layer is introduced with a simple and intuitive mathematical formulation. The organization is logical, progressing from the core idea to experiments that validate the optimization benefits, and finally to a deep analysis of the learned representations. The authors provide sufficient detail about their experimental setup, and the code is made publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it draws clear inspiration from LSTMs, the application of adaptive gating to create information highways in very deep feed-forward networks is a novel and powerful contribution. The paper does an excellent job of situating the work within the context of related ideas, such as normalized initialization, deep supervision, and fixed shortcut connections, while clearly articulating the advantages of its dynamic, data-dependent approach.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. It provides an elegant and practical solution to a major roadblock in deep learning research: the difficulty of training truly deep networks. By enabling the training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard SGD, this paper opens the door for the community to explore the benefits of extreme depth in a variety of domains. The concept of learned, dynamic information routing is a powerful one that is likely to influence future architectural designs. This work represents a clear advance in the state of the art for training deep neural networks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective architecture that successfully addresses the critical problem of training very deep networks.
*   **Pro:** The claims are backed by strong and convincing empirical results on multiple standard benchmarks.
*   **Pro:** The analysis of the network's internal dynamics (gate behavior, layer importance) is excellent and provides valuable scientific insight beyond just performance metrics.
*   **Pro:** The work is highly original, clearly written, and will be of great interest and significance to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   I have no significant criticisms of the paper. It is a very complete, well-executed, and polished piece of research.

**Minor Comments:**
*   There appears to be a minor typo in the "Comparison to State-of-the-art Methods" section (page 5, right column): "The results are tabulated in Table 3." It seems this should refer to two different tables, or there might be a missing reference.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It presents a novel and important technique, supports it with thorough experiments and analysis, and is written with exceptional clarity.
