**Summary**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, termed "highway networks," inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The core idea is to introduce learnable gates at each layer that dynamically control the flow of information. Each layer's output is a weighted sum of a non-linear transformation of its input and the input itself, with the weights determined by a data-dependent "transform gate." This allows information to pass through layers unimpeded, creating "highways" that prevent the degradation of gradients and enable effective training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent.

The authors position this work against previous approaches for training deep networks, such as specialized initialization schemes, better optimizers, and multi-stage training procedures like deep supervision or hint-based training. They argue that highway networks provide a more direct and flexible solution. The paper presents empirical results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, demonstrating that highway networks overcome the optimization difficulties faced by traditional "plain" networks as depth increases. Furthermore, an insightful analysis section investigates the learned behavior of the gates, showing evidence of data-dependent information routing and providing a method to evaluate the contribution of individual layers.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a simple, elegant, and highly effective architectural innovation. Adapting the gating principle from LSTMs to the feedforward domain is a novel and well-motivated idea that directly tackles one of the most important and timely problems in deep learning. The ability to train networks of unprecedented depth (hundreds or even a thousand layers) with simple SGD is a significant contribution that opens up new avenues for research into the role of depth in neural networks.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The controlled optimization experiment on MNIST (Figure 1) provides a clear and powerful demonstration that highway networks do not suffer from the optimization hurdles that plague plain networks at extreme depths. The results on CIFAR-10/100 are competitive with contemporary state-of-the-art methods (e.g., FitNets), and notably, the highway networks can be trained end-to-end in a single stage, which is a significant practical advantage over methods requiring complex, multi-stage training procedures.

3.  **Excellent Analysis:** A major strength of the paper is the detailed analysis in Section 4. The visualizations of gate activities and block outputs provide valuable insight into the network's internal workings. The analysis of class-conditional gate activations (Figure 3) convincingly suggests that the network learns dynamic, input-dependent information routing, rather than just a static set of skip connections. The layer lesioning study (Figure 4) is an excellent contribution, offering a novel way to probe the "effective depth" learned by the network for a given task and showing that the network can learn to utilize its depth on complex tasks while keeping layers idle on simpler ones.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are several points that could be clarified or improved to strengthen the work further.

1.  **Clarity on Gradient Flow:** Equation 5 and the surrounding text are slightly misleading. The equation presents the Jacobian as being either an identity matrix or `H'` depending on the gate output being exactly 0 or 1. However, since the transform gate `T` uses a sigmoid function, its output is always in the open interval (0, 1). The actual Jacobian is a smooth interpolation between these two extremes. The authors should correct this description to accurately reflect how gradients are computed during backpropagation to avoid any potential confusion for the reader.

2.  **Initialization Heuristic:** The initialization of the transform gate bias `b_T` is a key component of the training procedure, yet it is presented as a somewhat ad-hoc heuristic (e.g., values of -1, -2, -3 for depths 10, 20, 30). The paper would be more rigorous if it included an analysis of this hyperparameter's sensitivity. A plot of final performance versus the initial bias value would be very informative and would help substantiate the intuition that this bias acts as a regularizer that encourages "carry" behavior early in training.

3.  **Rigor in Comparisons:** The comparisons to other methods, such as FitNets, should be framed more carefully. The authors claim similar or better performance, but it is not explicitly stated whether the experimental settings (e.g., data augmentation, learning rate schedules) are identical. To make the claims fully transparent and verifiable, the authors should either confirm that the settings are the same or clearly list any differences.

4.  **Reporting of Results:** The paper states that results are reported as "Best (mean ± std.dev.) based on 5 runs wherever available." However, this practice is not applied consistently, particularly for competing methods. For scientific rigor and fair comparison, all reported results should include mean and standard deviation across multiple runs.

5.  **Potential for Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is primarily empirical, but the model has interesting theoretical properties that could be briefly discussed. The gating mechanism allows a layer to conditionally approximate an identity function, effectively letting the network learn its own functional depth for a given input. A short discussion from a learning-theoretic perspective on how this property might ease optimization could add another layer of depth to the contribution.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a significant and impactful contribution to the field. The proposed highway network architecture is novel, intuitive, and empirically shown to be highly effective at solving the problem of training very deep networks. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the idea, the strong empirical results, and the insightful analysis of the learned model—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarity and reporting that can be addressed in a final version. The work is likely to be highly influential and inspire a great deal of follow-up research. I recommend acceptance.
