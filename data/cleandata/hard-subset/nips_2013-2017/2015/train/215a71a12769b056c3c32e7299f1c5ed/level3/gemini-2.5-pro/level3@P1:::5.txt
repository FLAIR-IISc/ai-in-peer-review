This paper addresses the significant and persistent challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The central contribution is the introduction of "highway networks," a novel architecture inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The authors propose augmenting standard feedforward layers with adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates, which learn to regulate the flow of information. This allows information to pass unimpeded across many layers (on "information highways"), effectively mitigating the vanishing gradient problem that plagues deep plain networks. The authors demonstrate experimentally that highway networks with depths up to 100 layers can be trained directly and efficiently using standard SGD, a feat that is difficult or impossible for conventional networks of similar depth. The analysis provides compelling evidence that these gates learn data-dependent routing strategies, dynamically adjusting the effective depth of the network based on the input.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of applying an LSTM-like gating mechanism to deep feedforward architectures is, to my knowledge, novel and highly significant. It provides a simple, elegant, and effective solution to a well-known and important problem. While skip connections have been used before, the adaptive, data-dependent nature of the gates is a crucial and powerful innovation. This work directly enables the exploration of network architectures that are orders of magnitude deeper than what was previously feasible with direct SGD training, opening up new avenues for research. The authors are to be congratulated for being the first to successfully demonstrate this connection.

2.  **Quality of Experiments and Analysis:** The paper is technically sound and supported by a strong set of experiments.
    *   The optimization experiments in Section 3.1 provide a clear and convincing demonstration of the proposed method's primary advantage, showing that highway networks do not suffer from performance degradation with increasing depth, unlike plain networks.
    *   The comparison to FitNets is particularly strong, showing that highway networks can achieve comparable or better performance without the need for a complex two-stage training procedure.
    *   The analysis in Section 4 is a major strength. The visualizations of gate biases and activities provide valuable insight into the network's learned behavior. The layer lesioning study (Figure 4) is particularly insightful, demonstrating that the network learns to utilize different effective depths for problems of varying complexity (MNIST vs. CIFAR-100). This goes beyond simply reporting state-of-the-art results and contributes to a deeper understanding of the model.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the highway network formulation is presented concisely, and the experiments are well-described. The connection to LSTMs is explained well and serves as a strong conceptual anchor.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper has no major flaws. My comments below are intended as points for discussion and suggestions for future work that could further strengthen the contribution.

1.  **Alternative Conceptual Framing:** The paper frames its contribution primarily in terms of enabling greater *depth*. An alternative perspective is that a highway layer constitutes a richer, more complex computational block than a standard affine-plus-nonlinearity layer. In this view, a highway network might be seen not just as "deeper" but as a sequence of more powerful, adaptive modules. This framing connects the work to other efforts that build more complex "sub-layers" or "micro-architectures," such as the Inception module in GoogLeNet or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG that create a larger effective receptive field. It would be interesting for the authors to briefly discuss their work in this context. Does a highway network learn something fundamentally different than a plain network with fewer, but wider or more complex, layers?

2.  **Potential for Further Analysis:** The finding that the network utilizes fewer layers for MNIST than for CIFAR-100 is fascinating. This suggests a compelling experiment: could a single "thin-and-tall" highway network be trained jointly on both tasks? For instance, one could train a shared network trunk with separate classification heads for MNIST and CIFAR-100. It would be illuminating to analyze the gate activation patterns for MNIST inputs versus CIFAR-100 inputs within this single, multi-task network. Would the inference patterns for each task mirror those seen in the separately trained networks? This would provide powerful evidence for dynamic, task-dependent routing and could shed light on how efficiently such a deep hierarchy can share parameters across tasks of varying difficulty.

3.  **Scalability to Larger Datasets:** The experiments on MNIST and the CIFAR datasets are well-executed and sufficient to demonstrate the core principles of the architecture. However, the ultimate test for a new architecture is its performance on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. While this is likely beyond the scope of a conference paper revision, demonstrating that this approach scales and achieves competitive performance in that regime would significantly amplify the paper's impact and encourage wider adoption.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a simple, well-motivated, and effective architectural innovation. The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism convincingly solves the problem of training very deep feedforward networks. The experimental evidence is strong, and the analysis of the learned gate behavior is insightful. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance, and is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
