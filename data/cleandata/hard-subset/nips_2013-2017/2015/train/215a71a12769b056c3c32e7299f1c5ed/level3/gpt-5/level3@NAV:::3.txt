Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that borrows LSTM-style gating (transform and carry gates) to enable unimpeded information flow across many layers. By biasing transform gates negatively at initialization, layers default to carrying inputs, mitigating vanishing/exploding gradients and easing optimization with plain SGD. The authors demonstrate successful training up to 100 layers on MNIST and CIFAR, with competitive generalization and insightful analyses of information routing and layer importance. This builds on and contrasts with prior efforts in NIPS and elsewhere: normalized/variance-preserving initialization (Glorot, He), better optimizers (Martens et al.), local competition/maxout, skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and teacher-student distillation (FitNets). The key novelty is data-dependent, learnable gating for depth, rather than fixed shortcuts or staged supervision.

Quality: Technically sound with clear claims supported by controlled optimization studies across depths, competitive results under standard augmentation, and careful analyses (gate activity, class-conditional routing, layer lesioning). The paper would benefit from a direct comparison to plain networks with identity-initialized layers plus small noise (near-identity residual mapping) to disentangle the benefits of data-dependent gates versus fixed identity shortcuts. Similarly, an ablation of C=1−T vs learning C independently, and reporting gradient norms across depth, would strengthen the case.

Clarity: Exceptionally well-written and easy to follow; code availability is appreciated. Specific correction: in Section 4.1, the sentence should read “most transform blocks are not active on average” (rather than “are active”), to match the observed sparsity.

Originality: High. Adapting LSTM-style gating to very deep feedforward nets is a novel and well-motivated idea that goes beyond prior skip connections and distillation. Please also explicitly compare transform gates to the simpler identity-initialized layers-with-noise scheme, clarifying how adaptive, input-dependent routing differs from a fixed near-identity initialization.

Significance: Strong. Demonstrates direct SGD training of very deep networks, provides compelling analyses of routing and layer importance, and offers a practical recipe (gate biasing) likely to influence future architectures.

Pros for acceptance:
- Original, well-motivated architecture enabling training up to 100 layers
- Strong empirical evidence and insightful analyses (routing, lesioning)
- Clear writing, open code, and solid methodological care

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing head-to-head with identity-initialized plain/residual-like baselines
- Limited ablations on gate design and gradient diagnostics

Overall: Excellent paper and a pleasure to read; I recommend acceptance.
