This paper introduces the concept of "highway networks," a new architecture designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep neural networks. The authors take inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and use adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow, allowing unimpeded information flow across many layers. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the learned networks' gate-output patterns and demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) without suffering from increasing depth.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the area of deep learning. The authors discuss the importance of network depth in achieving state-of-the-art results in various machine learning tasks and highlight the challenges of training very deep networks. They also review existing approaches to addressing these challenges, including the use of better optimizers, initialization strategies, and skip connections.

The strengths of the paper include its well-described idea, fairly convincing experiments, and strong analysis of learned networks' gate-output patterns. The authors provide a clear explanation of the highway network architecture and its components, including the gate unit, transform gate, and carry gate. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep networks without sacrificing ease of training or generalization ability.

However, there are some weaknesses and concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the use of a sigmoid function in the gate unit T means that the outputs are never exactly 0/1, which affects the gradients in equation 5. The authors must explain exactly how backpropagation is implemented in this case. Secondly, the initialization of the gate unit biases with constant negative values by depth is unsatisfying, and the authors should provide a plot of performance vs initial bias to demonstrate the effect of this regularization. Additionally, the comparative claims made in the paper should be clarified, and the authors should state the differences in experimental settings between their work and recent studies.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the highway network architecture and its components, and the experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner. However, some of the notation and terminology used in the paper may be unfamiliar to non-experts, and the authors could provide more explanations or references to help readers understand these concepts.

The originality of the paper is high, as it introduces a new architecture for training very deep neural networks. The use of adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow is a novel approach, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in training very deep networks.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it addresses a major challenge in deep learning and provides a new approach to training very deep neural networks. The results of the paper have the potential to impact the field of deep learning and inspire new research in this area.
