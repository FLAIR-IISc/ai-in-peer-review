This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, called highway networks, designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep networks. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and propose an adaptive gating mechanism to regulate the information flow across layers. This mechanism allows for unimpeded information flow, creating "information highways" that enable the training of extremely deep networks using simple gradient descent.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and its underlying principles. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the learned networks' gate-output patterns, which helps to understand the behavior of the highway networks. The experiments are fairly convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep networks with hundreds of layers.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, the authors use a sigmoid function for the gate unit, which means that the outputs are never exactly 0 or 1. This raises questions about the correctness of the gradients in Equation 5, and the authors should provide a clear explanation of how backpropagation is implemented in this case.

Another concern is the initialization of the biases in the transform gates. The authors use a constant negative bias, which is unsatisfying and may not be optimal. It would be helpful to provide a plot of performance vs. initial bias, as the bias acts like a regularizer favoring "longer highways."

The comparative claims in the paper could be clarified by stating the differences between the experimental settings used in recent studies and those used in this paper. This would allow for a more direct comparison between the results.

Furthermore, the authors should report the mean and standard deviation of the results for every method, rather than just the maximum accuracy. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the performance of the highway networks.

Finally, it would be interesting to include a learning-theory analysis of the gate units, which allow layers to act as conditional identities and enable inputs to propagate through many selective layers. This could provide further insights into the behavior of the highway networks and their potential applications.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. However, some of the concerns mentioned above could be addressed to further improve the paper.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The originality of the paper lies in the proposed highway network architecture, which is a novel combination of familiar techniques. The paper adequately references related work and provides a clear explanation of how it differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to advance the state of the art in deep learning. The results are important, and other researchers may use these ideas or build upon them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and provides a unique approach to training very deep networks.
