This paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces input-dependent gating (transform gate T and carry gate C=1−T) inspired by LSTMs to enable information “highways” across many layers. The key idea is to allow each layer to either transform its input or carry it forward, learned via sigmoid gates, which facilitates gradient flow and eases optimization at extreme depths. The authors provide (1) controlled optimization studies on MNIST showing that plain networks degrade as depth grows while highway networks with 50–100 layers remain trainable and converge faster; (2) convolutional highway results on MNIST/CIFAR-10/100 competitive with fitnets and contemporary baselines at comparable parameter/compute budgets, trained in a single stage; and (3) analyses of gate activations, data-dependent routing, and lesioning, indicating that early layers dominate on simple tasks (MNIST), while many layers contribute on harder tasks (CIFAR-100). Relative to prior work, this unifies ideas from LSTMs [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber], skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision [e.g., Raiko et al., Lee et al.], and teacher-student distillation [Romero et al.], but differs by learning multiplicative, input-dependent routing rather than adding fixed shortcuts or external guidance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The training results are convincing and the analyses are thoughtful. However, there is a critical clarity gap in the gradient description. Equation 5 states dy/dx = I or H′ when T is 0 or 1, but because T=σ(·) ∈ (0,1) and depends on x, the true Jacobian must include terms from the dependence of T on x (and from the product rule): for y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), dy/dx contains J_H·diag(T) + diag(H)·J_T + I − diag(T) − diag(x)·J_T. Please provide the exact backprop expressions used (including gradients wrt x, WH, WT, and bT) and discuss their numerical stability. As written, Eq. 5 is only an intuition for limiting cases but may mislead readers about the flow of gradients through gates.
- Empirical evaluation: The optimization study is careful (matched parameter counts, random hyperparameter search, multiple depths) and the learned-mechanism analyses are a real strength. For CIFAR experiments, the comparisons to fitnets are helpful, but the reporting and setup need to be more rigorous (see below).
- Reproducibility: Code availability is a plus. Please add explicit training schedules, number of epochs, data augmentation details, and exact model specs for all reported results in the main paper or appendix.

Clarity
- The architectural description is clear and self-contained, and figures effectively illustrate gating behavior and routing. The gradient presentation needs correction (above).
- Initialization: The guidance to set negative gate biases (depth-dependent) is plausible, but currently heuristic. Since bT acts like a regularizer favoring “longer highways” (more carry), please provide a sensitivity analysis (plot/test error and training stability vs initial bT across depths and datasets) and discuss failure modes at extreme values. This will make the recipe more actionable and principled.
- Comparative claims: For each external comparison (e.g., fitnets), clearly state whether the data preprocessing, augmentation, training budget (epochs, learning-rate schedule), parameter count, and multiply-add budget match those in the cited work. If not, specify the differences so the committee can assess fairness.

Originality
- While inspired by LSTM gating and related to skip connections, the application of input-dependent, multiplicative gates to feedforward depth with a simple training protocol is novel at the time of writing. The diagnostic analyses (class-conditional gating patterns, lesioning) provide new insight into depth utilization and conditional computation.

Significance
- The ability to train networks with tens to hundreds of layers using plain SGD is important and timely. The results suggest highway gating can subsume both identity mapping and learned transformation, enabling depth without delicate tricks. The work is likely to influence subsequent architectures and practice, especially for thin-but-deep regimes and conditional computation.

Strengths
- Clear, motivated architecture bridging LSTM gating with deep feedforward nets.
- Strong optimization evidence across depths; single-stage training where prior work used teacher hints.
- Insightful analyses (gate bias dynamics, data-dependent routing, lesioning showing task-dependent depth usage).
- Public code and generally careful methodology.

Weaknesses and requests for improvement
- Gradients/Jacobian: Provide the full backpropagation formulas and clarify Eq. 5 as a limiting intuition; discuss gradient norms through T and H and any stabilizations used.
- Initialization: Add ablations quantifying sensitivity to initial bT and depth; consider framing bT as a prior over effective path length and discussing annealing schedules.
- Reporting: Do not report max accuracies alone; provide mean ± std for all methods and settings you report (including your own and reproduced baselines), with the number of runs.
- Comparability: Clearly align or differentiate experimental settings vs. prior work (augmentation, epochs, compute/parameter budgets). If you reimplement baselines, document any deviations.
- Theory: A lightweight learning-theoretic treatment of gating would strengthen the work. For example, analyze how gating enables conditional identities and bounds the effective path length and gradient attenuation; relate to conditional computation and mixture-of-experts. Even stylized results (e.g., deep linear H with sigmoid T) would be valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a central challenge (training very deep nets) with a simple, effective mechanism.
- Demonstrates both optimization benefits and competitive generalization without complex training tricks.
- Provides novel, interpretable analyses of how depth is used and routed.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing/incorrect gradient exposition may confuse readers; needs correction.
- Initialization scheme is heuristic without sufficient sensitivity analysis.
- Some comparisons and reporting lack full statistical rigor and exact setting alignment.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution. With the requested clarifications (especially on gradients) and more rigorous reporting/ablations, the paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
