## Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper presents a compelling approach to training very deep feedforward networks, dubbed “highway networks,” inspired by the gating mechanisms found in LSTMs. The core idea – allowing information to flow unimpeded across many layers via adaptive gating units – directly addresses the vanishing/exploding gradient problems that plague deep network training. The authors demonstrate successful training of networks with up to 100 layers, a significant achievement. This work builds upon previous efforts to improve deep network training through better optimizers (e.g., [13, 14, 15]), initialization strategies ([16, 17]), and skip connections ([22, 23, 24]), offering a novel architectural solution.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The experimental validation is thorough, including comparisons to plain networks, FitNets [25], and state-of-the-art methods on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The analysis of information routing and layer importance (Sections 4.1 and 4.2) is particularly insightful, providing a deeper understanding of how these networks function. The visualizations in Figure 2 and 4 are especially helpful. I agree with the author's correction regarding Section 4.1; changing "most transform blocks are active on average" to "not active on average" is accurate and improves clarity.

One potential area for improvement would be a more direct comparison to identity-initialized layers with added noise. While the authors mention initialization strategies, explicitly contrasting highway networks with this simpler approach – and explaining why the transform gates offer an advantage – would strengthen the argument for their architectural contribution. It’s not immediately clear *how* the gating mechanism differs from simply allowing gradients to flow more easily through identity mappings. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network architecture is a genuinely new approach to tackling the depth problem.
*   **Significance:** Successfully training networks with 100 layers is a substantial result with potential implications for various applications.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, well-supported by experiments, and demonstrates a clear understanding of related work.
*   **Clarity:** The writing is excellent, making the complex ideas accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   None significant. The suggested improvement regarding comparison to identity initialization is a minor point.

**Overall:** This is a great paper and a pleasure to read. It presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning and is highly recommended for acceptance to NIPS.
