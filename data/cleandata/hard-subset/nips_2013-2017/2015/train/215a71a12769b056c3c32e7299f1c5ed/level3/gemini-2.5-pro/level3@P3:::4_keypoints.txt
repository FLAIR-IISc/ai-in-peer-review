- Problem addressed: enables gradient-based training of very deep feedforward networks via a novel architecture called Highway Networks.  
- Core mechanism: introduces learnable transform and carry gates (inspired by LSTM) that combine a layer's non-linear transform with the input to create information highways.  
- Impact claim: mitigates vanishing gradients and allows direct training of networks with hundreds of layers using simple SGD.  
- Relation to prior work: generalizes fixed skip connections and complements improved initialization, optimizers, and deep supervision approaches like FitNets.  
- Empirical evidence: strong controlled experiments on MNIST and CIFAR showing highway nets train to 100+ layers while plain nets fail.  
- Comparison to FitNets: matches or outperforms FitNets without two-stage hint-based training, demonstrating practical simplicity.  
- Performance: CIFAR-10/100 results competitive with state-of-the-art methods of the time.  
- Originality: novel and principled adaptation of LSTM gating to feedforward architectures, offering data-dependent routing.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, with clear motivation, equations, and presentation of experimental setup and results.  
- Weakness — analysis scope: visualizations and lesion studies rely on single best models; would benefit from aggregated statistics and broader architecture/task exploration.  
- Weakness — theory: lacks formal theoretical analysis of gradient dynamics and why gating prevents vanishing gradients.  
- Question — gate bias behavior: observed decreases in transform gate biases raise whether negative bias initialization is a useful prior rather than just a training trick.  
- Suggested experiment: probe effects of forcing a layer's transform gates fully open (T=1) to separate importance of highway path vs transformation.  
- Missing detail: brief discussion of performance trade-offs for a more complex LSTM-like block explored in pilot experiments would be informative.  
- Overall conclusion: excellent, novel, and impactful paper with strong empirical support and minor analysis/theoretical gaps; recommended accept.
