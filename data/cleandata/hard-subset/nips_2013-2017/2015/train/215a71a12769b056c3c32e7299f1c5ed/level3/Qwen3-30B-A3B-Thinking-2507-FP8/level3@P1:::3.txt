

The paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture enabling direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via LSTM-inspired adaptive gating units. This work directly addresses a core challenge in deep learning: the degradation in optimization performance as network depth increases, which has historically limited empirical exploration of very deep architectures. While skip connections (e.g., [22, 23]) and layer-wise pretraining [26, 27] have been proposed to mitigate gradient flow issues, highway networks provide a unified, end-to-end trainable solution that avoids two-stage procedures. The approach builds on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) principles [29, 30] but adapts them to feedforward networks, using transform gates to regulate information flow across layers. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained via simple SGD without specialized initialization beyond a negative bias for transform gates, contrasting with prior work requiring complex initialization schemes [16, 17] or auxiliary training stages [25].

The paper excels in quality and clarity. The technical exposition is precise, with clear notation and a well-motivated derivation of the highway layer (Equation 3). The experiments are thorough and well-designed: on MNIST, 100-layer highway networks achieve near-identical performance to 10-layer plain networks (mean loss < 0.01 vs. 0.0001), while plain networks degrade by two orders of magnitude. On CIFAR-10/100, highway networks match or exceed FitNet [25] results in a single training stage, even with comparable parameter counts. The analysis of information routing (Section 4.1) is particularly compelling: transform gates exhibit input-dependent activity (sparse per-sample, but active on average), revealing dynamic routing—not static shortcuts—during inference. The layer importance study (Figure 4) further demonstrates that highway networks utilize depth adaptively: for complex tasks like CIFAR-100, removing early layers degrades performance, while MNIST networks idle ~60% of layers, confirming depth is problem-dependent.

Originality is strong. While skip connections [22, 23] and distilled training [25] exist, highway networks uniquely integrate gating into the core architecture, enabling *direct* training of arbitrary depth. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work: unlike identity-initialized layers (which require exact initialization to preserve gradients [17]), highway gates *learn* to regulate flow. The paper should explicitly contrast this in Section 2.2 (e.g., "Unlike identity mappings, which rely on precise initialization to avoid gradient collapse, highway gates adaptively control information flow via learned parameters"). This clarification would strengthen the contribution claim, as the current draft implies the gating mechanism is merely a more flexible identity mapping.

Significance is high. Highway networks enable the first empirical study of *extreme* depth (100+ layers) in standard feedforward networks, resolving a key bottleneck in deep learning. This opens avenues for investigating depth-accuracy trade-offs without architectural compromises. The results on CIFAR-100 (Table 3) show highway networks achieve state-of-the-art accuracy with minimal data augmentation, proving they generalize well. Crucially, the architecture is agnostic to activation function or transformation type (e.g., convolutional), broadening applicability.

Minor weaknesses include a misstatement in Section 4.1: "most transform gates are active on average" should read "most transform gates are *not* active on average" (per the key points). This correction is essential for accuracy. Additionally, the comparison to identity-initialized layers (a simpler alternative) is underdeveloped. While the paper notes negative bias initialization, it does not rigorously test whether highway gates offer *more* than a biased identity mapping (e.g., by comparing highway networks with identity-initialized gates to standard highway networks). Addressing this would further validate the gating mechanism’s necessity.

Overall, this is a high-quality, impactful contribution. The idea is novel, the experiments are rigorous and well-analyzed, and the significance for deep learning research is clear. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear figures (e.g., Figure 2’s information highway visualization) and concise explanations. It directly advances the state of the art by making "extremely deep" networks practical, moving beyond the limitations of prior approaches that required two-stage training or sacrificed depth. The minor correction and clarification on identity initialization are easily addressed in revision.

Pro: Original architecture enabling direct training of 100+ layer networks; compelling experiments on MNIST/CIFAR; insightful analysis of information routing and layer importance; clear writing; significant impact on deep learning practice.  
Con: Section 4.1 contains a misstatement; insufficient contrast with identity-initialized layers.  

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: technically sound (with minor editorial fixes), exceptionally clear, highly original, and significant for enabling deeper architectures. It is a pleasure to recommend for acceptance.
