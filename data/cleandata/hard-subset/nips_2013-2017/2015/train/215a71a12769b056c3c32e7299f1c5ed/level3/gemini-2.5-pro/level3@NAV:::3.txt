This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel feedforward architecture that enables the training of networks with extreme depth (e.g., 100 layers) using standard stochastic gradient descent. The core idea, inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to incorporate adaptive gating units that regulate information flow. These gates allow signals to pass unimpeded through "information highways," effectively mitigating the vanishing and exploding gradient problems that have historically hindered the training of very deep networks. This work provides an elegant architectural solution to a long-standing challenge in the field, previously addressed through complex initialization schemes or layer-wise training.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The adaptation of LSTM-style gating for deep feedforward networks is a highly original and significant contribution. It provides a practical and effective method for training networks of unprecedented depth, unlocking new possibilities for studying the relationship between depth and performance. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by other researchers.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with claims thoroughly supported by rigorous experiments on MNIST and CIFAR datasets. The authors present a compelling case by directly comparing highway networks to "plain" networks, which fail to train at comparable depths. The analysis in Section 4, particularly the investigation into dynamic information routing and the layer lesion study, provides valuable insights into the network's learned behavior and is a highlight of the paper.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clear, and well-organized. The core concepts are explained intuitively, making the work accessible and a pleasure to read.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The paper would be strengthened by a comparison to a simpler baseline. For instance, comparing the proposed learned gating mechanism to plain networks with fixed, identity-initialized skip connections (perhaps with added noise) would help clarify the specific benefits of the adaptive, data-dependent routing provided by the transform gates.
*   There appears to be a minor typo in Section 4.1. The text states, "...most transform gates are active on average," but the accompanying analysis seems to suggest the opposite—that gates are selective and often inactive on average. Perhaps this should read "are *not* active on average"? Clarification would be helpful.

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-executed idea. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It clearly advances the state of the art in training deep neural networks. I strongly recommend acceptance.
