Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces LSTM-style gating into very deep networks: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), with transform (T) and carry (1 − T) gates. With simple negative-bias initialization of T, the model creates “information highways” that preserve both activations and gradients across depth. The authors demonstrate successful direct training (plain SGD with momentum) of fully connected and convolutional networks up to at least 100 layers, and provide insightful analyses of information routing and layer importance.

The work is well-situated relative to prior art: it is inspired by LSTM gating for long-range credit assignment in recurrent nets, builds on variance-preserving initializations for deep feedforward nets [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], and compares to depth-assisting strategies such as maxout/locally competitive units, skip connections/deep supervision, layerwise pretraining, and teacher–student “FitNets.” The key novelty is input-dependent, learnable gating that generalizes fixed shortcut connections and allows dynamic routing, addressing exploding/vanishing gradients in very deep feedforward networks.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and correct, the initialization rationale is sensible (negative gate bias induces near-identity Jacobians early in training), and the experiments support the claims. The optimization study is convincing: plain thin networks degrade sharply beyond ~20 layers, while highway networks retain good trainability up to 100 layers and converge faster. CIFAR-10/100 results, achieved without aggressive augmentation, show that highway networks generalize competitively while being significantly deeper than comparable baselines. The qualitative analyses (gate statistics, lesioning) are particularly compelling and consistent with the design motivation.
- Empirical completeness: The paper includes MNIST optimization sweeps, CIFAR comparisons to FitNets, and ablations on gating behavior. Two omissions that would strengthen the case are (1) a direct comparison to identity-initialized layers (with small noise) or fixed skip connections, and (2) a cost analysis (parameter/compute overhead due to gates) at equal accuracy.
- Honesty and care: The claims are measured (the goal is trainability, not SOTA), code/hyperparameters are shared, and limitations are acknowledged.

Clarity
The paper is very well written and organized. The layer equations, training setup, and figures are clear. The analyses in Sections 4.1–4.2 are exemplary in illuminating dynamic routing and the contribution of depth. One correction:
- Section 4.1: based on Figure 2 and the accompanying discussion of strongly negative biases and sparse per-example activity, the sentence “most transform gates are active on average” should read “most transform gates are not active on average.” The single-example panels show selective activity; the mean activity is low overall (especially with more negative biases), which supports the “selective routing” interpretation.

Originality
The application of LSTM-style gating to arbitrarily deep feedforward networks is original and, to my knowledge, first at this scale. While related to prior skip/shortcut ideas, the learned, input-dependent gating and the simple but effective initialization are novel twists that make the very deep regime directly trainable.

Significance
Enabling direct end-to-end training of 50–100+ layer networks is a substantial contribution. The work addresses a core obstacle (credit assignment across depth), introduces a general architectural principle (gated highways) applicable to FC and conv layers, and provides tools to analyze depth utilization. I expect the ideas to be widely adopted and extended.

Strengths
- Clear, elegant architecture that directly targets vanishing/exploding gradients.
- Demonstrates training up to 100 layers with standard SGD; faster convergence than plain nets.
- Thoughtful analyses: data-dependent routing and lesioning convincingly show learned depth usage.
- High-quality exposition; code and hyperparameters provided.
- Competitive CIFAR-10/100 performance with thin, deep models trained in a single stage.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Comparison to identity-initialized layers and fixed shortcuts: Please add experiments contrasting highway networks with (a) plain layers initialized to identity plus small noise (for both FC and conv), and (b) fixed additive skip connections (y = H(x) + x) with standard initialization. This would clarify how transform gates differ from a simple identity-preserving scheme. Key distinctions to articulate/measure:
  - Input-dependent modulation: T(x) enables dynamic routing per example and per unit, whereas identity-initialization is static and cannot adapt routing.
  - Gradient control: With T ≈ 0, the Jacobian is near identity regardless of H; identity init only preserves signals at initialization and can quickly drift without an explicit mechanism.
  - Capacity/regularization: Vector-valued gates enable fine-grained depth selection; identity skips force uniform propagation.
  - Robustness to depth and optimization hyperparameters.
- Report compute/parameter overhead and wall-clock for highway vs. plain/skip baselines at matched accuracy.
- Minor ablation: effect of coupling C = 1 − T vs. learning C independently; per-layer scalar gates vs. per-unit gates.

Pro and con arguments
Pros:
- Addresses a central challenge (training very deep nets) with a principled, general solution.
- Strong empirical evidence across datasets; insightful analyses of learned routing and layer utility.
- Simple to implement; minimal tuning beyond gate bias; reproducibility emphasized.
- High clarity and originality.

Cons:
- Lacks direct comparisons to identity-initialized or fixed-skip baselines, which would isolate the benefit of input-dependent gates.
- Limited theoretical analysis beyond intuition for gradient flow.
- No detailed compute/parameter trade-off analysis.

Minor comments and typos
- Section 3.3.1: “backpropogation” → “backpropagation.”
- Please clarify how gate bias schedules were chosen across depths (e.g., sensitivity to bT).
- Consider adding a brief note on stability when gates saturate (training dynamics for very negative bT).

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. This is an original, carefully executed, and clearly presented paper that makes training very deep networks practical. The routing and lesioning experiments are especially insightful. The paper was a pleasure to read.
