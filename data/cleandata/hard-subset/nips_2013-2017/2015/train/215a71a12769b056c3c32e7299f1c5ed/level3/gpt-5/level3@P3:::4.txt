Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Highway Networks, a generalization of feedforward architectures that adds adaptive, learned “information highways” via elementwise transform and carry gates. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where T(x) is a sigmoid gate and H may be convolutional or fully connected. Inspired by LSTM gating, the authors propose initializing transform gate biases to negative values to encourage carry behavior early in training, enabling very deep networks to propagate signals and gradients. Experiments show that highway networks of tens to hundreds of layers are trainable with vanilla SGD and achieve strong generalization.

The work relates to: (1) skip connections and deep supervision used to improve information flow and credit assignment; (2) teacher–student approaches (FitNets) that enable deeper “student” models via distillation; (3) improved initializations and activation functions (e.g., ReLU, variance-preserving schemes) that stabilize moderately deep nets; and (4) LSTM-style gates for long-range credit assignment in time. The novelty lies in bringing LSTM-inspired, input-dependent gating to deep feedforward nets, yielding per-unit, per-example routing rather than fixed skip connections or auxiliary losses.

Strengths
- Clear idea and principled design. The T/C gating formulation elegantly interpolates between identity mapping and non-linear transformation, with an intuitive LSTM-inspired initialization strategy.
- Solid empirical evidence. Across MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, very deep highway networks converge faster, are easier to optimize as depth increases, and match or surpass comparable-capacity baselines (including FitNets) without multi-stage distillation.
- Careful optimization study. The MNIST depth sweep isolates optimization behavior, showing stark degradation for plain nets vs. stability for highway nets to 100 layers under matched capacity and initialization.
- Insightful analyses. Gate activity visualizations, class-conditional routing differences, and layer “lesion” experiments provide evidence that gating is data-dependent and that depth is utilized adaptively (many layers contribute on CIFAR-100, fewer on MNIST).
- Significance. By enabling direct training of increasingly deep networks with simple SGD, the approach is broadly applicable and likely to influence architecture design in vision and beyond.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations against alternative cross-layer schemes. A direct comparison to fixed identity shortcuts (e.g., y = H(x) + x) and to deep supervision under the same budgets would contextualize gains attributable to adaptive gating vs. any skip.
- Incomplete analysis of design choices. The paper fixes C = 1 − T; the benefits of learning C separately, tying/untied gates, or sharing gates across channels/positions are not explored. Sensitivity to the transform-gate bias is only partly reported.
- Scope of datasets. Results are strong on CIFAR/MNIST; scaling to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) is not demonstrated here, leaving open questions about performance/efficiency at scale.
- Computational overhead reporting. The extra parameters and compute for T (and optionally C) are not quantified systematically, nor is the runtime/memory overhead relative to plain nets matched for accuracy.

Quality
Technically sound and well executed. Claims about trainability with depth and improved accuracy over comparable-capacity baselines are supported by experiments. The optimization study is well controlled; the generalization results are competitive with prior work under modest augmentation. The analysis section is informative, though it could benefit from more varied ablations (e.g., fixed vs. learned gates, separate C, or removing gates post-training to test redundancy). Reproducibility is aided by public code and fixed hyperparameters across datasets.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The formulation is precise, and training details are sufficient to follow, with practical guidance on gate-bias initialization. Figures effectively illustrate gate behavior and layer importance. The analysis sections could include more varied details or additional illustrations (e.g., gradient norm propagation across depth, histograms of gate saturation) to further support the narrative.

Originality
High. While cross-layer connections and deep supervision were known, the introduction of LSTM-style, input-dependent gating into feedforward depth is a substantive and original step that generalizes prior “fixed” skip techniques. The method is sufficiently distinct and well positioned relative to related work cited.

Significance
High. Enabling stable optimization of very deep networks with simple training procedures is impactful for multiple application domains. The work advances the state of practice by reducing reliance on multi-stage training or specialized architectures and offers a flexible mechanism for depth-wise routing that others can build upon.

Pros
- Elegant, general mechanism for depth-wise information flow with clear LSTM inspiration
- Strong optimization improvements with depth; robust convergence with vanilla SGD
- Competitive or better accuracy vs. FitNets without teacher distillation
- Insightful gate/routing/lesion analyses; clear communication of main messages
- Public code and practical initialization guidance

Cons
- Missing direct comparisons to fixed identity shortcuts and deep supervision under equal budgets
- Limited ablations on gating design choices and sensitivity to gate-bias initialization
- No large-scale dataset evidence; compute/memory overhead not thoroughly quantified

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (a) fixed identity shortcuts (no gates), (b) separate carry gate C, (c) shared vs. unshared gates, (d) removing gates after training. Report impact on optimization and accuracy.
- Quantify overhead: parameters, FLOPs, runtime, and memory vs. plain nets for matched accuracy.
- Include gradient-propagation diagnostics across depth; show how gating affects signal/gradient norms.
- Broaden analysis figures (e.g., saturation distributions, per-layer gate histograms) and add qualitative visualizations for convolutional layers.
- Discuss interplay with normalization layers or other stabilization techniques and whether they are complementary.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the initial transform-gate bias across datasets and depths? Is there a principled way to set bT?
- Did you test separate carry gates (C ≠ 1 − T)? If so, how does it affect optimization/generalization?
- For convolutional gates, are T parameters shared spatially or per-location, and what is the effect on capacity and cost?
- Can you report precise compute and memory overheads for your CIFAR models?
- How far can depth be pushed in practice (e.g., 200–1000 layers) before diminishing returns or optimization issues appear?

Overall evaluation
Strong accept. The paper proposes a novel, well-motivated, and general mechanism for training very deep networks, demonstrates compelling empirical benefits, and provides insightful analyses. While additional ablations and larger-scale experiments would strengthen the case further, the contribution is timely, original, clear, and significant. Confidence: high.
