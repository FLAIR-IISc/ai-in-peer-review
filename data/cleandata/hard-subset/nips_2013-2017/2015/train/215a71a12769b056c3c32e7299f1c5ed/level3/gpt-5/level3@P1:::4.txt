This paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture with learned, LSTM-inspired gates that create “information highways” across many layers. Each layer outputs y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where T(x) = σ(W_T x + b_T) is a transform gate and H is any non-linear transform (fully connected or convolutional). With a simple negative bias initialization for T, the network is initially biased to carry inputs forward, enabling gradient flow and stable optimization at great depth. The authors show that hundreds of layers can be trained directly with SGD, without layerwise pretraining or teacher–student schemes. They evaluate on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, compare to FitNets, and include analyses of gate behavior and layer lesioning. The approach is grounded in prior insights on gating in LSTMs [29,30], improved initializations [16,17], skip connections and deep supervision [22–24], and student–teacher “hints” [25], but differs by learning data-dependent routing at each layer rather than relying on fixed shortcuts or auxiliary losses.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is straightforward and well-justified. The gate parameterization and biasing strategy are sensible and supported by training curves showing that plain networks degrade with depth while highway networks remain optimizable and converge faster. The analyses (gate statistics, data-dependent routing, lesioning) offer insight into what the gates learn.
- Empirical support: Results on MNIST and CIFAR are competitive with or better than depth-matched baselines at similar parameter/multiplication budgets, including single-stage training of thin/deep models comparable to FitNets. Optimization studies convincingly isolate depth effects with matched capacity per layer. Code availability strengthens reproducibility.
- Missing/limited evidence: Evaluation is limited to relatively small benchmarks; no large-scale ImageNet results are shown. Ablations isolating the contribution of gating (vs. simple skip connections), the effect of different gate parameterizations (C independent vs. C=1−T), and the cost/benefit trade-offs of additional parameters are limited. Reporting on compute time and memory beyond parameter counts would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The notation for the layer equation and training setup is easy to follow, and the architectural description for both FC and convolutional variants is adequate.
- The analysis figures are informative (especially the gating activity and lesion studies), but could be expanded with additional qualitative/quantitative examples to illustrate input-dependent routing.
- Minor issues: there are a few typos and duplicated words; the dimensionality-change discussion could be more explicit about when plain layers are inserted and their effect on training.

Originality
- The idea of gating to control information flow is known from LSTMs, and skip connections have precedent, but this work is original in applying adaptive gating to deep feedforward networks to create trainable identity pathways. The mechanism generalizes fixed skip connections by making routing data-dependent.
- The relation to prior NIPS and contemporaneous work (normalized init, rectifiers, deep supervision, FitNets) is adequately cited, and the differences are clearly stated.

Significance
- Enabling stable, single-stage training of very deep networks is important. The method is simple, broadly applicable (FC and conv), and potentially impactful for practitioners constrained by parameter/compute budgets who still want depth.
- The analysis suggests a useful property: the network can utilize depth when needed (e.g., CIFAR-100) and effectively idle layers on simpler tasks (MNIST), which is desirable and hard to obtain with plain architectures.
- Wider significance would be bolstered by results on larger-scale datasets and by stronger comparisons to other depth-enabling techniques (e.g., fixed identity skip connections without gates).

Strengths
- Clear and simple architectural change with strong intuition and a principled initialization.
- Convincing optimization results demonstrating depth robustness (10–100+ layers).
- Competitive CIFAR performance without multi-stage distillation.
- Insightful analyses of gating behavior and layer importance.
- Public code and training details.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation scope limited to small/medium datasets; no ImageNet evidence.
- Comparisons miss ablations against plain skip/identity connections without gates under matched budgets.
- Limited exploration of gate design choices (e.g., independent carry gate, regularization of T, effect sizes of b_T).
- Incomplete reporting on training efficiency (wall-clock, memory), which matters for very deep models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (a) plain residual/skip layers without gates; (b) independent C vs. C=1−T; (c) sensitivity to b_T and activation choices; (d) removing gates after training to assess reliance.
- Report compute and memory metrics and training stability across random seeds.
- Evaluate on an additional dataset (e.g., SVHN) and, if feasible, a scaled ImageNet experiment.
- Provide more visualizations of input-dependent routing and gate sparsity patterns.
- Clarify architectural choices when changing dimensionality and the role of 1×1 convs.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, general mechanism that directly addresses the difficulty of training very deep networks.
- Solid empirical evidence that depth no longer hinders optimization; good generalization on CIFAR.
- Clear writing, useful analyses, and released code increase impact and reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of large-scale validation; uncertain performance at ImageNet scale.
- Missing strong ablation against simpler skip connections may overstate the necessity of gating.
- Some experimental comparisons could be more comprehensive and controlled.

Questions for the authors
- How do highway networks compare head-to-head with identity skip connections without gates under identical depth/width and initialization?
- What is the sensitivity to the transform-gate bias and to using C independent of T?
- Can gates be pruned or quantized post hoc with minimal impact on accuracy?
- What are the training-time and memory overheads relative to plain/residual baselines?

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that materially advances our ability to train very deep architectures. I lean toward acceptance.
