

The paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) using standard SGD. It addresses a critical limitation of plain deep networks—degrading optimization performance with depth—by introducing adaptive gating units inspired by LSTM recurrent networks. The highway layer computes outputs as a weighted combination of transformed inputs and carried inputs: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x)), where T(x) = σ(W^T x + b^T) is the transform gate. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that highway networks overcome the depth barrier observed in plain networks (e.g., 100-layer highway networks outperform similarly sized plain networks by >100x on MNIST), while maintaining strong generalization. This work extends prior concepts like skip connections [22, 23, 24] and FitNets [25] by enabling single-stage training without teacher networks or complex initialization, and it clearly distinguishes itself from LSTMs [29, 30] and related work [31, 32].

The paper excels in **quality** and **clarity**. The technical foundation is sound: the gating mechanism is theoretically grounded (Eq. 4–5), and experiments rigorously compare highway vs. plain networks across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The analysis (Fig. 2–4) provides deep insights into network behavior, showing that transform gates dynamically route information based on input (not static shortcuts), with layer importance varying by task complexity (e.g., MNIST uses ~40% of layers, while CIFAR-100 utilizes nearly all). The empirical results are comprehensive, including ablation studies (e.g., lesioning layers in Fig. 4) and comparisons to state-of-the-art (e.g., Highway networks match or exceed FitNets [25] in single-stage training). The writing is exceptionally clear, with well-structured sections and sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., initialization strategy: b^T = -1 to -3).

**Originality** is strong. While highway networks draw inspiration from LSTMs and skip connections, the *adaptive, input-dependent gating* for feedforward networks is novel. The paper explicitly contrasts with prior work: it avoids FitNets’ two-stage training, solves the depth issue without relying on specialized activation functions (e.g., maxout [20]), and provides a unified architecture that works for both convolutional and fully connected networks. The related work section is thorough, correctly citing LSTM [29, 30], FitNets [25], and the connection to competitive neural networks [38].

**Significance** is high. Highway networks enable the practical exploration of extreme depth—a key direction for deep learning—without compromising ease of training or generalization. The results (e.g., 32-layer highway networks outperforming shallow teacher networks on CIFAR-10) demonstrate immediate applicability. Crucially, the analysis reveals that highway networks learn *data-dependent routing* (Fig. 3), making them more expressive than static skip connections. This opens new avenues for studying layer utilization and depth requirements (Fig. 4), a problem intractable for plain networks.

Weaknesses are minimal. The sole editorial note is a typo ("Tables 3 and 3" on p.5), which is trivial and easily corrected. The paper thoroughly addresses potential concerns: it acknowledges similarities to LSTMs [31, 32] and FitNets [25], clarifies distinctions (e.g., highway gates are adaptive vs. FitNets’ fixed soft targets), and validates the approach across multiple datasets. The experimental setup (e.g., hyperparameter search on CIFAR-10) is robust and well-justified.

**Pro-accept arguments**:  
- Solves a core problem (training depth) with a simple, generalizable architecture.  
- Demonstrates unprecedented depth (100+ layers) with single-stage training.  
- Provides unique insights into network dynamics (dynamic routing, layer importance).  
- Strong, reproducible results across diverse benchmarks.  
- Clear, well-organized writing with comprehensive analysis.  

**Con-accept arguments**:  
- None identified. The work is technically rigorous, novel, and impactful.  

This paper makes a significant contribution to deep learning. It transforms a fundamental limitation (training depth) into an opportunity, providing both a practical solution and deeper theoretical understanding of deep network behavior. The clarity, thoroughness, and novelty align perfectly with NIPS’ mission to advance machine learning science. The minor typo is inconsequential; the paper is otherwise exemplary. I strongly recommend acceptance.
