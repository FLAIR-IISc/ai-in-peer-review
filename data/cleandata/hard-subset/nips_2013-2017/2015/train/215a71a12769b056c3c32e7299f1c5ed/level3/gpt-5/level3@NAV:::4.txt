This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture with learnable “information highways” implemented via LSTM-inspired transform/carry gates that enable gradients and activations to propagate across many layers. The authors relate the idea to gating in LSTMs, skip connections and deep supervision, distillation/teacher-student (FitNets), careful initialization (Glorot/He), and competitive nonlinearities (maxout). Empirically, they demonstrate that highway networks with tens to hundreds of layers are trainable by plain SGD and yield improved optimization and competitive or better classification accuracy on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 under standard augmentation. Analysis sections probe gate activity, input-dependent routing, and layer importance via lesion studies.

Quality: The work is technically sound and presented as a complete contribution. Claims about ease of optimization with depth are well supported by careful comparisons to plain networks under matched parameter budgets and by random search for hyperparameters. The experimental evaluation is solid, with multiple datasets and diagnostic analyses. Some limitations remain: no theoretical characterization of gradient flow, limited ablations (e.g., on the C=1−T constraint, gate bias schedules), and no large-scale benchmark (e.g., ImageNet). It would also help to quantify parameter/FLOP overhead from gating.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The core equations and initialization strategy are precise, and the main messages are easy to follow. The analysis sections could benefit from more varied visualizations and quantitative summaries (e.g., sparsity metrics, per-layer contribution statistics with confidence intervals).

Originality: The approach is a novel and principled generalization of cross-layer/skip connections, introducing adaptive multiplicative gates for dynamic routing. The relation to prior NIPS work is appropriate; authors may wish to discuss contemporaneous residual/identity shortcuts (e.g., He et al., 2015 “Deep Residual Learning”) to further situate the contribution.

Significance: Enabling direct training of very deep networks is impactful. The method is likely to be used and extended by practitioners and researchers, and it advances the state of the art in optimization of depth.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, original idea that generalizes cross-layer techniques with adaptive gating
- Strong empirical evidence for trainability and accuracy gains
- Insightful analyses of gating and layer contributions
- Practical guidance (initialization, code availability)

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing larger-scale evaluation and broader SOTA comparisons
- Limited theoretical analysis and ablation studies
- Overhead of gating not thoroughly characterized
- Analysis visuals could be richer and more quantitative
