Summary
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces adaptive, input-dependent gating to facilitate information flow across many layers. Each layer outputs y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is the usual nonlinear transform and T is a sigmoid “transform gate” (with the complementary carry gate C = 1 − T). Inspired by LSTM gating, the authors initialize gate biases negatively to bias early learning toward carrying, enabling gradients to propagate without attenuation. The paper presents optimization studies showing that, unlike plain deep nets with standard variance-preserving initialization, highway networks maintain trainability as depth increases to 50–100 layers (and reportedly beyond). On MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, thin but deep highway networks trained by standard SGD achieve accuracy competitive with or better than FitNets of comparable parameter/computation budgets, without multi-stage teacher-student training. The paper includes analyses of gate activations, input-dependent routing, and layer lesioning that suggest depth is adaptively used when needed.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on ideas of LSTM gating for preserving information over long temporal dependencies and adapts them to depth. It relates to prior attempts to mitigate depth-related optimization issues: better initialization (Glorot+Bengio; He et al.), novel nonlinearities and local competition (maxout), skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and teacher-student hint training (FitNets). The novelty here is learned, per-layer, input-dependent gating that yields continuous paths of near-identity mappings through arbitrarily many layers, trained end-to-end with standard SGD.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea with strong motivation from LSTMs; the gating equation is easy to implement in fully connected and convolutional settings.
- Convincing optimization evidence: at matched capacity per layer, highway networks train reliably at depths where plain nets struggle; convergence is faster.
- Practical training recipe: negative gate-bias initialization is simple and robust across activations; authors report stability up to >100 layers and pilot success beyond.
- Useful analyses: gate visualizations, class-conditional activity, and lesion studies provide insight into how depth is used and how routing is input-dependent.
- Competitive generalization under constrained budgets, matching or outperforming FitNets on CIFAR-10/100 without teacher hints or multi-stage procedures.
- Reproducibility: code and hyperparameter search details are provided; multiple runs with mean/std are reported.

Weaknesses
- Empirical scope is limited to MNIST and CIFAR; no large-scale benchmark (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate significance at scale.
- Comparisons omit strong baselines that were becoming standard (e.g., batch normalization; more recent initialization/architectures), making it hard to separate the benefits of gating from other modern training aids.
- Overhead and trade-offs are not quantified: gating doubles transform parameters per layer and adds computation; wall-clock training time and memory comparisons are not reported.
- Ablations are limited: the complementary constraint C = 1 − T is assumed; no comparison with independent carry gates, fixed skip connections, or untied gating granularity (per-unit vs per-channel).
- Theoretical analysis is qualitative; apart from Jacobian intuition, there is no formal treatment of gradient flow or depth scaling.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully executed within scope. Claims about trainability with depth are well supported by optimization curves and lesion analyses. Generalization results are competitive for the chosen setting but would benefit from broader baselines (e.g., BN) and larger datasets. Methodological details are largely sufficient, with code available, though explicit reporting of compute and parameter overheads would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The architectural definition, training scheme, and experimental setup are easy to follow. The analyses (Figures 2–4) are informative and explained clearly. It would help to specify gating granularity for conv layers (per-feature-map vs per-position), exact parameter counts per model, and learning-rate schedules in more detail.

Originality
Applying LSTM-style gating to depth with complementary transform/carry gates is a novel and elegant idea that differentiates this work from prior skip-connection and deep supervision approaches. The input-dependent routing interpretation and lesion-based depth-usage study are also original contributions.

Significance
If the reported ease of training scales, highway networks could materially impact how very deep models are built and trained, offering a simple mechanism to maintain gradient flow without complex pretraining or distillation. Even absent large-scale results, demonstrating stable training at 50–100 layers with standard SGD on CIFAR is meaningful. The approach may also inspire further gated/shortcut architectures.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architectural innovation that substantially improves trainability with depth.
- Competitive results under constrained budgets without distillation; insightful analyses of gating and depth usage.
- Reproducible and broadly applicable to FC and conv nets.

Arguments for rejection
- Lack of large-scale experiments and omission of strong contemporary baselines (e.g., batch normalization) limit the strength of empirical claims.
- Limited ablations leave open questions about which components are essential and the cost-benefit trade-off of gating.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons with batch-normalized deep baselines and/or other modern training aids, controlling for parameter count and depth.
- Report wall-clock training time, memory usage, and FLOPs to quantify the overhead of gating, and compare with equivalently parameterized plain/skip models.
- Include ablations: (a) independent carry gate vs C = 1 − T, (b) fixed skip (residual) connections without gates, (c) per-channel vs per-unit gating, (d) different gate bias values/schedules and their effect on gradient norms.
- Extend experiments to a larger dataset (e.g., ImageNet) or at least provide stronger CIFAR baselines (wider/deeper models with current best practices).
- Analyze gradient propagation empirically (e.g., norm statistics across depth during training) to substantiate the “information highway” claim quantitatively.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that addresses a central challenge in deep learning—training very deep networks—through a simple, general architectural mechanism. With broader empirical comparisons and ablations, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
