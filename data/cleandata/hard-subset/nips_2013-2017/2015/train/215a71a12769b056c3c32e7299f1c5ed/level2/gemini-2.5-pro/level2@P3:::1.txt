This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, termed "highway networks," designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward models. The central contribution is a gating mechanism inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. Each layer in a highway network learns to dynamically regulate the flow of information, balancing between transforming its input and carrying it through unmodified. This allows for unimpeded gradient flow across many layers, effectively creating "information highways" that overcome the optimization difficulties, such as vanishing gradients, that plague conventional deep networks. The work demonstrates empirically that highway networks with depths of up to 100 layers can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent, a feat that is shown to be difficult or impossible for traditional "plain" networks of comparable depth.

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a series of controlled experiments. The optimization experiments on MNIST (Section 3.1) provide a direct and convincing comparison between plain and highway networks, clearly illustrating that the proposed architecture does not suffer from performance degradation as depth increases. The experiments on CIFAR-10/100 further demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, showing that highway networks can achieve competitive or superior performance compared to contemporary methods like FitNets, while requiring a simpler, single-stage training procedure.

A particular strength is the analysis section (Section 4), which investigates the internal dynamics of the trained networks. The visualizations of transform gate biases and activations provide valuable insight into how the network learns to route information. The lesion study offers an interesting method for evaluating the contribution of individual layers, suggesting that the network can learn to use its depth adaptively based on task complexity. This analysis elevates the work beyond a simple presentation of a new architecture and performance numbers.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core concept of the highway layer is introduced with a simple and intuitive mathematical formulation (Equation 3). The motivation for the architecture and its connection to LSTMs is explained well. The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner. The provision of public source code is a commendable step towards ensuring reproducibility.

To further improve clarity, the descriptions of the network architectures used in the CIFAR experiments could be more detailed. While the main text provides a high-level overview, a table in an appendix specifying the exact layer configurations (e.g., number of filters, kernel sizes, stride, padding, pooling) would be beneficial for readers aiming to precisely reproduce or build upon these results.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While the idea of skip connections between layers existed in prior work, this paper introduces a crucial innovation: making the information flow data-dependent and learnable through a gating mechanism. The conceptual leap of adapting the temporal gating of LSTMs to the spatial dimension of network depth is novel and insightful. The paper clearly situates its contribution within the context of previous attempts to train deeper networks, such as specialized initialization schemes, new activation functions, and hint-based training, and effectively demonstrates the advantages of its architectural solution.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is substantial. It directly addresses one of the most critical challenges in deep learning at the time: the difficulty of training very deep networks. By providing a simple and effective architectural solution, the paper opens the door to exploring the benefits of extreme depth. The principles demonstrated here—facilitating gradient flow through identity-like mappings—have the potential to heavily influence future research in deep network design. Researchers and practitioners working on a wide range of problems are likely to adopt or build upon these ideas. The finding that the network can learn to effectively use its depth based on problem complexity is an important result that could guide the design of more efficient and adaptive models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution to the fundamental and highly relevant problem of training very deep networks.
*   The effectiveness of the proposed highway network architecture is demonstrated through strong and well-controlled empirical evidence.
*   The analysis of the network's internal behavior provides valuable scientific insight beyond just state-of-the-art performance.
*   The work is well-written, clearly motivated, and has a high potential for significant impact on the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale datasets (MNIST, CIFAR). While sufficient to prove the principle, demonstrating the approach on a larger benchmark like ImageNet would further solidify claims of its practical utility.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The interpretation of the lesion study (Section 4.2) could be slightly more nuanced. The conclusion that certain layers are "idle" for the MNIST task might be an oversimplification. It would be valuable to consider and discuss alternative possibilities, such as the network learning a robust, distributed representation where the contribution of any single layer is small, but the collection of layers is still vital.
*   As noted under Clarity, providing more detailed architectural specifications for the CIFAR models would enhance reproducibility.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant contribution to the field. It introduces an original and effective architecture that tackles a key problem, supported by strong experiments and insightful analysis. The potential impact is high, and the work is a clear candidate for acceptance.
