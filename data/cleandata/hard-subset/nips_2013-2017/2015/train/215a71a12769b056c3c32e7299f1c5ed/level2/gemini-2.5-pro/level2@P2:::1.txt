This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, "Highway Networks," which is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The core idea is to introduce adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates at each layer. These gates dynamically control how much of the input is transformed by the layer's non-linearity versus how much is passed through unchanged. This creates "information highways," allowing unimpeded information and gradient flow across many layers. The authors demonstrate that this architecture allows for the stable training of networks with up to 100 layers (and suggest success with up to 1000) using standard stochastic gradient descent, a feat not achievable with conventional "plain" network architectures of similar depth.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper provides a simple and effective solution to a major roadblock in deep learning research: the difficulty of training truly deep networks. By enabling the direct training of networks with hundreds of layers, this work opens up new possibilities for exploring the role of extreme depth in learning complex representations. The proposed method is general and can be applied to both fully-connected and convolutional layers. This is a highly significant contribution that is likely to be influential.

2.  **Originality:** The primary novelty lies in the elegant adaptation of the gating principle from LSTMs to a feedforward context. While skip connections have been used in the past, the proposed data-dependent, learned gating mechanism is a novel and powerful extension. This provides a more flexible way to control information flow than fixed, architectural shortcuts. The proposed initialization scheme, where gate biases are set to negative values to encourage carry behavior at the start of training, is also a clever and effective contribution.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The claims are very well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments.
    *   The optimization experiment on MNIST (Section 3.1, Figure 1) is a direct and convincing demonstration of the central claim. It clearly shows that while plain networks degrade severely with depth, highway networks maintain their performance and training stability up to 100 layers.
    *   The comparison to FitNets on CIFAR-10 is compelling. The ability to train deep, thin networks in a single stage with SGD—where a more complex, multi-stage "hint-based" procedure was previously required—is a strong practical argument for the proposed architecture.
    *   The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, showing that the architecture not only trains well but also generalizes effectively.

4.  **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, and the architecture is described concisely. Furthermore, the analysis in Section 4 is a major strength. The visualizations of gate activity and the lesioning study (Figure 4) provide fascinating insights into the network's behavior. The demonstration that the network learns to use more layers for a complex task (CIFAR-100) than for a simple one (MNIST) is particularly insightful, suggesting that highway networks can learn an "adaptive depth."

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison with Fixed Skip Connections:** The paper mentions prior work on skip connections but does not directly compare against them. The key innovation here is the *gating* mechanism. A valuable addition would be an ablation study comparing the highway network to a simplified version where the gating is removed and a fixed identity skip connection is used (i.e., `y = H(x, WH) + x`). This would help to isolate the benefit of the adaptive, data-dependent gates versus a simpler, static shortcut, and would further strengthen the claims about the importance of the proposed mechanism.

2.  **Sensitivity to Gate Initialization:** The negative bias initialization for the transform gates is presented as an important component for successful training. It would be helpful to include a brief analysis of the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter. For instance, how does training proceed if the biases are initialized to zero or a positive value? Understanding the robustness of the training process to this initialization would be valuable for practitioners.

3.  **Dimensionality Changes:** The paper states that dimensionality is changed using a plain layer. While this is a practical solution, it feels somewhat disconnected from the core highway formulation. It would be interesting if the authors could comment on potential ways to integrate dimensionality change more elegantly into the highway block itself, for example, by using a projection on the carry path `x`. This is a minor point but could be a fruitful direction for future work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of deep learning. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective architecture that successfully overcomes a major obstacle in training very deep networks. The experiments are thorough, the results are strong, and the analysis provides valuable insights. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to potential additions that would further strengthen an already compelling paper. I strongly recommend acceptance, and believe this work is of sufficient quality and impact for an oral presentation.
