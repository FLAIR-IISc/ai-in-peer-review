

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to enable training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks through adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTM networks. The authors propose a layer transformation where each layer computes an output as a weighted combination of a transformed input and the input itself, controlled by a transform gate. The key contribution is demonstrating that highway networks can be trained directly using SGD even with hundreds of layers, contrasting with plain networks that become increasingly difficult to optimize as depth increases.

The paper presents strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. Experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 show highway networks maintain high accuracy and fast convergence as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly. The 100-layer highway network outperforms similarly-sized plain networks by over two orders of magnitude. The analysis in Section 4 provides valuable insights into how information flows through highway networks, demonstrating that they learn to dynamically route information based on input, rather than using static skip connections.

The paper excels in quality with well-designed experiments, thorough ablation studies (particularly the layer importance analysis in Figure 4), and clear mathematical formulation. The architecture is theoretically grounded with Jacobian analysis showing how highway layers can smoothly transition between identity and transformation behaviors. The writing is clear, well-organized, and adequately supports the main claims.

The work demonstrates significant originality. While inspired by LSTM networks, the application to feedforward networks with adaptive gating is novel. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior approaches to training deep networks, including skip connections, knowledge distillation, and layer-wise training. The paper does not cite ResNets (which were submitted to CVPR 2015 but not yet published), which is understandable, but it would be helpful to acknowledge that similar ideas were emerging around the same time.

The significance of this work is high. Solving the problem of training extremely deep networks directly with simple gradient descent enables exploration of deeper architectures without complex training procedures. The highway architecture provides new insights into how information flows through deep networks, as evidenced by the analysis of transform gate activities. The paper also demonstrates that highway networks can match or exceed state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 while using fewer parameters than competitive approaches.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed. First, the comparison to contemporary architectures is limited. The paper compares to plain networks and FitNets, but doesn't compare to other leading architectures of 2015 like VGG or GoogLeNet, which were achieving state-of-the-art results. This weakens the significance claim, as it's unclear how highway networks would perform against these top approaches.

Second, the paper doesn't adequately address the computational overhead of the highway gates. Each highway layer requires additional parameters for the transform gate, which increases model size and potentially training time. The paper states highway networks "obtain similar or higher accuracy" compared to FitNets, but doesn't quantify the parameter or computational cost increase.

Third, the experimental results on CIFAR-100 (Table 3) show that highway networks don't significantly outperform existing methods, which weakens the significance claim. The paper acknowledges this by stating they "only performed experiments in the more common setting," but doesn't explore how highway networks would perform with more aggressive data augmentation or better hyperparameters.

The paper also lacks thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity. The authors suggest specific bias initialization values (-1, -2, -3) as "general guidelines," but don't demonstrate how performance varies with different bias values or how sensitive the architecture is to this choice.

Finally, the paper's claim that highway networks "allow us to examine how much computation depth is needed" is interesting, but the analysis in Figure 4 is limited to MNIST and CIFAR-100. More comprehensive analysis across multiple datasets would strengthen this claim.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to deep learning. The highway network architecture is well-motivated, properly evaluated, and provides valuable insights into deep network training. While the experimental comparisons could be expanded and the computational overhead better addressed, the core contribution is compelling. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons to contemporary state-of-the-art architectures and a deeper exploration of the computational trade-offs. With these improvements, it would be a highly valuable contribution to the field.
