

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to overcome the challenges of training very deep neural networks. The authors draw inspiration from LSTM recurrent networks, proposing adaptive gating mechanisms (transform and carry gates) that enable information flow across hundreds of layers via "information highways." The key innovation lies in Equation (3), where the output is a weighted combination of transformed input and the input itself, controlled by a gating function. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly via SGD without specialized initialization or two-stage procedures, achieving state-of-the-art results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with significantly deeper architectures than prior work.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The highway architecture is well-motivated and distinct from prior skip connections (e.g., [22, 23]) or knowledge distillation (e.g., [25]). The gating mechanism provides a continuous, learnable alternative to fixed shortcuts. The theoretical analysis (Equations 4–5) clearly explains how highway layers interpolate between identity and transformation, facilitating gradient flow.  
2. **Empirical Rigor:** Experiments comprehensively compare highway networks to plain networks and FitNets across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The 100-layer highway network outperforms similarly sized plain networks by >100× in optimization (Section 3.1), and Highway A/B match or exceed Fitnet accuracy in single-stage training (Table 2). The ablation studies (Figures 2–4) provide valuable insights into information routing and layer importance.  
3. **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The architecture is concisely defined, with clear implementation details (e.g., negative bias initialization for transform gates). Code and hyperparameters are publicly shared, and the paper avoids overclaiming (e.g., noting that highway networks are not universally superior for all tasks).  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Contextualization of Novelty:** While the paper cites related work (e.g., LSTMs [29, 30], skip connections [22, 23]), it does not sufficiently contrast highway networks with contemporaneous approaches like ResNets (He et al., 2015, NIPS), which also used residual connections but without gating. This omission slightly undermines the claim of novelty. *Suggestion:* Add a brief comparison to ResNets in Section 1 or 5 to clarify the distinct mechanism (adaptive gating vs. fixed identity mapping).  
2. **Depth Limitations:** The paper claims "hundreds of layers" can be trained, but experiments max out at 100 layers (Figures 1, 4). For modern contexts, this depth is modest. *Suggestion:* Explicitly state that highway networks enable deeper architectures *relative to prior work* (e.g., FitNets capped at ~5 layers [25]) and acknowledge that ResNets later achieved greater depths (e.g., 152 layers [36]).  
3. **Analysis of Gating Dynamics:** The observation that transform gate biases *increase* during training (Figure 2) is intriguing but lacks deeper interpretation. Why do biases rise despite initial negative values? Does this affect generalization? *Suggestion:* Add a sentence speculating on the implications (e.g., "This suggests gates become more selective over training, aligning with the sparse activity observed in Figure 2, Column 3").  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* High novelty in architecture design, strong empirical validation of training scalability, clear theoretical grounding, and practical impact on enabling deeper networks without complex training procedures.  
*Con:* Minor contextual gap regarding ResNets (a major contemporaneous work), and limited exploration of gating dynamics beyond observations.  

**Overall Recommendation:**  
This is a strong contribution to NIPS, addressing a fundamental challenge in deep learning with a well-executed, novel solution. The paper’s clarity, thorough experiments, and insightful analysis (e.g., Figure 4) make it highly relevant to the community. While the novelty is slightly tempered by the proximity of ResNets (published concurrently), the highway network’s adaptive gating mechanism remains a valuable addition to the toolbox. The weaknesses identified are minor and addressable in a revision. I recommend **acceptance**.  

*Minor suggestions for revision:*  
- Clarify the relationship to ResNets in Section 5 (e.g., "Unlike ResNets’ fixed identity connections, highway networks learn dynamic routing via adaptive gates").  
- In Table 2, specify the parameter count for Highway networks to directly compare with Fitnets.  
- Add a sentence to Figure 2 caption explaining the "gradient" in biases (bottom row, CIFAR-100).
