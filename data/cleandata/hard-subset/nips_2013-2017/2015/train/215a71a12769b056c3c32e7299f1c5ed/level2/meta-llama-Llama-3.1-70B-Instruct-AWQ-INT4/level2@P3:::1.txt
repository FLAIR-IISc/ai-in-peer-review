This paper introduces a new neural network architecture called "highway networks" that allows for the training of very deep networks. The key innovation is the use of adaptive gating units, inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, to regulate the flow of information across layers. This enables the training of networks with hundreds of layers using simple gradient descent.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed architecture, including its mathematical formulation, training procedure, and experimental results on several benchmark datasets. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained to achieve state-of-the-art performance on image classification tasks, while also providing insights into the inner workings of the network.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture that addresses a significant challenge in deep learning.
* The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the mathematical formulation and training procedure.
* The experimental results are thorough and well-presented, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art architectures, such as residual networks.
* Some of the experimental results, such as the performance on CIFAR-100, could be improved with additional hyperparameter tuning.
* The paper could provide more insights into the interpretability of the learned representations and the role of the gating units in the network.

Originality:

* The paper presents a novel architecture that combines elements of LSTM and feedforward networks.
* The use of adaptive gating units to regulate the flow of information across layers is a new and innovative approach.
