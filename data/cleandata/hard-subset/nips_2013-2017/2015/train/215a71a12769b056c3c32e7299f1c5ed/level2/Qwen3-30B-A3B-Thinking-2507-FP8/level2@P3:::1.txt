

The paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks through adaptive gating inspired by LSTM recurrent networks. The core innovation is Equation (3), where each layer's output combines transformed input (via H) and carried input (via T), with T dynamically controlling information flow. This contrasts with prior work on fixed skip connections [22,23,24] and teacher-student distillation [25], as highway networks learn routing mechanisms rather than relying on static paths.

The work demonstrates strong empirical support for its claims. On MNIST, highway networks maintain near-identical performance across 10–100 layers, while plain networks degrade by over two orders of magnitude at 100 layers. On CIFAR-10, highway networks match or exceed Fitnet results in single-stage training, avoiding the two-stage procedure required for plain networks. The analysis in Section 4 provides valuable insights: Figure 2 reveals that transform gates become selective during training (not merely static), and Figure 4 shows that layer importance varies by dataset complexity (e.g., CIFAR-100 requires more layers than MNIST), offering new perspectives on depth utilization.

Quality is well-supported through rigorous experiments, including hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and consistent results across five runs. The architecture design avoids reliance on problem-specific initialization schemes, unlike prior approaches [16,17]. The clarity is high: equations, figures, and notation are consistently explained, and the highway network formulation is logically derived from LSTM concepts. The significance is substantial, as it directly addresses the core challenge of training very deep networks—a bottleneck that had limited exploration prior to 2015.

The main weaknesses involve contextualization of related work and scope. The paper notes a similar LSTM-inspired model [32] but does not clarify its relationship to this work (e.g., whether concurrent, prior, or overlapping). This omission weakens the novelty claim, as it is unclear whether [32] presents a distinct contribution or overlaps substantially. The discussion of highway networks as a precursor to later skip-connection methods (e.g., ResNets) is also underdeveloped; while the paper predates ResNets, it could better acknowledge how its gating mechanism conceptually influenced subsequent work.

The paper could strengthen its significance by addressing computational overhead. The gating mechanism adds parameters (e.g., transform gate weights), yet the analysis does not quantify inference cost or parameter efficiency relative to plain networks. For instance, Table 1 shows highway networks achieve competitive results with fewer parameters than state-of-the-art methods, but a direct comparison of parameter counts versus accuracy would better highlight this advantage. Additionally, while the MNIST and CIFAR experiments are thorough, the absence of ImageNet results limits the demonstration of scalability to larger tasks, though this may be justified given the paper's focus on depth rather than scale.

The claims about "extremely deep" networks (100+ layers) are well-supported by the MNIST experiments, though the paper could more explicitly discuss the trade-offs between depth and parameter efficiency. The layer importance analysis (Figure 4) is a notable strength, as it provides a method to identify redundant layers—a capability absent in plain networks—but the paper does not explore whether these unused layers can be pruned to improve efficiency.

Overall, the paper makes a high-quality, original contribution to deep learning by enabling direct training of very deep networks. The experimental evidence is robust, the architecture is well-motivated, and the insights into information routing are valuable. With minor improvements in contextualizing related work and expanding the discussion of practical implications (e.g., parameter efficiency, computational cost), the work would be even stronger. The significance is clear: highway networks provide a practical pathway to explore deeper architectures without complex training procedures, advancing the state of the art in deep network design. The paper meets all NIPS criteria and should be accepted.
