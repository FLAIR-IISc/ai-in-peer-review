Summary
This paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture that augments each layer with input-dependent gates, inspired by LSTM. Each highway layer outputs y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where T is a sigmoid “transform gate” and the complementary carry gate passes input forward. With a simple negative bias initialization for T to favor carry at the start of training, the authors show that very deep networks (100+ layers) can be trained with vanilla SGD. Empirical results include: (i) optimization studies on thin fully connected networks up to 100 layers on MNIST showing that plain networks degrade with depth while highway nets do not; (ii) competitive performance on MNIST with 10-layer convolutional highway networks; (iii) CIFAR-10/100 experiments demonstrating that thin, relatively deep highway nets can be trained in a single stage without teacher hints and match or exceed FitNets under comparable computational budgets; and (iv) analyses of gate behavior and layer “lesioning,” suggesting input-dependent routing and that deeper problems utilize more layers.

Relation to prior work is discussed, including initialization schemes, alternative activations, skip/error-injection connections, layer-wise pretraining, and hint-based training (FitNets). The architectural connection to LSTM gating is emphasized; the authors argue that multiplicative, input-dependent shortcuts ease information and gradient flow better than fixed skip connections.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea with a principled motivation from LSTM gating, bridging a gap between RNNs and deep feedforward nets.
- Strong evidence for improved trainability with depth: controlled MNIST optimization study with similar parameter counts per layer; consistent faster convergence than plain networks.
- Practical, easy-to-apply recipe (bias T to be initially negative) that is architecture-agnostic and does not depend on delicate weight-scaling derivations.
- Empirically demonstrates that thin deep models can be trained in one stage on CIFAR-10/100 without hints, competitive with FitNets given similar budgets.
- Insightful analyses: visualization of gate bias/activity, class-conditional routing patterns, and lesioning studies that quantify layer contribution.
- Code availability and reuse of hyperparameters across datasets improve transparency and reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing ablations to isolate the contribution of gating vs fixed shortcuts. A direct comparison to otherwise identical networks with fixed additive identity skips (or T fixed to a constant) would clarify whether input-dependent gating is essential, or whether simple residual connections suffice.
  Suggestion: Add baselines with ungated identity skip connections and report optimization/generalization under the same settings.
- The assumption C = 1 − T constrains the layer; the authors mention a more general form but do not evaluate it.
  Suggestion: Include an ablation with independent carry and transform gates to assess whether the coupling limits performance or stability.
- The “hundreds/thousands of layers” claim is not fully substantiated beyond pilot notes; most reported convolutional experiments use ≤32 layers, and fully connected results reach 100 layers on MNIST.
  Suggestion: Provide quantitative results for substantially deeper convolutional networks (e.g., 50–100 layers) and/or runtime/memory tradeoffs to back the scalability claim.
- Fairness of comparisons could be strengthened. The FitNet comparison is encouraging, but broader baselines are limited. Batch normalization and other contemporary techniques that ease training of deep nets are not evaluated here. The “plain” baselines are known to struggle without such methods.
  Suggestion: Compare against strong baselines that include modern initialization and normalization (e.g., batch norm) and competitive activation functions, controlling for parameter count and compute.
- Reproducibility details for CIFAR architectures are somewhat sparse in the main text (filter sizes and exact layer sequences are not fully enumerated).
  Suggestion: Move key architectural and training details from supplementary/materials to the main paper or include a concise appendix summary to ensure an expert can reimplement without external code.
- Computational overhead of gating (extra parameters and multiplies) is not quantified.
  Suggestion: Report parameter counts and FLOPs explicitly for all models to demonstrate that improvements are not due simply to increased capacity.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated. The optimization study is carefully controlled. The CIFAR experiments show that highway nets are easy to train and competitive under constrained budgets, though state-of-the-art is not the goal. Some central claims (extreme depth; necessity of gating vs fixed skips) would benefit from additional experimental substantiation and ablations. Analyses are insightful and go beyond raw accuracies.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The architectural definition is concise, with helpful intuition (information highways) and gradients discussion. Figures aid understanding. More exact architectural/training hyperparameters in the main paper would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The idea of input-dependent gating of layer-wise skip connections in feedforward nets, directly borrowing from LSTM, is novel and distinct from prior fixed skip or deep supervision approaches discussed in the paper. The negative bias initialization recipe is a simple and practically useful contribution.

Significance
If broadly validated, highway networks offer a simple, general mechanism to train very deep models and to study depth/width trade-offs, with potential impact across architectures. The analyses of routing and layer importance are valuable. The absence of stronger baselines and deeper conv results limits the immediate empirical impact, but the conceptual contribution is significant.

Pros
- Simple, general, and well-motivated architecture enabling deep training.
- Solid optimization evidence; competitive results without staged training.
- Insightful qualitative/quantitative analyses; code availability.

Cons
- Lacks ablations vs fixed (residual) skips and independent carry gates.
- Limited depth demonstrated in convolutional settings; “hundreds” claim underdocumented.
- Comparisons omit some strong contemporary baselines (e.g., batch normalization).

Recommendation
Lean accept. The architectural idea is timely and promising, the empirical evidence is convincing for the core claim of improved trainability, and the analyses are insightful. Strengthening the baselines and adding ablations would further bolster the case.
