

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep neural networks using simple gradient descent. The authors claim that by incorporating adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTM networks, highway networks overcome the optimization difficulties that plague very deep plain networks. The primary claim is that highway networks can be trained directly with hundreds of layers, whereas plain networks become intractable with increasing depth.

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims through extensive experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The results demonstrate that highway networks with 100 layers significantly outperform similarly sized plain networks (by over two orders of magnitude in training loss) while maintaining good generalization. The analysis of transform gate behavior and information routing provides valuable insights into how highway networks function. The paper also effectively compares against recent approaches like Fitnets, showing that highway networks achieve comparable or better results without requiring two-stage training.

Strengths include the clear novelty of the highway network architecture, comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets, and the practical demonstration that extremely deep networks can be trained directly. The paper also provides sufficient details for reproducibility, including source code availability.

The main weakness is that the paper doesn't fully address how highway networks scale to even deeper architectures beyond 100 layers, which would be crucial for demonstrating the true potential of the approach. While the MNIST experiments show that many layers remain unused, the paper doesn't thoroughly analyze whether this represents a fundamental limitation or simply reflects the simplicity of the task.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS as it addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning. The highway network concept represents a significant improvement over previous approaches for training very deep networks, and the experimental results are compelling. The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately and provides a clear path for future work.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in training deep networks. The highway network architecture is both novel and practically useful, and the experimental results are well-supported. The paper meets all quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
