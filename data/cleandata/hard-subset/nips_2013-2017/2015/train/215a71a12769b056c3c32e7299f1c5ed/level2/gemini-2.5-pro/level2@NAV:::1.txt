This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel feedforward neural network architecture designed to enable the training of extremely deep models. The core idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to introduce adaptive transform and carry gates at each layer. These gates dynamically regulate the flow of information, allowing parts of the input to be carried unchanged across many layers, creating "information highways." This mechanism directly addresses the degradation problem where adding more layers to a "plain" network leads to higher training error, a well-known obstacle that has been tackled by various methods including specialized initializations, optimizers, and fixed skip connections.

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear and compelling empirical validation. The authors present a direct comparison between highway networks and plain networks on MNIST, convincingly demonstrating that their proposed architecture does not suffer from optimization difficulties as depth increases to 50 or 100 layers, whereas plain networks fail. This is a significant result. Furthermore, experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that deep highway networks can achieve competitive or superior performance to other state-of-the-art models (like FitNets) without complex multi-stage training procedures.

The originality of the work is high. While skip connections have been used before, the introduction of a trainable, data-dependent gating mechanism for feedforward networks is a novel and powerful contribution. The analysis in Section 4 is particularly insightful, visualizing the learned gate biases and activities to reveal that the network learns sparse, data-dependent routing patterns. The lesion study effectively demonstrates that for complex tasks, the network utilizes its depth, rather than simply ignoring the extra layers.

One minor weakness is the lack of a formal theoretical analysis of the gradient flow, although the empirical evidence for its effectiveness is very strong. Additionally, as the authors note, the results on CIFAR could likely be improved with more dedicated hyperparameter tuning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the critical and previously unsolved problem of training extremely deep feedforward networks.
*   Presents a novel and elegant architectural solution with strong biological and theoretical motivation (from LSTMs).
*   Provides extensive and convincing experimental results that robustly support the main claims.
*   The work is of major significance and is highly likely to influence future research in deep learning architectures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   None of significance.

This is an excellent paper that presents a foundational contribution. It is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and tackles a problem of great importance to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
