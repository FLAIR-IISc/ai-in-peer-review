Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture with LSTM-inspired transform gates that enable data-dependent routing of information and unimpeded gradient flow across many layers. The main claims are: (1) very deep networks (hundreds to >1000 layers) can be trained directly with SGD using adaptive gating and simple initialization; (2) such networks achieve competitive generalization on MNIST/CIFAR and allow analysis of depth usage. The work builds on skip connections and deep supervision, and is closely related to FitNets’ teacher–student training, but differs by learning per-layer, per-unit gating rather than adding fixed shortcuts or auxiliary losses. The connection to LSTM gating is clear and well-motivated.

Quality: The optimization study on MNIST convincingly shows that plain networks degrade with depth while highway networks do not and converge faster. CIFAR-10/100 results under modest augmentation are competitive with fitnets at similar parameter/compute budgets and without multi-stage training. The analysis of gate statistics and layer lesioning is insightful. However, the strongest claim (“even with hundreds/1000 layers”) is supported primarily by MNIST optimization curves and anecdotal pilot runs; large-scale or more challenging benchmarks are missing. The compute/memory overhead of the gates and fair wall-clock comparisons are not quantified. Some references are incomplete.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear, with precise equations and an intuitive interpretation of “information highways.” Training details (learning-rate schedule, gate bias ranges) are described, and code is promised. A compact pseudocode block and a clearer architectural diagram would further help reproducibility.

Originality: The gating formulation y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)) provides a novel, learnable alternative to fixed skip connections and teacher hints, and the analysis of routing and layer importance is original.

Significance: If broadly validated, a mechanism that stabilizes and accelerates training of very deep nets is highly impactful. The data-dependent routing could also inspire follow-up work on conditional computation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, principled mechanism enabling deep training; strong optimization evidence.
- Competitive CIFAR performance without distillation.
- Insightful analyses (gate activity, lesion studies); code availability.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical scope (no large-scale tasks; limited baselines).
- Overhead/efficiency not rigorously evaluated; missing ablations (e.g., independent carry gate, fixed skips vs gates).

Constructive suggestions:
- Add comparisons to fixed identity skip/shortcut baselines and deep supervision under matched budgets.
- Report wall-clock, memory, and parameter overhead; gradient-norm diagnostics across depth.
- Ablate C=1−T vs independent gates; sensitivity to gate-bias initialization.
- Extend to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or SVHN, and provide more runs with confidence intervals.
