- Problem: Training very deep feedforward networks is hard because repeated nonlinear transformations impede information and gradient flow, making standard optimization difficult.
- Main contribution: Proposes "Highway Networks" that add learned transform and carry gates to each layer to regulate information flow.
- Mechanism: Each layer outputs a learned combination of a non-linear transform and the input, creating "information highways" for direct gradient propagation.
- Optimization result: Highway networks enable stable training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard SGD with momentum.
- MNIST experiment: Plain networks degrade beyond ~20 layers, while highway networks train effectively up to 100 layers and converge faster.
- CIFAR-10 comparison: Highway networks match or surpass FitNets without requiring complex multi-stage hint-based training.
- CIFAR-100 behavior: More layers perform active transformations for complex tasks, showing task-dependent usage of depth.
- Gate analysis: Visualization of learned gates shows data-dependent routing and that the network adapts its effective depth to task complexity.
- Lesion study: Layer-wise lesion analysis provides insight into individual layer contributions and supports the effective-depth claim.
- Technical quality: High—method is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by careful experiments and clear equations.
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally clear and well-presented, with illustrative figures aiding understanding.
- Originality: Novel application of LSTM-style gating to feedforward networks, making shortcuts dynamic and learnable.
- Significance: High—provides a simple, general solution enabling exploration of previously intractable depths and likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Strengths: Novel idea, effective in practice, simple to implement, strong empirical support, and insightful analysis.
- Weaknesses: Lacks large-scale (e.g., ImageNet) experiments to demonstrate scalability; sensitivity to negative initialization of transform gate bias bT is not fully analyzed.
- Recommendation: Strong acceptance—minor weaknesses do not diminish overall impact.
