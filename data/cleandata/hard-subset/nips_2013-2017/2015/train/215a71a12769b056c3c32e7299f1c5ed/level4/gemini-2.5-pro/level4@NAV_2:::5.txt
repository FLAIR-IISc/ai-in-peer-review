This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel deep feedforward architecture designed to ease the training of networks with a very large number of layers. The core contribution is the introduction of an adaptive gating mechanism, inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, which regulates the flow of information through the network. These gates allow each layer to dynamically balance between transforming its input and carrying it through to the next layer, creating "information highways" that facilitate unimpeded gradient and information flow. This approach directly addresses the well-known optimization difficulties that arise in traditional "plain" networks as depth increases. The authors provide strong empirical evidence showing that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained directly with SGD, demonstrating superior optimization performance compared to plain networks on MNIST. Furthermore, they show that these models achieve competitive results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, outperforming comparable "FitNet" architectures that require a more complex, multi-stage training procedure.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The adaptation of LSTM-style gating to feedforward architectures is an elegant and effective solution to the significant and long-standing problem of training very deep networks. By enabling direct end-to-end training of networks with hundreds of layers, this work opens up new avenues for exploring the role of extreme depth in representation learning.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and its claims are well-supported by rigorous experiments. The controlled optimization experiments (Section 3.1) provide a clear and convincing demonstration of the architecture's benefits over plain networks. The analysis of the trained models is particularly insightful, especially the lesion study (Section 4.2) which effectively illustrates how the network learns to utilize its depth according to task complexity.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core idea is introduced intuitively, the mathematical formulation is straightforward, and the experimental setup and results are presented thoroughly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The authors might consider discussing an alternative perspective on their architecture. A very deep highway network could be viewed as a moderately deep network where each layer is a complex computational block with internal sub-structure (i.e., the transform and carry paths). This framing could help relate the work to other architectures that achieve effective depth through complex blocks, such as the Inception module in GoogLeNet or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG models.
*   While the experiments on MNIST and CIFAR are compelling, demonstrating the architecture's effectiveness on a larger-scale dataset like ImageNet would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and highlight its practical significance for state-of-the-art computer vision tasks.
*   A fascinating future experiment could involve multi-task learning. For instance, training a single, thin-and-deep highway network on both MNIST and CIFAR (with forked outputs) could reveal how the network allocates its resources. It would be interesting to see if layers that are underutilized for the simpler MNIST task (as suggested by the lesion study) are actively repurposed for the more complex CIFAR task, providing direct evidence of efficient hierarchical feature sharing.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a simple, elegant, and effective idea with solid empirical validation and insightful analysis. The proposed highway network architecture represents a significant contribution to the field.
