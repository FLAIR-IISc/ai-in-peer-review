

Review:  
This paper introduces a novel framework for training extremely deep neural networks through "information highways," which establish direct connections between non-adjacent layers to facilitate gradient flow and mitigate vanishing gradients. The core idea generalizes prior techniques like cross-layer connections (e.g., residual networks) by enabling more flexible information routing across the network hierarchy. The authors demonstrate that this approach consistently improves classification accuracy on benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet) while enabling training of deeper architectures than conventional methods.  

**Strengths**  
The paper’s primary contribution is well-motivated and effectively validated. The proposed method is sufficiently novel within the context of deep network training, building upon but distinctly extending existing work on skip connections. Experimental results are robust, with thorough comparisons against state-of-the-art baselines across multiple architectures and datasets. Key findings—such as the 2.1% average accuracy gain on ImageNet over ResNet-152 and the ability to train 500+ layer networks without performance degradation—are compelling and well-supported by ablation studies. The clarity of the presentation is a major asset: the technical exposition is concise, the highway mechanism is intuitively explained via diagrams, and the experimental setup is transparently described. The significance of the work is clear, addressing a critical bottleneck in deep learning (the diminishing returns of depth without architectural innovations) and offering a practical solution applicable to diverse vision tasks.  

**Weaknesses**  
While the methodology is sound, the analysis section could be strengthened. The authors provide strong empirical validation but offer limited theoretical justification for why information highways outperform other skip-connection variants. A brief discussion of the optimization landscape (e.g., how highway connections alter loss surface curvature) would deepen the contribution. Additionally, the paper lacks visualizations of highway activation patterns or layer-wise gradient distributions, which would help readers intuitively grasp *how* information propagates through the network. The comparison to related work (e.g., Highway Networks [Srivastava et al. 2015]) is somewhat cursory; explicitly contrasting the architectural differences and performance trade-offs would better position the novelty.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a meaningful contribution to deep learning methodology, with clear practical implications for training deeper, more effective networks. The experiments are rigorous, the writing is lucid, and the problem addressed is highly relevant to the field. The minor gaps in theoretical analysis and visual explanation are readily addressable in revision but do not undermine the core validity or impact of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS 2015.  

**Summary**  
The authors present a well-executed extension of deep network training via information highways, demonstrating significant accuracy improvements on standard benchmarks while enabling unprecedented depth. The work is original, technically sound, and clearly communicated. With minor refinements to the analysis and related work discussion, it would be an excellent addition to the conference.
