- Introduces highway networks with adaptive transform/carry gates that create "information highways" enabling direct SGD training of extremely deep (100+ layer) feedforward nets.  
- Gating mitigates vanishing gradients by interpolating between identity mapping and learned transforms.  
- Empirical results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 show highway networks remain stable with increasing depth while plain networks degrade beyond ~20 layers.  
- Highway networks achieve competitive accuracy with fewer parameters than methods like Fitnets and allow single-stage training without teacher-student distillation.  
- Strong technical rigor: clear theoretical foundation (Eq.3), controlled experiments (10–100 layers), and analyses (transform gate biases, layer importance) that support claims.  
- Exceptionally clear writing and organization, with effective figures; minor clarity opportunity regarding relation to "thin & tall" architectures.  
- Novel contribution: LSTM-inspired adaptive gating embedded in feedforward architecture, distinct from skip connections and two-stage methods.  
- High significance: enables practical exploration of extreme depth, agnostic to activation/layer type, and applicable to large-scale tasks (ImageNet suggested).  
- Minor suggestions: evaluate shared MNIST/CIFAR networks to probe input-dependent routing and clarify relationship to VGG-style stacks.  
- Overall conclusion: strong, elegant, and broadly impactful work that meets NIPS criteria; recommendation to accept.
