Summary
The paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses LSTM-inspired gating to enable effective information flow across many layers. Each layer computes a transformed signal H(x) and blends it with the input x using a transform gate T(x) and an implicit carry gate 1 − T(x), allowing the layer to interpolate between passing inputs through and applying a learned transform. With a simple negative bias initialization of T, the authors demonstrate that even networks with hundreds of layers can be trained directly with SGD. Experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 show strong optimization behavior and competitive generalization without special training tricks. The paper also provides insightful analyses of gate activity, data-dependent routing, and layer importance via lesioning. This work connects to prior efforts on vanishing/exploding gradients, improved initialization, maxout units, skip/shortcut connections, and teacher-student training, but distinguishes itself by introducing adaptive, input-dependent gates within deep feedforward networks.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is clearly defined, training procedures are reasonable, and claims are supported by experiments. The negative bias scheme for T is simple and effective. Analyses (gate activity visualizations and lesion studies) substantiate the “information highway” intuition.
- Empirical support: The optimization experiments convincingly show that depth severely hurts plain networks but not highway networks; convergence speed is also improved. Generalization on MNIST and CIFAR is competitive given modest data augmentation and parameter budgets.
- Limitations: Comparisons are focused on plain nets and FitNets; broader ablations (e.g., fixed skip/identity shortcuts, varying C vs. 1 − T) would strengthen the case. Larger-scale evaluation is not included. The method’s sensitivity to the transform-gate bias is discussed but not extensively explored.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The architectural description is precise, and figures effectively illustrate the learned routing and contribution of layers. One phrasing in Section 4.1 could be clarified (see Specific comments). Including a brief discussion of computational overhead from the gates and a concise parameter/ops comparison versus baselines in the main text would improve readability.

Originality
The core idea—bringing LSTM-style gating to deep feedforward networks—is novel and well motivated by the vanishing-gradient problem and by earlier work on skip connections and deep supervision. The input-dependent gates go beyond static identity shortcuts by enabling dynamic routing.

Significance
Making very deep networks trainable with standard optimizers is important. The work offers a practical recipe (architecture + initialization) that scales depth without elaborate pretraining or teacher signals. The analysis tools (per-layer lesioning and gate activity patterns) are also valuable for understanding deep computation.

Pros
- Simple, elegant architectural change with strong practical impact.
- Demonstrated training of 50–100+ layer networks via plain SGD.
- Competitive accuracy on CIFAR-10/100 with moderate model sizes and limited augmentation.
- Insightful analyses of gating behavior, input-dependent routing, and layer importance.
- Clear writing; code and scripts are available.

Cons
- Limited ablations vs. simpler alternatives (e.g., fixed identity shortcuts, C ≠ 1 − T).
- Comparisons to other deep training strategies could be expanded.
- Sensitivity to gate-bias initialization is only lightly explored.
- Experiments are on small/medium-scale datasets; large-scale results would bolster significance.

Specific comments and questions
- Section 4.1 wording: The text states that “most transform gates are active on average,” yet the single-sample activations appear sparse. If the intended point is that average activity is high but per-sample activity is selective, please clarify; if not, consider revising to indicate “not active on average” or quantify average T across layers and classes.
- Identity initialization vs. highway gates: Initializing each layer near identity (possibly with small noise) provides static skip-like behavior at the start of training, but it lacks adaptive, input-dependent control and may drift as training progresses. In highway layers, the learned gates:
  - Modulate the effective Jacobian, preserving gradient flow even as H changes.
  - Enable per-sample routing (as shown in your class-conditional gate analyses).
  - Allow the network to selectively engage depth as needed (supported by lesion studies).
  A brief discussion contrasting these approaches would be useful, and an ablation with fixed identity shortcuts could further highlight the benefits of gating.
- Minor: there are a few typos (e.g., “backpropogation” → “backpropagation”).

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes an original and practically important contribution, is clearly presented, and provides convincing empirical and analytical evidence. It was a pleasure to read.
