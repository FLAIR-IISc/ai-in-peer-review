- Introduces highway networks that use adaptive gates (inspired by LSTM) to balance transform vs. carry pathways per layer.  
- Gating enables unimpeded gradient flow, allowing effective training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100 layers).  
- Empirical results show highway networks train with standard SGD where comparable plain networks fail.  
- Analysis of learned gate behavior reveals data-dependent routing and a method to assess layer importance.  
- Significance and originality: addresses a major problem in deep learning with an elegant, novel adaptation of gating to feedforward nets.  
- Technical quality: solid experimental comparisons (optimization experiments, CIFAR-10/100) supporting the claims and showing strong generalization.  
- Clarity and analysis: paper is well-written, with useful visualizations (gate activities) and lesioning studies that illuminate model behavior.  
- Equation 5 / Jacobian discussion is slightly misleading — presenting identity vs. H' ignores that sigmoid gates are strictly between 0 and 1; needs clarification and explicit gradient form.  
- Transform gate bias initialization (small negative biases) is a crucial heuristic that depends on depth and requires a systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Results reporting lacks statistical robustness: tables often show only the best run rather than consistently reporting mean ± std.dev. across runs.  
- Comparisons to prior work (e.g., FitNets) need clearer detailing of parameter/operation counts and experimental setup differences (data augmentation, optimization, hyperparameter searches).  
- Overall recommendation: strong, novel contribution with convincing experiments and insightful analysis, supporting acceptance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: simple effective architecture enabling very deep nets, strong empirical evidence, and explanatory analyses likely to inspire future work.  
- Arguments for rejection: reliance on an underanalyzed bias initialization heuristic, incomplete statistical reporting, and minor clarity issues in Jacobian description.
