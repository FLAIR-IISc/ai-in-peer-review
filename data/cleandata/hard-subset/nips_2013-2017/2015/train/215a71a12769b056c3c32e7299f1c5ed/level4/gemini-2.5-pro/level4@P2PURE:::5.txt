This paper describes an idea inspired by LSTMs that enables the training of very deep feedforward networks. The proposed method is simple and elegant, and the analysis is solid. I had previously considered a similar approach of using a gating mechanism to manage gradient flow, and I congratulate the authors on being the first to successfully implement and analyze it.

A possible alternative perspective is to view a very deep network as a shallower stack of complex "macro-layers," where each macro-layer consists of several sub-layers and thus exhibits more complex non-linear behavior than a traditional layer. This compositional view is a useful way to understand architectures like GoogLeNet's Inception modules or VGG's stacked 3x3 convolutions. I wonder if the authors believe this is also a valid framework for interpreting their highway networks.

I would also suggest one additional experiment to explore the dynamics of feature sharing in this deep architecture. The authors could train a single, deep network on both MNIST and CIFAR-10, with task-specific output layers branching off from the shared trunk. The primary question would be whether the layer-utilization patterns observed at inference time for separately trained networks persist in this multi-task setting. Specifically, it would be illuminating to see if parameters underutilized by the simpler MNIST task are effectively recruited for the more complex CIFAR-10 task. Such an experiment would provide valuable insights into how representational capacity is shared and allocated within a very deep hierarchy.

I have no major complaints. To further strengthen the paper, I encourage the authors to evaluate their network on larger datasets such as ImageNet.
