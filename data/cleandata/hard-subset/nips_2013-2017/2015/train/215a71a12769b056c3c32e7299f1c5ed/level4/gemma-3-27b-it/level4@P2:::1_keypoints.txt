- Introduces highway networks with LSTM-inspired adaptive gating to create “information highways” enabling unimpeded flow across many layers.
- Demonstrates highway networks can be trained directly with SGD at depths of hundreds of layers, outperforming traditional feedforward networks.
- Provides informative analysis of learned gate patterns showing the gating mechanism is learned and contributes to performance.
- Positions the work relative to prior advances (optimizers, initialization, activations, skip connections) and distinguishes itself by requiring no specialized training procedures.
- Strength: novel architecture offering a compelling solution to the vanishing gradient problem via gating.
- Strength: strong empirical results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, competitive or superior in some settings.
- Strength: clear exposition and concise mathematical formulation.
- Weakness: inconsistency between claiming sigmoid gates never reach 0/1 and using a Jacobian that assumes exact 0/1—needs clarification of backprop implementation.
- Weakness: initialization scheme based on negative biases appears ad-hoc and depth-dependent; requires systematic justification (e.g., performance vs. bias plots).
- Weakness: vague phrasing about comparisons to recent studies—requires direct, concrete comparisons or justification for their absence.
- Weakness: reporting only maximum accuracies is insufficient—mean and standard deviation should be reported for statistical assessment.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis explaining why gating facilitates training (effects on optimization landscape or gradient propagation).
- Argument for acceptance: novel, effective architecture addressing a major deep learning challenge with promising empirical evidence.
- Argument against acceptance: unresolved concerns about backprop through gates, ad-hoc initialization, and limited theoretical grounding.
- Overall: strong paper recommended for acceptance contingent on addressing gradient calculation, initialization analysis, comparative reporting, and providing firmer theoretical or empirical justification.
