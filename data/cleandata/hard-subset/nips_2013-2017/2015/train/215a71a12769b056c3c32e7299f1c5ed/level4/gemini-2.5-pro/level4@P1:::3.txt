This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. Each layer includes adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates that regulate the flow of information, allowing parts of the input to pass through unmodified across many layers. This structure effectively mitigates the vanishing gradient problem, enabling networks with hundreds of layers to be trained directly with standard stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate this capability and provide an insightful analysis of the resulting information flow.

This is a high-quality, well-written paper that addresses a central and long-standing challenge in deep learning: how to effectively train networks of increasing depth. The difficulties of credit assignment and gradient flow in deep architectures have been a major bottleneck for the field, and this work provides an elegant and effective solution.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of adapting LSTM-style gating for feedforward architectures is both novel and insightful. The results are highly significant, demonstrating a straightforward method to train networks with 50 or 100 layers, which was a substantial leap forward. This work opens the door for researchers to explore the benefits of depth on a scale that was previously impractical.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed architecture is sound, and the empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The direct comparison in Section 3.1, showing that deep highway networks train well while plain networks of the same depth fail catastrophically, provides clear evidence for the architecture's effectiveness. The performance on standard benchmarks like CIFAR-10/100 is competitive, validating that these deep networks also generalize well.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** Section 4 is a particular highlight of the paper. The visualizations of gate activations provide fascinating insights into how the network learns to dynamically route information based on the input. The layer lesioning study is an excellent method for interpreting the "effective depth" required for different tasks, showing that the network can learn to utilize its depth for complex problems while effectively ignoring unnecessary layers for simpler ones. This is a very desirable property for deep models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making a novel concept easy to understand.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Authors:**

The paper is very strong, but I have a couple of minor points and questions for the authors:

1.  **Clarification on Gate Activity:** In Section 4.1, the paper states that for CIFAR-100, "most transform gates are active on average." Based on Figure 2 (column 2), the mean activity for many gates appears to be relatively low (i.e., biased toward carrying information). Could the authors clarify this interpretation? Perhaps providing the color bar scale or elaborating on the threshold for being considered "active" would help the reader.

2.  **Comparison to Identity Initialization:** The transform gates allow layers to default to a "carry" behavior, which is similar to an identity transformation. A simple baseline for a plain network that was not discussed would be to initialize each layer's weights to approximate the identity function (e.g., using an identity matrix with small added noise for the weight matrix and zero for the bias). This would also facilitate information flow at the beginning of training. A brief discussion on how the adaptive, data-dependent gating of highway networks provides an advantage over this simpler, static initialization scheme would strengthen the paper's claims.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel and important contribution to the field. It convincingly demonstrates a practical method for training very deep neural networks and provides valuable analysis of their behavior. The work is original, clearly presented, and highly significant. This paper was a pleasure to read, and I strongly recommend its acceptance.
