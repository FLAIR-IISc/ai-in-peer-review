Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses input-dependent gating—borrowed conceptually from LSTM recurrent networks—to enable efficient training of very deep models. Each layer blends a transformed pathway H(x) with a “carry” pathway that passes the input forward, controlled by a transform gate T(x) (with C(x) = 1 − T(x)). With appropriate bias initialization on T, gradients and activations can propagate across hundreds of layers, allowing direct training via standard SGD without layerwise pretraining or teacher-student distillation.

The work is well-situated in the literature on depth and trainability. It relates to: (i) LSTM’s gating mechanisms for preserving information and gradients over time; (ii) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision for improving information flow; (iii) recent initialization schemes and optimization strategies (e.g., normalized initialization, ReLUs) that enable moderately deep nets; and (iv) fitnets/distillation where deeper students learn from shallow teachers. The experiments compare against plain networks with careful initialization and to FitNet-style models on CIFAR, and provide analyses of gate behavior and layer importance.

A useful conceptual lens is to view very deep networks as “not-so-deep” networks whose layers contain sublayers or internal routing mechanisms. In this sense, highway layers act as compound layers with richer nonlinearity and data-dependent subpaths, akin to Inception modules (GoogLeNet) or stacks of small filters (VGG), but learned adaptively via gates rather than being hard-wired. Highway networks unify the benefits of depth with flexible, input-dependent shortcuts.

Quality
The method is technically sound and clearly specified. The gating formulation, dimensionality constraints, and initialization strategy are straightforward. Experiments convincingly demonstrate optimization advantages as depth increases: highway networks of 50–100 fully connected layers train faster and attain significantly lower loss than plain networks with matched capacity. On vision benchmarks, thin and deep convolutional highway networks achieve competitive accuracy on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 without complex data augmentation, and they match or surpass FitNet-style models while avoiding multi-stage training.

The analysis sections strengthen the paper: visualizations show sparse, input-dependent gate activity; negative gate biases become more selective rather than simply “closed”; and lesion studies quantify layer contribution, revealing that networks use more depth on harder datasets (CIFAR-100) while leaving many layers idle on MNIST. These are thoughtful diagnostics that improve interpretability.

Points that could improve the empirical case:
- Larger-scale validation (e.g., ImageNet) would test generalization of the claimed optimization benefits under more realistic data regimes and with modern data augmentation.
- Ablations on the choice C = 1 − T vs. independent carry/transform gates, and on the importance of the bias initialization magnitude, would clarify design trade-offs.
- A more comprehensive comparison to contemporary skip-connection approaches (beyond fitnets and plain nets) would further position the contribution.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The layer equations and intuition behind gates are clear, as are the training details. Figures illustrating gate statistics and lesion studies are informative. Minor suggestions:
- Explicitly state any parameter sharing between H and T in convolutional blocks (they appear independent) and clarify the computational overhead of gating relative to a plain layer.
- Include a compact schematic of the full convolutional highway block and “downsampling” transitions to help readers reconstruct architectures.

Originality
The core novelty is adapting LSTM-style input-dependent gating to feedforward depth to create data-dependent information highways. While skip connections and teacher-student training are known, using multiplicative gates to modulate transform vs. carry pathways within each layer is a distinct, elegant mechanism that enables direct training of very deep networks without auxiliary losses or multi-stage procedures. The analytical explorations of routing and layer importance are also original and insightful.

Significance
If broadly validated, highway networks offer a practical recipe for training very deep models with standard optimizers, potentially impacting a range of architectures (fully connected, convolutional, and possibly recurrent blocks along depth). The results are competitive on standard benchmarks and suggest that depth can be scaled effectively without elaborate tricks. The gating perspective may inspire further work on dynamic routing and conditional computation.

Specific suggestions and additional experiments
- Multi-task depth sharing: Train a single thin-and-tall highway network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR (forking only at the input stem and final classifiers). Analyze whether layers that are underutilized for MNIST become active for CIFAR, and whether gate activity patterns resemble those of single-task models. This would illuminate how sharing happens in a very deep hierarchy and whether the gating mechanism efficiently allocates capacity across tasks.
- Larger-scale benchmark: Evaluate on ImageNet (even with modest augmentation) to assess optimization stability and accuracy at practical scales, and to benchmark computational overhead vs. depth gains.
- Ablations: Compare C = 1 − T to independent C and T; vary the initial gate bias systematically; and report the parameter/compute overhead of gates relative to accuracy and convergence speed.
- Robustness: Report sensitivity to activation choices for H (e.g., ReLU vs. tanh/ELU) and to different normalized initializations.

Pros
- Simple, principled architecture that enables training of very deep networks via standard SGD.
- Strong optimization results across depth; competitive accuracy on MNIST and CIFAR without complex training pipelines.
- Insightful analyses of gate behavior, input-dependent routing, and layer importance.
- Clear connection to and extension of LSTM-style gating to feedforward depth.

Cons
- Limited large-scale validation and modest comparisons to other skip-connection methods.
- Some design choices (C = 1 − T, bias initialization magnitude) are not fully ablated.
- Computational overhead of gating not thoroughly quantified.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper presents a clean and impactful idea, supports it with solid experiments and analyses, and is likely to influence how the community thinks about depth, routing, and gradient flow in very deep networks.
