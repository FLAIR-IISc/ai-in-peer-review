The paper presents an intriguing idea and effectively describes the experiments, which are convincing to a certain extent. One of the strengths of the paper is the authors' effort to analyze the learned networks with respect to the pattern of gate outputs.

However, there are several aspects that require clarification and improvement. Firstly, the gate unit T is modeled using a standard sigmoid activation, which means the response value is never exactly 0 or 1, as acknowledged by the authors. Consequently, the gradients in equation 5 are not accurate. The authors should provide a detailed explanation of how backpropagation is performed in these networks to address this discrepancy.

Secondly, the impact of initialization is a complex issue, and the authors' proposal to use constant negative biases based on the number of levels is not entirely satisfactory. A more comprehensive analysis would be beneficial, such as a plot illustrating the performance with respect to the initial value of the bias. The initial bias serves as a regularizer, with a high negative bias favoring networks with "longer highways," a comparison that is noteworthy.

Thirdly, the statement "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is unclear. To facilitate a meaningful comparison, the authors should either use the same experimental setting as the referenced studies or explicitly highlight the differences in their setup. This clarification is essential to ensure a fair evaluation of the proposed method.

Fourthly, presenting "max" accuracies in tables is unconventional and statistically unsound. Instead, the authors should provide mean accuracy and standard deviation for each method, as well as any available additional metrics. This would offer a more comprehensive understanding of the results.

Lastly, it would be interesting to explore the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The paper introduces a novel way to incorporate additional gate units in neural networks, allowing layers to act as an identity function. The gate unit response, which depends on the input, enables the propagation of inputs through a large number of highly selective layers. A deeper analysis of this concept could provide valuable insights into its theoretical implications.
