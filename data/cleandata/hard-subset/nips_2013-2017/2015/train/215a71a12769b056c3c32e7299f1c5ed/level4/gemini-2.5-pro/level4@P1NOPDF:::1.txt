This paper introduces a compelling idea that is clearly described and supported by largely convincing experimental results. A particular strength of the work is the authors' analysis of the learned networks, specifically their investigation into the activation patterns of the gate outputs.

Despite the strengths, there are several points that require clarification and revision:

1.  The gate unit T is modeled with a standard sigmoid activation. As the authors themselves note, this means the gate's output value is never exactly 0 or 1. This raises a question regarding the gradient formulation in Eq. 5, which appears to assume a discrete binary output. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how backpropagation is performed in these networks to resolve this apparent discrepancy.

2.  While the effect of initialization is often difficult to analyze, the proposed strategy of using a constant negative bias dependent on the number of layers seems somewhat ad-hoc. To better justify this choice, I would suggest including a sensitivity analysis. A plot of model performance versus the initial bias value would be particularly insightful. As the paper implies, this initial bias effectively acts as a regularizer, with a strong negative bias encouraging the formation of what the authors aptly call "longer highways." An empirical analysis of this relationship would significantly strengthen the paper.

3.  The authors compare their results to those "reported by recent studies in similar experimental settings." This phrasing is ambiguous and potentially misleading. For a direct comparison to be valid, the experimental settings must be identical, not merely similar. The authors should clarify this point. If the settings are indeed identical, they should state so explicitly. If there are differences, these must be detailed in the paper, along with a discussion of how they might affect the reported performance.

4.  Reporting "max" accuracies in the tables is unconventional and statistically questionable. Standard practice is to report the mean and standard deviation of accuracy over multiple experimental runs to provide a more robust and reliable measure of performance. The authors should revise their tables to include these statistics for all evaluated methods. If these statistics are not available for baseline methods cited from previous work, this should be explicitly mentioned.

5.  From a learning theory perspective, this work raises interesting questions. The proposed architecture uses input-dependent gate units that allow layers to approximate the identity function, thereby enabling information to propagate through a large number of very selective layers. A formal analysis of the optimization landscape or the representational power of such networks would be a valuable contribution and an exciting avenue for future research.
