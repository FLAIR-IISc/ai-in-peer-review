### Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The central contribution is the introduction of "highway networks," a novel architecture inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The core idea is to incorporate adaptive gating units that regulate information flow across layers. These gates can learn to either pass information through a layer's non-linear transformation or carry it directly to the next layer, creating unimpeded "information highways." This mechanism allows for the direct training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD), a feat that is notoriously difficult with conventional "plain" network architectures due to issues like vanishing gradients.

The authors position their work relative to previous approaches for training deep networks, such as specialized initialization schemes (e.g., Glorot & Bengio, He et al.), advanced optimizers, and methods involving skip connections or auxiliary training targets (e.g., FitNets). They argue that highway networks offer a more fundamental, architectural solution that is less dependent on specific activation functions or complex training procedures. The paper's empirical results on MNIST and CIFAR datasets support these claims, demonstrating that highway networks not only overcome the optimization hurdles associated with depth but also achieve competitive performance.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed architecture is well-motivated and clearly formulated. The claims are substantiated by a series of well-designed experiments. The optimization experiments on MNIST (Section 3.1) provide a direct and compelling demonstration of the core hypothesis: plain networks degrade in performance as depth increases, while highway networks do not. The comparison with FitNets on CIFAR-10 is particularly strong, showing that a complex, multi-stage training procedure can be replaced by a simpler, direct training approach with comparable or better results. The analysis in Section 4, especially the lesion study (Figure 4) that visualizes layer importance, is insightful and adds significant value by demonstrating how the network learns to utilize depth depending on task complexity.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core concept of the information highway is introduced with clear notation and an effective analogy to LSTM gates. The motivation is established upfront, and the experiments are presented logically to build the authors' case. Figures are informative and support the text well; for instance, the visualization of gate activations (Figure 2) provides a compelling intuition for the network's learned behavior. The authors provide sufficient detail for the work to be reproducible, including a link to their source code and hyperparameters.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it draws inspiration from LSTMs and relates to the concept of skip connections, the formulation of learnable, data-dependent gating for feedforward architectures is a novel and powerful contribution. Unlike fixed skip connections, the highway network's gates can dynamically route information based on the input, a more flexible and expressive mechanism. This adaptation of a core idea from the recurrent network literature to solve a fundamental problem in feedforward networks is a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. At the time of this research, the field was increasingly recognizing the power of depth (e.g., VGG, GoogLeNet) but was simultaneously constrained by the difficulty of training deeper models. This paper provides an elegant and effective architectural principle to break past this barrier. The ideas presented have the potential to directly influence the design of future deep learning models and enable researchers to explore the properties and capabilities of extremely deep architectures. The finding that networks can learn to effectively use or ignore layers based on problem complexity is an important insight for the community.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The analysis in Section 4 is a major strength of the paper. To further enhance it, the authors could consider:
1.  A more detailed exploration of the effect of the initial transform gate bias. While a negative bias is shown to be effective, an analysis of how different initializations affect the final learned gate activations and network performance could provide deeper insights.
2.  Visualizing the feature maps or activations from `H(x, WH)` at different depths. This could help clarify what kind of transformations are being learned by the layers that are "active" (i.e., where the transform gate is open).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and elegant architecture that effectively solves a well-known, critical problem in deep learning.
*   **Impact:** Has the potential to significantly influence future research in deep network architectures and enable the exploration of previously intractable model depths.
*   **Empirical Soundness:** The claims are backed by strong, clear, and well-executed experiments that directly support the central hypothesis.
*   **Clarity and Insight:** The paper is exceptionally clear, and the analysis section provides valuable insights into the network's learned behavior, going beyond simple performance metrics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The performance on CIFAR benchmarks, while strong, does not set a new state-of-the-art across the board. However, this is a minor point, as the paper's primary contribution is architectural and methodological, not just achieving a new record on a specific dataset.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a simple yet powerful idea with significant implications for the field. It is well-motivated, clearly explained, and thoroughly validated. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
