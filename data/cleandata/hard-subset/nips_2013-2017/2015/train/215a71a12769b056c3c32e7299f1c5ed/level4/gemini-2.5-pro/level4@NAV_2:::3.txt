This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to enable the training of networks with very substantial depth. The central idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. By incorporating adaptive transform and carry gates, the architecture allows information to flow unimpeded across many layers, creating so-called "information highways." This mechanism is proposed as a solution to the optimization difficulties, such as vanishing gradients, that have traditionally hindered the training of very deep feedforward networks. The authors demonstrate that networks with hundreds of layers can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent.

**Strengths of the paper:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The work addresses a central and long-standing challenge in the field. As researchers push for deeper models, the difficulty of propagating signals and gradients has been a major limiting factor. This paper offers an elegant and effective solution. The concept of using adaptive, LSTM-inspired gates to control information flow in a feedforward architecture is a novel and powerful contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The claims are well-supported by solid empirical results. The optimization experiments on MNIST compellingly demonstrate that highway networks do not suffer from performance degradation as depth increases, in stark contrast to conventional "plain" networks. Furthermore, the experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this architecture can achieve competitive results, even outperforming models like FitNets that require more complex, multi-stage training procedures.
*   **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. A particular highlight is the analysis in Section 4, which provides valuable insights into the network's behavior. The visualizations of data-dependent information routing and the layer lesioning study are particularly insightful, demonstrating that the network learns to use depth effectively depending on task complexity. This analysis goes beyond simply presenting performance metrics and helps build an understanding of *why* the method works.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is very strong overall. The following points are minor suggestions for further clarification and strengthening the work.

*   In the analysis of information routing (Section 4.1), the authors state that "most transform gates are active on average" for the CIFAR-100 network. This is contrasted with the observation that activity is very sparse for any single example. This is an interesting finding, but the phrasing could be slightly confusing. It might be beneficial to clarify if this means the average gate activation is simply non-zero, or if it is high. Perhaps illustrating this with a distribution of the mean activation values across gates would add further clarity.
*   A simple alternative for enabling training of deep networks is to initialize the weight matrix of each layer to be close to the identity matrix (potentially with added noise), forcing the initial function to be near-identity. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion on how the proposed dynamic gating mechanism compares to this simpler, static initialization scheme. This would help contextualize the contribution and underscore the benefits of an adaptive, input-dependent routing mechanism.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the training of very deep neural networks. The method is novel, well-motivated, and convincingly validated. The analysis provides valuable insights, and the work is likely to be highly influential. It was a pleasure to read.
