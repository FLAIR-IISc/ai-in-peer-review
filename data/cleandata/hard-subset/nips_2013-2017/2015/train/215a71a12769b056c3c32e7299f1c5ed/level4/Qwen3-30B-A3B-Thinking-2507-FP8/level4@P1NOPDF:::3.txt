

This paper proposes using LSTM units to train extremely deep neural networks, offering a compelling solution to a long-standing challenge in the field. The work is excellent—well-written, technically sound, and clearly motivated. The core problem it addresses is critical: while adding layers typically improves performance, vanishing/exploding gradients and poor information flow have severely hindered the practical adoption of very deep architectures for decades. This paper directly tackles these issues and demonstrates successful training of networks exceeding 100 layers, a significant advance.

The contributions are both theoretically and empirically strong. The experimental analysis of information routing and layer-wise importance is particularly compelling, providing valuable insights into how the proposed mechanism enables deep learning. The results are convincing and well-supported by thorough ablation studies.

Specific comments:
- In Section 4.1, the claim that "most transform blocks are active on average" appears inconsistent with the data presented. This should be revised to state that "most transform blocks are inactive on average," aligning with the observed sparsity in the routing mechanism.
- The authors should clarify how the transform gates in their approach differ from the simpler strategy of initializing each layer with an identity matrix (possibly with minor noise). This comparison would strengthen the discussion of the method's novelty relative to existing initialization techniques.

Overall, this is an excellent contribution that effectively resolves a major barrier to deep network training. The paper is a pleasure to read, and the results are both novel and impactful. The authors have made a clear, valuable advance in the field.
