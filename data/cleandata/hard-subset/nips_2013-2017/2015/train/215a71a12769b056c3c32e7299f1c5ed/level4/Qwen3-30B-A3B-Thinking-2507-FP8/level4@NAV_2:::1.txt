

The paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via stochastic gradient descent. Building on NIPS traditions, this work extends prior approaches like residual connections [22, 23, 25] and LSTM-inspired gating [29, 30], but addresses the core challenge of vanishing gradients in very deep networks through adaptive information highways. The authors demonstrate that highway networks generalize well on MNIST and CIFAR datasets while maintaining training stability and performance comparable to or exceeding shallower architectures, with insightful analysis of gate dynamics and layer utilization.

**Strengths**  
*Originality*: The highway architecture provides a principled, LSTM-inspired alternative to fixed skip connections, enabling dynamic information routing. It significantly advances beyond prior work on depth (e.g., FitNets [25]) by eliminating two-stage training.  
*Significance*: The paper enables rigorous study of extreme depth (100+ layers) without compromising training efficiency or generalization, addressing a key open problem in deep learning.  
*Quality*: Experiments are thorough (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100), with clear ablation studies (Figures 2–4) showing how gates adaptively route information and how layer importance varies with task complexity.  
*Clarity*: The architecture and analysis are well-explained, with Figure 2 offering compelling visual evidence of "information highways."

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*: The sigmoid-based gate (T) never reaches exact 0/1 (as noted), invalidating the gradient analysis in Equation 5 (dy/dx = I or H'). The authors must clarify how backpropagation handles the continuous gate outputs.  
*Quality*: The proposed bias initialization scheme (e.g., -1 for depth ~10) lacks empirical validation. A performance curve across bias values (e.g., -10 to 0) would strengthen this claim, as the current heuristic appears ad hoc.  
*Clarity*: The phrase "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" (Section 3.3.1) is ambiguous. The authors should explicitly contrast their experimental setup (single-stage SGD) with prior work (e.g., FitNets' two-stage training) to justify comparisons.  
*Quality*: Reporting only "max" accuracy (e.g., Table 2) is statistically unsound. Mean ± standard deviation across multiple runs (as used for highway networks) must be provided for all methods to ensure reproducibility.  
*Significance*: A deeper theoretical exploration of how highway networks’ selective gating enhances capacity (e.g., relating to circuit complexity [8, 9] or residual networks [36]) would further elevate the contribution.

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*: Enables direct training of 100+ layer networks with no two-stage procedure; provides first insights into layer utilization dynamics via gate analysis; robust empirical validation.  
*Con*: Incomplete gradient analysis for continuous gates; insufficient justification for bias initialization; ambiguous experimental comparisons; insufficient statistical reporting.  

The paper makes a valuable contribution to deep learning by solving a fundamental training barrier, but requires technical clarifications and statistical rigor to meet NIPS standards. The core idea is compelling and well-executed, with the gate analysis being a standout strength. Addressing the weaknesses would substantially strengthen the paper’s impact.
