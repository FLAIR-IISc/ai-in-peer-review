Summary
This paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces data-dependent gating, inspired by LSTM, to facilitate information flow across many layers. Each highway layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where H is the usual nonlinear transform and T is a learned transform gate (with carry C = 1 − T). By biasing T toward “carry” at initialization, the network provides paths along which signals and gradients can pass with minimal attenuation, enabling direct training of very deep models using SGD. Empirically, the authors show that thin highway networks with 50–100 layers train faster and optimize substantially better than depth-matched plain networks with normalized initialization. On MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, highway networks match or exceed the accuracy of fitnets and maxout baselines of similar parameter/compute budgets without multi-stage teacher-student training. The paper further analyzes learned gate activations, shows data-dependent routing across layers, and uses lesion studies to assess layer importance. The work relates to prior NIPS and broader literature on depth/expressivity, initialization, maxout/locally competitive activations, skip connections, and knowledge distillation.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable mechanism that directly targets the practical obstacle of training very deep feedforward nets.
- Strong optimization results: depth scaling to 100+ layers with consistent convergence advantages over plain nets.
- Thoughtful analyses: gate activity patterns, class-conditional routing, and layer lesioning provide insight beyond accuracy numbers.
- Practical guidance (gate-bias initialization) and evidence that the approach works across fully connected and convolutional settings.
- Relevance to NIPS: advances in training deep architectures, connections to LSTM gating, distillation, and skip/shortcut ideas.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
1) Gradients/Jacobian (Eq. 5): Because T(x) = σ(WTx + b) is never exactly 0 or 1, Eq. 5 illustrates limiting cases rather than the general Jacobian. In general, y depends on x through both H and T, so dy/dx includes terms from H′(x) and from T′(x) modulated by H(x) − x. Please provide the exact backpropagation equations (gradients w.r.t. x, WH, and WT), and clarify whether any approximations are used in practice.

2) Initialization sensitivity: The constant negative bias for T is central to the training story. Please add a sensitivity analysis plotting performance (optimization curves and final accuracy) vs. initial gate bias across depths and datasets. This will help determine whether the bias acts like a regularizer (favoring “longer highways”) and how robust results are to its choice.

3) Comparability of settings: Statements such as “reported by recent studies in similar experimental settings” are ambiguous. Please tabulate precise experimental settings for each baseline comparison (data preprocessing/augmentation, architecture details, parameter counts, FLOPs, training schedule), and explicitly note any differences that preclude a direct comparison.

4) Reporting statistics: Reporting “best” accuracies is uncommon and can be misleading. Please emphasize mean ± standard deviation across multiple runs for all methods when available, and de-emphasize max performance. If external baselines lack variance estimates, state this clearly.

5) Theory and capacity: The proposed gating allows input-dependent identity paths and selective transformation, suggesting a data-dependent effective depth. A brief learning-theoretic or capacity discussion (e.g., path-wise view, effect on gradient flow and function class, implicit regularization via gate bias) would sharpen the contribution and situate it relative to fixed skip connections and layer-wise training.

Additional suggestions
- Include an ablation where C is learned independently rather than fixed to 1 − T, to test whether the coupling is necessary or beneficial.
- Report training time per epoch and total wall-clock convergence comparisons (plain vs. highway) to quantify practical efficiency.
- Provide results on the effect of removing gating (T ≡ 1, C ≡ 0) to separate the role of gating from architecture depth alone.
- Clarify convolutional block design (e.g., whether H includes BN/normalization, and whether T uses shared or separate receptive fields) and how dimensionality changes are handled between stages.
- Discuss parameter overhead from T and its impact on capacity vs. pure skip connections.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The empirical evidence for improved optimization with depth is convincing, and analyses are insightful. However, the paper would be stronger with full gradient derivations, clearer experimental comparability, and comprehensive statistical reporting. Ablations on T/C design and initialization sensitivity are important for causal claims.
- Clarity: Generally well written. Please revise ambiguous comparisons, add backprop details, and expand the methodological description of gating in convolutional blocks. Figures aid understanding; tables should prioritize mean ± std.
- Originality: The application of LSTM-like gating to deep feedforward networks with input-dependent routing is novel relative to prior skip connections and teacher-student methods at the time, and the analysis is a welcome addition.
- Significance: Enabling direct training of very deep feedforward/convolutional networks is impactful. Even where accuracy is not state-of-the-art, the methodological contribution and diagnostic analyses are valuable and likely to influence subsequent architectures and training practices.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, effective mechanism that materially eases optimization at depth.
- Solid empirical evidence across datasets with insightful analyses.
- Broad applicability and clear relevance to the NIPS community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing general gradient expressions and limited ablations leave some mechanistic claims under-supported.
- Comparability of baselines needs clearer documentation; statistical reporting should be improved.
- Limited theoretical framing of why/when gating yields gains over fixed shortcuts.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper addresses a central challenge in deep learning with a clear idea, credible empirical support, and thoughtful analysis. Addressing the gradient derivations, initialization sensitivity, reporting, and comparison clarity would make the contribution stronger and more reproducible.
