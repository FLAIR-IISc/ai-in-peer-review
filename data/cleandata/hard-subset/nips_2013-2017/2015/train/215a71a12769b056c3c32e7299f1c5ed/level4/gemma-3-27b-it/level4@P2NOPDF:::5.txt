This paper presents a compelling approach to training very deep feedforward networks, inspired by the gradient-trapping mechanism found in LSTMs. The core idea is both simple and elegant, and the accompanying analysis appears sound. I congratulate the authors on successfully implementing this technique, as I had independently considered a similar approach but had not yet begun implementation.

An alternative perspective on these very deep networks may be helpful. One could view them not as single, monolithic structures, but as networks comprised of multiple, shallower layers – where each ‘layer’ itself contains complex, non-linear sub-layers. This is reminiscent of architectural elements like the inception modules in GoogLeNet or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG, and it raises the question of whether highway networks can be similarly interpreted. Exploring this conceptual framing might provide further insights.

I suggest an experiment to investigate the potential for parameter sharing and efficient representation across different domains. Training a single, “thin and tall” network on both MNIST and CIFAR-10 (with a branching point at the input for domain-specific processing) could reveal interesting dynamics. Specifically, I would be interested in observing whether, during inference, this shared network exhibits similar patterns to those found in networks trained separately for each dataset. Furthermore, it would be valuable to determine if parameters underutilized when processing MNIST are effectively leveraged during CIFAR-10 inference. This experiment would illuminate how parameter sharing manifests within a deep hierarchical structure and assess its potential efficiency. 

While I have no major criticisms of this work, I strongly encourage the authors to evaluate their approach on larger and more challenging datasets, such as ImageNet. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method’s scalability and generalizability. Overall, this is a well-conceived and executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning.
