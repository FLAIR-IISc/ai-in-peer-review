

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks via adaptive gating inspired by LSTMs. The authors demonstrate that highway networks—where information flows through "highways" via transform and carry gates—can be trained effectively with SGD up to 100+ layers, contrasting sharply with plain networks that degrade rapidly with depth. Key prior work includes skip connections (e.g., [22,23]), knowledge distillation [25], and layer-wise training [26,27], but highway networks uniquely allow seamless gradient flow without auxiliary training stages. The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous empirical validation across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, showing highway networks match or exceed state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters while maintaining generalization. The analysis of gate behavior (e.g., Figure 2) provides compelling insights into how information routes through layers, revealing dynamic, input-dependent gating patterns rather than fixed shortcuts.

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality:** Experiments are thorough and well-designed. Highway networks consistently outperform plain networks (e.g., 100-layer highway vs. plain on MNIST: 100× lower loss), and ablation studies (Figure 4) demonstrate that deeper networks effectively utilize all layers for complex tasks like CIFAR-100.  
- **Originality:** The gating mechanism is a novel architectural innovation distinct from skip connections or distillation. It bridges LSTM-inspired dynamics with feedforward networks, enabling direct training of unprecedented depth without specialized optimizers.  
- **Significance:** The work fundamentally advances the feasibility of studying "extreme depth" in neural networks, a critical open problem. Highway networks provide a practical tool to explore depth-related benefits without compromising trainability.  
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, with clear notation (Equations 1–3) and insightful visualizations (Figures 2–4). The highway concept is intuitively explained, and implementation details (e.g., bias initialization) are sufficiently specified.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Technical Accuracy (Quality):** The claim that gradients follow Equation 5 is incorrect. Since the sigmoid gate *never* reaches exactly 0 or 1 (σ(x) ∈ (0,1)), the Jacobian simplification is misleading. The authors must clarify how backpropagation handles the continuous gate values (e.g., via chain rule on T(x)).  
- **Initialization Justification (Quality):** The proposal to set transform gate biases to fixed negative values (-1, -2, -3) based on depth lacks empirical support. A plot showing test accuracy vs. bias values (e.g., -10 to 0) would strengthen this claim and address whether the bias acts as an implicit regularizer.  
- **Experimental Rigor (Quality/Clarity):** Reporting only "best" accuracies (Tables 1–3) is statistically unsound. The authors should report mean ± standard deviation across multiple runs (as done for MNIST) to enable proper comparison. The statement "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is vague; the paper must explicitly compare setup differences (e.g., data augmentation, hyperparameters) with prior work like FitNets [25].  
- **Theoretical Depth (Significance):** The paper lacks a learning-theoretic analysis of why highway networks avoid vanishing gradients. While the empirical results are convincing, a brief discussion on how gating preserves gradient magnitude (e.g., via Jacobian conditioning) would deepen the contribution.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pros:*  
- Solves a fundamental problem (training very deep networks) with a simple, generalizable architectural fix.  
- Strong empirical evidence across multiple benchmarks, including ablation studies on layer utility.  
- High practical impact: enables direct training of deep networks without two-stage procedures.  
*Cons:*  
- Critical technical oversight in gradient computation (Equation 5).  
- Insufficient justification for bias initialization strategy.  
- Statistical reporting practices undermine reproducibility.  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a significant contribution to deep learning by enabling the practical study of extreme depth. The core idea is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. While the technical and reporting issues are serious, they are fixable with minor revisions. The paper’s significance outweighs these weaknesses, and it aligns strongly with NIPS’ focus on advancing the state of the art in machine learning. I recommend *acceptance* with the following revisions:  
1. Correct the backpropagation explanation (Equation 5) and clarify gradient computation.  
2. Add a bias sensitivity plot (accuracy vs. initial bias) and justify the proposed values.  
3. Report mean ± std. dev. for all accuracy results (not just "best").  
4. Explicitly compare experimental setups with prior work (e.g., FitNets [25]).  
5. Include a brief theoretical discussion of gradient preservation via gating.  

The paper is a strong candidate for NIPS 2015. Its insights into information routing in deep networks offer valuable directions for future work, and the highway architecture is likely to influence subsequent research on scalable deep learning.
