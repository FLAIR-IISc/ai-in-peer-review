Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses LSTM-inspired gating (transform and carry gates) to facilitate information flow across many layers, enabling the direct training of very deep networks with standard SGD. The authors show that even networks with tens to hundreds of layers can be optimized effectively, overcoming the degradation observed in similarly sized plain networks despite variance-preserving initialization. Empirical results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrate both ease of optimization and competitive generalization, including comparisons to FitNets trained with hint-based distillation. The analysis further examines gate biases and activities, data-dependent routing, and layer importance via lesion studies, providing insight into how depth is utilized.

The approach is clearly connected to LSTM gating for preserving gradients over long time horizons, and it complements prior work on improved initialization, alternative activation functions, skip connections, maxout units, and hint-based training. Unlike fixed skip connections, highway gates learn input-dependent routing paths (“information highways”) that can selectively forward or transform activations.

I had independently considered using LSTM-style gating to preserve gradients as a way to scale feedforward depth, but had not yet pursued it. I congratulate the authors for being the first to carry this through convincingly.

A possible conceptual perspective:
One can view a very deep network as a shallower network whose “layers” consist of sub-layers, i.e., each effective layer implements a richer nonlinearity than a standard rectifier/sigmoid layer. This is how I think about Inception modules in GoogLeNet and stacked 3×3 convolutions in VGG. I wonder if highway layers can be interpreted similarly: as forming composite, input-adaptive sub-layer structures that effectively act as more complex layers.

Suggested experiment:
I suggest training a single thin-and-tall highway network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR (with a fork only at the top for the two domains). The goal is to test whether, at inference time, the gate activity patterns resemble those found in models trained separately, and whether parameters that are underutilized for MNIST become actively used by CIFAR. This would clarify how sharing occurs in a very deep hierarchy and whether the gating leads to efficient reuse of depth across tasks. Analyses analogous to Figures 2–4 (mean gate activity, sample-specific gate activity, and lesion studies) could directly address this.

Strengths:
- Simple, elegant architectural idea—LSTM-inspired gates—that directly addresses the optimization challenges of depth.
- Strong optimization results showing stable training across increasing depth, with substantial advantages over plain networks.
- Solid empirical comparisons to FitNets without requiring multi-stage distillation.
- Insightful analyses of gate biases/activities, data-dependent routing, and layer contributions via lesioning.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The evaluation is limited to MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 under modest augmentation. I encourage the authors to test on a larger-scale dataset such as ImageNet to establish robustness and practical impact at scale.
- The paper could further discuss how highway layers relate to the notion of “complex layers” formed by sub-layer compositions (e.g., Inception/VGG-style stacks), and whether this view helps guide architecture design.

Arguments for and against acceptance:
Pros:
- Clear and impactful contribution addressing a central challenge in deep learning: training very deep feedforward networks.
- Method is conceptually simple, broadly applicable (not tied to specific nonlinearities), and effective in practice.
- Convincing experimental evidence and informative diagnostics.

Cons:
- Lack of large-scale experiments leaves open questions about performance at ImageNet scale.
- Additional discussion on the relationship to composite-layer architectures could strengthen the positioning.

Overall assessment:
This paper presents an LSTM-inspired mechanism that enables training very deep feedforward networks. The idea is simple and well-motivated, the experiments and analyses are solid, and the results are promising. I have no major complaints and recommend acceptance, with encouragement to extend the evaluation to larger datasets and to consider the proposed multi-task experiment to probe sharing and routing in very deep hierarchies.
