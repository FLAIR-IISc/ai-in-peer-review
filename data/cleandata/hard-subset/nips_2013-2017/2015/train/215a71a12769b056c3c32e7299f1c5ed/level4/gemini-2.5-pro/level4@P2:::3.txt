### **Summary**

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel deep neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of networks with hundreds or even thousands of layers. The core problem this work addresses is the degradation of performance and difficulty in training as network depth increases, a long-standing challenge in the field. Drawing inspiration from the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, the authors propose adding adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates to each layer. These gates dynamically regulate the flow of information, allowing parts of the input to be carried unchanged across many layers (on an "information highway") while other parts are transformed by the layer's non-linear function. The authors demonstrate empirically that this architecture allows for the direct training of extremely deep networks (up to 100 layers in their experiments) using standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD), a feat that is not possible with conventional "plain" deep networks. The paper also provides insightful analysis into the behavior of these gates, showing they learn data-dependent information routing and that network layers learn to contribute meaningfully to the final computation.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly significant problem in deep learning. The difficulty of propagating gradients and information through many layers has historically been a major bottleneck, limiting the exploration of very deep architectures. By providing a simple and effective solution, this work opens the door for researchers to investigate the properties and benefits of network depth on a scale that was previously impractical. The ability to train 100-layer networks with simple SGD is a major step forward.

2.  **Originality and Elegance:** The core idea is both novel and elegant. While skip-connections have been used before, the introduction of a dynamic, learnable gating mechanism inspired by LSTMs is a clever and powerful conceptual leap. It transforms the static architecture of skip-connections into a flexible, data-dependent routing system. The connection to LSTMs is well-motivated and insightful.

3.  **Technical Quality and Experimental Rigor:** The claims are well-supported by a strong set of experiments.
    *   The optimization experiments in Section 3.1 provide a clear and direct comparison against plain networks, convincingly demonstrating that highway networks do not suffer from performance degradation with increasing depth.
    *   The comparisons to state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10/100 and the competitive results with FitNets show that the architecture is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical tool that achieves strong performance.
    *   The analysis in Section 4 is particularly impressive and a major strength of the paper. The visualizations of gate activity and the layer lesioning study provide valuable insights into *why* the method works. This goes beyond simply reporting better metrics and helps build a deeper understanding of the model's behavior, showing that layers learn to be selectively active and contribute to the computation in a meaningful way.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out convincingly, the method is described precisely, and the experimental results are presented in an organized manner. The authors have done an excellent job of communicating a powerful idea effectively.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors (for Improvement)**

The weaknesses of this paper are minor and mainly relate to opportunities for further clarification or discussion.

1.  **Clarification of Gate Activity Analysis:** In Section 4.1, the authors state that for the CIFAR-100 case, "most transform gates are active on average," while for a single example, the activity is very sparse. However, the visualization in Figure 2 (second column, bottom row) appears to show very low average activity across most layers, with the mean activity being close to zero. Could the authors please clarify this point? Perhaps "active on average" is meant in a relative sense, but the wording could be made more precise to avoid potential confusion with the figure.

2.  **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** A simple alternative to enable training of very deep networks is to initialize each layer's weight matrix to be close to the identity transformation (e.g., identity matrix plus a small amount of noise), which would also promote "carry" behavior at the beginning of training. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on how the proposed learnable gating mechanism offers advantages over this simpler, non-adaptive scheme. The ability to learn routing is a clear advantage, but explicitly contrasting it with this baseline would make the contribution even clearer.

### **Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper presenting a high-quality, original, and significant contribution. It provides a clean and effective solution to a well-known, difficult problem in deep learning. The experimental validation is thorough, and the analysis of the model's internal workings is particularly insightful. This work is likely to have a substantial impact on the field and inspire future research into very deep architectures. I strongly recommend acceptance.
