I, too, had considered utilizing the LSTM trick of controlling gradient flow to train deeper networks, but hadn't initiated the work yet. Congratulations to the authors for beating me to it.

There's an alternative perspective worth considering: a very deep network can be viewed as a less-deep network with layers that have sub-layers, each exhibiting more complex nonlinear behavior than traditional rectifier or sigmoid layers. This is how I personally interpret the inception of GoogLeNet, the stacked 3x3 layers in VGG, and I wonder if this perspective can also be applied to highway networks.

One experiment I'd like to suggest to the authors is training a single, thin-and-tall network on both MNIST and CIFAR datasets (with a fork at the top for the two domains). I'm interested in seeing whether, at inference time, the network exhibits the same pattern as separately trained networks for each domain, and whether the underutilized model parameters in MNIST are picked up by CIFAR. In other words, I'd love to see how sharing occurs in a very deep hierarchy and whether it's as efficient as possible.

No major complaints. I encourage the authors to try their network on larger datasets such as ImageNet.

This paper presents an LSTM-inspired idea that enables the training of very deep feedforward networks. The idea is simple and elegant, and the analysis is solid.
