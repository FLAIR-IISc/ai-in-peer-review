

The paper presents a compelling approach for learning networks with selective layer bypasses via gate units, with the most valuable contribution being the analysis of gate output patterns. While the core idea is well-motivated and experiments provide reasonable evidence, several technical and presentation issues require clarification to strengthen the work.

(1) **Gradient Calculation for Sigmoid Gates**: The gate unit T employs a standard sigmoid activation, meaning its output never exactly reaches 0 or 1 (as noted by the authors). Consequently, the gradients derived in Equation 5 are inaccurate, as they assume boundary values. The authors must explicitly clarify how backpropagation handles this non-zero output range, including whether they use approximations (e.g., straight-through estimators) or alternative gradient schemes. Without this, the training dynamics remain ambiguous.

(2) **Bias Initialization Justification**: The proposal to use constant negative biases dependent on the number of layers lacks sufficient empirical support. While the analogy of "longer highways" is insightful, the claim that high negative biases regularize for deeper networks requires validation. The authors should include a sensitivity plot showing performance variation across initial bias values (e.g., from -0.5 to -5.0) to demonstrate the robustness of their initialization strategy. Currently, the justification appears speculative.

(3) **Comparison with Prior Work**: The phrase "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is vague and misleading. Either the cited studies use identical protocols (enabling direct comparison) or they do not (making comparison invalid). The authors must explicitly detail experimental differences (e.g., dataset splits, hyperparameters, evaluation metrics) between their work and prior methods to justify any claimed improvements. Without this, the comparison is unconvincing.

(4) **Accuracy Reporting**: Reporting only "max" accuracies in tables is statistically inappropriate and non-standard. The field expects mean accuracy with standard deviation to account for variance across runs. The authors should replace max values with mean ± std dev for all methods, including baselines, to enable proper statistical assessment of performance differences. This is particularly critical given the small sample size implied by single-run max values.

(5) **Theoretical Perspective**: A learning theory analysis would significantly strengthen the contribution. The paper describes gate units enabling identity propagation through selective layers, but does not explore *why* this is effective theoretically. For instance:  
- How does the input-dependent gate response prevent gradient vanishing?  
- What is the relationship between gate selectivity and generalization?  
- Does the approach relate to concepts like residual networks or skip connections in a formal sense?  
Addressing these would elevate the work beyond empirical validation.

**Overall Assessment**: The paper introduces a novel mechanism for layer-wise identity propagation with promising empirical results. However, the technical gaps in gradient handling, bias justification, experimental comparison, and statistical reporting weaken its impact. Addressing these points would substantially improve clarity and rigor. The core idea is strong, but the current presentation does not fully justify its advantages. I recommend **major revision** to resolve these issues before publication.
