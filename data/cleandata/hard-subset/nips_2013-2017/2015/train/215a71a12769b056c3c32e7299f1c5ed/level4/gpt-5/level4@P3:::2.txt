Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that incorporates input-dependent gating to regulate information flow across very deep stacks of layers. Each highway layer blends a transformed signal H(x) with a “carried” version of the input via a transform gate T(x) (with carry gate 1−T), enabling paths through the network where signals and gradients can propagate with little attenuation. The approach is explicitly inspired by LSTM gating in recurrent networks, adapted here to depth rather than time. The work situates itself among strategies to train deep networks, including careful initialization, improved optimizers, local competition, skip connections, deep supervision, and teacher–student distillation (FitNets). The authors compare directly to FitNets and discuss how learned, input-dependent routing differs from fixed shortcut connections.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates that highway networks mitigate the optimization difficulties that plague plain deep networks, enabling successful training of fully connected networks up to 100 layers and convolutional networks of substantial depth. On MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, highway networks are competitive with or better than baselines under comparable parameter and computation budgets. The analysis section probes gate behavior, depth utilization, and data-dependent routing, including “lesion” studies that quantify the contribution of each layer.

Strengths:
- Clear architectural idea with strong motivation from LSTMs, adapted thoughtfully to feedforward nets.
- Compelling evidence that optimization remains stable as depth grows, with much faster convergence than plain networks and no need for special activation functions beyond ReLUs or exotic optimizers.
- Careful experimental comparisons to FitNets under similar parameter/compute budgets, and additional MNIST/CIFAR evaluations with reasonable augmentation.
- Insightful analysis of gating behavior (biases, sparsity, class-conditional routing) and lesion experiments showing how depth is selectively used depending on task complexity.
- Practical training guidance (gate bias initialization), and released code/resources.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited ablations: a direct comparison to fixed skip connections of comparable depth/width (without gates) would help isolate the benefit of learned gating versus architectural shortcuts.
- Computational overhead: the paper does not quantify parameter and FLOP overhead introduced by gates, nor the runtime/memory cost relative to plain or skip-connected networks.
- Sensitivity to gate bias initialization is discussed but not systematically evaluated; stability across datasets and depths would be useful.
- Results on CIFAR use modest augmentation; while this isolates optimization effects, stronger baselines under richer augmentation would clarify competitiveness.
- Some experiments are described as “pilot” or “sanity checks,” and hyperparameters are sometimes reused across datasets; fuller tuning could strengthen claims of state of the art.

Quality:
Overall technically sound. The formulation is simple and well-motivated; training details are adequate, and the evidence that depth ceases to hinder optimization is convincing. The lesion and gate-activity analyses substantiate claims about data-dependent routing and depth utilization. A few additional ablations (e.g., ungated skip baselines; computational cost) would further solidify conclusions.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The notation and layer equations are easy to follow, and figures effectively illustrate gate behavior. Training guidance (e.g., negative gate bias) is concise and actionable. Minor issues: the text refers to “Tables 3 and 3” (p.5); fix numbering and cross-references. Consider briefly summarizing architectural variants (e.g., where dimensionality changes) in one place.

Originality:
While related to LSTM gating and prior skip/shortcut/distance supervision methods, the application of input-dependent gates to enable ultra-deep feedforward training is novel and well distinguished from FitNets and fixed skip connections. The analysis of learned routing within very deep feedforward networks is also a valuable contribution.

Significance:
The ability to train very deep nets with simple SGD, without elaborate pretraining or multi-stage distillation, is important. The technique is likely to influence architecture design and inspire further combinations with residual/skip ideas. The analysis could inform future work on conditional computation and depth-efficient models.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide a parameter/FLOP breakdown for highway vs. plain/skip baselines, and report wall-clock training time and memory usage.
- Include an ablation comparing highway layers to identity skip connections (same depth/width) to isolate the contribution of learned gating.
- Report sensitivity to gate bias initialization across depths and datasets; does performance degrade gracefully if bT is mis-set?
- Explore whether gates saturate at test time; do they induce conditional computation opportunities (e.g., pruning/skipping blocks)?
- Clarify how dimensionality changes are handled in deeper conv stacks (e.g., downsampling) and whether gates are shared or per-channel.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, well-motivated architectural idea with strong empirical evidence.
- Enables training of very deep networks using standard tools.
- Thorough and insightful analyses of gating and depth utilization.
- Competitive results under fair training budgets; open-source code.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing ablations versus fixed skip connections and incomplete accounting of computational overhead.
- Limited hyperparameter tuning and augmentation in some experiments; claims of SOTA are not the focus but could be strengthened.
- Some reliance on pilot experiments without exhaustive evaluation.

Minor issues:
- Fix “Tables 3 and 3” on p.5.
- A few typographical/formatting inconsistencies in references (authors/venues).

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper presents a clear and impactful architectural contribution with solid empirical validation and analysis. Even if some ablations and cost accounting are deferred, the work addresses a central challenge—training very deep networks—and is likely to have significant impact.
