Summary
This paper proposes a method for training neural networks with many stacked layers by introducing “information highways,” i.e., direct routes for information to flow to higher layers. The approach is presented as a generalization of existing cross-layer connection techniques and is shown through experiments to enable training of very deep networks and to improve classification accuracy. Overall, the idea is clearly explained and empirically well supported.

Relation to prior work
The method extends prior neural network techniques that use cross-layer connections, emphasizing improved information flow in deep architectures. While it builds on these ideas, the proposed approach is framed as a broader, more effective generalization.

Quality
- The core idea appears novel within the context of deep network training, and the experimental evaluation is solid.
- The empirical results convincingly demonstrate that the “information highway” mechanism facilitates optimization of very deep models and improves accuracy.
- The analysis sections are generally informative, but they could benefit from more diverse details or additional illustrations to deepen understanding.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The main messages are communicated effectively.
- The presentation makes it straightforward to grasp the motivation and the practical impact of the proposed method.

Originality
- The approach is sufficiently original while being a generalization of earlier techniques (e.g., cross-layer or skip connections).
- The contributions are positioned as an effective way to address the known difficulties of training very deep networks.

Significance
- The work is significant for the field: it addresses the pressing need to train deeper networks to achieve better performance in practical applications.
- The experimental evidence indicates that the technique not only simplifies optimization for deep architectures but also leads to improved classification accuracy, suggesting likely impact and adoption.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling motivation for enabling information flow in very deep networks.
- Strong experimental results demonstrating improved trainability and accuracy.
- Method is conceptually simple yet broadly applicable as a generalization of prior cross-layer techniques.
- Well-presented and easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- The analysis section could be strengthened with a broader variety of details or more illustrative examples/visualizations to better explain how and why the method works in practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practically meaningful approach to training very deep networks.
- Solid empirical support showing effectiveness and improved accuracy.
- Clear writing and strong presentation of the main ideas.
- High potential impact given the growing importance of very deep architectures.

Arguments against acceptance
- Analysis could be more thorough, with richer details or illustrations to enhance interpretability and insights.

Conclusion
This paper presents an effective method for training very deep neural networks via information highways (direct connections to upper layers). Although it generalizes prior techniques, the contributions are novel and well supported by experiments. The paper is clear, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. The primary area for improvement is a richer analysis section with more diverse details or illustrations.
