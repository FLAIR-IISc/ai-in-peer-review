This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward models. Drawing inspiration from the gating mechanisms in LSTMs, the authors propose inserting "transform" and "carry" gates at each layer. These gates learn to dynamically regulate information flow, allowing portions of the input to be passed unchanged across multiple layers—forming "information highways"—while other parts are processed by the layer's non-linear transformation. This architecture enables unimpeded gradient flow, directly addressing the optimization challenges that hinder the training of traditional deep networks. The authors provide strong empirical evidence, demonstrating that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained directly with standard SGD, significantly outperforming plain networks of equivalent depth in optimization tasks. Furthermore, they show that these models achieve competitive performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets and provide an insightful analysis of the learned gate behaviors, revealing that the networks learn to route information in a data-dependent manner.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a simple, elegant, and highly effective solution to the significant and timely problem of training very deep networks. Adapting LSTM-style gating for feedforward architectures is a novel contribution that directly tackles the gradient vanishing/exploding issues that had previously been a major barrier to increasing network depth. This work is highly significant and has the potential to be very influential, enabling researchers to explore the benefits of extreme depth across a wide range of applications.
*   **Technical Quality:** The claims are well-supported by a series of convincing experiments. The optimization experiments in Section 3.1 provide a clear and direct comparison against plain networks, compellingly demonstrating the architectural advantage of highways. The performance on CIFAR benchmarks is strong, showing that the ease of training does not come at the cost of generalization ability.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** A major strength of this paper is the analysis presented in Section 4. The authors go beyond simply reporting performance metrics and investigate *how* the trained networks operate. The visualizations of gate activities, the analysis of class-dependent routing, and the layer lesioning study provide valuable insights into the function of the gates and the effective depth utilized by the network for different tasks. This analysis significantly elevates the paper's contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core concept of the highway layer is explained intuitively and formally, and the motivation is well-grounded in prior work.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, several areas could be improved to strengthen the contribution further:

1.  **Clarity on Gradient Calculation:** Equation 5 presents the layer Jacobian for the idealized cases where the transform gate `T` is exactly 0 or 1. As the authors note, a sigmoid gate never reaches these exact values. While this equation is useful for building intuition, it could be slightly misleading. It would be beneficial to explicitly state that this is an illustrative simplification and perhaps briefly clarify the actual form of the derivative used in backpropagation to avoid any confusion.
2.  **Analysis of Bias Initialization:** The strategy of initializing the transform gate biases to small negative values is presented as a crucial element for successful training, but it is treated as a heuristic with general guidelines based on depth. The paper would be strengthened by a more systematic sensitivity analysis. A plot showing final performance as a function of the initial bias value, for a few different depths, would provide valuable insight and more concrete guidance for practitioners. This could also help explore the interesting interpretation of the initial bias as a regularizer that encourages the initial formation of long highways.
3.  **Statistical Rigor in Results:** The reporting of experimental results could be more rigorous. The authors state their intention to report mean and standard deviation over 5 runs, but many results in the tables (e.g., Tables 2 and 3) are presented as single performance figures. To provide a more robust evaluation and facilitate reliable comparisons, it is important to consistently report both the mean and standard deviation for all experimental results.
4.  **Precision in Experimental Comparisons:** When comparing with state-of-the-art methods, it is critical to be precise about the experimental settings. Any differences in data augmentation, model capacity, or optimization details between the authors' setup and that of the work being compared to should be explicitly stated to ensure the comparisons are interpreted fairly.

### Further Comments

The paper focuses primarily on an empirical demonstration, but the highway architecture has fascinating theoretical implications. The ability of the network to learn input-dependent routing and dynamically vary its effective depth suggests connections to theories of network expressivity and the optimization landscape of deep models. While a full theoretical treatment is beyond the scope of this paper, a brief discussion of these potential theoretical avenues would be a welcome addition and a great direction for future work.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, important, and impactful idea. The strengths—particularly the originality of the architecture and the insightful analysis of its learned behavior—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to details of presentation and experimental reporting and can be addressed in a revision. The work represents a significant advance in our ability to train very deep neural networks. I strongly recommend acceptance.
