This paper presents a simple and elegant idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in LSTMs, to enable the training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the accompanying analysis is solid. The extension of this gradient-trapping "trick" from recurrent to feedforward architectures is a natural and insightful step, and the authors are to be congratulated for this valuable contribution.

The paper prompts an interesting alternative perspective on network depth. One could view a very deep network, like the proposed highway network, not as a monolithic stack of hundreds of simple layers, but rather as a moderately deep network composed of more complex computational "blocks" or "macro-layers." In this view, each block (comprising several highway layers) learns a more complex non-linear transformation than a traditional rectifier or sigmoid layer. This interpretation aligns with the architectural motifs seen in models like GoogLeNet's Inception module or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG nets. I would be interested to hear the authors' thoughts on whether this is a valid way to conceptualize the behavior of their highway networks.

To further investigate the properties of this architecture, I would suggest an additional experiment involving multi-task learning. Specifically, the authors could train a single deep and narrow network on both MNIST and CIFAR-10 simultaneously, with separate output heads for each task. The central question is whether the network's dynamic behavior during inference would mirror the layer utilization patterns observed in the networks trained on individual tasks. For example, would the network learn to allocate deeper, more complex layers to the CIFAR-10 task while primarily using shallower layers for the simpler MNIST task? Such an experiment would provide valuable insights into the dynamics of feature sharing and specialization within a very deep hierarchy and test the efficiency of its representational capacity.

The paper has no major weaknesses. To further strengthen the empirical results and demonstrate the scalability of the approach, I would encourage the authors to test their architecture on a larger and more complex dataset, such as ImageNet. This would provide a more comprehensive validation of its effectiveness for challenging, real-world problems.
