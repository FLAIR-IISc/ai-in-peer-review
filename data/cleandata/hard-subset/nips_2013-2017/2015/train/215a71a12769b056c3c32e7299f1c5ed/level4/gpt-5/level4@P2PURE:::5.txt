I had also considered using an LSTM-style trick to preserve/“trap” gradients in order to grow networks much deeper, but I had not started that work. Congratulations to the authors for getting there first.

Another perspective: a very deep network can be viewed as a shallower network whose layers contain sub-layers, so each “layer” exhibits richer nonlinear behavior than a traditional rectifier/sigmoid layer. This is how I interpret the Inception modules in GoogLeNet and the stacks of three 3×3 convolutions in VGG. I wonder if highway networks can be understood in the same way.

I suggest one experiment: train a single thin-and-tall network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR, branching only at the top for the two domains. I am interested in whether, at inference time, the shared trunk exhibits the same patterns seen in networks trained separately on each dataset, and whether parameters underutilized by MNIST are picked up by CIFAR. In other words, I would like to see how sharing emerges in a very deep hierarchy and whether it is as efficient as possible.

No major complaints. I encourage the authors to test the network on larger datasets such as ImageNet.

This paper presents an LSTM-inspired idea that enables the training of very deep feedforward networks. The idea is simple and elegant, and the analyses are solid.
