This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture called "highway networks" that allows for the training of very deep networks. The authors argue that the depth of neural networks is crucial for their success, but training becomes increasingly difficult as depth increases. They draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and introduce an adaptive gating mechanism that enables unimpeded information flow across many layers. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and achieve competitive performance on several benchmark datasets.

The paper is well-written, and the idea is clearly described. The experiments are convincing to a certain degree, and the authors have made an effort to analyze the learned networks with respect to the pattern of gate outputs. However, there are a few issues that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the gate unit T is modeled with a standard sigmoid activation function, which means that the response value is never exactly 0 or 1. The authors should explain in the paper how backpropagation is exactly performed in these networks, as the gradients in Equation 5 are not correct.

Secondly, the effect of initialization is always tricky to analyze. The authors propose to use constant negative biases depending on the number of levels, but this is not entirely satisfying. It would be helpful to see a plot of the performance with respect to the initial value of the bias. The initial bias acts as a kind of regularizer, supporting networks with "longer highways." A more detailed analysis of the initialization scheme would strengthen the paper.

Thirdly, the authors mention "recent studies in similar experimental settings" but do not clarify the differences in the experimental setups. This makes it difficult to compare the results directly. The authors should provide more details about the experimental settings and how they differ from other studies.

Fourthly, the authors report "max" accuracies in tables, which is uncommon and not reasonable from a statistical point of view. It would be more informative to provide mean accuracy and standard deviation for every method, especially when multiple runs are performed.

Lastly, it would be interesting to study the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The paper describes how to use additional gate units in neural networks that allow for layers to simply act as an identity. The gate unit response depends on the input and therefore allows propagating inputs through a large number of very selective layers. A more in-depth analysis of the theoretical implications of this approach would be valuable.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to training very deep networks, and the experiments demonstrate its potential. However, addressing the above issues would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel neural network architecture that allows for the training of very deep networks.
* The authors demonstrate competitive performance on several benchmark datasets.
* The paper provides a clear description of the idea and the experiments.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how backpropagation is performed in highway networks.
* The initialization scheme is not thoroughly analyzed, and a more detailed study is needed.
* The experimental settings are not clearly compared to other studies, making it difficult to evaluate the results.
* The paper reports "max" accuracies, which is not statistically reasonable.
* The theoretical implications of the proposed approach are not thoroughly explored.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a detailed explanation of how backpropagation is performed in highway networks.
* Analyze the initialization scheme more thoroughly and provide a plot of the performance with respect to the initial value of the bias.
* Clarify the differences in the experimental setups and provide more details about the settings.
* Report mean accuracy and standard deviation for every method.
* Explore the theoretical implications of the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective.
