

The paper presents a compelling approach to modeling gate units in neural networks, with a particularly strong contribution being the analysis of learned gate patterns and their relationship to network behavior. The core idea is well-motivated and the experimental results provide reasonable validation. However, several critical clarifications and methodological refinements are required before the paper can be accepted. Below, I detail the key concerns that must be addressed.

First, the modeling of the gate unit T with a standard sigmoid activation function introduces a significant technical oversight. As noted by the authors, the sigmoid never outputs exactly 0 or 1, which directly impacts the gradient computation in Equation 5. Specifically, the gradients derived assume binary gate behavior, but the continuous nature of the sigmoid means the backpropagation procedure must account for the derivative of the sigmoid (i.e., σ'(x) = σ(x)(1-σ(x))). The authors must explicitly clarify how backpropagation is implemented in this context—whether they use the sigmoid derivative as standard, or if they employ a straight-through estimator (e.g., for gates close to 0/1). Without this detail, the theoretical foundation of the method remains ambiguous.

Second, the initialization strategy for the gate biases (using constant negative values proportional to the number of layers) lacks empirical justification. While the authors describe this as a "regularizer" that encourages "longer highways" (a useful analogy), the choice of bias magnitude is arbitrary without evidence of its impact. The authors should include a sensitivity analysis plot showing performance (e.g., validation accuracy) across a range of initial bias values. This would demonstrate whether the proposed initialization is robust or if it critically depends on a specific hyperparameter, which is essential for reproducibility and practical adoption.

Third, the reference to "recent studies in similar experimental settings" (Section 3.2) is vague and undermines the paper’s comparative claims. Either the cited works use identical experimental protocols (allowing direct comparison), or they do not (precluding meaningful comparison). The authors must explicitly delineate the differences in experimental setups (e.g., dataset splits, hyperparameter ranges, or network architectures) between their method and prior work. Without this, the comparison is unsubstantiated and potentially misleading.

Fourth, the reporting of "max" accuracy values in Tables 1–2 is statistically inappropriate and non-standard in machine learning research. Maximum accuracy ignores the variance across multiple runs, which is critical for assessing model reliability. The authors should replace all "max" entries with mean accuracy and standard deviation (e.g., "78.2 ± 0.8%"), as this is the convention for evaluating neural network performance. If the authors wish to include maximum values for completeness, they must still report the mean and standard deviation alongside them.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a brief exploration of the method’s theoretical properties. The authors describe how gate units enable identity propagation through selective layers, but they do not connect this to learning theory. For instance: Does the gating mechanism facilitate deeper network training by reducing the vanishing gradient problem? Could the gate response function (e.g., sigmoid) be analyzed for its impact on the optimization landscape? A concise discussion of these aspects would strengthen the paper’s contribution and contextualize the empirical results within broader theoretical principles.

In summary, the paper’s core idea is promising and its experimental results are largely convincing. However, the technical gaps in the gradient derivation, the unverified initialization strategy, the unclear comparison to prior work, the inappropriate reporting of accuracy metrics, and the absence of theoretical insights must be resolved. Addressing these points would significantly elevate the paper’s rigor and clarity. The authors should prioritize clarifying the backpropagation procedure, providing empirical validation for the bias initialization, explicitly comparing experimental setups, adopting standard statistical reporting for accuracy, and adding a short theoretical discussion. With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.
