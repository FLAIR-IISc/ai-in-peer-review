

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks that enables the direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks through adaptive gating mechanisms. The key innovation is the introduction of transform and carry gates that regulate information flow across layers, allowing networks with hundreds of layers to be trained effectively using standard stochastic gradient descent without requiring specialized initialization schemes or two-stage training procedures. The authors demonstrate that highway networks maintain strong performance as depth increases, unlike plain networks where performance degrades significantly with depth.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning by addressing a fundamental challenge in training very deep networks. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, demonstrating how the adaptive gating mechanism allows the network to smoothly transition between identity mappings (when gates are closed) and transformed representations (when gates are open). This insight is well-supported by extensive experimental results across multiple datasets, including MNIST and CIFAR-10/100. The authors' experiments show that highway networks consistently outperform plain networks in terms of both training stability and final performance, with 100-layer highway networks significantly outperforming similarly-sized plain networks.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, the core idea is elegant and conceptually simple, making it accessible to a broad audience while effectively solving a significant problem in deep learning. Second, the experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, comparing highway networks to plain networks across multiple architectures and datasets. The authors provide convincing evidence that highway networks can be trained directly without the need for specialized initialization or two-stage training procedures. Third, the analysis of the learned gating mechanisms provides valuable insights into how information flows through deep highway networks, revealing that the network dynamically routes information based on input rather than relying on fixed shortcut connections.

The paper also makes a thoughtful connection to previous work, particularly to LSTM networks and the recent "Fitnets" approach, positioning their contribution clearly within the broader literature. The authors' comparison to Fitnets is particularly compelling, showing that highway networks achieve comparable or better performance with a simpler, single-stage training procedure.

The primary weakness of the paper is that it does not explore the relationship between highway networks and other architectural approaches that also aim to improve information flow through deep networks, such as GoogLeNet's inception modules or VGG's stacked 3x3 convolutions. The reviewer finds it interesting to consider whether highway networks can be viewed as a different perspective on deep network architecture, where each "layer" effectively contains sub-layers with more complex non-linear behavior. This connection could provide valuable context for the community.

The reviewer would be particularly interested in seeing the authors explore parameter sharing across different tasks. Specifically, it would be valuable to test whether a single highway network trained on both MNIST and CIFAR-10 could effectively share parameters between the two domains, with the network utilizing different parts of its depth for each task. This would provide deeper insight into how highway networks dynamically route information based on input, and whether the architecture enables more efficient parameter sharing than conventional deep networks.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical detail to allow for reproduction of the results. The authors have carefully addressed potential concerns about the practicality of their approach, demonstrating that highway networks generalize well to unseen data and can be trained efficiently even with hundreds of layers.

In conclusion, this is a strong contribution to the field that effectively addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning. The idea is novel, the implementation is sound, and the experimental validation is comprehensive. The paper makes a compelling case that highway networks represent a significant step forward in enabling the training of very deep networks. The reviewer recommends acceptance at NIPS.

The authors are encouraged to explore the potential of highway networks on larger-scale benchmarks like ImageNet, as this would further demonstrate the scalability of their approach to more challenging computer vision tasks. The simplicity and effectiveness of the highway network architecture suggest it could become a valuable tool for deep learning practitioners.
