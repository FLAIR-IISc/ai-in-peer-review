This is a well-crafted and timely paper. The core idea is clearly presented, and the empirical results are convincing to a reasonable extent. The most compelling aspect is the attempt to analyze the learned networks through the distribution and patterns of gate outputs, which provides valuable insight beyond raw performance numbers.

Major comments

1) Backpropagation through the gate unit T: The gate uses a sigmoid activation, so its output is never exactly 0 or 1, as the authors note. However, the gradients stated in Eq. 5 appear inconsistent with a fully differentiable formulation. Please provide an explicit derivation of the gradients used for backpropagation through the gated block, including partial derivatives with respect to:
- The gate parameters (weights and biases),
- The transform path parameters,
- The inputs to the block.
If any approximation (e.g., straight-through estimator, gradient clipping, or stopping gradients through certain paths) is used, this should be stated and justified. Clarifying this will resolve ambiguity between a conceptual “hard gating” interpretation and the implemented “soft gating” dynamics.

2) Initialization and the role of the bias: The proposed constant negative bias for T that scales with depth is plausible but not fully satisfying without a sensitivity analysis. Since the initial bias effectively regularizes the network by encouraging longer “highway” paths early in training, please include:
- A sweep over initial bias values (per-depth or per-block), reporting performance curves,
- Training dynamics (e.g., gradient norms, convergence speed, stability),
- Quantitative measures of “effective depth,” such as the empirical distribution of T across layers and its evolution over training.
This would clarify how robust the method is to initialization and how the bias shapes the learned routing behavior.

3) Comparisons and experimental settings: The statement “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is too vague. Either the settings are the same (in which case direct comparison is warranted) or they differ (in which case direct numerical comparison should be avoided or clearly qualified). Please detail and align the following where possible: datasets and splits, preprocessing/augmentation, model capacity (depth/width/parameters), training budget (epochs, compute), optimization (learning rate schedules, regularization), and evaluation protocol. If exact alignment is not possible, qualify the comparisons accordingly or provide in-house reimplementations of key baselines under matched settings.

4) Reporting best vs. mean performance: Reporting “max” accuracy is uncommon and statistically uninformative. Please report mean accuracy and standard deviation (or standard error) over multiple independent runs for all methods, along with the number of runs. Where appropriate, include median and interquartile ranges or confidence intervals. This will improve the reliability and interpretability of the results and avoid overstating improvements that may be within run-to-run variance.

5) Learning-theoretic perspective and selectivity: The approach effectively augments deep networks with input-dependent gating that allows layers to act as (near) identities, enabling information to traverse many layers with selective transformations. This invites a more formal perspective. While a full theory may be beyond scope, it would strengthen the paper to:
- Discuss how gating may reduce effective capacity or act as a data-dependent regularizer (e.g., limiting active paths),
- Relate to path-based capacity measures and implicit ensemble effects,
- Quantify selectivity via metrics such as sparsity of open gates, distribution of path lengths, or mutual information between gate activations and inputs/labels,
- Connect to related mechanisms (e.g., residual connections, dropout, conditional computation) and clarify similarities and differences.

Additional suggestions for clarity and analysis
- Provide a concise algorithmic description (pseudo-code) of forward and backward passes through a gated block, including shapes and parameter counts, to ease reproducibility.
- Include diagnostic plots of gate outputs across depth and training time, and correlate these with performance. For example, show how the mean and variance of T per layer evolve and whether certain layers consistently saturate.
- Report computational overhead (parameters, FLOPs) and wall-clock training/inference time relative to comparable baselines.
- When using depth-dependent biases, clarify whether these are learned thereafter or fixed, and whether tying/untiying biases across layers affects results.

Overall assessment

The paper addresses an important problem and offers a principled and practically useful mechanism for facilitating information flow in deep networks. The empirical section is promising, and the analysis of gating behavior is a notable strength. Addressing the issues above—particularly the precise gradient formulation, a more thorough initialization study, properly controlled comparisons, and statistically sound reporting—would substantially improve the paper’s rigor and impact. I am inclined to recommend acceptance conditional on these clarifications and additions.
