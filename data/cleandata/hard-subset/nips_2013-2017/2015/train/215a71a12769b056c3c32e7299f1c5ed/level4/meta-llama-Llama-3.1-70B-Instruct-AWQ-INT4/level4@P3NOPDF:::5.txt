This paper presents an intriguing idea inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) that enables the training of very deep feedforward networks. The concept is simple and elegant, and the analysis is solid. I commend the authors for exploring this idea and presenting it in a clear manner.

Upon reading the paper, I couldn't help but think that I, too, had considered utilizing the LSTM trick of trapping gradients to grow a network deeper, but hadn't yet begun working on it. Kudos to the authors for beating me to it!

From a different perspective, one could argue that a very deep network is essentially a not-so-deep network where layers have sub-layers. Each "layer" exhibits more complex non-linear behavior than a traditional rectifier or sigmoid layer. This is how I personally perceive the inception of GoogLeNet, the three stacked 3x3 layers in VGG, and I wonder if this viewpoint is also applicable to highway networks.

One experiment that I would suggest the authors consider is training a single, thin and tall network on both MNIST and CIFAR datasets (with a fork at the top for the two domains). I am interested in seeing whether, at inference time, the network exhibits the same pattern as found in networks trained separately for each domain. Specifically, I would love to see whether the model parameters that are underutilized in MNIST are picked up by CIFAR. In other words, I am curious about how sharing occurs in a very deep hierarchy and whether it is as efficient as possible.

Overall, I have no major complaints about the paper. I encourage the authors to test their network on larger datasets, such as ImageNet, to further demonstrate its capabilities.
