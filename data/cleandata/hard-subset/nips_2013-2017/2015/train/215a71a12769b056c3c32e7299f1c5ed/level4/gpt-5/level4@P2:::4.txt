This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses input-dependent gates—borrowed conceptually from LSTM recurrent networks—to enable information and gradients to traverse very deep stacks of layers without attenuation. Each layer blends a transformed signal with a carried signal via a transform gate T(x) and an implicit carry gate 1−T(x). With gates initialized toward carry behavior (negative gate biases), even networks with tens to hundreds of layers can be trained directly with SGD. The authors present optimization studies showing that plain networks degrade rapidly with depth while highway networks train reliably and quickly. On MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, highway networks achieve competitive or better accuracy than comparably sized baselines and train without multi-stage distillation (FitNets). Analyses of gate activities and lesion experiments suggest data-dependent routing and that deeper models can learn to utilize many layers when the task is complex.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself well within research on depth and optimization, including normalized initializations, alternative optimizers, locally competitive activations, skip/shortcut connections, deep supervision, and distillation (FitNets). The key distinction is learned, input-dependent gating rather than fixed skip paths, making the method closer in spirit to LSTM gating but applied across depth rather than time.

Quality: The architectural formulation is clear and technically sound. The optimization experiments are convincing: as depth increases, highway networks maintain or improve training loss and converge faster than plain networks with similar parameter budgets. The empirical results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 are competitive given modest augmentation and fixed hyperparameters. The analysis sections (gate activity profiles, class-conditional routing, and layer lesioning) are informative and support the claimed mechanism. That said, the experimental scope is limited to small/medium-scale datasets; demonstrating performance on a larger dataset (e.g., ImageNet) or providing stronger comparisons against contemporary very deep CNNs would strengthen the claims about significance. Ablations on the choice C=1−T versus learning C independently, sensitivity to gate bias initialization, and compute/parameter efficiency would also be valuable.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The architecture and training procedure are explained succinctly with the right level of mathematical detail, and figures clearly illustrate gate behavior and “information highway” effects. Minor proofreading issues appear in the draft (e.g., “varying varying,” “propogation”), but they do not impede understanding. Adding more intuition about how gating evolves during training and a brief computational cost breakdown per layer would further improve clarity.

Originality: Applying LSTM-like gating to feedforward depth with learned, input-dependent routing is a novel and well-motivated idea that generalizes fixed skip connections. The work is distinct from FitNets (teacher-student training) and from deep supervision; it also goes beyond simple residual/shortcut designs by making routing adaptive. The related work is cited appropriately.

Significance: The contribution is meaningful. By enabling straightforward SGD training of very deep networks without complex initialization or multi-stage procedures, highway networks open practical avenues for deeper architectures and provide analytical tools (e.g., lesioning) to study depth utilization. The demonstrated accuracy gains under constrained budgets are encouraging, though broader validation would solidify impact.

Pros:
- Simple, elegant architecture that directly addresses optimization barriers in very deep nets.
- Strong evidence that training remains stable as depth grows, with faster convergence than plain nets.
- Competitive performance on CIFAR-10/100 without teacher networks or heavy augmentation.
- Insightful analyses demonstrating input-dependent routing and layer importance.
- Clear connection to LSTM gating; method is easy to implement.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation is limited to small/medium datasets; no results on large-scale benchmarks.
- Comparisons to the strongest contemporary deep models are limited; more thorough baselines would help.
- Limited ablation on design choices (e.g., independent carry gate, bias schedules, activation functions).
- Computational overhead from gates is not quantified (parameters, FLOPs, memory).
- Some minor editorial issues.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include ablations on C learned independently versus C=1−T, gate bias initialization values, and activation functions for H.
- Report compute/memory overhead and parameter efficiency relative to plain and skip-connection baselines.
- Add larger-scale results or at least deeper CIFAR models with stronger augmentation to test scalability.
- Expand comparisons to alternative shortcut-based architectures and/or knowledge-distillation approaches under matched budgets.
- Provide more runs and variance for very deep settings (50–100+ layers) and show robustness to hyperparameters.

Recommendation: I lean toward acceptance. The paper delivers a clear architectural advance with solid evidence that it enables training of very deep networks, provides insightful analyses, and is likely to influence subsequent work on depth and routing in neural networks.
