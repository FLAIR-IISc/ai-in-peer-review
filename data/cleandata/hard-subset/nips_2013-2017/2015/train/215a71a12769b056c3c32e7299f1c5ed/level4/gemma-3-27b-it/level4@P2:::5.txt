This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. Inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, highway networks incorporate “information highways” that allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers, addressing the challenges of vanishing gradients and poor propagation of activations that typically hinder the training of deep networks. The core idea—adaptive gating units regulating information flow—is both simple and elegant.

The authors relate their work to a substantial body of previous research addressing the difficulties of training deep networks. They appropriately cite efforts focused on improved optimizers (e.g., [13, 14, 15]), initialization strategies ([16, 17]), activation functions ([20, 21]), skip connections ([22, 23, 24]), and knowledge distillation ([25, 26]). The paper also correctly positions highway networks in relation to recent advances like FitNets [25]. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network architecture presents a novel approach to addressing the challenges of training very deep networks. While inspired by LSTMs, its application to feedforward networks is a significant contribution.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical justification, drawing parallels to LSTM, is convincing. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that highway networks can be trained to significantly greater depths than traditional networks using standard gradient descent.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, architecture, and experimental setup of highway networks. The notation is well-defined, and the mathematical formulation is accessible.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 provide strong empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of highway networks. Comparisons to existing methods, like FitNets, demonstrate competitive performance.
*   **Analysis:** The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figure 2 & 4) provides valuable insights into the inner workings of highway networks, showing how they dynamically route information and potentially utilize their full capacity.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results are promising, the experiments are primarily focused on relatively small datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100). Evaluating the performance of highway networks on larger and more complex datasets, such as ImageNet, would further strengthen the findings.
*   **Alternative Perspective:** The authors briefly acknowledge an alternative interpretation – viewing a very deep network as a network with complex sub-layers – but could explore this connection more fully. Relating highway networks to existing techniques like Inception modules and stacked convolutional layers in VGG would be insightful.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the transform gate bias as a hyperparameter, and while the suggested guidelines are helpful, a more thorough investigation into the sensitivity of performance to this and other hyperparameters would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning – training very deep networks.
*   The proposed highway network architecture is a novel and effective solution.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of these networks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The evaluation is limited to relatively small datasets.
*   The connection to other related architectures (e.g., Inception, VGG) could be explored more thoroughly.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The highway network architecture is a promising approach to training very deep networks, and the experimental results are convincing. While further evaluation on larger datasets and a more detailed analysis of hyperparameters would be beneficial, the paper’s novelty, soundness, and clarity justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2015.

I also find the reviewer's suggestion of testing a single thin-and-tall network on multiple datasets intriguing and would encourage the authors to consider this experiment in future work. It could reveal interesting properties of the learned representations and the potential for knowledge transfer within a very deep hierarchy.
