**Summary**

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to enable the training of extremely deep feedforward models. The core idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, is to introduce adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates at each layer. These gates dynamically regulate the flow of information, allowing parts of the input to be carried unchanged across many layers (on "information highways") while other parts are transformed by the layer's non-linear function. This structure effectively mitigates the optimization difficulties, such as vanishing gradients, that typically plague very deep "plain" networks. The authors demonstrate empirically that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained directly with SGD, outperforming plain networks of similar depth on MNIST. They further show competitive results on CIFAR-10/100, matching or exceeding the performance of more complex training schemes like FitNets, and provide insightful analysis into the learned behavior of the gating units.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The core idea is explained with excellent clarity, and the connection to LSTMs provides a strong intuitive foundation. The claims are well-supported by a series of rigorous experiments. The optimization comparison in Section 3.1 provides a direct and convincing demonstration of the architecture's primary advantage over plain networks. The analysis in Section 4 is a particular strength, offering valuable insights into the model's inner workings. The visualizations of gate biases and activities, along with the layer lesioning study, effectively show that the network learns to utilize its depth in a data- and task-dependent manner, which is a compelling and desirable property. The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written.

**Originality and Significance**

The application of an LSTM-style gating mechanism to ease the training of very deep feedforward networks is a novel and elegant contribution. While skip connections have been used previously, the adaptive, data-dependent nature of the highway gates is a significant conceptual step forward. This work directly addresses a fundamental and widely recognized challenge in the deep learning community: how to effectively train networks of increasing depth. The ability to train networks of hundreds of layers with simple SGD is a significant achievement that could unlock new research into the properties and capabilities of extremely deep architectures. The results suggest that for complex problems, highway networks can learn to utilize their full depth, while for simpler problems, they can effectively "turn off" unneeded layers—a behavior that is difficult to obtain in conventional deep networks. This work has the potential to be highly influential and widely used by other researchers.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Solution:** The paper presents a simple, intuitive, and highly effective architectural solution to the critical problem of training very deep networks. The inspiration from LSTMs is both clever and well-justified.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate that highway networks overcome the optimization hurdles of deep plain networks. The performance on MNIST and CIFAR benchmarks is strong, particularly the ability to match FitNets without a complex multi-stage training procedure.
3.  **Excellent Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 provides deep insights into the network's behavior, demonstrating dynamic, data-dependent information routing and task-dependent layer utilization. The lesioning study is particularly effective in quantifying the contribution of individual layers.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** By making it feasible to train extremely deep networks, this work opens up new avenues for research in representation learning and could have a significant impact on how deep models are designed.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Arguments against Acceptance are minor)**

1.  **Dataset Scale:** While the experiments on MNIST and CIFAR are solid, the full potential and robustness of the architecture would be more compellingly demonstrated on a larger-scale dataset like ImageNet. This would allow for a direct comparison with other prominent deep architectures like VGGNet and GoogLeNet. I strongly encourage the authors to pursue this in future work.
2.  **Relation to other Architectures:** The paper could benefit from a brief discussion on how highway networks conceptually relate to other architectures that create complex transformations within a "block," such as GoogLeNet's Inception modules or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGGNet. One could view these as creating a more complex, hand-crafted layer function, whereas highway networks learn to dynamically blend a simple transformation with an identity mapping. Exploring this connection could further contextualize the contribution.

**Questions and Further Suggestions**

A potentially insightful experiment would be to explore the multi-task learning capabilities of this architecture. For instance, one could train a single deep, thin highway network on both MNIST and CIFAR-10 simultaneously (perhaps with a shared trunk and separate output layers). This could provide valuable insights into how the gating mechanism facilitates feature sharing and specialization across tasks of varying complexity. It would be fascinating to see if the layers underutilized for MNIST (as suggested by Figure 4) are effectively recruited and utilized for the more complex CIFAR task within a single shared model. This could be a powerful demonstration of the network's ability to learn efficient parameter allocation.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a simple but powerful idea with strong empirical support and insightful analysis. It addresses a significant problem in the field and is likely to have a lasting impact.
