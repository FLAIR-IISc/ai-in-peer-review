### **Summary**

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to enable the training of extremely deep feedforward models. The core idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to introduce adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates at each layer. These gates dynamically regulate the flow of information, allowing parts of the input to be carried unchanged across many layers (on an "information highway") while other parts are transformed by the layer's non-linear function. This architectural modification directly addresses the optimization difficulties, such as vanishing gradients, that have historically prevented the effective training of plain networks beyond a few dozen layers. The authors demonstrate that highway networks with hundreds of layers can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and achieve strong performance on benchmark datasets like MNIST and CIFAR.

### **Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance**

This is an excellent paper with a clear and significant contribution to the field. Its strengths are numerous and align well with the NIPS criteria.

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and well-known problem: the difficulty of training very deep neural networks. While previous work has tackled this through specialized initialization schemes, advanced optimizers, or complex multi-stage training procedures (e.g., FitNets), this work proposes a simple, elegant, and powerful architectural solution. The adaptation of LSTM-style gating from the temporal domain to the depth dimension of feedforward networks is a highly novel and insightful idea. This contribution has the potential to be very impactful, as it makes the exploration of extreme network depth substantially more accessible.

2.  **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is high. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that are both rigorous and thoughtfully designed.
    *   The optimization experiments (Section 3.1) provide a direct and convincing comparison against plain networks, clearly demonstrating that the highway architecture overcomes the degradation problem as depth increases from 10 to 100 layers.
    *   The comparison with FitNets (Section 3.3.1) is particularly strong, as it shows that a very recent and relevant method requiring a complex two-stage training procedure can be matched or surpassed by a highway network trained in a single stage.
    *   The analysis in Section 4 is a standout feature. The authors go beyond simply reporting performance metrics and provide valuable insights into the network's learned behavior. The visualizations of gate activations, the analysis of data-dependent information routing, and the layer lesioning study (Figure 4) are excellent. This investigation demonstrates that the network learns to use depth effectively for complex problems (CIFAR-100) while effectively ignoring unneeded layers for simpler ones (MNIST), which is a highly desirable property.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established, the proposed architecture is explained concisely, and the experimental results are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The authors do a great job of positioning their work within the context of existing literature, clearly explaining the connections to LSTMs and the distinctions from other deep learning techniques.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is very strong, and the weaknesses are minor. The following are suggestions for further strengthening the work.

1.  **Gate Mechanism Simplification:** The paper simplifies the gating to `C = 1 - T`, where `C` is the carry gate and `T` is the transform gate. While this is effective, it couples the two decisions. It would be interesting to discuss the potential of a more flexible mechanism where the carry and transform gates are controlled independently, more akin to the forget and input gates in an LSTM cell. While this would add parameters, it might allow for more complex learned behaviors. This could be mentioned as a direction for future work.

2.  **Sensitivity to Gate Bias Initialization:** The authors propose initializing the transform gate biases with small negative values to encourage carry behavior early in training. This is a sensible heuristic inspired by prior work on LSTMs. While the paper provides a general guideline, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the training process to this hyperparameter would be beneficial. For example, does training fail completely if the bias is initialized at zero?

3.  **Quantitative Analysis of Information Flow:** The analysis of data-dependent routing in Section 4.1 is very insightful but largely qualitative. A possible extension could involve a more quantitative measure. For instance, could one compute an "average effective path length" for inputs from different classes to see if the network learns to route "harder" examples through more transformations? This is merely a suggestion for further research inspired by the excellent analysis already present.

### **Minor Comments**

*   On page 5, the text references "Table 3" twice in the final paragraph of Section 3.3.2. The second reference should likely be to another table or figure, or the text should be rephrased. (It appears it should be Tables 2 and 3).

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a very solid and well-executed paper that presents a novel, simple, and effective solution to a major problem in deep learning. The proposed highway network architecture is well-motivated, and its efficacy is demonstrated through strong empirical results and an impressive in-depth analysis of the trained models. The work is of high quality, original, clearly communicated, and significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
