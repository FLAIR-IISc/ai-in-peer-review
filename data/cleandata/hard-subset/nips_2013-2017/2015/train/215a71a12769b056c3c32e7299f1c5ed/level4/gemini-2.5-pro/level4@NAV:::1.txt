This paper presents a valuable contribution, with a clearly articulated core idea and experimental results that are largely persuasive. A particular strength of the work is the authors' analysis of the learned networks through the lens of their gate output patterns.

(1) The gate unit T utilizes a standard sigmoid function, meaning its output asymptotically approaches but never reaches 0 or 1, a point the authors acknowledge. This raises a concern regarding the strict mathematical correctness of the gradients presented in Equation 5. To address this, the authors should provide a detailed explanation of the exact implementation of backpropagation within their proposed architecture.

(2) While acknowledging the difficulty of analyzing initialization, the proposed strategy of setting constant negative biases based on the number of levels is not fully justified. To better understand its impact, I suggest including an ablation study that plots performance against the initial bias value. This is especially relevant given that the initial bias seems to act as a regularizer, with larger negative values favoring the "longer highways" described in the paper—a very effective analogy.

(3) The reference to "similar experimental settings" is ambiguous. For a comparison to be valid, the settings must be identical; otherwise, a direct comparison is not appropriate. The authors should precisely clarify the extent to which the experimental setups align with or differ from the cited studies to substantiate their comparative analysis.

(4) The practice of reporting "max" accuracies in the results tables is unconventional and lacks statistical robustness. For a more rigorous and meaningful comparison, the authors should instead provide the mean and standard deviation of the accuracy, calculated over multiple runs, for all evaluated methods for which this information is available.

(5) A promising direction for future work would be to analyze this architecture from a learning theory standpoint. The mechanism of using input-dependent gates to allow layers to approximate an identity function, thereby enabling information to propagate through highly selective deep networks, seems ripe for theoretical exploration.
