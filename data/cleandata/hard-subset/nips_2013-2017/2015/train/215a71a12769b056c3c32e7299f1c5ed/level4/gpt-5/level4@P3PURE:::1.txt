I like this paper. The core idea is clearly presented, and the experiments are convincing to a certain degree. The most compelling aspect, in my view, is the authors’ attempt to analyze the learned networks with respect to the pattern of gate outputs.

(1) The gate unit T is modeled with a standard sigmoid activation. As acknowledged by the authors, the response is never exactly 0 or 1, which raises questions about the correctness of the gradients in Eq. 5. As written, these gradients do not appear to be correct. The paper should explicitly and rigorously explain how backpropagation is performed in these networks, including the precise gradients used for the gates.

(2) Initialization effects are notoriously difficult to analyze, and the proposal to use constant negative biases that depend on the number of levels is not fully satisfying. Please provide a plot of performance as a function of the initial bias value. In this setup, the initial bias effectively serves as a regularizer, with a large negative bias favoring networks with “longer highways” (I appreciate this analogy). A systematic study would clarify the sensitivity and robustness of the method to this choice.

(3) The phrase “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is unsatisfying. Either the methods use the same experimental setting—which would allow a direct comparison—or they do not, in which case a direct comparison is inappropriate. The paper should clearly delineate the differences among experimental setups to justify any comparisons.

(4) Reporting “max” accuracies in tables is uncommon and not statistically sound. Please report mean accuracy and standard deviation for every method (where available). This is important for a fair and interpretable comparison.

(5) It would be interesting to study the proposed approach from a learning-theoretic perspective. The paper shows how adding gate units allows layers to act as near-identities when appropriate. Because the gate response depends on the input, the network can propagate inputs through many layers in a highly selective manner. A theoretical treatment of this mechanism and its implications for optimization and generalization would substantially strengthen the work.

Overall, this is a promising contribution with a clear idea and interesting analyses. Addressing the issues above—especially clarifying the backpropagation details for the gates, providing a systematic study of bias initialization, and reporting statistically meaningful metrics—would significantly improve the paper.
