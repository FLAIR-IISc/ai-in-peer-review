

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via stochastic gradient descent. Inspired by LSTM gating mechanisms, highway networks incorporate adaptive transform and carry gates that dynamically regulate information flow across layers, effectively creating "information highways" where activations can propagate without attenuation. The authors demonstrate that highway networks overcome the optimization difficulties plaguing plain deep networks, achieving state-of-the-art results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 with significantly greater depth than prior work, while also enabling detailed analysis of layer-wise behavior. This work directly addresses a key challenge in deep learning—training very deep networks—and extends related ideas like skip connections and knowledge distillation (e.g., FitNets [25]) by introducing a dynamic, input-dependent gating mechanism rather than fixed shortcuts.

**Strengths**  
The paper’s primary strength lies in its compelling empirical validation of the core hypothesis: highway networks remain trainable at extreme depths where plain networks fail. The experiments on MNIST (Figure 1) and CIFAR-10 (Table 2) clearly show highway networks matching or exceeding the accuracy of shallower plain networks (e.g., 100-layer highway vs. 20-layer plain) while training directly with SGD—eliminating the need for two-stage distillation. The analysis of gate behavior (Figure 2) is particularly insightful, revealing how gates dynamically route information based on input (e.g., sparse activation patterns for specific classes in Figure 3) and how layers contribute to performance (Figure 4). This provides unprecedented interpretability into deep network dynamics, demonstrating that highway networks do not merely "skip" layers but actively learn to utilize depth adaptively. The clarity of the architecture description and the thorough experimental design (including hyperparameter robustness and statistical reporting where available) further strengthen the contribution.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Gradient calculation in Equation (5) is incorrect.** The transform gate uses a sigmoid activation, so *T* ∈ (0,1) for all inputs. Thus, the Jacobian conditions *dy/dx = I* (when *T* = 0) and *dy/dx = H’* (when *T* = 1) are mathematically unattainable. The authors must clarify how backpropagation handles this (e.g., via continuous gradients) and revise Equation (5) to reflect the actual gradient behavior.  
2. **The bias initialization strategy lacks justification.** The recommendation to use constant negative biases (e.g., -1, -2, -3) based solely on network depth is arbitrary. A plot showing performance sensitivity to initial bias values (e.g., across -10 to 0) would strengthen the claim that this is a robust hyperparameter. Without it, the approach risks appearing as a heuristic rather than a principled design.  
3. **Ambiguous comparison to "recent studies."** The phrase "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" (Section 3.3.1) is misleading. If experimental setups differ (e.g., data augmentation, architecture details), direct comparisons are invalid. The authors must explicitly clarify methodological differences with FitNets [25] and other baselines to justify their claims.  
4. **Inappropriate reporting of accuracy.** Tables 1–3 report only "max" accuracy, ignoring statistical variability. For reproducibility and rigor, the authors should consistently report mean ± standard deviation across multiple runs (as they do for some experiments). Reporting only the best result obscures the reliability of their method.  
5. **Lack of theoretical grounding.** While the paper notes that gates enable identity propagation, it omits a learning-theoretic perspective. A brief discussion of how the gating mechanism affects the network’s capacity or generalization bounds (e.g., relating to the "number of linear regions" [10]) would elevate the contribution beyond empirical validation.

**Pro and Con Acceptance**  
*Pros:*  
- Solves a fundamental problem (training depth) with a simple, effective architecture.  
- Enables direct training of networks >100 layers without distillation.  
- Provides novel insights into layer utilization and input-dependent routing (Figures 2–4).  
- Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks with clear comparisons.  

*Cons:*  
- Critical technical oversight in gradient calculation (Equation 5).  
- Initialization strategy requires empirical justification beyond arbitrary values.  
- Statistical reporting is incomplete (no mean ± std. dev.).  
- Ambiguous experimental comparisons lack methodological transparency.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to deep learning, advancing the state of the art in training very deep networks. The highway architecture is original, well-motivated, and demonstrably effective. While the weaknesses noted above are important for completeness, they do not undermine the core technical contribution. The paper’s clarity, empirical rigor, and novel analysis of network dynamics align strongly with NIPS’ criteria for quality, significance, and originality. The proposed approach is likely to influence future work on deep network design and training.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a critical open problem with a novel solution. The suggested revisions (particularly clarifying the gradient calculation and improving statistical reporting) are straightforward to address in a camera-ready version. The work represents a meaningful step toward realizing the full potential of deep architectures.
