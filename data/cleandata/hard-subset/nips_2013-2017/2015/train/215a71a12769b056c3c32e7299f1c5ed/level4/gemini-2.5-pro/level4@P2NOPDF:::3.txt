This paper introduces a novel network architecture, inspired by the gating mechanisms of LSTMs, to enable the effective training of extremely deep neural networks. This is a high-quality, well-written paper presenting an original idea with compelling results.

The machine learning community has long recognized that increasing network depth is a crucial factor for improving model performance. However, practical depth has been severely constrained by optimization challenges, particularly the problem of vanishing and exploding gradients. These difficulties have arguably limited the widespread success of very deep architectures for decades. This paper directly tackles this fundamental challenge by proposing "highway networks," which allow unimpeded information flow across many layers. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by successfully training networks with up to 100 layers, a significant achievement.

The experiments are thoughtfully designed and presented. The analyses of the network's internal dynamics, particularly those showing how information is routed and which layers are dynamically active, are especially insightful. These visualizations provide a clear and fascinating understanding of the function of the gating mechanisms and substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.

Specific Comments:

1.  In Section 4.1, the authors state that "most transform blocks are active on average." This seems counter-intuitive, as one might expect the carry gates to be more active to preserve information flow in such deep networks. Could the authors please verify this statement or clarify its meaning? It is possible this is a typo and should read "not active on average."

2.  The paper would benefit from a brief discussion comparing highway networks to a simpler baseline for deep network training. For instance, a very deep plain network could be initialized such that each layer's weight matrix is close to the identity matrix (perhaps with a small amount of noise), which would also facilitate gradient flow. A comment on how the proposed dynamic gating mechanism provides an advantage over such a static initialization scheme would further highlight the novelty and utility of the transform gates.

In summary, this is an excellent and timely paper that presents a simple yet powerful solution to a major challenge in deep learning. The proposed architecture is elegant, and the work was a pleasure to read. It represents a significant step forward in our ability to train truly deep neural networks.
