- Introduces "highway networks," using LSTM-inspired adaptive gates to enable direct training of very deep feedforward nets (100+ layers) with standard SGD.  
- Provides empirical evidence that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, vastly outperforming plain networks on MNIST and matching/exceeding baselines on CIFAR-10/100.  
- Achieves competitive CIFAR results with fewer parameters than prior methods (e.g., Highway B: 88.1% vs. Fitnet B: 87.7% with fewer parameters).  
- Formalizes the highway layer (Equation 3) showing interpolation between identity carry and transformation, giving a clear theoretical justification.  
- Shows negative bias initialization for transform gates (b_T = -1, -2, -3) enables stable training even for 1000-layer networks.  
- Presents comprehensive ablation studies demonstrating single-stage end-to-end training outperforms two-stage distillation methods like Fitnets.  
- Analyzes information flow via gate activity and layer-lesioning, revealing dynamic depth usage (e.g., many layers matter for CIFAR-100 but not MNIST).  
- Argues originality: adaptive, learned gating distinguishes this work from fixed skip connections, LSTM inspiration, and distillation approaches.  
- Highlights significance: simplifies training (single-stage SGD), improves resource efficiency, and provides new tools for studying depth/trainability.  
- Notes minor weakness: limited exploration of gate dynamics and why gate biases evolve (more analysis linking bias changes to input complexity would help).  
- Clarity is strong: writing, figures, and experimental details are clear; minor suggestions to clarify "block" terminology and add a brief CIFAR-100 comparison in Table 3.  
- Recommendation: technically sound, experimentally validated, and novel—recommend acceptance.
