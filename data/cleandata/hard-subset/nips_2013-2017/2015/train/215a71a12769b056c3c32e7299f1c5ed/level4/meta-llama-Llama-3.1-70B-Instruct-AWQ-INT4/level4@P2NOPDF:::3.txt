This paper proposes an innovative approach to training very deep networks by leveraging Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units. The manuscript is of exceptionally high quality, with well-organized content and engaging writing style. The concept presented is original, and the results are intriguing and thought-provoking.

A common challenge in machine learning is the trade-off between increasing the number of layers in neural networks to improve performance and overcoming the difficulties associated with initialization and decaying or exploding gradients. Historically, these difficulties have hindered the widespread adoption of deep neural networks for nearly three decades. This paper directly addresses this problem and successfully demonstrates a method for training 100-layer networks.

The experiments presented, particularly those illustrating the routing of information and layer importance, are noteworthy and provide valuable insights. These findings have significant implications for the development of deep neural networks and highlight the potential of the proposed approach.

Specific comments and suggestions for improvement include:

In Section 4.1, it appears that the statement 'most transform blocks are active on average' should be revised to 'not active on average' to maintain consistency and accuracy.

One potential approach to initializing each layer of a very deep network is to use the identity matrix, possibly with a small amount of added noise. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on how the transform gates in highway networks differentiate from this simple initialization scheme, providing further context and depth to the analysis.

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that presents a novel method for training very deep neural networks. The manuscript is a pleasure to read, and the ideas and results presented are likely to have a significant impact on the field of machine learning.
