This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate training of very deep neural networks. The core idea—inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks—is to incorporate adaptive gating mechanisms that allow information to flow unimpeded across many layers, creating “information highways.” The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained directly with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) even with hundreds of layers, a significant improvement over traditional feedforward networks which struggle with optimization at depth. The inclusion of analysis regarding the learned gate patterns is a strong point of this work.

The paper builds upon a growing body of research addressing the challenges of training deep networks. Recent advances have focused on improved optimizers (e.g., [13, 14, 15]), initialization strategies ([16, 17, 18, 19]), activation functions ([20, 21]), and skip connections ([22, 23, 2, 24]).  Related techniques such as FitNets [25] and neural history compression [26] attempt to improve training through knowledge transfer or layer-wise approaches. However, these methods often require specialized training procedures or remain limited in depth. This work distinguishes itself by demonstrating successful training of extremely deep networks with a straightforward application of SGD.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network architecture provides a fresh perspective on addressing the vanishing gradient problem in deep networks. The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism offers a compelling approach to enabling information flow across many layers.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrate that highway networks can be effectively trained to achieve competitive results, even surpassing existing methods in certain configurations.
*   **Analysis of Learned Networks:** The analysis of gate outputs and layer importance (Figures 2 & 4) provides valuable insights into the inner workings of highway networks and suggests that the gating mechanism is indeed learned and contributes to performance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are clearly explained. The motivation for the approach is well-articulated, and the mathematical formulation is concise.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gradient Calculation:** The authors state that the gate unit T’s sigmoid activation never precisely reaches 0 or 1, yet the provided Jacobian in Equation 5 assumes these exact values. This discrepancy needs clarification – a detailed explanation of how backpropagation is implemented within these networks is required.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** While the authors propose an initialization scheme based on negative biases, the justification for the specific values chosen (dependent on network depth) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic investigation, perhaps presented as a plot of performance versus initial bias value, would strengthen this aspect. The observation that the bias acts as a regularizer favoring longer highways is insightful and warrants further discussion.
*   **Vague Comparison:** The phrase "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" lacks specificity.  The authors should either provide a direct comparison with concrete results from those studies or clarify why a direct comparison is not possible due to differences in experimental setup.
*   **Reporting of Results:** Reporting only maximum accuracies in tables is not standard practice.  Mean accuracy and standard deviation should be provided for all methods to enable a proper statistical assessment.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a theoretical justification for why highway networks facilitate training. Exploring the impact of these gating mechanisms from a learning theory perspective—considering their effect on the optimization landscape or gradient propagation—would significantly enhance the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the benefits of highway networks on several benchmark datasets.
*   The analysis of learned gate patterns provides valuable insights into the behavior of these networks.
*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in deep learning – training very deep networks – and offers a compelling solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a detailed explanation of backpropagation with the proposed gate mechanism is a concern.
*   The initialization scheme feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification.
*   The limited theoretical analysis leaves open questions about the underlying principles that drive the success of highway networks.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea and convincing experimental results. While some aspects—particularly the gradient calculation and initialization analysis—require further clarification, the potential impact of this work is significant. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the identified weaknesses in a revised version.
