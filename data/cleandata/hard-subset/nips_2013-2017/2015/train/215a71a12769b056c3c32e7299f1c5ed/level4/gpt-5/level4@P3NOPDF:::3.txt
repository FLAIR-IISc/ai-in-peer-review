This paper adapts LSTM-style gating to deep feedforward architectures (highway networks), enabling the effective training of networks with on the order of 100 layers. The manuscript is clear, technically sound, and presents an original and timely idea. By using transform and carry gates to regulate information flow, the method directly addresses vanishing/exploding gradients and initialization difficulties that have historically limited very deep networks. The empirical demonstrations are compelling, and the analysis of information routing and layer importance is particularly insightful.

Significance and novelty:
- The work tackles a core obstacle in deep learning—maintaining stable signal and gradient propagation across many layers—by incorporating learnable gates that allow data-dependent shortcut pathways. This provides a principled mechanism for adaptive depth and gradient flow, going beyond ad hoc initialization strategies.
- Demonstrating successful training of 100-layer networks is noteworthy and of practical importance, especially given the historical difficulty of optimization in such regimes.
- The experiments that visualize which layers and pathways are used provide valuable intuition about how depth is exploited, underscoring the interpretability benefits of the proposed gating scheme.

Technical quality and empirical evaluation:
- The approach is well-motivated, with clear connections to LSTM gating and a coherent training recipe. The design encourages identity-preserving behavior at initialization while allowing the model to learn complex transformations where beneficial.
- Experimental results convincingly show that very deep models can be trained and that the gating mechanism leads to meaningful, data-dependent routing. The analyses of layer utilization are a highlight.

Clarity:
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow. Figures and analyses are informative and support the main claims.

Specific comments and suggestions:
- In Section 4.1, the sentence “most transform blocks are active on average” appears inconsistent with the subsequent analysis. Based on the presented histograms/metrics, it seems that most transform blocks are not active on average. Please clarify and, if appropriate, correct the wording to avoid confusion.
- It would be helpful to explicitly contrast highway networks with a deep network initialized to the identity (perhaps with small noise). While identity initialization can ease early optimization, the transform/carry gates in highway networks provide data-dependent, learnable routing and dynamic skip behavior that persist throughout training, which is not captured by a fixed identity initialization. A brief discussion or ablation would strengthen the case that gating offers a qualitatively different and more powerful mechanism than initialization alone.
- If space permits, a short note on computational overhead introduced by gating (parameters and runtime) versus the optimization benefits would aid practitioners.

Overall assessment:
This is an excellent and impactful paper. It presents a clear and original idea, addresses a central challenge in training very deep networks, and supports its claims with thoughtful analyses and strong empirical results. The insights on information routing and layer importance are particularly compelling. With minor clarifications (notably in Section 4.1) and a brief discussion contrasting with identity initialization, I strongly recommend acceptance.
