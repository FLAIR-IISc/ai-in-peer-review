I like this paper: the core idea is clearly presented and the experiments are reasonably convincing. I especially appreciate the effort to analyze learned networks through the distribution and patterns of gate outputs.

1) The gate unit T uses a standard sigmoid, so its output is strictly between 0 and 1. As a result, the gradients in Eq. 5 appear incorrect or at least incomplete. Please provide the exact backpropagation derivation for these networks, making explicit how gradients flow through the gates, whether any approximations (e.g., straight-through) are used, and how the coupling between transform and carry components is handled.

2) Initialization is always delicate, and the recommendation to use constant negative biases that depend on depth is not entirely satisfying. Please include a sensitivity analysis showing performance as a function of the initial gate bias (e.g., a sweep/curve). In this setup, the initial bias effectively acts as a regularizer: strong negative values encourage “longer highways” by favoring identity paths early in training. Quantifying this effect would make the design choice more principled.

3) The phrase “reported by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is too vague. Either the compared methods share the same experimental protocol (allowing fair comparison) or they do not (precluding direct numerical comparisons). Please clarify any differences in data preprocessing, augmentation, architectures, training budgets, hyperparameters, and evaluation protocols, and qualify the comparisons accordingly.

4) Reporting “max” accuracy is uncommon and statistically weak. Please report mean and standard deviation (or confidence intervals) over multiple runs for all methods where possible, and specify the number of trials. If prior work only reports a single number, note this clearly and avoid placing undue emphasis on maxima.

5) It would be valuable to complement the empirical results with a learning-theoretic perspective. The proposed gates enable layers to act as identities in an input-dependent manner, effectively supporting very long selective paths. Formal insights into the induced function class, capacity/regularization effects of the gate bias, conditions under which identity paths dominate, and implications for gradient flow and effective depth (e.g., via path-based complexity measures or Rademacher complexity) would strengthen the contribution and help explain the observed robustness.

Overall, this is a promising and well-motivated paper. Addressing the gradient clarification, initialization sensitivity, statistical reporting, and comparison protocol would substantially improve clarity and rigor. A theoretical treatment—even preliminary—would further elevate the work.
