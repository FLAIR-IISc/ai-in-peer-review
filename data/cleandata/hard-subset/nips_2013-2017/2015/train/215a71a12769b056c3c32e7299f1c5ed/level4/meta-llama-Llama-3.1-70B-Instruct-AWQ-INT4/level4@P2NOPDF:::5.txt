I commend the authors for exploring the concept of training very deep feedforward networks, a notion that I had also considered, particularly with regards to leveraging the LSTM technique of controlling gradient flow. The authors' innovative approach is well-deserved of recognition.

An alternative perspective on this concept is that a very deep network can be viewed as a not-so-deep network, where each layer is comprised of sub-layers. This structure allows for more complex non-linear behavior within each "layer," deviating from traditional rectifier or sigmoid layers. This perspective resonates with the inception of GoogLeNet, the stacked 3x3 convolutional layers in VGG, and I wonder if this viewpoint can also be applied to understanding highway networks.

To further explore the capabilities of this approach, I propose an experiment where a single, thin yet tall network is trained on both MNIST and CIFAR datasets, with a fork at the top to accommodate the two domains. I am particularly interested in observing whether the network exhibits similar patterns during inference as separately trained networks for each domain. Moreover, I would like to see whether the model parameters underutilized in MNIST are adopted by CIFAR, shedding light on the efficiency of sharing in a very deep hierarchy.

Overall, the paper presents a simple yet elegant idea inspired by LSTM, allowing for the training of very deep feedforward networks. The analysis provided is thorough and well-supported. My only suggestion is to test the network on larger datasets, such as ImageNet, to further demonstrate its capabilities.
