This paper proposes the use of an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism, called highway networks, as a method of training very deep networks. This is a high-quality, well-written paper. The idea is original, and the results are quite interesting.

Machine learning researchers frequently find that they get better results by adding more and more layers to their neural networks, but the difficulties of initialization and decaying/exploding gradients have been severely limiting. Indeed, the difficulties of getting information to flow through deep neural networks arguably kept them out of widespread use for 30 years. This paper addresses this problem head-on and demonstrates one method for training 100-layer nets.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the existing literature on deep neural networks and identify the key challenges in training very deep networks. They then propose a novel architecture, highway networks, which are designed to overcome these challenges. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their proposed method.

The experiments presented in the paper are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep neural networks. The results show that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent, and they achieve state-of-the-art performance on several benchmark datasets.

The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the behavior of highway networks, including the routing of information and layer importance. These experiments are particularly interesting, as they provide insight into how highway networks work and how they differ from traditional neural networks.

Specific comments:

* In Section 4.1, it seems that the phrase "most transform blocks are active on average" should be changed to "not active on average."
* One could initialize each layer of a very deep network with the identity matrix (possibly with a small amount of noise). It might be worth commenting on how transform gates differentiate highway networks from this simple scheme.
