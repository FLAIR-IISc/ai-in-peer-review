This paper presents an interesting approach to constructing deep neural networks with explicitly modeled “highways” facilitated by novel gate units. The core idea is clearly articulated and the experimental results, while not conclusive in all aspects, are generally convincing. A particularly strong aspect of this work is the authors’ attempt to analyze the learned network behavior through the lens of gate output patterns, providing valuable insight into how these highways are utilized during training.

However, several points require clarification and/or further investigation to strengthen the contribution. 

First, the implementation of the gate unit ‘T’, utilizing a standard sigmoid activation, inherently prevents the gate from reaching truly binary states (0 or 1), as acknowledged by the authors. This has implications for the gradients presented in Equation 5, which appear to assume a discrete gate behavior. The paper needs to explicitly detail how backpropagation is performed through these networks *given* the continuous nature of the gate output. A precise description of the gradient calculation is crucial for reproducibility and understanding.

Second, while acknowledging the sensitivity to initialization is prudent, the proposed strategy of employing constant negative biases scaled by the number of levels feels somewhat *ad hoc*. The rationale behind this specific scaling is not fully explained, and its effect appears regularization-like, promoting networks with effectively "longer highways," a comparison I find insightful. To better understand this relationship, I recommend including a plot demonstrating performance as a function of the initial bias value. This would illustrate the impact of this initialization scheme and potentially reveal an optimal range.

Third, the phrase “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” lacks precision. A direct comparison is meaningful only if the experimental setups are genuinely comparable. The authors should either explicitly define the similarities and differences between their experimental protocol and those cited, justifying the comparison, or refrain from making such a broad statement. Clarity on this point is essential.

Fourth, reporting only “max” accuracies in the tables is statistically weak and deviates from standard practice. To provide a more robust evaluation, the authors should consistently report the mean accuracy *and* standard deviation for all methods presented. This will allow for a proper assessment of statistical significance and variability.

Finally, beyond the empirical results, exploring the theoretical underpinnings of this approach would significantly strengthen the paper. The proposed architecture effectively introduces additional gate units enabling layers to function as identity mappings. The input-dependent nature of these gates allows for the propagation of information through potentially numerous highly selective layers. It would be valuable to investigate this mechanism from a learning theory perspective. Does this architecture offer advantages in terms of representational power, generalization, or optimization landscape compared to traditional deep networks? Addressing such questions would elevate this work beyond a compelling empirical study.



In summary, this paper introduces a promising approach to building deep networks with explicitly defined highways. Addressing the points above – specifically detailing the backpropagation process with continuous gates, justifying the initialization scheme, clarifying experimental comparisons, and adopting standard statistical reporting practices – would significantly enhance the clarity and impact of this work. Furthermore, exploring the theoretical implications of this architecture would offer a valuable contribution to the field.
