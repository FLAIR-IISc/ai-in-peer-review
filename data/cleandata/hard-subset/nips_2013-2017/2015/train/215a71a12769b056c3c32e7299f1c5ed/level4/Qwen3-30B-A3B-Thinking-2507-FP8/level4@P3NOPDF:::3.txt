

This paper presents a compelling approach to training extremely deep neural networks using LSTM units as a core component, effectively addressing the persistent challenges of vanishing/exploding gradients and poor information flow that historically constrained deep network adoption for decades. The work is exceptionally well-executed, with a clear, rigorous contribution that significantly advances the state of the art in deep learning architecture design. The authors demonstrate that their method enables stable training of networks exceeding 100 layers—far surpassing prior practical limits—and provide insightful experimental analysis of information routing dynamics within these architectures.

The core innovation lies in leveraging LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms to explicitly control information flow across layers, thereby mitigating the fundamental obstacles that have plagued deep network training since the 1980s. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a principled architectural shift that directly tackles the root causes of gradient instability. The experimental results are robust and illuminating, particularly the analysis of layer activation patterns and the quantification of "layer importance" through information flow metrics. These findings are not only technically significant but also provide valuable theoretical intuition about how deep networks process information—a rare and welcome contribution in the literature.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to validation. The technical exposition is precise, and the figures (especially those visualizing information routing) are highly effective in conveying complex concepts. The authors demonstrate thoroughness in their experimental design, including ablation studies that isolate the contribution of their gating mechanism and comparisons against relevant baselines. The results consistently show clear performance gains across multiple benchmarks, underscoring the practical utility of the approach.

Specific minor suggestions for improvement:
1.  In Section 4.1, the phrase "most transform blocks are active on average" appears to be a typo. Given the context of the analysis (where the authors discuss *reduced* activation in deeper layers), this should read "most transform blocks are *not* active on average" to align with the observed results and the paper's narrative.
2.  The paper would benefit from a brief discussion contrasting the authors' transform gates with a simple initialization strategy where each layer is initialized to the identity matrix (with minor noise). While such initialization can improve stability for shallow networks, the transform gates in this work provide a *learned*, adaptive mechanism for information flow control that dynamically adjusts per layer and per input—offering a significant advantage over fixed initialization. Explicitly highlighting this distinction would strengthen the paper's conceptual contribution.

The significance of this work cannot be overstated. By enabling the reliable training of networks with 100+ layers, the authors open the door to architectures capable of modeling vastly more complex functions, potentially leading to breakthroughs in domains requiring extreme representational capacity. The method is generalizable and could influence future deep learning frameworks beyond the specific experiments presented. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS, offering both a practical solution to a long-standing problem and deep theoretical insights into deep network dynamics.

This is a high-impact, technically sound contribution that exemplifies the best of NIPS research: rigorous, innovative, and clearly communicated. The minor points noted above are purely editorial and do not detract from the paper's overall quality or significance. It was a pleasure to review this exceptionally well-executed work.
