I had also considered using the LSTM trick of gradient clipping to enable training deeper networks, but hadn’t begun implementation yet. I congratulate the authors on being the first to successfully explore this approach!

There’s potentially a different way to conceptualize this work: a very deep network can be seen as a shallower network composed of layers that themselves contain sub-layers. Each of these “layers” exhibits more complex non-linear behavior than a traditional rectified linear unit (ReLU) or sigmoid layer. This is how I interpret the inception modules of GoogLeNet, the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG, and I wonder if a similar perspective applies to highway networks as well.

I would suggest one experiment to the authors: train a single, tall-and-thin network on both MNIST and CIFAR-10 (perhaps with a branching point at the input for the two domains). I’m interested in whether, at inference time, this network exhibits similar patterns to those trained separately for each domain. Specifically, would model parameters underutilized during MNIST training be effectively leveraged for CIFAR-10? In other words, I would like to see how sharing occurs within a very deep hierarchy, and whether this sharing is optimally efficient.

I have no major criticisms. I encourage the authors to evaluate the network’s performance on larger datasets such as ImageNet.

This paper presents an idea inspired by LSTMs that enables the training of very deep feedforward networks. The idea is both simple and elegant, and the analysis is thorough.
