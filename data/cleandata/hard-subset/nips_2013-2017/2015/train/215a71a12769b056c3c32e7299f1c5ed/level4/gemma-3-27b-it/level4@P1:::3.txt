This paper proposes the use of LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms – termed “highway networks” – to facilitate training of very deep neural networks. The central idea is to allow for unimpeded information flow across many layers, addressing the challenges of vanishing gradients and poor propagation of activations that typically hinder the training of deep feedforward networks.

This is a high-quality and well-written paper. The approach is original, and the results are compelling. Machine learning research has consistently shown that increasing network depth often leads to improved performance, but practical limitations due to difficulties in initialization and vanishing/exploding gradients have been significant obstacles. The challenges of information flow through deep networks historically limited their widespread adoption for decades. This paper directly addresses this problem and demonstrates a method for successfully training networks with 100 layers or more. 

The experiments analyzing the routing of information and the importance of individual layers are particularly insightful. The paper demonstrates that highway networks not only train effectively but also learn to utilize their depth in a meaningful way, with different layers contributing to different aspects of the computation.

Specific comments and suggestions:

In Section 4.1, the phrase "most transform blocks are active on average" appears counterintuitive. Perhaps the intended meaning is that “most transform blocks are *not* active on average,” or a clarification could be provided.

While the paper effectively demonstrates the benefits of highway networks, it might be valuable to briefly discuss how the adaptive gating mechanism differentiates them from simpler approaches such as initializing each layer with the identity matrix (potentially with added noise). This could highlight the learned behavior of the transform gates and underscore the contribution of the proposed architecture beyond merely enabling signal propagation. 

Overall, this is an excellent paper demonstrating a robust method for training very deep neural networks, and it was a pleasure to read.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture is well-motivated, clearly described, and supported by experimental results.
*   **Originality:** The use of LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms in feedforward networks to address the depth problem is a novel approach.
*   **Significance:** The ability to train significantly deeper networks than previously possible opens up new avenues for research and potentially improved performance on challenging machine learning tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the topic.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   While the results are strong, the practical benefits on real-world applications beyond MNIST and CIFAR are not fully demonstrated. Further exploration on larger and more complex datasets would strengthen the paper. (This is a minor point, however).
