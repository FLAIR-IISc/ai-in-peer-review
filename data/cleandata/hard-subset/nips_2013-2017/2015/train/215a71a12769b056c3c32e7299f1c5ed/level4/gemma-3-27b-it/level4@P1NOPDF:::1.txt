I like this paper; the idea is well described and the experiments are convincing to a degree. The most valuable aspect, in my opinion, is the authors’ attempt to analyze the learned networks by examining the patterns of gate outputs.

(1) The gate unit T is modeled using a standard sigmoid activation function. Consequently, the response value is never exactly 0 or 1 (as the authors acknowledge), and the gradients in equation 5 appear to be incorrect. The authors should explain in the paper precisely how backpropagation is implemented in these networks.

(2) Analyzing the effect of initialization is always challenging. However, the authors’ proposal to use constant negative biases dependent on the number of levels feels somewhat ad hoc. I would like to see a plot showing performance as a function of the initial bias value. The initial bias acts as a form of regularization in this case, with a highly negative bias favoring networks with “longer highways”—a comparison I particularly appreciate.

(3) The phrase “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is unsatisfyingly vague. Either these methods employ the same experimental setting, allowing for direct comparison, or they do not, in which case a direct comparison is not valid. The authors need to clarify the differences in experimental setups.

(4) Reporting only “max” accuracies in tables is uncommon and statistically questionable. The authors should consistently report mean accuracy and standard deviation for each method (when available).

(5) It would be interesting to study the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The paper demonstrates how to incorporate additional gate units into neural networks, enabling layers to effectively act as identity mappings. Because the gate unit response depends on the input, this allows for the propagation of signals through a large number of highly selective layers.
