This paper addresses the well-known problem of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, termed "highway networks," inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The core idea is to introduce adaptive gating units that regulate the flow of information through the network. For each layer, a "transform gate" learns to combine the output of a standard non-linear transformation with the unmodified input from the previous layer. This mechanism creates "information highways" that allow gradients to propagate smoothly across many layers, mitigating the optimization difficulties that plague traditional deep "plain" networks. The paper's primary contribution is demonstrating that this architecture allows for the direct training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent. This work relates to previous efforts in deep network training, such as specialized initialization schemes, better optimizers, and layer-wise or hint-based training, but offers a more direct architectural solution that is dynamic and input-dependent, unlike fixed skip-connections.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly relevant problem in deep learning. The proposed solution, adapting LSTM-style gating for feedforward architectures, is an elegant and novel approach that enables the exploration of network depth on an unprecedented scale.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The optimization experiments in Section 3.1 provide convincing evidence that highway networks overcome the degradation problem seen in plain networks as depth increases. The performance on benchmark datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 is competitive, demonstrating that the architecture generalizes well and can be practically applied.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** A major strength of this work is the analysis of the trained networks' internal dynamics in Section 4. The investigation into the transform gate activities, information routing patterns for different classes, and the layer lesioning study provides valuable insights into how these networks function. This analysis shows that the gates learn meaningful, data-dependent computational paths and that deeper layers are utilized for more complex tasks.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

*   **Clarity on Gradient Computation:** In Equation 5, the paper presents a simplified Jacobian for the layer transform under the idealized conditions that the transform gate T is exactly 0 or 1. As the authors note, a sigmoid gate never reaches these values. This simplification could be misleading. The paper would be strengthened by explicitly stating the full gradient equation used for backpropagation to avoid any ambiguity about the training procedure.
*   **Initialization Heuristics:** The strategy for initializing the transform gate biases is presented as a heuristic (e.g., -1, -2, -3 for depths of 10, 20, 30). While this is a practical starting point, the paper's claims would be more rigorously supported by a sensitivity analysis. Including a plot of final performance as a function of the initial bias value would provide a clearer understanding of this hyperparameter's impact.
*   **Statistical Reporting of Results:** The practice of reporting results in the format "Best (mean ± std.dev.)" is unconventional and can be misleading, as it emphasizes the single best-performing run. For robust and fair comparison, the authors should report the mean and standard deviation calculated across all runs for each experimental configuration.
*   **Comparison to Prior Work:** The comparisons to methods like FitNets would be more compelling if the authors explicitly clarified the degree to which the experimental settings (e.g., parameter counts, computational budgets, data augmentation) are matched. Ensuring and detailing a controlled comparison is crucial for substantiating claims of superior performance or training efficiency.
