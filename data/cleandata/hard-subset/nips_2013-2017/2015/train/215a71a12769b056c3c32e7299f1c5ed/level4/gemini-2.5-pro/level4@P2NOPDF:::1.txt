This paper introduces a novel architecture using gated units that allow layers to dynamically act as near-identity transformations, enabling the training of very deep networks. The core idea is well-described, and the proposal to analyze the learned networks by examining the gate output patterns is a particularly strong contribution. The experiments are promising, though several aspects of the methodology and reporting could be clarified and strengthened to improve the manuscript.

1.  **Clarity on Gradient Calculation:** There appears to be a discrepancy in the description of the backpropagation algorithm. The transform gate unit, T, uses a sigmoid activation, meaning its output is continuous in the range (0, 1), as the authors note. However, the gradient formulation in Equation 5 seems to implicitly assume a binary (0 or 1) output. This raises questions about the correctness of the stated gradient. The authors should provide a more detailed derivation or explanation of how gradients are computed and backpropagated through these gates in practice.

2.  **Analysis of Bias Initialization:** While acknowledging the difficulty of analyzing initialization schemes, the proposed method of using a constant negative bias for the transform gates feels heuristic. This initial bias effectively acts as a regularizer, encouraging the network to initially favor information flow through the "highways"—an effective analogy. To make this choice more principled, it would be beneficial to see an analysis of the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter. A plot showing performance as a function of the initial bias value would significantly strengthen the paper by providing insight into its regularizing effect.

3.  **Rigor in Experimental Comparisons:** The paper's comparison to prior work is weakened by ambiguous phrasing such as results being from "similar experimental settings." For a direct and fair comparison, experimental conditions must be identical. If the settings are indeed the same, the authors should state this explicitly. If there are differences, these must be clearly detailed to allow the reader to properly contextualize the results and understand the limitations of the comparison.

4.  **Statistical Reporting of Results:** The use of "max" accuracy in the results tables is unconventional and not statistically robust. Performance can vary significantly across different random initializations and data shuffles. To provide a more reliable and complete picture of performance, the authors should report the mean and standard deviation of accuracy over multiple runs for all methods presented (for their own method and for prior work, when available).

5.  **Potential for Theoretical Analysis:** The proposed architecture is intriguing from a learning theory perspective. By allowing layers to conditionally act as identities based on the input, the network can propagate information through a great depth of highly selective layers. While beyond the scope of this initial work, it would be interesting for the authors to briefly comment on potential theoretical avenues this approach opens up.

In summary, this is a valuable contribution with a compelling core idea. Addressing the points above—particularly regarding the gradient calculation, initialization analysis, and experimental rigor—would significantly improve the quality and impact of the manuscript.
