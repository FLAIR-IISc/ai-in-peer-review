This paper presents a simple and elegant mechanism, inspired by LSTM gating, that enables the successful training of very deep feed-forward networks via “highway” connections. The motivation is clear, the analysis is solid, and the experiments provide convincing initial evidence that the approach mitigates optimization difficulties associated with depth.

I particularly appreciate the conceptual contribution: by introducing transform and carry gates that modulate the flow of information and gradients, the network can learn to allocate effective depth dynamically without relying on brittle initialization schemes or complex optimization tricks. The exposition is generally clear, and the empirical results on MNIST and CIFAR support the main claims.

An alternative perspective that may help readers connect this work to the broader architecture landscape is to view a very deep network as a not-so-deep network whose layers consist of sub-layers, yielding richer nonlinear transformations per “macro-layer.” This perspective aligns with prior designs such as Inception modules (GoogLeNet) and stacks of small convolutions (e.g., three 3×3 convolutions in VGG). Highway networks fit naturally into this framing: a layer combined with its gating can be seen as a macro-unit with controllable internal depth, dynamically interpolating between an identity mapping and a learned transformation. If the authors find this perspective useful, articulating it briefly could enhance intuition and situate the contribution relative to existing architectures.

Suggested experiment: Train a single thin-and-tall highway network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR with a shared trunk and two task-specific heads (forking near the top). At inference time, analyze whether the network exhibits task-dependent usage patterns similar to models trained separately. Specifically:
- Do gate activations and effective depths differ by task, with MNIST tending toward fewer active transformations and CIFAR engaging more layers?
- Are parameters that are underutilized on MNIST effectively repurposed for CIFAR?
- How efficient is hierarchical sharing compared to separate models, in terms of accuracy, parameter utilization, and depth-wise activity?

This study would illuminate how sharing emerges in very deep hierarchies and whether gating enables adaptive and efficient allocation of capacity across tasks.

I also encourage evaluation on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability and confirm that the training advantages persist with increased data complexity and model capacity.

Minor suggestions to strengthen the paper:
- Provide more intuition and evidence on gate behavior across depth: initialization strategies, evolution during training, and sensitivity to regularization.
- Include ablations isolating the roles of the transform and carry gates, and compare against plain deep rectifier networks with careful initialization to disentangle the benefits of gating from other factors.
- Report qualitative diagnostics (e.g., distributions of gate values by layer and input) to support claims about gradient flow, adaptive computation, and effective depth.

Overall assessment: A timely and impactful idea, clearly presented and supported by solid analysis and promising experiments. I have no major concerns. The additional experiments above would further strengthen the contribution and help establish its generality and practical value.
