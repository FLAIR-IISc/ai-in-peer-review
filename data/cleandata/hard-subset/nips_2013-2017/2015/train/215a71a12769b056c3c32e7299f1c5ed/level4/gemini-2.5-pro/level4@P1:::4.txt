This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to ease the training of networks with very large depth. Drawing inspiration from the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, the core idea is to introduce adaptive gates that regulate the flow of information between layers. These gates can dynamically route information, allowing it to either be transformed by the layer's non-linear function or be carried through unchanged. The authors show that this architecture enables the direct training of feedforward networks with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD), overcoming the optimization difficulties that plague conventional "plain" networks as they get deeper. The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated through a series of experiments on the MNIST and CIFAR datasets.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and its claims are well-supported by solid experimental evidence. The optimization experiments provide a clear and compelling comparison, showing that while the performance of plain networks degrades significantly with increased depth, highway networks maintain stable and effective training even at 100 layers. The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are also strong, demonstrating that the proposed architecture can achieve performance comparable to or better than competing methods (like FitNets) without requiring complex, multi-stage training procedures. The analysis in Section 4, which visualizes gate activations and studies the effect of "lesioning" individual layers, provides valuable insight into the network's inner workings. The analysis sections, while insightful, could perhaps be strengthened with a greater variety of details or illustrations to further probe the learned data-dependent routing patterns.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for the problem is well-established, and the highway network concept is explained intuitively and formulated precisely. The connection to LSTMs is used effectively to build intuition for the proposed gating mechanism. The figures are informative and effectively communicate the key results, from the improved optimization dynamics to the layer utilization analysis. The main message is delivered successfully and is easy for the reader to follow.

**Originality:** The proposed method is highly original. While the general idea of using skip connections to aid gradient flow has been explored before, the key innovation here is the introduction of a dynamic, data-dependent gating mechanism. This elevates the concept from a fixed architectural choice to a learned routing strategy, allowing the network to control the information flow between transformation and preservation at each layer. This clever translation of a core idea from recurrent networks (LSTMs) to the feedforward domain represents a novel and significant contribution.

**Significance:** This work is highly significant as it provides a practical and effective solution to a central problem in deep learning: how to successfully train extremely deep networks. By demonstrating that networks with hundreds of layers can be trained directly with SGD, this paper opens the door for researchers to explore the benefits of depth on a scale that was previously intractable. The ideas presented here are likely to have a broad impact, influencing the design of future deep architectures and enabling progress in various application areas that benefit from very deep models. The paper not only presents a method but also provides analysis tools that help to understand the role of depth, making a valuable contribution to the field.

In summary, the paper introduces an effective and well-motivated method for training very deep neural networks. The core idea is novel, and the contributions are well-supported by strong experimental evidence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the critical and challenging problem of training very deep networks.
*   The proposed highway network architecture is simple, elegant, and highly effective.
*   Contributions are supported by thorough experiments and insightful analysis.
*   The work is original and significant, with the potential to influence future research in deep network architectures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis of the learned routing behavior is interesting but could be explored in greater depth. However, this is a minor point and does not detract from the paper's main contributions.
