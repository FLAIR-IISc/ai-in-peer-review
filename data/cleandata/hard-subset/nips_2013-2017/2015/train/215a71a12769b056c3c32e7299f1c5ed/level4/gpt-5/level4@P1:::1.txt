This paper proposes highway networks, an LSTM-inspired architecture that introduces adaptive transform gates to enable unimpeded information flow across many layers. By setting C = 1 − T, each layer can interpolate between applying a learned transform and carrying its input forward, which helps avoid attenuation of activations and gradients. The authors show that very deep highway networks can be trained directly with SGD, even at depths where plain networks fail, and present experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. A particular strength is the analysis of gating behavior, information routing, and layer importance via lesioning.

Strengths
- The core idea is simple, well-motivated, and clearly described, drawing a convincing analogy to LSTM gating for easing credit assignment.
- Empirically, highway networks train substantially more easily than plain networks at large depths, with competitive generalization performance.
- The analysis of transform gate biases/activities, data-dependent routing, and layer lesion studies is insightful and helps demystify what very deep networks are doing.

Points for clarification and improvement
1) Backpropagation and Eq. 5: The transform gate T uses a sigmoid, so its outputs are never exactly 0 or 1 (as the authors also note). Consequently, the Jacobian forms shown in Eq. 5 are not strictly correct except as limiting cases. Please explain precisely how backpropagation is implemented for Eq. 3: provide the exact Jacobian of the highway layer y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), and clarify how gradients propagate through both H and T. If Eq. 5 is intended as intuition only, the paper should explicitly state and then present the correct expressions used in training.

2) Initialization by negative gate biases: While the reported negative transform-gate biases appear effective, the recommendation to set a constant negative bias based on depth is not entirely satisfying without further evidence. Since this initialization effectively regularizes the network toward “longer highways” (a useful analogy), please include a sensitivity analysis: e.g., a plot of performance (training stability and test accuracy) as a function of the initial gate bias, ideally across several depths. This would clarify the robustness of the method and help practitioners choose reasonable defaults.

3) Comparability of experimental settings: The phrase “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is not sufficiently clear. Either the exact experimental setting matches prior work (enabling direct comparison) or it differs (precluding direct comparison). Please explicitly detail any differences in preprocessing, data augmentation, architecture size, computational budget, training schedule, and evaluation protocol relative to the baselines you cite. Clear documentation of these differences is necessary to support fair comparisons.

4) Statistical reporting: Reporting “max” accuracies in tables is uncommon and statistically uninformative. Please report mean and standard deviation (over multiple runs) for each method whenever available, including your own models and any baselines you reimplement. This would also align with how some of your results are already summarized in the text.

5) Learning-theoretic perspective: It would be valuable to discuss the approach from a learning-theory viewpoint. The architecture introduces input-dependent gates that allow layers to act as near-identities, enabling inputs to propagate through many layers selectively. A theoretical perspective on the capacity/regularization effects of such input-dependent identity paths, their impact on effective depth, and generalization would strengthen the contribution and help position the work beyond empirical practice.

Overall assessment
I like the paper. The idea is clearly presented, the empirical results are convincing to a reasonable degree, and the analysis of gate outputs and routing is a genuine highlight. Addressing the points above—especially clarifying the exact backpropagation equations for the highway layer, providing a robustness study on gate-bias initialization, ensuring fair and transparent experimental comparisons, and reporting mean±std—would significantly improve the paper’s rigor and reproducibility. A discussion of the learning-theoretic implications of input-dependent gating would further enhance its significance.
