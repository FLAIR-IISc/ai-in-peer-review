This paper introduces a simple yet elegant idea, inspired by LSTMs, that enables the effective training of extremely deep feed-forward networks. The core concept is well-motivated, and the accompanying analysis is solid. The idea of using gating mechanisms to manage information and gradient flow in very deep architectures is a compelling one; I congratulate the authors for being the first to successfully formulate and implement this approach.

I believe there is an alternative perspective worth considering. One can view a very deep network not as a monolithic stack of simple layers, but as a shallower composition of more complex modules. From this viewpoint, each effective "layer" exhibits more complex non-linear behavior than a traditional rectifier or sigmoid layer. This interpretation aligns well with architectures like GoogLeNet's Inception modules or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG. I would be interested to hear the authors' thoughts on whether this is also a valid way of interpreting their Highway Network architecture.

To further explore the properties of this model, I would suggest one additional experiment. A single, deep, and narrow ("thin and tall") network could be trained to perform multi-task learning on both MNIST and CIFAR-10, forking at the final layers to produce task-specific outputs. The primary interest would be to analyze the behavior of the transform gates at inference time. Would the network exhibit similar gating patterns to those observed in networks trained on each domain individually? More specifically, would parameters that are largely bypassed (i.e., underutilized) for the simpler MNIST task become actively engaged for the more complex CIFAR task? Such an experiment could provide valuable insights into how parameter sharing occurs in a very deep hierarchy and would be a compelling test of the model's capacity for efficient knowledge transfer.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with no major weaknesses. To further demonstrate the scalability and practical impact of this method, I strongly encourage the authors to evaluate their network on larger-scale benchmarks, such as the ImageNet dataset.
