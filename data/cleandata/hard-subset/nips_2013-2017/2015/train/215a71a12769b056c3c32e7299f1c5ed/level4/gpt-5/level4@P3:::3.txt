Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses LSTM-inspired gating to enable unimpeded information flow through very deep networks. Each highway layer computes y = H(x) ⊙ T(x) + x ⊙ (1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transformation (e.g., affine + nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a learnable transform gate with a sigmoid output; the carry gate is set to 1 − T. By initializing T with a negative bias, the network is initially biased toward carrying inputs forward, creating “information highways” that preserve activations and gradients across many layers. The authors show that with this architecture, deep networks with tens to hundreds of layers can be trained directly with SGD.

The work is well positioned within the literature on training deep networks. It builds on insights from LSTM gating for long-range credit assignment, and relates to prior attempts to improve training of deep nets via better initialization (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.), advanced optimizers (Martens; Dauphin et al.), skip connections and deep supervision, and model compression/distillation (FitNets). In contrast to fixed skip connections, the gates provide input-dependent, learnable routing. The paper also references a shorter earlier report on highway networks and notes contemporaneous LSTM-inspired feedforward designs.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation is simple and well motivated. The choice C = 1 − T keeps the parameterization compact and stable. The initialization scheme for T is clear and grounded in LSTM practice.
- Empirical support: The optimization experiments are carefully controlled on MNIST, matching parameter counts and showing that plain networks degrade with depth while highway networks train effectively up to 100 layers. Convolutional experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 demonstrate that highway networks train in one stage and achieve accuracy comparable to or better than FitNets under similar parameter/computation budgets. The analysis sections (gate activity, data-dependent routing, and lesion studies) are particularly insightful and substantiate the intended behavior of the gates.
- Limitations: Experimental scope is primarily small/medium-scale vision benchmarks with modest augmentation; there is no ImageNet-scale evaluation. Ablations isolating the effect of gating versus added parameters (from T) are limited for convolutional settings. Additional diagnostics (e.g., gradient norms through depth) would further strengthen the case that improvements come from optimization ease rather than capacity alone.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized, with equations and architectural descriptions that are easy to follow. The qualitative visualizations (gate biases/activities and block outputs) effectively convey the “information highway” behavior. Minor issues:
- Typos: “varying varying depths,” “backpropogation,” and a few formatting inconsistencies (e.g., footnote markers).
- Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted.
- Section 4.1’s phrasing on gate activity could be clarified (see comments below).

Originality:
While inspired by LSTM gating and related skip-connection ideas, the application of adaptive, input-dependent transform/carry gates to very deep feedforward networks is novel and well motivated. The proposed training bias for the transform gate and the systematic analysis of routing and layer importance add originality beyond earlier skip-connection or deep supervision approaches.

Significance:
Enabling stable training of 50–100+ layer networks with vanilla SGD is an important contribution. The method is simple to implement in common architectures (fully connected and convolutional) and shows competitive generalization without heavy data augmentation. The analysis offers a compelling lens on depth utilization and data-dependent routing, which is valuable for both practitioners and researchers exploring very deep models.

Specific comments and questions:
- Section 4.1 wording: The paper states that “most transform gates are active on average, while they show very selective activity for a single example.” This is consistent with sparse per-example routing and dense average activity. To avoid confusion, consider adding a brief explanation or a histogram showing per-unit average activity versus per-sample sparsity, and clarifying that “active on average” reflects averaging over inputs with different active subsets.
- Identity initialization vs. highway gates: Please comment on how highway networks differ from initializing each plain layer to the identity (with small noise). Identity initialization can yield an initial Jacobian near identity, but the bypass path is not explicitly parameterized and can degrade as weights drift. In highway networks, the carry path is maintained throughout training, and gates provide input-dependent routing and multiplicative control, which may preserve gradient flow more robustly than fixed identity-initialized layers. An ablation comparing identity-initialized deep plain nets to highway nets with similar parameter counts would be informative.
- Parameter/cost accounting: For convolutional models, T introduces additional parameters and compute. It would help to report parameters and FLOPs for H and T separately, and to provide an ablation where T shares receptive fields/weights or reduced width, to disentangle optimization benefits from capacity increases.
- Sensitivity to gate bias: The paper provides heuristics for initial bT. A sensitivity plot (accuracy/optimization speed vs. bT) across depths would make the guidance more actionable. Also, results for C ≠ 1 − T (i.e., independent carry gates) would be useful to understand trade-offs.
- Gradient diagnostics: Reporting gradient norms/variance across layers during training for plain vs. highway nets would directly support the claim of improved credit assignment.
- Scope: A brief discussion of how the approach might extend to sequence models (e.g., highway layers in RNNs/CNNs for sequences) would broaden impact.

Pros:
- Simple, well-motivated architecture that demonstrably enables training of very deep networks with SGD.
- Strong optimization results with careful controls; compelling analyses of gate behavior, routing, and layer importance.
- Competitive performance on CIFAR-10/100 without heavy augmentation; code availability supports reproducibility.
- Clear writing and useful visual diagnostics.

Cons:
- Evaluations limited to small/medium-scale datasets; no large-scale benchmark.
- Limited ablations on the cost/benefit of gates and on alternative carry/transform parameterizations.
- Minor clarity and reference formatting issues; Section 4.1 wording can confuse readers.

Overall assessment:
This is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution. The paper addresses a central challenge in deep learning and provides a practical, well-analyzed solution. I recommend acceptance after minor clarifications and with consideration of the suggested ablations in a revision or supplementary material. It was a pleasure to read.
