Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses input-dependent gating to regulate the flow of information across layers. Each layer blends a transformed signal with a “carried” (approximately identity) pathway via a transform gate T and an implicit carry gate 1−T, enabling signals and gradients to traverse hundreds of layers without attenuation. The design is inspired by LSTM gating but adapted to deep feedforward and convolutional settings. The authors show that, with simple SGD and a bias initialization that favors carry behavior, very deep networks (including >100 layers) can be trained directly. They compare against plain networks with variance-preserving initialization and document substantially improved optimization behavior and competitive generalization on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The paper also provides insightful analyses of gating behavior, input-dependent routing, and layer importance via lesioning.

The work connects to several strands of prior research: LSTM gating (conceptual inspiration), skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision (aiming to improve information flow), and FitNets (teacher–student training for deeper models). Unlike teacher–student approaches, highway networks train end-to-end in a single stage; unlike fixed skip connections, the routing is data-dependent through learned gates.

Quality:
The methodology is technically sound. The formulation is clear, the initialization strategy is simple and well-motivated, and the experiments are carefully controlled. Optimization studies show that depth severely hurts plain networks but not highway networks, which converge faster and to lower training loss. Classification results on MNIST and CIFAR are competitive given modest model sizes and minimal augmentation. The analysis section is a notable strength: visualizing gate biases/activities, demonstrating input-dependent routing, and lesioning layers to assess contribution all support the central claims. A few additional ablations (e.g., independent carry gates versus C=1−T, sensitivity to gate bias, overhead from gating) would further strengthen the empirical picture, but the current evidence is persuasive.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The architectural description is precise, and the figures effectively illustrate the gating dynamics and the notion of “information highways.” Minor editorial issue: “Tables 3 and 3” on p. 5 appears to be a typo.

Originality:
The idea of importing LSTM-style gating into deep feedforward architectures to create data-dependent, layer-wise routing is novel and distinct from previous skip connections and distillation approaches. The work articulates the differences to related methods and provides direct comparisons where appropriate (e.g., FitNets-style settings).

Significance:
The contribution is important. Enabling stable training of very deep networks with standard SGD and without teacher models lowers the barrier to exploring deeper architectures. The analysis suggests that highway networks can adaptively utilize depth, keeping layers idle on simple tasks while leveraging many layers on harder datasets. Given the widespread interest in deeper models, the proposed mechanism is likely to influence practice and inspire follow-up work.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled architecture with simple, effective initialization.
- Strong optimization results demonstrating robustness to depth.
- Competitive accuracy on MNIST/CIFAR with modest budgets.
- Insightful analyses of gating, data-dependent routing, and layer importance.
- Solid positioning with respect to LSTMs, skip connections, and FitNets.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Limited ablations: consider evaluating independent carry gates (C learned separately from 1−T) and quantifying the parameter/computation overhead due to gates.
- Broader comparisons: while the focus is on ease of optimization, additional baselines (e.g., alternative skip-connection schemes) would be informative.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: provide a brief study of performance versus transform-gate bias initialization and depth.
- Minor typographical issue: “Tables 3 and 3” on p. 5.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a central challenge (training very deep nets) with a simple, general mechanism.
- Empirical evidence is convincing, with careful analysis beyond headline accuracies.
- Well-written and likely to have practical impact.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some missing ablations and broader comparisons limit a full understanding of trade-offs.
- Results are competitive but not state-of-the-art under heavy augmentation (though this is not the stated goal).

Overall assessment:
This is a strong paper: original, clearly presented, technically sound, and supported by thoughtful empirical analysis. The proposed gating mechanism for feedforward depth is compelling and practically useful. I recommend acceptance.
