This paper presents a compelling idea, clearly described and supported by reasonably convincing experiments. A particularly strong aspect of the work is the authors’ analysis of the learned networks, focusing on the patterns of gate outputs.

(1) The gate unit *T* utilizes a standard sigmoid activation, resulting in output values that are never precisely 0 or 1, as acknowledged by the authors. Consequently, the gradients presented in equation 5 are inaccurate. The paper should detail the exact backpropagation procedure employed within these networks.

(2) While the impact of initialization is often difficult to assess, the proposed use of constant negative biases, scaled by the number of levels, feels insufficiently justified. I recommend including a plot illustrating performance as a function of the initial bias value. This initial bias effectively functions as a regularizer; a strongly negative bias appears to favor networks with “longer highways,” a comparison I found insightful. 

(3) The phrase "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is vague. For a meaningful comparison, either the referenced methods should employ identical experimental setups, or the authors should clearly delineate the differences between their setup and those of other studies.

(4) Reporting only “max” accuracies in the tables is atypical and statistically questionable. The authors should consistently report the mean accuracy and standard deviation for each method presented (when available).

(5) A learning theory perspective on this approach would be valuable. The paper introduces additional gate units that enable layers to function as identity mappings. Since the gate unit’s response is input-dependent, this allows for the propagation of signals through a large number of highly selective layers – a potentially powerful mechanism worthy of further theoretical investigation.
