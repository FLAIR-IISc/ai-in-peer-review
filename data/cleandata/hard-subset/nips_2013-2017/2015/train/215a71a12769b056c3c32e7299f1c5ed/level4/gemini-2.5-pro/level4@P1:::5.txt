This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel feedforward architecture designed to enable the training of extremely deep networks. The core idea, inspired by the gating mechanism in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to introduce adaptive gates that regulate information flow. These gates allow unimpeded data flow along "information highways," carrying parts of the input directly across many layers, which effectively mitigates the optimization challenges that typically hinder the training of very deep "plain" networks. The authors provide strong empirical evidence that Highway Networks with tens or even hundreds of layers can be trained directly with standard SGD, and they offer valuable analysis into how these networks learn to utilize their depth.

The work's primary strength is its simple, elegant, and highly effective solution to the significant and long-standing problem of training very deep neural networks. The adaptation of LSTM's gating concept to a feedforward architecture is both novel and intuitive, and the authors are to be commended for this insight. The potential impact is substantial, as this method allows the research community to explore network depths that were previously intractable.

The paper is supported by solid analysis and experiments. The direct comparison with "plain" networks clearly demonstrates the proposed architecture's advantage in optimization as depth increases. The analysis in Section 4 is particularly insightful, especially the layer lesioning study (Figure 4). This experiment compellingly shows that the network learns to use more of its depth for more complex problems (CIFAR-100 vs. MNIST), directly addressing the potential concern that the network might simply learn to keep most of its gates closed and behave like a shallow network. The visualizations of data-dependent information routing further strengthen the contribution.

The paper is well-written and has no major flaws. My comments below are intended as constructive suggestions for future discussion and exploration.

One potential point for discussion is an alternative perspective on very deep architectures. Instead of being viewed as a long chain of simple layers, they can also be conceptualized as a shorter stack of more complex computational blocks. This perspective is implicit in architectures like GoogLeNet's Inception modules or VGG's use of stacked 3x3 convolutions. It would be interesting for the authors to discuss how the Highway Network architecture fits within, or perhaps challenges, this viewpoint.

Furthermore, a compelling experiment to further investigate the network's dynamic routing capabilities would be to explore a multi-task learning scenario. For instance, one could train a single, "thin-and-deep" network on both MNIST and CIFAR-100, with a shared trunk that branches into task-specific heads. The key question would be whether the deeper layers, which the current analysis shows are underutilized when training on MNIST alone, become actively engaged for the more complex CIFAR-100 task within the same shared model. Such an experiment could provide powerful insights into efficient feature sharing in very deep hierarchies.

Finally, while the experiments on MNIST and CIFAR are convincing, the true potential of this architecture would be best demonstrated on a larger-scale benchmark like ImageNet. Successfully training a Highway Network with over 100 layers on ImageNet would be a very significant result and would fully solidify the claims made in the paper.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a simple and powerful idea with strong empirical support and insightful analysis. I recommend acceptance.
