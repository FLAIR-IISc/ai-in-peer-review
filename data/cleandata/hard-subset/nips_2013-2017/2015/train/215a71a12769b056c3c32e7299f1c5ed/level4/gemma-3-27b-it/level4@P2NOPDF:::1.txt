This paper presents an interesting and well-described idea, and the experimental results are generally convincing. A particularly strong aspect of the work is the authors’ attempt to analyze the learned networks by examining the patterns of gate outputs. However, several points require clarification and improvement.

(1) The gate unit *T* employs a standard sigmoid activation function, resulting in outputs that are never precisely 0 or 1, as acknowledged by the authors. Consequently, the gradients presented in equation 5 appear inaccurate. The paper needs to provide a detailed explanation of how backpropagation is implemented within these networks, given this characteristic of the sigmoid output. A clear description of the gradient calculation is essential.

(2) While the influence of initialization is notoriously difficult to assess, the proposed strategy of using constant negative biases scaled by the number of levels feels somewhat ad hoc. To better understand this choice, I recommend including a plot illustrating performance as a function of the initial bias value. The initial bias effectively acts as a regularizer; the observation that a highly negative bias favors networks with “longer highways” is insightful and should be further explored with this analysis. Visualizing the performance landscape with varying initial biases would significantly strengthen the justification for the chosen approach.

(3) The phrase “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is imprecise. Either the cited studies utilize identical experimental setups, permitting direct comparison, or they do not, precluding such comparison. The authors must clearly delineate the similarities and differences between their experimental setup and those employed in related work. Specific details are needed to assess the relevance of these comparisons.

(4) Reporting only “max” accuracies in the tables is atypical and statistically weak. To provide a more robust evaluation, the authors should consistently report the mean accuracy and standard deviation for all methods presented (where standard deviation is available). This will allow for a proper assessment of statistical significance and the reliability of the results.

(5) Finally, it would be valuable to consider the proposed approach through the lens of learning theory. The paper introduces a mechanism for incorporating gate units that enable layers to function as identity mappings. The input-dependent response of these gate units allows for the propagation of signals through a potentially large number of highly selective layers. Further investigation into the theoretical properties of this architecture – for example, its capacity to represent complex functions or its potential for avoiding vanishing gradients – could significantly enrich the work. Examining the learning dynamics and generalization capabilities from a theoretical perspective would be a worthwhile extension.
