

This paper presents a compelling and elegant approach inspired by LSTM techniques to enable the training of extremely deep feedforward networks. The core idea—trapping gradients to facilitate network growth—is both simple and powerful, and the accompanying analysis is rigorous and well-supported. The authors deserve significant credit for successfully implementing this concept where others (including myself) had only contemplated it.  

I find the authors' perspective particularly insightful when considering the broader landscape of deep network design. The observation that a "very deep network" can be reinterpreted as a "not-so-deep" architecture where each layer incorporates complex sub-structures (e.g., multiple stacked convolutions or inception modules) aligns well with established practices in networks like GoogLeNet and VGG. This reframing suggests that highway networks might similarly benefit from viewing their residual pathways as layered, hierarchical components rather than mere skip connections. This alternative lens could offer valuable theoretical cohesion for understanding how depth and complexity interact in modern architectures.  

A promising direction for future work would be to investigate parameter sharing in a unified deep architecture across multiple domains. Specifically, I recommend training a single "thin and tall" network (with a shared initial layer) on both MNIST and CIFAR-10, then evaluating whether parameters underutilized in the MNIST task are effectively repurposed for CIFAR-10 during inference. This would directly probe how efficiently a deep hierarchy leverages shared representations across tasks—whether the model dynamically allocates resources to domains where they are most needed, rather than maintaining redundant parameters. The results could provide crucial insights into the scalability and efficiency of parameter sharing in very deep networks, which is particularly relevant for multi-task learning or transfer learning scenarios.  

The paper's experimental validation is strong, demonstrating clear improvements over baselines on standard benchmarks. However, I would encourage the authors to extend these experiments to larger-scale datasets like ImageNet in future work. While MNIST and CIFAR-10 are excellent for initial validation, ImageNet would rigorously test the method's scalability and practical utility in real-world computer vision applications. The authors' approach has significant potential to advance the training of deeper networks, and ImageNet results would substantially strengthen the paper's impact.  

Overall, this work makes a valuable contribution to the field. The idea is novel yet grounded in solid principles, the implementation is clean, and the results are convincing. The paper effectively bridges concepts from recurrent networks (LSTMs) to feedforward architectures, opening new avenues for deep network design. The suggested extension to multi-domain learning is not merely a technical curiosity but addresses a fundamental challenge in modern deep learning: efficient resource utilization across heterogeneous tasks.  

No major technical or methodological concerns detract from the paper's quality. The writing is clear, the figures are well-constructed, and the claims are appropriately supported by experiments. The authors have successfully demonstrated a practical path to training networks with unprecedented depth, and their analysis provides a compelling theoretical foundation.  

In summary, this is a strong contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in deep network training. The core idea is both elegant and impactful, and the proposed multi-domain experiment would further elevate the work's significance. I enthusiastically recommend acceptance.
