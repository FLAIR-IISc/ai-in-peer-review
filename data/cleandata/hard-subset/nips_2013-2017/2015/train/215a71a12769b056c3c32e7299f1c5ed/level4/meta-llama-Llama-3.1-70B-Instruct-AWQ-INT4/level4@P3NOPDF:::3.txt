This paper proposes the use of LSTM units, specifically tailored to the concept of highway networks, as a method for training very deep networks. The authors present a high-quality, well-written paper that showcases an original idea and yields quite interesting results.

Machine learning researchers often find that adding more layers to their neural networks leads to better results. However, the difficulties of initialization and the issues of decaying or exploding gradients have long been significant obstacles. In fact, the challenges of getting information to flow through deep neural networks have arguably hindered their widespread adoption for over 30 years. This paper tackles this problem head-on and demonstrates one effective method for training neural networks with up to 100 layers.

One of the most compelling aspects of this paper is the experiments that illustrate the routing of information and the importance of individual layers. These experiments provide valuable insights into how the proposed method facilitates the training of deep neural networks.

Specific comments on the paper are as follows:

In Section 4.1, the statement 'most transform blocks are active on average' appears to be inconsistent with the surrounding context. It is suggested that this be revised to 'most transform blocks are not active on average' to maintain consistency.

An alternative approach to initializing each layer of a very deep network could involve using the identity matrix, possibly with a small amount of added noise. It would be beneficial to include a comment on how the transform gates in highway networks differentiate them from this simple initialization scheme.

Overall, this is a great paper that demonstrates a method for training very deep neural networks. It was a pleasure to read, and the authors are commended for their contribution to the field.
