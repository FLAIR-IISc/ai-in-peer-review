This paper describes an idea inspired by LSTM that allows the training of very deep feedforward networks. The idea is simple and elegant, and the analysis is solid.

Theoretical and empirical evidence suggests that network depth is crucial for success in deep learning, but training becomes increasingly difficult with greater depth. This paper addresses this challenge by introducing “highway networks,” a novel architecture designed to facilitate information flow across many layers. Inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks, highway networks utilize adaptive gating units to regulate information flow, enabling the training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard gradient descent.

The authors clearly articulate the problem of vanishing gradients in deep networks and position their work within the existing landscape of solutions, including improved optimizers [13, 14, 15], initialization strategies [16, 17, 18, 19], novel activation functions [20, 21], and skip connections [22, 23, 2, 24]. They also appropriately contextualize their approach in relation to recent work on knowledge distillation [25, 26] and layer-wise training [26, 27]. The connection to LSTM [29, 30] is well-motivated, and the authors accurately highlight the limitations of previous techniques.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The core idea of highway networks is intuitively appealing and well-explained. The mathematical formulation (Equation 3) is concise and clear. The experimental results, particularly Figure 1, convincingly demonstrate the ability to train significantly deeper networks than previously possible with standard techniques. The comparisons to Fitnets [25] (Table 2) and state-of-the-art methods (Table 3) are relevant and supportive of the authors’ claims. Furthermore, the analysis of the learned gate biases and layer importance (Figures 2 and 4) provides valuable insights into the behavior of these networks. The public availability of code and hyperparameters is also commendable.

However, there are a few points that could be further addressed. While the authors acknowledge that the transform gate bias initialization is best treated as a hyperparameter, a more systematic exploration of this parameter and its impact on performance would be beneficial. Additionally, the discussion of how highway networks relate to other methods of increasing effective depth, such as the stacking of smaller convolutional layers (e.g., Inception modules in GoogLeNet, multiple 3x3 convolutions in VGG), could be expanded. I wonder if a highway network can be viewed as a more general formulation of these techniques, where the gating mechanism learns to effectively combine the outputs of sub-layers.

I would encourage the authors to consider an experiment involving a single, very deep network trained on both MNIST and CIFAR-10. Specifically, training a single "thin and tall" network and forking the network at the top for the two domains. The research question would be whether the network leverages the increased capacity to share representations between the two datasets; whether parameters underutilized for MNIST are effectively utilized for CIFAR. This could provide insights into the efficiency of parameter sharing in a very deep hierarchical structure. 

Finally, while the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of highway networks on relatively small datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100), extending the experiments to larger, more challenging datasets such as ImageNet would further strengthen the impact of this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and elegant solution to the problem of training very deep networks.
*   Strong experimental validation demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks.
*   Clear and concise writing with a well-defined research question.
*   Insightful analysis of the learned network parameters.
*   Publicly available code and hyperparameters.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimentation on larger datasets.
*   Further exploration of the transform gate bias initialization could be beneficial.
*   Deeper discussion on the relationship to other depth-increasing techniques could enhance the paper.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The highway network architecture offers a promising approach to overcoming the challenges of training very deep networks, and the authors have provided compelling evidence to support their claims. I recommend acceptance.
