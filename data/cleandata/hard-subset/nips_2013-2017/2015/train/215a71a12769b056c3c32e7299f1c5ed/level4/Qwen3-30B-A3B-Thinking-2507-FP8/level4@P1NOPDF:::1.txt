

The paper presents a compelling approach to neural network architecture design, with a well-articulated core idea and generally convincing experimental results. The most valuable contribution lies in the authors' analysis of learned networks through the lens of gate output patterns.

(1) The gate unit T employs a standard sigmoid activation, meaning its output never reaches exactly 0 or 1 (a point the authors acknowledge). Consequently, the gradients described in Equation (5) are inaccurate. The authors must clarify the exact backpropagation procedure used for networks incorporating these gate units.

(2) While initialization effects are inherently complex, the proposed use of constant negative biases dependent on the number of levels is unsatisfactory. A plot showing performance as a function of the initial bias value would be highly informative. This bias parameter effectively functions as a regularizer; a strongly negative bias encourages networks with "longer highways" (a useful analogy, by the way).

(3) The phrase "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is vague and unhelpful. Either the experimental setups are comparable, enabling direct comparison, or they are not, making comparison invalid. The authors must explicitly clarify the differences between their experimental setup and those of prior work.

(4) Reporting only "max" accuracies in tables is statistically inappropriate and uncommon practice. For all methods, the authors should consistently report mean accuracy alongside standard deviation, whenever available, to provide a proper statistical assessment.

(5) A learning theory perspective on the proposed approach would be insightful. The paper describes a method using additional gate units to enable identity mapping through layers. Crucially, the gate response depends on the input, allowing inputs to propagate through many highly selective layers. Exploring this from a theoretical learning perspective would strengthen the paper significantly.
