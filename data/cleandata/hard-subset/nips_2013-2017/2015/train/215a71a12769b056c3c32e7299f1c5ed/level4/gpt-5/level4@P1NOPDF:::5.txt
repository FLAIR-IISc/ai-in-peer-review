I had also considered using the LSTM idea of preserving (“trapping”) gradients to grow substantially deeper feedforward networks, but had not started the work yet. Congratulations to the authors for getting there first.

A potentially useful perspective is to view a very deep network as a not-so-deep network whose layers contain sublayers, so that each “layer” implements a richer nonlinearity than a standard rectifier/sigmoid layer. This is how I interpret modules like Inception in GoogLeNet and the stacks of 3×3 convolutions in VGG. I wonder whether highway networks can be fruitfully understood the same way.

I have one experimental suggestion. Train a single thin-and-tall (narrow and deep) network jointly on MNIST and CIFAR, branching only at the top into task-specific heads. At inference time, does this shared network exhibit activation/usage patterns similar to those found in networks trained separately for each domain? Do parameters that are underutilized on MNIST get recruited by CIFAR? In short, I would like to see how sharing emerges in a very deep hierarchy and how efficient that sharing is.

I have no major complaints. I encourage the authors to evaluate the network on larger datasets such as ImageNet.

Overall, the paper presents an LSTM-inspired mechanism that enables training very deep feedforward networks. The idea is simple and elegant, and the analysis is solid.
