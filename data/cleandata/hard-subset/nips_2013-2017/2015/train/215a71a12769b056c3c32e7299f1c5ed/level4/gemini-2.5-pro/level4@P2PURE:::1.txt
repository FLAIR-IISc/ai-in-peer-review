The paper presents a compelling idea that is well-described, and the experiments are largely convincing. A key strength of this work is the analysis of the learned networks through the activation patterns of the proposed gate units. However, there are several points that require clarification and improvement:

(1) The gate unit `T` is modeled with a sigmoid activation, which, as the authors note, produces outputs that are not exactly 0 or 1. This raises a question about the correctness of the gradient formulation in Equation 5. The paper should provide a more detailed explanation of how backpropagation is implemented in this context.

(2) While the effect of initialization can be difficult to analyze, the proposal to use a constant negative bias that depends on network depth is not fully justified. A more thorough analysis, such as a plot of performance versus the initial bias value, would be valuable. The initial bias appears to act as a regularizer, where a large negative value encourages the formation of "long highways" (a very effective analogy).

(3) The comparison to "recent studies in similar experimental settings" is ambiguous. For a fair comparison, the experimental setups must be identical. If there are any differences, the authors must explicitly detail them to justify the comparison.

(4) Reporting "max" accuracies in the tables is unconventional and not statistically robust. For all methods (where available), the authors should report the mean accuracy and standard deviation across multiple runs to enable a fair and meaningful evaluation.

(5) From a theoretical standpoint, the proposed approach is intriguing. By introducing input-dependent gate units that can effectively create identity mappings, the architecture allows information to propagate through many highly selective layers. A discussion of the method from a learning theory perspective could further strengthen the paper.
