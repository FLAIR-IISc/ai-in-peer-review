

This paper introduces Highway Networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) using standard SGD. The core innovation adapts LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanisms to control information flow across layers, creating "information highways" that allow unimpeded signal propagation. Unlike prior work on skip connections [22, 23, 25] or layer-wise pretraining [26, 27], Highway Networks dynamically route information through multiplicative gating (transform/carry gates), eliminating the need for two-stage training or fixed shortcut paths. The authors demonstrate that highway networks maintain training stability and generalization as depth increases, outperforming plain networks by over two orders of magnitude at 100 layers on MNIST, and matching/exceeding state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 with comparable parameter budgets.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The paper delivers technically rigorous validation. Experiments include thorough ablation studies (e.g., layer lesioning in Fig. 4 showing depth utilization varies by dataset complexity), statistical significance (5-run mean±std results), and comparisons against relevant baselines (e.g., Fitnets [25]). The theoretical justification via Jacobian analysis (Eq. 5) and empirical demonstration of information routing (Figs. 2–3) strongly support claims. The initialization scheme (negative bias for transform gates) is well-motivated and effective across diverse architectures.  
*Originality*: While inspired by LSTMs [29, 30] and skip connections [22, 23], Highway Networks introduce a *learned, adaptive* gating mechanism distinct from fixed skip paths or teacher-student distillation [25]. The paper clearly differentiates itself by enabling end-to-end training of ultra-deep networks without auxiliary techniques—a key advance over prior work [25, 26, 32].  
*Significance*: This work directly addresses a fundamental barrier in deep learning: the inability to train very deep networks due to vanishing gradients. By enabling direct training of 100+ layer networks, Highway Networks open new avenues for studying depth effects (e.g., layer importance in Fig. 4), which was previously infeasible. The results on CIFAR-10/100 demonstrate practical relevance without requiring specialized data augmentation or hyperparameter tuning beyond standard practices.  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with clear notation (Section 2), intuitive figures (Figs. 2–4), and concise explanations of the highway mechanism. The experimental setup (Section 3) and analysis (Section 4) are transparent and reproducible, with code and hyperparameters publicly shared.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Minor technical clarification**: In Section 4.1, the phrase "most transform blocks are active on average" (p. 9) is misleading. The paper correctly states that gates exhibit *high average activity* but *sparse per-sample activation* (Fig. 2, column 3). This should be reworded to avoid confusion (e.g., "gates show high average activity but sparse per-sample activation").  
2. **Comparative context**: The paper notes highway networks avoid two-stage training [25], but could better contrast with ResNets (which emerged shortly after this NIPS 2015 submission). While ResNets use fixed skip connections, Highway Networks’ adaptive gating provides a more flexible routing mechanism (as shown in Fig. 3). This distinction merits explicit discussion.  
3. **Depth threshold**: The claim that "very deep networks" (e.g., 100 layers) are feasible is supported, but the paper does not define "very deep" quantitatively (e.g., beyond what prior work achieved). Adding a sentence comparing the depth limits of plain networks [16, 17] versus highway networks would strengthen the significance argument.

**Recommendation**  
This is a high-quality, impactful contribution to deep learning. The paper’s core idea—adaptive information highways enabling direct training of ultra-deep networks—is original, rigorously validated, and addresses a critical bottleneck in the field. The experimental results are compelling, the writing is clear, and the work provides a foundation for future studies on depth and architecture design. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable in revision and do not undermine the paper’s strengths.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pro*:  
- Solves a fundamental problem (training very deep networks) with a simple, effective architecture.  
- Enables new analyses (e.g., layer importance, data-dependent routing) impossible with plain networks.  
- Strong empirical validation across datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100) with rigorous ablation.  
- Clear, reproducible implementation (code shared).  

*Con*:  
- Minor textual ambiguity in Section 4.1 (easily fixed).  
- Limited comparison to ResNets (a natural contemporary parallel, though ResNet was not yet published at NIPS 2015).  

**Final Assessment**  
This paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to deep learning. It advances the state of the art in network depth, provides a theoretically grounded and empirically validated solution, and opens new research directions. The work meets all NIPS quality criteria and is highly relevant to the conference’s scope. I recommend **strong acceptance**.
