The paper proposes a novel architecture, termed highway networks, designed to facilitate the training of very deep neural networks. The core idea centers around enabling unimpeded information flow across many layers via “information highways” inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent, even with hundreds of layers, addressing a key challenge in deep learning.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach to a significant problem in deep learning – the difficulty of training very deep networks. Claims are well-supported by a comprehensive experimental evaluation across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets. The comparisons to both plain networks and existing techniques like FitNets are compelling. The analysis of transform gate behavior and layer importance (Figures 2 & 4) provides valuable insight into the inner workings of highway networks. While the analysis is strong, exploring the sensitivity of performance to different gating function choices or regularization strategies would further strengthen the work. The paper feels complete, though a discussion of computational cost compared to alternative deep learning architectures would be valuable. 

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors effectively motivate the problem, introduce the highway network architecture with a clear and concise mathematical formulation, and present experimental results in a logical manner. The use of notation is consistent and the explanations are accessible to readers familiar with deep learning concepts. The inclusion of visual aids, such as Figure 2, enhances understanding.

**Originality:** While the concept of skip connections and gating mechanisms are not entirely new, the highway network architecture represents a novel and effective combination of these techniques. The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism applied to feedforward networks is a distinct contribution. The authors appropriately reference related work, including FitNets and other deep learning optimization strategies. The paper clearly articulates how highway networks differ from these previous contributions.

**Significance:** The ability to train significantly deeper networks without sacrificing ease of optimization is a substantial advance. This opens up the possibility of exploring new architectures and representations that were previously inaccessible due to training difficulties. The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate the practical utility of highway networks and their potential to improve performance on challenging image recognition tasks. The analysis of information routing within the network (Figure 3) also provides a valuable theoretical contribution. This work is likely to be influential and inspire further research in deep learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical challenge in deep learning – training very deep networks.
*   Presents a novel and effective architecture (highway networks) with strong empirical support.
*   Demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to existing techniques.
*   Provides insightful analysis of the learned representations and information flow within the network.
*   Well-written, clear, and thoroughly researched.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The analysis section could be expanded to include a wider range of experiments (e.g., sensitivity to different hyperparameters, comparison of computational cost).
*   While the paper demonstrates the benefits of highway networks, further investigation into the theoretical properties of the gating mechanism would be valuable.
