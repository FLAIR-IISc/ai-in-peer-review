Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture with gated skip pathways inspired by LSTM. Each layer produces a transformed signal and a transform gate; with a complementary carry gate (C=1−T), the layer adaptively blends the transformed input with the identity mapping. By initializing the transform gates with negative biases, the network is biased toward carrying information early in training, enabling gradients to flow across hundreds of layers and allowing direct optimization with standard SGD. Experiments demonstrate that very deep highway networks (fully connected and convolutional) train reliably, converge faster than plain networks with normalized initialization, and generalize competitively on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The paper also analyzes gate activity and layer importance via lesion studies, suggesting that highway networks perform input-dependent routing and use depth adaptively.

The approach is closely related to prior work on LSTMs for stable long-range credit assignment in time, skip connections and deep supervision for improved information flow, and methods for better initialization and activation functions. It differs by providing multiplicative, input-dependent “information highways” within deep feedforward networks (not only in time) and by demonstrating stable optimization at depths that challenge plain nets. The comparison to FitNets highlights that highway networks remove the need for staged distillation to train thin, deep models under tight parameter and compute budgets.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea that directly targets optimization difficulties of deep nets via learnable gating.
- Strong empirical evidence that depth does not hinder optimization: highway networks with 50–100 layers train as easily as much shallower ones, and converge faster than plain networks with variance-preserving initialization.
- Competitive generalization on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with relatively few parameters; code and hyperparameters are made available.
- Insightful analyses: visualization of transform gate biases and activities shows sparse, input-dependent routing; lesion studies quantify per-layer contributions and reveal adaptive use of depth depending on task complexity.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited scale: results are not reported on large-scale benchmarks such as ImageNet, where the practical value of training hundreds of layers is most consequential.
- Comparisons: while the FitNet comparison is relevant, additional comparisons to strong contemporary baselines (e.g., very deep plain nets with careful initialization/regularization, locally competitive networks) under matched parameter and augmentation budgets would strengthen the claims.
- Ablations: the choice C=1−T is natural but restrictive; experiments with independent carry gates could clarify whether complementary gating is critical. Similarly, a systematic ablation of gate bias initialization and schedules would be useful.
- Overhead: the computational and memory costs introduced by gating (parameters, multiplications) are not quantified; a runtime analysis would help practitioners.
- Architectural details: some implementation specifics (e.g., exact convolutional configurations, padding/stride policies, normalization or regularization used) could be described more precisely for full reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement and additional experiments
- Large-scale evaluation: train deep convolutional highway networks on ImageNet with standard data augmentation to assess scalability, parameter efficiency, and training speed relative to strong baselines.
- Depth vs. width trade-offs: systematically vary depth and width under fixed parameter or FLOP budgets to map when depth yields gains and when layers remain idle.
- Independent carry gates: compare C=1−T to learning C separately, and report training stability, convergence, and accuracy.
- Gating dynamics: report training curves for average gate activity, distribution of transform outputs, and the evolution of biases to better characterize optimization behavior.
- Cross-domain sharing experiment: train a single thin, very deep network with an early fork into two heads for MNIST and CIFAR. Analyze whether layers that are underutilized for MNIST become active for CIFAR at inference time, whether gate activity patterns mirror those observed in single-task models, and how efficiently the hierarchy shares computation across domains. This would directly test the hypothesis that highway gating enables efficient, input- and task-dependent routing.
- Robustness: evaluate sensitivity to initialization schemes and optimizers, and test with or without auxiliary techniques (e.g., batch normalization) to position highways within the broader training toolbox.

Assessment
- Quality: The technical proposal is sound and well-motivated by LSTM principles. Claims are supported by optimization studies, competitive results on standard datasets, and careful qualitative analyses. Some ablations and large-scale tests would further solidify the evidence.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. Minor editorial issues aside, the figures and analyses clearly illustrate the gating behavior and the concept of information highways. More precise architectural and runtime details would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: Using LSTM-style adaptive gates to create input-dependent skip pathways in deep feedforward networks is a novel and timely contribution that differs from fixed skip connections and staged distillation.
- Significance: High. If highways reliably enable training of extremely deep networks with standard optimization, the impact on practice and subsequent architectures is substantial. The analyses offer a useful lens on how depth is used and when.

Pros
- Simple, elegant mechanism enabling training of very deep networks
- Strong optimization results; faster convergence
- Competitive accuracy with thin models under limited budgets
- Insightful analyses of gating and layer importance; released code

Cons
- No large-scale experiments (e.g., ImageNet)
- Limited ablations and runtime analysis
- Comparisons could be broadened under matched budgets

Recommendation
Strong accept. The idea is compelling, the empirical evidence is solid for the scale tested, and the work is likely to influence how very deep networks are designed and trained. I encourage the authors to include larger-scale results and the suggested analyses to further strengthen the final version.
