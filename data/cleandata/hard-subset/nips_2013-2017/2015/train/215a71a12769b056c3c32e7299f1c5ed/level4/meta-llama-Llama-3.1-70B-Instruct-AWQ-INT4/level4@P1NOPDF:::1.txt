I like the paper, as the idea is well-described and the experiments are convincing to a certain degree. The most notable aspect, in my opinion, is that the authors attempted to analyze the learned networks with respect to the pattern of gate outputs.

However, there are several points that need to be addressed. Firstly, the gate unit T is modeled using a standard sigmoid activation, which means the response value is never exactly 0 or 1 (as the authors themselves noted). Consequently, the gradients in equation 5 are not correct. The authors should provide a clear explanation in the paper of how backpropagation is performed in these networks.

Secondly, analyzing the effect of initialization is often challenging, but the authors' proposal to use constant negative biases depending on the number of levels is not entirely satisfactory. I would like to see a plot of the performance with respect to the initial value of the bias. The initial bias acts as a kind of regularizer, with a high negative bias supporting networks with "longer highways" (a comparison I find particularly apt).

Thirdly, the statement "as reported by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is somewhat misleading. Either the methods use the same experimental setting, allowing for a direct comparison, or they do not, which would preclude such a comparison. The authors need to clarify the differences in experimental setups in the paper.

Fourthly, presenting "max" accuracies in tables is unconventional and statistically unreasonable. Instead, the authors should consistently provide mean accuracy and standard deviation for every method (and when available).

Lastly, it would be intriguing to study the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The paper describes how to use additional gate units in neural networks that allow layers to act as an identity. The gate unit response depends on the input, enabling the propagation of inputs through a large number of very selective layers.
