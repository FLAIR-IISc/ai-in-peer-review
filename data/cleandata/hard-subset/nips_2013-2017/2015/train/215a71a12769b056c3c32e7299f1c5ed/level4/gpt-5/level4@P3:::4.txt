Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses learnable gates, inspired by LSTM recurrent networks, to facilitate information flow across many layers. Each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transformation (e.g., affine + nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a transform gate (with a complementary carry gate C = 1 − T). By biasing T toward carrying (via negative gate biases), the model creates “information highways” that mitigate vanishing signal and gradient issues in very deep nets. The authors show that highway networks with dozens to hundreds of layers can be trained directly with SGD, without special pretraining or multi-stage procedures.

The paper situates the method within a broad literature on deep architectures, including initialization schemes for stable training, activation functions with local competition, skip/auxiliary connections, and teacher-student distillation (e.g., FitNets). The key novelty is replacing fixed skip connections with input-dependent, multiplicative gating—an LSTM-inspired mechanism—for dynamic routing in feedforward networks. Experiments compare highway networks against plain nets with normalized initialization, and against FitNets on CIFAR-10/100, with additional analyses on gate behavior and layer importance.

Strengths:
- Clear architectural idea linking LSTM-style gating to very deep feedforward networks; well-motivated by the need to preserve information and gradients as depth grows.
- Strong optimization results: unlike plain nets, highway nets maintain trainability as depth increases, converging faster and to better minima on MNIST.
- Competitive generalization on CIFAR-10/100 with relatively modest augmentation, and single-stage training compared with FitNets’ teacher-student scheme.
- Insightful analyses of gate activations, dynamic routing, and layer lesioning that support the claim that gating is both an optimization aid and part of the computation.
- Simple, practical initialization heuristic (negative gate bias) that appears robust across depths and architectures.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical scope is limited to MNIST and CIFAR; results on larger-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) would better substantiate claims about training “hundreds of layers.”
- Comparisons to alternative stabilization techniques (e.g., batch normalization, other forms of residual/skip connections) are not presented; this makes it hard to disentangle the unique benefits of gating versus simpler identity skip paths.
- Ablations are incomplete: the effect of the C = 1 − T constraint, independent carry gates, gate parameterization (depthwise shared vs layer-specific), and sensitivity to the gate bias are not systematically evaluated.
- Computational overhead (parameters, FLOPs, inference latency) relative to plain and skip-connected baselines is not reported; it is important to quantify the cost of gating.
- Some training details and hyperparameter choices (e.g., extent of random search across methods, regularization) could be more clearly described for fair comparisons.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound and well-grounded in prior work on gating. Claims about optimization are supported by controlled experiments across depth, and the analyses are thoughtful. However, broader baselines (e.g., batch norm, fixed skip/residual connections) and larger-scale experiments would strengthen the evidence for superiority beyond trainability on small/medium datasets.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The architectural definition is clear, and the analyses are informative. A few notational details could be streamlined (e.g., explicitly state dimensionality requirements and choices when changing widths; clarify when nonlinearity is applied in H). Including a schematic block diagram and pseudo-code for a highway layer would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: The approach is novel in combining LSTM-style gates with feedforward depth to enable dynamic, input-dependent shortcuts. While related to prior skip connections and deep supervision, the multiplicative gating and its initialization represent a distinct contribution.
- Significance: Enabling successful training of very deep networks is important. The method provides a practical recipe and analytical tools for understanding depth usage. The impact would be higher with stronger head-to-head comparisons against contemporary alternatives and with results on more challenging datasets.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Include comparisons to networks with fixed identity skip connections (and, if feasible, batch normalization), matching parameter counts and depth, to isolate the advantage of gating.
- Report parameter counts, FLOPs, training time, and inference time for highway vs plain/skip baselines.
- Provide ablation studies: independent carry gates vs C = 1 − T; different gate nonlinearities; effect of removing negative bias initialization; sharing gate parameters across layers; varying the proportion of highway vs plain layers.
- Evaluate deeper convolutional highway networks (e.g., >50 layers) on CIFAR-10/100 and, if possible, a preliminary ImageNet result to substantiate scalability claims.
- Clarify the hyperparameter search space and ensure equivalent search effort across baselines; detail regularization (dropout, weight decay) and data augmentation.
- Minor editorial: fix small typos (e.g., duplicated words), unify notation, and add a simple layer diagram.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually elegant and practically effective architecture that addresses the depth-related optimization bottleneck.
- Demonstrates direct, single-stage training of very deep models with good generalization.
- Provides valuable analyses (gating behavior, dynamic routing, and lesion studies) that deepen understanding of depth utilization.
- Simple implementation and initialization, likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks strong baselines against alternative stabilization techniques.
- Computational trade-offs of gating are not quantified.
- Some key design choices (e.g., C = 1 − T) are not ablated, leaving open questions about the minimal ingredients needed.

Overall assessment:
This is a compelling and timely contribution that advances our ability to train very deep networks via LSTM-inspired gating. While the empirical scope and baselines could be expanded, the paper’s core idea, demonstrated trainability, and insightful analyses make it a valuable addition to the literature on deep architectures. I recommend acceptance, conditional on clarifying comparisons and expanding ablations where feasible.
