This paper proposes using LSTM-inspired gating to enable the training of very deep networks. It is a high-quality, well-written manuscript. The idea is original, and the empirical results are compelling.

While adding layers often improves performance, practical depth has long been constrained by initialization challenges and vanishing/exploding gradients—issues that arguably hindered widespread adoption of very deep networks for decades. This work tackles those obstacles directly and demonstrates a concrete method for successfully training networks on the order of 100 layers.

The analyses on information routing and layer-wise importance are particularly insightful and help illuminate how depth is utilized in practice.

Specific comments:
- In Section 4.1, the statement “most transform blocks are active on average” appears to be a typo or misstatement. Based on the surrounding discussion and figures, I believe it should read “not active on average.”
- One can initialize each layer of a very deep network to the identity matrix (perhaps with small noise). It would be useful to clarify how the transform gates in highway networks differ from, and improve upon, this simple identity-initialization baseline. In particular, please discuss the benefits the gates provide beyond preserving signal flow at initialization (e.g., dynamic routing, selective transformation, or training stability over the course of learning).

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a clear and effective method for training very deep neural networks. It was a pleasure to read.
