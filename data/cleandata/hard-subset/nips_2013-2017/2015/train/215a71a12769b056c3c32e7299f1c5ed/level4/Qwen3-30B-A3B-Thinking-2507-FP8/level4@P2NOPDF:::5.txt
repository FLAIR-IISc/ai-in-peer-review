

This paper presents a compelling extension of the LSTM gradient-trapping technique to train extremely deep feedforward networks, offering a simple yet elegant solution to vanishing gradients. The approach is well-motivated, the analysis is rigorous, and the results demonstrate clear empirical advantages over standard deep networks. The contribution is significant for advancing the practical training of very deep architectures.

I appreciate the authors' insight into the relationship between their method and hierarchical network design. Their work aligns with a broader perspective where "very deep" networks can be viewed as "not-so-deep" networks composed of blocks containing complex sub-layers. This resonates with architectural innovations like GoogLeNet's inception modules and VGG's stacked 3×3 convolutions, where depth is achieved through layered complexity rather than sequential layer stacking. I find it particularly intriguing whether highway networks—where residual connections enable signal propagation—can be interpreted through this lens of hierarchical sub-layers, potentially unifying these design philosophies. The authors might explore this connection further in their discussion.

Regarding experimental validation, I recommend a single-network multi-domain test to probe parameter sharing efficiency. Specifically, train one thin-and-tall network (with an early branching point) on both MNIST and CIFAR-10. At inference, examine whether the model exhibits similar parameter utilization patterns as two separately trained networks. Crucially, assess whether features underutilized for MNIST (e.g., high-frequency detail detectors) become active for CIFAR-10, revealing how domain-specific knowledge is shared across the deep hierarchy. This would clarify whether the network's sharing mechanism is optimal or if architectural constraints limit efficiency. The authors' current experiments focus on single-domain performance; this extension would provide deeper insight into the network's representational capacity under multi-task learning.

The paper’s core idea is robust, with thorough ablation studies supporting its claims. I have no major technical concerns. The authors should consider expanding the evaluation to larger benchmarks like ImageNet to demonstrate scalability beyond MNIST and CIFAR-10, which would strengthen the practical relevance of their approach. While the current results are convincing, ImageNet validation would address potential limitations in generalizing to more complex visual recognition tasks.

In summary, this work makes a valuable contribution to deep network training. The LSTM-inspired technique is elegantly applied, the theoretical foundation is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The suggested multi-domain experiment would further enrich the discussion of parameter sharing in deep hierarchies. I strongly support publication.
