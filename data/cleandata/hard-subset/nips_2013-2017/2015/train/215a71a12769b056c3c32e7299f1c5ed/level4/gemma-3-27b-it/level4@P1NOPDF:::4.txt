The paper proposes a generalization of training for deep, stacked neural networks. The “information highway” concept is well-illustrated through experimentation, and the authors demonstrate improved classification accuracy using this method.

**Quality:** The idea appears novel, and the experimentation is solid. The analysis sections could benefit from a greater variety of details or illustrative examples.

**Clarity:** The paper’s main messages are well-delivered and clear.

**Originality:** The proposed method is sufficiently original, representing a generalization of existing neural network techniques.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates significant relevance to the field, as training increasingly deep neural networks is crucial for achieving better results in many application areas. 

The paper describes an effective method for training very deep neural networks through the use of “information highways”—direct connections built to upper network layers. While a generalization of prior techniques like cross-layer connections, the authors convincingly demonstrate the method’s effectiveness through experimentation. The contributions are novel and well-supported by experimental evidence.

The core concept of information highways – allowing information to flow more easily through deep networks – feels intuitively important. The paper clearly explains how these highways are constructed and integrated into the network architecture. The experimental results are presented in a straightforward manner, and demonstrate consistent improvements in classification accuracy compared to baseline models. 

However, the analysis of *why* these information highways work so well could be expanded. While the authors present results, a deeper investigation into the learned highway weights and their influence on gradient flow would be valuable. For example, visualizing which connections are most frequently used, or analyzing the magnitude of the weights, could provide insights into the network’s learning process. It would also be helpful to see ablation studies examining the impact of different highway configurations (e.g., varying the number of highways, or their placement within the network). 

The comparisons to existing techniques are reasonable, but a more thorough comparison with other methods specifically designed to address the vanishing gradient problem in deep networks (e.g., batch normalization, residual networks) would strengthen the paper. It’s unclear how the proposed method compares to these techniques in terms of performance, computational cost, and ease of implementation.

Despite these minor shortcomings, the paper presents a promising approach to training deep neural networks. The idea is well-motivated, the experimental results are compelling, and the writing is generally clear. I believe this work has the potential to influence future research in this area, and would recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
