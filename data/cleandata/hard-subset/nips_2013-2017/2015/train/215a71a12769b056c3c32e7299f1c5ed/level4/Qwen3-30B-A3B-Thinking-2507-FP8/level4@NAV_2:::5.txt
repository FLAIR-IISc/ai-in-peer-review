

The paper introduces highway networks, an architecture inspired by LSTM recurrent networks, designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via simple gradient descent. This work builds upon key NIPS contributions such as LSTM [30], Fitnets [25] (which required two-stage training with soft targets), and previous studies on depth limitations [16, 17, 28]. The authors demonstrate that adaptive gating mechanisms (transform and carry gates) allow information to flow unimpeded across layers, overcoming the vanishing gradient problem that plagues plain networks. Crucially, highway networks achieve superior optimization and generalization compared to plain networks of equivalent depth, as validated on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

**Strengths**  
- **Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with rigorous experiments showing highway networks outperform plain networks by >2 orders of magnitude at 100 layers (Section 3.1). The analysis of gate biases (Figure 2) and layer importance (Figure 4) provides compelling evidence for the mechanism’s efficacy.  
- **Originality**: The adaptive gating mechanism represents a novel architectural innovation distinct from prior work (e.g., fixed skip connections [22, 23] or Fitnets’ two-stage training [25]). It directly addresses depth-related training challenges without relying on problem-specific initialization.  
- **Significance**: By enabling direct training of very deep networks, the work opens new avenues for studying depth-accuracy relationships, advancing the state-of-the-art in network depth scalability beyond prior methods. The results on CIFAR-10 (Table 2) demonstrate competitive performance with significantly fewer parameters than state-of-the-art approaches.  
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-structured, with clear notation (Section 2) and thorough experimental details (Section 3). The highway network formulation (Equation 3) elegantly generalizes plain networks while enabling dynamic information routing.

**Weaknesses**  
- **Scope Limitation**: The evaluation is confined to MNIST/CIFAR datasets; testing on ImageNet would strengthen claims about scalability to larger, more complex tasks.  
- **Gating Mechanism Sensitivity**: While the negative bias initialization (Section 2.2) is effective, the paper does not explore how sensitive results are to this hyperparameter across diverse architectures or datasets.  
- **Comparison to ResNets**: The discussion omits comparison to Residual Networks (ResNets) [35], which also use skip connections and have achieved similar depth scalability. Clarifying how highway networks differ (e.g., dynamic vs. fixed routing) would enhance the contribution.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments**  
*Pro*:  
- Solves a fundamental problem (training very deep networks) with a simple, generalizable architecture.  
- Experimental results are robust, reproducible, and demonstrate clear advantages over prior methods.  
- Provides new insights into layer utilization (Figure 4) and information routing (Section 4.1).  

*Con*:  
- Limited to small-scale datasets (no ImageNet results).  
- Insufficient comparison to ResNets, a dominant approach for deep networks.  

This paper makes a significant contribution to deep learning by enabling direct training of extremely deep networks, advancing the field beyond the limitations of prior work. It is technically rigorous, well-motivated, and addresses a critical problem in network depth. With minor revisions (e.g., ImageNet experiments and ResNet comparison), it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
