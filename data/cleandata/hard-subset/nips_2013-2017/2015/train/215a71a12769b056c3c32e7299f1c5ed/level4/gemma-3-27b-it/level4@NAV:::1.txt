This paper presents a promising approach, with a clearly articulated idea and experiments that provide supporting evidence, though with some limitations. A significant strength lies in the authors’ attempt to interpret the learned networks by analyzing gate output patterns.

(1) The gate unit T utilizes a standard sigmoid activation, resulting in outputs strictly between 0 and 1, as acknowledged by the authors. Consequently, the gradients presented in equation 5 appear inaccurate. The paper would benefit from a detailed explanation of the backpropagation procedure employed in these networks.

(2) While the impact of initialization is generally complex to assess, the proposed strategy of using constant negative biases scaled by the number of levels requires further justification. Including a plot illustrating performance variations with respect to different initial bias values would be valuable. The initial bias effectively functions as a regularizer, favoring networks with “longer highways” – a comparison I found insightful. 

(3) The statement “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” lacks precision. The authors should either explicitly demonstrate comparability by detailing identical experimental settings, or acknowledge and delineate the differences preventing a direct comparison. Clarity on these experimental setups is crucial.

(4) Reporting only maximum accuracies in tables is atypical and statistically questionable. I recommend consistently presenting mean accuracy alongside standard deviation for all methods, where available.

(5) Exploring the theoretical underpinnings of this work from a learning theory perspective would be highly impactful. The paper introduces additional gate units that effectively enable layers to function as identity mappings. Given the input-dependent gate response, this allows for signal propagation through numerous highly selective layers, a potentially significant contribution worthy of further theoretical investigation.
