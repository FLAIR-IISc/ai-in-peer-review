I find the paper promising: the core idea is clearly presented and the empirical results are reasonably persuasive. What I especially appreciate is the attempt to probe the learned models by examining the gating patterns.

(1) The transform gate T uses a standard sigmoid, so its outputs are never exactly 0 or 1 (as the paper also notes). Consequently, the Jacobian expressions in Eq. 5 are not strictly correct as stated. Please clarify precisely how backpropagation is performed in these networks, including the exact derivatives used through the gating pathway.

(2) While initialization effects are notoriously hard to disentangle, recommending fixed negative gate biases as a function of depth is not fully satisfactory. Please include a plot showing performance as a function of the initial transform-gate bias. In this setup, the initial bias effectively acts as a form of regularization, with more negative values favoring “longer highways” (a useful analogy).

(3) The phrase “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is too vague. Either the experimental settings are identical, enabling a fair comparison, or they differ, in which case a direct comparison is not warranted. Please make the differences in experimental protocols explicit in the paper.

(4) Reporting “max” accuracies in tables is unusual and statistically uninformative. Please report mean accuracy and standard deviation for each method (when available) throughout.

(5) It would be valuable to examine the approach from a learning-theoretic perspective. The proposed gating mechanism allows layers to approximate an identity mapping, and because the gate outputs depend on the input, information can be routed across many layers in a highly selective, data-dependent manner. A theoretical treatment of this property would substantially strengthen the contribution.
