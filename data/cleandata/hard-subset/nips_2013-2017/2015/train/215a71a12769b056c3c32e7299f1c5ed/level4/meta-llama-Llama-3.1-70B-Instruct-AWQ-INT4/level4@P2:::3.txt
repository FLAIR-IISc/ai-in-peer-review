This paper proposes the use of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units as a method of training very deep networks by introducing a new architecture called highway networks. 

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere in that it addresses the problem of training very deep networks, which has been a long-standing challenge in the field of machine learning. Many recent empirical breakthroughs in supervised machine learning have been achieved through large and deep neural networks, but the difficulties of initialization and decaying/exploding gradients have limited their use. The authors draw inspiration from LSTM recurrent networks and use adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow in highway networks.

The paper is well-written and of high quality. The idea is original, and the results are quite interesting. Machine learning researchers frequently find that they get better results by adding more and more layers to their neural networks, but the difficulties of initialization and decaying/exploding gradients have been severely limiting. Indeed, the difficulties of getting information to flow through deep neural networks arguably kept them out of widespread use for 30 years. This paper addresses this problem head-on and demonstrates one method for training 100-layer nets.

The strengths of the paper include its original idea, well-designed experiments, and interesting results. The experiments showing the routing of information and layer importance are particularly noteworthy. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), and they provide evidence that the transform gates in highway networks help to route information through the network in a way that is dependent on the input.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods for training very deep networks. However, the authors do provide some comparisons with plain networks and FitNets, which suggests that highway networks are a promising approach.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

The originality of the paper lies in its proposal of a new architecture for training very deep networks. The authors draw inspiration from LSTM recurrent networks and use adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow in highway networks. This is a novel combination of familiar techniques, and it is clear how this work differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to advance the state of the art in deep learning. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly using SGD, and they provide evidence that the transform gates in highway networks help to route information through the network in a way that is dependent on the input. This could have important implications for a variety of applications, including image and speech recognition.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a new architecture for training very deep networks, which is a significant contribution to the field of machine learning.
* The authors provide well-designed experiments and interesting results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods for training very deep networks.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their approach.

Specific comments:

* In Section 4.1, it seems that 'most transform blocks are active on average' should be changed to 'not active on average.'
* It might be worth commenting on how transform gates differentiate highway networks from a simple scheme of initializing each layer of a very deep network with the identity matrix (possibly with a small amount of noise).

Overall, this is a great paper that demonstrates a method for training very deep neural networks. It was a pleasure to read, and I would recommend it for acceptance.
