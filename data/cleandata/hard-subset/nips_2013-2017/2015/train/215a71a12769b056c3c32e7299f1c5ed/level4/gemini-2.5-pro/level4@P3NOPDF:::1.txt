This paper introduces a novel architecture, termed "Highway Networks," designed to enable the effective training of extremely deep neural networks. The core idea is to incorporate gating units that adaptively control the flow of information through the network, allowing unimpeded "highways" for gradients to travel. The concept is well-described and intuitive, and the empirical results are convincing to a degree. A particular strength of this work is the authors' effort to analyze the behavior of the learned networks by examining the patterns of the gate outputs, which provides valuable insight into their function.

While the paper presents a promising direction, there are several areas that require clarification and improvement to strengthen the contribution.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarity on Backpropagation with Sigmoid Gates:** The transform gate unit, T, is modeled with a standard sigmoid activation. As the authors correctly state, the output of this gate is therefore always in the open interval (0, 1) and never exactly 0 or 1. However, the simplified gradient derivation in Equation 5 appears to assume a binary outcome for the gate. This simplification is conceptually useful but technically incorrect for the actual implementation. For the sake of clarity and reproducibility, it is crucial that the authors explicitly provide the full gradient equations used during backpropagation, which must account for the continuous-valued nature of the gates and the gradients flowing through both the transformation path and the identity path simultaneously.

2.  **Analysis of Transform Gate Bias Initialization:** The proposal to initialize the transform gate biases with a constant negative value is an interesting and practical heuristic, but it is not fully justified. This initial bias acts as a form of regularization, predisposing the network to carry information through the identity "highways," which likely eases optimization in the early stages of training. However, the sensitivity of the model to this specific choice of initialization is not explored. To strengthen the paper, an ablation study investigating the model's performance across a range of initial bias values would be highly valuable. A plot of final accuracy as a function of the initial bias would provide a more principled understanding of this mechanism's robustness and impact.

3.  **Rigor in Experimental Comparisons:** The paper compares its performance to results "reported by recent studies in similar experimental settings." This phrasing is too ambiguous for a rigorous scientific comparison. To claim state-of-the-art performance, the experimental conditions must be identical, or any differences must be clearly articulated and their potential effects discussed. The authors need to clarify whether they re-implemented and re-ran the baseline methods under their exact experimental setup or, if quoting results from other papers, meticulously detail any discrepancies in data preprocessing, augmentation, network architecture, or other hyperparameters that could influence the final performance.

4.  **Statistical Soundness of Results:** The results in the tables are presented as "max" accuracies. This is unconventional and statistically fragile, as a single outlier run can produce an unrepresentatively high score. Standard practice requires reporting the mean and standard deviation over multiple trials (e.g., with different random seeds) to provide a fair assessment of a method's expected performance and stability. The authors should revise their tables to include mean and standard deviation for all reported results, both for their own model and for the baselines, where available.

**Minor Comments & Future Directions:**

The proposed approach is also interesting from a theoretical standpoint. By using input-dependent gates, highway networks create a functional architecture of adaptive depth, where simpler inputs might traverse fewer transformative layers. It would be interesting to see a discussion of how this work relates to concepts from learning theory, such as conditional computation or dynamic model regularization. While not essential for acceptance, exploring this connection could broaden the paper's impact.

In summary, this is a well-written paper with a strong central idea. Addressing the points above—particularly regarding the gradient formulation, bias initialization analysis, and rigor of the experimental evaluation—would significantly improve the quality and credibility of this work.
