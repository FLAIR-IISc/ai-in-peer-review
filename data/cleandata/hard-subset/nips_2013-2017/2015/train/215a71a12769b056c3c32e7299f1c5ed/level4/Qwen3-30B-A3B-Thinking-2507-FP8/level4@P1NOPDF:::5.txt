

I also considered adapting the LSTM gradient-stabilization technique to grow deep feedforward networks, but hadn't initiated this work yet—congratulations to the authors for implementing it successfully.  

An intriguing perspective emerges: very deep networks may be reinterpreted as "not-so-deep" architectures where individual layers incorporate complex sub-structures. Each layer thus exhibits richer non-linear behavior than standard rectifier/sigmoid units. This aligns with the design philosophy behind GoogLeNet’s inception modules, VGG’s stacked 3×3 convolutions, and potentially extends to highway networks. I wonder if this framing offers a valid lens for understanding the proposed approach.  

I recommend an additional experiment: train a single tall-and-thin network to handle both MNIST and CIFAR-10 by splitting the architecture early (e.g., sharing weights up to a certain layer, then branching for domain-specific processing). The key question is whether the network’s parameter utilization at inference mirrors that of separately trained models. Specifically, does CIFAR-10 effectively leverage parameters underutilized by MNIST, and how efficiently does parameter sharing occur within the deep hierarchical structure? This would clarify whether shared representations are optimally exploited.  

The paper presents a simple, elegant idea inspired by LSTM gradient management, enabling stable training of extremely deep feedforward networks. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. No significant concerns arise. I encourage the authors to validate the approach on larger benchmarks like ImageNet to assess scalability and broader applicability.
