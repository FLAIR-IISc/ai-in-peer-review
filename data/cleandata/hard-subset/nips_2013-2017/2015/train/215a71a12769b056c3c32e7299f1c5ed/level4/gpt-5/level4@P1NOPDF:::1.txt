I like the paper: the core idea is well presented, and the experiments are convincing to a certain degree. The strongest aspect, in my view, is the authors’ attempt to analyze the learned networks through the patterns of gate outputs.

1) The gate unit T is modeled with a standard sigmoid activation. As the authors note, its output is never exactly 0 or 1. Consequently, the gradients in Eq. 5 are not correct as written. The paper should explain precisely how backpropagation is performed in these networks: how gradients are computed through the gates, and whether any approximations or surrogate gradients are used.

2) The effect of initialization is difficult to analyze, but the proposal to use constant negative biases that depend on the number of levels is not fully satisfactory. I would like to see a plot of performance as a function of the initial bias value. In this setting, the initial bias effectively acts as a regularizer: a strongly negative bias favors networks with “longer highways” (I like this analogy), while less negative biases may encourage more transformation. Quantifying this trade-off would strengthen the claims.

3) The sentence “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is unsatisfying. Either the methods use the same experimental setting, which permits a fair comparison, or they do not, which precludes a direct comparison. The paper should clearly describe the differences among the experimental setups to justify any cross-paper comparisons.

4) Reporting “max” accuracies in tables is uncommon and not statistically sound. Please report mean accuracy and standard deviation for every method (when available), based on multiple runs. This will make the comparisons more reliable and reproducible.

5) It would be interesting to study the proposed approach from a learning-theoretic perspective. The paper describes adding gate units that allow layers to act as identities. Because the gate response depends on the input, the network can propagate inputs through many layers in a highly selective manner, effectively routing information along “highways.” Formal analysis of when and why this improves optimization or generalization would be valuable.

Overall, this is a promising paper with a clear idea and interesting analysis of gate behaviors. Addressing the points above—especially clarifying backpropagation through the gates, providing a systematic study of initialization via bias sweeps, tightening the experimental comparisons, and reporting proper statistics—would substantially strengthen the work.
