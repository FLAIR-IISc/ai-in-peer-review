This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate training of very deep neural networks. The core idea – inspired by LSTM recurrent networks – is to incorporate adaptive gating mechanisms allowing for unimpeded information flow across many layers. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be successfully trained with standard gradient descent, even with hundreds of layers, and provide analysis of the learned gate behavior. This work addresses a critical challenge in deep learning: the difficulty of optimizing very deep architectures.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, proposed method, and experimental results. The connection to LSTM networks is insightful, and the analysis of the learned gate activations (Figure 2 and 4) offers valuable intuition into how these networks function. The experimental validation on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of highway networks compared to plain networks and existing methods like FitNets. The authors appropriately relate their work to previous research on deep learning, initialization strategies, and skip connections. 

However, there are some areas for improvement. Firstly, the gate unit *T* employs a sigmoid activation, meaning its output is never exactly 0 or 1. The authors acknowledge this but should clarify precisely how backpropagation is implemented with this constraint, and whether the stated Jacobians in Equation 5 are strictly accurate. Secondly, while the negative bias initialization for the transform gates is effective, a more detailed analysis of its impact would be beneficial. A plot showing performance versus the initial bias value could illuminate its role as a regularizer, supporting the authors' observation regarding “longer highways”.  Thirdly, the phrase "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is vague and requires clarification. The authors should explicitly detail the differences (or similarities) between the experimental setups being referenced. Fourthly, reporting only “max” accuracies in Tables 1-3 is statistically questionable; the authors should consistently report mean accuracy and standard deviation. Finally, it would be valuable to explore this work from a learning theory perspective. The introduction of gate units allowing for layers to behave as identity mappings offers an interesting point for theoretical analysis regarding representational capacity and optimization landscapes.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training very deep networks.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated architecture (highway networks) inspired by successful recurrent network mechanisms.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating effective training and generalization on multiple datasets.
*   Insightful analysis of the learned gate behavior, providing valuable intuition into the network’s operation.
*   Clear writing and a thorough review of related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor technical inaccuracies regarding the Jacobian calculation with the sigmoid gate.
*   Limited analysis of the impact of the transform gate bias initialization.
*   Vague comparative statements lacking specific details on experimental setups.
*   Statistically weak presentation of results (reporting only maximum accuracies).
*   Lack of theoretical analysis regarding the learning-theoretic implications of the proposed architecture.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. While some refinements are needed, the novel architecture, solid experimental validation, and insightful analysis warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
