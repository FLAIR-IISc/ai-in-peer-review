

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture enabling direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units. The authors demonstrate that highway networks overcome the core challenge of vanishing gradients in very deep networks, allowing simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD) training without specialized initialization or multi-stage procedures. This addresses a fundamental limitation in deep learning that has constrained network depth for decades. The work builds directly on prior NIPS contributions, including LSTM networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, NIPS 1997), skip connections (Sermanet et al., NIPS 2013), and knowledge distillation (Romero et al., NIPS 2014), but provides a more general solution that avoids the need for teacher networks or layer-wise pretraining.

**Strengths**  
*Originality and Significance*: The highway architecture represents a significant conceptual advance. By introducing adaptive gating units that regulate information flow across layers, the authors enable direct training of networks with unprecedented depth—demonstrated on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. This directly tackles the "depth barrier" that has hindered progress in deep learning. The method’s generality (applicable to convolutional, fully connected, and recurrent networks) and its ability to train networks with 100+ layers using standard SGD make it highly impactful. The paper’s experimental results are compelling: 100-layer highway networks outperform similarly sized plain networks by >100× in optimization error, while achieving competitive accuracy on CIFAR-10 (92.7% vs. 85.5% for plain networks) without requiring multi-stage training.  

*Quality and Clarity*: The technical exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-motivated mathematical formalism (Equations 2–5) and intuitive explanations of the "information highways" concept. The experimental design is rigorous, including ablation studies on layer importance (Figure 4), analysis of gate dynamics (Figure 2), and controlled comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (Fitnets, Ciresan et al.). The analysis of data-dependent gating (Section 4.1) is particularly insightful, revealing how highway networks dynamically route information based on input—evidenced by sparse gate activation per sample despite high average activity. The code and hyperparameters are publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.  

**Weaknesses**  
The paper could strengthen its connection to prior work on identity initialization. While the negative bias initialization for transform gates is well-justified (inspired by LSTM gating), it would be valuable to explicitly contrast highway networks with the simpler approach of initializing layers with identity matrices (or near-identity transforms). The authors note that highway networks "are not equivalent to identity initialization" (Section 2.2), but a brief discussion of why this is insufficient for deep training would clarify the novelty. For example: *Identity initialization alone fails to adaptively regulate information flow across layers, whereas highway gates learn to selectively transform or carry information per input, enabling stable gradients at scale*.  

Section 4.1 contains a minor but important clarification: the phrase "most transform blocks are active on average" (p. 12) should be "most transform gates are active on average" (consistent with the paper’s terminology). The current phrasing risks confusion with "blocks" (the computational units), whereas the analysis refers to gate activation.  

**Recommendation**  
This is a high-quality, technically sound contribution that advances the state of the art in deep learning. The highway network architecture resolves a long-standing challenge in training very deep networks with minimal overhead (a single gating layer per highway layer), and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The work is directly relevant to NIPS’ focus on foundational machine learning advances, with clear implications for practitioners building deep architectures.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Solves a critical problem (training depth) with a simple, generalizable solution.  
- Demonstrates clear empirical superiority over prior methods (plain networks, Fitnets).  
- Provides novel insights into information routing and layer utilization.  
- Well-written, reproducible, and aligned with NIPS scope.  

*Con*:  
- Minor clarification needed on the distinction from identity initialization (addressed above).  
- The paper could briefly acknowledge related work on residual networks (He et al., CVPR 2016), though these emerged post-NIPS 2015.  

The paper’s significance is underscored by its potential to enable deeper, more efficient architectures across vision, speech, and NLP. Given its technical rigor, novelty, and impact, it merits acceptance. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field, and this work will likely become a standard reference for deep network training.
