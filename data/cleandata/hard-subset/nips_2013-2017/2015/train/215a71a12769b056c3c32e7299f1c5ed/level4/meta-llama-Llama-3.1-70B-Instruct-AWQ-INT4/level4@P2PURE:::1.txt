I appreciate the paper, as the idea is well-articulated and the experiments are convincing to a certain extent. The most notable aspect, in my opinion, is the authors' effort to analyze the learned networks in terms of the pattern of gate outputs.

However, there are several concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the gate unit T is modeled using a standard sigmoid activation function, which means the response value never reaches exactly 0 or 1, as also acknowledged by the authors. Consequently, the gradients in Equation 5 are not accurate. The authors should provide a detailed explanation of how backpropagation is performed in these networks.

Secondly, the impact of initialization is often challenging to analyze, but the authors' proposal to use constant negative biases based on the number of levels is not entirely satisfactory. A plot illustrating the performance with respect to the initial bias value would be helpful. The initial bias effectively acts as a regularizer, with a high negative bias favoring networks with "longer highways," a comparison that I find insightful.

Thirdly, the statement "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is unclear. If the methods employ the same experimental setting, it would allow for a direct comparison; otherwise, it would be inappropriate to compare them directly. The authors should clarify the differences in experimental setups within the paper.

Fourthly, presenting "max" accuracies in tables is unconventional and statistically unreasonable. Instead, the authors should consistently report mean accuracy and standard deviation for each method, whenever available.

Lastly, it would be intriguing to examine the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The paper describes how to utilize additional gate units in neural networks, enabling layers to act as an identity function. The gate unit response, which depends on the input, facilitates the propagation of inputs through a large number of highly selective layers.
