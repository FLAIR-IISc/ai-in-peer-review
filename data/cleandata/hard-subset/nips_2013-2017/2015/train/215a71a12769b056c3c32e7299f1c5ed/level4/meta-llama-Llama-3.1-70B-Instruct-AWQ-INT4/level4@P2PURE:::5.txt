I, too, had considered employing the LSTM trick of controlling gradient flow to grow networks deeper, but had yet to initiate the work. I congratulate the authors on being the first to explore this concept.

An alternative perspective on the proposed approach is that a very deep network can be viewed as a not-so-deep network comprising layers with sub-layers, where each "layer" exhibits more complex non-linear behavior than traditional rectifier or sigmoid layers. This perspective is reminiscent of the inception of GoogLeNet, where 3 stacked 3x3 layers are used in VGG, and it is intriguing to consider whether this viewpoint can be applied to highway networks as well.

One potential experiment that I would suggest the authors consider is training a single thin and tall network on both MNIST and CIFAR datasets (with a fork at the top for the two domains). I am particularly interested in determining whether, at inference time, the network exhibits the same pattern as separately trained networks for each domain, and whether the model parameters underutilized in MNIST are leveraged by CIFAR. In other words, I would like to see how sharing occurs in a very deep hierarchy and whether it is as efficient as possible.

Overall, I have no major complaints. I encourage the authors to apply their network to larger datasets, such as ImageNet, to further demonstrate its capabilities. This paper presents an LSTM-inspired idea that enables the training of very deep feedforward networks. The concept is simple and elegant, and the analysis is solid.
