This paper presents an intriguing idea that is well-explained, and the accompanying experiments are convincing to a certain degree. One of the strengths of the paper is the authors' effort to analyze the learned networks with respect to the pattern of gate outputs.

However, there are several aspects that require clarification and improvement. Firstly, the gate unit T is modeled using a standard sigmoid activation function. Consequently, the response value is never exactly 0 or 1, as noted by the authors. This discrepancy raises concerns about the accuracy of the gradients in Equation 5. The authors should provide a detailed explanation of how backpropagation is performed in these networks to address this issue.

Secondly, the effect of initialization is a complex aspect that is often challenging to analyze. The authors' proposal to use constant negative biases depending on the number of levels is not entirely convincing. To provide a more comprehensive understanding, it would be beneficial to include a plot illustrating the performance with respect to the initial value of the bias. The initial bias serves as a regularizer, with high negative biases favoring networks with "longer highways," a comparison that is both apt and insightful.

Thirdly, the statement "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is ambiguous. If the methods employ the same experimental setting, a direct comparison is warranted. Conversely, if the settings differ, a comparison is not feasible. The authors should clarify the differences in experimental setups to facilitate a more accurate assessment.

Furthermore, presenting "max" accuracies in tables is unconventional and statistically unsound. Instead, the authors should provide mean accuracy and standard deviation for every method, as well as any available additional information. This would offer a more comprehensive and reliable evaluation of the proposed approach.

Lastly, exploring the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective could yield valuable insights. The paper demonstrates how to utilize additional gate units in neural networks, enabling layers to act as an identity function. The gate unit response, which depends on the input, allows for the propagation of inputs through a large number of highly selective layers. A deeper analysis of this concept could provide a more profound understanding of the underlying mechanisms and their implications for neural network design.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea and promising results, addressing these concerns and providing additional clarification would strengthen the manuscript and enhance its contribution to the field.
