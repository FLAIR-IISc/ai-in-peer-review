This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate training very deep feedforward networks. The core idea is to enable unimpeded information flow across many layers via adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. This allows gradients to propagate more effectively, enabling successful training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent – a significant achievement given the difficulties encountered when training very deep “plain” networks.

The paper effectively situates its contribution within the broader context of recent advancements in deep learning. It acknowledges the crucial role of network depth in achieving state-of-the-art results in areas like image classification (citing Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Szegedy et al., 2015; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014) and references prior work attempting to address training difficulties, including improved optimizers (Martens et al., 2013), initialization strategies (Glorot & Bengio, 2010), and skip connections (Ciresan et al., 2012; Romero et al., 2015). The connection to LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) is clearly articulated as a key source of inspiration.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The highway network architecture offers a compelling solution to the vanishing gradient problem that plagues very deep networks. Second, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its experimental evaluation. The notation is consistent and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive. Third, the originality of the work is high; while building on existing concepts like LSTMs and skip connections, the highway network architecture represents a novel combination that demonstrably improves training and performance. Fourth, the experimental results are convincing, demonstrating that highway networks can be trained effectively and generalize well to unseen data on datasets like MNIST and CIFAR-10/100. Finally, the detailed analysis of the trained networks (Figures 2 & 4) provides valuable insights into how the gating mechanisms operate and how information is routed through the network – an aspect of the work that is particularly commendable. This level of investigation goes beyond simply demonstrating performance gains and offers a deeper understanding of the model’s behavior.

However, there are minor weaknesses. A typographical error exists on page 5 ("Tables 3 and 3"). While the paper references related work effectively, briefly discussing the limitations of FitNets (Romero et al., 2015) regarding computational budget might further highlight the advantage of highway networks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
*   Clear and concise writing with a well-organized presentation.
*   Strong experimental results on multiple datasets.
*   Insightful analysis of the learned gating mechanisms.
*   Significant contribution to the field of deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor typographical error.
*   Could benefit from a more detailed comparison to limitations of other recent techniques like FitNets.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed highway network architecture is a clever and effective solution to the challenges of training very deep networks, and the accompanying analysis provides valuable insights into the model’s behavior. Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper overwhelmingly outweigh them. I strongly recommend acceptance.
