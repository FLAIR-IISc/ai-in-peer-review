This paper presents a simple and elegant idea, inspired by LSTMs, that enables the training of very deep feedforward networks. The concept is well-motivated and the accompanying analysis is solid. The idea of using LSTM-like gating mechanisms to manage gradient flow in deep architectures is insightful, and the authors are to be commended for being the first to successfully implement and validate this approach.

An alternative perspective on this work could be considered. Rather than viewing the architecture as simply having many layers, one could see it as a moderately deep network composed of more complex "meta-layers" or blocks, each with a richer non-linear behavior than a standard layer. This conceptual framing is common for architectures like GoogLeNet's Inception modules or VGG's stacked 3x3 convolutions. It would be interesting if the authors discussed whether their highway networks can also be interpreted through this lens.

To further probe the properties of this deep architecture, I would suggest an experiment in a multi-task setting. For instance, a single, deep but narrow network could be trained to handle both MNIST and CIFAR-10, with a shared trunk that forks into separate heads for each domain. The key question would be to analyze if the parameter utilization patterns observed during inference on a single task are replicated in the multi-task model. Specifically, does the network learn to allocate parameters underutilized by the simpler MNIST task to the more complex CIFAR-10 task? Such an experiment would provide valuable insights into how feature sharing and hierarchical representation learning operate in these very deep models.

The paper has no major weaknesses. To further demonstrate the scalability and practical impact of the proposed method, I would encourage the authors to evaluate their network on larger-scale datasets, such as ImageNet.
