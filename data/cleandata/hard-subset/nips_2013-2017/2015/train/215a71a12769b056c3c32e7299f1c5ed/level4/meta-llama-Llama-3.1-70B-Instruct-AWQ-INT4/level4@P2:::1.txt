This paper introduces a new architecture called highway networks, designed to overcome the difficulty of training very deep neural networks. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and propose an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism to regulate information flow across layers. The paper provides a clear explanation of the highway network architecture and its components, including the transform gate and carry gate.

The authors relate their work to previous research on deep neural networks, highlighting the importance of network depth in achieving state-of-the-art results in various tasks. They also discuss the challenges of training deep networks, including the vanishing gradient problem and the difficulty of propagating information through multiple layers.

The paper presents several experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep neural networks. The authors show that highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent, whereas plain networks become increasingly difficult to optimize as depth increases. They also report competitive results on several benchmark datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

The strengths of the paper include its clear explanation of the highway network architecture, its thorough analysis of the learned networks, and its convincing experimental results. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of the potential benefits and limitations of their approach.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. One concern is the use of a standard sigmoid activation function for the gate unit T, which may not be ideal for this application. The authors should provide more details on how backpropagation is performed in these networks, particularly with respect to the gradients in Equation 5.

Another issue is the effect of initialization on the performance of highway networks. The authors propose using constant negative biases depending on the number of levels, but this approach may not be optimal. A more thorough analysis of the initialization scheme, including a plot of performance with respect to the initial value of the bias, would be helpful.

Additionally, the authors should clarify the differences in experimental setups when comparing their results to those of previous studies. Providing mean accuracy and standard deviation for every method, rather than just the maximum accuracy, would also be more informative and statistically sound.

Finally, it would be interesting to see a more in-depth analysis of the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The authors could explore how the use of additional gate units in neural networks affects the learning dynamics and the representational power of the network.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel architecture for training very deep neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of the highway network architecture and its components.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of highway networks in training very deep neural networks.
* The paper provides a detailed discussion of the potential benefits and limitations of the proposed approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The use of a standard sigmoid activation function for the gate unit T may not be ideal for this application.
* The effect of initialization on the performance of highway networks is not thoroughly analyzed.
* The authors could provide more details on how backpropagation is performed in these networks.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective.
