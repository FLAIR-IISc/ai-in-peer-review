This paper introduces an approach for training exceptionally deep neural networks by adapting the gating mechanisms found in LSTMs. This is a well-executed and clearly articulated paper that presents a novel idea with compelling results.

It is widely recognized in the machine learning community that network performance can be enhanced by increasing depth. However, progress has been hampered by significant challenges related to parameter initialization and vanishing or exploding gradients. Indeed, these difficulties with information propagation through many layers have arguably been a primary obstacle to the widespread adoption of deep architectures for several decades. This work directly confronts this long-standing issue, presenting a viable method for successfully training networks with as many as 100 layers.

The experimental analyses, particularly those illustrating information routing and the dynamic importance of individual layers, are a notable highlight of the work.

Specific comments:
*   In Section 4.1, the phrase "most transform blocks are active on average" appears to be a typo and should likely read "not active on average."
*   The authors might consider adding a brief discussion on how the proposed transform gates compare to a simpler baseline, such as initializing layers with a near-identity matrix (e.g., the identity matrix plus some noise). Clarifying the distinction between this scheme and highway networks would further strengthen the paper's contribution.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution by demonstrating an effective method for training very deep neural networks. The manuscript was exceptionally clear and a pleasure to review.
