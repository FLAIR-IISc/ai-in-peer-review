This paper presents a novel idea that is well-described and supported by reasonably convincing experiments. A particular strength of the work is the analysis of the learned networks, specifically regarding the pattern of gate outputs. However, there are several points that should be addressed:

1.  The gating unit, T, is modeled with a standard sigmoid activation, meaning its output never reaches exactly 0 or 1, a point the authors acknowledge. Consequently, the gradient formulation in Equation 5 appears to be an oversimplification. The authors should provide a more precise explanation of how backpropagation is implemented in their networks.

2.  While analyzing the effect of initialization is admittedly challenging, the proposed heuristic of using a constant negative bias dependent on the number of levels is not fully satisfying. To better understand its impact, I would suggest including a plot showing performance as a function of the initial bias value. As the paper insightfully notes, the initial bias acts as a regularizer, where a strong negative bias encourages the formation of "longer highways"—an excellent analogy. A sensitivity analysis would provide valuable insight into this regularization effect.

3.  The paper compares its results to those "reported by recent studies in similar experimental settings." This phrasing is ambiguous and should be clarified. For a fair comparison, experimental settings must be identical. If they are not, a direct comparison is inappropriate, and the authors must explicitly state any differences between their experimental setup and those of the cited works.

4.  Reporting "max" accuracies in the tables is unconventional and statistically unsound. For all methods (where available), the authors should report the mean accuracy and standard deviation across runs to provide a more robust and meaningful evaluation.

5.  From a broader perspective, the proposed gating mechanism is an interesting contribution. These input-dependent gates, which allow layers to approximate an identity transformation, enable the propagation of information through a large number of highly selective layers. It would be interesting to see this approach analyzed from a learning theory perspective in future work.
