This paper proposes a novel architecture for very deep networks, termed “highway networks,” which enables unimpeded information flow across many layers via adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. This allows for the training of significantly deeper networks than typically achievable with standard feedforward architectures, addressing a key open problem in deep learning. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained directly through stochastic gradient descent, even with hundreds of layers, and generalize well to unseen data.

The paper effectively situates its contribution within the existing literature. It acknowledges the recent empirical successes driven by increasing network depth (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) and the challenges associated with training such deep networks, referencing work on optimizers [13, 14, 15], initialization strategies [16, 17, 18, 19], activation functions [20, 21], and skip connections [22, 23, 24, 25, 26]. The inspiration drawn from LSTMs [29, 30] is clearly articulated, and the distinction between highway networks and prior approaches like FitNets [25] is well-defined.  The related work section is comprehensive and demonstrates a clear understanding of the field. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. Technically, the proposed highway network architecture is sound and logically motivated. The empirical results, particularly in Section 3.1, convincingly demonstrate the ability to train extremely deep networks where standard architectures fail. The analysis of the trained networks (Section 4), including the visualization of gate biases, activities, and information routing, is particularly insightful and provides valuable understanding of how these networks function. This analysis directly addresses potential concerns about unused layers and provides a compelling justification for the architecture. The clarity of the paper is also commendable; it is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear notation and explanations. The authors have considered and addressed potential limitations, and the code availability is a significant positive aspect.

However, there are a few minor weaknesses. There are a few instances of repetitive phrasing ("Tables 3 and 3" on p5). Additionally, while the comparisons to FitNets and state-of-the-art methods are valuable, a more in-depth discussion of the computational cost and parameter efficiency of highway networks relative to other approaches would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating the efficacy of highway networks.
*   Insightful analysis of the learned gating mechanisms and information flow.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Publicly available code.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor repetitive phrasing and minor opportunities for more detailed cost/efficiency analysis.
*   The method builds upon LSTM ideas, and a reader familiar with those may not find the core idea *radically* new, although the adaptation to feedforward networks is significant.

**Overall:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed highway network architecture is a promising approach for overcoming the challenges associated with training very deep networks, and the thorough analysis provides valuable insights into its behavior. The strengths of the paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance.
