This paper presents an innovative approach to training very deep feedforward networks, inspired by the gradient-handling mechanisms of LSTMs. The core idea – enabling gradient flow through deep networks via “highway connections” – is both simple and elegant, and the accompanying analysis is sound. I commend the authors for successfully implementing this concept, as it’s a technique I’ve also been considering.

A potentially useful perspective on this work is to view very deep networks not as a large number of simple layers, but as a smaller number of layers each containing complex, sub-layered structures. This is analogous to the inception modules of GoogLeNet or the stacked convolutional layers in VGG, where each apparent "layer" exhibits a richer, more non-linear behavior than a traditional rectified linear unit (ReLU) or sigmoid layer. Framing highway networks within this context could offer a different lens for understanding their effectiveness.

I believe a particularly insightful experiment would involve training a single, highly parameterized “thin and tall” network on both MNIST and CIFAR-10, forking the network at the input layer to accommodate the different data domains. This would allow investigation into parameter sharing within the deep hierarchy. Specifically, I am interested in observing whether, at inference time, the network exhibits similar patterns to those observed in networks trained independently for each dataset. Crucially, does the model leverage parameters underutilized during MNIST training when processing CIFAR-10 data? This experiment could shed light on the efficiency of parameter sharing in extremely deep networks and reveal the extent to which capacity is truly utilized across diverse tasks.

While the current results are compelling, I encourage the authors to evaluate the network’s performance on larger, more challenging datasets such as ImageNet. This would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s scalability and generalizability.

Overall, this is a strong contribution. The highway network concept is a significant step towards training substantially deeper networks, and the paper presents a clear and well-supported argument for its efficacy. I have no major complaints and believe this work is worthy of acceptance.
