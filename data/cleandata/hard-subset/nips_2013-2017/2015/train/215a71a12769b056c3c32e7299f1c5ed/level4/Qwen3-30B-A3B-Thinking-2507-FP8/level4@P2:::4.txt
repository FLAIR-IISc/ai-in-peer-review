

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) using standard SGD. The core innovation adapts LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms to create "information highways" that allow unimpeded signal flow across layers, contrasting with traditional "plain" networks where depth degrades trainability. The authors demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases—outperforming plain networks by orders of magnitude at 100 layers on MNIST—while achieving competitive results on CIFAR-10/100 with fewer parameters than prior methods like Fitnets [25]. Their approach generalizes prior work on skip connections [22, 23, 24] and knowledge distillation [25, 26] by introducing *adaptive* gating instead of fixed shortcuts, enabling dynamic information routing.

**Quality**  
The technical contribution is robust and well-supported. The paper provides rigorous experimental validation across multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100), demonstrating consistent performance gains with increasing depth where plain networks fail. Key strengths include:  
- Clear theoretical justification for the highway layer (Equation 3), showing how it interpolates between identity (carry) and transformation (T=0/1), and empirical evidence that negative bias initialization for transform gates (b_T = -1, -2, -3) enables stable training of 1000-layer networks.  
- Comprehensive ablation studies (Sections 3.1–3.3) showing highway networks match or exceed Fitnets [25] in single-stage training while using fewer parameters (e.g., Highway B achieves 88.1% CIFAR-10 accuracy vs. Fitnet B’s 87.7% with 1.25M vs. 1.3M parameters).  
- Insightful analysis of information flow (Section 4), including layer lesioning (Figure 4) revealing that highway networks dynamically utilize depth (e.g., 40+ layers matter for CIFAR-100 but not MNIST), a capability absent in plain networks.  

A minor weakness is the limited exploration of gate dynamics: while Figure 2 shows bias evolution, deeper analysis of *why* biases decrease (e.g., correlation with input complexity) would strengthen the interpretation. The paper also focuses on fully-connected and convolutional networks but does not test on recurrent architectures, though this is reasonable given the scope.

**Clarity**  
The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized. Key contributions are concisely summarized in Section 1, and the highway layer formalism (Equation 3) is elegantly motivated. Figures 1–4 effectively visualize core findings (e.g., training curves, gate activity, information highways), and the experimental setup (Section 3) is transparent with hyperparameters and baselines well-defined. Minor suggestions: The "block" terminology (Section 2) could be clarified earlier, and Table 3’s CIFAR-100 results would benefit from a brief comparison to the 2014 NIPS benchmark [34].

**Originality**  
The work is sufficiently original within the NIPS context. While inspired by LSTM gates [30] and skip connections [22, 23], the *adaptive gating mechanism* for feedforward networks represents a distinct conceptual leap. Crucially, the authors explicitly contrast their approach with prior work:  
- Unlike fixed skip connections [22, 23], highway networks learn dynamic routing per input (Section 4.1).  
- Unlike Fitnets [25] (which requires two-stage training with a "teacher" network), highway networks train end-to-end with SGD (Section 3.3.1).  
- Unlike normalized initialization [16, 17], which fails beyond 20 layers, highway networks avoid initialization sensitivity (Section 3.1).  
This positions the paper as a meaningful advancement over recent NIPS contributions [25, 32] and avoids being a mere incremental extension.

**Significance**  
The significance is strong. As networks grow deeper (e.g., ImageNet top-5 accuracy jumping from 84% to 95% [2, 3]), the ability to train very deep architectures *without* specialized techniques (e.g., layer-wise pretraining [26, 27] or distillation [25]) is crucial for practical applications. Highway networks enable:  
1. **Simplified training**: Single-stage SGD replaces complex two-stage pipelines.  
2. **Resource efficiency**: Competitive accuracy with fewer parameters than state-of-the-art (Table 1).  
3. **New insights**: The "information highway" abstraction (Figure 2) and layer-lesioning analysis (Figure 4) provide novel tools to study depth requirements—a problem previously intractable for plain networks.  
This advances the field beyond the *capacity* of deeper networks to the *trainability* of architectures that harness that capacity.

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a compelling contribution to the core NIPS themes of scalable deep learning. It addresses a fundamental barrier (training depth) with a technically sound, experimentally validated solution that is both conceptually novel and broadly applicable. The weaknesses noted are minor and do not undermine the core findings. This work is a significant step toward the goal of training arbitrarily deep networks, with clear implications for future architectures like ResNets [36] (which shares conceptual similarities but emerged later). I recommend **acceptance**.
