The paper presents a compelling approach to training very deep networks, termed “highway networks,” inspired by the gating mechanisms of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. The core idea—allowing unimpeded information flow across many layers via adaptive gating units—is well-motivated by the challenges of vanishing gradients in deep feedforward networks. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained effectively using standard stochastic gradient descent, even with hundreds of layers, and generalize well to unseen data. A particularly strong aspect of the work is the insightful analysis of the learned networks, examining the patterns of gate outputs to understand information routing.

The paper is well-structured, clearly explaining the motivation, proposed architecture, and experimental results. It appropriately situates the work within the context of previous research on deep learning, including techniques for optimization, initialization, and improving information flow (e.g., skip connections, FitNets). The experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 are convincing and demonstrate the benefits of highway networks compared to plain networks and existing methods. 

However, there are several points that could be clarified or strengthened:

(1) The transform gate, T, utilizes a sigmoid activation, meaning its output is never precisely 0 or 1, as the authors acknowledge. This impacts the validity of the gradients presented in Equation 5. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of the backpropagation process within these networks, specifying how the gradient computation handles the non-exact 0/1 values of T.

(2) The initialization strategy, particularly the use of negative biases dependent on network depth, deserves further investigation. While the authors suggest a range of values, a plot illustrating the performance of the networks as a function of the initial bias would be valuable. This would demonstrate the sensitivity to this hyperparameter and support the assertion that the bias acts as a regularizer, favoring networks with "longer highways"—a helpful analogy.

(3) The phrase “report by recent studies in similar experimental settings” is vague and requires clarification.  The authors should either explicitly state that comparable experimental setups exist, allowing for direct comparison, or clearly delineate the differences between their experimental setup and those of other studies. This avoids ambiguity and strengthens the validity of any comparisons drawn.

(4) Presenting only “max” accuracies in the tables is not standard practice and doesn't provide a complete statistical picture. The authors should consistently report the mean accuracy and standard deviation for all methods presented, enabling a more robust evaluation of their results.

(5) It would be interesting to explore the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed approach. The paper introduces additional gate units that effectively allow layers to act as identity mappings. The gate response is input-dependent, enabling selective propagation of information through many layers. Investigating this from a learning theory perspective—perhaps analyzing the representational capacity or generalization bounds of highway networks—could further strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning—training very deep networks—with a novel and effective approach.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the benefits of highway networks across multiple datasets.
*   The analysis of the learned gate patterns provides valuable insights into the internal workings of the networks.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The explanation of backpropagation with non-exact 0/1 gate values is incomplete.
*   The rationale for the bias initialization strategy could be strengthened with further analysis.
*   The comparison to related work lacks sufficient clarity in some instances.
*   The presentation of results lacks statistical rigor (reliance on max accuracy).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed highway networks offer a promising solution to the challenges of training very deep networks, and the analysis provides valuable insights into their behavior. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper and make it an even more valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the points raised above.
