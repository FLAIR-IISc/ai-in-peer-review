Summary
This paper introduces highway networks, a feedforward architecture that uses LSTM-inspired, input-dependent transform gates T and implicit carry gates C=1−T to enable information to flow across many layers. By allowing layers to either transform or pass through their inputs, the model facilitates gradient propagation and makes very deep networks (tens to hundreds of layers) trainable with standard SGD. The authors provide optimization studies on MNIST showing that highway networks avoid the degradation seen in plain deep nets, report competitive CIFAR-10/100 results under modest augmentation, and analyze learned gating patterns and layer importance via lesioning. The work relates to prior efforts on initialization (Glorot/He), locally competitive/maxout networks, skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and knowledge distillation (FitNets). The key distinction is the use of adaptive, data-dependent gates at each layer to create learnable “information highways,” rather than relying on fixed skip links or auxiliary losses.

Strengths
- Clear and simple architectural idea with a strong motivation from LSTM gating.
- Compelling optimization evidence: very deep models train reliably and converge faster than plain counterparts of similar capacity.
- Competitive performance on CIFAR-10/100 with relatively thin networks and restrained augmentation.
- Insightful analyses: visualizations of gate activities, evidence of input-dependent routing, and lesion studies that quantify layer contributions.
- Practical guidance on initialization and availability of code/hyperparameters support reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Gradients and backpropagation through gates: Equation (5) describes only limiting cases (T=0 or T=1), yet T∈(0,1) for sigmoid gates. As written, the Jacobian there is not the one used in practice. Please present the exact Jacobian for y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)) and detail how backpropagation is implemented, including gradients through both H and T and any numerical considerations. Clarifying parameter sharing or structure in T (e.g., per-unit vs. per-channel) would also help reproducibility.

2) Initialization sensitivity: The recommended negative biases for T as a function of depth are plausible but ad hoc. Since bT effectively regularizes the length of “open highways,” please report sensitivity to bT (e.g., performance vs. initial bT, convergence speed, and resulting gate activity) for several depths. This would substantiate the claim that training is robust and guide practitioners in choosing bT.

3) Comparability of experimental settings: Phrases like “reports by recent studies in similar experimental settings” are too vague. Please specify the exact differences in data preprocessing, augmentation, model size (parameters and FLOPs), training schedules, and stopping criteria when comparing to FitNets, maxout, and other baselines. Where settings differ, avoid direct performance claims or provide controlled ablations to isolate architectural effects.

4) Statistical reporting: Reporting “best” accuracies is uncommon and potentially misleading. Please report mean ± standard deviation over multiple runs for all methods where feasible (your own models already include such statistics in places). For baselines lacking variance estimates, note that limitation explicitly.

5) Theoretical perspective: The architecture explicitly enables identity mappings and input-dependent routing. A brief theoretical discussion—e.g., how the gates mitigate vanishing gradients, how the effective capacity/regularization changes with bT, or connections to analyses of deep linear networks—would strengthen the contribution and may explain when and why depth is exploited (as suggested by the lesioning results across MNIST vs. CIFAR-100).

Quality
The empirical validation is solid for optimization behavior and provides convincing case studies on MNIST/CIFAR. Clarifying the exact gradients and expanding sensitivity analyses would improve technical rigor. The analysis of gating and layer usage is a notable plus.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. It would benefit from a precise derivation of backprop through the gating mechanism, a more detailed description of T’s parameterization, and a clearer account of how dimensionality changes are handled. Replace ambiguous comparative language with explicit protocol details.

Originality
While related to LSTM gating, skip connections, and FitNets/distillation, using adaptive gates in deep feedforward nets to create learnable identity paths is novel and well motivated. The combination of simplicity and effectiveness is a strength.

Significance
Enabling direct training of very deep networks with standard optimizers is important. The results suggest practical benefits (deeper, thinner models; faster convergence) and open avenues for analyzing depth usage. With clearer methodology and stronger statistical reporting, the work should be impactful.

Pros
- Simple, elegant mechanism that materially eases optimization of very deep nets.
- Competitive accuracy under modest budgets; broad applicability (FC and conv layers).
- Insightful empirical analyses (gates, routing, lesioning) and released code.

Cons
- Incomplete gradient derivation; Equation (5) is only intuitive, not operative.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for critical initialization hyperparameters.
- Some comparisons lack strictly matched settings; statistical reporting could be stronger.
- Limited theoretical grounding of why/when depth is utilized.

Overall, this is a strong, well-motivated paper with clear contributions. Addressing the gradient derivations, initialization sensitivity, and comparative/statistical reporting would substantially improve clarity and rigor.
