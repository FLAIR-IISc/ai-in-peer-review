I appreciate the paper's well-articulated idea and convincing experiments, with the notable strength being the authors' effort to analyze the learned networks in relation to the pattern of gate outputs.

However, there are several areas that require attention:

(1) The standard sigmoid activation used for the gate unit T results in response values that never exactly reach 0 or 1, as acknowledged by the authors. Consequently, the gradients in eq. 5 are inaccurate. The authors should provide a clear explanation of how backpropagation is performed in these networks.

(2) While analyzing the effect of initialization can be challenging, the proposed use of constant negative biases based on the number of levels is not entirely satisfactory. A plot illustrating the performance in relation to the initial bias value would be beneficial. The initial bias effectively acts as a regularizer, with high negative biases favoring networks with "longer highways" – a comparison that is noteworthy.

(3) The statement "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is unclear. The authors should either compare their methods using the same experimental setting or explicitly state the differences in experimental setups, as direct comparisons are otherwise not feasible.

(4) Presenting "max" accuracies in tables is unconventional and statistically unreasonable. Instead, the authors should consistently report mean accuracy and standard deviation for each method, along with any available additional information.

(5) It would be intriguing to examine the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The paper demonstrates how additional gate units can be incorporated into neural networks, allowing layers to function as an identity. The gate unit response, dependent on the input, enables the propagation of inputs through multiple selective layers.
