This paper presents a simple and elegant idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in LSTMs, to enable the effective training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The core contribution is a novel architecture, the "highway network," which uses learned gating units to regulate information flow across layers. This approach directly addresses the well-known difficulty of optimizing very deep networks due to vanishing or exploding gradients. The concept is intuitive, the connection to LSTMs is insightful, and the accompanying analysis is solid. The empirical results on standard benchmarks like MNIST and CIFAR demonstrate the promise of this method.

The core idea of adapting the "gradient trapping" principle from recurrent networks to feedforward architectures is a significant insight. Many in the community have been searching for effective ways to build deeper models, and the authors should be congratulated for being the first to formalize and successfully implement this particular approach.

One point for discussion is the conceptual framing of this work in the context of other deep architectures. An alternative perspective is that many recent advances in deep learning do not simply add more identical layers, but instead create shallower networks composed of more complex "meta-layers" or blocks. For instance, the Inception module in GoogLeNet or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG can be seen as single, powerful non-linear transformations rather than a series of simple ones. I wonder if the authors believe highway networks fit into this perspective. Can a stack of highway layers be viewed as a single, more expressive layer block that learns its own complex, depth-dependent function? A brief discussion on how this work relates to or differs from the "networks of networks" philosophy could further contextualize its contribution.

To further probe the properties of this architecture, I would suggest a potential experiment for future work. A multi-task learning setup using a single, deep "thin-and-tall" highway network could be very informative. For example, the network could be trained jointly on both MNIST and CIFAR, with task-specific classification heads forking from the final layer. The key question would be to analyze the learned gating behavior at inference time for each task. Does the network exhibit different effective depths or information flow patterns for the simpler (MNIST) versus the more complex (CIFAR) domain? This could provide fascinating insights into whether underutilized parameters for one task are effectively repurposed for another, and more generally, how resource and parameter sharing occurs within such a deep, dynamic hierarchy. Such an experiment could beautifully demonstrate the data-dependent routing capabilities of the architecture.

The paper is strong, with no major flaws. The primary suggestion for improvement is to demonstrate the scalability of the proposed method. While the results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 are convincing initial evidence, the true test for an architecture designed for depth is its performance on large-scale, challenging datasets like ImageNet. Applying highway networks to a state-of-the-art ImageNet model would substantially strengthen the paper's impact and provide a clear measure of its practical utility.

In summary, this is an excellent paper introducing a novel, well-motivated, and effective architecture for training very deep networks. The idea is elegant, and the initial experiments are compelling. I strongly support its acceptance. The suggestions above are intended to encourage the authors to explore the full potential of their idea and further solidify its position in the field.
