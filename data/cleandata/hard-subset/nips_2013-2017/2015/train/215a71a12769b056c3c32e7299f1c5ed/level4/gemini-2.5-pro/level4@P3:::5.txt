### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep feedforward models. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. The authors propose augmenting standard feedforward layers with "transform" and "carry" gates that adaptively control how much of the input is transformed by the layer's non-linearity versus how much is passed through unchanged. This mechanism creates unimpeded "information highways," allowing gradients to flow smoothly across hundreds of layers.

The authors demonstrate empirically that while standard "plain" deep networks degrade in performance as depth increases beyond a certain point, highway networks can be trained directly with SGD even at depths of 50, 100, or more layers, without suffering from this degradation. The paper includes a thorough analysis of the network's behavior, showing that the gates learn to dynamically route information in a data-dependent manner and that the network can learn to utilize its depth effectively for complex tasks while leaving layers idle for simpler ones.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the critical and widely recognized problem of training very deep neural networks. The proposed solution—adapting the LSTM gating mechanism for feedforward architectures—is both novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to overcome the optimization challenges that have historically limited the depth of feedforward models, opening the door to exploring the benefits of extreme depth. This is a significant contribution with the potential to have a high impact on the field.

2.  **Technical Quality and Thorough Analysis:** The claims are well-supported by a series of carefully designed experiments. The direct comparison of plain vs. highway networks at varying depths (Sec 3.1) provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the architecture. Beyond just reporting performance metrics, the analysis in Section 4 is particularly insightful. The visualization of gate activities and the "lesion study" (Fig. 4) provide a deeper understanding of *how* the network functions, demonstrating data-dependent routing and adaptive use of depth. This level of analysis is a major strength.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the proposed architecture is explained precisely, and the experiments are described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. The connection to LSTMs provides a strong conceptual anchor for the reader.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Contextualization with other Deep Architectures:** While the paper is well-situated in the context of optimization difficulties, it could be strengthened by a brief discussion of its conceptual relationship to other contemporary architectures that increase effective depth and representational power. For example, how does a highway block compare to an "Inception" module in GoogLeNet or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG nets? One could view a highway block not just as a standard layer in a deeper network, but as a single, more complex layer with adaptive, input-dependent behavior. Discussing this perspective could further contextualize the contribution.

2.  **Scalability to Larger Datasets:** The experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 are convincing for demonstrating the core principles and optimization benefits. However, a key question regarding the practical significance of this work is its performance on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. While not strictly necessary for acceptance, demonstrating effectiveness on such a benchmark would substantially bolster the paper's impact and show that the benefits generalize beyond smaller-scale problems. I encourage the authors to pursue this in future work.

**Questions and Detailed Comments for the Authors**

*   The analysis of data-dependent information routing is fascinating. A compelling follow-up experiment could involve multi-task learning, for instance, training a single deep highway network on both MNIST and CIFAR (with separate output heads). It would be highly insightful to investigate whether the network learns to use a shallow portion for the simpler task (MNIST) while engaging deeper layers for the more complex one (CIFAR). This would provide strong evidence that the network can dynamically allocate its effective depth based on input complexity, and would offer insights into how feature sharing occurs across a very deep hierarchy.

*   In the analysis of layer importance (Fig. 4), it's noted that many layers in the MNIST network appear unused. Is it possible that these layers could be pruned post-training without affecting performance, leading to a more efficient inference-time model?

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a simple, powerful, and important idea. The proposed architecture is a significant contribution to the field, directly addressing a fundamental obstacle in deep learning. The strengths—a novel and elegant solution to a major problem, supported by strong empirical evidence and insightful analysis—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily suggestions for future work and broader contextualization. This work is very likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
