

The paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via stochastic gradient descent. Building on NIPS 2013-2015 work (e.g., LSTM-inspired gating [30], FitNets [25], and deep supervision [24]), the authors propose adaptive transform/carry gates that dynamically regulate information flow, mimicking "information highways." This addresses a critical bottleneck in deep learning: the degradation of optimization performance with depth, which prior methods (e.g., layer-wise pretraining [26, 27] or distillation [25]) failed to resolve without multi-stage training. The core innovation lies in the gating mechanism (Equation 3), where negative bias initialization biases gates toward "carry" behavior, enabling stable gradient flow. Experiments demonstrate highway networks outperform plain networks by orders of magnitude in depth (e.g., 100-layer highway vs. impractical plain networks on MNIST), while matching or exceeding state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 with fewer parameters than maxout networks [20] or FitNets [25].

**Strengths:**  
1. **Quality & Significance:** The experimental design rigorously validates the core claim: highway networks enable *direct* training of ultra-deep networks (100+ layers) without multi-stage procedures. Results on MNIST (Figure 1) and CIFAR (Table 2) are compelling, showing consistent generalization and convergence speed. The analysis of gate dynamics (Figure 2-4) is particularly strong—revealing how networks learn *input-dependent routing* (Section 4.1) and *layer importance* (Section 4.2), providing unprecedented insights into depth utilization. This advances the field by enabling systematic study of depth effects, previously infeasible with plain networks.  
2. **Originality:** While inspired by LSTMs [30] and skip connections [22, 23], highway networks introduce a *feedforward* adaptation with *adaptive gating* (not fixed skip connections), distinguishing them from prior work. The paper properly contextualizes this against NIPS 2013-2015 contributions (e.g., FitNets [25] requires teacher networks; deep supervision [24] hurts thin networks [25]). The negative bias initialization strategy is novel and well-motivated by LSTM practices [30].  
3. **Clarity:** The architecture (Section 2) and experiments (Section 3) are clearly described. The notation is consistent, and Figures 1-4 effectively visualize key findings (e.g., gate activity patterns in Figure 2). The public code release enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Technical Precision (Quality):** Equation 5 claims gradients are *exactly* I or H' when T=0/1, but the sigmoid gate (T = σ(W_Tx + b_T)) never reaches 0/1 (as noted in the paper). This misrepresents the gradient dynamics; backpropagation uses approximate gradients near these limits. The authors should clarify this in Section 2.2 (e.g., "gradients approach I or H' as T → 0/1") to avoid technical inaccuracy.  
2. **Initialization Analysis (Quality):** The recommendation to set initial bias to -1/-2/-3 for depths ~10/20/30 lacks empirical support. The paper states this is "suggested as a general guideline" but provides no ablation (e.g., performance vs. bias value in Figure 1). A plot showing accuracy as a function of initial bias (e.g., -10 to 0) would strengthen this claim and address the reviewer’s concern.  
3. **Statistical Reporting (Clarity):** Tables 1-3 report only "best" accuracies, which is statistically inappropriate. The paper claims "Best (mean ± std.dev.)" in Section 3, but Tables 2-3 omit standard deviations. For consistency and reproducibility, all results should include mean ± std.dev. (as done in Section 3.1).  
4. **Comparison Context (Originality):** The FitNet comparison (Section 3.3.1) states "highway networks can be trained in a single stage," but the paper does not explicitly contrast with [32] (a similar LSTM-inspired model). Clarifying why highway networks differ from [32] (e.g., feedforward vs. recurrent adaptation) would better establish novelty.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong contribution with clear significance: it resolves a fundamental barrier to training deeper networks, enabling new research directions (e.g., studying depth limits). The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and validated through rigorous experiments. The minor issues (gradient equation, bias analysis, statistical reporting) are fixable in revision and do not undermine the core advance. The paper’s clarity and depth of analysis (Figures 2-4) make it a valuable addition to NIPS 2015.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The work is technically rigorous, original, and impactful—directly advancing the state of the art in deep network training. The weaknesses are minor and editorial in nature.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Enables direct training of >100-layer networks; clear analysis of gate dynamics; outperforms prior single-stage methods (FitNets); significant experimental validation.  
*Con:* Gradient equation inaccuracy (minor); bias initialization lacks ablation; statistical reporting inconsistent; need to distinguish from [32].  

The paper exemplifies high-quality NIPS work: it identifies a critical problem (depth-induced optimization failure), proposes a novel solution (adaptive gating), and provides robust evidence through experiments and analysis. The authors’ focus on *why* highway networks work (Section 4) elevates the contribution beyond incremental improvements. With minor revisions addressing the points above, this paper will be a valuable resource for the community.
