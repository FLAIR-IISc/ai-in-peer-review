This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to introduce adaptive gates that regulate information flow across layers. Each layer can dynamically balance between transforming its input and carrying it through unchanged. This mechanism allows for unimpeded gradient flow through potentially hundreds of layers, overcoming the optimization difficulties that plague traditional "plain" deep networks. The authors demonstrate empirically that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained effectively using standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD), whereas plain networks of similar depth fail to train. Furthermore, they provide an insightful analysis of the learned gate behavior, showing how the network learns data-dependent information routing and how layer importance can be assessed.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly significant and challenging problem in deep learning: the effective training of very deep networks. The proposed solution is elegant and novel, successfully adapting a key concept (gating) from recurrent networks to the feedforward domain. This work enables the exploration of network depths that were previously impractical to train directly, representing a substantial contribution to the field.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The claims are well-supported by a solid set of experiments. The optimization experiments in Section 3.1 provide a clear and convincing comparison against plain networks, demonstrating the fundamental advantage of the highway architecture as depth increases. The comparisons on CIFAR-10/100 against FitNets and other state-of-the-art methods show that this ease of training translates into strong generalization performance.

3.  **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper is well-written and the core concept of information highways is explained intuitively. A particular strength is the analysis in Section 4, which goes beyond simply reporting performance metrics. The visualizations of gate activities and the layer lesioning study provide valuable insights into what these very deep networks are learning. The demonstration of data-dependent routing (Figure 3) and the ability to effectively "turn off" unneeded layers (Figure 4, MNIST) are compelling and enhance the reader's understanding of the model's properties.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Gradient Calculation:** The discussion surrounding Equation 5, which describes the Jacobian of a layer, is slightly misleading. It presents the Jacobian as being either the identity matrix **I** or **H'** depending on whether the transform gate **T** is 0 or 1. However, since **T** is implemented with a sigmoid function, its output is always in the open interval (0, 1) and never reaches these exact values. While this is likely intended as a conceptual illustration, it could be confusing. The authors should clarify that this is an idealized simplification and perhaps explicitly state the actual form of the gradient used for backpropagation to ensure full reproducibility and technical precision.

2.  **Analysis of Gate Bias Initialization:** The strategy of initializing the transform gate biases to small negative values (e.g., -1 to -3) is crucial for training to succeed, as it biases the network towards carry behavior initially. While the authors propose this as a practical heuristic, this introduces a sensitive hyperparameter that depends on network depth. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more systematic analysis of this initialization. A study showing performance as a function of the initial bias value would provide a clearer understanding of its role and how robust the training is to this choice. This bias acts as a form of regularizer encouraging simpler identity mappings early in training, and a deeper exploration of this effect would be valuable.

3.  **Statistical Robustness of Results:** In Section 3, the authors state they will report results in the format "Best (mean ± std.dev.)". However, the tables primarily present the accuracy of the best-performing run. To provide a more robust and statistically sound evaluation, the mean and standard deviation across all runs should be reported consistently for the proposed highway network models. Reporting only the best result can obscure performance variability and makes it harder to assess the stability and reliability of the training procedure.

4.  **Clarity in Experimental Comparisons:** When comparing to prior work like FitNets, the authors state that the number of parameters and operations are "comparable." For a fair and transparent comparison, it would be beneficial to be more explicit about any differences in the experimental setups, such as data augmentation protocols, optimization details, or hyperparameter search ranges.

**Summary of Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. The proposed highway network architecture provides a simple and effective solution to the important problem of training very deep networks. The experiments are convincing and the analysis of the learned network properties is insightful. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and the need for more thorough analysis in specific areas, which could be addressed in a final version. The paper's strengths, particularly its originality and potential impact on future research into deep architectures, strongly support its acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, simple, and effective architecture to address the critical problem of training very deep networks.
*   Provides strong empirical evidence that highway networks can be trained at depths where plain networks fail.
*   Includes insightful analysis that helps explain *how* the architecture works, demonstrating properties like data-dependent routing.
*   Likely to be highly influential and inspire further research into network architectures.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The crucial gate bias initialization is treated as a heuristic without a thorough sensitivity analysis.
*   Reporting of experimental results could be more statistically rigorous (i.e., consistently providing mean and standard deviation).
*   Minor lack of clarity in the description of the layer Jacobian.
