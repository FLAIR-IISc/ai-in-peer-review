This paper presents a simple and elegant idea, inspired by LSTMs, that enables the training of very deep feed-forward networks. The proposed method is well-motivated and the analysis is solid. The core idea of using a gating mechanism, similar to the one in LSTMs, to manage gradient flow in very deep networks is an insightful one. I congratulate the authors on being the first to successfully implement and analyze this approach.

An alternative perspective on this work might be to view very deep networks not as a long chain of simple layers, but as a shallower stack of more complex composite layers. In this view, each 'layer' (e.g., an Inception module in GoogLeNet or the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG) implements a more complex non-linear transformation than a traditional single transformation layer. I wonder if the authors consider this a valid interpretation of their highway networks as well.

I would suggest one additional experiment to explore the representational properties of these deep hierarchies. The authors could train a single deep, narrow network on both MNIST and CIFAR simultaneously (perhaps by forking the network near the output layer for each task). The key question would be whether, at inference, the layer-wise information flow patterns observed for each task individually are replicated in the multi-task model. This could also reveal if parameters underutilized by the MNIST task are effectively repurposed by the CIFAR task, providing insight into how feature sharing occurs in such deep models and whether this sharing is efficient.

Overall, I have no major complaints about the paper. I would encourage the authors to further validate their approach by applying it to larger-scale datasets, such as ImageNet.
