I found this work to be a compelling and timely exploration of deep network training, and commend the authors for successfully implementing a gradient-trapping mechanism reminiscent of LSTMs – an approach I had also considered.

An alternative framing of very deep networks might be as shallow networks composed of layers with internal complexity. These internal structures, akin to the multi-branching found in architectures like GoogLeNet and VGG, introduce richer non-linear behavior within each apparent “layer.” It would be interesting to consider whether highway networks could also be viewed through this lens.

To further investigate the network’s representational capacity, I suggest an experiment involving a single, deep network trained on both MNIST and CIFAR-10, with a branching point to accommodate the differing input requirements. I am particularly interested in observing whether, at inference, this shared network exhibits similar parameter utilization patterns to those trained independently on each dataset – specifically, whether parameters underutilized during MNIST training are leveraged for CIFAR-10. This would provide valuable insight into the efficiency of parameter sharing within a deep hierarchical structure.

Overall, this is a strong paper with no major weaknesses. I encourage the authors to evaluate the network’s performance on larger, more challenging datasets such as ImageNet. 

This paper presents a novel and insightful approach to training very deep feedforward networks, inspired by the principles of LSTM. The core idea is both simple and elegant, and the accompanying analysis is thorough and well-supported.
