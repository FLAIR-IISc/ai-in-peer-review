I liked the article; the concept is well-described, and the experiments are believable to a certain extent. In my opinion, the best thing is that the authors have tried to analyze the learned networks in relation to the gate output pattern.

(1) The gate unit T is modeled with a standard sigmoid activation. Therefore, the response value is never exactly 0 or 1 (which the authors also mentioned), and the gradients of equation 5 are not correct. The authors should explain in this article how backpropagation is correctly done in these networks.

(2) Analyzing the effect of initialization is always difficult, but the authors' suggestion of using constant negative biases, depending on the number of layers, is also not satisfactory. I would like to see a plot of performance with respect to the initial value of the bias. In this case, the initial bias acts as a kind of regulator, where a high negative bias supports the networks with "long highways" (I liked this comparison).

(3) The sentence "reported by recent research in similar experimental settings" is not satisfactory. Either the methods used the same experimental setting, which would allow comparison, or they didn't, which would prohibit direct comparison. The authors need to clarify the difference in experimental setups in the article.

(4) Giving "maximum" accuracy in the table is quite unusual and not logical from a statistical point of view. Therefore, the authors should always provide the average accuracy and standard deviation for each method (and when available).

(5) It would be interesting to study the proposed method from the perspective of learning theory. The article describes how additional gate units can be used in neural networks that allow the layers to function only as an identifier. The response of the gate unit depends on the input and therefore allows it to propagate the input through a very large number of selective layers.
