This paper describes an idea inspired by LSTM that allows the training of very deep feedforward networks. The idea is simple and elegant, and the analysis is solid. I, too, considered leveraging the LSTM gradient trapping mechanism to enable training deeper networks but had not yet begun implementation; I congratulate the authors for being the first to explore this direction successfully.

There is a potentially alternative perspective worth considering: a very deep network can be viewed as a series of not-so-deep networks, where each layer is comprised of sub-layers. Each of these “layers” then exhibits more complex non-linear behavior than a traditional rectifier or sigmoid layer. This is how I interpret architectures like GoogLeNet’s inception modules and the stacked 3x3 convolutions in VGG, and I wonder if this lens might also be a valid way to understand highway networks. 

I would suggest one further experiment. The authors could train a single, thin and tall network on both MNIST and CIFAR, forking the network at the input to handle the two domains. I am particularly interested in whether, at inference time, the network exhibits the same parameter utilization patterns observed in networks trained separately for each domain – specifically, whether model parameters underutilized during MNIST training are effectively employed when processing CIFAR data. In other words, I would be keen to see how sharing occurs within a very deep hierarchical structure and whether this sharing is as efficient as possible.

I have no major complaints. I encourage the authors to evaluate the network's performance on larger datasets, such as ImageNet.
