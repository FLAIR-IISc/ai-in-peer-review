### Summary

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to enable the training of extremely deep feedforward models. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The authors introduce adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates at each layer, which dynamically regulate the flow of information. These gates allow a layer to either transform its input, as in a conventional network, or pass the input through unmodified. This structure creates "information highways" that allow unimpeded gradient and information flow across many layers, directly addressing the optimization difficulties (e.g., vanishing gradients) that have historically plagued the training of very deep networks. The authors provide strong empirical evidence showing that highway networks with hundreds of layers can be trained effectively using standard stochastic gradient descent, significantly outperforming conventional "plain" networks of comparable depth on image classification tasks (MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100). Furthermore, the paper presents an insightful analysis of the learned behavior of these networks, visualizing the data-dependent information routing and providing a novel method to assess the functional contribution of individual layers.

### Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a central and long-standing challenge in deep learning: how to effectively train networks of unprecedented depth. The proposed highway architecture is an elegant and original solution that cleverly adapts a successful concept from the domain of recurrent networks (gating) to feedforward architectures. This work provides a practical and effective method for exploring the benefits of depth on a scale that was previously intractable for many researchers.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The controlled comparison in Section 3.1, which demonstrates that the performance of plain networks degrades with increasing depth while highway networks remain robust, is a powerful and convincing validation of the architecture's effectiveness. The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are competitive with other contemporary methods and show that the ease of training does not come at the cost of generalization performance.

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Insight:** A major strength of the paper is the analysis presented in Section 4. The layer lesioning study (Figure 4) is particularly compelling, as it provides a direct method for probing the "effective depth" that the network learns to use for a given problem. The finding that the network utilizes more layers for a complex task like CIFAR-100 than for a simple one like MNIST is a fascinating result. The visualizations of the gate activations offer valuable insights into the learned data-dependent routing of information, suggesting the gating mechanism acts not just as a training aid but as an integral part of the network's computation.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The core concept is explained concisely and intuitively, making a potentially complex idea highly accessible.

### Weaknesses / Questions for Authors (Arguments against acceptance)

While this is a very strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen the contribution:

1.  **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** The paper effectively contrasts highway networks with "plain" networks. However, a simpler approach to enabling information flow in deep networks would be to initialize each layer's weight matrix to be close to the identity matrix (with some added noise to break symmetry). This would also initially bias each layer towards a carry-like behavior. A discussion of how the proposed adaptive gating mechanism differs from and improves upon such a fixed, identity-based initialization scheme would be valuable. The key advantage of the highway network appears to be its ability to *learn* the routing dynamically; clarifying this against simpler, static alternatives would underscore the originality and importance of the gating mechanism.

2.  **Clarification on Gate Activity Interpretation:** In Section 4.1, the authors state, "Especially for the CIFAR-100 case, most transform gates are active on average." Based on a visual inspection of Figure 2 (bottom row, second column), the mean activity for many gates appears to be around or below the 0.5 mark. Could the authors please clarify what is meant by "active on average"? For example, does this imply a mean value greater than a certain threshold, or is there another interpretation of the plot? A more precise explanation would help readers fully grasp the interesting data-dependent routing behavior being described.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a high-quality and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It presents a well-motivated and effective solution to a fundamental problem, backed by strong empirical results and insightful analysis. The work is original, clearly presented, and is likely to inspire a great deal of future research into deep network architectures and optimization. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to requests for clarification and comparison to another potential baseline. I strongly recommend acceptance.
