- Proposal: Highway networks add learned gating y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)) to enable information flow across many layers, inspired by LSTM.  
- Empirical claim: Thin highway nets with 50–100 layers train faster and optimize substantially better than depth-matched plain nets and match/exceed fitnets/maxout on MNIST/CIFAR without teacher-student training.  
- Analysis: Paper inspects learned gate activations, shows data-dependent routing, and uses lesion studies to assess layer importance.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to LSTM gating, skip/shortcut ideas, distillation, and literature on depth/expressivity.

Strengths
- Mechanism is conceptually simple and broadly applicable to ease training of very deep feedforward nets.  
- Strong optimization results demonstrating reliable depth scaling to 100+ layers.  
- Thoughtful analyses (gate patterns, class-conditional routing, lesioning) add insight beyond accuracy numbers.  
- Provides practical guidance (gate-bias initialization) and shows applicability to both fully connected and convolutional settings.  
- Highly relevant to the conference: advances in deep training and links to prior architectures.

Weaknesses / requests for clarification
- Missing exact gradient/backprop derivations: Eq.5 shows limiting cases but paper should provide full gradients w.r.t. x, WH, WT and state any approximations.  
- Initialization sensitivity: need experiments showing performance vs. initial gate bias across depths/datasets to assess robustness and regularization effects.  
- Comparability of settings: baseline comparisons are ambiguous; request a table listing preprocessing, architectures, parameter counts, FLOPs, and training schedules.  
- Reporting statistics: reporting only “best” accuracies is insufficient—report mean ± std across runs and clarify when external baselines lack variance.  
- Theory/capacity discussion: add a brief learning-theoretic or capacity perspective on data-dependent effective depth, gradient flow, and implicit regularization from gate bias.

Additional suggestions
- Ablation: learn C independently instead of fixing C = 1 − T to test coupling necessity.  
- Report wall-clock training time per epoch and total convergence time to quantify practical efficiency.  
- Provide results for disabling gating (T ≡ 1) to isolate gating’s role from depth.  
- Clarify convolutional block design (does H include BN, does T share receptive fields, how are dimensionality changes handled).  
- Discuss parameter overhead of T and its impact compared to pure skip connections.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Empirical evidence for improved optimization is convincing, but stronger with full gradient derivations, clearer baselines, statistical reporting, and ablations.  
- Clarity: Generally well written; needs clearer comparisons, backprop details, and gating descriptions for conv blocks; figures help but tables should show mean ± std.  
- Originality: Novel application of LSTM-like gating to feedforward nets with input-dependent routing compared to fixed shortcuts and teacher-student methods.  
- Significance: Impactful contribution enabling direct training of very deep feedforward/convolutional networks, likely to influence architectures and training practices.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, effective mechanism that materially eases optimization at depth.  
- Solid empirical evidence across datasets with insightful analyses.  
- Broad applicability and clear relevance to the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing general gradient expressions and limited ablations weaken mechanistic claims.  
- Baseline comparability and statistical reporting need improvement for reproducibility.  
- Limited theoretical framing on when and why gating yields gains over fixed shortcuts.

Overall recommendation
- Lean toward acceptance, conditional on addressing gradient derivations, initialization sensitivity, statistical reporting, and clearer baseline comparisons to strengthen reproducibility and causal claims.
