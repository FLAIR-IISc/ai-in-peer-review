

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture enabling direct training of extremely deep neural networks (up to 100+ layers) via adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTMs. The authors address a core challenge in deep learning: the degradation of training performance as network depth increases, which has historically limited practical applications. Prior work on deep networks (e.g., [25, 29, 30]) often relied on layer-wise pretraining, two-stage distillation (FitNets), or specialized initialization—none of which enabled straightforward optimization of very deep architectures. Highway networks overcome this by introducing transform and carry gates that dynamically regulate information flow, allowing gradients to propagate effectively without vanishing. Crucially, the method requires no specialized initialization beyond a negative bias for transform gates and works with standard SGD.

The paper demonstrates exceptional quality through rigorous experimentation. On MNIST, 100-layer highway networks achieve near-identical performance to 10-layer plain networks (mean loss < 10⁻⁴), while similarly sized plain networks fail catastrophically (Fig 1). This stability extends to CIFAR-10/100, where highway networks match or exceed FitNet accuracy in a single training stage—eliminating the need for teacher-student distillation [25]. The analysis of information routing (Fig 2–4) provides profound insights: transform gates are largely inactive on average (contrary to a draft comment suggesting "most are active"), yet exhibit input-dependent sparsity (Fig 3), enabling dynamic routing. Layer importance analysis (Fig 4) further validates that highway networks exploit depth effectively for complex tasks (CIFAR-100), while simpler problems (MNIST) utilize only a subset of layers—unattainable with plain networks.

The work excels in originality and significance. While skip connections [22, 23] and distillation [25] exist, highway networks uniquely enable *direct training* of ultra-deep models via learnable gating, not fixed shortcuts. This resolves a fundamental barrier to studying depth’s theoretical benefits (e.g., efficient function representation [7, 10]), advancing the field beyond incremental improvements. The method’s generality—applicable to convolutional networks, various activations, and without specialized initialization—makes it broadly impactful. The significance is underscored by the paper’s ability to train networks with 100+ layers (previously infeasible [28]) while maintaining strong generalization, as evidenced by competitive CIFAR results with fewer parameters than state-of-the-art methods [34, 35].

Clarity is excellent. The highway mechanism (Eq. 3) is concisely defined, with intuitive behavior at gate extremes (Eq. 4). Experiments are meticulously designed (Section 3.1), with hyperparameter tuning and statistical reporting (Best (mean ± std.dev.)) ensuring reproducibility. The analysis (Section 4) effectively connects architectural choices to empirical results, and the manuscript is well-organized, adhering to NIPS standards.

Minor weaknesses include: (1) The draft comment about initializing layers with identity matrices misses a key distinction—highway gates *learn* routing, unlike fixed identity initialization, which would still suffer from gradient issues in deep chains. This should be clarified. (2) While the paper shows highway networks generalize well, a brief discussion of computational overhead (e.g., gate parameters vs. depth gains) would strengthen the practical assessment.

Overall, this is a landmark contribution. It directly addresses a persistent bottleneck in deep learning (training stability) with a simple yet profound architectural innovation. The experiments are robust, the analysis insightful, and the implications for future research (e.g., studying depth’s role in representation learning) are substantial. The work significantly advances the state of the art beyond prior approaches [25, 29, 30] and aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, scientifically rigorous contributions. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and offers a practical pathway to exploit extreme depth. Accept.
