This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture called highway networks, designed to overcome the difficulties of training very deep networks. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks and propose the use of adaptive gating units to regulate the information flow across layers. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed architecture, including its theoretical foundations, experimental results, and comparisons with existing methods.

The authors' primary contribution is to show that extremely deep highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), in contrast to traditional feedforward networks which become increasingly difficult to optimize as depth increases. The experimental results demonstrate that highway networks can be trained effectively, even with hundreds of layers, and that they generalize well to unseen data.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed architecture and its advantages. The experimental results are convincing, and the comparisons with existing methods are helpful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

However, there are a few areas that require clarification or improvement. Firstly, the authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how backpropagation is performed in highway networks, particularly with respect to the gate unit T. The gradients in Equation 5 are not correct, as the response value of the sigmoid activation function is never exactly 0 or 1.

Secondly, the effect of initialization on the performance of highway networks is not thoroughly analyzed. The authors propose using constant negative biases depending on the number of levels, but it would be helpful to see a plot of the performance with respect to the initial value of the bias. This would provide a clearer understanding of how the initialization affects the training process.

Thirdly, the authors should clarify the differences in experimental setups when comparing their method with existing approaches. The sentence "report by recent studies in similar experimental settings" is unclear, and it would be helpful to provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental conditions.

Fourthly, the authors should consider providing more statistical information about the performance of their method, such as mean accuracy and standard deviation, rather than just reporting the maximum accuracy.

Finally, it would be interesting to see a more in-depth analysis of the proposed approach from a learning theory perspective. The paper provides some insights into how the gate unit response depends on the input, but a more detailed analysis of the theoretical foundations of highway networks could provide a deeper understanding of their strengths and limitations.
