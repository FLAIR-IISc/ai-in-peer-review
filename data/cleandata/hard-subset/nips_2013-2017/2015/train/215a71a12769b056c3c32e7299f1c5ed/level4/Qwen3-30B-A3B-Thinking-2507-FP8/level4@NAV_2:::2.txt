

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture enabling direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) through LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanisms. The authors clearly position their work within the context of prior NIPS contributions: they extend the understanding of depth limitations in neural networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, NIPS 1997; Glorot & Bengio, AISTATS 2010), contrast with recent approaches like FitNets (Romero et al., NIPS 2014) that require two-stage training, and differentiate their adaptive gating from fixed skip connections (e.g., ResNet concepts pre-dating publication). The core innovation—transform gates that dynamically regulate information flow via carry/transform paths—directly addresses the vanishing gradient problem in very deep networks, a persistent challenge noted in prior work (Bengio et al., ICML 1994; Sutskever et al., ICML 2013).

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The experimental design is rigorous, with 100 random hyperparameter searches per depth on MNIST, clear ablation studies (Fig 4), and comprehensive comparisons to plain networks and FitNets (Table 2). The theoretical analysis of Jacobian behavior (Eq. 5) and empirical validation of gate dynamics (Fig 2–4) robustly support claims.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with precise notation (Section 2), intuitive explanations of gate mechanics, and insightful visualizations (Fig 2–4). The analysis of class-specific gate behavior (Fig 3) and layer importance (Fig 4) exemplifies clarity in communicating complex mechanisms.  
*Originality:* While inspired by LSTMs and FitNets, the highway architecture’s *adaptive* gating (vs. static skip connections or teacher-student distillation) is distinctly novel. The paper explicitly contrasts this with prior work (e.g., Section 1, "Unlike FitNets..."), and the information highway concept provides a new conceptual framework for depth.  
*Significance:* The work enables direct training of networks far deeper than previously feasible (100+ layers vs. FitNets’ 5-layer limit), with state-of-the-art results on MNIST/CIFAR (Table 1–3) while maintaining generalization. It fundamentally advances the ability to study depth effects in neural networks—a critical open problem in the field.

**Weaknesses:**  
The analysis of computational efficiency (beyond parameter counts) is limited; a comparison of FLOPs or training time vs. ResNet-like architectures (though not yet published) would strengthen significance. The paper does not explore the impact of gate initialization beyond negative bias values, though this is acknowledged as a hyperparameter. The MNIST experiments (Section 3.2) use simpler architectures than CIFAR-100, potentially underestimating scalability.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Groundbreaking architecture enabling direct training of ultra-deep networks; thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets; exceptional clarity in explaining the mechanism; directly addresses a core NIPS challenge (depth limitations).  
*Con:* Limited exploration of computational cost and direct comparison to contemporary deep network designs (e.g., ResNets).  

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to deep learning. The analysis of gate behavior (Fig 2–4) is particularly valuable for understanding how highway networks achieve their performance. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the core contribution, which is both novel and impactful. The typo "Tables 3 and 3" (p.5) should be corrected to "Tables 3 and 4." This is a strong, high-quality contribution to NIPS.
