

This paper introduces highway networks, an architecture inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units, designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via standard stochastic gradient descent. The key innovation is adaptive gating mechanisms (transform and carry gates) that regulate information flow across layers, creating "information highways" that mitigate vanishing gradients. The authors demonstrate that highway networks maintain training stability and generalization performance as depth increases, unlike conventional "plain" networks which degrade significantly beyond 20 layers. Experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 show highway networks achieve competitive results with fewer parameters than state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Fitnets), while enabling direct single-stage training without teacher-student distillation.

**Quality**  
The paper excels in technical rigor. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for the highway layer (Equation 3), demonstrating how the gating mechanism interpolates between identity mapping (T=0) and standard transformation (T=1). Experiments are meticulously designed: they compare highway vs. plain networks on MNIST with controlled depth (10–100 layers), using identical parameter counts and normalized initialization. Results (Figure 1) conclusively show highway networks avoid the accuracy degradation of plain networks at depth (e.g., 100-layer highway achieves >2 orders of magnitude lower loss than plain networks). The analysis of transform gate biases and layer importance (Figure 4) further supports the architecture’s validity by revealing how networks dynamically utilize layers based on input complexity. The work addresses a fundamental limitation in deep learning (training instability at scale) with a solution that does not rely on problem-specific initialization or two-stage training, making it broadly applicable.  

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The notation (Section 2) is concise and self-contained, with intuitive explanations of the highway layer’s behavior (Equations 4–5). Figures 1–4 effectively visualize key insights: training dynamics (Figure 1), transform gate behavior (Figure 2), and layer importance (Figure 4). The discussion of how highway networks differ from prior approaches (e.g., skip connections in ResNets [36], Fitnets [25], or layer-wise pretraining [26]) is thorough and contextualizes the contribution within NIPS’ recent trajectory. The authors correctly note that highway networks generalize beyond the LSTM-inspired block design (Section 2.2), enhancing accessibility. Minor improvements could include clarifying the relationship between highway networks and the "thin & tall" architectures (e.g., VGG, Inception) mentioned in the draft, but this is not essential to the core contribution.  

**Originality**  
This work represents a significant, novel departure from prior approaches to deep network training. While skip connections [22, 23, 24] and knowledge distillation [25] have been used to improve training, highway networks uniquely embed adaptive gating *within* the network architecture itself, eliminating the need for external training procedures. The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism (Section 2) is a fresh application of recurrent concepts to feedforward networks, directly addressing the "vanishing gradient" challenge in very deep networks. The authors properly contextualize this against related work (Section 1), noting that prior methods either require two-stage training (Fitnets [25]) or suffer from depth limitations (e.g., maxout networks [20]). This is a meaningful advance over NIPS 2013–2014 work (e.g., [20, 25, 26]) and aligns with the conference’s focus on foundational improvements to deep learning.  

**Significance**  
The significance is high. Highway networks enable the practical exploration of extreme depth (100+ layers), a regime previously inaccessible due to training instability. This opens new avenues for studying depth-accuracy trade-offs in deep learning (e.g., Figure 4 quantifies layer utility). The paper demonstrates clear utility: highway networks match or exceed state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 with fewer parameters than Fitnets [25] and avoid the complexity of teacher-student training. Crucially, the architecture is agnostic to activation function or layer type (convolutional, fully connected), making it broadly applicable. While the current experiments are limited to MNIST/CIFAR, the authors correctly suggest ImageNet as a natural next step (Section 5), and the methodology could substantially impact large-scale vision tasks.  

**Minor Suggestions**  
1. The draft’s suggestion to test a shared MNIST/CIFAR network (forking at the top) is valuable. The authors could investigate whether highway networks exhibit *input-dependent routing* (Figure 3) across domains, revealing how shared hierarchies adapt to different tasks. This would strengthen the claim that highways enable efficient information routing.  
2. Clarify how highway networks relate to "thin & tall" architectures (e.g., VGG’s stacked 3×3 convolutions). While highway networks *enable* deeper stacks, they provide a distinct mechanism for gradient flow beyond mere layer stacking.  

**Conclusion**  
This is a strong, well-executed contribution that directly addresses a critical barrier in deep learning: the difficulty of training very deep networks. The architecture is elegant, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are compelling. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: technically sound (Quality), clearly presented (Clarity), genuinely novel (Originality), and broadly significant (Significance). The minor suggestions are constructive rather than critical. I recommend **acceptance**. The work provides a practical, general-purpose solution that advances the state of the art and will likely influence future deep learning research.
