- Introduces Highway Networks: gating mechanism y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)) enabling direct training of very deep feedforward networks.  
- Claims highway networks overcome optimization issues and successfully train 100+ layer models on MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100.  
- Situates work among initialization, skip connections, and distillation literature but fails to engage with ResNet (He et al., 2015).  
- Strength: clear problem statement and strong motivation for training very deep networks.  
- Strength: MNIST experiments show highway networks maintain performance with depth while plain networks degrade.  
- Strength: insightful analyses (Figures 2–4) reveal dynamic routing and layer importance differences by task complexity.  
- Strength: architecture is simple, effective, and easy to implement.  
- Major weakness: no comparison to ResNet, which uses simpler skip connections and became the dominant approach.  
- Weakness: limited theoretical justification—Jacobian analysis is useful but insufficient to explain optimization benefits.  
- Weakness: additional computational and parameter overhead from transform gates is not discussed.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of Batch Normalization, another technique enabling deep network training.  
- Weakness: claim about training with SGD is overstated because success depends on specific negative-bias initialization for gates.  
- Significance: original gating idea is interesting, but practical impact is reduced by ResNet’s simpler, more effective approach.  
- Contribution: offers a way to study needed computation depth per problem, though residual connections can achieve similar insight.  
- Overall assessment: well-written with strong experiments, but significance limited by omission of ResNet comparison—recommendation: Weak Accept.  
- Suggested improvements: add ResNet comparison, emphasize critical negative-bias initialization, analyze computational overhead, acknowledge BatchNorm, and provide clearer theoretical explanation.
