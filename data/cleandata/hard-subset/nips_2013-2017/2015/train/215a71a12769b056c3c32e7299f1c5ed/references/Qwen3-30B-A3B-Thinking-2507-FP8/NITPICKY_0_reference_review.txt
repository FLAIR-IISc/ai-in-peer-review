

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper proposes "highway networks" as a modification to deep feedforward networks to facilitate training of extremely deep architectures. The authors draw inspiration from LSTM networks, introducing adaptive gating mechanisms (transform and carry gates) that allow information to flow across layers without attenuation. The key claim is that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD without the optimization difficulties encountered by plain networks as depth increases.

## Quality

The paper contains several significant technical errors and omissions that undermine its claims. The most critical issue is a fundamental mathematical error in Equation (5), where the authors claim the Jacobian is piecewise defined as dy/dx = I when T=0 and H'(x,WH) when T=1. In reality, the correct Jacobian for the highway layer is dy/dx = T(x,WT) * H'(x,WH) + (1-T(x,WT)) * I, not the piecewise function presented. This error fundamentally misrepresents the mathematical properties of the proposed architecture.

The paper incorrectly states that "for particular values of T, y = {x, if T=0, H(x,WH), if T=1}" (Equation 4), yet immediately acknowledges that T(x,WT) ∈ (0,1) due to the sigmoid activation, meaning these conditions can never be met exactly. This creates a misleading presentation of the architecture's behavior.

The paper claims "100-layer highway networks perform more than 2 orders of magnitude better than similarly-sized plain networks" (Section 3.1), but fails to provide the actual performance metrics for comparison. Without these numbers, the claim lacks empirical support.

The experimental section contains multiple inconsistencies. For instance, the authors state "each layer has 50 blocks for highway networks and 71 units for plain networks, yielding roughly identical numbers of parameters (≈5000) per layer" (Section 3.1), but the relationship between blocks and units is never properly explained. How do 50 blocks equate to 71 units? This lack of clarity makes the experimental setup difficult to interpret.

The paper mentions "a more complex block design closely resembling an LSTM block 'unrolled in time'" but only reports results for a "simplified form" (Section 2.2). This creates confusion about which architecture was actually used in the experiments.

## Clarity

The paper suffers from multiple formatting and presentation issues that significantly reduce clarity. Figures 1-4 and Tables 1-3 are referenced but not included in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the claimed results. The phrase "best3 50 hidden layer" (Section 4) appears to contain a typo ("best3" instead of "best 3").

The paper uses inconsistent notation (e.g., "x" vs "x̂" for dimensionality adjustment) and has incomplete reference citations (e.g., "Deep learning made easier by linear transformations in perceptrons" lacks year and page numbers). The claim that "the bias for the transform gates decreased further during training" (Section 4) lacks proper statistical support or context.

## Originality

The paper's core idea (using gating mechanisms to improve training of deep networks) has significant overlap with recent work, particularly residual networks (ResNets) which were published around the same time. The authors briefly mention skip connections in the related work section but fail to properly contextualize their contribution relative to ResNets, which would have been a critical omission given the timing of both papers.

The paper's reference to "LSTM-inspired" architecture is appropriate, but the authors don't sufficiently explain how their approach differs from previous LSTM-based techniques for feedforward networks.

## Significance

While the idea of enabling deeper networks is significant, the paper's claims are overstated due to the technical errors and incomplete experimental validation. The paper's claim that highway networks can be trained "without sacrificing ease of training or generalization ability" is not adequately supported by the provided evidence.

The paper also fails to discuss the computational cost of the additional gating mechanism. Highway networks require additional parameters and computations for the transform gates, which could offset the benefits of deeper architectures in resource-constrained settings.

## Strengths

The paper correctly identifies the challenge of training very deep networks and proposes an interesting architectural modification. The pilot experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10 suggest some promise, and the analysis of transform gate behavior (Section 4) provides interesting insights about information routing.

## Weaknesses

1. Fundamental mathematical error in Equation (5) that misrepresents the layer's behavior
2. Inconsistent parameter count explanation (50 blocks vs 71 units)
3. Lack of actual performance metrics for the claimed 2-orders-of-magnitude improvement
4. Missing figures and tables referenced throughout the paper
5. Incomplete citations and inconsistent formatting
6. Failure to properly contextualize the work relative to ResNets and other contemporary approaches

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some interesting insights, the technical errors, presentation issues, and lack of sufficient evidence for the key claims significantly undermine its scientific contribution. The fundamental mathematical error in Equation (5) alone is sufficient to question the validity of the proposed architecture's behavior. The paper would require substantial revision to correct these issues before it could be considered for publication.
