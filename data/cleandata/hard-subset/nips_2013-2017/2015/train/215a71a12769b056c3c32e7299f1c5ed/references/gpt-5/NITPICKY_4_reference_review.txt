Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “highway networks,” deep feedforward architectures that add elementwise, input-dependent gates to each layer: y = H(x, WH) · T(x, WT) + x · (1 − T(x, WT)), with T(x) = σ(WT x + bT). By biasing bT negative at initialization, layers initially default to carrying inputs forward, enabling gradient flow across many layers akin to LSTM memory cells. The paper argues this mitigates vanishing gradients in very deep feedforward nets and allows successful SGD training with depth >100. Empirically, the authors show: (i) on MNIST, plain MLPs degrade sharply with depth, while highway MLPs do not; (ii) convolutional highway networks train easily on CIFAR-10/100 and match or exceed FitNet results without teacher hints; (iii) qualitative analyses of gate activities suggest input-dependent routing; (iv) lesion studies indicate many layers are used on harder tasks.

This is closely related to LSTM gating [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber], local competition [Srivastava et al.], distillation/fitnets [Romero et al.], normalized initialization [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], and skip/shortcut connections. The novelty here is a gated, elementwise, learnable highway for feedforward nets, different from fixed skip connections and from deep supervision.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem (training very deep nets) with a simple, general architectural mechanism transferable across MLPs and CNNs.
- Clear intuition rooted in LSTM gating and an initialization heuristic (negative gate bias) that is easy to implement.
- Demonstrates convincing optimization advantages with depth in controlled MNIST MLP experiments (matched parameter count per layer).
- Provides competitive CIFAR-10/100 results in the limited-augmentation regime and matches FitNet performance without hints.
- Includes useful qualitative analyses (gate activities, class-conditional gating, layer lesioning) that provide insight into learned routing and layer utility.

Weaknesses and concerns (technical and methodological)
- Missing ablations and controls:
  - No comparison to fixed skip/shortcut connections (e.g., identity mapping without gates), which is an obvious baseline to isolate the contribution of gating vs. architectural bypass. The discussion mentions “hard-wired shortcut connections,” but there are no experiments.
  - No control for computational cost: gates introduce a second per-layer transform (T) with parameters and compute. While the MLP setup adjusts width to equalize parameters per layer, there is no FLOPs/time report or inference-time analysis to show fairness in optimization speed comparisons.
  - Carry gate is fixed to C = 1 − T. The effect of learning an independent carry gate C is not evaluated, nor is any ablation on gate parameterization (e.g., constrained vs. unconstrained, temperature scaling).
  - The negative bias initialization is central; yet there is no systematic study of its sensitivity beyond a brief guideline. How robust are results to bT, depth, activation choice, and optimizer?
- Experimental reporting gaps:
  - Hyperparameters: momentum, weight decay, learning rate schedule (“fixed schedule”) are not fully specified; number of epochs, batch size, and data augmentation magnitudes (“small translations and mirroring”) are not precisely described. Reproducibility suffers.
  - Results are sometimes reported as “best” across random searches; means and standard deviations are inconsistently provided. Model selection on the test set is implied in some places (e.g., using “best networks”), and lesioning is evaluated on the training set rather than validation/test, which is unusual.
  - CIFAR experiments reuse hyperparameters selected on CIFAR-10 for CIFAR-100; this may undercut the strength of CIFAR-100 conclusions and does not control for dataset differences.
  - No comparison to contemporaneous techniques likely to ease very deep training (e.g., batch normalization) or to modern ReLU/He initialization baselines in CNNs with matched depth/width. It is unclear whether similar depths would be trainable with BN and fixed skips.
- Clarity and notation issues:
  - Typos and grammar: “varying varying,” “backpropogation,” several awkward sentences.
  - Notation inconsistencies: use of · vs ∗ for elementwise product; xi vs x in block equation; H′(x, WH) in Eq. 5 is ambiguous; the definition σ(x) = 1/(1 + e^−x) is typeset incorrectly; T never exactly 0 or 1, yet the Jacobian discussion emphasizes limiting cases without quantifying intermediate behavior.
  - Footnote markers “1”, “2”, “3” appear in the text but the footnotes are absent.
  - Figures are referenced but complete figure captions and axes descriptions are not provided in the submission text; quantitative axes for lesion plots are not described.
  - The URL includes a space and a nonstandard tilde glyph and may not resolve as written.
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted: many entries lack years, venues, page numbers; “[cs], , , ,” placeholders; improper capitalization; inconsistent author name accents. Several canonical works on skip connections and batch normalization are missing or under-cited given the topic.
- Claims outpace evidence:
  - The statement “pilot experiments did not stall for >1000 layers” is anecdotal; no quantitative results or details are given.
  - The recommendation for bias values by depth is presented as a general guideline without systematic support.
  - The claim that highway networks “allow us to examine how much computation depth is needed” is interesting, but the lesioning is limited to single best models and conducted on the training set.

Quality
Technically plausible and mostly sound; however, baselines and ablations are insufficient to isolate the specific contribution of gating versus simple skip pathways and to rule out confounds due to parameter/compute increases. Reporting and reproducibility details are lacking.

Clarity
Readable at a high level but marred by numerous typos, missing footnotes, notation drift, incomplete figure and experimental details, and poor reference formatting.

Originality
Moderately novel: applies LSTM-style gating to feedforward depth with per-unit gates. Related ideas (skip connections, deep supervision, local competition) exist, but the specific highway mechanism is a meaningful addition.

Significance
Potentially high. If substantiated with stronger baselines and cleaner experiments, this could influence how practitioners design and train very deep nets. Current empirical evidence is promising but not definitive.

Pros
- Simple, general architectural idea with clear intuition.
- Strong optimization benefits with depth in controlled MLP experiments.
- Competitive CIFAR results without teacher hints.
- Insightful qualitative analyses (gates, routing, lesioning).

Cons
- Missing key baselines (fixed skips, BN), ablations, and compute/time reporting.
- Incomplete experimental details; inconsistent statistical reporting.
- Numerous clarity/formatting errors and incomplete references.
- Some claims anecdotal or weakly supported.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The idea is promising, but the paper needs stronger baselines (including fixed identity shortcuts and BN), thorough ablations (gate parameterization and bias sensitivity), precise experimental details and reproducibility, compute/time comparisons, corrected notation and references, and more rigorous analysis (test-set lesioning, multiple runs). With these addressed, the work could be impactful. Confidence: high.
