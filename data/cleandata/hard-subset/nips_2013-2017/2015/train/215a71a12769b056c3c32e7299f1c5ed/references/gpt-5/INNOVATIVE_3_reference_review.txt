Summary
This paper proposes Highway Networks: very deep feedforward architectures whose layers include LSTM-inspired gates that adaptively route information. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where H is the usual nonlinear transform and T is a learned transform gate (with carry gate coupled as 1 − T). With a simple negative bias initialization for T, the network is initially biased toward carrying inputs forward, creating “information highways” that mitigate optimization difficulties with depth. The authors demonstrate: (i) strong optimization behavior on MNIST for fully connected nets up to 100 layers, where plain deep nets degrade but highway nets do not; (ii) competitive generalization on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with thin/deep convolutional highway networks trained in a single stage, matching or exceeding FitNets without hint-based training; and (iii) analyses of gate activations, data-dependent routing, and layer lesioning that provide insight into how depth is used.

Relation to prior work
The approach is explicitly inspired by LSTM gating (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber; Gers et al.; Grid LSTM) and connects to prior attempts to ease training of deep nets through initialization (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.), activation functions (ReLUs, Maxout), skip/auxiliary connections and deep supervision, and student-teacher distillation (FitNets). The key difference is a general, per-layer gating mechanism that provides learnable, input-dependent highways for both activations and gradients, rather than fixed skip paths or multi-stage training.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: A simple, elegant architectural change—gated carry/transform—directly targets depth-related optimization barriers, drawing a principled connection to LSTM gating.
- Empirical optimization evidence: Rigorous depth sweeps on MNIST with controlled parameter counts show highway nets remain easy to optimize as depth increases (up to 100 layers), unlike plain nets.
- Practical impact: Single-stage training of thin, deeper CIFAR models that are competitive with or better than FitNets, obviating hint-based training under comparable budgets.
- Insightful analysis: Gate bias trajectories, per-class gate activity, and layer lesioning convincingly show input-dependent routing and selective use of depth.
- Simplicity and generality: The mechanism is orthogonal to the choice of H (fully connected or convolutional) and compatible with standard SGD; the negative gate-bias trick is easy to apply.
- Reproducibility: Code and scripts are released; hyperparameter selection procedure is reasonably described.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations: No comparison to hard-wired identity shortcuts or additive shortcuts, and no ablation of the C = 1 − T coupling versus independent carry gates. The role of gate bias initialization could be probed more systematically.
- Missing modern baselines: No comparisons to batch normalization or PReLU when assessing depth and optimization (though related initialization is cited).
- Scale: Results are on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100; no evidence on larger datasets. Claims of >1000-layer feasibility are only in pilots and not reported.
- Metrics and costs: Limited reporting on training time, compute overhead, and parameter efficiency versus plain/skip alternatives, beyond rough parameter matching per layer.
- Some clarity gaps: The notion of “blocks” versus “units” could be defined earlier; a concise architectural figure would help. Minor typos (e.g., “varying varying”).

Quality
Technically sound and careful overall. The optimization study is well controlled for parameter count. The CIFAR experiments are fair given the stated goal (not SOTA chasing), though stronger baselines (BN, additive shortcuts) would bolster the case. Analyses are thoughtful and informative. A more thorough ablation (independent carry gate, bias values, removal of gates) and gradient-norm diagnostics would further substantiate claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The gating formulation and initialization are clear. Some implementation details (exact depths/widths per CIFAR architecture, compute budgets, training schedules) could be summarized more compactly in the main text for quick comparison. Figures showing gate activities/lesioning are valuable.

Originality
High. While skip connections and LSTM gates are known, introducing per-layer LSTM-like gating into deep feedforward networks as a general mechanism for trainability and data-dependent routing is a novel and bold idea. The “information highway” perspective is original, and the analyses provide new insights into depth utilization.

Significance
High. If broadly adopted, this mechanism could unlock much deeper architectures trained with vanilla SGD and without multi-stage procedures. The analyses suggest a new way to think about adaptive routing over depth. Even if later variants supersede the exact formulation, the paper likely catalyzes a fruitful direction.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a new architectural primitive (gated carry/transform) with clear motivation and strong intuition.
- Demonstrates practical training of very deep networks and competitive performance without teacher hints.
- Provides novel analyses illuminating how deep networks use depth via gating.
- Simple, general, and likely to influence subsequent architectures; code available.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of comparisons to alternative skip/normalization strategies that also ease optimization.
- Limited ablation on gate coupling and initialization.
- Experiments confined to small/medium-scale datasets; no large-scale validation.
- Sparse reporting on compute/training-time overhead.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a direct comparison to hard identity shortcuts or additive skip layers of equal depth/width.
- Include an ablation with independent carry gates (C learned, not 1 − T) and with different T-bias initializations; report gradient norms across depth.
- Compare to batch normalization and/or PReLU baselines in the depth-scaling study.
- Report training time and FLOPs per forward/backward pass to quantify overhead of gates.
- Provide a concise architectural diagram clarifying “blocks” and parameter sharing.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling, novel mechanism for training very deep networks, provides solid empirical evidence and insightful analyses, and opens a promising direction. Minor experimental gaps do not outweigh the conceptual contribution.

Confidence
High.
