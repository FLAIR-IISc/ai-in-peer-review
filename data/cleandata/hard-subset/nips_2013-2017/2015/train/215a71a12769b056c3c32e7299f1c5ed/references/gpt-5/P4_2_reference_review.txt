Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces data-dependent gating across depth to ease optimization of very deep networks. Each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine + nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a learned transform gate (sigmoid). Setting the carry gate C = 1 − T ensures dimensional consistency and keeps the parametrization simple. Inspired by LSTM gating, the authors initialize the transform gates with negative biases to bias layers toward carrying inputs at the start of training, thereby creating “information highways” that alleviate attenuation of activations and gradients across many layers.

The work is positioned relative to: (a) difficulties in training deep nets (vanishing/exploding gradients, optimization challenges); (b) improved initializations and activations (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.; maxout); (c) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision; (d) distillation/teacher-student methods (FitNets); and (e) LSTM-style gating. The paper claims that highway networks allow direct SGD training of extremely deep nets without layerwise pretraining or teacher supervision.

Empirically, the paper shows: (1) optimization comparisons on MNIST with fully connected nets up to 100 layers: plain nets degrade with depth; highway nets maintain trainability and converge faster; (2) convolutional highway nets on MNIST achieve competitive accuracy with fewer parameters; (3) on CIFAR-10/100, thin deep highway nets match or exceed FitNets’ accuracy without teacher hints, under comparable parameter and computation budgets; (4) analyses of gate activations and “lesioning” layers suggest input-dependent routing and that more complex datasets (CIFAR-100) use more layers than simple ones (MNIST).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architectural formulation is clear and consistent. The gating mechanism is well-motivated by LSTM analogies. The negative bias initialization for T is a simple, plausible heuristic backed by experiments. Results show strong optimization advantages with increasing depth and reasonable generalization on standard benchmarks.
- Empirical support: The MNIST optimization study is systematic with random search over hyperparameters, matched parameter budgets per layer, and depth scaling to 100 layers. CIFAR experiments compare to FitNets under similar budgets and training regimes. Some runs report mean±std over 5 seeds. Analyses (gate statistics, class-conditional gating, lesioning) are informative.
- Limitations: (i) Comparisons exclude important contemporaneous baselines that improve deep training, notably batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy), and simple shortcut/skip architectures without gating (fixed identity residual connections). (ii) CIFAR results are not state-of-the-art and are limited to modest depth (~32 conv layers) and small data augmentation; no ImageNet experiments. (iii) The cost in parameters and compute for gates is not quantified, nor is training/inference time overhead. (iv) The coupling C = 1 − T is assumed but not ablated versus independent carry gates. (v) Sensitivity to the transform-gate bias and gate dimensionality (per-unit vs per-channel vs per-layer scalars) is only partially explored. (vi) There is no theoretical analysis of gradient propagation through highway layers beyond intuitive arguments.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The notation is consistent, and the architectural description is precise. The experimental protocols are mostly clear; code and hyperparameter search artifacts are promised to be public. Suggestions:
- Provide exact architectural details for convolutional highway blocks (e.g., whether T uses shared 1×1 convolutions or matches H’s receptive field; parameter counts for T vs H).
- Report training/inference time and memory overhead due to gates.
- Clarify the rationale for choosing C = 1 − T and whether independent C was tried.

Originality
The core novelty is to bring LSTM-like gating to depth in feedforward nets, enabling adaptive routing across layers. While skip connections and deep supervision have been explored, and Grid LSTMs relate conceptually, a general, layer-local gating formulation with simple initialization for very deep feedforward/convolutional nets is original at the time. The analysis via lesioning and gate statistics is also a useful contribution.

Significance
Highway networks address a central challenge—training very deep networks—without auxiliary teachers or complex optimization methods. The demonstrated optimization robustness to depth is compelling and likely to influence architectures and training practice. Empirical gains on CIFAR are moderate but suggest practical utility under constrained model budgets. The broader impact would be strengthened by comparisons to fixed skip/identity connections and batch normalization and by results on larger-scale datasets.

Strengths
- Simple, general architectural idea with clear motivation.
- Strong evidence that optimization does not degrade with depth; faster convergence.
- Works with standard SGD; no teacher/distillation required for deep thin nets.
- Insightful analyses of gating behavior, input-dependent routing, and layer importance.
- Public code and hyperparameter details enhance reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Add baselines with: (a) fixed identity skip connections (same depth/width), and (b) batch normalization, to disentangle benefits of gating vs generic shortcuts/normalization.
- Quantify parameter, FLOP, and wall-clock overhead due to gates; include speed/memory comparisons.
- Provide ablations: independent carry gate C; per-layer scalar gates vs per-unit; effect of gate bias initialization across datasets; replace sigmoid with other squashing functions.
- Include gradient-norm or Jacobian spectrum measurements across depth to support the “information highway” claim.
- Explore deeper conv highway nets and/or larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to strengthen significance.
- Report more seeds for key benchmarks and include confidence intervals.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a novel, simple mechanism.
- Clear empirical evidence of improved trainability with depth.
- Reproducible and broadly applicable across MLPs and CNNs.
- Provides useful analytical tools and insights into deep computation paths.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines (batch normalization, fixed skip connections).
- Limited scale and state-of-the-art performance; impact demonstrated mostly on small datasets.
- Overhead and design choices (C = 1 − T) not thoroughly justified or ablated.
- Lacks theoretical analysis beyond empirical observations.

Overall, this is a solid contribution introducing a new architectural primitive that materially improves training of very deep networks, with convincing optimization results and thoughtful analysis. Strengthening the experimental comparisons and scalability evidence would further increase its impact.
