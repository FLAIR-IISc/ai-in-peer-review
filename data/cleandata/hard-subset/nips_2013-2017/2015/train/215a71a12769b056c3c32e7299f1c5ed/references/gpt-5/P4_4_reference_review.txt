Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that adds data-dependent gating to each layer: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where T is a sigmoid “transform gate,” coupled to a “carry gate” C = 1 − T. With appropriate negative bias initialization of T, information can flow across many layers with minimal attenuation, inspired by LSTM’s gating for temporal credit assignment. The authors argue this architecture eases optimization of very deep networks without specialized layer-wise training, teachers, or complex initialization. They present results on fully connected (MNIST) and convolutional settings (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100) and analyze gate activations, data-dependent routing, and layer importance via lesion studies.

This work builds on: (a) challenges of training deep nets and associated initialization/optimization (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.; Martens & Sutskever), (b) competitive/locally selective activations and maxout (Srivastava et al.; Goodfellow et al.), (c) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and (d) LSTM gating (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber; Gers et al.). It is closely related to ideas of routing and soft attention via multiplicative interactions and to recently proposed Grid LSTM. Compared to FitNets, which require a teacher-student procedure, the proposed architecture aims for single-stage training of deep thin networks with comparable or better accuracy under similar parameter/compute budgets.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea: a simple gated interpolation between transformed features and identity carry enables continuous paths of near-identity mapping, plausibly improving gradient flow.
- Practical training recipe: negative bias initialization for transform gates is simple and effective across settings; no reliance on a specific H (works with fully connected and convolutional blocks).
- Empirical evidence on optimization: as depth increases (10→100 layers), plain nets degrade substantially while highway nets maintain trainability and converge faster; pilot evidence of successful training even beyond 100 layers.
- Generalization: competitive test accuracy on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 under standard augmentation, with thin, deep models and parameter/compute budgets comparable to FitNets, without teacher distillation.
- Insightful analysis: visualization of gate biases/activities, evidence of input- and class-dependent routing, and lesion studies showing which layers matter for different datasets (many layers idle on MNIST; broader utilization on CIFAR-100).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited breadth of comparisons: while skip connections and deep supervision are cited, there is no direct empirical comparison to fixed identity shortcut baselines of comparable capacity, leaving open how much the learned gating contributes beyond simple residual pathways.
- Ablation depth: core design choices are not thoroughly ablated (e.g., C = 1 − T vs. independent carry gate; effect size of bT; freezing T during training; computational overhead of gates). This constrains understanding of which components are critical.
- Scope of datasets and scale: results are on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with relatively small models and modest augmentation. Claims about “very deep” training would be stronger with larger-scale benchmarks or substantially deeper convolutional models, given the stated success with >100 layers.
- Fairness/controls: plain baselines are reported with normalized initialization but lacking additional training aids; it would help to control for width/parameter-count more tightly across all depths and to include strong plain baselines with skip connections or other regularization to isolate the contribution of gating.
- Theoretical support: the intuition from LSTMs is compelling, but there is no analysis (e.g., of Jacobian spectra or gradient norms) quantifying how gating mitigates vanishing/exploding across depth.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent, the gate parameterization is standard, and the initialization heuristic is justified by analogy to LSTMs. Experimental methodology is reasonable (multiple runs, reporting mean±std for some settings, public code). However, some experiment descriptions lack detail (e.g., exact parameter budgets per variant, compute overhead), and key ablations are missing.
- Empirical support: Clear optimization plots show the core claim—trainability with depth—on MNIST FC networks. CIFAR experiments demonstrate competitive accuracy at constrained budgets and single-stage training versus FitNets. Additional large-scale evidence would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The gating mechanism and constraints on dimensionality are clearly stated, and practical guidance on bT is helpful. Minor issues: a few typos (“varying varying”), and some equations and footnotes are awkwardly presented; more explicit details on architectural widths and exact parameter counts per layer/depth would aid reproducibility (though code availability mitigates this).

Originality
- The core contribution—learned, per-block gating enabling near-identity carry across depth—is a novel application of LSTM-like gating to feedforward depth. While related to prior skip connections and deep supervision, the learnable, input-dependent gating distinguishes the approach. The analysis of routing and layer lesioning is also a useful addition.

Significance
- Enabling stable optimization of very deep networks is an important problem. The method is simple, broadly applicable (FC and conv), and yields competitive performance without multi-stage training. The potential for impact is high, though broader benchmarks and stronger baselines would better establish state-of-the-art advancement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a direct baseline with fixed identity skip connections (no gates) and matched parameter counts to quantify the benefit of learnable gates.
- Provide ablations: (i) independent carry gate vs. C = 1 − T, (ii) freezing T after initialization, (iii) varying bT and its schedule, (iv) effect of H nonlinearity and normalization, and (v) compute/memory overhead of T relative to plain and skip baselines.
- Report results for substantially deeper convolutional models (e.g., >50 layers) and/or a larger dataset to substantiate “very deep” claims in the conv setting.
- Expand training details: exact parameter counts per architecture, learning rate schedules, and hyperparameter ranges; ensure all reported “best” curves correspond to comparable budgets.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general architectural mechanism with clear empirical benefits for training very deep networks.
- Competitive accuracy at constrained budgets without teacher networks.
- Insightful analyses demonstrating dynamic routing and layer utilization.
- Public code and reproducible setups.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing direct comparisons to strong skip-connection baselines and limited ablations of core design choices.
- Evaluation confined to small/medium-scale datasets and relatively modest conv depths.
- Lack of theoretical analysis to support the optimization claims.

Overall evaluation
A technically sound and clearly presented architectural contribution that addresses an important challenge with promising empirical evidence and insightful analysis. Strengthening baselines/ablations and expanding experimental scope would further solidify the case.
