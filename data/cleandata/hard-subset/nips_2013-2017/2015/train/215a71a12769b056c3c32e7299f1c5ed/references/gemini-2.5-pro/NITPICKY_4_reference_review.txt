### Summary

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel feedforward neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep models. The core problem addressed is the difficulty of propagating information and gradients through many layers in traditional "plain" networks. The proposed solution is inspired by the gating mechanisms of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. Each layer is augmented with a "transform gate" and a "carry gate," which dynamically control the flow of information. The output of a highway layer is an adaptive combination of a non-linear transformation of the input and the input itself, creating "information highways" that allow signals to pass unimpeded across many layers. The authors demonstrate empirically that highway networks with depths of up to 100 layers can be trained directly with SGD, a feat not achievable with plain networks of similar depth. They also show that highway networks can match or outperform architectures like FitNets, which require complex multi-stage training procedures. The paper further provides an analysis of the learned gate behavior, suggesting that the network learns data-dependent information routing.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and significant approach to a critical problem in deep learning: the effective training of very deep networks. The adaptation of LSTM-style gating to a feedforward, layer-wise context is an insightful and powerful idea. At a time when increasing network depth is a primary driver of performance, this work provides a practical architectural solution that demonstrably succeeds where conventional architectures fail.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the efficacy of highway networks. The optimization experiment in Section 3.1 is particularly effective, clearly showing the stark contrast between the trainability of deep highway networks and the failure of deep plain networks on MNIST. The ability to directly train models that outperform FitNets without a complex, hint-based procedure is another strong result.

3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4, which visualizes the gate activations and performs lesion studies, offers valuable insights into the network's internal mechanics. The evidence for data-dependent routing (Section 4.1) is a key finding, as it suggests the gating mechanism is not merely a training aid but an integral part of the learned computation, distinguishing it from static skip connections. The layer importance analysis (Section 4.2) cleverly uses the architecture to probe which layers are utilized for different tasks.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core contribution, the paper is marred by a considerable number of methodological issues, formatting inconsistencies, and a general lack of rigor that undermine its quality.

1.  **Major Methodological Flaw:** Footnote 3 reveals that the hyperparameter search for the analysis in Section 4 was performed by minimizing the *training set error*. This is a fundamental methodological error. Tuning hyperparameters on the training set encourages overfitting and invalidates any claims about the model's generalization or internal behavior. The analysis of the "best" network found this way is therefore based on a model that was not optimized according to sound machine learning practice.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The crucial design choice of using a plain layer to change feature map dimensionality (Section 2.1) is mentioned but not detailed. It is unclear when and how this is applied in the convolutional architectures, which hinders reproducibility.
    *   The simplification of setting the carry gate `C = 1 - T` (Equation 3) is made "for simplicity" but is not justified. This forces a hard trade-off between carrying and transforming information. An ablation study or discussion of what is lost compared to two independent gates would have been appropriate.
    *   The reporting metric "Best (mean ± std.dev.)" is non-standard and confusing. Reporting the single best run alongside the mean suggests a degree of cherry-picking. Standard practice is to report the mean and standard deviation across all runs to provide a robust measure of performance.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:**
    *   A critical baseline is missing. Given that the paper cites prior work on skip connections, a direct comparison to a simple, parameter-free residual connection (`y = H(x) + x`) would be essential to justify the additional complexity and parameters of the learnable gating mechanism. Without this comparison, it is unclear if the adaptive gating is truly necessary.
    *   The proposed initialization of the transform gate bias `b_T` is presented as a key element for successful training. However, its treatment is cursory. The recommended values seem ad-hoc, and the paper lacks a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with this crucial hyperparameter.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted. Multiple entries are incomplete, missing conference or journal venues (e.g., [1], [14], [15]). Several references contain placeholder text like `[cs],, , ,` (e.g., [2], [3], [17]). One reference [28] is a master's thesis in German. This level of carelessness in the references reflects poorly on the overall quality of the manuscript.
    *   There are minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., using `·` in Equation 2 but `*` in Footnote 2 for the same operation) and typos ("varying varying depths" in Section 3.1).

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

While the core idea of highway networks is innovative and potentially impactful, the paper in its current form does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS. The combination of a major methodological flaw (tuning hyperparameters on the training set), a lack of essential experimental baselines, insufficient detail for reproducibility, and a striking number of formatting errors is too substantial to overlook. The work shows considerable promise, but it requires a significant revision to address these fundamental issues of scientific rigor and presentation. The authors should correct their experimental methodology, include a comparison to parameter-free skip connections, provide a more thorough analysis of their proposed initialization scheme, and meticulously proofread and format their manuscript before resubmission.
