

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors address the fundamental challenge of training very deep networks by incorporating an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism that allows information to flow across layers without attenuation, creating "information highways." The key innovation is the highway layer transformation: y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), where T is a transform gate (initialized with negative bias) that controls how much of the input is transformed versus carried through.

The paper relates its work to several previous approaches: normalized initialization [16,17], skip connections [22,23,2,24], knowledge distillation [25,26], and layer-wise training [26,27]. The authors clearly differentiate highway networks from these approaches by emphasizing the adaptive nature of the gating mechanism, which allows the network to dynamically route information based on input, unlike fixed skip connections. While the paper acknowledges a recent similar LSTM-inspired model [32], it demonstrates that highway networks provide a more general solution applicable to feedforward networks.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-motivated theoretical foundations. The highway layer formulation (equation 3) is mathematically rigorous, and the authors provide clear justification for why this architecture might overcome depth-related optimization challenges (equations 4-5). The experimental design is comprehensive, with multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100), varying network depths (up to 100 layers), and careful comparison to plain networks with normalized initialization. The results are well-supported, showing highway networks outperforming plain networks significantly at greater depths (100-layer highway networks performing >100x better than similarly-sized plain networks on MNIST). The authors demonstrate honesty about limitations, noting that some layers may remain unused but showing this doesn't harm performance.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The notation is consistent, the architecture is well-explained with appropriate equations, and the experimental methodology is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The analysis of information routing (Figures 2-4) provides valuable insights into the behavior of highway networks, with clear visualizations of transform gate biases, activity patterns, and layer importance. The public availability of source code and hyperparameter details further enhances reproducibility.

**Originality**: The highway network architecture represents a novel combination of familiar techniques (LSTM gates + feedforward networks) that significantly advances the field. While related to skip connections and knowledge distillation, the adaptive gating mechanism distinguishes this work from previous approaches. The paper adequately references related work, though it could have more explicitly contrasted with ResNets (published around the same time but after this paper), which share conceptual similarities but use fixed skip connections rather than adaptive gates.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training very deep networks directly without resorting to complex two-stage procedures. The results demonstrate that highway networks enable direct training of networks with 100+ layers, which was previously difficult with plain networks. The findings have significant implications for model design, as they show that depth can be increased without sacrificing generalization or training ease. The ability to analyze layer importance (Figure 4) provides a new tool for understanding how much depth is needed for different problems, which is not easily achievable with plain networks.

**Strengths**:
- Clear theoretical justification for the highway network architecture
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and network depths
- Insightful analysis of information routing and layer importance
- Well-written, well-organized, and reproducible
- Demonstrates a fundamental advance in training very deep networks

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited comparison to ResNets (published shortly after this paper)
- No thorough analysis of computational cost (beyond parameter counts)
- Depth experiments only go up to 100 layers, leaving open questions about scalability
- Slightly limited theoretical analysis of convergence properties

**Recommendation**: Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to deep learning by introducing highway networks, a novel architecture that enables direct training of very deep networks. The technical soundness, thorough experimental validation, and clear theoretical motivation make this a strong NIPS submission. The paper advances the state of the art by demonstrating that depth-related optimization challenges can be overcome with a simple architectural modification, with clear implications for future deep learning research. While the comparison to ResNets would have strengthened the paper, this does not diminish the novelty and significance of the highway network concept. The work represents an important step toward understanding how to effectively leverage depth in neural network architectures.
