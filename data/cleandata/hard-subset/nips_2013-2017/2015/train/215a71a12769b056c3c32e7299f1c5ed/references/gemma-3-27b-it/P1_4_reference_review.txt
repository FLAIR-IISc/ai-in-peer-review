## NIPS Review: Training Very Deep Networks

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the longstanding challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose "Highway Networks," inspired by LSTM, which incorporate adaptive gating mechanisms to facilitate information flow across many layers. These gates allow layers to dynamically control whether to transform their input or simply "carry" it forward, alleviating the vanishing/exploding gradient problem that plagues deep plain networks. The authors demonstrate through extensive experimentation on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 that highway networks can be trained effectively to significantly greater depths than plain networks, achieving competitive or superior performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a significant body of work addressing deep learning challenges. It acknowledges prior efforts in optimizer design (e.g., Adam, momentum), initialization strategies (e.g., Glorot/Xavier initialization), and architectural modifications like skip connections and FitNets. Importantly, it frames its approach as drawing inspiration from the success of recurrent networks, specifically LSTMs, in handling long-range dependencies, recognizing that similar mechanisms could benefit feedforward architectures. The authors also correctly position their work relative to recent advancements like FitNets which require multi-stage training, arguing that highway networks enable single-stage training for comparable or better performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of highway networks is a novel approach to tackling the depth problem. While inspired by LSTMs, the application of gated mechanisms to feedforward networks in this manner is a significant contribution.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling experimental evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks.  The results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, particularly the comparison to plain networks and FitNets, are strong and convincing. The ablation study on layer importance (Figure 4) is particularly insightful.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a clear explanation of how the gating mechanism addresses the gradient flow issues in deep networks, referencing the Jacobian analysis (Equation 5).
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concept of highway networks.  The notation is consistent and the equations are easy to follow.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide links to source code, hyperparameter search results, and scripts, fostering reproducibility.
* **Analysis:** The visualization of gate biases, activities, and layer importance provides a valuable insight into how highway networks operate.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the transform gate bias is a hyperparameter needing tuning.  While results are shown for various values, a more thorough investigation into the sensitivity of performance to this (and other) hyperparameters would strengthen the work.
* **Computational Cost:** While the paper demonstrates the ability to train deeper networks, it doesn't explicitly address the computational cost associated with the increased number of parameters and operations introduced by the gating mechanism. A discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Gate Function:** The paper primarily uses a simple sigmoid function for the transform gate. While effective, it would be interesting to explore the potential benefits of other gating functions or more complex gate architectures.
* **Figure 2 Interpretation:** The interpretation of the patterns observed in Figure 2 regarding the gradients in bias and activity is somewhat speculative and could benefit from more rigorous analysis.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, claims are well-supported by experimental results, and the authors acknowledge limitations. It feels like a complete and well-executed piece of work.
* **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-written and easy to understand, even for readers not deeply familiar with the specific area.
* **Originality:** High. The highway network architecture is a novel approach, building on existing ideas in a creative and effective way.
* **Significance:** High.  The ability to train significantly deeper networks with standard gradient descent has the potential to impact a wide range of applications.  This work represents a substantial step towards realizing the potential benefits of very deep architectures.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a novel and effective method for training very deep neural networks.
* Achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on several benchmark datasets.
* Provides insightful analysis into the inner workings of highway networks.
* Contributes to a better understanding of the challenges and opportunities associated with deep learning.
* Well-written, clear, and reproducible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.
* Computational cost of the gating mechanism isn't fully addressed.
* Limited exploration of alternative gate functions.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This is a high-quality paper that addresses an important problem in deep learning with a novel and effective solution. The experimental results are compelling, and the analysis provides valuable insights. While some areas could benefit from further investigation, the strengths of the work far outweigh its weaknesses. This paper is a significant contribution to the field and is well-deserving of acceptance to NIPS.
