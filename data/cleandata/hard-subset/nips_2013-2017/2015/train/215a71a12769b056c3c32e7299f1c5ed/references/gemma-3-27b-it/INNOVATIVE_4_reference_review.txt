This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel architectural modification to deep feedforward networks inspired by the gating mechanisms of LSTMs. The core idea—allowing information to flow more easily across many layers via “information highways”—is compelling and addresses a central challenge in training very deep networks: vanishing/exploding gradients and poor propagation of activations. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained successfully to significantly greater depths than traditional "plain" networks using standard SGD, a result that is, frankly, quite impressive. The paper’s clarity is good, and the experimental validation, while not exhaustive, is convincing, particularly the optimization experiments demonstrating the ability to train 100-layer networks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network concept is genuinely novel and represents a potentially important architectural contribution. It's a clever way to adapt concepts from recurrent neural networks (specifically LSTMs) to feedforward architectures to address depth-related training difficulties.
*   **Significance:** Overcoming the depth limitation in feedforward networks is a major goal in deep learning. If this approach proves broadly applicable, it could unlock the potential for even more powerful models. The demonstrated ability to train such deep networks *at all* is a significant result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core concept is explained clearly. The notation is reasonably clear, and the equations are easy to follow.
*   **Empirical Support:** The optimization experiments (Section 3.1) provide strong evidence that highway networks address the depth problem. The results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 are supportive, though more extensive comparisons would be beneficial.
*   **Analysis (Sections 4.1 & 4.2):** The investigation into information routing and layer importance, while preliminary, offers valuable insights into how these networks function internally.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a deeper theoretical understanding of why highway networks work so well. While the LSTM inspiration is noted, a more formal analysis of the gradient flow properties within highway networks would strengthen the contribution. The Jacobian discussion is a promising start, but could be extended.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The initialization scheme for the transform gates (negative bias) is presented as important, and experiments show it is necessary. However, the paper doesn't systematically explore the sensitivity of performance to this hyperparameter or provide a strong theoretical justification for its effectiveness.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:** While comparisons to FitNets and state-of-the-art methods are included, they are somewhat cursory. A more comprehensive comparison, perhaps including a fairer architectural comparison (matching parameter counts more closely), would be more persuasive.
*   **Scale of Experiments:** Although the results are encouraging, the experiments are limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to more complex datasets and architectures would further validate the approach.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of highway networks is both novel and potentially impactful.
*   The experimental results, particularly the successful training of 100-layer networks, are compelling.
*   The paper introduces a new direction for research in deep network architectures.
*   The authors have made their code and hyperparameters publicly available, which promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of deeper theoretical analysis is a weakness.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.
*   The comparisons to existing methods could be more thorough.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a genuinely novel and promising approach to training very deep feedforward networks. While the paper has some weaknesses, the novelty and potential significance of the work outweigh these concerns. I am willing to overlook the lack of extensive theoretical analysis and the limited scope of the experiments, given the compelling experimental results and the clear potential for future research. I recommend **acceptance**. This is a paper that has the potential to spark a significant amount of follow-up work in the field.
