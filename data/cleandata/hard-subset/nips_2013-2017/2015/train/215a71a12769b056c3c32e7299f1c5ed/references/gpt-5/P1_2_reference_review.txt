Summary
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture with data-dependent gating inspired by LSTM. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where H is the usual nonlinear transform and T is a sigmoid “transform gate.” Setting C = 1 − T ensures that the layer interpolates between carrying its input forward unchanged and applying H. Initializing gate biases negatively biases layers to carry early in training, facilitating gradient flow across many layers (“information highways”). The authors show: (i) superior optimization behavior of highway vs plain networks as depth increases (up to 100 fully connected layers) on MNIST; (ii) competitive or better generalization on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 under constrained parameter/computation budgets, including surpassing FitNets trained with hints while training highway nets in a single stage; and (iii) analyses of learned gating patterns and lesion studies indicating dynamic, input-dependent routing and depth utilization contingent on task complexity. The work is clearly related to LSTM gating, skip connections and deep supervision, and knowledge distillation (FitNets), and to initialization methods (e.g., Glorot, He).

Quality
Strengths:
- The architectural formulation is simple, well-motivated, and clearly specified (equations and constraints). The interpolation view and Jacobian discussion are helpful.
- Optimization experiments are convincing: normalized-initialized plain nets degrade sharply with depth, while highway nets of 50–100 layers train well and converge faster.
- Convolutional experiments are fair given the budget constraints; single-stage training with SGD is a concrete practical advantage over two-stage FitNet training.
- Analysis is thoughtful: gate bias/activity visualizations, per-class differences (routing), and lesion experiments support claims that gates both ease optimization and perform meaningful computation rather than degenerating to identity.
- Reproducibility is aided by code and hyperparameter details; multiple runs with mean/std are reported.

Weaknesses:
- Baselines: There is no direct comparison to architectures with explicit fixed skip/shortcut connections of equal depth (without gating). Such a baseline is crucial to isolate the benefit of learned gates vs identity shortcuts.
- Limited ablation: The choice C=1−T is reasonable but not evaluated against independent C and T; the effect of gate bias initialization is not systematically explored beyond guidelines; no ablation on removing gates after training (e.g., pruning thresholds).
- Theory: There is no formal analysis of gradient propagation (e.g., expected Jacobian spectra, gradient norms across depth), despite the central claim about alleviating vanishing gradients.
- Scope of depth: The claim that training does not stall for >1000 layers is only supported by pilot observations; no main-text results beyond 100 layers are shown.
- Comparisons to other training aids (e.g., batch normalization, additional regularization) are absent; it would help to establish whether highways remain beneficial when combined with such methods.
- CIFAR results are not state-of-the-art; while not the paper’s goal, more competitive baselines under the same augmentation regime would strengthen the significance.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The layer formulation and training scheme are easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Specify more precisely the convolutional gating: is T computed per spatial location and channel with the same receptive field as H? Is there any sharing across channels?
- Detail the parameter/computation overhead introduced by gates (percentage over plain nets) and training/inference time.
- Clarify how dimensionality changes are handled in deeper conv stacks (where plain layers are used), and whether those layers limit highway depth.
- Report training curves and gradient norm statistics for conv experiments analogous to Figure 1.

Originality
The core idea—input-dependent gating that blends transformed and carried signals layer-wise in deep feedforward nets—is novel and well-differentiated from prior work:
- It extends LSTM-style gating to depth rather than time.
- It goes beyond fixed skip connections by learning continuous, input-conditioned routing.
- It provides an alternative to teacher-student distillation for training thin deep nets.
Related work is broadly cited. A stronger comparison to fixed shortcuts would better position the contribution.

Significance
Enabling reliable training of very deep networks with standard SGD is important. The method is simple, broadly applicable (fully connected and convolutional), and shows practical wins (single-stage training, deeper-than-FitNet models within similar budgets). The analyses provide insight into how depth is used and when layers matter. Given how central depth is to modern architectures, the contribution is likely to influence future designs.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple architectural idea with solid empirical support.
- Demonstrated optimization benefits at substantial depths; faster convergence.
- Practical improvements under constrained budgets; removes need for two-stage hint training.
- Insightful analyses (gating patterns, lesioning) supporting the mechanism.
- Reproducible with released code and described hyperparameters.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baseline: deep networks with identity shortcuts/no gates.
- Limited ablations (independent carry gate, gate bias sensitivity, gate removal).
- No formal analysis of gradient flow; extreme-depth (>1000) claim not substantiated with results.
- CIFAR performance not SOTA and limited augmentation; missing comparisons to widely used training aids (e.g., batch norm).

Questions and suggestions
- Please include a baseline with fixed identity skip connections of equal topology to quantify the value of gating.
- Explore independent carry gates C(x) (not constrained to 1−T) and report stability/performance.
- Provide ablations on initial gate biases and their learned distributions; analyze sensitivity and general rules.
- Report gradient norms across layers during training to substantiate improved credit assignment.
- Quantify computational/parameter overhead of gates and end-to-end training/inference time.
- Clarify the gating granularity in conv layers (per-pixel, per-channel, or per-feature-map); discuss regularization (e.g., dropout) and compatibility with batch normalization.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a novel and practical architectural mechanism that addresses a central challenge in training very deep networks, with convincing evidence and insightful analysis. With stronger baselines and a few ablations, it would be a clear accept. Confidence: high.
