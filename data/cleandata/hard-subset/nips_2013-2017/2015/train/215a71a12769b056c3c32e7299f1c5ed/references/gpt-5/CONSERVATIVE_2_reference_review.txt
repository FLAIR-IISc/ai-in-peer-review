Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “highway networks,” feedforward architectures augmented with LSTM-like transform and carry gates across depth: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)). The central claim is that these gates create “information highways” that facilitate gradient flow, enabling direct SGD training of very deep networks. The authors initialize the transform-gate bias negatively to bias early training toward carry behavior, echoing LSTM forget-gate initialization. Experiments compare highway networks to plain multilayer networks with variance-preserving initialization on MNIST (fully connected), and to FitNets/maxout on CIFAR-10/100 (convolutional). Additional analyses visualize gate activities and lesion layers to argue that depth is utilized adaptively.

This builds on (i) initialization schemes for deep nets (Glorot/Bengio; He et al.), (ii) skip/shortcut connections used historically in nets and more recently for information flow, (iii) maxout and locally competitive units, and (iv) LSTM-style gating in recurrent nets. The idea of gating across depth is novel, though related to LSTM and to prior work with skip connections and linear passthroughs.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea tying depth-wise gating to LSTM intuition; simple formulation and initialization.
- Empirical evidence that thin, very deep fully connected networks train better than plain counterparts, with faster convergence as depth increases.
- Demonstration that thin, deeper conv nets can be trained via standard SGD on CIFAR-10/100 without multistage teacher-student “FitNet” training, at comparable parameter/compute budgets.
- Useful qualitative analyses (gate activity statistics, class-conditional routing) and lesion studies suggesting input-dependent routing and non-uniform layer utility.
- Code and scripts are provided, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baselines are limited. The most relevant comparator—non-gated skip/residual connections—is not evaluated. A direct comparison to y = H(x) + x (or other fixed skip schemes) and to batch normalization, both widely used to ease optimization in deep nets, is essential to isolate the benefit of gating vs. merely adding identity paths.
- Ablations are insufficient. There is no systematic study of (a) gating only H or only the carry path, (b) C learned independently versus C = 1 − T, (c) the effect of gate bias magnitudes across depths/datasets, or (d) removing the T(x) dependence (e.g., constant T). The claims about gradient flow hinge on T but the derivative analysis is qualitative; no bounds or empirical sensitivity analyses are provided.
- Evaluation scope is modest. Results are on MNIST/CIFAR with light augmentation; no results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or substantially deeper conv nets. Some claims (training >1000 layers) are mentioned but not backed by reported experiments.
- Fairness/controls: While parameter counts are roughly matched per layer in MNIST FC experiments, it is unclear whether compute and parameter budgets are matched across CIFAR comparisons. Gate parameters add nontrivial capacity and compute; wall-clock training time and FLOP comparisons are not reported.
- The argument that gradients can pass “without attenuation” is overstated. Since σ never equals 0 or 1, the layer Jacobian contains additional T′(x) terms; the paper does not analyze when highway layers effectively approximate identity mappings and how this impacts gradient norms across depth.
- Hyperparameter selection is inconsistent: a random search for MNIST but reused schedules for CIFAR-10/100; only 5 runs reported. Stronger statistical reporting and per-dataset tuning would strengthen conclusions.
- Related work on skip connections and deep supervision is discussed but not experimentally disentangled. Several references are incomplete.

Quality
Technically, the architecture is sound and implementable, and the experiments support the feasibility of training deeper nets. However, the evidence is not yet sufficient to attribute the observed benefits uniquely to gating. Key missing comparisons (residual/skip, BN), quantitative compute/parameter matching, and ablations limit the strength of the causal claims. The qualitative analyses are interesting but do not replace controlled experiments.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. Equations and the construction are easy to follow. Some reference entries are incomplete. More explicit architectural details (per-layer widths, exact parameter counts and FLOPs) would aid reproducibility beyond code release.

Originality
Applying LSTM-inspired gating across depth in feedforward nets is a novel and interesting idea. The connection to prior skip-connection work is acknowledged, but differences from simpler, non-gated skips should be made explicit and tested.

Significance
If validated against strong baselines, highway networks could be impactful. As it stands, the significance is tempered by limited empirical scope and missing comparisons. The paper is promising but premature for broad claims of enabling “very deep” training versus established alternatives.

Questions for the authors
1. How do highway networks compare to residual connections y = H(x) + x and to batch-normalized deep nets under matched budgets?
2. What is the effect of learning C independently versus fixing C = 1 − T? Does gating only one path suffice?
3. Provide sensitivity plots for the transform gate bias across depth and datasets; how robust is training to this hyperparameter?
4. Report parameter counts, FLOPs, and wall-clock training times for all models; quantify the overhead of gating.
5. Can you scale the conv experiments to >50–100 layers and/or a larger dataset (e.g., ImageNet) to substantiate “very deep” claims?

Pros
- Clear, simple architectural idea with plausible motivation
- Demonstrated optimization benefits on MNIST and CIFAR
- Code available; thoughtful qualitative analyses

Cons
- Missing key baselines (residual/BN), ablations, and compute-matched comparisons
- Limited dataset scope and depth for conv nets
- Overstated gradient-flow claims without theoretical or empirical backing

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the empirical validation is not yet rigorous enough relative to established alternatives. A revision with thorough comparisons to residual/BN baselines, comprehensive ablations, and stronger, compute-matched results on more challenging settings would significantly strengthen the case.
