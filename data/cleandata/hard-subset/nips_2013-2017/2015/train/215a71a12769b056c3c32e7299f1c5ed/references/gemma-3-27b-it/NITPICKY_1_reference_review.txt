This paper proposes “highway networks,” a modification to feedforward neural network architectures inspired by LSTM, aiming to facilitate training of very deep networks by introducing adaptive gating mechanisms to ease information flow. The central idea is sound, addressing a well-known challenge in deep learning – vanishing/exploding gradients and difficulty in optimization as depth increases. The authors demonstrate, through experimentation on MNIST, CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, that highway networks can be trained to depths exceeding those achievable with plain networks, and achieve competitive performance.

However, despite the potentially significant contribution, the paper is riddled with minor, yet pervasive, imperfections that detract from its overall quality and impact. While the core idea is valuable, the presentation, analysis, and level of detail fall short of NIPS standards.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network concept is a reasonable and potentially impactful approach to address the training difficulties of deep networks. The inspiration from LSTM is clearly articulated.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that highway networks *can* be trained to greater depths than plain networks, and maintain competitive accuracy. The comparative analysis with FitNets is helpful.
*   **Accessibility:** The authors provide code and hyperparameters, promoting reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Ablation Studies:** The paper mentions experimenting with different initial bias values for the transform gates, but lacks a systematic ablation study evaluating the sensitivity of performance to this crucial hyperparameter. Similarly, the effect of different activation functions within the H block (beyond ReLU) is not explored adequately.
*   **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** The decision to set C = 1-T is presented without sufficient justification. While simplifying the equations, the rationale behind this specific choice (instead of allowing C to be a learned parameter) is unclear.
*   **Overstated Claims of Simplicity:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the simplicity of highway networks, yet the introduction of transform and carry gates introduces additional parameters and complexity. The claim that “derivation of a suitable initialization scheme is not essential” is misleading; the specific negative bias initialization is crucial for initial carry behavior and deserves more detailed discussion.
*   **Presentation and Clarity Issues:** The notation, while generally standard, suffers from minor inconsistencies. For instance, the subscript ‘l’ is used for layer index in Section 2, but then abruptly disappears in the experiments (Section 3). Equation 5, stating the Jacobian, appears without context and lacks a clear explanation of its relevance.
*   **Figure Quality & Description:** Figure 2, illustrating the inner workings, is difficult to interpret. The axes aren’t clearly labelled. The claim that transform gate biases “decrease further during training” needs quantitative support (e.g., average bias values). The explanation of "stripes" is hand-wavy.
*   **Minor Technical Errors:** The statement that the number of parameters in highway blocks and plain layers are “roughly identical” (Section 3.1) is questionable and requires verification. The claim of achieving "competitive" performance on MNIST (Section 3.2) is vague and lacks comparison to established baselines.
*   **Inconsistent Depth Reporting:** The paper refers to 50/100 layer highway networks, but doesn't clearly state whether this refers to *total* layers including the initial plain layer, or just the highway blocks.
*   **Referencing Inconsistencies:** References are not always consistently formatted. Some references are given without years.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The core idea is novel and addresses an important problem in deep learning. The experiments demonstrate that highway networks can be successfully trained to greater depths. Code availability is commendable.
*   **Con:** The paper suffers from a large number of minor flaws in presentation, analysis, and justification. The lack of rigorous ablation studies and insufficient explanation of design choices raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results. The numerous inconsistencies and imprecisions detract from the paper's credibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a worthwhile idea, but the execution is subpar. The abundance of minor errors and omissions suggests a lack of meticulous attention to detail. While the core concept may be valuable, the current manuscript requires significant revision to meet NIPS quality standards. I recommend **rejection** with the strong encouragement that the authors address the issues outlined above before resubmitting. The potential of the idea is real, but the current presentation falls short.
