Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture with LSTM-inspired gates that regulate per-unit routing between a transformed path and a “carry” (identity) path: y = T(x)·H(x) + (1 − T(x))·x, with T(x) = σ(W_T x + b_T). By initializing b_T to negative values, layers are initially biased to carry, permitting information and gradients to propagate across many layers without attenuation. The authors argue this alleviates the optimization difficulties that plague very deep “plain” networks. They provide empirical evidence on fully connected and convolutional models (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100), comparisons to FitNets within similar parameter/compute budgets, and analyses of the learned gating patterns and layer importance via lesioning.

This idea is positioned relative to: (i) LSTM gates for long-range credit assignment in time (here adapted to depth); (ii) normalized/variance-preserving initialization and rectifiers; (iii) local-competition activations (maxout, ReLU variants); (iv) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision; and (v) teacher-student/hint training (FitNets). The contribution is to provide a simple, general feedforward gating mechanism that makes very deep networks (50–100+ layers) trainable by standard SGD in a single stage, without pretraining or distillation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The layer formulation is clear and general (applies to FC and conv). The training recipe (sigmoid gates with negative bias) is simple and justified by analogy to LSTMs. The experimental methodology includes random search for hyperparameters on optimization studies, multiple runs with mean/std reporting, and matched parameter counts between plain and highway nets in the MNIST optimization benchmark. Code availability improves reproducibility.
- Empirical support: Strong evidence that optimization degrades rapidly for plain nets with depth while highway nets maintain low training loss up to 100 layers; convergence is also faster. On MNIST/CIFAR, highway nets achieve competitive accuracy under constrained budgets and train directly without teachers. Analyses (gate statistics, class-conditional gate patterns, lesioning) substantiate the claims about information routing and depth utilization.
- Limitations: 
  - Baselines: No direct comparison to identity skip connections without gates (i.e., y = H(x) + x), which would isolate the benefit of adaptive gating vs. fixed residual pathways. Given the architectural proximity, this comparison is crucial to attribute gains to gating rather than to the existence of any bypass.
  - Missing modern training components: Batch normalization (widely known by now) is not evaluated; it is a strong baseline for training deep plain nets and interacts with residual/skip designs. 
  - Scale: Results are limited to MNIST and CIFAR; ImageNet-scale evidence would strengthen the significance of “very deep” claims.
  - Ablations: Sensitivity to the transform-gate bias b_T is only qualitatively discussed; more systematic ablations (varying b_T, learning-rate schedules, different H forms) would help. Also, the choice C = 1 − T is convenient but restrictive; effects of learning C independently are not tested.
  - Claims not fully substantiated: Training “more than 1000 layers” is mentioned as a pilot finding without presented results.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The core idea, equations, and implementation details are easy to follow; the initialization heuristic is stated concretely. The empirical sections specify datasets, architectures, and training schedules; results include best and mean±std across 5 runs. Analyses (gate biases/activities, lesioning) are insightful and clearly explained. Minor issues: some reference formatting is inconsistent; a few implementation details (exact parameter/FLOP counts per model, details of augmentation) could be tabulated for transparency.

Originality
Adapting LSTM-style gating to spatial depth in feedforward nets, with per-unit transform/carry gates, is a novel and elegant idea at the time. It unifies identity shortcuts and input-dependent routing, going beyond fixed skip connections and beyond prior hint-based training for deep thin nets. Concurrently related models (e.g., Grid LSTM) are cited, but the application to depth as a general architectural pattern for vision/classification is distinct.

Significance
The work addresses a central bottleneck—training very deep nets with simple SGD—offering a broadly applicable architectural mechanism. Even without pushing state of the art, showing that deep thin models can be trained directly and match/surpass teacher-assisted FitNets under similar budgets is practically meaningful. The qualitative analyses (data-dependent routing, depth utilization) provide rare insight into deep model computation. If validated at larger scales and compared against identity-residual baselines, the impact is likely high and influential.

Pros
- Simple, general mechanism enabling training of very deep nets.
- Solid optimization evidence: depth robustness and faster convergence.
- Competitive accuracy under constrained budgets, without distillation.
- Insightful analyses (gating patterns, class-conditional routing, lesion studies).
- Code and scripts provided, multiple-run reporting.

Cons
- Missing crucial baseline: identity residual connections without gates.
- No batch normalization comparisons; limited ablations on gate design and initialization.
- Results limited to MNIST/CIFAR; no large-scale validation.
- Some claims (1000+ layers) asserted without presented experiments.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Compare against residual layers y = H(x) + x (with/without BN) under matched budgets and depths; quantify the incremental value of gating.
- Report sensitivity to b_T and the effect of learning independent carry gates C(x) vs. C = 1 − T.
- Provide parameter and FLOP overhead of gates and their runtime impact.
- Analyze gradient norms and Jacobians across depth to substantiate the “information highway” claim quantitatively.
- Explore ImageNet-scale experiments or, at least, deeper/wider CIFAR models with stronger augmentation to probe generality.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The idea is simple, original, and addresses an important problem; the empirical evidence is convincing within scope and the analyses add substantial value. The primary weakness is the absence of comparisons to identity-residual baselines and BN, which could clarify whether gating is essential or merely one path to enabling skip connections. With those comparisons, this could be a strong accept; as is, it is a solid, impactful contribution.
