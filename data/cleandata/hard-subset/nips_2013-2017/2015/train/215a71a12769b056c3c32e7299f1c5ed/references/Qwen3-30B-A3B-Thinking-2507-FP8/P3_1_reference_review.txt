

# Peer Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary and Context
This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose modifying standard network layers with an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism (transform gate T and carry gate C) such that the layer output becomes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x)). This architecture allows information to flow across many layers without attenuation, creating "information highways." The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained with SGD even at extreme depths (e.g., 100+ layers), while plain networks suffer from optimization difficulties. The work builds on prior research on skip connections, distillation, and better initialization strategies, but proposes a simpler, more direct solution.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Impact**: The paper effectively articulates the fundamental challenge of training very deep networks, which has been a bottleneck in the field. The demonstration that highway networks can be trained directly without two-stage procedures (like distillation) is significant for both theory and practice.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 are comprehensive and well-designed. The authors provide convincing evidence that highway networks outperform plain networks at extreme depths (e.g., 100-layer highway networks outperform similarly-sized plain networks by 2 orders of magnitude). The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figures 2-4) provides valuable insights into how these networks operate.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The simplicity of the highway architecture—requiring only a minor modification to standard layers—makes it highly implementable. The authors provide clear guidance on initialization (negative bias for transform gates) and demonstrate successful training with standard SGD.

4. **Theoretical Insight**: The paper offers valuable analysis of how highway networks differ from plain networks in terms of information flow (Figures 2-4), showing that they learn data-dependent routing rather than static connections. This is a meaningful contribution to understanding deep network behavior.

## Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Omission of Critical Related Work**: The most significant weakness is the failure to cite and discuss ResNet (He et al., 2015), which was published around the same time and presented a very similar idea (identity mappings via skip connections) that became the dominant approach for training very deep networks. The paper mentions "a similar LSTM-inspired model was also proposed [32]" but doesn't acknowledge that ResNet was the influential work that built directly on this concept. This omission undermines the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides empirical evidence of effectiveness, it lacks a theoretical analysis explaining *why* highway networks work better for deep architectures. Why does the gating mechanism specifically address the vanishing gradient problem better than other approaches? This is a significant gap.

3. **Comparison to Contemporary Architectures**: The paper doesn't compare highway networks to ResNet or DenseNet (which also emerged around this time), which would have provided a more complete picture of where highway networks stand in the landscape of deep network architectures. The comparison to "Fitnets" is limited and doesn't address the most impactful recent work.

4. **Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't analyze the additional computational cost of the gating mechanism or how it scales with network depth. For practical applications, this is a crucial consideration.

5. **Theoretical Analysis of Depth**: The paper claims highway networks "do not suffer from increasing depth," but doesn't provide theoretical bounds on the maximum depth that can be trained without degradation. The empirical results are strong, but a theoretical foundation would strengthen the claims.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting and practical approach to training very deep networks, with strong empirical results that validate the core hypothesis. The highway network architecture is simple, effective, and offers valuable insights into information flow in deep networks. However, the failure to acknowledge ResNet (which was published in the same year) significantly undermines the novelty claim, as ResNet essentially implements the same conceptual idea with a simpler identity mapping (y = H(x) + x) rather than a gating mechanism.

The paper would be substantially stronger if it:
1. Added a thorough comparison with ResNet and explained the relationship between the two approaches
2. Provided a theoretical analysis of why highway networks work
3. Addressed the computational cost of the gating mechanism
4. Included a discussion of how highway networks relate to other recent deep network architectures

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

While the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with its empirical demonstration of training extremely deep networks, the omission of ResNet (which was published in 2015 and became the dominant approach for deep networks) is a major flaw. The highway network concept is interesting but appears to be a precursor to ResNet's simpler identity mapping approach, which later proved more effective. The paper would be more compelling with a clearer distinction from ResNet and a stronger theoretical foundation.

The authors should be encouraged to revise the paper to acknowledge ResNet, explain how highway networks differ from and relate to ResNet, and provide a more comprehensive theoretical analysis of why the gating mechanism helps with training very deep networks. With these revisions, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field.

This work represents an important step in the development of deep learning architectures, but as presented, it lacks sufficient novelty to stand out among the significant advances in deep network design that emerged in 2015. The empirical results are solid, but the paper's contribution is better understood as a precursor to ResNet rather than a standalone innovation.
