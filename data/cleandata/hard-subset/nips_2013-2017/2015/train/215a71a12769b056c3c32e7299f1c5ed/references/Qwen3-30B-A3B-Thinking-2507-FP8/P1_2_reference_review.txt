

# Peer Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces "highway networks" as a solution to the challenge of training extremely deep neural networks. The authors propose modifying feedforward networks with an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism (transform gate and carry gate) that creates "information highways" enabling stable training of networks with 100+ layers using standard SGD. The paper builds on previous work in deep learning initialization [16, 17], skip connections [22, 23], and distillation techniques [25], but introduces a novel architectural modification rather than just a training procedure.

The core innovation is the highway layer transformation: y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), where T is the transform gate. This allows the network to smoothly transition between transforming inputs (T=1) and simply passing inputs through (T=0). The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained to extreme depths (100+ layers) with significantly better performance than plain networks, while maintaining good generalization.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets. The authors provide thorough comparisons between highway and plain networks across varying depths, and their experimental setup is rigorous (e.g., 5 random runs for statistical significance). The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figures 2-4) provides valuable insights into how the highway architecture functions.

However, the paper lacks theoretical justification for why highway networks avoid the vanishing gradient problem. While the experimental results are convincing, a formal analysis of gradient flow would strengthen the claims. The authors acknowledge the need for negative bias initialization as a hyperparameter, but don't thoroughly explore how this affects generalization or whether it's truly necessary for all architectures.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation for the highway layer transformation. Figures 1-4 effectively illustrate key findings about network behavior and performance. However, some details could be improved:

- The relationship to ResNets (which were introduced around the same time) isn't discussed, despite being a very relevant related work.
- The paper mentions a similar LSTM-inspired model [32] but doesn't clarify how it differs from the proposed highway networks.
- Network architectures for CIFAR experiments aren't specified in sufficient detail for complete reproducibility.

## Originality
The highway network architecture represents a novel modification to feedforward networks that builds on LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms. While the paper acknowledges related work on skip connections and distillation, it clearly differentiates itself by introducing a dynamic gating mechanism rather than fixed skip connections.

The key original contribution is demonstrating that highway networks can be directly trained to extreme depths (100+ layers) without special techniques like distillation or two-stage training, which was previously required for networks of comparable size. This is a significant practical contribution to deep learning.

## Significance
The problem of training very deep networks is of fundamental importance in deep learning. The paper's results show that highway networks can train to extreme depths while maintaining or improving accuracy over plain networks, which has significant implications for network design. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can match or exceed the performance of wider, shallower networks with fewer parameters, which is valuable for practical applications.

The analysis of information routing (Figures 2-4) provides new insights into how deep networks can effectively utilize their depth, which is a contribution beyond just a new architecture. This could inform future work on understanding and designing deep networks.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Compelling empirical evidence that highway networks can be trained to extreme depths (100+ layers) with standard SGD
2. Thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets and network architectures
3. Insightful analysis of transform gate behavior and information routing
4. Clear distinction from related work on skip connections and distillation

**Weaknesses:**
1. Lacks comparison to ResNets, which were introduced around the same time and have become the dominant approach for deep networks
2. Insufficient theoretical justification for the proposed architecture
3. Doesn't clarify how this work differs from the recently proposed LSTM-inspired model [32]
4. Limited discussion of how highway networks might scale to even deeper architectures

## Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning by demonstrating a practical solution to training extremely deep networks. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened and comparisons to ResNets would be beneficial, the experimental results are convincing and the highway network architecture represents a meaningful advancement.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning with clear experimental validation. The results are significant for both researchers and practitioners, as they provide a practical approach to training deeper networks without requiring specialized training procedures.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper has strong technical quality, clear significance, and meaningful contributions to the field. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted (particularly regarding comparison to ResNets and clarification of relationship to [32]), this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
