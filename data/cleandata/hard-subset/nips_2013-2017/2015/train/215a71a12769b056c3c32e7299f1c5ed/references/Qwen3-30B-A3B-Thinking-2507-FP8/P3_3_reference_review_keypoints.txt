- Proposes Highway Networks with LSTM-like transform gates (y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x))) to enable training of very deep feedforward nets.  
- Empirically shows highway networks maintain performance up to 100+ layers on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 while plain networks fail.  
- Analyzes transform gate biases and activity patterns, demonstrating dynamic, data-dependent routing of information through layers.  
- Experiments are technically sound, well-executed, and reproducible with effective illustrative figures.  
- Provides only intuitive justification (Jacobian-based) for optimization benefits; lacks rigorous theoretical analysis of why the architecture helps.  
- Omits comparison to Residual Networks (ResNets), a major contemporaneous method that addresses the same problem with a simpler mechanism.  
- Writing is clear and well-organized, though the “simplified LSTM” reference could be better explained for clarity.  
- The adaptive gating idea is novel in feedforward networks and properly cites related LSTM and skip-connection work.  
- Offers a practical contribution by enabling extreme depths without complex initialization or staged training.  
- Impact is somewhat reduced by ResNet’s emergence; the paper would be stronger with explicit comparison and differentiation from ResNets.  
- Strengths: clear empirical advantages over plain nets, multi-dataset validation, insightful analysis of routing, and simple implementation.  
- Weaknesses: missing ResNet comparison, limited theoretical grounding, and minor clarity issues about the LSTM analogy.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, specifically to address comparison to ResNets in the final version.
