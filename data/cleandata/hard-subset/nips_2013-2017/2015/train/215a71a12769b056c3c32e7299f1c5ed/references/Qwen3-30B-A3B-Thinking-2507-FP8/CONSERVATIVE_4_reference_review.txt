

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper proposes "Highway Networks" as a method to train extremely deep feedforward networks by incorporating an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism. The authors claim this architecture enables direct training of networks with over 100 layers using standard SGD, unlike plain networks which suffer from performance degradation as depth increases. The paper compares highway networks to plain networks and Fitnets on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets, claiming highway networks achieve comparable or better performance with greater depth.

## Strengths
The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the highway network architecture, with a solid theoretical foundation for the gating mechanism. The experimental results on MNIST demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, unlike plain networks. The analysis of transform gate behavior (Section 4) provides interesting insights into how information flows through the network. The authors also thoughtfully address the potential concern that many layers might remain unused, demonstrating through layer lesioning that highway networks can learn to utilize their depth for complex tasks.

## Weaknesses

### Insufficient Comparison to Established Methods
The paper's most significant weakness is its inadequate comparison to the emerging Residual Networks (ResNets), which were published in the same year (2015) and solved the same problem with a simpler, more effective mechanism. The authors cite "skip connections" as prior work but fail to acknowledge that the ResNet approach (which uses a fixed identity connection rather than adaptive gating) has since become the dominant method for training very deep networks. The paper's claim that highway networks "can be directly trained using stochastic gradient descent" is misleading because ResNets achieve the same result with a simpler, more effective architecture. The comparison to Fitnets is also limited, as Fitnets were already superseded by ResNets when this paper was written.

### Limited Experimental Validation
The experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) with moderate network depths (max 100 layers). The paper does not demonstrate the method's effectiveness on larger-scale problems like ImageNet, which was the primary context for the "deep networks" research community at the time. The performance gains claimed (e.g., "2 orders of magnitude better" for 100-layer highway networks) lack context - it's unclear whether these results represent state-of-the-art performance on these datasets or merely an improvement over poorly initialized plain networks.

### Theoretical Justification
The paper provides limited theoretical justification for the highway mechanism. While the Jacobian analysis (Equation 5) is interesting, it doesn't explain why the highway architecture would be more effective than simpler alternatives like ResNets. The authors claim that highway networks "do not rely on specific non-linear transformations" and that "derivation of a suitable initialization scheme is not essential," but they don't provide evidence that this is actually true or that the highway architecture provides a fundamental advantage over simpler mechanisms.

### Overstated Claims
The paper overstates the significance of its contribution by implying it solves a fundamental problem in deep learning. However, the problem of training very deep networks was already being solved by ResNets, which were published in the same conference year. The paper's claim that "extremely deep highway networks can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent" is not novel, as ResNets demonstrated the same capability with a simpler approach. The paper also fails to address why the highway mechanism would be preferable to ResNets, which have since become the standard for deep network architectures.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the highway network architecture is theoretically interesting, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or superiority over existing methods, particularly ResNets which were published around the same time and have since become the dominant approach for training very deep networks. The experimental validation is limited to small datasets without comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art methods. The paper does not provide compelling evidence that highway networks represent a meaningful advancement over established techniques, which is a fundamental requirement for NIPS acceptance.

The paper's claims about "extremely deep networks" would have been more convincing if they had demonstrated performance on ImageNet or compared directly to ResNets, which were published within the same year. Without these critical comparisons, the paper appears to be an incremental modification to existing skip connection approaches rather than a significant contribution to the field. The field has already moved toward simpler, more effective solutions like ResNets, making the highway approach largely obsolete for practical applications.

The paper's main contribution appears to be an interesting theoretical exploration of gating mechanisms, but it does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these limitations, particularly by comparing to ResNets and demonstrating superior performance on larger-scale problems, before it would be suitable for NIPS.
