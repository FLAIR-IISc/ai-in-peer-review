Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces adaptive, elementwise gating—borrowed from LSTM ideas—to ease information and gradient flow across many layers. Each highway layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transformation (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate” whose complement carries the input forward. The key practical ingredient is initializing the gate bias negatively so the network starts biased toward carry behavior, enabling unobstructed information flow and making very deep nets trainable with plain SGD.

The work is well-situated within the literature on deep networks and optimization difficulties (vanishing/exploding gradients, initialization [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], second-order/advanced optimization [Martens, Dauphin et al.]), architectures encouraging information flow (skip connections, deep supervision, layerwise pretraining), and LSTM gating. It contrasts with recent FitNets (teacher-student distillation) by showing that thin, deep networks can be trained from scratch in a single stage with SGD if equipped with gates. It also relates to locally competitive activations/maxout and prior skip connections but emphasizes learnable, input-dependent routing rather than fixed shortcuts.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The proposed layer is simple, differentiable, and clearly defined. The analysis of Jacobians highlights the potential for unimpeded gradient flow when gates favor carry. Training guidance (negative gate biases) is sensible and empirically validated.
- Empirical support: The optimization study on MNIST with thin fully-connected networks across depths (10–100 layers) convincingly shows that plain networks degrade while highway networks maintain performance and converge faster. Classification experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 demonstrate competitive accuracy at comparable parameter/compute budgets to FitNets, trained in one stage. Additional analyses (gate bias evolution, activity patterns, per-class routing differences, and lesion studies) provide insight into how and where depth is used.
- Weaknesses in evaluation: Some comparisons and ablations are limited. There is no direct comparison to identity skip (ungated) shortcuts of equal capacity. The choice C=1−T is not ablated against a free carry gate C. Sensitivity to the gate bias hyperparameter is only partially explored. The reported CIFAR results use modest augmentation and reuse hyperparameters across CIFAR-10/100, which is conservative but may understate the achievable performance; however, the claim is primarily about trainability, not SOTA. Computational overhead (parameters/FLOPs) from the gating pathways is not thoroughly quantified.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The architectural definition, training recipe (including gate biasing), and experimental protocols are described succinctly. The visualization/lesion analyses are informative and help interpret the mechanism. It would help to include exact layer-wise shapes and parameter counts for each CIFAR architecture in the main text or appendix, and to quantify the overhead from T relative to H. Minor typos aside, the manuscript is readable, and code availability strengthens reproducibility.

Originality:
The core idea—LSTM-inspired gates for feedforward depth—is novel and distinct from prior skip connections and deep supervision. While skip connections have been used historically, the contribution here is a learnable, input-dependent gating mechanism that creates “information highways” and a practical initialization strategy that unlocks very deep training with standard SGD. The analysis of routing and layer importance is also original and insightful. Related work is cited appropriately.

Significance:
The contribution is important. Making very deep networks trainable with off-the-shelf optimizers without resorting to two-stage training/distillation can impact practice and spur deeper architectures across modalities (vision, speech, potentially sequence models with convolutional/recurrent H). The paper offers both a practical recipe and conceptual insights (data-dependent routing, effective depth measurement via lesioning). Even when not maximizing SOTA, the demonstrated ease of training and generalization at scale suggests that others will build on these ideas.

Strengths:
- Simple, general architectural modification enabling training of very deep networks with SGD.
- Clear, LSTM-motivated gating with effective negative-bias initialization.
- Rigorous optimization study showing robustness to depth (up to 100 layers) vs plain nets.
- Competitive CIFAR results at constrained budgets without teacher guidance.
- Insightful analyses: gate activity patterns, class-conditional routing, and lesioning to assess layer utility.
- Code release enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- Missing ablations: free carry gate C vs C=1−T; comparison to fixed/identity skip connections with equal parameter/FLOP budgets.
- Limited sensitivity analysis to gate bias and activation choices across datasets.
- Computational overhead (params/FLOPs/latency) of gates not quantified; no runtime comparisons.
- Experiments stop short of larger-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) where depth benefits are most salient.
- Some architectural/training details for CIFAR models are summarized rather than exhaustively specified in the paper.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add controlled comparisons to identity skip connections and to deep supervision under the same budgets to isolate the benefit of gating.
- Ablate C=1−T vs learned C and report effects on performance, trainability, and stability.
- Provide parameter/FLOP counts and measured training/inference times for all models; quantify overhead due to gates.
- Expand sensitivity analysis on gate bias initialization and discuss practical heuristics.
- Include at least a preliminary ImageNet experiment (even with reduced training) to support scalability claims.
- Report gradient norm statistics across layers to empirically confirm improved gradient flow.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive is performance to the initial gate bias across different depths and datasets? Are there failure modes?
- Did you try hard or soft constraints other than C=1−T (e.g., independent C with regularization)? Any stability issues?
- How do highway networks compare to ungated identity shortcuts with the same H, depth, and parameter budget?
- What is the computational overhead (parameters, memory, wall-clock) of adding T relative to H?
- Can the gating be structured (e.g., channel-wise vs element-wise) to trade accuracy/overhead?

Recommendation: Accept (strong). The paper offers a clear, novel architectural advance with practical impact and solid empirical evidence, along with useful analysis of how very deep models utilize depth. Confidence: High.
