Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that adds LSTM-inspired, elementwise gating—transform T(x) and carry C(x)=1−T(x)—to each layer: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)). This creates “information highways” that adaptively route activations and gradients through many layers with minimal attenuation, addressing depth-related optimization issues without resorting to complex pretraining or teacher-student schemes. A simple but effective initialization (negative biases for T) biases early training toward carry behavior to ease optimization, analogous to LSTM forget-gate biasing.

The work is clearly situated among efforts to train deeper nets: improved optimizers (Martens, Sutskever), variance-preserving inits (Glorot, He), local competition/maxout (Goodfellow), skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, FitNets/hints (Romero), and LSTM-style gating (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber). The paper also acknowledges concurrent LSTM-inspired models and related skip connections. Empirically, the authors show that (i) very deep fully connected highway nets (50–100 layers) optimize easily on MNIST compared to plain nets, and (ii) convolutional highway nets can be trained directly on CIFAR-10/100 in one stage, matching or surpassing comparable-capacity FitNets that require hint-based training. Analyses include transform-gate activity, data-dependent routing, and lesioning experiments to assess layer utility.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architectural formulation is simple and correct, with clear gradients and an interpretable interpolation between identity and H(x). The negative-bias gate initialization is well motivated by LSTM practice. The optimization experiments are carefully controlled (thin networks, matched per-layer parameter counts and normalized initialization for both plain and highway nets), and they support the main claim: depth severely hurts optimization for plain nets but not for highway nets. The CIFAR experiments demonstrate that single-stage training of deeper, thinner models is feasible and competitive.
- Empirical support: Results are convincing for optimization behavior and for competitive accuracy versus FitNets under similar parameter/compute budgets. The lesioning and gate-activity analyses provide valuable insight that the gates learn nontrivial routing and that layer importance depends on task complexity.
- Limitations: The study is limited to MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with relatively modest architectures; there is no large-scale (e.g., ImageNet) evaluation. Comparisons to other widely used mechanisms to ease training (e.g., Batch Normalization, simple residual/skip connections without gates) are missing, so it is hard to disentangle how much of the gain comes from gating versus skip pathways per se. The decision to tie C=1−T is reasonable but not ablated; independent carry/transform gates might matter. The compute/memory overhead of gating (extra parameters and elementwise multiplies) versus plain or residual layers is not quantified. Some hyperparameter selection relied on random search with “best” curves emphasized; while means/stds are sometimes reported, broader sensitivity analyses would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The formulation and intuition are easy to follow, and the analysis figures (gate biases, activities, lesioning) substantiate the narrative. A few details could be clearer for reproducibility: full architectural specifications per experiment (exact filter counts per layer, padding/stride), precise training schedules per dataset, and explicit parameter/compute counts for all baselines. Minor typos exist but do not impede understanding. Making the C=1−T design choice and its implications more explicit would help.

Originality
The key novelty is integrating LSTM-like gates into deep feedforward layers to build adaptive, learnable skip paths. While skip connections and LSTM gating are known, their combination in a general feedforward/convolutional setting and the systematic demonstration that this enables direct SGD training of 50–100+ layer nets is original at the time. The data-dependent routing and lesioning analyses are also insightful. The paper acknowledges related/concurrent ideas.

Significance
Training very deep networks reliably is a central challenge. This work offers a practical architecture that:
- Improves optimization sharply with depth, enabling 50–100+ layers trained end-to-end with vanilla SGD.
- Avoids two-stage teacher-student procedures for thin, deep convnets, matching or improving FitNet accuracy under comparable budgets.
- Provides a mechanism for input-dependent routing, and a way to assess layer utility.
These contributions are likely to influence architectural design and inspire subsequent gated/skip variants. The absence of large-scale experiments and comparisons to batch norm and simple residual connections limits the immediate empirical impact, but the idea is important and broadly useful.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated architectural change with strong intuition and clear gradients.
- Convincing optimization evidence: deep highway nets train easily where plain nets fail.
- Competitive CIFAR results without distillation; thin 32-layer conv highways feasible with single-stage training.
- Insightful analyses (gate activity, class-conditional routing, lesioning) that shed light on depth utilization.
- Practical guidance for gate-bias initialization; code availability aids reproducibility.

Cons
- No comparison to batch normalization or to ungated residual/skip connections; unclear relative contributions of gating vs skipping.
- No large-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet), so generality to more challenging regimes is untested.
- Limited ablations: tying C=1−T, gate bias magnitude, alternative H activations, and computational overhead not systematically evaluated.
- Some reporting emphasizes best runs; more comprehensive statistics would strengthen claims.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines with: (a) identity skip connections without gates (residual-style) and (b) batch normalization, to isolate the benefit of gating.
- Report parameter and FLOP counts and training wall-clock for all models; quantify overhead of gating.
- Ablate C=1−T vs independent gates; study different gate initializations and activations.
- Provide gradient norm/activation propagation diagnostics to directly test the “information highway” hypothesis.
- Expand to at least one larger-scale dataset or a deeper/wider CIFAR architecture to demonstrate robustness.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a clear, original architectural idea with solid evidence that it materially improves the trainability of very deep networks, plus thoughtful analyses of how and when depth is used. While additional baselines and larger-scale experiments would strengthen the case, the contribution is timely and significant.

Confidence: 4/5
