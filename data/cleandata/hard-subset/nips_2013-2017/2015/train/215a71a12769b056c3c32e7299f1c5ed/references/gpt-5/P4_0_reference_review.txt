Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that incorporates adaptive, LSTM-inspired gates to facilitate information and gradient flow across many layers. Each highway layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transformation (e.g., affine + nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate.” With negative gate bias initialization, the network is initially biased toward the carry path, providing near-identity information flow across depth and easing optimization. This design is motivated by the success of LSTM gating in recurrent nets and relates to prior skip connections and deep supervision, but differs by using learned, input-dependent multiplicative gates for routing. The paper empirically demonstrates that very deep (50–100+) highway networks train with plain SGD, whereas similarly parameterized plain networks become hard to optimize despite modern initializations. On CIFAR-10/100, highway networks match or surpass FitNets under comparable parameter and compute budgets without hint-based training. Analyses of gate activations suggest data-dependent routing and selective use of depth; lesion studies show layer importance varies by dataset complexity. This work builds on and compares with normalization-based initialization for deep nets [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], locally competitive networks, FitNets (hint-based training), and skip/error injection connections, positioning highway networks as a general mechanism to mitigate optimization difficulties due to depth.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The layer formulation is clear, differentiable, and justified: when T≈0 the layer approximates identity, enabling unobstructed forward and backward signal flow; when T≈1 it behaves like a standard layer. While the intuition is strong, the paper lacks formal analysis (e.g., bounds on gradient norms across depth with stochastic gates), and conditions under which gates avoid saturation are not theoretically characterized.
- Empirical validation: The optimization study is careful in controlling capacity (comparable parameters per layer), using random search for hyperparameters on MNIST and showing clear depth-dependent degradation for plain nets vs. stable performance for highway nets up to 100 layers. CIFAR experiments compare favorably to FitNets under similar budgets without teacher hints. The analysis of gate behavior and lesioning offers informative diagnostics. However, comparisons omit strong contemporaneous baselines that also ease deep training (e.g., Batch Normalization), and large-scale evaluations (e.g., ImageNet) are absent. Reporting of compute/FLOPs and exact parameter counts for each comparison would improve rigor.
- Completeness and honesty: Limitations are partially acknowledged (e.g., not optimizing architectures or hyperparameters for CIFAR-100, simplified block design). The claim of successful pilot training beyond 1000 layers is not backed by results presented in the paper.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The highway layer and training scheme are clearly specified, with practical initialization guidance for gate biases. Figures for gate statistics and lesioning are informative. A few issues could be improved:
- Expand on convolutional gate implementation details (per-channel vs. per-spatial-location gating; use of 1×1 vs. same receptive field as H; parameter and compute overhead).
- Provide complete equations for Jacobians and discuss the gradient contributions from the carry path explicitly.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “varying varying”), and ensure all figures/tables report exact parameter and operation counts for fair comparisons.

Originality
The core idea—learned, input-dependent gating to control per-layer transform vs. carry—is a novel adaptation of LSTM gating to the depth dimension of feedforward nets. While skip connections and deep supervision are related, they are static; FitNets introduce training hints rather than architectural mechanisms. The work is distinct in proposing a general, modular gating mechanism that can be paired with arbitrary H (fully connected or convolutional), with a simple biasing scheme enabling very deep training.

Significance
The contribution addresses a central challenge—training very deep networks—offering a simple, general solution with clear empirical benefits. The method enables training at depths where plain nets fail and obviates teacher-based multi-stage training under constrained budgets. The analyses provide insights into dynamic routing and effective depth utilization. The impact would be stronger with comparisons to other depth-easing techniques (e.g., BatchNorm), larger-scale datasets, and ablations (e.g., independent carry gate vs. C=1−T). Nonetheless, the work is likely to influence architectures and training practices for deep networks.

Pros
- Clear, general architectural mechanism enabling very deep training with plain SGD.
- Strong optimization study: depth scaling where plain nets fail.
- Competitive CIFAR-10/100 results under constrained budgets without teacher hints.
- Insightful gate and lesion analyses demonstrating data-dependent routing and layer importance.
- Practical initialization guidance; code availability aids reproducibility.

Cons
- No theoretical guarantees or quantitative gradient-flow analysis.
- Missing comparisons to Batch Normalization and other contemporaneous deep training techniques.
- Limited large-scale validation; reliance on MNIST and CIFAR.
- Incomplete reporting of compute/parameter overhead due to gates; fairness of some comparisons could be clearer.
- Gate design choice C=1−T not ablated vs. independent C and T.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide FLOPs and parameter counts for H and T separately, and total overhead vs. plain/FitNets.
- Compare against plain deep nets with BatchNorm and/or careful initialization on CIFAR, and ideally on a larger dataset.
- Ablate C=1−T vs. independent carry/transform gates; study the effect on optimization and accuracy.
- Report gradient norm statistics across depth to substantiate the “information highway” claim.
- Analyze sensitivity to gate bias initialization and learning rate schedules; provide guidance beyond heuristic values.
- Clarify gating granularity in convolutional layers (per-channel vs. per-pixel) and its impact on performance/compute.

Scope
The paper is squarely within NeurIPS topics (deep learning architectures, optimization, representation learning).

Overall assessment
This is a technically sound and novel architectural contribution with clear empirical evidence that it simplifies training of very deep networks and offers competitive performance under constrained budgets. Strengthening comparisons to other deep training techniques and expanding empirical scope would further solidify its significance. The paper makes a meaningful scientific contribution and is suitable for NeurIPS.
