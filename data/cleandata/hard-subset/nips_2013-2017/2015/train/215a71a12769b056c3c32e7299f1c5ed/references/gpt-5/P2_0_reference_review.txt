Summary
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that augments each layer with element-wise gates that blend between a nonlinear transform H(x) and the identity x: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)). The transform gate T is a sigmoid with a negative bias initialization to favor carry behavior at the start of training. This LSTM-inspired mechanism creates “information highways” that preserve activations and gradients across many layers, aiming to remove depth-related optimization barriers. The authors show:
- Optimization studies on thin MNIST MLPs: plain nets degrade rapidly with depth, while 50–100 layer highway nets train reliably and faster.
- Classification results on MNIST, CIFAR-10/100 with convolutional highway layers, including comparisons to FitNets, showing comparable or better accuracy trained in a single stage without a teacher.
- Analyses of gate biases/activities and lesion experiments that suggest data-dependent routing and that deeper problems (CIFAR-100) utilize more layers than simpler ones (MNIST).

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely inspired by LSTM gating (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber) and joins a line of work on depth and optimization: normalized/variance-preserving initialization (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.), improved optimizers (Martens; Dauphin et al.), competitive activations/maxout (Goodfellow et al.; Srivastava et al.), and skip connections/deep supervision (Raiko et al.; Lee et al.). FitNets (Romero et al.) mitigate training difficulty via distillation. The novelty here is to introduce per-unit, input-dependent gates that learn to balance identity vs. transform pathways in deep feedforward networks, rather than fixed shortcuts or external supervision.

Evaluation against criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is well-defined, with clear gating equations and a simple, effective initialization (negative gate bias). The use of C = 1 − T ensures dimensional consistency and stability.
- Empirical support: The optimization study is carefully controlled for parameter count per layer and uses random search to choose hyperparameters, showing a marked gap in trainability between plain and highway nets as depth increases. The vision experiments show competitive test accuracy without resorting to multi-stage training or heavy augmentation.
- Analysis: Gate activity visualizations and layer lesioning provide insight into dynamic routing and effective depth usage.
- Caveats: Some key ablations are missing: (i) comparison to identity skip connections without learned gates (a very strong and relevant baseline), (ii) decoupling C from 1 − T to test whether tied gates are essential, (iii) sensitivity to the transform gate bias (what happens with zero/positive bias), and (iv) comparisons to batch normalization or other modern training stabilizers. Claims of >1000-layer training are anecdotal (no results shown). Computational/parameter overhead from gates is not quantified.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The core idea, equations, and training protocol are easy to follow. The initialization guidance is practical.
- Some experimental details are terse (e.g., full CIFAR architectures, exact schedules), though the claim of releasing code/hyperparameters helps reproducibility.
- The analysis section is informative and strengthens understanding of what the gates learn.

Originality
- The key contribution—LSTM-like gating to create adaptive, learnable identity paths in very deep feedforward/convolutional nets—is a novel combination of ideas at the time of writing. Unlike earlier skip/shortcut connections, the routing is input-dependent and learned at a fine granularity.
- The lesion and routing analyses are also original and thought-provoking.

Significance
- Tackling the optimization barrier for very deep networks is a central problem; providing a simple architectural mechanism that allows direct SGD training of 50–100+ layer networks is impactful.
- The practical benefit (single-stage training, competitive accuracy without a teacher) increases the likelihood of adoption.
- The idea is broadly applicable (fully-connected, convolutional, potentially recurrent).
- While not claiming state-of-the-art, the results are strong enough to show viability and likely to spur follow-up work.

Pros
- Clear, simple architectural idea with strong intuition and practical initialization.
- Convincing optimization evidence that depth no longer harms trainability.
- Competitive CIFAR results trained in one stage; avoids reliance on distillation.
- Insightful analyses (gating patterns, lesioning) suggesting data-dependent routing and adaptive depth usage.
- Code and hyperparameters reportedly available.

Cons
- Missing critical baselines: identity skip connections without gating; batch normalization; ablations on tying C = 1 − T and on gate bias sensitivity.
- Limited datasets (MNIST/CIFAR) and modest augmentation; unclear performance on larger-scale tasks.
- Computational/parameter overhead of gates is not analyzed.
- Some claims (e.g., >1000 layers) are not substantiated with empirical results.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a baseline with fixed identity shortcuts (no gates) to separate the benefits of gating vs. skip connections.
- Include ablations: untie C and T; sweep initial gate bias including zero/positive; report training stability/accuracy.
- Quantify parameter and FLOP overhead from gates, and measure wall-clock training speed vs. baselines.
- Compare against batch normalization and/or other contemporary depth-enabling methods under matched capacity/compute.
- Provide fuller architectural details in the main text or appendix for reproducibility.

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper: technically sound, clearly written, novel, and significant. It presents a compelling solution to training very deep networks and provides both empirical evidence and insightful analysis. The main weaknesses are the absence of contemporary baselines and some missing ablations. Nevertheless, the contribution stands on its own and is likely to influence subsequent architectures.

Recommendation: Accept.
