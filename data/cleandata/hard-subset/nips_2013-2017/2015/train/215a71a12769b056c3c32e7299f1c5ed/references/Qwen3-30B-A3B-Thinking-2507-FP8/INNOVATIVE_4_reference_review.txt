

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel architecture for training extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The key innovation is an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism that allows information to flow across layers without attenuation. The authors propose a layer transformation equation y = H(x)·T + x·(1-T), where T is a transform gate that controls the balance between transforming input (via H) and carrying it through. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly using SGD even with over 100 layers, while plain networks suffer from vanishing gradients and poor optimization as depth increases.

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of the highway network architecture, positioning it as a bold departure from previous approaches to deep network training. The authors convincingly demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, unlike plain networks, and provide insightful analysis of information routing through the network. The experimental results on MNIST and CIFAR datasets show that highway networks can match or exceed the performance of shallower networks while being significantly deeper.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** The highway network concept represents a genuinely new direction in deep learning architecture design. The idea of using adaptive gating mechanisms to control information flow across layers is inspired by LSTMs but applied in a novel way to feedforward networks, addressing a fundamental challenge in deep learning.

2. **Bold Approach:** The authors tackle the "very deep networks" problem head-on with a simple yet powerful architectural innovation. The paper demonstrates that networks with 100+ layers can be trained directly using standard SGD, which is a significant conceptual advance.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence through rigorous optimization experiments, ablation studies (e.g., lesioning experiments in Figure 4), and analysis of information routing (Figure 2-3). The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of the highway architecture.

4. **Insightful Analysis:** The paper offers valuable insights into how information flows through highway networks, revealing that different layers are utilized for different inputs, which suggests the gating mechanism serves as a dynamic routing system rather than just a training aid.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparison to ResNets:** The most significant weakness is the paper's failure to acknowledge or compare with Residual Networks (ResNets), which were published in the same year (2015) and have since become the dominant approach for training very deep networks. ResNets use a simpler skip connection (y = H(x) + x) that achieves similar information flow properties. The paper cites works from 2015 but misses the most relevant contemporaneous work. This oversight significantly undermines the novelty claim and the paper's significance.

2. **Incomplete Methodological Comparison:** While the paper compares highway networks to plain networks, it does not provide a comprehensive comparison to other modern approaches for training deep networks (e.g., batch normalization, residual connections). This limits the paper's ability to establish the true contribution.

3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Impact:** The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained deeper, but doesn't clearly establish why this matters for practical applications. It would have been stronger to demonstrate that highway networks achieve better results on standard benchmarks, rather than just matching shallower networks.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and bold approach to training very deep networks, with a clear theoretical foundation and strong experimental validation. The highway network concept is genuinely innovative and addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning. However, the failure to acknowledge and compare with ResNets (which were published in the same year) significantly diminishes the paper's novelty claim and impact. While the highway network idea is interesting, ResNets (with their simpler formulation and superior empirical results) have since become the standard approach.

Given my preference for novelty and bold approaches, I would have been inclined to accept this paper if it had properly positioned itself against the emerging ResNet approach. However, the lack of comparison to ResNets is a critical flaw in a field where the race to train deeper networks was already well underway in 2015. The paper's contribution would have been more significant had it acknowledged this prior work and positioned highway networks as a complementary or alternative approach to ResNets.

**Recommendation: Rejection**

While the highway network concept is novel, the paper's failure to engage with the most relevant contemporaneous work (ResNets) makes it difficult to assess its true contribution. The paper would need to be substantially revised to include this comparison before it could be considered for publication. Without this crucial context, the paper appears to be an incremental contribution rather than a significant new direction, despite its bold claims. The field has since moved to ResNets, which outperform highway networks in practice, making this paper's contribution less significant than it initially appears.
