

# Peer Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors propose an LSTM-inspired adaptive gating mechanism that controls information flow across layers, creating "information highways" where information can propagate without attenuation. The key innovation is the transform gate (T) and carry gate (C=1-T) that allow each layer to smoothly transition between transforming input and simply carrying it forward. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained with 100+ layers without the typical degradation seen in plain networks, and that they generalize well to unseen data. The work builds on previous ideas like skip connections and LSTM gating mechanisms but introduces a novel, adaptive approach to information routing in deep feedforward networks.

The paper relates well to prior work at NIPS, particularly the LSTM architecture [29,30] and recent advances in deep network training [16,17,20,25]. It distinguishes itself from previous skip connection approaches by introducing a learnable, adaptive gating mechanism rather than fixed shortcut connections.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a well-designed experimental setup. The authors conduct thorough experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, comparing highway networks to plain networks with normalized initialization. The results consistently demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly. The analysis of gate behavior (Figure 2) and layer importance (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how information flows through these networks. The paper also thoughtfully addresses potential limitations (e.g., showing that unused layers don't hurt performance).

**Originality:** The highway network architecture represents a novel approach to addressing the challenges of training very deep networks. While building on previous work with skip connections and LSTM gating, the authors introduce a learnable, adaptive gating mechanism that fundamentally differs from fixed skip connections. The concept of "information highways" is a compelling and original way to conceptualize information flow in deep networks. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in the introduction and discussion.

**Significance:** The ability to train extremely deep networks (100+ layers) directly with SGD is a significant contribution to deep learning. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can match or exceed the performance of shallower, wider networks with fewer parameters, which is particularly valuable for resource-constrained applications. The insights about information routing and layer importance provide fundamental understanding that could inform future network design.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the experimental results are strong, the paper lacks rigorous theoretical analysis to explain why highway networks overcome the vanishing gradient problem. The paper references the Jacobian analysis (Equation 5) but doesn't fully leverage it to provide deeper theoretical insights into the training dynamics of highway networks.

**Comparison to Contemporary Work:** The paper was written before ResNets [36], but it doesn't adequately address how highway networks relate to or differ from later skip connection approaches. The paper's comparison to "Fitnets" [25] is good, but it could have included more comprehensive comparisons to other contemporary deep network approaches at the time of submission.

**Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze the computational cost of the additional gating mechanism. While the authors mention that highway networks can be trained with fewer parameters than shallower networks, they don't quantify the exact overhead of the gating mechanism or how it affects inference time.

**Limited Exploration of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on the success of highway networks but doesn't systematically explore scenarios where they might fail or where plain networks might outperform them. A more balanced discussion of limitations would strengthen the paper.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong contribution to deep learning that addresses a fundamental challenge in training very deep networks. The highway network architecture is novel, well-motivated, and supported by convincing experimental results. The paper provides valuable insights into information flow in deep networks and demonstrates that extremely deep networks can be trained directly with SGD. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened and the paper could better differentiate itself from later work (though this was not possible at the time of submission), the experimental results are robust and the approach is significant.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It is technically sound, clearly written, original, and addresses a significant problem with demonstrable impact on the field. The authors have clearly established the novelty of their approach through thorough comparisons with previous work.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to deep learning by introducing highway networks, which enable direct training of extremely deep networks. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, and the insights into information routing are valuable for the field. While there are minor weaknesses in theoretical analysis and comparative evaluation, these do not undermine the substantial contribution of the paper.

The work has already been influential in the field, with the highway network concept paving the way for later innovations like ResNets, which share the fundamental insight of enabling information flow through skip connections. This paper represents an important step toward the development of modern deep learning architectures.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. The authors could add a brief discussion comparing highway networks to ResNets (though they were published later) to help contextualize their contribution within the broader timeline of deep network research.

2. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the gating mechanism would be valuable for practitioners.

3. The paper could include a small set of experiments showing where highway networks might be less effective compared to plain networks, to provide a more balanced evaluation.

4. The theoretical analysis could be strengthened with additional mathematical insights about why the adaptive gating mechanism prevents vanishing gradients.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to deep learning research and addresses a fundamental problem in the field.
