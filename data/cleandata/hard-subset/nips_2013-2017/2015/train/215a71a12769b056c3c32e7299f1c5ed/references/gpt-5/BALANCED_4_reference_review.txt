Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture inspired by LSTM gating. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where T is a learned transform gate (sigmoid) and the residual “carry” path is 1 − T. With negative bias initialization for T, the network is initially biased to carry information forward, creating “information highways” that preserve activations and gradients across many layers and simplify optimization. The authors show that very deep networks (50–100+ layers) can be trained by plain SGD without specialized pretraining or distillation. Experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 indicate both improved optimization with depth and competitive generalization under modest augmentation. Analyses include gate activity visualizations and layer-lesion studies indicating input-dependent routing and that deeper layers contribute more on harder tasks.

This advances prior ideas on easing gradient flow and credit assignment: (1) it borrows adaptive gating from LSTMs [29,30] rather than fixed skip connections [22,23], (2) it obviates multi-stage “hint-based” training (FitNets [25]) for thin, deep models, and (3) it complements variance-preserving initialization [16,17] and locally competitive activations (maxout/ReLU) [20,21]. Compared to earlier skip/shortcut methods and deep supervision [24], the main novelty is per-neuron, input-conditioned gates that blend transform vs carry.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple architectural idea with strong intuition (carry paths for gradient and activation flow), grounded in LSTM precedent.
- Compelling optimization evidence: plain nets degrade badly with depth while highway nets train well up to 100 layers; convergence is faster.
- Practical recipe (negative gate bias) that is architecture-agnostic and works with standard SGD.
- Competitive results on CIFAR-10/100 under modest augmentation; single-stage training matches two-stage FitNet baselines at similar parameter/compute budgets.
- Insightful analyses: gate activity patterns, class-conditional routing, and lesion studies that probe effective depth and layer utility.
- Code and hyperparameter details are made available, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical scope is limited to MNIST and CIFAR-10/100; no large-scale benchmark (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability.
- Missing ablations disentangling contributions: How much of the gain comes from gating vs extra parameters vs identity shortcuts? A direct comparison to an ungated skip/identity baseline of matched capacity is missing.
- Limited exploration of initialization and training design space (e.g., batch normalization, alternative T parameterizations, separate carry gate C ≠ 1 − T).
- Comparisons to state of the art are modest; under stronger augmentation, results may lag top-performing models.
- Computational cost is not fully quantified: gate overhead per layer, memory footprint, and training speed vs plain/skip baselines.

Quality:
Technically sound with a coherent formulation. The negative-bias heuristic is reasonable and empirically validated. The optimization experiments are carefully controlled per-layer parameter counts, and the analyses are thoughtful. However, stronger ablations are needed: (i) highway vs identity-skip (no gates) with matched parameters and identical training; (ii) effect of gate bias values and schedules; (iii) using separate carry gate C to test whether 1 − T is essential; (iv) gradient-norm diagnostics across depth. Claims of training “>1000 layers” are anecdotal; including even a small controlled study would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized. The core equation and intuition are clear. Some architectural/training details are underspecified (exact layer widths, receptive fields, FLOPs/parameters per model in CIFAR comparisons, learning-rate schedules), making it harder to fully assess fairness and reproducibility from the paper alone (the code mitigates this).

Originality:
Introducing LSTM-like gates into feedforward layers for depth scaling is novel and distinct from prior fixed skip connections and deep supervision. The framing as input-dependent routing is interesting and supported by analyses. The idea is a natural but nontrivial adaptation of recurrent gating to deep feedforward nets.

Significance:
Potentially high: simplifying the training of very deep networks is impactful and widely relevant. If validated at larger scale and across modalities (e.g., speech, NLP, sequence models), this could influence mainstream practice. Current empirical evidence is promising but not yet definitive, as it lacks large-scale benchmarks and stronger baselines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add direct comparisons to identity/residual skip connections without gates, matched in parameter count, training scheme, and depth, to isolate the value of gating vs skipping.
- Provide compute metrics (parameters and FLOPs) and wall-clock to contextualize the gating overhead and convergence benefits.
- Expand ablations: (a) separate carry gate C; (b) different initializations for bT and schedules; (c) with/without batch normalization; (d) different H (e.g., tanh vs ReLU vs maxout).
- Include gradient flow diagnostics (activation/gradient norms across layers) to substantiate the optimization claims.
- Evaluate on a larger dataset (even a modest-scale ImageNet-32/64 or a reduced ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability.
- Clarify architectural details and hyperparameters in the main text (not only supplementary/code), to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Elegant, general architectural mechanism that materially eases optimization of very deep nets.
- Solid empirical evidence on multiple datasets and insightful analyses of gating behavior.
- Practical, easy-to-implement method with public code.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical scope; no large-scale demonstration and incomplete baselines against fixed skip connections.
- Missing ablations that isolate which components are essential.
- Modest performance relative to state of the art under strong augmentation.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is clean and promising, the optimization benefits are convincing on standard datasets, and the analyses are insightful. With stronger ablations and a larger-scale evaluation, this could be a clear accept.
