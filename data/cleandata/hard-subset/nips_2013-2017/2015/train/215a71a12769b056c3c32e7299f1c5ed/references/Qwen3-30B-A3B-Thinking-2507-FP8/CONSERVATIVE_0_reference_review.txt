

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper proposes "highway networks," a modification to standard deep neural networks that introduces an adaptive gating mechanism inspired by LSTMs. The authors claim that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD even at extreme depths (100+ layers), unlike plain networks that become increasingly difficult to optimize as depth increases. The key innovation is a transform gate that controls how much information is transformed versus carried through each layer, creating "information highways" that allow gradients to flow more easily.

The paper presents experimental results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 showing that highway networks achieve better performance than plain networks at greater depths. The authors also compare against Fitnets (a two-stage training approach) and report that highway networks can achieve similar or better results in a single training stage. The paper includes analysis of gate behavior, information routing, and layer importance, suggesting highway networks learn to dynamically route information based on input.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides thorough experimental validation on multiple datasets.
- The gating mechanism is theoretically well-motivated and the Jacobian analysis in Section 2.1 provides useful insights.
- The experimental design for the optimization study (Section 3.1) is rigorous, with multiple runs and hyperparameter search.
- The analysis of gate behavior (Section 4) offers interesting insights into how highway networks process information.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to established methods:** The paper fails to compare against ResNets (which were published in the same year, 2015, and use a simpler identity shortcut connection approach). ResNets have since become the standard for training very deep networks, and the paper's "highway" approach appears to be a more complex version of the same concept. The authors cite [22, 23, 2, 24] as related work on skip connections but don't acknowledge or compare to ResNets, which was a major contribution to the field. This omission significantly undermines the novelty claim and the paper's significance.

2. **Insufficient evidence for "extreme depth" claims:** The paper states "in pilot experiments, SGD did not stall for networks with more than 1000 layers," but provides no experimental evidence to support this claim. The main experiments only go up to 100 layers, which is not "extreme" by modern standards. The claim that highway networks can train networks with "more than 1000 layers" lacks supporting evidence.

3. **Questionable novelty:** The highway network's gating mechanism is essentially a more complex version of the skip connections used in ResNets, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how it differs from or improves upon ResNets. The paper cites [32] as a similar LSTM-inspired model but doesn't discuss how their approach compares to this work or to ResNets.

4. **Lack of theoretical justification:** While the Jacobian analysis provides some insight, the paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis explaining why highway networks overcome the vanishing gradient problem. The connection to LSTM theory is somewhat superficial.

5. **Inadequate baseline comparison:** The comparison to Fitnets (Section 3.3.1) is limited to Fitnet A and B, but doesn't include more recent state-of-the-art methods or provide a comprehensive comparison to the simplest possible baseline (ResNets).

6. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims highway networks "yield highway networks, as opposed to traditional 'plain' networks," but doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this is a significant improvement over the simpler skip connections in ResNets, which have since become the dominant approach for training very deep networks.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting architectural modification to deep neural networks, but its significance is substantially undermined by the lack of comparison to ResNets, which were published around the same time and have since become the standard approach for training very deep networks. The claim of being able to train networks with "more than 1000 layers" is not substantiated by the experimental evidence provided. The paper's contribution appears to be a more complex implementation of a concept that was later simplified and made more effective by ResNets.

While the experimental results are generally sound, the paper fails to position itself against the most relevant prior work in the field. The authors should have acknowledged ResNets (or at least provided a comparison to them) to properly contextualize their contribution. Without this, the paper reads as a slightly different approach to the same problem that ResNets solved more elegantly.

**Recommendation:** Rejected. The paper's contribution is not sufficiently novel or significant compared to the standard approach (ResNets) that has since become dominant in the field. The experimental evidence does not adequately support the claims made, particularly regarding the ability to train networks with "extreme" depths. The paper would be significantly stronger if it had compared against ResNets and provided a more rigorous theoretical foundation for the highway mechanism. As it stands, the paper fails to demonstrate a clear advancement over existing approaches.
