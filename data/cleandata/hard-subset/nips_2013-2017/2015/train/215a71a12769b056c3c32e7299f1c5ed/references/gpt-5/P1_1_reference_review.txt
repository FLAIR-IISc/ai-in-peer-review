Summary
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture inspired by LSTM gating that facilitates information flow across many layers. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution) and T is a learnable transform gate (sigmoid). Setting the carry gate C = 1 − T ensures dimensional compatibility and yields a continuum between identity mapping and full transformation. A simple initialization—negative bias for T—biases layers to initially carry inputs, easing optimization. The authors show that very deep networks (50–100 fully connected layers; 10–30 conv layers) can be trained directly with SGD and that highway networks both optimize faster and generalize competitively on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, without teacher-student distillation. Analyses visualize gate activity, suggest data-dependent routing, and use lesion experiments to quantify layer importance.

Relation to prior work
The work is well-situated among research on training deep nets: improved optimizers and initialization [13–19], activation functions (ReLU, Maxout) [16,20], skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision [22–24], knowledge distillation/fitnets [25], and LSTM gating [29–31]. The proposed layer reduces to identity (T≈0) or H (T≈1), connecting to classic skip connections while emphasizing adaptive, input-dependent routing via multiplicative gates. Compared to prior thin/deep networks requiring hint-based training [25], highway networks train in a single stage while achieving comparable or better accuracy under similar parameter budgets.

Strengths
- Clear architectural contribution: a simple, general, and easily implemented gating mechanism that turns depth from a liability into an asset.
- Strong optimization evidence: with matched parameter counts per layer, highway nets maintain trainability as depth grows (50/100-layer MLPs), while plain nets degrade severely; they also converge faster.
- Practical recipe: gate parameterization and negative bias initialization are simple, effective, and architecture-agnostic.
- Generalization: competitive results on CIFAR-10/100 with modest augmentation and without teacher supervision, matching or surpassing FitNet baselines under similar budgets.
- Insightful analyses: visualization of gate biases/activities, class-conditional routing patterns, and layer lesioning quantify how much depth is used per dataset.

Weaknesses
- Missing baselines: no comparison to batch normalization (widely effective by now) or to ungated skip/residual connections that simply add x (fixed skip vs gated skip), making it hard to isolate the value of gating per se.
- Limited scale: experiments are primarily MNIST and CIFAR; the claim of >1000-layer trainability is only anecdotal. No ImageNet-scale validation.
- Ablations: few quantitative studies on the role of C=1−T vs independent C, sensitivity to T bias, cost-benefit of gating parameters, or the effect of using 1×1 bottlenecks.
- Clarity/details: some architecture descriptions are terse (exact shapes/parameter counts, training schedules per experiment), and the “more complex block” that worked in pilots is mentioned but not reported.
- Fairness: FitNet comparisons are useful but could be complemented with contemporary, strong fully convolutional baselines (e.g., All-CNN) under the same augmentation and compute budgets.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully executed. The optimization study is particularly convincing, with matched capacity and random hyperparameter search. Claims about generalization are supported on standard datasets with multiple runs. However, the absence of BN and residual-like baselines leaves a gap in causal attribution: is the gain due to gating, to carrying identity paths, or both? More ablations would strengthen the causal story. Complexity (time/memory) overhead of gates is not quantified.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The intuition for gradient flow via identity paths is clear; equations and notation are straightforward. Suggestions: clearly define “block” vs “unit” consistently; tabulate per-architecture layer shapes and parameter counts; include training schedules (λ, γ, epochs) in the main text; explicitly report the distribution of T biases used and sensitivity curves.

Originality
Highway layers bring LSTM-like gating to depth-wise information flow in feedforward nets. While skip connections existed, the per-unit, input-dependent gating that interpolates between identity and transform is novel and conceptually elegant. The routing/lesion analyses are a nice addition.

Significance
Significant. The method addresses a central obstacle—training very deep nets—with a general-purpose layer usable across MLPs and CNNs. The ability to train deep, thin models without teacher supervision is practically relevant, and the analysis suggests broader applicability (dynamic routing). Even without state-of-the-art CIFAR numbers, the contribution is architectural and enabling, likely to influence subsequent designs.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please compare to: (a) plain skip connections (y = H(x) + x) without gates; (b) batch normalization + deep plain networks. This will isolate the benefit of gating.
- Provide ablations on: independent carry gate C vs C=1−T; sensitivity to initial gate bias; fixing T to a constant; measuring gradient norms across depth.
- Report compute/memory overhead due to gating and any wall-clock speed impact.
- Clarify whether the same T shares parameters across spatial locations in conv layers (it appears convolutional), and whether 1×1 gates are used.
- Consider an ImageNet-scale result or at least deeper convs (>30 layers) to substantiate scalability beyond MNIST/CIFAR.

Pros
- Elegant, general architecture enabling training of very deep networks.
- Strong optimization evidence and competitive generalization under fair budgets.
- Simple, reproducible recipe and insightful analyses (routing, lesioning).

Cons
- Missing key baselines (BN, ungated residual) and ablations to pinpoint the source of improvements.
- Limited scale and scope of experiments; no large-scale image benchmarks.
- Some implementation/training details could be clearer.

Recommendation
Accept. The architectural idea is original and important, the empirical evidence for improved trainability is compelling, and the work is likely to spur follow-up research and practical adoption. With stronger baselines/ablations (ideally in the final version), the paper would be even more convincing.

Confidence
High.
