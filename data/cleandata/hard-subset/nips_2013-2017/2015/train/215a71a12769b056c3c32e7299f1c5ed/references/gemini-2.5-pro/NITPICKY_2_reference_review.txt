**Summary**

This paper addresses the well-known difficulty of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors hypothesize that the degradation in performance observed in "plain" deep networks is due to poor information propagation (of both activations and gradients) across many layers. To mitigate this, they propose a novel architecture called "Highway Networks," inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The core idea is to introduce adaptive gates that control the flow of information, allowing parts of the input to be carried directly ("on a highway") to subsequent layers, while other parts are transformed by the layer's non-linearity. The authors demonstrate empirically on MNIST and CIFAR datasets that these networks can be trained directly with SGD to depths of 100 layers or more, overcoming the optimization obstacles that plague plain networks. Furthermore, an analysis of the trained gates suggests that the network learns a data-dependent information routing mechanism.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—adapting the LSTM gating concept from the temporal domain to the depth domain—is a highly novel and insightful idea. It provides an elegant and effective solution to the problem of training very deep networks, a significant challenge for the community at the time of writing. The proposed architecture directly precedes and shares conceptual similarities with other landmark architectures like Residual Networks, highlighting the importance of this work.

2.  **Compelling Empirical Evidence:** The experiments in Section 3.1 provide a clear and compelling demonstration of the core hypothesis. The direct comparison between plain and highway networks of increasing depth (10, 20, 50, 100 layers) on MNIST convincingly shows that while plain networks degrade, highway networks maintain or improve performance with depth. This is a strong piece of evidence supporting the architectural innovation.

3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 goes beyond simply presenting performance metrics. The visualization of the transform gate activities and the "lesioning" study provide valuable insights into the network's learned behavior. The suggestion that the network learns dynamic, data-dependent routing and can effectively "ignore" unnecessary layers for simpler tasks is an interesting and important finding.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are strong, the paper is undermined by a significant number of minor flaws, technical inaccuracies, and a general lack of polish that, in aggregate, call the rigor of the work into question.

1.  **Formatting and Manuscript Preparation:** The manuscript is replete with formatting errors and signs of haste.
    *   The reference list is highly inconsistent. Formatting varies wildly between entries (e.g., [1] vs. [6]), some entries appear incomplete (e.g., "[cs],, , ,"), and there are clear errors (e.g., the dangling "tasks." in reference [6]).
    *   There are critical citation issues. Footnote 1 mentions a report [31] which is not present in the bibliography. A key piece of text ("...which is connected to the next layer.2") is followed by a footnote marker, but the corresponding footnote is missing entirely. Footnote 3 is not formatted as a footnote but appears as floating text.
    *   There are numerous typos and stylistic issues, such as "varying varying depths" in Section 3.1, the informal ampersand in the Section 1 title, and inconsistent use of `·` and `*` for element-wise multiplication.

2.  **Technical Inaccuracies:**
    *   The equation for the sigmoid function in Section 2 is written as `σ(x) = 11+e−x`, which is mathematically incorrect. It should be `1/(1+e−x)`. This is a fundamental error in a core definition.
    *   The claim in Section 3.1 that plain networks with 71 units and highway networks with 50 blocks have "roughly identical numbers of parameters" seems questionable. A highway layer block includes parameters for both the transform `H` and the gate `T`, likely resulting in nearly double the number of weights per block compared to a standard unit. This lack of precise justification undermines the fairness of the crucial comparison in Figure 1.

3.  **Methodological Opacity:**
    *   The initialization of the transform gate bias `bT` is presented as a critical component for successful training. However, its selection is described as an ad-hoc hyperparameter, with a "general guideline" offered without theoretical or strong empirical justification. The sensitivity to this initialization is not adequately explored.
    *   The simplification `C = 1 - T` is made for convenience but its implications are not discussed. An analysis of whether independent gates would offer benefits is a missed opportunity.
    *   The paper states that for changing dimensionality, a plain layer was used, but does not justify this choice over the other mentioned alternatives (sub-sampling or zero-padding).
    *   The description of the FitNet comparison is vague. The paper claims the highway architectures are "based on" the FitNet architectures, but fails to specify if they are identical modulo the highway layers. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison.

4.  **Strength of Claims:** The conclusion from the lesioning study (Section 4.2) that "60% of the layers don’t learn to contribute" on MNIST may be an oversimplification. The experiment only removes layers one at a time; it does not rule out the possibility of distributed or redundant computation where the collective is important. The claim that this is the "first time" such an analysis is possible seems overly strong.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel, elegant, and significant architectural solution to the critical problem of training very deep networks.
*   The primary experimental result directly and convincingly supports the main claim of the paper.
*   The analysis of the trained network provides valuable insights into its function.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript suffers from a high density of errors, from fundamental mathematical typos and broken references to pervasive formatting issues, which suggests a lack of care and rigor unbecoming of a NIPS publication.
*   The fairness of the central experimental comparison in Section 3.1 is questionable due to an unsubstantiated claim about parameter counts.
*   Key methodological choices (e.g., bias initialization, dimensionality reduction) are not sufficiently justified, hindering reproducibility and understanding.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the core contribution of this paper is novel and potentially of high impact, the execution of the manuscript is severely lacking. The sheer number of careless errors, including a mathematical mistake in the definition of the sigmoid function and a broken reference list, falls well below the standard expected for a top-tier conference. These issues, combined with a lack of clarity and justification for key methodological and experimental design choices, prevent a confident assessment of the work's technical soundness. The paper should be thoroughly revised to fix these extensive issues and resubmitted. The ideas are promising, but the paper itself is not ready for publication.
