

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces "highway networks," a modification to deep neural networks that incorporates an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism to enable information flow across many layers. The key innovation is the highway layer equation: y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), where T is a transform gate controlling the flow of information between layers. The authors claim that highway networks can be trained directly using SGD even when extremely deep (up to 100 layers), while plain networks become increasingly difficult to optimize with depth.

The paper makes several claims: (1) highway networks can be trained with depth exceeding 100 layers, (2) they generalize well to unseen data, and (3) they can match or exceed the performance of state-of-the-art methods with fewer parameters. The authors support these claims with experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, comparing to plain networks and Fitnets.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The highway network concept is explained with sufficient mathematical detail, and the experimental results on MNIST provide some evidence that the approach works for that dataset. The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figure 2) offers interesting insights into how the gating mechanism operates during training.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its lack of novelty in the face of existing work. The authors cite ResNets (He et al., 2015) as "recent" work but fail to acknowledge that ResNets, published just months before this paper's submission, use a very similar skip connection approach to solve the same problem of training very deep networks. The highway network's gating mechanism is functionally similar to ResNets' identity mapping, with the key difference being that highway networks use a learned gate while ResNets use a fixed skip connection (equivalent to T=1 for all layers). The paper does not adequately distinguish its approach from ResNets, which were already published at the time of this submission.

The experimental validation is insufficient. The authors claim they can train networks with "more than 1000 layers" based on "pilot experiments," but provide no concrete results or evidence for such networks. Their most substantial experiments are on MNIST (a relatively easy dataset) and CIFAR-10/100, but they don't compare to state-of-the-art methods like ResNets, which would have been available at the time. The CIFAR experiments only show that highway networks perform similarly to Fitnets, not that they outperform existing methods.

The theoretical justification is weak. The paper claims that highway networks "do not suffer from increasing depth," but provides no rigorous analysis of why this is the case. The Jacobian analysis in Equation 5 is superficial and doesn't address the fundamental challenges of training very deep networks.

The paper also doesn't adequately address potential limitations. For instance, the authors claim that "many layers might remain unused" but don't investigate the computational cost of the additional gating parameters or how this affects inference time compared to plain networks or ResNets.

**Significance:**
The paper's significance is limited by its lack of novelty and insufficient experimental validation. The core idea of using skip connections to enable training of very deep networks was already established by ResNets, which were published in the same year. The highway network approach appears to be a minor variation on this idea without clear advantages over ResNets, which would have been the natural point of comparison.

**Recommendation:**
This paper should be rejected. While the highway network concept is interesting, the work lacks sufficient novelty to warrant publication at NIPS, especially given that the core idea has been independently developed and published by others (ResNets). The paper fails to properly situate its contribution within the existing literature, provides insufficient experimental validation, and makes claims that aren't adequately supported by the results presented. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these issues, including a thorough comparison to ResNets and stronger theoretical justification for their approach.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
Pro: Well-written, clear explanation of the highway network concept, some interesting analysis of transform gate behavior.
Con: Lack of novelty (ResNets already solved this problem), insufficient experimental validation, no comparison to state-of-the-art methods, weak theoretical justification, overclaims about training networks with "more than 1000 layers."
