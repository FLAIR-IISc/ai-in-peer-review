Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture inspired by LSTM gating that facilitates information flow across many layers. Each layer output is y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or conv+ReLU) and T is a sigmoid “transform gate.” Setting C = 1 − T yields an input-dependent convex combination of the transformed signal and the identity carry. The key practical insight is to initialize gate biases negatively so that early in training layers default to carry behavior, improving gradient/activation propagation in very deep nets.

This connects to: (a) LSTM/GRU gates for long-range dependencies, (b) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision aimed at improving information flow, (c) initialization schemes (e.g., variance-preserving), and (d) hint-based training (FitNets). The authors argue their gating provides learnable, input-dependent routing rather than fixed shortcuts; they show direct training with SGD of thin networks up to 100 layers and pilot results for even deeper.

Methodology and experiments
- Architecture: both fully connected and convolutional highway layers; transform gates share the same dimensionality and receptive fields as H. C is tied to 1 − T.
- Training: momentum SGD, simple learning-rate schedules; negative gate bias initialization; ReLU for H in conv nets. Comparisons with plain nets use normalized initialization.
- Optimization study: On MNIST, thin fully connected nets of varying depth show that plain networks degrade with depth, while highway nets of 50–100 layers train as well as shallow ones, converging faster and to much lower loss.
- Generalization: Competitive MNIST results with relatively few parameters; on CIFAR-10/100, highway nets trained end-to-end match or improve upon FitNets with comparable parameter/FLOP budgets, without teacher hints.
- Analysis: Visualizations of gate biases/activities indicate sparse, input-dependent gating; lesion experiments (closing transform gates in one layer) suggest many layers are essential for CIFAR-100 but less so for MNIST, supporting adaptive use of depth.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea with strong motivation from LSTM gating; the identity carry path is always present and modulated by gates, which directly addresses gradient/activation attenuation with depth.
- Demonstrates that very deep, thin networks can be optimized with vanilla SGD, without auxiliary losses or teacher distillation.
- Solid optimization study on controlled thin architectures; consistent training speed and loss improvements with depth.
- Insightful diagnostics (gate activity patterns and per-layer lesioning) that go beyond reporting accuracies and support the interpretation of adaptive routing.
- Code availability and reasonable training details improve reproducibility.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope of empirical validation is limited to MNIST and CIFAR-10/100. Claims of training “more than 1000 layers” are mentioned as pilot observations without quantitative evidence, and no large-scale (e.g., ImageNet) experiments are reported.
- Comparisons to alternative depth-easing strategies are incomplete. In particular:
  - No direct comparison to fixed skip/shortcut connections without gates (e.g., identity mappings) to isolate the added value of gating versus simple residual pathways.
  - Batch normalization and its interaction with highway layers are not discussed, though BN is an important concurrent technique for deep nets.
  - Maxout/better-regularized baselines and stronger all-convolutional networks are only partially covered; fairness of parameter/FLOP matching relative to FitNets could be documented more precisely.
- Architectural ablations are limited. Key choices remain underexplored:
  - The tying C = 1 − T vs. learning independent carry gates (stability vs. flexibility trade-off).
  - Sensitivity to gate bias initialization, schedules, or annealing; behavior with positive/zero biases.
  - Cost/benefit of gating (parameter and compute overhead for T) versus performance and optimization gains.
- Theoretical grounding is minimal. While empirical evidence is compelling, formal analysis of gradient propagation (e.g., Jacobian norms along information highways) would strengthen claims about alleviating vanishing/exploding issues.
- Clarity: Some architectural details (exact CIFAR architectures, schedules, augmentation, per-layer widths/receptive fields, and FLOP counts) are summarized but not fully specified in the main text; reproduction may require digging into the code.

Questions for the authors
- How does a “residual-only” variant (y = H(x) + x) or skip-with-scaling compare in the same thin/deep regimes and with the same initialization, both in optimization and final accuracy? This would isolate the contribution of input-dependent gating.
- Have you evaluated independent carry gates C(x) (untied from T), or a linearized gate (e.g., hard-sigmoid) to reduce saturation? What is the impact on stability and performance?
- Can you quantify gate usage (e.g., fraction of units with T < 0.1 per layer per sample) and relate it to effective depth and compute? Is there potential for conditional computation savings?
- How robust is training to the choice of gate bias bT across datasets and depths? Would scheduled bias annealing help move from carry to transform as training progresses?
- What is the overhead (parameters and wall-clock) introduced by the gates relative to the observed gains?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations on C = 1 − T vs. untied C, initial gate bias, and a residual-only baseline matched for parameters.
- Include gradient norm/Jacobian statistics across depth to substantiate the “easy information flow” claim.
- Report FLOPs and training times; consider small-scale ImageNet or SVHN to broaden validation.
- Explore interaction with batch normalization and other modern training tricks.
- Provide precise architectural and hyperparameter specifications in an appendix for full reproducibility.

Quality: Technically sound with careful experiments in the studied regimes; some claims (very deep >1000 layers) would benefit from quantitative evidence. Analysis sections are a strong point.

Clarity: Generally well-written and motivated; could benefit from fuller architectural details and tighter notation around blocks/units.

Originality: Novel and timely import of LSTM-style gating to deep feedforward nets with a principled initialization strategy; clearly distinguished from prior skip connections and FitNet hinting.

Significance: High. The method provides a practical path to train very deep networks end-to-end and suggests a general mechanism (learnable, input-dependent carry) that is likely to influence subsequent architectures.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated idea with strong empirical evidence for deeper optimization.
- Input-dependent routing and analysis provide valuable insights.
- Improves over FitNets without teacher guidance under comparable budgets.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited dataset scope and lack of comparisons to strong fixed-skip baselines.
- Missing ablations on key design choices and limited theory.
- Overhead and scalability not fully quantified.

Overall: A compelling contribution that advances training of very deep networks; with stronger baselines/ablations and broader validation, it would be even more convincing. I lean toward acceptance.
