Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Highway Networks: feedforward layers augmented with learned, input-dependent gates that interpolate between transforming the input via H(x, WH) and carrying it forward unchanged. Each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), with T(x) = σ(WT x + bT). The design is explicitly inspired by LSTM gating, aiming to create “information highways” that preserve activations and gradients across many layers. A simple but important practical ingredient is initializing the transform-gate biases bT to negative values to bias early training toward carry behavior, analogous to LSTM forget-gate initialization.

The authors position their contribution relative to: (i) difficulties in training deep nets and prior remedies (normalized variance-preserving initializations, specialized optimizers), (ii) skip connections and deep supervision, (iii) teacher–student “hints” (FitNets), and (iv) local competition activations. Their key claim is that, unlike plain deep nets, highway nets can be trained to depths of 50–100+ with vanilla SGD and generalize well, without special supervision or multi-stage training.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Adapting LSTM-style gating to feedforward depth with learnable, input-dependent skip/carry offers a clear, principled mechanism to improve information flow. This goes beyond fixed skip connections by learning the routing per input.
- Empirical evidence of trainability: Careful optimization study on MNIST shows that plain nets degrade quickly with depth while highway nets of 50–100 layers train well (and faster), despite matched per-layer parameter counts and normalized initialization for baselines.
- Practical recipe: The negative gate-bias initialization is simple, effective, and architecture-agnostic. The authors report training success even with >1000 layers in pilots.
- Generalization and analysis: Competitive test accuracy on MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 in thin/deep regimes, matching or surpassing FitNets without teacher hints. Ablations via lesioning layers and gate-activity visualizations provide insight into depth utilization and data-dependent routing.
- Reproducibility: Code references and details on hyperparameter search help replication.

Weaknesses
- Scope of baselines: While plain nets with normalized initialization and FitNets are reasonable, comparisons to other strong training aids available at the time (e.g., batch normalization, pre-activation variants, or alternative skip designs) are missing. This limits the strength of the comparative claim that highways are “easy to train” relative to the broader ecosystem.
- Limited datasets/metrics: Results focus on MNIST and CIFAR; no large-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) or wall-clock/throughput comparisons are provided. Gains are shown mainly for optimization and modest accuracy improvements rather than clear state-of-the-art generalization.
- Ablation depth: The paper fixes C = 1 − T; it would be informative to evaluate uncoupled carry gates, the sensitivity to bT values across depths, and the overhead introduced by gating (params/FLOPs) versus benefits. Some architectural specifics (exact layer widths, schedules) are described at a high level; more detail would strengthen reproducibility.
- Theoretical guarantees: While intuitions are sound (Jacobian can be close to identity when gates are closed), there is no formal analysis of gradient flow or conditions under which training remains stable as depth grows.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clean and grounded in well-understood gating mechanisms. The negative bias trick is plausible and empirically validated. The optimization experiments include a reasonable hyperparameter search and matched-capacity controls. Claims about improved trainability are well supported; claims about superior generalization are supported in thin/deep regimes but not comprehensively across stronger baselines.
- Completeness: The work reports multiple datasets, comparisons to FitNets, and insightful analyses (gate activity, lesioning). Missing are ablations for C ≠ 1 − T and broader baselines (e.g., batch norm), and any computational cost analysis.
- Honesty: The authors acknowledge that they do not target SOTA and reuse hyperparameters, and they discuss potential concerns (unused layers) with evidence (lesion studies).

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, with precise equations and intuitive explanations (Equations 1–5). The notion of blocks and gating is easy to follow. Figures (training curves, gate activities, lesion impacts) help understanding. Some implementation details (exact architectures per table, training schedules) could be expanded, though code availability mitigates this.

Originality
- The key idea—LSTM-inspired, learnable, input-conditioned carry/transform gates in deep feedforward networks—is novel relative to prior fixed skip connections and deep supervision. The negative gate-bias initialization applied to depth is a practical and original adaptation.

Significance
- The work addresses an important bottleneck (training very deep networks) with a generally applicable architectural mechanism. Even without SOTA, the demonstrated ease of optimization at large depth and the analyses of data-dependent routing are likely to influence subsequent architectures and practice. The contribution is methodologically significant and of broad interest.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general architectural idea that materially improves trainability at depth.
- Solid empirical evidence of optimization benefits; competitive accuracy without teacher hints.
- Insightful analyses (gating patterns, lesioning) and practical initialization guidance.
- Relevance and likely impact for the NeurIPS community.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing comparisons to some contemporaneous strong techniques (e.g., batch norm, alternative residual/skip designs).
- Limited scale of experiments and lack of compute/parameter overhead analysis.
- Incomplete ablations on gate design choices and sensitivity.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide results with batch normalization and discuss interactions with highway gating.
- Report computational overhead (params, FLOPs, wall-clock) due to gates and any throughput impacts.
- Explore uncoupled carry gates (C learned independently) and ablate the effect of tying C = 1 − T.
- Include sensitivity analysis of bT initialization across depth and tasks.
- Consider larger-scale experiments or qualitative results on more complex data.

Overall recommendation
- I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a clear, novel architectural mechanism with strong empirical evidence that it alleviates depth-related optimization issues, along with thoughtful analyses. Despite some missing baselines and ablations, the anticipated impact and methodological value are high.
