Summary
This paper proposes highway networks: feedforward architectures whose layers combine a standard nonlinear transform H(x) with an element-wise gated carry of the input x. Specifically, each layer outputs y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where T is a sigmoid “transform gate” initialized with a negative bias to favor carry at the start of training. The design is inspired by LSTM gating for temporal credit assignment but applied across depth to ease gradient flow. The authors argue that highway layers create “information highways,” enabling effective SGD-based training of very deep networks.

Empirically, the paper reports: (a) on MNIST, thin fully connected highway nets (up to 100 layers) optimize much better than similarly sized plain nets and converge faster; (b) on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, convolutional highway nets trained in one stage (no teacher) match or exceed FitNet-style student performance under comparable parameter/compute budgets; (c) qualitative analyses of learned gates, per-layer lesioning, and class-conditional gate activity, suggesting data-dependent routing and depth utilization for harder datasets. Code and scripts are said to be public.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-positioned relative to: initialization work (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.), alternative optimizers (Martens; Dauphin), locally competitive activations/maxout, skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and teacher-student distillation (FitNets). The main novelty vs. prior skip connections is the learned, input-dependent gating akin to LSTM, not fixed additive shortcuts. The work is also related to Raiko et al.’s linear passthrough and selective routing mechanisms.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea with plausible motivation: gating creates low-impedance paths for activations/gradients at initialization.
- Consistent optimization advantage vs. plain nets as depth increases, with careful control of parameter counts.
- Competitive CIFAR-10/100 results vs. thin/maxout/fitnet baselines using single-stage training and modest augmentation.
- Useful analyses: gate statistics across depth, class-conditional gating patterns, and lesioning to assess layer importance.
- Practical guidance (negative gate bias initialization) and claimed open-source release improve reproducibility.

Weaknesses
Quality/methodology
- Evidence is largely limited to MNIST and CIFAR with relatively small models. Claims of “extremely deep” training are supported by FC nets up to 100 layers and conv nets up to 32 layers; no results beyond these depths or on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) are provided.
- Key ablations are missing:
  - Fixed identity shortcuts vs. gated shortcuts under matched capacity/compute to isolate the value of learned gates over simple residual passthroughs.
  - Separate carry and transform gates (C ≠ 1 − T) vs. the tied version used here.
  - Effect of varying the transform bias initialization and its sensitivity across datasets.
- The gating increases parameter counts and compute; a detailed accounting of parameter/compute overhead vs. baselines is not systematically presented beyond rough parity claims.
- The optimization story lacks quantitative diagnostics of gradient flow (e.g., gradient norms/Jacobians across depth), relying instead on training curves.
- The MNIST optimization comparison shows large depth gaps but does not report test accuracy vs. depth under matched capacity, making it hard to separate optimization from regularization effects.

Clarity
- Overall readable and well-organized. However, some architectural details are under-specified for exact reproduction (e.g., per-layer filter sizes/strides/padding, precise learning rate schedules, momentum, weight decay per experiment; “random search of 100 runs” lacks bounds for all hparams). These may be in the code, but the paper should be self-contained to the extent possible.
- Figures referenced (training curves, gate plots) are central to claims; precise axis scales and numerical summaries would help.

Originality
- The application of LSTM-style gating to depth is novel and timely. That said, the idea sits close to existing skip/shortcut connections; the paper would benefit from a deeper contrast to contemporaneous fixed-shortcut approaches (even a simple identity-addition baseline).

Significance
- If broadly validated, highway layers could be useful for training deeper networks without multi-stage distillation. However, current results do not surpass state-of-the-art on CIFAR; they mainly show parity with/without teachers and better optimization than plain nets. The community impact is contingent on demonstrating clear, consistent gains over strong shortcut baselines and on larger-scale tasks.

Specific suggestions/questions
- Provide a controlled comparison against identical architectures with fixed identity shortcuts (additive residual connections) under matched parameter/compute. Is learned gating materially better than a simple residual?
- Report gradient norm statistics across layers over training to substantiate the “easier credit assignment” claim.
- Include ablations on: (i) separate carry gate vs. tied C = 1 − T; (ii) different gate nonlinearities; (iii) bias initialization values and schedules.
- Quantify parameter and FLOP overhead attributable to gates and discuss memory/runtime implications.
- Expand conv-depth experiments beyond 32 layers to support “very deep” claims and test generalization vs. overfitting.
- Clarify hyperparameter search ranges and fairness across baselines; where possible, tune baselines comparably.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated architectural contribution with practical training benefits.
- Solid empirical optimization evidence and competitive performance without teacher signals.
- Insightful analyses of gating behavior and layer importance.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical scope (datasets, depths, and baselines); no large-scale validation.
- Missing critical ablations, especially vs. fixed skip/residual connections.
- Overstated claims relative to evidence; optimization advantages do not translate to clear state-of-the-art accuracy.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and plausibly impactful, but the current evidence base and comparisons are not yet strong enough to endorse over established (and simpler) shortcut-based baselines. With additional controlled comparisons and broader experiments, this could become a strong contribution.

Confidence
High on the assessment of experimental sufficiency and needed baselines; medium on the ultimate potential of the approach pending further validation.
