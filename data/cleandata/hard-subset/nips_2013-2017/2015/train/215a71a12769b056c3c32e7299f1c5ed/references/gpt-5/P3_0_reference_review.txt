Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces LSTM-inspired gated shortcuts across layers to ease training of very deep networks. Each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine + nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid-gated “transform gate” (with the “carry gate” tied as 1 − T). A simple initialization—negative bias on T—biases layers toward carry behavior early, enabling unattenuated information and gradient flow across many layers. The authors show: (1) strong optimization advantages over plain nets as depth increases (up to 100 layers) on MNIST with matched parameter budgets, (2) competitive generalization on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 without teacher-student distillation, and (3) qualitative analyses of gating behavior, input-conditional routing, and layer lesioning that suggest dynamic, data-dependent use of depth.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well situated relative to difficulties in training deep nets (vanishing gradients; initialization; Hessian-free; activation choices; skip/error injection, deep supervision, and fitnets/distillation). The key novelty is a trainable, elementwise gating of residual carry paths inspired by LSTMs, as opposed to fixed skip connections or auxiliary losses. The paper distinguishes itself from related “locally competitive” activations and teacher-student methods by enabling direct single-stage training of very deep, thin networks. The connection to LSTM gating and the idea of “unrolling across depth” are appropriately acknowledged; the contemporaneous Grid LSTM is cited.

Quality
- Method: The formulation is simple, differentiable, and compatible with standard SGD. The gating-induced Jacobian offers an intuitive explanation of improved gradient flow (identity vs H′ in the extreme cases), though no formal analysis is provided for the realistic case T ∈ (0,1).
- Initialization: Negative biases on T are essential in practice; the authors provide empirical guidelines and a justification by analogy to LSTM forget-gate biasing.
- Experiments: The MNIST optimization study is carefully set up with parameter-matched plain vs highway FC networks and random hyperparameter search. The CIFAR studies show that highway variants of FitNet-like architectures can be trained in a single stage and achieve comparable or better accuracy without hints. The analyses (gate statistics, class-conditional routing, lesioning) provide valuable interpretability.
- Missing comparisons: There is no comparison to batch normalization or to fixed skip/identity mappings; compute/parameter overheads of gating are not quantified; and large-scale ImageNet results are absent.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The architectural description is concise; however, a schematic of a highway block would help non-experts. Some experimental details are deferred (e.g., exact CIFAR architectures and augmentation settings), and numerical results in tables/figures are referenced but not all are reproduced here. The analysis figures are insightful. Reproducibility is bolstered by released code and scripts.

Originality
Introducing trainable, input-dependent carry/transform gates across depth in feedforward nets is a novel and elegant extension of LSTM ideas to deep feedforward networks. While skip connections and hint-based training existed, the specific gating formulation and the demonstration of training very deep thin nets without teachers are new. The qualitative analyses (gating selectivity, routing, and per-layer lesioning) add original insights.

Significance
Highway Networks address a central challenge—training very deep networks—with a general mechanism applicable to fully connected and convolutional settings. The work is likely to influence architectures (indeed, gated or residual pathways have become foundational), and the analysis of learned depth usage is of broad interest. While the paper does not push absolute SOTA, it convincingly demonstrates ease of optimization and competitive performance, which is significant.

Strengths
- Simple, principled architecture that materially eases optimization at great depths.
- Practical, robust initialization heuristic; works with ReLU and standard SGD.
- Careful parameter-matched optimization study demonstrating depth scaling advantages.
- Single-stage training on CIFAR comparable to FitNets without teacher hints.
- Insightful analyses: data-dependent routing, layer lesioning showing adaptive depth usage.
- Code and hyperparameter search artifacts released.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theoretical support is limited to intuition; no analysis of effective gradient norms or path lengths under T ∈ (0,1). Suggest adding empirical gradient-flow diagnostics (e.g., per-layer gradient norms vs depth) and/or a simple mean-field analysis of signal propagation with gates.
- C = 1 − T ties carry and transform; this restricts expressivity. An ablation comparing tied vs untied carry gates (C independent of T) and their optimization/generalization trade-offs would strengthen the case for the chosen simplification.
- Comparisons lack strong baselines prevalent at the time: batch normalization, fixed identity shortcut connections, and deeper plain conv nets with He initialization. Even a subset of these on CIFAR would clarify how much the learned gates add beyond fixed skips/normalization.
- Computational cost is not discussed. Gates add parameters and an extra transform (T) per layer. Please report parameter and FLOP overheads, and wall-clock convergence comparisons for matched accuracy.
- Sensitivity analyses are light: more systematic study of the transform-gate bias initialization, its schedule (e.g., learned vs fixed decay), and robustness across activations/datasets would be useful.
- CIFAR evaluations use limited augmentation and reused hyperparameters; this helps fairness but underplays potential. Present at least one strong configuration to establish headroom and avoid the impression of cherry-picked limits.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of tying C = 1 − T? Does learning a separate carry gate help or hurt optimization?
- Have you tried batch normalization within H and/or on x? Does BN obviate the need for negative T-bias or improve training further?
- What are the parameter/FLOP multipliers introduced by gating in your CIFAR models, and what is the practical training-time overhead?
- Do gates saturate during training? Can you report gate activation histograms across depth and epochs to show how the network “opens up”?
- Can you quantify routing diversity, e.g., entropy of T across samples, or per-class path-length statistics?

Pro/con arguments
Pro:
- Addresses an important problem with a broadly applicable, elegant mechanism.
- Strong evidence of improved optimization at great depth.
- Competitive results without auxiliary supervision; good analyses and released code.

Con:
- Missing comparisons to batch norm and fixed skip connections.
- Limited theoretical grounding and sensitivity analyses.
- No large-scale benchmarks; compute/parameter overheads not quantified.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a clear architectural advance with demonstrated benefits in training very deep networks and provides insightful analyses. Strengthening baselines and quantifying overheads would further improve the work, but the core contribution is significant and timely. Confidence: high.
