

# Review: Training Very Deep Networks

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel architectural modification designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a gating mechanism inspired by LSTM networks, where each layer computes an output as y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), with T being the transform gate. This allows information to flow across layers through "information highways," addressing the challenge of vanishing gradients in very deep networks. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly using SGD even for depths exceeding 100 layers, in contrast to plain networks which become increasingly difficult to optimize with depth.

**Quality**
The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical grounding in the gate mechanism. The experimental methodology is rigorous, with careful comparison between highway and plain networks across multiple depths and datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100). The authors provide thorough ablation studies (Figures 2-4) that demonstrate how the highway architecture enables dynamic information routing and layer utilization. The paper addresses potential limitations (e.g., the possibility of unused layers) through lesioning experiments, showing that highway networks can effectively utilize all layers for complex tasks like CIFAR-100. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility, and the training procedure requires only standard SGD with a simple negative bias initialization for the transform gates.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation and well-designed figures that effectively illustrate key concepts. The explanation of the highway network architecture is accessible, and the experimental results are presented in a logical manner. However, the paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison with residual connections (which were developed around the same time), as this would clarify the relationship between these approaches. The distinction between highway networks and residual networks is somewhat blurred in the discussion, and the paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison to this contemporaneous work.

**Originality**
The core idea of highway networks is novel in the context of feedforward networks, representing a significant advancement over previous approaches like skip connections (which are fixed) or teacher-student distillation (which requires multi-stage training). The authors clearly relate their work to previous contributions, including LSTM networks, skip connections, and knowledge distillation. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by the concurrent development of residual connections (He et al., 2015), which share the same fundamental concept of enabling information flow across layers but with a simpler architecture (just adding identity connections). The paper does not adequately address this parallel work, which weakens the claim of novelty. While the highway network formulation is mathematically distinct, the residual connection approach ultimately proved more effective and simpler to implement.

**Significance**
The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training very deep networks. The demonstrated ability to train networks with over 100 layers directly using SGD is significant, as it removes the need for complex initialization schemes or multi-stage training procedures. The results show that highway networks can match or exceed the performance of shallower networks with more parameters, which is important for resource-constrained applications. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the fact that residual connections, which were developed around the same time, became the dominant approach for training deep networks and outperformed highway networks in practice. The paper's significance would be stronger if it provided a clear comparative analysis with residual networks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Presents a clear, mathematically sound solution to a fundamental problem in deep learning
- Demonstrates the ability to train extremely deep networks (over 100 layers) directly with SGD
- Provides thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets and network depths
- Includes insightful analysis of information routing and layer utilization
- The approach is simple to implement and can be applied to various network architectures

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Novelty is limited by the concurrent development of residual connections, which share the same core concept
- Paper does not adequately compare with or acknowledge residual networks, which were developed around the same time
- The performance gains shown are not as impressive as those achieved by residual networks
- The paper does not establish a clear advantage of highway networks over the simpler residual approach

**Conclusion**
This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of training very deep networks, introducing a novel architectural modification that enables direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks. While the technical quality is strong and the experimental results are well-supported, the novelty is somewhat compromised by the concurrent development of residual connections, which share the same fundamental insight but with a simpler implementation. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a thorough comparison with residual networks, which would better establish the relative merits of the highway approach. Given the field's subsequent adoption of residual connections as the dominant approach, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the timing of its publication relative to this related work. Nevertheless, the paper provides valuable theoretical insights into information flow in deep networks and demonstrates a viable alternative to other approaches for training very deep networks.
