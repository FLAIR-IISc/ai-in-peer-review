Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks: feedforward architectures in which each layer outputs a convex combination of a transformed input H(x) and the input itself, using data-dependent gates T(x) (with C=1−T). The gating is inspired by LSTM’s input/forget gates, but applied across depth rather than time, thereby creating “information highways” that allow signals and gradients to propagate through many layers without attenuation. A simple but crucial training heuristic is to initialize the transform-gate biases to negative values to bias early training toward carry behavior.

The authors position the work relative to: (i) the empirical benefits of depth in CNNs on ImageNet and elsewhere; (ii) optimization aids such as normalized/variance-preserving initializations and better optimizers; (iii) architectural devices facilitating information flow, including local competition/maxout, skip connections, and deep supervision; and (iv) staged training via teacher-student distillation (FitNets). They present experiments showing that very deep plain MLPs become hard to optimize with depth, whereas highway MLPs train well up to 100 layers with SGD; that 10-layer convolutional highway nets generalize well on MNIST; and that on CIFAR-10/100, highway CNNs match or exceed FitNets at similar parameter/compute budgets without staged training. They also analyze gate statistics and perform “lesion” studies to quantify layer importance.

Quality
- Soundness: The layer formulation is clear, the gating mechanism is well-motivated by LSTM, and the training heuristic is simple and effective. The optimization study on MNIST (depth sweep, random hyperparameter search) convincingly shows that depth hurts plain nets but not highway nets. CIFAR results are competitive under modest augmentation, and the lesion/gate analyses are informative.
- Empirical completeness: While the comparisons to plain nets and FitNets are appropriate, some important baselines are missing or understated:
  - Batch normalization and/or carefully tuned He-initialized deep plain networks as depth increases.
  - Identity or fixed skip connections without learned gates (to isolate the value of gating vs. shortcutting).
  - Separate carry gate C vs. tying C=1−T (an ablation).
  - Sensitivity to gate-bias initialization and learning-rate schedules beyond MNIST/CIFAR-10 tuning.
  - Parameter/FLOP counts and wall-clock costs of adding gates.
There is no formal analysis of gradient flow, though the intuition is strong; even a simple bound showing non-vanishing gradients along carry paths when T≈0 would strengthen the claims.
- Reproducibility: Code and scripts are released; many hyperparameters are described. Architecture details for CIFAR models could be more explicit (exact layer shapes, receptive fields, strides, and full training schedules).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. The notation is clean; the “block” abstraction is helpful. A few suggestions:
- Clearly distinguish “unit,” “block,” and “layer,” and tabulate the exact architectures used for CIFAR models (filter sizes, feature-map counts per layer).
- Report parameter counts and multiply-adds for all models, and the overhead introduced by gates.
- Move key training hyperparameters from appendix/scripts into the main text or a concise table.

Originality
Applying LSTM-style gating to depth in feedforward nets is novel and conceptually elegant. While skip connections and deep supervision are known, learnable, data-dependent gates that interpolate between identity and transform at each layer appear to be a new and meaningful addition. The lesion and gate-activity analyses are also original and provide insight into the learned computation.

Significance
The work addresses a central obstacle—training very deep nets with SGD—by proposing a simple architectural change that demonstrably eases optimization without sacrificing generalization. The potential impact is high: if gating reliably enables much deeper models under standard training, practitioners can scale depth without elaborate initialization or multi-stage distillation. The CIFAR results show practical benefits under constrained budgets, though state-of-the-art under heavy augmentation is not the focus here.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, LSTM-inspired mechanism that directly targets depth-related optimization issues.
- Strong optimization evidence: highway MLPs train at 50–100 layers where plain nets fail.
- Competitive CIFAR performance at comparable budgets, without teacher-student training.
- Insightful analyses: gate bias/activity visualizations, class-conditional routing, and lesion studies.
- Open-source code and scripts.

Weaknesses
- Missing baselines: batch normalization and identity-skip (“residual”) plain nets; effect of gating vs. fixed shortcuts.
- Limited ablations: C=1−T vs. separate C; effect of gate-bias initialization; activation choices; removing gates in subsets of layers.
- Limited scale: no results on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or substantially deeper convolutional nets beyond ~30–50 layers.
- Incomplete reporting of compute/parameter overhead and training time.
- No theoretical guarantees or gradient-flow analysis to complement empirical findings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add controlled ablations:
  - Compare to identity skip connections with the same topology but no gates.
  - Untie carry gate (learn C) vs. enforce C=1−T; evaluate both performance and training stability.
  - Vary gate-bias initialization systematically; report sensitivity curves.
- Include batch normalization baselines and/or combine BN with highway layers; report whether highways still help.
- Scale up experiments: deeper conv highway nets (e.g., >50 layers) and at least one larger dataset; report convergence speed and accuracy vs. depth.
- Provide parameter/FLOP counts, training time, and memory usage for all models; quantify the overhead of gating.
- Offer a simple analytical argument bounding gradient norms along carry paths under typical T values to support the optimization claims.
- Clarify architectural details and hyperparameters in a single reproducibility table.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated architectural idea with clear empirical benefits for training very deep networks.
- Strong optimization evidence and competitive accuracy under fair training settings.
- Insightful analysis advancing understanding of how very deep gated architectures behave.
- Practical relevance and code release.

Arguments for rejection
- Missing key baselines (especially identity skips and BN) make it hard to isolate the unique value of learnable gates.
- Limited scale and dataset breadth; current results may not generalize to large-scale vision tasks.
- Insufficient ablations and theoretical support.

Recommendation: Accept (clear contribution to enabling very deep networks; likely to influence subsequent architectures), but encourage authors to add the suggested baselines/ablations and more detailed reporting.

Reviewer confidence: High on empirical assessment and related work; moderate on broader scalability claims pending larger-scale experiments.
