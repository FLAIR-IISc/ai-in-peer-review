### Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary**

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to enable the training of very deep feedforward models using simple stochastic gradient descent. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanism in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. Each layer in a highway network includes a "transform gate" and a "carry gate" that dynamically regulate the flow of information. These gates control how much of the input is passed through a standard non-linear transformation and how much is carried over unchanged to the next layer. The authors hypothesize that by allowing unimpeded information flow across many layers (forming "information highways"), this architecture mitigates the optimization difficulties, such as vanishing gradients, that plague traditional "plain" deep networks. The paper provides experimental evidence on MNIST and CIFAR datasets, demonstrating that highway networks can be trained at depths of up to 100 layers, significantly outperforming plain networks of similar depth. The authors also present an analysis of the learned gate behavior, suggesting that the network learns data-dependent information routing.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The paper tackles a well-known and significant challenge in deep learning: the difficulty of effectively training networks as their depth increases. The motivation is clear and compelling.

2.  **Intuitive and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed architecture is a clever adaptation of the successful gating mechanism from LSTMs to the feedforward domain. The formulation is simple, elegant, and provides a clear intuition for why it might alleviate optimization issues.

3.  **Strong Optimization Results:** The experiments in Section 3.1 provide a convincing demonstration that highway networks are substantially easier to optimize than plain networks at significant depths (50-100 layers). Figure 1 clearly illustrates that while plain networks degrade with depth, highway networks maintain stable training performance. This is the paper's strongest empirical result.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is a valuable contribution. The visualization of gate activities and the lesion study offer compelling insights into the network's learned behavior. The finding that the network can learn to functionally ignore unnecessary layers for simpler tasks like MNIST, while utilizing most layers for a more complex task like CIFAR-100, is particularly interesting and supports the authors' claims about adaptive depth.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The primary baseline throughout the paper is the "plain" feedforward network. While the comparison is stark, it is widely accepted that such networks are notoriously difficult to train at depth without specialized techniques. The paper's argument would be significantly stronger if it included comparisons to other established methods for enabling deep training. The concept of shortcut or "skip" connections is not new (e.g., Szegedy et al., 2014; deep supervision in Lee et al., 2014), yet the paper does not compare against a network with simple, fixed identity skip connections. The central innovation here is the learnable gating mechanism; without an ablation study that compares directly to a non-gated identity shortcut (`y = H(x, WH) + x`), it is impossible to determine if the added complexity and parameters of the gates are truly necessary. The performance benefits might simply come from the shortcut path itself, rather than its dynamic gating.

2.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the application of LSTM-style gating to feedforward architectures is new, the underlying principle of using shortcut connections to improve gradient flow is an existing idea. The contribution is thus a dynamic, learnable version of this principle. Given the lack of comparison to fixed shortcuts, the degree of practical innovation remains unclear.

3.  **Reliance on a Specific Initialization Strategy:** The authors state that training is facilitated by initializing the transform gate biases to negative values (e.g., -1 to -3). This biases the network towards carry behavior at the start of training. While presented as a simple scheme, this introduces a new, sensitive hyperparameter. This feels less like a fundamental architectural improvement and more like trading one initialization "trick" (e.g., careful weight variance scaling) for another. The paper provides only a heuristic for setting this bias based on depth, which feels ad-hoc and lacks rigorous justification.

4.  **Performance on Standard Benchmarks:** The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, while respectable, do not establish a new state-of-the-art. The authors themselves state their goal is to demonstrate trainability, not to push performance. However, for a new and more complex architecture to gain widespread adoption, it should ideally demonstrate a clear advantage in terms of final accuracy over simpler, established methods. As it stands, the paper demonstrates that one *can* train very deep networks this way, but not that doing so leads to superior results over well-tuned, shallower, or alternatively-stabilized architectures.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant architectural idea to address the important problem of training very deep networks.
*   It provides strong empirical evidence that the method successfully overcomes the optimization hurdles faced by plain networks at extreme depths.
*   The analysis of the trained models provides valuable scientific insight into how such an architecture functions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The experimental validation is incomplete. The lack of a comparison to a simple, non-gated identity shortcut baseline is a critical omission that weakens the paper's central claim about the importance of the gating mechanism.
*   The novelty is arguably incremental over the general concept of skip connections, and the practical benefit of the added complexity (the gates) is not sufficiently demonstrated.
*   The method's effectiveness appears to depend on a specific gate bias initialization scheme, which introduces a new, un-theorized hyperparameter.
*   The final performance on benchmark datasets is not compelling enough to justify the adoption of this more complex architecture over existing methods.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated idea. The core contribution—showing that an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism can enable stable SGD training of very deep networks—is supported by the optimization experiments. However, the work is let down by a lack of rigorous comparison to more relevant and established baselines, particularly fixed identity shortcuts. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the true value of the proposed gating mechanism. While the architecture is promising, the evidence provided is not yet sufficient to endorse it over simpler, existing approaches for training deep networks.
