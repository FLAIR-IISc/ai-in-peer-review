Summary
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces per-unit, input-dependent gating inspired by LSTM to enable information to flow across many layers without attenuation. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where H is an arbitrary transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate.” With a negative bias initialization for T, layers are initially biased to “carry” (i.e., act as identity), facilitating gradient flow across depth. The authors show that very deep highway networks (50–100 layers, with pilot experiments beyond 1000) are trainable by vanilla SGD with momentum, whereas similarly sized plain networks become hard to optimize as depth increases. On MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, highway networks train reliably, match or exceed FitNets at comparable parameter/computation budgets without teacher supervision, and exhibit interesting learned routing behavior evidenced by gate visualizations and layer lesion studies.

Relative to prior work, the paper connects to: (1) vanishing gradient remedies via initialization (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.), activation functions and local competition (maxout), and advanced optimizers; (2) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision; and (3) teacher-student training (FitNets). Its key distinction is the adaptive, multiplicative gating that enables input-dependent routing, in contrast to fixed skip connections. The paper builds on the authors’ earlier short report on Highway Networks and acknowledges related LSTM-inspired depth-wise gating. Code and hyperparameters are released.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea with strong intuition (LSTM-inspired gates) and a practical negative-bias initialization that is independent of the particular H.
- Convincing optimization study: at controlled parameter budgets, plain deep networks degrade sharply with depth, while highway networks maintain trainability and converge faster.
- Empirical scope beyond toy problems: convolutional variants on CIFAR-10/100 with competitive results at modest budgets and simple augmentation; fit-to-budget comparisons vs. FitNets without multi-stage hint training.
- Useful analyses: gate activity visualizations, class-conditional routing patterns, and lesion experiments showing which depths contribute on tasks of differing complexity; suggests dynamic and selective depth usage.
- Reproducibility: implementation details, hyperparameter search, and code availability.

Weaknesses
- Limited breadth and strength of baselines: no comparison to batch normalization, PReLU, or other contemporaneous techniques known to ease optimization; no ablation vs. fixed identity skip/residual connections (i.e., T ≡ 1).
- Results are not state of the art; the goal is trainability rather than SOTA, but stronger baselines would bolster significance.
- Some evaluations emphasize best runs from random search; broader statistics (e.g., mean/median over seeds for the optimization curves) would increase robustness.
- Additional parameters and compute due to gating are only roughly controlled by reducing width; a more rigorous accounting of parameter/compute parity across baselines would be helpful.
- Limited analysis of key design choices: C fixed as 1−T vs. independent carry gate; sensitivity to gate bias across depths/datasets; gradient norms across depth.
- Scope restricted to MNIST and CIFAR; no large-scale evaluation (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and the claims are supported by experiments and analyses. The optimization study is carefully designed to control for capacity across depths. The empirical support for “dynamic routing” is suggestive rather than conclusive, but the lesion and class-conditional gate analyses are informative. Missing comparisons to batch norm and non-gated skip connections limit the strength of the empirical case that gating is necessary (vs. simple identity shortcuts). Still, as a contribution focused on an architectural mechanism for enabling depth, the evidence is solid.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written and organized. The core idea and equations are easy to follow. The constraint that x, H(x), y, and T(x) share dimensionality is stated, and practical solutions (padding, plain layers for resizing) are discussed. Training details are adequate and code is provided. Minor issues: some typos (“varying varying,” “backpropogation”) and a missing general Jacobian expression (beyond the limiting cases) where an explicit derivative could aid readers.

Originality
The central idea—input-dependent, LSTM-like gating across layer depth—appears novel in the feedforward setting and is clearly distinguished from fixed skip connections and deep supervision. The paper appropriately references related LSTM/gating and skip-connection literature, and acknowledges a contemporaneous similar idea and the authors’ own previous short report. The combination of gating, negative bias initialization, and systematic depth scaling contributes original insights.

Significance
Enabling very deep networks to be trained reliably with standard SGD is important. Highway Networks offer a pragmatic mechanism that practitioners can adopt, potentially influencing subsequent architectures. While not SOTA, the results at constrained budgets and the analyses (routing, lesioning) are valuable. The significance would be further strengthened by stronger baselines and larger-scale experiments.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please compare to fixed identity skip connections (no gating), and to batch normalization. How much of the benefit derives from adaptive gating vs. simply adding identity paths?
- Provide more comprehensive statistics for the optimization curves (e.g., mean/median over seeds) and report parameter/compute counts precisely for each comparison.
- Analyze sensitivity to the transform-gate bias initialization across depths and datasets; show gradient-norm profiles across layers to directly evidence improved signal propagation.
- Consider ablations with an independent carry gate (C ≠ 1−T) and report any accuracy/regularization trade-offs.
- Discuss computational overhead of gating at training and inference, and whether sparse gate activations can be exploited for efficiency.
- If possible, add a larger-scale experiment to demonstrate scalability.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated architectural mechanism with clear practical benefits for training very deep networks.
- Strong optimization evidence and informative analyses (routing and lesioning).
- Competitive results at modest budgets without teacher supervision; reproducible with code.

Cons
- Missing key baselines (batch norm, identity skips) and ablations; limited datasets.
- Emphasis on best runs for some plots; less robust statistics.
- Limited theoretical analysis and incomplete discussion of overhead and sensitivity.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The idea is impactful and well supported by experiments and analyses focused on optimization and depth, though the paper would benefit from stronger baselines and broader evaluation. The contribution is likely to influence subsequent work on deep architectures.
