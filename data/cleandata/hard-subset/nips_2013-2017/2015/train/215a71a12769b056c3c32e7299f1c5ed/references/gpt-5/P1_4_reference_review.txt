Summary
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces data-dependent, LSTM-inspired gating across depth to facilitate information and gradient flow in very deep networks. Each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is a general transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a learned transform gate (with carry gate C = 1 − T). By initializing T’s bias to a negative value, layers are initially biased toward carrying inputs forward, creating “information highways” that ease optimization. The authors show: (1) fully connected highway networks with up to 100 layers are easy to optimize on MNIST, unlike plain nets with normalized initialization; (2) convolutional highway networks achieve competitive generalization on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, matching or exceeding FitNets without multi-stage hint-based training; (3) analyses of gate activities and lesion experiments suggest data-dependent routing and selective usage of depth: early layers are critical, and depth usage scales with dataset complexity.

Relation to prior work is well-drawn. The architecture is explicitly inspired by LSTM/forget gates for temporal credit assignment, adapted to depth. It contrasts with previous techniques for deep feedforward training: careful initialization (Glorot, He), improved optimization (HF, momentum, saddle-point), locally competitive activations (maxout), skip/error-injection connections, deep supervision, and knowledge distillation (FitNets). The authors argue their learned multiplicative gates provide dynamic, input-dependent routing rather than fixed shortcuts.

Quality
- Empirical support: The MNIST optimization study convincingly shows that, at fixed per-layer parameter budget, deeper plain nets become hard to train while highway nets do not; convergence is faster and final training loss much lower. Convolutional highway nets on CIFAR-10/100 reach accuracy competitive with or better than similarly budgeted FitNets using single-stage SGD. Analyses of gate biases/activities and layer lesioning are informative, supporting the claims of data-dependent routing and selective use of depth.
- Fairness/controls: Comparisons to plain nets control for parameter count per layer in the MNIST FC setting. On CIFAR, comparisons to FitNets are plausible but would benefit from clearer parameter/FLOP accounting given that gates add parameters and compute. The effect of the gating on capacity vs optimization is not fully disentangled.
- Missing ablations: Important design choices are not thoroughly ablated: (i) using C = 1 − T versus learning an independent carry gate; (ii) the magnitude of the negative bias and its interaction with depth/activation; (iii) the contribution of additional parameters due to gates; (iv) replacing H with identity to isolate the role of routing; (v) comparisons to other skip-connection variants (e.g., fixed shortcuts) available at the time. The paper mentions pilot results beyond 1000 layers but does not provide quantitative evidence.
- Rigor: The theoretical discussion (Jacobian behavior) is intuitive but limited. Strong optimization claims are empirical, which is acceptable, but a bit more analytical insight into gradient flow (e.g., conditions for non-vanishing Jacobians due to gates) would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear motivation, definition of the layer, and training procedure. Figures (as described) appear to illuminate behavior.
- Notational consistency could be improved (· vs element-wise ∗), and the gating parameterization for convolution (spatial vs channel gating, parameter counts) should be specified more precisely.
- Experimental details are mostly adequate; reporting Best (mean ± std) over 5 runs is appreciated. Please add exact parameter/FLOP counts for all CNNs, the precise LR schedule, momentum, weight decay, and data augmentation details for reproducibility.

Originality
- The core idea—LSTM-like gates across depth in feedforward nets—is novel and nontrivial. While skip connections and deep supervision had been explored, learned multiplicative, input-dependent gates that interpolate between identity and H are a substantive contribution. The initialization strategy of negatively biased gates tailored to depth is also a useful practical insight. The work is well-situated with respect to relevant prior art (initialization, maxout, FitNets, local competition, deep supervision).

Significance
- Addressing the optimization barrier to training very deep networks is important. Highway Networks provide a simple, general mechanism that appears to work across FC and convolutional settings, and the analyses suggest interesting computational properties (dynamic routing, adaptive use of depth). Even if not state-of-the-art on CIFAR with heavy augmentation, the method’s generality and training stability make it likely to impact practice and subsequent research. Public code availability enhances impact.

Pros
- Simple, general architectural modification enabling training of very deep nets with SGD.
- Strong empirical evidence that optimization does not degrade with depth; faster convergence.
- Competitive CIFAR results without multi-stage distillation; single-stage training.
- Insightful analyses of gating, routing, and layer importance; code release.

Cons
- Limited ablations disentangling optimization benefits from added capacity due to gates.
- Choice C = 1 − T not justified vs independent carry; lack of analysis on its effect.
- Comparisons could include additional baselines (fixed skip connections, deep supervision) and more precise compute/parameter matching.
- Theoretical treatment is minimal; some claims (e.g., >1000 layers) not quantitatively substantiated in the main experiments.

Questions for the authors
- How do results change with an independent carry gate C(x) (e.g., sigmoid with its own bias), and with constraints T + C ≤ 1 versus C = 1 − T?
- How sensitive is performance to the initial transform-gate bias and its learning rate? Can you provide a sweep across depths and datasets?
- What is the exact parameter and FLOP overhead introduced by T for each CNN architecture? Are the CIFAR comparisons strictly parameter/compute matched?
- Do gates saturate at inference time (near 0 or 1), and does this vary across classes? Could pruning be guided by gate statistics?
- How do highway networks interact with regularization methods (dropout, weight decay) and with alternative normalizations?

Minor comments
- Unify notation for element-wise operations. Fix typos (e.g., “varying varying”).
- Clarify whether T is per-feature-map, per-channel, or per-spatial-location in conv layers.
- Consider adding pseudocode for a highway layer block and initialization.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper introduces a novel and practical architectural mechanism with clear empirical benefits for training very deep networks, provides insightful analyses, and is likely to influence subsequent work. My confidence is high.
