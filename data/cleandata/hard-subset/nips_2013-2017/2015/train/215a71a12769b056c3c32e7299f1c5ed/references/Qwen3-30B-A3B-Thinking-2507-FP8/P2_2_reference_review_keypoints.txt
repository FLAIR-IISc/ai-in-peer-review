- Proposes "highway networks" using LSTM-inspired transform and carry gates to enable information flow in very deep feedforward nets.
- Demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD to 100+ layers, unlike plain networks which struggle with depth.
- Provides experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 comparing highway networks to plain nets and Fitnets.
- Presents analysis of gate behavior showing data-dependent routing and layer importance (e.g., Figure 4).
- Architecture and equations are clearly defined and the experimental setup is thorough and convincing.
- Empirical results show dramatic improvements in training loss for deep highway vs. plain networks (e.g., 100-layer MNIST).
- Lacks theoretical justification for why highway networks improve optimization and for the heuristic gate initialization.
- Comparison to Fitnets and other methods is limited in scope and doesn’t explore alternate hyperparameters or architectures.
- Clarity and organization are excellent, with effective visualizations (Figure 2) aiding understanding.
- Exposition of the transform gate could be clearer for readers unfamiliar with LSTM, and computational-efficiency discussion is missing.
- Novel idea at the time: applying LSTM-style gating to feedforward networks, distinct from prior work like Fitnets and pre-training.
- Addresses the fundamental problem of training extremely deep networks and provides a useful tool for probing layer contributions.
- Significance is tempered because CIFAR results do not show clear accuracy gains over state of the art—mostly shows training feasibility rather than superior performance.
- Strengths: solid experiments, ability to train 100+ layers, insightful gate analysis, and novel architectural idea.
- Weaknesses: no theoretical analysis, limited SOTA comparisons, missing computation-efficiency evaluation, and modest gains on benchmarks.
- Recommendation: Accept — valuable contribution despite limitations, provides a strong foundation for later developments (e.g., ResNet).
