## Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and significant contribution to the field of deep learning by introducing Highway Networks, a novel architecture that demonstrably enables the training of extremely deep feedforward networks using standard stochastic gradient descent. The paper is well-written, clearly explains the motivation and methodology, and provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. While not entirely novel in its inspiration (drawing from LSTM), the specific application to feedforward networks and the resulting performance gains are substantial. I recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the difficulty of training very deep feedforward networks. The authors observe that increasing depth, while theoretically beneficial, often leads to vanishing gradients and optimization challenges.  Inspired by the gating mechanisms of LSTMs, they propose “Highway Networks” – feedforward networks augmented with “transform” and “carry” gates allowing for more direct information flow across layers. This effectively creates pathways for gradients to propagate more easily, mitigating the issues associated with depth.

The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing literature.  It acknowledges prior work on optimizers (Adam, Momentum), initialization strategies (Glorot/He initialization), activation functions, skip connections, and knowledge distillation. The connection to LSTM is clearly articulated, and the authors acknowledge related work by Srivastava et al. (2015) and others proposing similar LSTM-inspired mechanisms.  The framing of the problem – the inherent difficulty of training very deep *plain* networks – is well-established and accurately presented.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:**  While inspired by LSTM, applying the gating mechanism to standard feedforward networks to overcome depth-related training issues is a significant contribution. The ability to train networks with hundreds of layers directly with SGD is a substantial advance.  The paper demonstrates that depth *can* be effectively leveraged without resorting to complex training procedures.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing.  The comparison between plain and highway networks on MNIST demonstrates a clear advantage in training deeper architectures. The results on CIFAR-10/100, showing competitive performance with FitNets and state-of-the-art methods (especially considering the relative simplicity of the architecture) are particularly noteworthy.  The ablation studies (layer importance analysis) provide valuable insight into how the network functions.
* **Clear Explanation & Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind the highway network architecture. The mathematical formulation is concise and easy to understand.  The analogy to information "highways" is intuitive and aids in understanding the core concept.
* **Thorough Analysis:** The analysis of the transform gate biases and activity patterns (Figure 2) provides valuable insight into the network's internal workings.  Demonstrating data-dependent routing and the ability to identify important layers adds further depth to the study.
* **Code Availability:**  Publicly available code and hyperparameter search results are excellent for reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper demonstrates good performance, the selection of hyperparameters (especially the initial transform gate bias) seems somewhat heuristic. More systematic exploration and discussion of the sensitivity of the network to these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Gate Complexity:** The paper primarily focuses on a relatively simple form of the gating mechanism (T = 1-C). While this simplicity is a strength in terms of ease of implementation, it would be interesting to explore more complex gating functions or adaptive gate weights.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the paper shows that training is possible, the computational cost of training *very* deep highway networks might still be substantial. A more detailed discussion of the computational requirements compared to other deep learning architectures would be helpful.
* **Theoretical Understanding:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis explaining *why* highway networks are more trainable. A deeper understanding of the optimization landscape and the role of the gating mechanism would be valuable.



**4. Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant improvement in training deep feedforward networks.
* Strong empirical validation on multiple datasets.
* Clear and well-written explanation of the proposed method.
* Publicly available code.
* Demonstrates the possibility of efficiently utilizing extreme depth.

**Con:**

* Hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.
* Limited exploration of alternative gating mechanisms.
* Lacks a comprehensive theoretical justification.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on the systematic exploration of the initial transform gate bias?  What is the impact of different bias values on training speed and final performance?
* Have you experimented with more complex gating functions or adaptive gate weights? If so, what were the results?
* What is the approximate computational cost (training time, memory usage) of training a 100+ layer highway network compared to a similarly performing deep convolutional network without highway connections?
* Do you have any intuition or preliminary theoretical analysis explaining why the negative initialization of the transform gates is so effective?



**Final Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The proposed Highway Networks represent a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning, offering a practical and effective solution to the challenge of training very deep feedforward networks.  While there are some areas for future exploration, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and its potential impact on the field is significant.  This paper fits well within the scope of NIPS and is likely to be of broad interest to the research community.
