Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that adds learned, LSTM-inspired gating between layers to enable information to flow across very many layers without attenuation. Each highway layer computes y = T(x)·H(x) + (1−T(x))·x, where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate” initialized with negative biases to favor carry behavior early in training. By allowing data-dependent interpolation between a transformed signal and the identity, the model creates “information highways” across depth. The authors provide optimization studies on thin fully connected nets (up to 100 layers) showing dramatic improvements over plain nets with modern variance-preserving initialization, convolutional results on MNIST, and CIFAR-10/100 comparisons to FitNets without teacher hints. They also analyze gate behaviors, data-dependent routing, and “lesioning” layers to infer effective depth.

This work connects to several lines: (a) skip/shortcut connections for improved gradient flow and credit assignment; (b) LSTM gating to preserve and regulate information across time—here repurposed across depth; (c) improved initialization (Glorot/He), which still leaves very deep plain nets hard to train; (d) student–teacher training (FitNets), which this paper aims to obviate with a single-stage training procedure; and (e) locally competitive activations (maxout/ReLU variants). The paper also acknowledges contemporaneous LSTM-like models operating across spatial/computation dimensions (e.g., Grid LSTM).

Quality
- Soundness: The core idea is clearly defined, and the training recipe (negative gate bias) is reasonable and well-motivated by LSTM practice. The optimization experiments are carefully controlled on MNIST with matched parameter counts per layer, demonstrating a clear depth scaling advantage and faster convergence. CIFAR experiments show that highway nets can match or surpass FitNets without hints at comparable budgets.
- Empirics and support: While the optimization evidence is strong, the generalization evidence is mixed. CIFAR results are competitive but not state-of-the-art in the standard augmentation regime; the study intentionally avoids heavy augmentation and very wide models. Important ablations are missing: (1) comparison to fixed skip/residual connections without gates; (2) the impact of enforcing C=1−T versus learning both gates; (3) gate parameterization granularity (per-unit vs per-channel) and computational/parameter overhead; (4) sensitivity to the initial transform-gate bias; and (5) gradient flow diagnostics (e.g., gradient norms across depth) to support the mechanistic claim. The paper claims successful pilot training with >1000 layers but reports quantitative results only up to 100 layers.
- Analysis: The qualitative analyses (gate biases, mean/single-sample activities, class-conditional routing, layer lesioning) are insightful and support the narrative that gates perform input-dependent routing and that effective depth adapts to task complexity. A minor technical point: the Jacobian discussion (Eq. 5) omits the dependence of T on x; the stated extremes are heuristic (as acknowledged). A more precise statement or an appendix derivation would improve rigor.
- Reproducibility: Code availability is a plus. Some experimental details are sparse (exact CIFAR architectures, schedules, training time, compute overhead), limiting full reproducibility without digging into the released scripts.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-structured, and the figures are informative. A few issues: typos (“varying varying,” “backpropogation”), several incomplete or inconsistently formatted references, and occasional ambiguity about architecture specifics (e.g., gating tensor shape in conv layers, per-feature-map vs per-pixel gating). The MNIST/CIFAR architectures and training schedules could be described more concretely in the main text.

Originality
Introducing learned, LSTM-style gates across depth in feedforward networks is novel and conceptually elegant. While skip connections have a long history, the learned, input-dependent interpolation between identity and transform distinguishes this approach from fixed shortcuts and auxiliary-loss methods. The connection to LSTM gate bias initialization is thoughtfully leveraged.

Significance
The work addresses a central challenge—training very deep networks—with a simple, general mechanism that applies to fully connected and convolutional layers. Even without cutting-edge accuracy, the contribution is likely to influence architectures and training practice. The routing and lesion analyses offer a valuable perspective on how deep models use their depth. The potential impact would be strengthened by broader benchmarks and comparisons to ungated residual shortcuts.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea grounded in well-understood LSTM gating.
- Strong optimization evidence showing stable training with depth and faster convergence versus plain nets.
- Demonstrates single-stage training of thin-and-deep models matching FitNets without hints.
- Insightful analyses (gate activity, class-conditional routing, lesioning) that illuminate the mechanism.
- Code and scripts released.

Weaknesses
- Limited benchmarks and absence of larger-scale results (e.g., ImageNet).
- Missing direct comparison to fixed skip/residual connections; unclear tradeoff between gating benefits and added compute/parameters.
- Sparse ablations (C=1−T vs free C, gate granularity, bias sensitivity).
- Claims of extreme depth (>1000 layers) not substantiated with quantitative results.
- Some clarity/referencing issues.

Questions for the authors
1) How does a highway network compare to a residual/identity-skip network of matched capacity and depth on the same tasks? Does gating help beyond fixed addition?
2) What is the compute and parameter overhead of T (and C if decoupled) relative to H for conv layers, and how does that impact accuracy/efficiency tradeoffs?
3) How sensitive is performance to the initial gate bias, and can you report gradient norm statistics across depth?
4) Have you tried per-channel or spatially pooled gates to reduce cost?
5) Does relaxing C=1−T improve performance, and does it compromise stability?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a novel, general, and simple mechanism.
- Strong optimization results and clear empirical evidence of easier training with depth.
- Insightful analyses that enhance understanding of deep computation and routing.
- Public code, likely to be adopted and extended.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical scope is limited; lacks direct comparison to ungated residual shortcuts.
- Missing ablations leave some design choices and their necessity unquantified.
- Moderate performance on standard vision benchmarks relative to state-of-the-art.
- Minor clarity and reference issues.

Recommendation
Lean toward acceptance. The architectural idea and the optimization evidence are compelling and of broad interest to the community. Strengthening the paper with a direct comparison to fixed identity skip connections, basic ablations (C free vs 1−T; gate granularity), and clearer architectural/training details would make the contribution even more definitive.
