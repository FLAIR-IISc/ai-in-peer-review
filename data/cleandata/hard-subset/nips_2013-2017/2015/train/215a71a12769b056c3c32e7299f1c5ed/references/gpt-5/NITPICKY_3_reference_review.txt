Summary
This paper proposes Highway Networks: feedforward layers endowed with elementwise gates that interpolate between transforming the input and carrying it forward unchanged. A highway layer computes y = H(x, WH) · T(x, WT) + x · (1 − T(x, WT)) with T(x) = σ(WT x + bT) and C fixed as 1 − T. Biasing bT negative initializes the network to favor carry paths, facilitating gradient flow across many layers (LSTM-inspired). The authors show that fully connected highway nets of 50–100 layers train easily on MNIST, whereas similarly sized plain nets degrade with depth, and demonstrate competitive convolutional results on MNIST/CIFAR-10/100 without elaborate augmentation or teacher hints. They analyze learned gate statistics and perform layer “lesioning” to assess layer importance, arguing for dynamic, input-dependent routing and automatic use of required depth.

Relation to prior work
The idea is positioned among efforts to enable depth: improved optimizers [13–15], variance-preserving initialization [16,17], locally competitive activations [20,21], skip connections [22–24], distillation/fitnets [25], and layerwise pretraining [26,27]. The gating mechanism is explicitly LSTM-inspired [29,30]. Compared to earlier skip/shortcut connections, highways add multiplicative, input-dependent gating instead of fixed bypasses. The CIFAR experiments compare to FitNets [25], showing single-stage training of deeper/thinner models with comparable parameter/multiply budgets.

Strengths
- Simple, generic architectural motif (applies to fully connected and convolutional layers) with a practical initialization rule that works across depths and nonlinearities.
- Clear empirical evidence that depth per se becomes tractable: optimization curves show large gaps versus plain nets under matched per-layer parameter counts.
- Useful analyses: gate bias evolution, sparsity/mean activity, class-conditional gating patterns, and lesion experiments to probe effective depth.
- Reproducibility is helped by code release and reporting of mean ± std over 5 seeds in several experiments.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/formal:
- The Jacobian discussion is incomplete/misleading. Equation (5) only states limiting cases T=0 or T=1, which never occur for sigmoid gates; the general Jacobian should include terms from both H′ and ∂T/∂x (e.g., dy/dx = diag(1 − T) + diag(T) H′ + additional terms from (H − x) ⊙ T′). Omitting this makes the gradient-flow argument qualitative.
- Fixing C = 1 − T unduly restricts expressivity versus independent carry/transform gates (as in LSTM). No ablation is provided comparing independent C vs 1 − T.
- The “blocks” terminology is underspecified; it appears each block yields Hi(x) and Ti(x) with equal dimensionality, but whether gates are per-unit, per-channel, or per-spatial-location (in conv layers) is not crisply defined. This matters for parameter/computation counts and inductive bias.

Experimental methodology:
- Compute/parameter accounting is insufficiently precise. While per-layer parameter parity is matched in MNIST FC experiments (50-dim highway: WH 50×50 and WT 50×50 ≈ 5k; plain 71×71 ≈ 5k), the convolutional experiments double filters for gates. Exact parameter and multiply counts should be tabulated per model in Tables 1–3, including gate overhead, to substantiate “comparable budgets” to FitNets.
- MNIST/CIFAR results are presented without the actual tables/figures in the submission excerpt; it is difficult to verify statistical significance or fairness. Some results are reported as “Best (mean ± std)”, but not consistently.
- Hyperparameter selection: learning-rate schedules are tuned on CIFAR-10 and reused for CIFAR-100; while conservative, this may mask the method’s potential and complicates fairness to baselines. Random search for plain vs highway nets on MNIST is appropriate, but details of search ranges and budget for CIFAR are unclear.
- Missing baselines: no comparison to identity skip connections without gating (i.e., residual connections) under the same settings, nor to batch normalization, which is a strong depth enabler contemporaneously. A direct comparison would clarify whether gating is essential beyond skip paths.
- Claims of training “>1000 layers” are mentioned as pilot successes but no quantitative results or configurations are provided.
- The lesioning analysis indicates many mid-depth layers are unused on MNIST; an ablation showing performance vs truncated depth would corroborate the claim that highways adaptively use needed depth.

Clarity/presentation:
- Several typos and inconsistencies: “varying varying depths”; “backpropogation”; missing transposes in T definition (“WTTx” likely WT^T x); dangling footnote markers; inconsistent notation between vectors/matrices; equations lack bias terms then reintroduce them later.
- Reference list is incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing venues/years, duplicated author names, placeholders “[cs]”, etc.). Some important related works on skip/shortcut connections and normalization are insufficiently discussed.
- Details for convolutional gating (kernel sizes for T, per-channel vs per-location gates) are brief; a schematic would improve clarity.

Significance/originality
The central idea—learnable gating of skip/carry paths to enable very deep feedforward training—is novel at the time of writing and potentially impactful. If properly substantiated, it addresses a core problem (depth) with a simple mechanism and provides diagnostic tools (gates) not available with fixed skips. However, the absence of stronger contemporary baselines and precise compute accounting weakens the empirical case for broad significance.

Questions for authors
- Provide the full Jacobian derivation and discuss how the T′ terms affect gradient flow.
- Report exact parameter and multiply counts for each CIFAR model (including gating), and wall-clock training time vs plain nets.
- Clarify the gating granularity in conv layers (per-unit vs per-channel vs per-spatial-location). Did you try cheaper gates (e.g., per-channel 1×1)?
- Show ablations: independent carry gate vs C = 1 − T; identity skip without gates; effect of gate bias bT across depths; batch normalization with/without highways.
- Provide quantitative results for the claimed >1000-layer pilot and truncated-depth ablations suggested by the lesion findings.

Minor edits
- Fix typos and notation inconsistencies; specify WT^T x; ensure all equations define dimensions and include biases consistently.
- Clean and complete the bibliography (venues, years) and correct citation ranges.
- Avoid ambiguous terms like “blocks” without formal definitions.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and likely important, but the submission has numerous methodological and presentation issues (some minor but many in aggregate) and lacks key baselines and precise resource accounting. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, I would lean to accept in a revised form.

Confidence
High (familiar with deep architectures, gating, and depth-enabling techniques).
