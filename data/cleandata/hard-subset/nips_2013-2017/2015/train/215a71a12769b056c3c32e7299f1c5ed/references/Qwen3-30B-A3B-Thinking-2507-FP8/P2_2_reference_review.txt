

# Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces "highway networks" as a novel architecture for training extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The key innovation is the incorporation of transform gates and carry gates inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, which allow information to flow through the network without attenuation. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD up to 100+ layers, whereas plain networks suffer from optimization difficulties as depth increases. The paper includes extensive experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, comparing highway networks to plain networks and Fitnets, and provides analysis of how the gating mechanisms behave during training.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-designed experiments. The highway network architecture is clearly defined (Equation 3), and the authors provide convincing empirical evidence that the approach enables training of very deep networks. The experiments on MNIST show that 100-layer highway networks outperform similarly-sized plain networks by more than two orders of magnitude in training loss. The analysis in Section 4 provides valuable insights into how the transform gates behave during training, demonstrating data-dependent routing of information.

However, the paper lacks theoretical justification for why highway networks train better than plain networks. While the authors present a heuristic initialization strategy (negative bias for transform gates), they don't provide any theoretical analysis of how this affects the optimization landscape. The comparison to Fitnets is compelling but limited to specific network architectures without exploring whether highway networks could achieve better performance with different hyperparameters or architectures.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical formulation of highway networks is precise and easy to follow. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, and the results are presented with appropriate figures and tables. The concept of "information highways" is effectively visualized in Figure 2, which helps readers understand how information flows through the network.

Minor improvements could be made to the exposition of the transform gate mechanism, which might be confusing for readers unfamiliar with LSTM. The paper could also provide a more detailed comparison of computational efficiency between highway networks and plain networks.

## Originality
The core idea of applying LSTM-style gating to feedforward networks is novel for the time of this paper. While the authors acknowledge inspiration from LSTM networks, they successfully extend this concept to a new domain (feedforward networks) where it was previously unapplied. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on deep networks by addressing the specific challenge of training networks with 100+ layers directly with SGD.

The paper is distinct from related work like Fitnets [25], which uses a two-stage training procedure with soft targets from a teacher network. It also differs from approaches like layer-wise pre-training [26, 27], which are less efficient than direct training. While later work like ResNet (published in 2015) would build on similar concepts, this paper was published before ResNet and represents an important early contribution to the problem of training very deep networks.

## Significance
The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training extremely deep networks. By demonstrating that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD up to 100+ layers, the paper enables researchers to explore the benefits of greater depth without resorting to complex two-stage training procedures. The ability to directly examine the contribution of each layer (Figure 4) provides a valuable new tool for understanding network architecture.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the results on standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) don't represent major improvements over the state of the art. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained, but doesn't show that they achieve superior accuracy compared to other approaches at similar depth. The paper would have been stronger if it had demonstrated significantly better performance than existing methods rather than merely showing that training is possible.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Clear, well-executed experimental validation of the highway network concept
- Demonstrated ability to train networks with 100+ layers directly with SGD
- Valuable analysis of gate behavior and layer importance
- Novel application of LSTM-inspired gating to feedforward networks

**Weaknesses:**
- Lack of theoretical analysis to explain why highway networks train better
- Limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods beyond Fitnets
- No detailed discussion of computational efficiency compared to other approaches
- Results on CIFAR datasets don't demonstrate significant accuracy improvements

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that extremely deep networks can be trained directly with SGD through a simple architectural modification. While the paper has some limitations in theoretical justification and comparative performance, it addresses an important practical problem in deep learning with clear experimental evidence. The highway network concept has influenced later work (including ResNet, which was published around the same time), and the paper provides a solid foundation for understanding how to train very deep networks. The experiments are thorough, the writing is clear, and the results are significant for the field of deep learning. The paper represents a meaningful step forward in understanding how to train very deep networks, making it suitable for publication at NIPS.
