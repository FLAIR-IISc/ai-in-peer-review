Summary
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture with adaptive, element-wise “transform” and “carry” gates inspired by LSTMs. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), enabling input-dependent routing of information along paths with low attenuation (“information highways”). A simple negative bias initialization for T makes early training biased toward carry behavior, facilitating gradient flow. The authors show that very deep fully connected and convolutional highway networks (50–100+ layers, with pilot runs >1000 layers) can be trained directly with SGD, without layer-wise pretraining or teacher hints. Empirically, highway networks optimize reliably with increasing depth (unlike plain nets), match or exceed FitNets on CIFAR-10 in a single-stage training regime, and achieve competitive results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 without heavy augmentation. Analyses visualize gate activations, show data-dependent routing across samples/classes, and include a lesion study demonstrating which layers are functionally used.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several lines: LSTM-style gated information flow; skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision for improved gradient propagation; normalized initialization for deep nets; and hint-based training (FitNets). Unlike fixed skip connections, the highway layer learns input-dependent gates, generalizing the idea of bypass paths. Compared to teacher distillation/hints, the method trains thin/deep networks in a single stage using standard SGD. The paper also contrasts with optimizer-centric work and variance-preserving initialization, emphasizing an architectural solution.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: A clear, principled architectural mechanism that directly tackles the depth/optimization barrier by importing gating from RNNs into feedforward/convolutional nets. The formulation is simple, general, and easily composable.
- Empirical evidence on depth: Convincing optimization study showing that performance of plain nets degrades with depth while highway nets remain stable and converge faster; pilot successes with extreme depth.
- Practicality: A simple, general initialization heuristic (negative bias on T) that appears robust across activations and settings. No reliance on complex training protocols; code is shared.
- Insightful analysis: Visualizations of gate biases/activities, sample- and class-conditioned routing, and a lesion study that quantifies layer importance. These provide rare visibility into learned computation depth and dynamic routing.
- Significance: If broadly validated, this enables training much deeper models with standard tooling; the idea is broadly applicable (FC, conv, possibly recurrent grids).

Weaknesses and questions
- Experimental scope: Results are limited to MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with modest augmentation. There is no large-scale validation (e.g., ImageNet) nor stronger baselines (e.g., contemporary deep conv nets with aggressive augmentation). The claims focus on optimization ease rather than SOTA accuracy, but a larger experiment would strengthen significance.
- Baselines for skip connections: The paper motivates adaptive gates versus fixed skip connections but does not empirically compare to depth-matched architectures with identity shortcuts (or other recent skip-connection variants). This comparison is essential to isolate the value of learned gating vs. simply providing bypass paths.
- Ablations: More systematic ablations would help:
  - C = 1 − T versus independent carry and transform gates (parameterization choice, stability, performance).
  - Scalar/per-channel gates versus full element-wise gates (compute/memory/accuracy trade-offs).
  - Necessity and sensitivity of the negative bias; show failure modes without it and performance curves versus bias values.
  - Effect of the architecture of T (depth, receptive field size) and of sharing gates across layers/locations.
- Computational overhead: There is limited discussion of parameter/FLOP overhead from gates and the runtime/throughput impact. A breakdown would help practitioners.
- Reporting clarity: Some tables/figures (not visible here) are described qualitatively. More precise numbers (mean ± std) against strong baselines would aid assessment.
- Theory: Beyond intuitive Jacobian observations, there is no formal analysis of gradient propagation or expressivity benefits under gating; even simple analyses (e.g., expected singular values of layer Jacobians under gate bias) would bolster the argument.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-motivated. Claims are broadly supported by experiments, though limited in scope and missing key baselines. The analyses are thoughtful and go beyond raw metrics. Code availability improves reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core layer equation and intuition are easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Clarify whether gates are per-unit/per-feature-map and how gates are implemented in convolution (spatial vs channel-wise gating).
- Provide exact parameter counts and FLOPs for major models and the gate overhead.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “varying varying”, “backpropogation”) and unify notation.

Originality
High. While skip connections and deep supervision are known, using LSTM-style gating to create input-dependent paths through depth is a substantial conceptual advance and a fresh architectural direction.

Significance
High. The approach addresses a central obstacle—training very deep networks—with a simple, general mechanism. Even without SOTA accuracy, demonstrating robust optimization at depth and dynamic routing is impactful and likely to influence subsequent architectures and training practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a significant new architectural concept (gated skip pathways) that enables very deep training with SGD.
- Strong evidence of improved optimization with depth; insightful analyses of routing and layer utility.
- Simple, practical initialization; code released.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing empirical comparison to fixed skip connections.
- Limited dataset scope and absence of large-scale results.
- Insufficient ablations on gate design and initialization sensitivity; limited reporting on computational cost.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper introduces a compelling architectural idea with clear potential to shape deep learning practice. Despite some experimental limitations and missing baselines, the novelty and demonstrated benefits justify acceptance.

Confidence
High.
