

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks using standard SGD. The authors address a fundamental challenge in deep learning: as networks grow deeper, they become increasingly difficult to train due to poor gradient and activation propagation. Their key insight is to adapt LSTM's gating mechanism to feedforward networks, creating "information highways" that allow information to flow across many layers without attenuation.

The authors propose a simple yet powerful modification to standard feedforward layers: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x)), where T(x) is a transform gate (a sigmoid output) controlling how much of the layer's output comes from transforming the input versus carrying it through. This architecture allows the network to smoothly transition between "transforming" and "carrying" behaviors, creating pathways for information to flow across many layers. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD even at depths of 100+ layers, in contrast to plain networks which become intractable at similar depths.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, the core idea is genuinely novel - while skip connections have been used before (e.g., [22, 23, 2, 24]), the highway network's continuous gating mechanism that allows dynamic information routing is a significant conceptual advance. Second, the experimental validation is thorough, showing consistent improvements across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets. Third, the paper provides valuable insights into how highway networks function, particularly the "information highways" phenomenon visualized in Figure 2, where many layers maintain constant output patterns across layers.

The significance of this work is high. The ability to train networks with 100+ layers directly (without two-stage training) opens up new possibilities for exploring the limits of depth in neural networks. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can match or exceed the performance of shallower networks with comparable computational budgets, and their analysis of layer importance (Figure 4) provides new insights into how deep networks actually utilize their layers.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the experimental setup is well-documented. The authors acknowledge limitations appropriately (e.g., the need for careful initialization of the transform gate bias) and provide clear comparisons to previous work.

However, there are notable weaknesses. Most significantly, the paper does not properly contextualize its contribution relative to Residual Networks (ResNets), which were published in the same year (2015) and also addressed the problem of training very deep networks. ResNets use skip connections with identity mappings to create similar information pathways, but without the continuous gating mechanism. The paper's claim of novelty is weakened by this omission, as ResNets were presented at the same venue (ICLR 2016, though the work was available earlier) and are directly relevant. The authors should cite and discuss ResNets in their related work section.

Another minor issue is the lack of theoretical analysis explaining why highway networks are easier to train than plain networks. While the experimental evidence is compelling, a more rigorous theoretical foundation would strengthen the paper.

Despite these weaknesses, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution. The highway network architecture is a bold and novel approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper's main contribution is not merely a technical improvement, but a new conceptual framework for understanding and enabling information flow in very deep networks.

The authors have demonstrated that extremely deep networks can be trained directly with SGD, which is a major practical advance. The concept of "information highways" is both intuitive and powerful, and the paper provides valuable insights into how these networks operate. While the omission of ResNets is a notable oversight, the core idea remains novel and impactful.

In summary, this paper introduces a genuinely novel architecture that addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning. The authors have provided strong experimental evidence supporting their claims, and their work opens up new possibilities for exploring very deep neural networks. Despite the minor omission of ResNets in related work, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field and should be accepted.

Pro: Novel architecture addressing a fundamental deep learning challenge; strong experimental validation; clear conceptual insights into information flow; significant potential impact on deep learning research.
Con: Omission of ResNets in related work; lack of theoretical analysis; limited discussion of computational cost compared to ResNets.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty and significance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses, particularly given the reviewer's preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection.
