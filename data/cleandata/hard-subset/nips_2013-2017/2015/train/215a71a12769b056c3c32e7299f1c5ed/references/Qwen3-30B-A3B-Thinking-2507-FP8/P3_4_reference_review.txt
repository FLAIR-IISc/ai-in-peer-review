

# Peer Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces "Highway Networks" as a method to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks using stochastic gradient descent. The core innovation is a gating mechanism inspired by LSTMs, where each layer computes y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), with T being a transform gate controlling how much of the input is transformed versus carried through. The authors claim that highway networks overcome the optimization difficulties of very deep plain networks, demonstrating successful training of 100+ layer architectures on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

The paper correctly situates its work within the context of deep learning challenges, citing relevant prior work on initialization strategies, skip connections, and knowledge distillation. However, it misses a critical piece of related work: the Residual Network (ResNet) architecture, which was published in the same year (2015) and shares the fundamental concept of skip connections to enable training of very deep networks. While the authors acknowledge "skip connections" in the introduction, they don't engage with ResNet, which later became the dominant approach for very deep networks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation**: The paper effectively identifies the fundamental challenge of training very deep networks and the limitations of existing approaches.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The MNIST experiments (Section 3.1) clearly demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly. The 100-layer highway network outperforms similarly-sized plain networks by more than two orders of magnitude.

3. **Insightful Analysis**: Figures 2-4 provide valuable insights into how highway networks operate, particularly regarding the dynamic routing of information (Section 4.1) and layer importance (Section 4.2). The observation that highway networks can learn to utilize all layers for complex problems (CIFAR-100) but not for simpler ones (MNIST) is particularly compelling.

4. **Simple and Effective Architecture**: The highway network design is elegant and builds on existing concepts in a way that's easily implementable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to ResNet**: This is the paper's most significant weakness. ResNet (He et al., 2015) was published in the same year and directly addresses the same problem with a simpler skip connection approach. The paper should have explicitly compared highway networks to ResNets, as ResNets have become the dominant approach for deep networks. The authors' claim that "deep networks can be trained layer-wise to help in credit assignment" is largely obviated by ResNet.

2. **Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides empirical evidence, it lacks a strong theoretical explanation for why highway networks work better than plain networks. The Jacobian analysis (Equation 5) is insightful but doesn't fully explain the optimization benefits.

3. **Computational Overhead**: The paper doesn't address the additional computational cost of the transform gates. Highway networks require additional parameters for the gates, which could impact training time and memory usage compared to plain networks.

4. **Comparison to Batch Normalization**: The paper was written before Batch Normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), but it should have acknowledged that this technique also enables training of very deep networks, potentially making the highway network less necessary.

5. **Overstated Claims**: The claim that highway networks "can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent" is misleading. The paper relies on a specific initialization strategy (negative bias for transform gates), which is a critical component of their method but not presented as such.

## Significance and Originality

The paper makes an original contribution by introducing a flexible gating mechanism for deep networks, but its significance is diminished by the subsequent rise of ResNets. While the highway network's dynamic routing capability is interesting, ResNets' simpler fixed skip connections have proven more practical and effective in practice.

The paper's claim that highway networks "allow us to examine how much computation depth is needed for a given problem" (Section 5) is valuable, but this capability could also be achieved with residual connections.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with strong empirical results that addresses an important problem in deep learning. The highway network architecture is clever and provides useful insights into information flow in deep networks. However, the paper's significance is substantially reduced by its failure to engage with ResNet, which was published in the same year and has become the standard approach for training very deep networks.

The paper would have been much stronger if it had compared highway networks to ResNets, discussed why highway networks might be preferable in certain scenarios, or demonstrated that highway networks outperform ResNets in specific cases. Without this, the paper's contribution appears somewhat incremental in the context of the broader deep learning community's progress.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

This paper makes a valid contribution to the field, but its significance is limited by the lack of comparison to ResNet and the subsequent dominance of residual connections in deep network architectures. The experimental results are strong, but the paper would have been substantially more impactful if it had engaged with the most relevant contemporary work. If the authors can address the comparison to ResNet and provide a clearer explanation of when highway networks might be preferable to residual networks, this would be a strong paper. As it stands, it's an interesting but somewhat dated contribution that doesn't fully capture the direction the field has taken since 2015.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison to ResNet, discussing both similarities and differences in approach.
2. Clarify that the negative bias initialization is a critical component of their method, not just a simple initialization trick.
3. Discuss computational overhead and training time comparisons between highway networks and ResNets.
4. Acknowledge the relationship to Batch Normalization and other techniques that enable training of deep networks.
5. Provide a clearer theoretical explanation for why highway networks work better than plain networks.

While the paper's core idea is valuable, its historical context and relationship to the most successful approach for training deep networks (ResNet) need significant clarification to demonstrate its true contribution to the field.
