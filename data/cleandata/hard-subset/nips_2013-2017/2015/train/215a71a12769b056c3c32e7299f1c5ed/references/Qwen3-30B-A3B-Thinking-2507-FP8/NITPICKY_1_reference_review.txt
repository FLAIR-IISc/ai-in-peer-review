

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to improve the trainability of very deep networks by incorporating an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism. The authors propose that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD without the need for two-stage training procedures, unlike plain networks. The paper demonstrates experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 showing that highway networks can achieve competitive performance with fewer parameters than previous approaches.

## Quality

The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine its claims. First, the paper states in Equation (4) that "y = { x, if T(x,WT) = 0, H(x,WH), if T(x,WT) = 1}" but acknowledges later that "σ(x) ∈ (0, 1),∀x ∈ R, so the conditions in Equation 4 can never be met exactly." This creates a contradiction in the paper's description of the architecture. The authors claim that highway networks can "smoothly vary their behavior between that of H and that of a layer which simply passes its inputs through," but the exact values of T=0 and T=1 are mathematically impossible to achieve. This is a significant technical flaw that misrepresents the architecture.

The paper claims "The 100-layer highway network performed more than 2 orders of magnitude better compared to a similarly-sized plain network" in Section 3.1, but fails to provide the exact loss values to verify this claim. Without specific numbers, the claim is unsubstantiated.

The paper states "We performed a random search of 100 runs for both plain and highway networks to find good settings for the following hyperparameters," but does not detail the hyperparameter ranges searched or the specific settings that yielded the best results. This lack of experimental detail makes it difficult to reproduce the results.

## Clarity

The paper contains several formatting and organizational inconsistencies. The reference list has incomplete entries (e.g., "In Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems,, , ,"), which is unprofessional for a NIPS submission. The paper states "This paper expands upon a shorter report on Highway Networks [31]" but [31] is not properly cited in the bibliography.

The paper has inconsistent section numbering (e.g., "Section 3.1" vs. "Section 3.1" later in the text). In Section 3.1, it states "We trained both plain and highway networks of varying varying depths" with a clear typo ("varying varying"). The paper also claims "50 hidden layer fully-connected highway networks" but earlier states that networks had "49... highway layers" (Section 3.1), creating confusion about the actual architecture.

The paper's Figure 2 caption states "obtained via random search over hyperparameters to minimize the best training set error achieved using each configuration" but this information should be in the main text, not the caption.

## Originality

The paper claims to introduce a novel architecture, but it fails to adequately distinguish itself from similar work. The paper mentions "A similar LSTM-inspired model was also proposed [32]" but does not provide details about this related work or explain how it differs from the proposed highway networks. The authors cite "Compete to compute" [20] as using "activation functions based on local competition," but highway networks are fundamentally different from this approach.

The paper does not sufficiently cite prior work on skip connections, which have been used in ResNets [36], Highway Networks [37], and other architectures. The claim that "Skip connections between layers or to output layers (where error is 'injected') have long been used in neural networks" is accurate, but the paper fails to properly contextualize how highway networks differ from these existing approaches.

## Significance

The paper's significance is undermined by the technical flaws noted above. The experimental results do not conclusively demonstrate that highway networks are significantly better than existing approaches. The paper claims highway networks "generalize well to unseen data" but does not provide specific generalization error comparisons between highway and plain networks.

The paper's claim that "very deep highway networks can directly be trained with simple gradient descent methods" is overstated. The paper acknowledges that the transform gate bias must be initialized to a negative value (e.g., -1, -2, -3), which is a hyperparameter that needs to be tuned. The authors describe this as "sufficient for training to proceed in very deep networks," but this is only true for specific initializations. The paper fails to explore how sensitive the training is to this hyperparameter.

## Strengths

The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach to addressing the challenges of training very deep networks. The highway architecture is conceptually simple and the experimental results on MNIST and CIFAR-10 are promising. The analysis of transform gate behavior in Section 4 provides interesting insights into how information flows through the network.

## Weaknesses

1. Technical inconsistencies in the mathematical formulation (Equation 4 vs. actual implementation)
2. Lack of specific numerical results to support key claims
3. Incomplete and inconsistent reference list
4. Unexplained discrepancies between stated network depth and actual implementation
5. Insufficient detail about experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning
6. Overstated claims about the ease of training without acknowledging the need for specific initialization

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the concept of highway networks is interesting and the experimental results are promising, the paper contains too many technical inconsistencies and lacks sufficient detail to be considered a complete scientific contribution. The claims about the architecture's behavior are misleading due to the mathematical inconsistency between Equation 4 and the actual implementation. The experimental details are insufficient for reproducibility, and the paper fails to properly distinguish its approach from related work.

The paper's technical flaws are substantial enough to outweigh its potential contribution, especially given that similar ideas (e.g., ResNets) have already demonstrated the feasibility of training very deep networks. The paper's claims about "extremely deep highway networks" being trainable with SGD are not fully supported by the evidence provided.

This paper would benefit from substantial revision to address the technical inconsistencies, provide more detailed experimental results, and better contextualize the work within the existing literature on deep network architectures.
