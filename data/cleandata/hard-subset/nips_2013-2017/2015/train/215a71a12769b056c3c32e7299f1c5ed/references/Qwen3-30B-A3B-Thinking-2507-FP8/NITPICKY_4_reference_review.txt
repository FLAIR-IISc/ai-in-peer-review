

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper proposes highway networks as a solution to the training difficulties of very deep networks, introducing an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism that allows information to flow across many layers without attenuation. The authors claim that highway networks can be trained directly using SGD, unlike plain networks which become hard to optimize as depth increases.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel architectural modification that addresses a well-known challenge in deep learning. The experimental results on MNIST and CIFAR datasets are convincing, showing that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade. The analysis of transform gate behavior and layer importance provides valuable insights into how these networks operate. The approach is conceptually simple yet effective, and the paper's open-sourcing of code and hyperparameters is commendable.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Technical Inconsistencies**: The paper contains several critical technical errors. In Equation 3, the authors define y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), but T(x,WT) is defined as a scalar (T(x) = σ(WTTx + bT)), while x, y, and H are vectors. This creates a dimensionality mismatch since a scalar cannot be multiplied element-wise with a vector. The authors later refer to T as a vector in Equation 4, creating confusion. This fundamental error undermines the theoretical foundation of the proposed architecture.

2. **Misrepresentation of Prior Work**: The paper misrepresents the "FitNets" approach in reference [25], claiming it uses "soft targets" when it actually uses intermediate layer activations as hints. This inaccuracy damages the paper's credibility and suggests the authors may not have fully understood the prior work they're comparing against.

3. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The term "blocks" is used without clear definition (Section 2.1). The paper states "a highway network consists of multiple blocks such that the ith block computes a block state Hi(x) and transform gate output Ti(x)" but never specifies what constitutes a block or how it relates to layers.

4. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for 0 and 1 (vectors vs. scalars in Equation 4), and the term "highway" is capitalized inconsistently (e.g., "Highway Networks" vs. "highway networks").

5. **Experimental Details**: The paper claims "roughly identical numbers of parameters (≈5000) per layer" for highway (50 blocks) and plain (71 units) networks but provides no calculation to support this claim. The statement "Best (mean ± std.dev.) based on 5 runs wherever available" is vague and doesn't clarify how many runs were actually performed for each experiment.

6. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper suggests negative bias initialization for transform gates is "strongly inspired by the proposal [30] to initially bias the gates in an LSTM network," but fails to provide any theoretical justification for why this specific initialization strategy works for highway networks.

7. **Formatting Issues**: References contain inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Deep networks with internal selective attention through feedback connections, Marijn F Stollenga, Jonathan Masci, Faustino Gomez, Jürgen Schmidhuber, In NIPS, , , ,"). The paper also contains grammatical errors (e.g., "very deep networks can represent certain function classes far more efficiently than shallow ones" should be "much more efficiently").

8. **Misleading Claim**: The paper states "the n bit parity problem can in principle be learned by a large feedforward net with n binary input units, 1 output unit, and a single but large hidden layer. But the natural solution for arbitrary n is a recurrent net with only 3 units and 5 weights." This is factually incorrect - a recurrent network with 3 units cannot solve the n-bit parity problem for arbitrary n (it would require a counter that can count up to n).

## Conclusion

While the highway network concept is interesting and the experimental results are promising, the paper contains critical technical errors that undermine its scientific validity. The fundamental dimensionality mismatch in the proposed architecture (Equation 3) is particularly concerning, as it suggests a misunderstanding of basic neural network operations. The misrepresentation of prior work and numerous grammatical/typographical errors further diminish the paper's quality.

The paper's main contribution - that highway networks enable direct training of very deep networks - is significant, but the implementation details and theoretical foundation are not sufficiently rigorous. Given the technical errors, the paper would require substantial revision before it could be considered for publication. Without these issues being addressed, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for technical soundness and clarity.

The paper has potential but currently falls short of the quality expected for NIPS. I recommend rejection due to the critical technical errors and lack of theoretical grounding for the proposed approach.
