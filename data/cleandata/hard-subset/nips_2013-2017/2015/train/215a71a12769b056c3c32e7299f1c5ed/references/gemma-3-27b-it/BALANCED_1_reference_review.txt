This paper proposes “Highway Networks”, a novel neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. The key idea is to introduce “highway” connections between layers, inspired by the gating mechanisms in LSTMs, allowing information to flow more easily across many layers. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained with SGD to depths exceeding 100 layers, outperforming plain networks, and achieving competitive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the established trend of increasing network depth in achieving state-of-the-art results in machine learning. The authors clearly articulate the challenges associated with training very deep networks (vanishing/exploding gradients) and position their work as a direct response to these challenges, drawing parallels with the success of recurrent networks (specifically LSTMs) in handling long-range dependencies. The connection to LSTM is well-explained and serves as a strong conceptual basis for the proposed architecture.  The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the benefits of highway networks in terms of both trainability and generalization performance. The analysis of the learned transform gate biases and layer importance provides valuable insights into the inner workings of these networks.  The related work section is thorough, referencing relevant publications in the field of deep learning, initialization strategies, and alternative methods for training deep networks (e.g., FitNets).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network architecture represents a conceptually novel approach to addressing the challenges of training very deep feedforward networks.  While inspired by LSTMs, the application to feedforward networks and the resulting performance gains are significant.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture is mathematically well-defined, and the authors provide a clear explanation of its components and their interactions. The use of transform and carry gates, and the connection to Jacobian properties, adds to the technical rigor.
*   **Strong Experimental Validation:** The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100), demonstrating the effectiveness of highway networks. The comparison with plain networks and FitNets provides a clear benchmark of their performance.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of transform gate biases and layer importance provides valuable insights into how the network learns and utilizes its depth. This goes beyond simply demonstrating performance gains and contributes to a deeper understanding of the architecture.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, the specific details of the search process are not fully elaborated. It would be beneficial to provide more information about the range of hyperparameters explored and the criteria used for selection. The sensitivity to the initial transform gate bias, while acknowledged, could be further investigated.
*   **Computational Cost:** The addition of transform gates introduces additional parameters to the network, increasing its computational cost. The paper does not explicitly address the trade-off between increased depth and computational complexity.  A more detailed analysis of the computational cost, particularly in comparison to other deep learning architectures, would be valuable.
*   **Limited Exploration of Gate Variations:** The paper primarily focuses on a specific implementation of the transform and carry gates (C = 1-T). Exploring other variations of the gating mechanism could potentially lead to further performance improvements.
*   **Discussion of Alternatives:** While the related work section is comprehensive, the discussion of alternative approaches for training deep networks could be more nuanced. For instance, a more detailed comparison with techniques like batch normalization and residual networks (ResNets, which emerged concurrently and arguably provide a simpler solution) would be beneficial. Although the paper briefly touches upon residual networks in the discussion section, a more in-depth comparison is warranted.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound architecture for training very deep feedforward networks.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains compared to plain networks.
*   The analysis of the learned parameters provides valuable insights into the behavior of the network.
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in deep learning and offers a promising solution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the highway network architecture is not fully addressed.
*   The hyperparameter tuning process could be more thoroughly documented.
*   The comparison with alternative techniques like ResNets is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed highway network architecture is a novel and effective approach to training very deep feedforward networks. The experimental results are compelling, and the analysis provides valuable insights into the inner workings of the network.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept**. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.  I recommend the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above by providing more details about hyperparameter tuning, analyzing the computational cost, and offering a more in-depth comparison with ResNets in a revised version.
