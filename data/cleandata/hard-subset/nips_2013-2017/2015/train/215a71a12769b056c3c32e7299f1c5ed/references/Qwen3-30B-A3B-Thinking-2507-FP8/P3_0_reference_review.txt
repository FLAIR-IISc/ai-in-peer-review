

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (over 100 layers) using standard SGD. The key innovation is a gating mechanism inspired by LSTMs that allows information to flow through layers without attenuation, creating "information highways." The architecture uses transform gates (T) and carry gates (C) to dynamically control information flow: y = H(x)·T + x·(1-T). The authors demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, unlike plain networks, and achieve competitive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 compared to state-of-the-art methods.

## Quality
The paper presents rigorous experimental validation, with thorough comparisons between highway and plain networks across multiple datasets and architectures. The experimental design is sound, with statistical reporting (Best (mean ± std.dev.)) and multiple runs to account for random initialization effects. The authors convincingly demonstrate that highway networks can be trained directly with over 100 layers, while plain networks fail to converge at such depths.

However, the theoretical justification for the highway architecture is underdeveloped. While the paper correctly notes that the Jacobian can become the identity matrix when T=0 (equation 5), it fails to provide a formal proof that this prevents vanishing/exploding gradients across many layers. The claim that highway networks "do not suffer from increasing depth" requires stronger theoretical grounding beyond empirical observations.

The paper also lacks comparison to ResNets (He et al., 2015), which were published around the same time and use a similar concept of skip connections. The authors cite a similar LSTM-inspired model [32] but don't clarify whether this refers to ResNets, which would be a major omission given ResNets' subsequent dominance in deep learning.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, the highway network architecture is clearly defined, and the experimental results are presented with appropriate detail. The analysis section (Figure 2-4) provides valuable insights into how the transform gates behave during training. The paper is sufficiently detailed for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

## Originality
The paper draws reasonable inspiration from LSTM networks, but the key innovation (applying LSTM gating to feedforward networks) is not entirely novel. The paper's main contribution—demonstrating that highway networks can be trained directly without specialized initialization—has been largely superseded by the ResNet approach, which achieved similar benefits with a simpler architecture (additive skip connections instead of multiplicative gates).

The paper acknowledges related work but doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from ResNets, which were published around the same time. This is a significant oversight given ResNets' subsequent impact on the field. The highway network architecture is more complex than ResNets (requiring additional parameters for the gating mechanism) and has not demonstrated clear advantages in practice.

## Significance
The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training very deep networks. If the claims held, this would be a significant contribution. However, the significance is substantially diminished by the fact that ResNets were published around the same time and have since proven to be more effective in practice with a simpler architecture.

The experimental results are promising but not groundbreaking. Highway networks achieve competitive results on CIFAR-10, but the improvements over state-of-the-art (e.g., FitNets) are modest (0.5-1.0% accuracy gain), and the paper doesn't demonstrate significant advantages over simpler approaches like ResNets. The claim that highway networks "can match/exceed the accuracy of wide and shallow maxout networks" is not particularly novel, as similar results were reported with other deep learning approaches.

## Strengths
1. Clear demonstration that extremely deep networks can be trained with standard SGD
2. Well-designed experiments with thorough statistical reporting
3. Valuable analysis of transform gate behavior during training
4. Simple and intuitive architectural modification

## Weaknesses
1. Theoretical justification for why highway networks work is weak
2. Failure to properly distinguish from and compare to ResNets (a major related work)
3. Overlooks computational overhead of gating mechanism
4. Doesn't address scalability to networks with 1000+ layers (despite claiming "more than 1000 layers" in experiments)
5. Lacks comparison to other techniques like batch normalization

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem and presents promising results, its significance is limited by the timing of publication (coinciding with ResNets) and failure to adequately distinguish itself from this more impactful work. The highway network concept has been largely superseded by the simpler and more effective ResNet architecture.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A thorough comparison with ResNets
2. Stronger theoretical justification for the highway architecture
3. Analysis of computational overhead
4. Discussion of how highway networks compare to other modern deep learning techniques

Given the field's subsequent trajectory, I recommend rejection. While the work is technically sound, its contribution has been largely eclipsed by more effective approaches that appeared around the same time.

## Final Assessment
This paper presents a reasonable approach to training deep networks but fails to make a compelling case for its significance in the context of the field's development. The highway network concept is interesting but has been largely superseded by the simpler and more effective ResNet architecture. The paper's claims are not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS, especially given the availability of stronger alternatives.
