This paper investigates training very deep feedforward networks, a persistent challenge in the field. The core idea – highway networks inspired by LSTM gating mechanisms – is a reasonable approach to address vanishing/exploding gradient issues. The authors present a novel architecture with "transform" and "carry" gates, aiming to facilitate information flow across many layers. They demonstrate, through experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, that these networks can be trained to significant depths (over 100 layers) using standard SGD, outperforming plain networks of comparable size.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical formulation of highway layers is concise and easy to follow. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, including initialization strategies, skip connections, and FitNets. The inclusion of publicly available code and hyperparameters is commendable, promoting reproducibility. 

However, despite the clarity and completeness of presentation, I remain skeptical of the claimed contributions. The core innovation – adding gating mechanisms to feedforward networks – feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. LSTM networks have successfully employed gating for sequence modeling for decades; applying a similar concept to feedforward architectures, while potentially effective, doesn't represent a substantial departure in thinking. The novelty lies more in the *application* of a known technique than in the technique itself.

A significant weakness is the lack of thorough comparison to more established methods specifically designed for training deep networks. While comparisons to "plain" networks are presented, these comparisons feel somewhat unfair. Plain networks are notoriously sensitive to initialization; the authors utilize normalized initialization, but a more exhaustive exploration of initialization schemes (e.g., orthogonal initialization, more sophisticated adaptive initialization) might yield more competitive results for the baseline. Furthermore, the comparison to FitNets is limited. The authors claim to achieve comparable or better accuracy, but the architectures used for comparison (Fitnet A and B) are not state-of-the-art, even at the time of publication. A more rigorous evaluation against more recent and optimized deep network architectures would strengthen the claims. 

The analysis section is interesting, visualizing gate activity and layer importance. However, the conclusions drawn from these visualizations are not strongly supported by the data. For example, the observation of sparse gate activity doesn't necessarily indicate effective routing; it could simply mean the gates are learning to close frequently. The layer importance analysis, while insightful, doesn't account for potential interactions between layers.

The paper relies heavily on empirical results. While these results are positive, the justification for the observed performance improvements remains somewhat superficial. A theoretical analysis of why highway networks mitigate the vanishing gradient problem, or how the gating mechanism affects the representational capacity of the network, would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates that very deep networks *can* be trained with a relatively simple approach (highway connections and careful initialization).
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Publicly available code and hyperparameters.
*   Positive empirical results on standard datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental innovation; the core idea is not fundamentally new.
*   Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning techniques.
*   Limited theoretical analysis.
*   Conclusions drawn from the analysis section are not strongly supported by data.
*   The improvements, while present, aren’t dramatic enough to justify a significant shift in how deep networks are approached.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and presents interesting empirical results, the lack of substantial novelty, limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and weak theoretical justification do not meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS. The work feels more like a practical engineering solution than a fundamental contribution to the field. A more thorough evaluation and deeper analysis are needed to establish the true significance of highway networks.
