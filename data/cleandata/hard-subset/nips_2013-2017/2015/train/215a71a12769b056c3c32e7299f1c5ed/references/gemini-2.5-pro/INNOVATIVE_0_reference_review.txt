**Summary**
This paper addresses the fundamental difficulty of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, the "Highway Network," inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The core idea is to introduce adaptive gating units that regulate the flow of information through the network. Each layer can dynamically choose to pass some of its input directly through (the "carry" path) while transforming the rest (the "transform" path). This creates unimpeded "information highways" that allow gradients to propagate smoothly across hundreds of layers, overcoming the vanishing gradient problem that plagues traditional "plain" networks. The authors demonstrate that this architecture allows for the direct training of networks with 100 layers or more using standard SGD, a task that is shown to be intractable for plain networks of equivalent depth.

**Strengths**
The primary strength of this paper lies in its exceptional novelty and the significance of its core idea. The problem of training deep networks is one of the most persistent and important challenges in the field. While prior work has chipped away at this problem with improved initializations, activation functions, or static skip connections, this paper presents a fundamentally new and elegant solution.

1.  **Bold Conceptual Leap:** The adaptation of LSTM's temporal gating mechanism to a spatial, feedforward architecture is a highly creative and insightful conceptual leap. It moves beyond hard-wired shortcuts to a dynamic, data-dependent routing mechanism. This allows the network to learn not just a complex function but also *how* to route information to compute that function efficiently. This is a profound shift in thinking about deep architectures.

2.  **Effective and Simple Solution:** The proposed mechanism is remarkably simple, yet the empirical results are powerful. The direct comparison of plain vs. highway networks of increasing depth (Figure 1) provides a clear and convincing demonstration that the architecture solves the core optimization problem it sets out to address. The ability to train a 100-layer network to a loss orders of magnitude lower than a plain counterpart is a dramatic result.

3.  **Opens New Research Directions:** This work doesn't just present a better architecture; it opens up a new way to study and understand deep learning. The analysis in Section 4, which investigates learned gate behavior and performs a "lesion study" on layers, is particularly compelling. The finding that the network can learn to use only as much depth as a problem requires (e.g., using ~15 layers for MNIST but ~40 for CIFAR-100) is a fascinating insight into model capacity and resource allocation. This work provides the tools to empirically answer the question, "How much depth does a problem need?"

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The core idea is presented with a clean and intuitive mathematical formulation. The motivation is clear, the experiments are well-designed to test the central hypothesis, and the results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses**
The paper's weaknesses are minor and largely related to the scope of the empirical validation, which is understandable given the focus on introducing a new core concept.

1.  **Modest Benchmark Performance:** The reported results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, while competitive, do not set a new state-of-the-art. The authors are forthright that their goal was not to exhaustively tune hyperparameters for a specific dataset, but rather to demonstrate the trainability of the architecture. Given the conceptual significance of the work, this is an entirely acceptable tradeoff. The strength of the idea does not depend on beating the SOTA on CIFAR-10.

2.  **Limited Scale of Experiments:** The experiments are confined to smaller datasets (MNIST, CIFAR). While sufficient for a proof-of-concept, validation on a large-scale task like ImageNet would have made the claims even more impactful. However, the rigor of the controlled optimization experiments provides strong evidence that the principles will hold more generally.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is a clear and enthusiastic accept. It exemplifies the kind of bold, concept-driven research that NIPS should champion. It tackles a grand challenge in the field with a novel, elegant, and surprisingly simple idea. The potential impact is enormous, as it effectively removes a long-standing barrier to exploring the benefits of extreme network depth. While the experimental results could be more comprehensive (e.g., on ImageNet), the presented evidence is more than sufficient to validate the central claim and demonstrate the power of the approach. The insightful analysis of the network's learned behavior further elevates the work from a mere architectural proposal to a significant contribution to our understanding of deep models. The paper's conceptual novelty and significance far outweigh any minor limitations in its empirical scope. This work is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.
