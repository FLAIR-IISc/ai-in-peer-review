### Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary**
This paper introduces Highway Networks, a novel feedforward neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep models. The core idea, inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, is to introduce adaptive gates that control information flow across layers. Each layer can dynamically vary between transforming its input and carrying it through unchanged. This mechanism creates unimpeded "information highways" that allow for effective gradient propagation even through hundreds of layers. The authors empirically demonstrate that, unlike traditional "plain" networks which degrade in performance as depth increases, Highway Networks can be trained directly with SGD at depths of up to 100 layers on MNIST. They further show competitive performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 and provide an analysis suggesting that the networks learn to route information in a data-dependent manner and utilize an "effective depth" appropriate to the task's complexity.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Architecture:** The central idea of adapting LSTM-style gating to feedforward architectures is intuitive, elegant, and well-motivated. It provides a principled way to address the degradation problem in deep networks, connecting it to the well-studied problem of vanishing gradients in recurrent networks.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for Optimization:** The experiments in Section 3.1 provide a clear and compelling demonstration of the primary claim. While plain networks fail to train as depth increases, highway networks maintain strong performance up to 100 layers, effectively decoupling optimization difficulty from network depth.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** Section 4 offers valuable insights into the behavior of these networks. The visualization of gate activities and the lesioning study in Section 4.2 are particularly strong contributions. The finding that the network appears to learn an "effective depth" based on task complexity (i.e., using fewer layers for MNIST than for CIFAR-100) is an important result that could influence future architecture design.
4.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly significant problem in deep learning. By presenting a viable method for training extremely deep networks, it opens avenues for exploring the benefits of depth on a wider range of problems.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

Despite the compelling core idea, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in its presentation, methodological rigor, and argumentation.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript falls short of the expected quality for a top-tier publication.
    *   **Citation and Referencing:** The reference list is in a state of disarray. Formatting is inconsistent across entries, many are incomplete (e.g., [1], [2], [3] are missing venue details; [6] contains stray text), and some citations appear to be missing entirely (e.g., the paper mentions [32] as a similar model, but this reference is not in the provided list). This is a critical flaw that hinders the reader's ability to contextualize the work.
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** The dot operator `(·)` is defined as element-wise multiplication, but Footnote 2 introduces a `∗` operator without definition, creating ambiguity.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** The paper mentions a "more complex block design" and pilot experiments with "> 1000 layers" but provides no details or supporting data for these claims, reducing transparency and reproducibility.

2.  **Methodological Concerns:**
    *   **Model Selection:** Footnote 3 reveals a critical flaw: the "best" models used for the analysis in Section 4 were selected by minimizing *training set error*. This is a highly unorthodox procedure that promotes overfitting and invalidates any conclusions drawn about the model's generalization or learned representations. The entire analysis, a key part of the paper's contribution, is built on this questionable foundation.
    *   **Inconsistent Experimental Protocol:** The authors use two different learning rate schedules in their experiments (Section 3.1 vs. the rest) without a clear justification for the switch. Furthermore, hyperparameters tuned on CIFAR-10 were directly reused for CIFAR-100, which is suboptimal and weakens the CIFAR-100 results.
    *   **Ambiguous Reporting:** The results are reported in the format "Best (mean ± std.dev.)". It is unclear if "Best" refers to the single best run out of five, or the mean/std of the runs for the single best hyperparameter configuration. This ambiguity makes it difficult to interpret the stability and significance of the reported results.

3.  **Inconsistent Argumentation:**
    *   The authors claim in the Discussion that a key advantage of their method is that "derivation of a suitable initialization scheme is not essential." This directly contradicts their own methodology in Section 2.2, which relies entirely on a carefully chosen negative bias initialization for the transform gates. This heuristic is described as a hyperparameter that is crucial for training to proceed, undermining the claim of robustness.
    *   The simplification `C = 1 - T` is justified only "for simplicity," without any ablation or discussion of why an independent carry gate was not considered, which might offer more flexibility.

**Recommendation**

While the core concept of Highway Networks is significant and influential, this particular manuscript is not ready for publication at NIPS. The numerous flaws in presentation, the questionable methodological choices (especially the model selection criterion), and the internal contradictions in the paper's arguments are too substantial to overlook. The work presents a novel idea, but the execution and reporting lack the meticulous care and rigor required. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should be encouraged to substantially revise the manuscript to address these issues, as the underlying work is promising.
