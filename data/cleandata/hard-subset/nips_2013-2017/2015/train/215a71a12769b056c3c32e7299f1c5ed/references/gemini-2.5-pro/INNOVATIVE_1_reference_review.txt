**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the well-known difficulty of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a novel architecture, the "Highway Network," directly inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The core idea is to introduce adaptive gates for each layer that dynamically regulate the flow of information. Specifically, a layer's output is a weighted sum of its transformed input (the standard non-linear layer output) and its unmodified input. These weights are determined by a learned "transform gate." By setting the transform gate to zero, a layer can effectively become an identity mapping, creating an unimpeded "information highway" for gradients and activations to flow across many layers. The authors demonstrate empirically that this architecture allows for the direct training of networks with tens or even hundreds of layers using standard SGD, a feat that is not possible with conventional "plain" networks of similar depth. They provide strong experimental evidence on MNIST and CIFAR datasets and include insightful analysis into the learned behavior of the gates.

**Strengths**
This paper presents a bold, elegant, and highly significant contribution to the field. Its primary strengths are:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Clarity:** The central idea of adapting LSTM's gating mechanism to feedforward architectures is both clever and powerful. While skip connections have existed for some time, the concept of a *learned, dynamic, and data-dependent* information path is a major conceptual leap. The formulation `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 - T(x))` is simple, elegant, and directly addresses the information propagation problem by allowing layers to dynamically vary between transformation and identity mapping. This is precisely the kind of foundational architectural innovation that can unlock new research directions.

2.  **Direct and Effective Solution to a Fundamental Problem:** The paper tackles a critical and widely acknowledged challenge: the degradation of performance when training extremely deep networks. Instead of proposing incremental improvements to optimizers or initialization schemes, the authors offer a fundamental architectural solution. The experiments in Section 3.1 provide a striking demonstration of this, showing that while plain networks falter beyond 20 layers, highway networks can be trained effectively at 100 layers and beyond. The ability to train such networks directly, without complex multi-stage procedures like those required for FitNets, underscores the power and practicality of the approach.

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Insight:** The authors go beyond simply reporting error rates. Section 4 provides a fascinating analysis of the network's internal workings. The lesioning study (Figure 4) is particularly compelling. The finding that the network learns to use most of its depth for a complex task (CIFAR-100) but keeps many layers idle for a simple one (MNIST) is a beautiful result. It suggests that highway networks can learn their own "effective depth," making depth less of a risky hyperparameter and more of a capacity budget that the model learns to utilize as needed. This is a profound insight that could change how we approach model design.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation of the Core Idea:** The experiments are well-designed to support the central thesis. The direct comparison of plain vs. highway networks at increasing depths is the key result and is very convincing. The comparison against FitNets effectively demonstrates the superiority of a direct, single-stage training paradigm enabled by the highway architecture. While not breaking all SOTA records, the performance on CIFAR-10/100 is strong and clearly shows the method generalizes well.

**Weaknesses**
The paper is very strong, and its weaknesses are minor in comparison to its contributions.

1.  **Limited Exploration of State-of-the-Art Performance:** The experiments are primarily focused on demonstrating the feasibility of training deep networks, rather than pushing the state-of-the-art on benchmarks like CIFAR. While the authors are transparent about this goal, a more aggressive attempt to achieve SOTA performance with a very deep highway network could have made the paper's impact even more immediate. However, given the novelty of the core concept, this is a minor omission.

2.  **Relationship to Concurrent Work:** The footnote mentioning a similar, more recent model [32] (which I assume is the work on Residual Networks, given the timeline) is noted. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the relationship between highway networks and other forms of gated or additive skip connections, even if they appeared concurrently. This would help to better situate the work in the rapidly evolving landscape of deep architectures.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity of Gate Initialization:** The authors propose initializing the transform gate biases to negative values, which is a clever heuristic inspired by LSTM training. However, this introduces a new, potentially sensitive hyperparameter. While guidelines are provided, a more detailed analysis of the impact of this bias or an exploration of more adaptive initialization schemes would be welcome.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and significant architectural concept that directly solves the problem of training very deep networks.
*   The idea is elegant, well-motivated by analogy to LSTMs, and provides a more powerful, dynamic alternative to fixed skip connections.
*   The core claim is supported by strong, clear, and convincing experimental evidence.
*   The analysis section provides deep insights into the model's behavior, particularly the idea that networks can learn their own "effective depth," which is a contribution in itself.
*   This work opens up a new and important direction for research in deep network architectures.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental results, while demonstrating the core concept, are not pushed to the absolute state-of-the-art on competitive benchmarks.
*   The discussion of related and concurrent work could be more comprehensive.

**Conclusion:**
This is an outstanding paper that introduces a foundational new idea. The concept of "information highways" is a breakthrough for training very deep networks. The authors not only propose a novel and effective architecture but also provide excellent analysis that illuminates *why* it works and what it learns. The weaknesses are minor and pertain more to the completeness of the experimental survey than to the validity or importance of the core contribution. This is precisely the type of bold, conceptually-driven research that NIPS should champion. It is a landmark paper that will undoubtedly inspire a great deal of follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
