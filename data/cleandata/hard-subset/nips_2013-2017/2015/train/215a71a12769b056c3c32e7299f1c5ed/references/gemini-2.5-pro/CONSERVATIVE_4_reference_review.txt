### Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary:**
This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel feedforward network architecture designed to facilitate the training of very deep models. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanism in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The authors propose augmenting standard network layers with adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates. These gates dynamically control the flow of information, allowing a layer to either transform its input, as in a conventional network, or pass it through unchanged. This mechanism creates "information highways" that allow gradients to propagate smoothly across many layers without attenuation. The authors demonstrate empirically that Highway Networks with tens or even hundreds of layers can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent, overcoming the optimization difficulties that plague standard "plain" networks of comparable depth. Experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 show that these deep networks can be trained effectively and achieve competitive performance.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The difficulty of training very deep neural networks is a well-known and significant challenge in the field. The paper tackles this problem head-on with a clear and well-motivated approach.
2.  **Compelling Optimization Results:** The experiments in Section 3.1 provide a stark and convincing demonstration of the proposed method's effectiveness. While plain networks degrade significantly in performance as depth increases beyond 20 layers, Highway Networks maintain stable and effective optimization even at 100 layers on MNIST. This is a strong piece of evidence supporting the central claim that the architecture alleviates optimization issues.
3.  **Architectural Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed modification to a standard layer is conceptually simple and easy to implement. The inspiration from the well-established LSTM architecture provides a solid conceptual grounding for the gating mechanism.
4.  **Practical Advantages over Competing Methods:** The paper shows that Highway Networks can match or exceed the performance of FitNets on CIFAR-10 without requiring the complex, multi-stage "hint-based" training procedure. The ability to train deep, thin networks in a single stage with standard SGD is a significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Empirical Validation:** While the results on MNIST and CIFAR are promising, the claims of the paper would be substantially stronger if validated on a large-scale dataset where extreme depth is known to be critical, such as ImageNet. The most impactful breakthroughs in deep learning have been demonstrated on such challenging benchmarks. Without this, it remains an open question whether the observed benefits generalize to more complex, real-world problems.
2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The concept of using shortcut or "skip" connections to improve information flow in deep networks is not entirely new. The primary novelty here lies in making these connections adaptive through a gating mechanism directly borrowed from LSTMs. While this is a clever application, the paper does not sufficiently justify the necessity of this adaptive gating over a simpler, non-gated identity mapping. An ablation study comparing the proposed gated architecture to a fixed identity skip connection would be crucial for isolating the contribution of the gating mechanism itself versus the shortcut path. It is plausible that simply providing a clean path for information flow is the dominant factor, rendering the extra parameters and complexity of the gates unnecessary.
3.  **Introduction of New Hyperparameters:** The method introduces a new, sensitive hyperparameter: the initial bias of the transform gates (`bT`). The authors themselves note its importance and provide a heuristic guideline for setting it based on network depth. This reliance on careful initialization, while effective here, can be a practical drawback. A more thorough analysis of the network's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be necessary to understand its robustness.
4.  **Preliminary Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4, while intriguing, is largely qualitative. The visualizations of gate activations suggest data-dependent routing, but this claim would be more rigorous if supported by quantitative metrics (e.g., measuring the statistical divergence of gate patterns across different classes). Similarly, the lesion study demonstrates that layers contribute to the final prediction, but it is a coarse measure of importance. The finding that many layers are "unused" on MNIST might simply indicate that the architecture allows the model to learn a simpler function when the task does not demand great depth, which is a desirable property but does not necessarily require the specific highway formulation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core idea is explained effectively with clear notation in Section 2. The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a promising approach to a significant and timely problem. The core idea is intuitive and the optimization experiments on MNIST are compelling. However, the overall contribution is tempered by concerns about the scope of the empirical validation and the incremental nature of the architectural innovation. The lack of results on a large-scale benchmark like ImageNet is a major weakness for a paper making strong claims about training very deep networks. Furthermore, the contribution of the adaptive gating mechanism over simpler, non-gated skip connections is not established.

While the work is solid and presents interesting findings, it feels more like a strong proof of concept than a fully-validated, definitive solution. It opens up an important direction but leaves critical questions unanswered. Therefore, my enthusiasm is cautious.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Clearly demonstrates a solution to the optimization problem of very deep plain networks on standard benchmarks.
*   The proposed method is simple, elegant, and well-motivated by LSTMs.
*   Shows practical benefits over a recent, more complex training methodology (FitNets).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Lack of validation on a large-scale dataset (e.g., ImageNet), which is the primary domain where extreme depth has proven essential.
*   The necessity of the adaptive gating mechanism over simpler, fixed skip connections is not demonstrated.
*   The analysis of the network's behavior is preliminary and largely qualitative.
