

# Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces "Highway Networks" as a novel architecture designed to facilitate training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The key innovation is an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism that allows information to flow through layers without attenuation. The authors propose a layer transformation: y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), where T is a transform gate (sigmoid activation) that controls how much of the input is transformed versus carried through. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained effectively with 100+ layers, whereas plain networks suffer from increasing depth. The authors provide experimental results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 showing that highway networks match or exceed state-of-the-art methods with fewer parameters and better training dynamics.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a well-executed experimental study that rigorously supports its core claim: highway networks can be trained much deeper than plain networks. The experimental design is sound, with proper hyperparameter tuning, multiple runs, and clear comparisons to relevant baselines. The analysis in Section 4 provides valuable insights into how highway networks function, particularly the "information highways" phenomenon and data-dependent routing behavior. The authors acknowledge limitations appropriately, noting that their aim was to demonstrate training feasibility rather than achieve state-of-the-art performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation is clear and accessible, with well-chosen notation. The experimental results are presented with appropriate visualizations (Figures 1-4) that effectively illustrate key findings. The authors provide sufficient detail for replication, including hyperparameter settings and training protocols. The explanation of the highway network architecture and its relationship to plain networks is particularly clear.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning—training very deep networks—which is crucial for advancing the field. The demonstration that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD without the need for two-stage training or complex initialization schemes is valuable. The analysis of information routing and layer importance provides new insights into how deep networks function. The paper shows that highway networks can achieve competitive results with fewer parameters than state-of-the-art methods, which is significant for practical applications.

## Weaknesses

**Originality:** While the highway network concept is novel, the paper fails to properly contextualize its work relative to the most influential contemporary approach to training deep networks: residual networks (ResNets), which were published in the same year (2015) but received far more attention. ResNets use a simpler skip connection (y = F(x, W) + x) that achieves similar benefits without the gating mechanism. The paper cites related work [32] but doesn't acknowledge ResNets, which would be a major oversight for a paper about training very deep networks. Without proper comparison to ResNets, the paper's contribution appears less significant than it could be.

**Theoretical Depth:** The paper provides some theoretical justification (Jacobian analysis in Equation 5), but lacks deeper theoretical analysis explaining *why* highway networks work better than plain networks. A more comprehensive theoretical treatment of the optimization landscape would strengthen the paper. The authors also don't discuss the relationship between highway networks and other gating mechanisms (e.g., LSTMs, gated recurrent units).

**Comparative Analysis:** The paper's comparison to "Fitnets" (a two-stage training approach) is relevant, but it doesn't adequately compare to the most successful deep learning architectures of the time (ResNets). Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the true novelty and potential impact of highway networks. The paper's claim that highway networks can "match/exceed the accuracy of wide and shallow maxout networks" is somewhat limited, as ResNets were already demonstrating superior performance on ImageNet.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with clear experimental evidence supporting its main claim. The quality of the work is high, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The significance of addressing the challenge of training very deep networks is high, and the highway network concept provides valuable insights into information flow in deep networks.

However, the paper's originality is compromised by its failure to properly contextualize the work relative to ResNets, which were published around the same time and became the dominant approach for training very deep networks. The paper's contribution appears to be a precursor to ResNets, but without acknowledging this relationship, the paper's novelty is diminished.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a clear mechanism for training very deep networks and offering insights into information routing in deep architectures. The experimental results are solid and well-presented. The main issue is the lack of comparison to ResNets, which should be addressed in the revision.

Specific revisions needed:
1. Add a comparison to ResNets (He et al., 2015) in the related work and discussion sections, acknowledging their similar goals but different implementation.
2. Discuss the relationship between highway networks and ResNets, noting that the simpler ResNet approach ultimately proved more effective.
3. Clarify how highway networks differ from other gating mechanisms (e.g., LSTMs) and why the authors believe their approach offers advantages.

Without these revisions, the paper's contribution may be misunderstood as a significant advance when it was actually a precursor to a more successful approach. With these additions, the paper would provide a valuable historical context for the development of deep learning architectures.

## Pro/Con Summary
**Pros:** Strong experimental evidence, excellent clarity, valuable insights into information routing, addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning.

**Cons:** Limited contextualization relative to ResNets (major omission), lack of theoretical depth, limited comparison to most successful contemporary approach.

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field, but its impact would be significantly enhanced with proper contextualization relative to the ResNet approach, which ultimately became the standard for training very deep networks.
