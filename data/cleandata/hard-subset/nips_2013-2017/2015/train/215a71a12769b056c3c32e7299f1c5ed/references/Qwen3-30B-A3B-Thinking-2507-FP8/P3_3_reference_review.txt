

# Peer Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary:**
This paper introduces "Highway Networks" as a novel architecture for training extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose modifying standard feedforward layers with an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism that allows information to flow across many layers without attenuation. The key equation is y = H(x,WH)·T(x,WT) + x·(1-T(x,WT)), where T is a transform gate controlling how much of the output comes from transformation versus carrying the input directly. The authors demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases (up to 100 layers), while plain networks degrade significantly. The paper also provides analysis of how information flows through the network, showing highway networks learn dynamic routing of information based on input.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound with a clear and well-executed experimental design. The empirical results across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets are convincing, demonstrating that highway networks can train effectively at extreme depths (100+ layers) where plain networks fail. The analysis of transform gate biases and activity patterns (Figures 2-4) provides valuable insights into network behavior.

However, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of why the highway architecture improves optimization. While the authors provide intuition based on the Jacobian (Equation 5), a more rigorous analysis of the optimization landscape would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Additionally, the paper omits comparison to residual networks (ResNets), which were published in the same year (2015) and have since become the dominant approach for training very deep networks. This omission is significant as ResNets fundamentally address the same problem with a simpler mechanism.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The experimental setup is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts like information highways (Figure 2) and data-dependent routing (Figure 3).

One minor clarity issue is the reference to "a much simplified form" of an LSTM block in Section 2.2 without elaborating on the more complex form. This could confuse readers familiar with LSTM architectures. The paper would benefit from a brief explanation of why they chose the simplified form.

**Originality:**
The core idea of using an adaptive gating mechanism to enable information flow across many layers is novel in the context of feedforward networks. The authors properly cite related work including LSTMs [29,30] and skip connections [22,23,2,24]. However, the lack of comparison to residual networks (which also use skip connections but with a different mechanism) is a significant oversight. ResNets, published in the same year, have become the standard approach for training very deep networks, and the authors would have strengthened their paper by comparing their approach to this dominant method.

**Significance:**
The problem of training very deep networks is highly significant in deep learning, and the paper makes a practical contribution that could be widely adopted. Highway networks offer a simple mechanism to train extremely deep networks without complex initialization schemes or two-stage training. The insights into information routing (Figures 3-4) provide valuable theoretical understanding of how deep networks operate.

The paper's significance is somewhat diminished by the timing of ResNet's publication, as ResNets have since become the standard approach. The highway network concept is interesting, but the paper would have been more impactful with a clear comparison to ResNets and explanation of how highway networks differ from the simpler residual connections.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid technical contribution to the field of deep learning, making a meaningful advancement in training very deep networks. The highway network architecture is well-motivated and empirically validated. The paper provides valuable insights into how information flows through deep networks, which could inform future work.

**Strengths:**
- Clear empirical demonstration of highway networks' advantages over plain networks at extreme depths
- Well-executed experiments across multiple datasets
- Valuable analysis of information routing mechanisms
- Simple implementation that can be easily integrated into existing frameworks

**Weaknesses:**
- Lack of comparison to residual networks (published in same year)
- Insufficient theoretical analysis of optimization benefits
- Minor clarity issues with LSTM-inspired architecture description

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, but with the understanding that the paper should address the comparison to residual networks in the final version. The highway network concept is valuable and interesting, and the paper makes a valid contribution to the field. However, the omission of ResNet comparison is a significant weakness that should be addressed to strengthen the paper's impact.

The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the challenges in training deep networks and provides a practical solution that could be useful in practice. The insights into information routing through the network are particularly valuable, even if the highway network architecture has been largely superseded by residual networks in practice. With the addition of a comparison to ResNets, this would be a strong contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
