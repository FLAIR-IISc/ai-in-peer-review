Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces adaptive gating across layers to facilitate information and gradient flow in very deep networks. Each layer outputs y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate.” By setting the “carry gate” C = 1−T, the layer interpolates between passing input unchanged and applying H. The design is inspired by LSTM gating, adapted here to depth rather than time. A simple initialization with negative biases for T encourages early “carry” behavior to ease optimization.

The paper situates the work among challenges in training deep networks (vanishing/exploding gradients; prior progress via better initialization and activation functions [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], improved optimizers, skip connections, distillation [FitNets], and layer-wise training). It distinguishes itself by proposing input-dependent, multiplicative gating to form data-dependent information pathways (“information highways”), rather than fixed skip connections or auxiliary losses. The authors present experiments showing that very deep highway networks can be trained directly via SGD, converging faster and more reliably than similarly sized plain networks, and achieving competitive accuracy on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 under modest data augmentation. Analysis includes gate activity visualizations, class-conditional routing patterns, and lesion studies indicating which layers contribute to performance.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The layer formulation is well-defined, and the gating mechanism is a plausible adaptation of LSTM ideas to feedforward depth. The use of sigmoid gates and negative bias initialization is consistent with LSTM practice and supported empirically. The informal Jacobian discussion (Eq. 5) correctly conveys intuition at the limiting cases T≈0 or T≈1; however, a full derivative should acknowledge additional terms from T′(x), which may affect gradient flow when gates are in intermediate regimes. Empirical results support the optimization claims: for fixed per-layer parameter budgets, highway networks train reliably up to 100 layers (and pilots suggest >1000), while plain nets degrade with depth. Comparisons to FitNets demonstrate training of thin, deep models without multi-stage distillation. The analysis (gate activity, class-conditional routing, lesioning) is insightful and consistent with the architecture’s intent.
- Empirical rigor: The paper reports random hyperparameter search for both plain and highway nets, multiple runs (best, mean±std over 5 seeds where available), and public code. CIFAR experiments reuse a fixed schedule across CIFAR-10/100, suggesting conservative tuning. However, baselines omit some strong contemporaneous techniques (e.g., batch normalization), and there is no comparison to fixed identity skip connections without gates. The CIFAR results are competitive under limited augmentation but do not establish state-of-the-art. Ablations on the effect of C≠1−T, gate parameterization granularity, and training-time/inference-time cost are limited.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details are largely sufficient, and code availability is a strength. Architecture tables and exact parameter counts for all configurations would further improve reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The definition of blocks and gating in convolutional layers is understandable, though more explicit detail would help (e.g., per-unit vs per-channel gating; whether T shares parameters across spatial locations; exact padding/stride choices). A more precise Jacobian expression and short analysis of gradient norms through depth would strengthen the theoretical motivation. Minor editorial issues exist but do not impede comprehension.

Originality:
Adapting LSTM-style gating to depth with element-wise, input-dependent gates is a novel architectural contribution at the time, distinct from fixed skip connections or teacher-student distillation. The authors acknowledge a shorter prior report and related contemporaneous work, and position their contribution appropriately. The empirical analysis of learned routing and lesion-based layer importance adds novelty.

Significance:
The ability to train very deep networks directly with SGD without specialized pretraining or distillation is practically important. While the accuracy numbers under modest augmentation are not state-of-the-art, the main contribution is an optimization and architectural advance that can impact subsequent model design. The technique is simple to implement and broadly applicable (fully connected and convolutional). The significance would be further strengthened by broader comparisons and larger-scale experiments.

Strengths:
- Simple, principled architectural mechanism enabling training of very deep nets.
- Strong optimization evidence: deep highway nets converge faster and to better optima than plain nets of similar size.
- Competitive performance on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 under modest augmentation; deeper, thinner models trained in a single stage outperform FitNet baselines of comparable budget.
- Insightful analyses (gate activity, class-conditional routing, lesion studies) that support the intended behavior.
- Public code and multiple-run reporting.

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical analysis of gradient flow is mostly intuitive; Eq. 5 omits T′(x) terms outside limiting cases.
- Limited baselines: no comparisons to fixed identity skip connections, batch normalization, or other contemporaneous depth-enabling methods.
- Limited ablations: effect of setting C free vs C=1−T, gate granularity (unit/channel/layer), sensitivity to gate bias initialization, and compute/memory overhead.
- Results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) are absent; CIFAR results are competitive but not state-of-the-art.
- Some architectural details (exact layer configs, parameter counts, training time) could be more explicit.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide the full Jacobian and a brief analysis of gradient propagation, including the role of T′(x) and typical gate regimes during training.
- Add ablations: (i) C free vs C=1−T; (ii) per-channel vs per-unit gates; (iii) sensitivity to bias initialization; (iv) computational and parameter overhead vs accuracy.
- Compare against fixed identity skip connections (without gates) under matched budgets, and include batch normalization baselines.
- Report training time and memory usage for plain vs highway nets.
- Expand architecture tables for CIFAR experiments and include stronger augmentation and/or a larger dataset to better assess significance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, simple architectural idea with strong empirical evidence that it enables training very deep networks.
- Broad applicability and ease of implementation; code availability enhances impact.
- Novel, LSTM-inspired gating across depth; insightful analyses demonstrating data-dependent routing and layer utilization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical depth; gradient flow claims largely empirical.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete; no direct comparison to fixed skip connections or batch normalization.
- Absence of large-scale benchmarks; improvements over state-of-the-art are not demonstrated.

Overall, the paper offers a technically sound and practically useful architectural contribution with solid empirical support and good clarity. Strengthening baselines, ablations, and analysis would further bolster the case.
