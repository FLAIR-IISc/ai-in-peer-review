Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Highway Networks, feedforward architectures that insert LSTM-inspired, elementwise gates between layers: y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), with T(x) = σ(W_T x + b_T) and C set to 1−T. The key idea is to enable near-identity information and gradient flow across many layers via “information highways,” controlled by transform gates biased initially toward carry (negative b_T). The authors show: (i) MNIST optimization studies indicating that plain fully connected nets degrade with depth while highway nets (up to 100 layers) train well and faster; (ii) 10-layer conv highway nets get competitive MNIST accuracy with fewer parameters; (iii) CIFAR-10/100 experiments where highway nets train in a single stage and match or exceed FitNets (without teacher hints), using modest augmentation; (iv) qualitative analyses of gate biases/activities and a lesioning study that suggests many layers are used on CIFAR-100 but not on MNIST.

This builds on: depth benefits and optimization challenges [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], gating and long-range credit assignment in LSTM [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber], local competition and skip/shortcut connections [Srivastava et al.; Graves; Lee et al.], and student–teacher “FitNets” [Romero et al.]. The construct is closely related to gated residual connections; the paper notes similarity to an LSTM-inspired model [32], and extends an earlier report [31].

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable architectural idea (fully connected and convolutional).
- Strong empirical evidence that the architecture is easy to optimize at large depths versus plain nets; negative-gate-bias heuristic is practical.
- Useful qualitative analyses (gate statistics, data-dependent routing, lesioning) that shed light on behavior.
- Code promised; claims of training very deep nets with simple SGD are of high practical interest.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns:
Quality and rigor:
- Comparisons and selection bias: Section 3.1 reports “best” training curves after a 100-run random hyperparameter search per depth, comparing highway vs. plain nets, but uses different hyperparameter spaces (highway nets have an extra gate-bias hyperparameter). Showing only the best-of-search training loss (not test error, and no distribution across runs) risks overestimating advantage. Please report mean/median and variability across matched budgets, training and test metrics, and ensure identical search spaces and tuning budgets (including the extra hyperparameter for plain nets, e.g., allowing per-layer learning rates or regularizers).
- Fairness and detail in CIFAR comparisons: The exact parameter counts and FLOPs for Highway A/B vs FitNet A/B are not enumerated; “comparable” is insufficient. Provide exact counts, training time/epoch, total epochs, batch size, weight decay, momentum, LR schedule (λ, γ, milestones), augmentation specifics (crop size, padding pixels, flip probability), and number of runs with mean±std. Reusing CIFAR-10 hyperparameters for CIFAR-100 may handicap performance; please either retune or justify.
- Claims of training “>1000 layers” are not substantiated with quantitative results, architectures, or training curves. At minimum, include plots/metrics and hyperparameters for these pilots.
- The analysis infers data-dependent routing from single-sample visualizations and per-class averages. This is suggestive but not conclusive. Consider quantitative measures (e.g., gate entropy, mutual information with class, per-example sparsity histograms).
- Lesioning by forcing T=0 is informative, but note that changing gates at test time may violate the learned distribution; please also report performance when zeroing random subsets of layers and when lesioning preserves average gate activity (e.g., fixed T to its mean).
- No ablations: There’s no study of C learned independently vs. C=1−T, or of per-channel vs. per-unit gating, or of sensitivity to b_T initialization. These are important to isolate what is essential.

Technical clarity and correctness:
- Equation and derivative issues: The paper states σ(x) = 11+e−x (missing division slash). The dot operator is defined as elementwise multiplication, but the paper alternates between “·” and “∗”. Be consistent and avoid overloading the dot, which commonly denotes dot product. In Eq. (5), dy/dx is given as I when T=0 and H′ when T=1, ignoring the T′(x) terms. This holds only if T is constant (T′=0), which is not generally true unless gates saturate. Please provide the full Jacobian and discuss the gradient flow conditions rigorously (e.g., when T≈0 with T′≈0 the path becomes near-identity).
- Notation typos: T(x)=σ(WTTx+bT) appears to have an extra T; likely W_T x. “varying varying” (Sec. 3.1). “backpropogation”→backpropagation. “as show in Table 1”→shown. Footnote markers appear mid-text (“1”, “2”, “best3”), but footnotes are not formatted consistently; one footnote content appears inline.
- Architectural specifics for conv highways are underspecified: Are transform gates spatially varying per location or pooled per feature map? Are W_T and W_H shared identically (same receptive field, padding) and with what strides, dilations, and number of filters? How are dimension changes handled in practice (identity vs. learned projection vs. zero-padding), and where are plain layers inserted?
- Reproducibility: The LR schedules in Sec. 3 lack concrete values (λ, γ, decay steps). Momentum, weight decay, number of epochs, early stopping, and initialization distributions for W_H are unspecified beyond “normalized initialization.”
- Compute/parameter overhead: Please quantify the additional parameters and operations introduced by gates and discuss memory/runtime impact versus plain and maxout baselines.

Originality:
- The gating concept across depth is a novel and natural extension of LSTM-style gating to feedforward depth, and distinct from fixed skip connections. However, related contemporaneous ideas (e.g., residual connections without gates) are not discussed/compared. The paper does cite older skip-connection work, FitNets, and local competition.

Significance:
- If substantiated with rigorous, fair comparisons and clearer ablations, the contribution could be influential: enabling straightforward training of very deep nets via learnable gated skips is impactful. The qualitative analyses are insightful. As written, incomplete experimental details and selection bias temper the strength of the claims.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general architectural idea with strong intuition and potential high impact.
- Empirical evidence that deep networks become easy to optimize; interesting analyses (routing, lesioning).
- Code promised; practical initialization heuristic.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficiently rigorous and fair comparisons; best-of-search reporting; missing numerical tables and exact setups.
- Notational/formatting errors and some misleading derivative discussion.
- Underspecified convolutional gating details; limited ablations; unsubstantiated 1000-layer claim.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (many entries missing venues/pages; URL has a space).

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely significant, but the current submission has too many methodological and presentation issues. I encourage a revision that (i) provides full, fair, and reproducible experimental details and ablations; (ii) corrects notation and equations, with a clear gradient-flow analysis; (iii) quantifies parameter/compute trade-offs and reports averaged results with variability; and (iv) clarifies conv gating design and dimensionality handling.
