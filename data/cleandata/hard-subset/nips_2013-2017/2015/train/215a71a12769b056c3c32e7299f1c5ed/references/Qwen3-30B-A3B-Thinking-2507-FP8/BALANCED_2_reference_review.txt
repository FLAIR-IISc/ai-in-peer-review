

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to facilitate training of extremely deep feedforward networks through an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism. The authors propose modifying standard network layers with transform and carry gates (T and C) that allow information to flow across layers without attenuation, creating "information highways." The paper claims that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD even at extreme depths (100+ layers), unlike plain networks which become increasingly difficult to optimize as depth increases.

The paper relates its work to several strands of previous research, including normalized initialization [16,17], skip connections [22,23], and knowledge distillation [25,26]. However, it does not sufficiently distinguish itself from the emerging residual network (ResNet) approach that was being developed concurrently (He et al., 2015, "Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition" was submitted to NIPS 2015, though not yet published when this paper was written). The highway network concept shares significant conceptual overlap with the residual connections in ResNets, though the paper does not discuss this connection.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper presents a well-motivated problem and a clear, elegant solution to the depth optimization problem.
2. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, demonstrating consistent improvements over plain networks across multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) at various depths.
3. The ablation studies (Figures 2-4) provide valuable insights into the behavior of the transform gates and layer importance, which is rare in deep learning papers.
4. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited theoretical justification:** The paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis explaining why highway networks are easier to optimize than plain networks. The Jacobian analysis (Equation 5) is superficial and doesn't address the fundamental optimization challenges of deep networks.
2. **Insufficient comparison to residual networks:** The paper does not engage with the concurrent development of residual networks (which would later become the dominant approach for training deep networks). The highway network concept is similar to residual connections, and the paper should have explicitly compared the two approaches.
3. **Limited novelty claim:** While the highway network architecture is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently argue why this approach is fundamentally better than the residual connections that were emerging at the same time. The paper cites a recent similar LSTM-inspired model [32] but doesn't address how it differs from the residual approach.
4. **Experiments lack architectural comparison:** The paper doesn't compare highway networks to state-of-the-art architectures beyond the FitNet comparison, which is limited to a specific network design. It would be valuable to see how highway networks compare to more modern architectures.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-executed experiments, but lacks depth in theoretical justification. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., "the initial bias is best treated as a hyperparameter") and provide detailed experimental results. However, the theoretical foundation for why highway networks work better than plain networks is underdeveloped.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-organized sections, consistent notation, and helpful figures. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The highway network architecture is novel compared to standard feedforward networks, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate it from the emerging residual network approach that was gaining traction in the community. The connection to LSTM is interesting, but the paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of how this relates to other recent developments in deep network architectures.

**Significance:** The problem addressed (training very deep networks) is highly significant. The paper demonstrates that extremely deep networks can be trained directly without two-stage procedures, which is an important contribution. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the fact that residual networks (which share similar goals) ultimately became the dominant approach for training very deep networks.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a solid contribution to the field with clear experimental results and an interesting architectural innovation. However, the limited theoretical justification, insufficient comparison to residual networks (which were becoming the standard approach at the time), and lack of thorough discussion about how this work differs from concurrent developments weaken the paper's position. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a thorough comparison with residual networks (which were being developed concurrently) to clarify the relationship and differences between the approaches.
2. Provide deeper theoretical analysis explaining why highway networks are easier to optimize, not just empirical results.
3. Expand the discussion of how this work relates to other recent developments in deep network architectures.
4. Consider repositioning the paper to emphasize the unique insights about information routing and layer importance (Figures 2-4) rather than solely focusing on the depth aspect.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding deep network training, but would be stronger with better contextualization within the broader landscape of deep learning research at the time of submission. The core idea of information highways is interesting, but the paper's impact would be significantly enhanced by more complete engagement with the concurrent development of residual networks.
