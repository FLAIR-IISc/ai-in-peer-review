Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that adds input-dependent gating across layers to facilitate information flow in very deep nets. Each layer computes y = H(x) ⊙ T(x) + x ⊙ (1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transformation (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid transform gate; the carry gate is tied as C = 1 − T. The key idea is to create “information highways” through depth, analogous to LSTM gating across time, so that both activations and gradients can propagate without attenuation. A simple but crucial initialization is to set the transform gate bias bT negative so the network initially favors carry behavior, easing optimization.

Empirically, the authors show:
- Fully-connected nets up to 100 layers train effectively on MNIST whereas plain nets with normalized/variance-preserving initialization [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.] degrade sharply with depth.
- Convolutional highway networks achieve competitive accuracy on MNIST with far fewer parameters.
- On CIFAR-10/100, highway nets reach or exceed fitnets (teacher-student distillation) [Romero et al.] with comparable parameter/multiplication budgets, trained end-to-end in a single stage.
- Analysis indicates data-dependent routing via gates, and a lesion study shows early layers are critical on MNIST, while many more layers contribute on CIFAR-100.

The work is well-situated relative to LSTMs [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber], skip connections and deep supervision [e.g., earlier skip connections, deep supervision], improved initialization [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], maxout/locally competitive networks, and hint-based training [FitNets]. The gating mechanism can be seen as a learned, input-dependent variant of skip connections, inspired by LSTM gates rather than fixed shortcuts.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The architecture is simple and well-motivated. The gating enables identity mapping paths that alleviate vanishing/exploding gradients. The discussion of Jacobians at the extremes (T ≈ 0 or 1) is helpful, though the general-case derivative could be fleshed out for completeness.
- Empirical support: The optimization study across depths is convincing and carefully controlled (similar parameter counts per layer, same initialization schemes). CIFAR experiments demonstrate the practical benefit of single-stage training for deep, thin networks. The gate activity visualization and lesioning provide insightful diagnostics.
- Limitations: 
  - Baselines miss some strong contemporaneous methods, notably batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy) and identity shortcut connections without gating; it would be valuable to compare against deep residual-like architectures with variance-preserving init.
  - Ablations are limited. We do not see the effect of untied carry (C ≠ 1 − T), per-layer vs per-unit gates, different gate initializations, or removing the gating entirely while keeping skip connections.
  - Computational overhead of gating (extra parameters and multiplies) is not quantified; reporting training time, memory, and FLOPs would clarify trade-offs.
  - Results are on MNIST/CIFAR only; no larger-scale evaluation (e.g., SVHN or ImageNet), so claims about “very deep” training generality would benefit from broader evidence.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear, with consistent notation and helpful figures. The connection to LSTM gating and the intuition about information highways are well-explained.
- Some implementation details could be clearer: whether gates are per-unit, per-channel, or shared spatially in conv layers; exact padding/stride settings; and the schedule/values for learning rate and momentum across experiments.
- For completeness, include the full Jacobian for the general case y = H ⊙ T + x ⊙ (1 − T), and discuss when/why gradients through T might saturate.

Originality:
- The core idea—input-dependent gated skip paths across depth—is novel relative to prior fixed skip connections and deep supervision. The LSTM analogy is apt; leveraging gating to ease credit assignment through depth is a fresh contribution in feedforward nets.
- The paper acknowledges closely related concurrent work; clearer differentiation from “similar LSTM-inspired models” would strengthen the novelty case.

Significance:
- Enabling stable optimization of very deep networks with simple SGD is important. The work provides a generally applicable architectural tool that plausibly influenced later deep architectures.
- The diagnostic analyses (gate activity, class-conditional routing, lesioning) add insight into how depth is utilized, which is valuable beyond raw accuracy.
- The lack of comparisons to some strong baselines and to larger datasets tempers the immediate impact, but the architectural idea is likely to be adopted and extended.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Simple, general architectural mechanism with clear intuition and strong empirical optimization benefits.
- Single-stage training of deep, thin nets that match or exceed hint-based methods at comparable budgets.
- Insightful analyses of gating behavior and layer importance; code availability improves reproducibility.
- Broad applicability (fully-connected, convolutional) without specialized initializations beyond a simple bias.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Missing comparisons to batch normalization and fixed identity skip connections; no large-scale benchmarks.
- Limited ablation studies and cost analysis; unclear trade-offs in parameters and compute.
- Some implementation details and theoretical derivations are abbreviated.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines with: (a) deep plain nets + batch norm, and (b) fixed identity shortcut connections (no gating), matched in depth/width/parameters, to isolate the value of gating vs skipping.
- Provide ablations on: gate bias initialization; C untied from 1 − T; per-layer scalar gates vs per-channel/unit gates; and effect of removing gate dependence (static T).
- Report compute/memory overhead and wall-clock training speed vs baselines.
- Extend experiments to SVHN and a subset of ImageNet to test scalability.
- Include the full Jacobian expression and empirical gradient norm tracking to substantiate the gradient flow claims.
- Clarify gate parameterization in conv layers (spatial sharing, grouping).

Recommendation: Accept. Despite some missing baselines and limited scale, the architectural contribution is clear, well-motivated, and empirically supported, with analyses that improve understanding of very deep nets. The work is likely to be influential and useful to the community.
