

# Review: Training Very Deep Networks

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to facilitate training of extremely deep feedforward networks. The authors propose a gating mechanism inspired by LSTM networks that allows information to flow across layers without attenuation, creating "information highways." The key innovation is the transform gate (T) and carry gate (C) that control how much input is transformed versus carried through. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD without the diminishing returns seen in plain networks as depth increases, with strong experimental results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

**Strengths:**
1. **Strong Technical Contribution:** The highway network architecture provides a clear, theoretically grounded solution to the problem of training very deep networks. The gating mechanism elegantly bridges the gap between plain networks and more complex approaches like ResNets (which were emerging around the same time).
2. **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The authors provide compelling evidence through multiple experiments showing that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly. The 100-layer highway network outperforming similarly-sized plain networks by over two orders of magnitude is particularly impressive.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figures 2-4) reveals how highway networks dynamically route information based on input, which is a valuable theoretical contribution. The layer importance analysis demonstrates that highway networks can effectively utilize all layers for complex tasks, unlike plain networks.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with clear notation and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental setup is thorough and properly controlled.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Comparison to ResNets:** The paper was submitted around the time ResNets were being developed (ICLR 2015), but it does not compare with or cite this influential work. While the authors note "a similar LSTM-inspired model" [32], they miss a critical opportunity to position their work relative to the most successful deep network architecture of the time. This is a significant omission for a NIPS submission.
2. **Overstated Claims about "Extremely Deep" Networks:** While the paper demonstrates success with 100-layer networks, the title "Training Very Deep Networks" is somewhat misleading. The authors don't push to the limits of what's possible (e.g., ResNets later achieved 100+ layers with similar success), and the paper doesn't establish how highway networks compare to other modern approaches.
3. **Computational Cost Analysis Missing:** The paper doesn't quantify the computational cost of the gating mechanism (additional parameters, inference time), which is crucial for practical adoption. For example, the transform gate adds a small number of parameters per layer, but this could become significant at extreme depths.
4. **Limited Discussion of Broader Context:** While related work is cited, the paper doesn't fully contextualize its contribution relative to other contemporary approaches like batch normalization (which was also emerging around this time) or the later ResNet architecture.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning, and the experimental results are compelling. However, the omission of comparison with ResNets (which was a major NIPS/ICLR contribution in the same timeframe) is a significant weakness that needs to be addressed.

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**
1. Add a comparison section explicitly contrasting highway networks with ResNets (even if ResNets were published shortly after submission), explaining how the gating mechanism differs from residual connections and why highway networks might be preferable in certain scenarios.
2. Include a brief analysis of the computational cost of the gating mechanism (additional parameters, inference time) compared to plain networks and ResNets.
3. Discuss how highway networks might complement or differ from batch normalization, which was becoming standard practice around the same time.
4. Clarify the scope of "very deep" by comparing with the state of the art at the time (e.g., ResNets' 100+ layers) to avoid overstating the novelty.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Strong technical contribution that solves a real problem in deep learning; thorough experimental validation; insightful analysis of information flow.
*Con:* Missing comparison with ResNets (a major contemporary contribution); some overstatement of novelty given the timing; lacks computational cost analysis.

This paper makes a solid contribution to deep learning, particularly for the problem of training very deep networks. With the suggested revisions addressing the comparison to ResNets and computational considerations, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the problem and provided a well-motivated solution with compelling empirical results.
