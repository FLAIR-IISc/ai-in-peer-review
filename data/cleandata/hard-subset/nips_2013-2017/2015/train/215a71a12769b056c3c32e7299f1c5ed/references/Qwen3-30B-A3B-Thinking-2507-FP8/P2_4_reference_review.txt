

# Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces "Highway Networks" as a novel architecture for training extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The authors propose a gating mechanism inspired by LSTMs, where each layer has a transform gate (T) and carry gate (1-T) that control how much of the input is transformed versus passed through unchanged. The key innovation is that highway networks allow information to flow through "information highways" without attenuation, enabling direct training of networks with over 100 layers using standard SGD.

The paper relates to previous work by acknowledging challenges in training deep networks (vanishing gradients, poor propagation of activations), and builds upon related approaches like skip connections, distillation, and layer-wise training. However, it notably fails to reference the concurrent work on Residual Networks (ResNets) that appeared around the same time, which also used skip connections to enable training of very deep networks.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with clear mathematical formulations (Equations 2-3) and well-designed experiments. The authors provide rigorous comparisons between highway and plain networks across varying depths on MNIST and CIFAR datasets. The results (Figure 1) convincingly demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly.

The paper acknowledges limitations, noting that highway networks might have unused layers (as shown in Figure 4), but argues this doesn't harm performance. However, the theoretical justification for why highway networks work better than plain networks is weak. The authors don't provide a rigorous mathematical analysis of the optimization landscape or gradient flow properties, which would strengthen the claims.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistent, and key concepts (transform gate, carry gate, information highways) are well-explained. Figures 2-4 effectively illustrate the behavior of transform gates and information flow through highway networks. The manuscript would benefit from more detailed explanation of why the negative bias initialization works, and how it differs from standard initialization techniques.

## Originality

The core idea of using gates to control information flow is inspired by LSTMs, but the adaptation to feedforward networks is novel. However, the paper fails to adequately distinguish its contribution from ResNets, which were published in the same year (2015) and used a simpler skip connection approach (y = H(x) + x) that achieved similar results. The authors cite some related work but don't engage with the ResNet paper, which is a significant oversight given ResNets' profound impact on the field.

The paper's claim that highway networks are "more flexible" than plain networks is valid, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that this flexibility leads to better performance than the simpler ResNet approach. The comparison to Fitnets in Section 3.3.1 is somewhat misleading, as the Fitnet paper was published in 2014 while ResNets came later.

## Significance

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training very deep networks. The demonstration that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD at extreme depths (100+ layers) is significant and contributes to our understanding of how to build deeper networks. The analysis of information flow (Figures 2-4) provides valuable insights into how deep networks operate.

However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the paper's failure to compare with ResNets, which became the dominant approach for training very deep networks. The paper's results are comparable to state-of-the-art methods but don't demonstrate clear superiority. The authors claim highway networks "generalize well to unseen data," but don't provide compelling evidence that they outperform ResNets or other state-of-the-art methods on major benchmarks.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear demonstration that highway networks can be trained at extreme depths
- Detailed analysis of transform gate behavior and information flow
- Well-designed experiments with appropriate baselines
- Practical training guidelines (e.g., negative bias initialization)

**Weaknesses:**
- Failure to acknowledge or compare with ResNets, which was published around the same time and addressed the same problem more effectively
- Lack of theoretical analysis explaining why highway networks work better
- Limited comparison with other modern techniques (e.g., batch normalization)
- Marginal performance gains on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 compared to state-of-the-art

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valid contribution to the problem of training deep networks, but its significance is substantially reduced by the failure to engage with ResNets, which was the dominant approach for training very deep networks in the subsequent years. The paper's core idea has merit, but it doesn't clearly demonstrate that highway networks offer advantages over simpler skip connection approaches.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Acknowledging and comparing with ResNets
2. Providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis
3. Demonstrating clear performance advantages on standard benchmarks
4. Discussing computational efficiency compared to alternatives

Given the importance of the problem and the paper's technical soundness, I would recommend acceptance with major revisions, particularly to address the omission of ResNets and to clarify the relationship between highway networks and the more successful skip connection approaches that followed.

Without these revisions, the paper risks being seen as a less impactful contribution to a problem that was already effectively solved by other approaches. The field would benefit from a clearer understanding of the relative merits of different deep network architectures, and this paper would be more valuable if it provided that clarity.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and addresses an important problem, but its impact would be greatly enhanced by a more comprehensive comparison with the most influential work in this area.
