

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to enable the direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (over 100 layers) using standard stochastic gradient descent. The authors draw inspiration from Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to introduce an adaptive gating mechanism that allows information to flow across layers without attenuation. The key innovation is the transform gate (T) and carry gate (C=1-T) that determine how much of the output comes from transforming the input versus carrying it through. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained to greater depths than plain networks while maintaining good generalization performance on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a well-designed architecture with strong empirical validation. The authors conduct comprehensive experiments comparing highway networks to plain networks across multiple datasets and depths. The ablation studies (particularly the lesioning experiments in Figure 4) provide valuable insights into how highway networks utilize their layers. The negative bias initialization strategy for transform gates is well-motivated and empirically validated.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts (particularly Figures 2 and 3 showing the information highway behavior). The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the authors provide clear comparisons to relevant prior work.

**Originality:** The core idea of using an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism to facilitate information flow in deep feedforward networks is novel at the time of submission (2015). The authors effectively situate their work within the broader context of deep learning challenges, acknowledging prior work on normalization, activation functions, and skip connections. The highway network architecture represents a meaningful technical contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The problem of training very deep networks is highly significant in deep learning. The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained to 100+ layers, which was previously difficult with plain networks. The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that highway networks can match or exceed the performance of shallower networks with comparable computational budgets.

## Weaknesses

**Originality (Critical):** The paper's most significant weakness is its failure to acknowledge and compare with residual networks (He et al., 2015), which were published just months before this paper's submission to NIPS. Residual networks, which use simple skip connections rather than adaptive gating mechanisms, were shown to be more effective at training extremely deep networks. The authors would have been expected to discuss this crucial related work, as it directly addresses the same problem with a simpler and more effective solution. The paper's claim that highway networks "do not suffer from increasing depth" is particularly problematic in light of residual networks.

**Quality:** While the experimental results are strong, the theoretical analysis is limited. The paper provides minimal theoretical justification for why highway networks work better than plain networks (beyond the Jacobian analysis in Equation 5). A more thorough analysis of the information flow properties and convergence behavior would strengthen the paper.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is substantially diminished by the emergence of residual networks, which were published around the same time. The paper does not demonstrate that highway networks provide any significant advantage over residual networks, which were shown to be more effective in subsequent research. The paper's claim that highway networks "allow us to examine how much computation depth is needed for a given problem" is interesting but not sufficiently demonstrated.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper in its current form. While the paper presents a technically sound and well-executed study, its significance is severely undermined by the lack of comparison with residual networks, which were published around the same time and provide a simpler, more effective solution to the same problem. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address this critical oversight before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Acknowledge and compare with residual networks:** The authors must address the recent work by He et al. (2015) on residual networks, which solves the same problem with a simpler architecture. The paper should include a thorough comparison of highway networks and residual networks, including a discussion of why one might be preferred over the other.

2. **Strengthen theoretical analysis:** Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for why highway networks facilitate deeper training, including analysis of information flow properties and convergence rates.

3. **Expand experimental comparison:** Include experiments comparing highway networks to residual networks on standard benchmarks to demonstrate the relative advantages or disadvantages of each approach.

4. **Clarify the relationship to other work:** Better position the paper within the broader context of recent advances in deep learning, particularly the emergence of residual connections.

## Conclusion

"Training Very Deep Networks" presents an interesting architectural innovation that addresses a genuine problem in deep learning. The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written. However, the failure to acknowledge and compare with residual networks (which were published around the same time) is a critical flaw that substantially undermines the paper's significance and originality. Without addressing this issue, the paper cannot be considered for publication at NIPS. The authors should revise the paper to include a thorough comparison with residual networks and to clarify the relationship between their approach and this more recent, highly influential work.
