This paper proposes "highway networks," a modification to standard feedforward networks incorporating LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms to facilitate training of very deep networks. The core idea – allowing information to flow more easily through layers via adaptive gating – is intuitively plausible, given the well-known difficulties in training deep networks due to vanishing gradients. The authors demonstrate through experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 that highway networks can be trained to significantly greater depths than plain networks, achieving competitive or superior performance.

While the motivation and core idea are reasonable, and the experimental results are encouraging, I find myself skeptical of the claims of substantial novelty and the overall significance of this work as presented. The paper feels incremental, building on established LSTM principles rather than presenting a truly groundbreaking architectural innovation. The performance gains, while present, don’t appear to dramatically surpass existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly given the increased complexity introduced by the gating mechanism.

A major weakness lies in the presentation and comparison to existing literature. The paper spends considerable time reviewing prior work, but often lacks sufficient depth in discussing *how* highway networks specifically address shortcomings in those approaches. For example, the comparison to Fitnets (Romero et al.) is superficial. While the authors claim comparable performance, they don't adequately address the architectural differences, computational costs, or potential trade-offs. Similarly, the discussion of the limitations of deep supervision feels somewhat dismissive. 

The experimental setup, while comprehensive, also raises concerns. The parameter settings were determined via random search on CIFAR-10 and then fixed for CIFAR-100. This is a questionable practice, as optimal hyperparameters likely differ between datasets. A more thorough hyperparameter optimization on CIFAR-100 would strengthen the results. Furthermore, the analysis of layer importance (Figure 4) is interesting but doesn’t fully justify the added complexity of highway networks. If many layers become effectively unused, one could argue for simpler architectures.

The analysis section, especially Figure 2, is visually appealing but lacks rigorous quantification. Statements like “most biases decreased further during training” or “transform gates are more selective” lack statistical backing and are difficult to interpret without more concrete measurements. The authors allude to data-dependent routing, but the provided evidence is not compelling.

The paper's emphasis on simplicity (avoiding complex initialization schemes) is a positive aspect. However, the introduction of transform and carry gates *increases* the total number of parameters, potentially negating some of that simplicity. The justification for setting C = 1-T feels somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from further investigation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated ability to train significantly deeper networks than plain networks.
*   Competitive performance on standard image classification datasets.
*   Intuitive and well-motivated architectural modification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – builds heavily on LSTM concepts.
*   Lack of rigorous comparison to strong baselines and recent advances (e.g., ResNets).
*   Insufficient justification for the architectural choices (C = 1-T).
*   Superficial analysis and weak statistical support for key claims.
*   Hyperparameter tuning strategy is questionable.
*   Increased parameter count compared to simpler architectures.



Overall, I believe this paper presents a reasonable engineering contribution, but falls short of the bar for a top-tier NIPS publication. The novelty is limited, and the significance is overstated. While the experiments are well-executed, the analysis lacks depth and statistical rigor. I would recommend **rejection** with encouragement to submit a revised version to a venue more appropriate for incremental engineering work. The authors should focus on a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art methods, a more thorough hyperparameter optimization, and a more quantitative analysis of the gating mechanism’s behavior.
