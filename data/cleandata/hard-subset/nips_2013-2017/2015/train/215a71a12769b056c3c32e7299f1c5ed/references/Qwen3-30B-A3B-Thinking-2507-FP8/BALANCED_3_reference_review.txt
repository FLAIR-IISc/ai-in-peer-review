

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces highway networks, a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep neural networks using standard SGD. The authors address the well-known challenge of training very deep networks by incorporating an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism (transform gate and carry gate) that allows information to flow more easily across layers. The key insight is that highway networks can smoothly transition between "carrying" input through layers and transforming it, enabling the network to learn which information should flow through and which should be transformed.

The paper builds upon prior work in several important areas. It references the success of deep networks [1-6], the importance of initialization strategies [16-17], skip connections [22-24], and distillation techniques [25-26]. Crucially, it connects to LSTM networks [29-30], noting the similarity of the gating mechanism to LSTM's gates. The authors also acknowledge a very recent related work [32] that proposes a similar LSTM-inspired model, which provides context for their contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The highway network architecture is well-motivated and technically sound. The gating mechanism (Equation 3) is simple yet effective, with clear theoretical justification through the Jacobian analysis (Equation 5). The authors provide a thoughtful initialization strategy for the transform gates (negative bias initialization), which is crucial for the success of very deep networks.

2. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare highway networks to plain networks across multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) and network depths. The results clearly demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, while plain networks degrade significantly (Table 1-2, Figure 1). The 100-layer highway network outperforming similarly-sized plain networks by over two orders of magnitude is particularly compelling.

3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 provides valuable insights into how information flows through highway networks. The examination of transform gate activity (Figure 2-3) reveals that the gating system learns to route information data-dependently, which is a significant contribution beyond just enabling training. The layer importance analysis (Figure 4) is particularly novel and provides a way to assess how much depth is actually needed for a given problem.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear notation and effective use of figures to illustrate key concepts. The experimental setup is well-described, and the authors provide publicly available code and hyperparameter details, which enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty Concerns:** While the highway network concept is well-motivated, the paper acknowledges a very recent related work [32] that proposes a similar LSTM-inspired model. The authors should more clearly articulate how their work differs from [32] in terms of architecture, training methodology, and empirical results. Without this distinction, the novelty of the contribution is somewhat diminished.

2. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper focuses on demonstrating that highway networks can be trained to greater depths, but doesn't fully establish their superiority over the current state-of-the-art in terms of accuracy. While Table 3 shows competitive results, the authors don't compare against the best-performing architectures on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100, which would strengthen the significance argument.

3. **Theoretical Justification:** While the Jacobian analysis provides some theoretical foundation, the paper lacks deeper theoretical analysis of why highway networks avoid the vanishing gradient problem. A more thorough theoretical treatment of the optimization properties would strengthen the paper.

4. **Depth vs. Width Trade-off:** The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be made very deep, but doesn't explore how this compares to making networks wider (which is a common alternative for improving performance). A more comprehensive comparison between depth and width would provide additional context.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field by demonstrating a practical and effective method for training extremely deep networks. The highway network architecture is well-designed, the experiments are thorough, and the analysis provides valuable insights into information flow in deep networks. While the novelty is somewhat limited by the recent related work [32], the paper still presents a valuable and well-executed approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Clearly articulate the differences between this work and the recent LSTM-inspired model [32], highlighting the specific architectural, training, and empirical distinctions.

2. Add a comparison to state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 to better establish the significance of the approach.

3. Provide a deeper theoretical analysis of why highway networks avoid the vanishing gradient problem, perhaps by examining the spectral properties of the Jacobian.

4. Include a discussion of the trade-offs between depth and width in highway networks, comparing performance against wider but shallower architectures.

5. Consider extending the analysis to more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate the scalability of the approach.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant practical value for the deep learning community. The authors have successfully demonstrated that highway networks can be trained to extreme depths while maintaining good generalization. With the suggested improvements, particularly regarding novelty and comparison to state-of-the-art, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
