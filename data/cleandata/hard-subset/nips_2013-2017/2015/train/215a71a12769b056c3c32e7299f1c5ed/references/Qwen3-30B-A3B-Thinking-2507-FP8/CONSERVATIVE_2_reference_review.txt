

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a modification to deep feedforward networks that incorporates an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism to facilitate information flow across layers. The authors claim that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD even at extreme depths (100+ layers), unlike plain networks which become increasingly difficult to optimize with depth. The core innovation is a transform gate (T) and carry gate (C=1-T) that allow layers to either transform inputs or pass them through, with the gating mechanism enabling "information highways."

The paper is well-written and provides experimental validation on MNIST and CIFAR datasets. The authors demonstrate that highway networks outperform plain networks at greater depths, and they compare favorably to FitNets (a two-stage training approach) in terms of both accuracy and training simplicity. The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figure 2) is insightful, showing that gates become more selective during training and that information routing varies by input class.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a clear, well-structured methodology with thorough experimental validation on standard benchmarks. The analysis of transform gate activity and layer importance (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how information flows through deep networks. The initialization scheme (negative bias for transform gates) is practical and well-motivated by LSTM literature.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's primary weakness is its lack of adequate comparison to established skip connection approaches. The authors cite skip connections (e.g., [22, 23, 2, 24]) but fail to acknowledge that ResNet, which was published in 2015 (and presented at NIPS 2015), introduced a very similar concept of identity mappings through skip connections, which was already demonstrating superior performance on ImageNet at depths exceeding 100 layers. The paper's claim that highway networks "can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), in contrast to plain networks" is significantly weakened by this omission, as ResNet had already achieved similar results with a simpler architecture.

The paper's validation is also limited. The CIFAR experiments (Table 3) show competitive results but do not demonstrate clear superiority over ResNet or other established methods. The MNIST experiments with 100-layer networks are impressive but involve a relatively simple dataset where depth may not be necessary. The paper fails to demonstrate that highway networks provide meaningful improvements over ResNet on challenging benchmarks like ImageNet, which would be essential for establishing their significance.

The novelty claim is problematic. The highway architecture is fundamentally similar to residual connections, differing primarily in the gating mechanism (which is more complex than the simple identity mapping in ResNet). The paper does not convincingly argue why this added complexity is justified over the simpler ResNet approach, which had already demonstrated superior performance. The authors cite LSTM as inspiration but don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from the more successful ResNet architecture.

The paper also lacks comprehensive analysis of computational cost. While the authors claim highway networks can be trained "in a single stage" without "soft targets from a shallow teacher," they don't quantify the additional computational overhead of the gating mechanism compared to ResNet's simple identity mappings.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound approach with interesting experimental results, it fails to establish significant novelty or superiority over established methods, particularly ResNet. The field has already moved toward simpler skip connection approaches that achieve the same goal (direct training of very deep networks) with less complexity. The paper's claims about "extremely deep" networks are not sufficiently validated against the state of the art, and the omission of ResNet in the comparison is a critical oversight for a NIPS submission.

The paper's contribution is incremental at best, and it does not advance the field demonstrably beyond existing work. For a conference like NIPS, where the bar for novelty and significance is high, this paper falls short. The authors should have engaged more deeply with ResNet (which was published around the same time) and demonstrated clear advantages over this simpler, more effective approach before claiming their method as a significant advancement. Without such evidence, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for originality and significance.
