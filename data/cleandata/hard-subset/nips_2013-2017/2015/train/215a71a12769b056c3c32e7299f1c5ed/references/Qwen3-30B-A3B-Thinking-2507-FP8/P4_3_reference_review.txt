

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper introduces "highway networks" as a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward neural networks. The authors argue that conventional deep networks suffer from poor propagation of activations and gradients as depth increases, making them difficult to optimize. Their solution is inspired by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, introducing an adaptive gating mechanism (transform and carry gates) that allows information to flow across many layers without attenuation. This creates "information highways" that enable direct training of networks with 100+ layers using standard SGD, in contrast to plain networks that exhibit performance degradation as depth increases.

The paper relates its approach to previous work on deep network training, including better optimizers [13-15], normalized initialization [16-17], activation functions based on local competition [20-21], skip connections [22-24], and knowledge distillation [25-26]. The authors clearly distinguish their highway network approach from these methods by demonstrating that it enables direct training of very deep networks without requiring special initialization schemes or two-stage training procedures.

**Quality:** The paper presents strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrate that highway networks can achieve comparable or better performance than plain networks with similar parameter counts, while maintaining consistent performance as depth increases (up to 100 layers). The authors provide a thorough analysis of the learned transform gates (Figures 2-4), showing that highway networks dynamically route information based on input, which is a significant contribution. The lesioning experiments (Figure 4) demonstrate that highway networks actually utilize their depth rather than having many unused layers. The theoretical analysis of the Jacobian (Equations 4-5) provides a reasonable justification for the information highway concept. However, the paper lacks a rigorous theoretical proof that highway networks avoid vanishing gradients for arbitrary depths, relying primarily on empirical evidence.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The key equations (2-3) clearly define the highway network architecture, and the figures effectively illustrate the main findings. The methodology section is sufficiently detailed for reproduction, though some technical details about the initialization strategy could be more explicit. The paper follows a logical structure from problem statement to solution, experiments, and analysis.

**Originality:** The highway network architecture represents a novel combination of familiar techniques (LSTM gates, skip connections) applied to feedforward networks. While related to the "LSTM-inspired model" [32] mentioned in the paper, the authors' implementation and empirical demonstration of training extremely deep networks is distinctive. The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from previous work, particularly in Section 5. The concept of information highways for enabling direct training of very deep networks is a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: training extremely deep networks. By enabling direct training of networks with 100+ layers without performance degradation, highway networks provide a practical solution that could benefit practitioners. The demonstration that highway networks can achieve competitive results with fewer parameters than state-of-the-art methods (Table 1) has clear practical significance. The analysis of information routing (Figures 2-4) provides new insights into how deep networks can be designed to better utilize their depth, which is valuable for future research.

**Strengths:**
- Strong empirical evidence demonstrating training of 100+ layer networks
- Clear analysis of learned transform gates showing dynamic information routing
- Thorough comparison to relevant baselines including Fitnets [25]
- Practical demonstration of superior performance with fewer parameters
- Code and hyperparameter details provided for reproducibility

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical guarantee of vanishing gradient avoidance
- Comparison to the similar "LSTM-inspired model" [32] is not sufficiently detailed
- No analysis of computational overhead of the gating mechanism
- Experiments on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 are limited to a specific setting without extensive data augmentation
- Does not explore compatibility with other architectures like residual networks (which were introduced around the same time)

The paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of training very deep networks. The highway network architecture provides a practical solution that enables direct training of extremely deep networks without the need for complex initialization schemes or two-stage training procedures. The empirical results are convincing, and the analysis of information routing provides valuable insights. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the practical impact of the work is substantial.

This paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning with a novel yet practical approach that has clear significance for both practitioners and researchers. The work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers, particularly those on residual networks and knowledge distillation, and makes a compelling case for its novelty and significance.

Pro: Strong empirical evidence, clear architecture, practical significance, thorough analysis of information routing
Con: Limited theoretical justification, comparison to similar work [32] could be more detailed, no computational overhead analysis
