### **Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"**

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper addresses the well-known and critical problem of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors observe that as "plain" networks get deeper, their performance degrades due to optimization difficulties, specifically the challenge of propagating information and gradients through many non-linear layers. The central contribution is the "highway network," a novel architecture inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks.

The core idea is to introduce adaptive gates at each layer that regulate information flow. Each highway layer computes a transformed representation `H(x)` and a "transform gate" `T(x)`. The layer's output is a dynamic combination of the transformed input and the original input: `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x))`. This formulation allows the network to learn to pass information directly through layers (when `T` is close to 0), creating unimpeded "highways" for gradient and information flow. This elegantly circumvents the vanishing gradient problem and the degradation issue seen in plain deep networks. A simple but effective training strategy is proposed: initializing the transform gate biases to be negative, which encourages "carry" behavior at the start of training.

The paper situates its contribution within the context of existing approaches for training deep networks, such as advanced optimizers, careful initialization schemes (e.g., Glorot et al., He et al.), competitive activation functions (e.g., Maxout), and fixed skip connections. It particularly contrasts its method with the recent "FitNets" approach, which requires a complex, multi-stage "hint-based" training procedure for deep, thin networks. The proposed highway networks, by contrast, can be trained end-to-end with standard SGD.

**Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Elegance and Novelty:** The adaptation of LSTM's gating mechanism to feedforward architectures is both insightful and novel. It provides a principled way to control information flow, moving beyond static skip connections to a dynamic, data-dependent routing mechanism. The core equation is simple yet powerful.

2.  **Effective Solution to a Major Problem:** The paper provides a convincing solution to the network degradation problem, a significant bottleneck in deep learning research at the time. The optimization experiments in Section 3.1 are a clear and powerful demonstration: while plain networks falter beyond 20 layers, highway networks maintain strong performance at 50 and 100 layers. This is a crucial result.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The experimental section is thorough and well-designed.
    *   The direct comparison against FitNets is particularly compelling, demonstrating that highway networks can achieve comparable or better performance with a much simpler, single-stage training paradigm. This is a significant practical advantage.
    *   The analysis in Section 4 is a major strength. The visualizations of gate activations provide excellent insight into the network's learned behavior, showing sparse, data-dependent routing. The lesioning study (Figure 4) brilliantly illustrates the concept of "effective depth," where the network learns to use as many layers as needed for the task's complexity (many for CIFAR-100, fewer for MNIST). This is a unique and valuable analysis that other architectures cannot easily provide.

4.  **Generality:** The highway mechanism is general and can be applied to various layer types (fully-connected, convolutional) and is not dependent on a specific activation function for the transformation block `H`. This makes it a widely applicable tool.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Comparison to Concurrent Work (Residual Networks):** The most significant point of discussion is the paper's relationship to the concurrent work on Residual Networks (ResNets) [He et al., 2015]. The highway formulation `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x))` is a generalization of the core residual learning block `y = F(x) + x`. A ResNet block is effectively a highway block where the transform gate `T(x)` is hard-coded to 1 and the carry gate `C(x)` is also hard-coded to 1. While the highway network's gating is more expressive, subsequent research has shown that the simpler, parameter-free identity shortcut is often sufficient to train extremely deep networks. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of this connection, perhaps arguing for the benefits of learned gating (e.g., data-dependent depth) versus the simplicity and efficiency of the fixed identity mapping.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The initialization of the transform gate bias (`bT`) is presented as a key component for successful training. While the authors provide a reasonable heuristic (biasing more negatively for deeper networks), the sensitivity of the model to this hyperparameter is not fully explored. An ablation study showing the performance for different initial bias values would provide a clearer picture of the model's robustness.

3.  **Clarity on Simplification (`C = 1-T`):** The authors simplify the gating mechanism by setting the carry gate `C = 1 - T`. While this is a reasonable choice for simplicity and parameter efficiency, it would be interesting to know if learning `C` and `T` independently was explored and what the outcome was. This simplification couples the transform and carry operations, and decoupling them might offer additional flexibility.

**Assessment Against NIPS Criteria**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The claims are strongly supported by well-designed experiments and insightful analysis. The work is complete and rigorous.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very well-written and organized. The core idea is explained clearly with precise notation. The figures are illustrative and effectively support the main arguments. The work provides enough detail for reproducibility.
*   **Originality:** High. While inspired by LSTMs, the application and formulation of adaptive gating for very deep feedforward networks is a highly original and significant contribution. It represents a novel approach to a known problem.
*   **Significance:** Very High. This paper, along with the concurrent work on ResNets, fundamentally changed the way very deep neural networks are constructed. It provided one of the first and most effective mechanisms to break the depth barrier, directly enabling the development of the much deeper architectures that now dominate the field. The ideas of learnable routing and effective depth explored here remain highly relevant.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents an elegant, novel, and effective solution to the critical problem of training very deep networks.
*   The empirical evidence is strong and convincing, particularly the direct comparison with plain networks and the more complex FitNet training scheme.
*   The analysis of the learned gate behavior and the layer lesioning study provide deep insights that go beyond simply reporting a final accuracy number.
*   The work has had and will continue to have a major impact on the field of deep learning architecture design.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper lacks a discussion and comparison with the closely related and concurrent concept of Residual Networks, which has become the dominant paradigm. This is the main weakness but is understandable given the timing of the submission.

Despite the lack of comparison to ResNets, the novelty, quality, and significance of this work are undeniable. It is a landmark paper that introduces a powerful and general architectural principle. It is an excellent contribution to the field and a clear choice for acceptance at NIPS.
