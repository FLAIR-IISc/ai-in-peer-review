Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces learned, element-wise gating across depth, inspired by LSTM gating across time. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate” whose negative bias initialization encourages carry behavior at the start of training. This design creates “information highways,” i.e., computational paths with near-identity Jacobians that allow signals and gradients to traverse many layers. The authors show that very deep highway networks (50–100+ layers; pilot >1000) train reliably with SGD without special tricks and generalize competitively on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, including single-stage training of thin/deep nets that match or exceed FitNets trained with teacher hints. They also analyze gate activity and conduct lesion studies to assess layer contribution.

The work is well-situated within prior efforts to train deep nets: better optimizers (e.g., HF, saddle-point methods), variance-preserving initialization (Glorot/He), competitive/nonlinearities (maxout, local competition), skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and distillation/hint-based training (FitNets). The key novelty is to port LSTM-like gating to depth to make skip connections input-adaptive and trainable, going beyond fixed skip connections [e.g., earlier shortcut/skip practices] and related grid-LSTM ideas that include gating across dimensions.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: the gating formulation is orthogonal to the choice of H and applies to fully-connected, convolutional, and potentially recurrent layers.
- Clear optimization benefit: rigorous MNIST experiments show that plain nets degrade with depth while highway nets do not; deep highway nets converge faster and reach much lower training loss.
- Practical recipe: a simple negative bias for T suffices to enable training at large depths; the method avoids complex initialization or multi-stage procedures.
- Empirical competitiveness: on CIFAR-10/100, thin/deep highway nets trained in one stage match or outperform analogous FitNets trained with hints, under comparable parameter/compute budgets.
- Insightful analyses: gate activity visualizations, class-conditional routing differences, and lesion experiments demonstrate that (i) routing is input-dependent, and (ii) layer utilization scales with task complexity.
- Reproducibility: code and hyperparameter search results are released.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparison to fixed identity skip connections without gating (residual-style y = x + H(x)), which are a strong and closely related baseline for easing optimization. The paper cites skip connections but does not empirically test whether learnable gates are necessary.
- Limited scale: no ImageNet or similarly large-scale experiment to demonstrate scalability and competitive accuracy under standard augmentation/training regimes.
- Ablation depth: limited exploration of design choices such as C ≠ 1 − T, per-channel vs per-element gating, parameter sharing, or gate regularization; the effect and sensitivity of the transform-gate bias are discussed but not systematically quantified.
- Computational overhead: no detailed analysis of added parameters/compute and wall-clock impact of gating; it would help to quantify the trade-off between depth enabled and overhead incurred.
- Claims about “>1000 layers” are not substantiated with quantitative results beyond pilot remarks.
- Theoretical support is light: beyond the Jacobian intuition, no formal analysis of gradient propagation or optimization landscape is provided.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound, empirically validated on multiple datasets, and includes useful diagnostics (routing, lesioning). However, stronger baselines (identity skip) and more thorough ablations would bolster the claims about necessity and advantages of gating.
- Clarity: Overall clear and well-organized. Some implementation details could be clarified: precise definition of “block” vs “unit,” whether gates are per-feature-map vs per-spatial-location in conv layers, and exact training schedules. Figures are informative.
- Originality: Bringing LSTM-style gating to depth in feedforward nets is a novel and timely idea relative to existing skip connections and distillation. Related work is reasonably cited, including soft targets and earlier skip practices.
- Significance: The ability to train very deep networks directly with SGD is important and likely to influence architectures and practice. Even without state-of-the-art accuracy, the contribution is impactful as an enabling method and analysis of depth utilization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a strong residual baseline: compare highway networks to identity-skip networks with matched parameter counts (e.g., using 1x1 projections where needed). Measure optimization speed, final accuracy, and gate usage vs residual behavior.
- Systematic ablations: vary the initial gate bias; explore learning a separate carry gate C (vs 1 − T); compare per-channel vs per-element gates; assess parameter sharing across spatial dimensions; and test gate regularization/temperature.
- Report computational trade-offs: parameters, FLOPs, training time, and memory for each model; discuss whether gating introduces meaningful latency or memory penalties.
- Scale and breadth: include deeper CIFAR models (>100 layers) and, if feasible, an ImageNet or SVHN experiment to support scalability claims.
- Interaction with normalization: evaluate synergy with Batch Normalization (or similar) to clarify whether gates provide benefits beyond layer-wise normalization.
- Theory: provide a more formal analysis of gradient propagation through gated depth (e.g., expected Jacobian spectrum under gate statistics) to support the optimization claims.

Pro and con list
Pros:
- Simple, general architectural idea with clear intuition
- Strong evidence of improved trainability at great depths
- Competitive results against FitNets without teacher hints
- Insightful analyses of routing and layer importance
- Code release

Cons:
- No direct comparison to identity residual connections
- Limited large-scale evaluation and ablation studies
- Computational/parameter overhead not quantified
- Some claims (1000+ layers) lack experimental backing

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a clear and novel architectural mechanism that materially advances our ability to train very deep networks, with solid empirical evidence and insightful analyses. While additional baselines and ablations would strengthen the case, the contribution is significant and timely for NIPS.
