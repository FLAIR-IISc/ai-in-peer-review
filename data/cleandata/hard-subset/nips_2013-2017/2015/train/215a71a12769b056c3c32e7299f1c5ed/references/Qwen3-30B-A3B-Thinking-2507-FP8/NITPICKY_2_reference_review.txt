

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces "highway networks" as a novel architecture for training extremely deep feedforward networks. The authors propose a modification to the standard network layer that incorporates an adaptive gating mechanism inspired by LSTM networks. The key innovation is a transform gate and carry gate that allow information to flow across many layers without attenuation, creating "information highways." The paper demonstrates that highway networks can be trained directly with SGD even at extreme depths (up to 100 layers), outperforming plain networks which suffer from optimization difficulties as depth increases. The authors conduct experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets to support their claims.

## Strengths
- The highway network architecture is novel and well-motivated by the need to address the challenges of training very deep networks.
- The experimental results are generally convincing, showing that highway networks can be trained to greater depths than plain networks without sacrificing generalization.
- The analysis of transform gate behavior (Figure 2) and layer importance (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how highway networks operate.

## Weaknesses
While the paper presents an interesting idea, I identified numerous minor but significant issues that undermine the quality and clarity of the work. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance due to these inconsistencies:

1. **Critical formatting inconsistencies**: The reference list is poorly formatted with inconsistent punctuation, missing information, and non-standard venue names (e.g., "In IJCAI" instead of proper citation format). This is particularly unacceptable for a NIPS submission, where reference formatting is a standard expectation.

2. **Typos and grammatical errors**: "as show" should be "as shown" (Section 3.2), "LSTMinspired" should be "LSTM-inspired" (Introduction), and "a single but large hidden layer" (Introduction) should be "a single large hidden layer" for grammatical correctness.

3. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses "dy dx" in Equation 5 instead of the standard "dy/dx" for derivatives. The Jacobian is referred to as a derivative (dy/dx), which is technically incorrect since the Jacobian is a matrix.

4. **Missing critical details**: The paper claims "100-layer highway network performed more than 2 orders of magnitude better compared to a similarly-sized plain network" (Section 3.1) but fails to provide the actual numerical values. This is a critical omission that makes the claim difficult to evaluate.

5. **Confusing wording**: The caption for Figure 2 states "obtained via random search over hyperparameters to minimize the best training set error achieved using each configuration" - this is grammatically incorrect and confusing. It should be rephrased for clarity.

6. **Inconsistent parameter description**: In Section 3.1, the paper states "each layer has 50 blocks for highway networks and 71 units for plain networks, yielding roughly identical numbers of parameters (≈5000) per layer." However, 50 vs 71 is not "roughly identical" for a fair comparison, and the parameter count (≈5000) seems too low for a layer with 50 blocks or 71 units.

7. **Reference inconsistencies**: The paper cites "This paper expands upon a shorter report on Highway Networks [31]" but does not adequately differentiate this work from [31] or the similar LSTM-inspired model [32].

8. **Missing implementation details**: The paper lacks specific details about the hyperparameter search space used for the 100 random search runs mentioned in Section 3.1, which is essential for reproducibility.

9. **Inconsistent pluralization**: The paper inconsistently uses "network" (singular) when referring to multiple networks, which creates confusion (e.g., "networks with limited computational budget" vs "network's output").

## Overall Assessment
While the highway network concept is interesting and the experimental results generally support the main claim, the paper contains numerous minor but significant flaws that prevent it from being considered a high-quality NIPS submission. The formatting inconsistencies in the references, the lack of specific numerical evidence for key claims, and the grammatical errors collectively undermine the paper's credibility and professionalism.

The paper's technical contribution appears to be incremental rather than groundbreaking, as skip connections have been explored in prior work (e.g., ResNets). The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from these existing methods or demonstrate a clear advantage beyond what's already known.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

The paper's numerous minor formatting and technical inconsistencies, combined with the lack of critical details to support key claims, make it difficult to evaluate the work's true contribution. While the concept of highway networks is interesting, the paper's execution falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS. I recommend the authors revise the paper to address these issues before resubmission to a venue with less stringent formatting requirements.
