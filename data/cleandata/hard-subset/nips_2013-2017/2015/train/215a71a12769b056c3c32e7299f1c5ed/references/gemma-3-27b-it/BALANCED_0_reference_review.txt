This paper introduces Highway Networks, a novel architecture aimed at enabling the training of very deep feedforward neural networks. The core idea is to incorporate LSTM-inspired “highway” connections that allow for easier information flow across layers, mitigating the vanishing gradient problem. The authors demonstrate, through extensive experimentation on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, that these networks can be trained to significant depths (over 100 layers) without the optimization difficulties encountered with plain networks. They also show competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

This work builds upon a significant body of research concerning the challenges of training deep networks. Early work focused on initialization strategies (Glorot & Bengio, 2015; He et al., 2015) to address vanishing/exploding gradients. More recently, architectural innovations like Batch Normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) and residual connections (He et al., 2016) have shown substantial improvements.  The paper appropriately situates itself within this lineage, drawing inspiration from LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) as a successful paradigm for propagating information across many time steps, and relating its approach to related work on skip connections (Srivastava et al., 2015) and FitNets (Romero et al., 2015).  The connection to neural history compressors (Schmidhuber, 1992) is also well noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The highway network architecture, with its dynamically gated connections, presents a fresh approach to addressing the training difficulties of very deep networks. It offers a unique mechanism for regulating information flow, differing from fixed skip connections or normalization techniques.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation across several datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100).  The results consistently demonstrate the ability to train significantly deeper networks with highway connections compared to plain networks. The comparison to FitNets is particularly compelling, showing competitive performance with a simpler approach.
* **Clear Explanation:** The architecture is clearly explained, and the mathematical formulation is concise and understandable. The visualization of the transform gate activity and layer importance (Figures 2 and 4) provides valuable insight into the network's internal workings.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and hyperparameters publicly available, fostering reproducibility and further research.
* **Analysis:** The analysis of the transform gate biases and their impact on information flow is insightful and helps to understand the behavior of the network.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper demonstrates good performance, the optimization experiments (Section 3.1) involve a random search over a number of hyperparameters.  A more systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space and an analysis of sensitivity to these parameters would strengthen the results.  What is the impact of different bias initializations for the transform gates?
* **Computational Cost:** The introduction of the transform gates adds parameters to the network, increasing the computational cost compared to plain networks. While the paper focuses on demonstrating feasibility, a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between depth, accuracy, and computational cost would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to Recent Architectures:** While comparisons to FitNets and existing methods are provided, the paper doesn’t compare extensively to more recent, highly successful architectures like ResNets (He et al., 2016) or DenseNets (Huang et al., 2017). A more direct comparison to these state-of-the-art approaches would provide a clearer assessment of the benefits of highway networks.  Does the highway network perform comparably given similar computational budgets as ResNets?
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the LSTM inspiration is noted, a stronger theoretical justification for *why* highway networks alleviate the training difficulties of deep networks would be beneficial. Is there a connection to the spectral properties of the network, or the geometry of the loss landscape?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture for training very deep networks.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over plain networks.
* The code and hyperparameters are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.
* The analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the network.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost could limit the practical applicability of the approach.
* The comparison to recent state-of-the-art architectures is limited.
* The lack of a strong theoretical justification weakens the overall contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a promising new architecture for training deep networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of highway networks through a thorough empirical evaluation. While there are some weaknesses related to hyperparameter sensitivity, computational cost, and theoretical justification, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the weaknesses highlighted above in a revised version. Specifically, a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and a more comprehensive comparison to recent state-of-the-art architectures would significantly strengthen the paper.
