**Summary**

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel feedforward neural network architecture designed to facilitate the training of extremely deep models. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanism of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The architecture incorporates adaptive "transform" and "carry" gates that control the flow of information through each layer. Depending on the gate outputs, a layer can either transform its input, as in a conventional network, or pass it through unmodified. This mechanism creates unimpeded "information highways," allowing gradients to propagate effectively through hundreds of layers. The authors demonstrate experimentally that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained directly with SGD, significantly outperforming conventional "plain" networks of similar depth on MNIST. They also show competitive results on CIFAR-10/100, suggesting the architecture generalizes well.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality**

The paper's primary strength is its elegant solution to a significant and timely problem. The idea of using LSTM-style gating in a feedforward context is compelling and well-motivated. The experimental demonstration that a 100-layer highway network can be trained effectively, while a comparable plain network fails, is a powerful piece of evidence supporting the authors' claims. The analysis in Section 4, particularly the lesioning study (Figure 4), provides valuable insight, suggesting that the network learns to utilize depth as needed for a given task.

However, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from minor typos to significant methodological concerns, which undermine the quality of the work.

1.  **Flawed Experimental Comparison:** The central experiment in Section 3.1, comparing plain and highway networks, claims to use models with "roughly identical numbers of parameters." A quick calculation suggests this is not the case. A highway layer requires weight matrices for both the transformation `H` and the gate `T`, effectively doubling the parameters relative to a plain layer with the same input/output dimensions. The comparison of a 71-unit plain layer to a 50-unit highway block is misleading, as the latter has significantly more parameters per input dimension. This disparity is a critical confounding variable that weakens the conclusion that the architectural change alone is responsible for the improved trainability.

2.  **Questionable Analysis Methodology:** The analysis in Section 4 is performed on models selected to "minimize the best training set error" (Footnote 3). Analyzing a model that is explicitly optimized for training performance, rather than validation performance, is highly problematic. The insights gained may describe an overfitted model and may not be representative of a model that generalizes well.

3.  **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper mentions that in pilot experiments, "SGD did not stall for networks with more than 1000 layers." This is a remarkable claim, but it is presented without any supporting evidence, figures, or experimental details. Such statements should be either substantiated or omitted. Similarly, the claim that highway networks "consistently converged significantly faster" is not quantified.

**Clarity**

The paper's core concepts are explained reasonably well, but the overall presentation lacks the precision and polish expected for a NIPS submission.

1.  **Formatting and Typos:** The manuscript is riddled with formatting errors. The bibliography is in a state of disarray, with numerous incomplete entries (e.g., [1], [2], [3], [32]) that appear to be malformed BibTeX outputs. In the Notation section, the definition of the sigmoid function contains a typo (`11+e−x` instead of `1/(1+e^-x)`). In Footnote 2, the symbol for element-wise multiplication ('*') is used, which is inconsistent with the '·' operator defined in the Notation section.

2.  **Inconsistent Statements:** In Section 5, the authors claim that a "suitable initialization scheme is not essential." This directly contradicts the description in Section 2.2, where a specific negative bias initialization for the transform gates is introduced as a key technique, inspired by prior work and seemingly crucial for training to proceed.

3.  **Imprecise Mathematical Formulation:** Equations (4) and (5) present the layer behavior for the exact conditions `T(x,WT) = 0` or `T(x,WT) = 1`. However, as the authors later note, these conditions are never met since the sigmoid function's range is (0, 1). While the general idea is clear, this initial mathematical presentation is imprecise and could be confusing.

**Originality**

The application of LSTM-like gates to deep feedforward networks is a novel contribution. The connection is well-drawn and represents a clever transfer of ideas across different domains of deep learning. However, the authors note in Footnote 1 that "a similar LSTM-inspired model was also proposed [32]." Given the importance of this concurrent work (presumably Residual Networks), its relationship to highway networks should be discussed in the main body of the paper, not relegated to a footnote. A more detailed comparison of the two gating mechanisms (additive shortcut vs. learned multiplicative gating) is warranted to clearly delineate the contributions. The paper also states it expands on a shorter report [31], but the novel contributions beyond that report are not explicitly stated.

**Significance**

The work addresses a problem of high significance: enabling the training of very deep networks. The proposed architecture is a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to influence future architectural design. The finding that networks can learn to moderate their effective depth is an important result. However, the significance of the empirical results is dampened by the methodological issues mentioned above, particularly the flawed comparison in the core optimization experiment and the lack of truly state-of-the-art results on CIFAR benchmarks (which the authors themselves acknowledge).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
*   Addresses a problem of central importance to the deep learning community.
*   Provides strong empirical evidence that the proposed networks overcome optimization difficulties seen in plain networks of equivalent depth.
*   The analysis offers valuable insights into how deep networks can learn to utilize depth.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The manuscript suffers from a severe lack of polish, including numerous typos and a completely malformed bibliography, which is not acceptable for a top-tier publication.
*   The main experimental comparison in Section 3.1 appears to be methodologically flawed due to an unfair comparison of parameter counts.
*   The analysis in Section 4 is performed on models optimized for training error, calling the generalizability of its conclusions into question.
*   The paper fails to adequately discuss and contrast its contributions with highly relevant concurrent work [32].
*   Several claims are unsubstantiated or contradictory.

**Recommendation**

While the core idea of highway networks is innovative and significant, the execution and presentation of this manuscript fall well below the standards for NIPS. The combination of a flawed main experimental comparison, questionable analysis methodology, and an unacceptable number of presentation errors (especially in the bibliography) leads me to recommend **rejection**. The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution, but it requires a major revision to correct these fundamental issues.
