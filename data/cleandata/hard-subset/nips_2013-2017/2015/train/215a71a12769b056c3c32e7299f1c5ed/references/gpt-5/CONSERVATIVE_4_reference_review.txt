Summary
The paper proposes highway networks, a feedforward architecture that introduces elementwise gating inspired by LSTM to facilitate information flow across many layers. Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a sigmoid “transform gate” with a negative bias initialization to favor carry behavior early in training. The authors argue this enables training of very deep networks with simple SGD, mitigating optimization difficulties as depth grows. Experiments compare fully connected and convolutional highway networks against plain networks on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. They show improved optimization with depth (up to 100 layers in FC settings), competitive accuracy on MNIST with fewer parameters, and performance competitive with FitNets on CIFAR without teacher supervision. Analyses include visualizations of gate activities, class-conditional routing behavior, and lesion studies showing layer importance.

Relation to prior work is discussed with respect to LSTM gating, skip connections, maxout, teacher–student distillation, and initialization strategies. The work is closely related to, but distinct from, fixed skip/shortcut connections; here the skip/carry is gated and input-dependent.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea that generalizes skip connections with adaptive gating; easy to implement and conceptually well-motivated by LSTM.
- Empirical evidence that optimization does not degrade with depth for highway networks, in contrast to plain nets, including training curves and convergence speed.
- Some attention to reproducibility (code release) and reporting variability (5 runs for certain results).
- Interesting analyses of gating behavior (bias evolution, sparsity, class-conditional routing) and a lesion study indicating that depth usage depends on task complexity.
- Comparisons to FitNets suggest highway networks can train deeper/thinner models in a single stage without distillation.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong and modern baselines. In particular:
  - No comparison to identity skip connections (e.g., residual networks), which are the closest alternative and test whether gating is necessary vs. simple additive shortcuts.
  - No batch normalization baselines or combinations. BN is known to substantially ease optimization and could reduce the need for gating.
- Limited scale and scope. Claims of “very deep” training are demonstrated mainly on MNIST (FC up to 100 layers). Convolutional results are relatively shallow (e.g., 10–32 layers) and limited to CIFAR; no large-scale dataset (e.g., ImageNet) or very deep conv nets to substantiate the main claim in realistic settings.
- Fairness of comparisons and controls could be improved:
  - Parameter and FLOP matching is not consistently reported for convolutional settings; gating introduces extra parameters and compute that are not accounted for in runtime/throughput comparisons.
  - Optimization plots highlight the single best run per setting; aggregates (mean/variance across seeds) for the depth-scaling study would be more convincing.
- Insufficient ablations to isolate what matters:
  - Necessity of gating vs. fixed skip connections; a “residual” ablation (y = H(x)+x) is a critical control.
  - The choice C = 1 − T is restrictive; an ablation with independent carry gate C could clarify whether coupling helps or hurts.
  - Sensitivity to gate bias initialization is only anecdotally discussed; a systematic study is missing.
  - What happens if T is fixed (e.g., constant) or if the nonlinearity in T is changed? Are improvements due to added depth/parameters or gating per se?
- No analysis of gradient propagation (e.g., gradient norms or signal singular values across depth) to substantiate the “information highway” claim beyond empirical training curves.
- Practical aspects underreported: wall-clock convergence, inference cost, and memory overhead relative to baselines at matched accuracy.

Quality
The technical idea is sound and well-specified. However, evidence falls short of rigor expected for an architectural contribution: key baselines (residual/BN), strong ablations, and scaling to deep conv nets are missing. The FitNet comparison is useful but does not resolve whether gates outperform simpler shortcuts. The lesion and visualization analyses are insightful but do not substitute for stronger comparative experiments.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Notation is straightforward, and the architectural description is accessible. Some details could be improved:
- Define “block” and its dimensionality precisely for conv layers, including how padding/stride are handled.
- Report parameter counts and FLOPs per model, and training/inference time.
- Clarify whether validation sets were used exclusively for hyperparameter selection, and provide full hyperparameters for each experiment.

Originality
Moderate. The specific gating formulation for feedforward depth is novel and thoughtfully adapted from LSTM. However, the work is close in spirit to existing ideas on skip/shortcut connections and improved initialization, and the paper would benefit from a direct, explicit comparison to identity skip connections to delineate the unique benefit of gating.

Significance
Potentially significant if the approach consistently enables deeper training and outperforms simple residual connections. As presented, the significance is limited by the experimental scope; results on CIFAR are competitive but not clearly state-of-the-art, and the core claim is not validated on large-scale/deep conv settings.

Requests for revision/experiments
- Add baselines with identity shortcuts (residual networks) and with batch normalization; also test highway + BN. Match parameters and FLOPs.
- Provide ablations: independent carry gate C, no gating (residual), fixed T, different gate bias initializations, and report robustness across seeds.
- Include gradient flow diagnostics across depth.
- Report compute: training time to a fixed accuracy and inference throughput.
- Scale conv experiments to deeper networks on CIFAR and, ideally, a subset of ImageNet to support the “very deep” claim.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated architectural change with promising empirical behavior.
- Clear write-up, code released, analyses beyond accuracy.
- Demonstrates ease of training vs. plain deep nets; competitive CIFAR results without distillation.

Cons
- Lacks comparison to the most relevant modern baselines (residual, BN).
- Limited experimental scale; strongest claims not tested in conv nets at depth.
- Missing ablations to establish necessity of gating and design choices.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and could be impactful, but the current empirical validation does not meet the bar for an architectural paper at NIPS without direct comparisons to identity skip connections and batch normalization, stronger ablations, and deeper conv experiments.

Confidence
High.
