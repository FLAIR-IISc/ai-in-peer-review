- Proposes Highway Networks: per-layer LSTM-style transform gate T(x) so y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)) to adaptively route information and preserve identity-like Jacobians.  
- Introduces a negative-bias initialization for T to favor carry behavior early in training.  
- Shows large optimization improvements with depth on MNIST (10–100 layers) under controlled parameter budgets.  
- Demonstrates competitive CIFAR-10/100 performance with single-stage training of deep, thin models comparable to FitNets.  
- Analyzes gate statistics, class-conditional routing, and lesioning to show gates both ease training and become part of learned computation.  
- Conceptually grounded in LSTM gating and distinct from prior skip/shortcut or distillation approaches by using learned, input-conditional multiplicative skips.  
- Methodology is sound and clearly specified; using C = 1−T reduces parameters reasonably.  
- Empirical studies are insightful but lack comparisons to fixed identity/linear shortcuts and batch normalization.  
- Missing larger-scale experiments (e.g., ImageNet), formal gradient-propagation analysis, and compute/latency profiling.  
- Paper is clearly written, well organized, and provides code/hyperparameters for reproducibility.  
- The idea of depth gating and lesion analyses is original and informative.  
- Contribution is significant: simple, general architectural fix that mitigates depth-related optimization issues.  
- Strength: principled intuition (controllable Jacobians) and strong depth-robust optimization results.  
- Strength: single-stage training avoiding teacher hints and practical initialization heuristic.  
- Weakness: unclear necessity of gating vs. plain identity skips—missing direct baselines.  
- Weakness: interaction with batch normalization and sensitivity to gate bias or gating nonlinearities not explored.  
- Weakness: extra compute from gates—no FLOPs or wall-clock comparisons provided.  
- Suggestions: add identity-skip and BN baselines, FLOPs/latency/memory comparisons, ablations on gate bias/nonlinearity, and per-layer gradient statistics.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses core training issue with novel, general, and empirically supported architecture plus reproducible code.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete baselines (skip/Bn), limited dataset scope, and missing practical compute/ablation guidance.  
- Final recommendation: Accept; reviewer confidence: High.
