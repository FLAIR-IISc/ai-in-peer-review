Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes “highway networks,” a feedforward architecture inspired by LSTM gating that augments each layer with a transform gate T(x) and a carry gate C(x) (tied as C=1−T). Each layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), enabling data-dependent passthrough paths across depth. The authors argue this eases optimization in very deep networks, analogous to how LSTM gates support long-range credit assignment in time. They recommend initializing the transform gate bias negatively to bias toward carry at initialization. Experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 suggest highway nets train faster and maintain performance as depth increases; they outperform “plain” nets of comparable parameter counts and match/beat FitNets without teacher hints. Analyses include gate activity visualizations, class-conditional routing patterns, and layer “lesioning” to assess importance.

This connects to and extends: LSTM/GRU-style gating (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997; Gers et al. 2000), skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision (various), initialization schemes (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.), locally competitive mechanisms (maxout, etc.), and FitNets’ hint-based training. The paper differs by (i) introducing elementwise adaptive gates inside feedforward layers that enable identity paths, and (ii) offering a simple bias initialization to obtain deep trainability without layer-wise pretraining or teacher hints.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple architectural idea grounded in well-known gating mechanisms; the layer form interpolates between identity and H, plausibly improving gradient flow.
- Empirically demonstrates optimization advantages with depth on MNIST and competitive performance on CIFAR-10/100 within a light augmentation regime.
- Ablative analyses (gate activity, class-dependent routing, lesion experiments) are insightful and support the claim that gates are used for input-dependent routing and that depth utilization depends on task difficulty.
- Practical guidance on gate bias initialization and evidence that very deep (>50–100) models can be trained end-to-end with SGD.

Weaknesses (many are “minor” but numerous and material):
Quality and experimental rigor:
- Missing or underspecified experimental details impede reproducibility: exact learning rate schedules (λ, γ, decay epochs), momentum, weight decay, data augmentation specifics, batch size, number of training epochs, and exact architectures (per-layer widths, kernel sizes, padding/stride, where dimensionality changes occur).
- Comparisons to plain baselines: the effort to equalize parameter counts per layer is commendable (50 blocks vs 71 units), but the total parameter counts, FLOPs, and wall-clock/throughput are not reported. Since T requires an extra transform per layer (often a conv), compute overhead can be substantial; a fair comparison needs compute-matched baselines and timing.
- Limited ablations: no quantitative study of the effect of tying C=1−T vs learning C independently; sensitivity to transform-gate bias; activation choice in H; per-unit vs per-channel gating; effect of removing gate parameterization (e.g., fixed skip) while matching parameters; or using batch normalization. Claims that negative bias is “sufficient” are not supported with systematic experiments.
- Claims of training >1000 layers are anecdotal (“pilot experiments”); no quantitative results reported.
- CIFAR comparisons to FitNets: “comparable” params/ops are asserted but not tabulated; training protocols may differ (e.g., augmentation, regularization), weakening conclusions. Reporting Best(mean ± std) “wherever available” is inconsistent; statistical uncertainty is not always presented.
- Theoretical support is limited to informal Jacobian observations at T≈0 or T≈1; no analysis of gradient norms under realistic T∈(0,1), or bounds on signal/gradient preservation.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous formatting and typographical issues: duplicated words (“varying varying”), misspellings (“backpropogation”), inconsistent notation for elementwise product (· vs ∗), dangling footnote markers (1, 2) with missing content, mid-paragraph footnotes intruding into the text, incomplete/misplaced sentences (e.g., “Similarly, for the Jacobian...” followed by a footnote), and broken math (σ(x) formatted as 11+e−x). Figures and tables are referenced but not visible here; several key claims hinge on them.
- References are inconsistent and often incomplete (missing years/venues, improper formatting). Some key related works (e.g., modern skip/shortcut connections) are discussed broadly, but precise, complete citations are needed.
- The code URL appears with a stray space (“people. idsia.ch”), and reproducibility depends on external frameworks not fully specified (Caffe/Brainstorm versions, commit hashes).

Originality:
- The architectural form is a novel, simple application of gating to feedforward depth at the time, distinct from prior maxout/competition and fixed skip connections. The relation to LSTM gating is clear and well-motivated. Tying C=1−T and using elementwise gates are specific design choices that differentiate this from generic skip links.

Significance:
- Potentially substantial: if robust, the approach lowers the barrier to training very deep networks and provides a flexible, input-dependent routing mechanism. The analyses (lesioning, routing) are interesting and could spur follow-up work. However, the current empirical evidence, while promising, is not yet airtight due to missing details, limited ablations, and somewhat narrow benchmarks/settings.

Specific questions and suggestions:
- Provide full architecture specs (layer-by-layer), parameter counts, FLOPs, and training-time comparisons for all baselines and highway variants. Include batch size, epochs, λ/γ/schedule, momentum, weight decay, augmentation details, and random seeds.
- Add ablations: untied C, removing carry (C=0), per-channel vs per-unit T, effect of different bT, with/without batch norm, H activation choices. Quantify sensitivity and provide guidance beyond rules of thumb.
- The Jacobian/gradient discussion should include the full derivative of y wrt x (including dT/dx terms) and a qualitative/quantitative analysis of gradient norms when T∈(0,1).
- Clarify gating in convs: is T spatially and channel-wise? Any parameter sharing? Report the added parameter/compute overhead from T.
- Ensure fair FitNet comparisons (identical augmentation/regularization) and report standard deviations consistently.
- Clean up notation, fix typos, harmonize elementwise operator notation, and correct references; supply missing footnotes; fix the code URL.
- Substantiate the “>1000 layers” claim with at least one quantitative result.

Pros:
- Simple, well-motivated architectural idea with clear intuition.
- Strong optimization benefits with depth; competitive results without teacher hints.
- Insightful qualitative analyses of gating and layer importance.

Cons:
- Insufficient experimental detail and ablations; fairness and compute cost not rigorously addressed.
- Weak theoretical support beyond intuitive limits.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues and inconsistent references.
- Some claims (e.g., 1000-layer feasibility) are not empirically substantiated.

Recommendation:
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and likely impactful, but the accumulation of missing details, limited ablations, fairness concerns, and presentation issues prevents a confident, reproducible assessment. With a more thorough empirical study, clearer reporting, and cleaned-up presentation, this could become a strong contribution. My decision is driven by rigor and clarity rather than the novelty of the core idea, which I find compelling.
