- Proposes Highway Networks: layer output y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), with T a sigmoid transform gate and negative bias initialization to default to carry behavior early in training.  
- Idea motivated by LSTM/GRU gating, skip/shortcut connections, initialization schemes, and FitNet hinting; claims input-dependent routing vs. fixed shortcuts.  
- Implements both fully connected and convolutional highway layers; transform gates share dimensionality and receptive fields with H and use tied C = 1 − T.  
- Training uses momentum SGD, simple schedules, negative gate-bias init, and ReLU for H in conv nets; plain nets use normalized initialization for comparison.  
- Optimization study on MNIST shows plain nets degrade with depth while highway nets (50–100 layers) train well, converge faster, and reach much lower loss.  
- Generalization experiments: competitive MNIST results and CIFAR-10/100 performance matching or improving FitNets without teacher hints under similar parameter/FLOP budgets.  
- Analysis includes visualizations of gate biases/activities and lesion experiments indicating sparse, input-dependent gating and dataset-dependent layer importance.  
- Strength: Simple, well-motivated architecture that directly addresses gradient/activation attenuation via an always-available identity carry path.  
- Strength: Demonstrates training of very deep, thin networks with vanilla SGD without auxiliary losses or distillation.  
- Strength: Solid controlled optimization experiments showing consistent improvements with depth.  
- Strength: Diagnostics (gate patterns, lesioning) support adaptive routing interpretation.  
- Strength: Code release and training details aid reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Empirical validation limited to MNIST and CIFAR; claims of >1000 layers lack quantitative evidence and no large-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) evaluated.  
- Weakness: No direct comparison to residual/identity-only skip connections to isolate gating benefits.  
- Weakness: Interaction with batch normalization not discussed despite BN’s importance for deep nets.  
- Weakness: Partial coverage of strong baselines (maxout, all-conv) and unclear fairness of parameter/FLOP matching to FitNets.  
- Weakness: Architectural ablations limited—tying C = 1 − T, gate-bias sensitivity, independent carry gates, and gate overhead remain underexplored.  
- Weakness: Minimal theoretical analysis of gradient propagation (e.g., Jacobian/gradient norms) to substantiate vanishing/exploding claims.  
- Weakness: Some CIFAR architectural/hyperparameter/FLOP details omitted from main text, hindering reproduction.  
- Questions: How does a residual-only variant (y = H(x)+x) compare in optimization and accuracy under the same settings?  
- Questions: Have untied carry gates or alternative gate parameterizations (e.g., hard-sigmoid) been evaluated for stability/performance?  
- Questions: Can gate usage be quantified (e.g., fraction of units with T < 0.1) to relate to effective depth and conditional computation savings?  
- Questions: How robust is training to gate bias choice across datasets/depths, and would bias annealing be beneficial?  
- Questions: What is the parameter and wall-clock overhead introduced by gates relative to the observed gains?  
- Suggestions: Add ablations for C = 1 − T vs. untied C, initial gate bias, and a residual-only baseline matched for parameters.  
- Suggestions: Report gradient norm/Jacobian statistics across depth to support information-flow claims.  
- Suggestions: Report FLOPs and training times and consider experiments on small-scale ImageNet or SVHN to broaden validation.  
- Suggestions: Examine interaction with batch normalization and provide full architectural/hyperparameter details in an appendix.  
- Overall: Technically sound and original with strong empirical evidence in studied regimes; would benefit from stronger baselines, ablations, broader validation, and theoretical analysis—lean towards acceptance.
