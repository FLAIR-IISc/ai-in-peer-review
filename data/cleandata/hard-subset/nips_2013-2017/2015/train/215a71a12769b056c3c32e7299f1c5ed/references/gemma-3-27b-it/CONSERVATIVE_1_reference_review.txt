This paper introduces “highway networks,” a modification to standard feedforward networks designed to facilitate training of very deep architectures by incorporating LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms. The core idea is to allow for direct information flow across many layers, mitigating the vanishing gradient problem. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained with SGD to depths previously inaccessible to standard networks, and generalize well to unseen data.

While the motivation is sound – the difficulty of training very deep networks is a long-standing issue – and the initial results are promising, I have significant reservations about this submission in its current form. The paper feels somewhat exploratory and lacks the rigorous comparative analysis one expects for a NIPS publication.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Training very deep networks remains a significant challenge, and any progress in this area is valuable.
*   **Novel Architecture:** The highway network architecture, inspired by LSTMs, is a reasonably novel approach to addressing the vanishing gradient problem. The concept of dynamically routing information is interesting.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments on MNIST demonstrate the ability to train networks significantly deeper than previously possible with standard methods. The CIFAR-10/100 results are also encouraging.
*   **Availability of Code:** Publicly available code is a strong positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparisons to “plain” networks, while demonstrating the advantage of highway networks, are not sufficient. The authors mention FitNets and other state-of-the-art architectures, but the comparisons are superficial. A more comprehensive evaluation against established methods—including recent advances in initialization, optimization, and architectural designs (e.g., ResNets, DenseNets)—is crucial. The statement that highway networks "match/exceed the accuracy of wide and shallow maxout networks" needs to be demonstrably shown with a direct comparison, not just an assertion.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions random search over hyperparameters, but doesn't adequately address the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. The initialization scheme for the transform gates is presented as crucial, yet the best values are determined empirically without a detailed analysis of why certain values work better than others.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the LSTM inspiration is stated, a deeper theoretical understanding of *why* highway networks work is lacking. A more in-depth analysis of the gradients flowing through these networks could provide valuable insights. The Jacobian analysis in Section 2 is underdeveloped and doesn't offer substantial understanding.
*   **Analysis is Descriptive, Not Explanatory:** The analysis of the transform gate behavior (Section 4) is largely descriptive. While the observation that some layers become inactive is interesting, the authors don't provide a compelling explanation for this phenomenon. The connection between transform gate activity and class-specific features is suggestive but not conclusive.
*   **CIFAR comparisons are weak:** The claim of matching/exceeding Fitnet performance lacks specifics. Table 2 doesn't provide enough detail for a fair comparison (e.g. what were the exact hyperparameters used for Fitnet?).
*   **Overstated Claims:** Phrases like "easy to train" are overly enthusiastic. Training very deep networks *always* requires careful tuning and significant computational resources.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel architectural modification with promising initial results.
*   Addresses an important problem in deep learning.
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently rigorous evaluation against state-of-the-art baselines.
*   Lack of theoretical justification for the observed improvements.
*   Descriptive analysis lacking explanatory power.
*   Overstated claims about the ease of training.
*   Comparisons to other methods are too brief and lack important details.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea, but falls short of the standards typically expected for NIPS. The empirical results are encouraging, but the lack of comprehensive comparisons, theoretical analysis, and in-depth understanding of the underlying mechanisms prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I believe this work has potential, but requires substantial revision and further experimentation to convincingly demonstrate its significance. I suggest the authors focus on more thorough baselines, a more rigorous analysis of the learned gating mechanisms, and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach. A rejection is warranted, with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing these concerns.
