### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors identify that the degradation in performance for "plain" deep networks is due to difficulties in propagating information and gradients across many layers. To overcome this, they propose a novel architecture called "Highway Networks," inspired by the gating mechanism of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks.

The core idea is to introduce adaptive gates for each layer that regulate information flow. A layer's output is a dynamic combination of its transformed input, `H(x)`, and its unmodified input, `x`. This is controlled by a "transform gate," `T(x)`, such that the output is `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 - T(x))`. This structure creates "information highways" where data can flow unimpeded across many layers if the transform gates are "closed" (outputting values near 0). This mechanism allows for the direct training of networks with tens or even hundreds of layers using standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors propose a simple and effective initialization scheme where the biases of the transform gates are set to negative values, encouraging "carry" behavior at the start of training. The paper provides extensive empirical evidence on MNIST and CIFAR datasets, demonstrating that highway networks do not suffer from the optimization problems of plain networks as depth increases and can achieve strong performance.

The work builds on a long history of research into training deep architectures. It explicitly connects its gating concept to LSTMs [29, 30], which use gates to manage information flow over time. It contrasts its adaptive approach with prior work on fixed skip connections, improved optimizers [13], and specialized initialization schemes [16, 17]. It also presents itself as a more direct, single-stage alternative to hint-based training methods like FitNets [25], which require a pre-trained teacher network.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—adapting the LSTM gating principle to manage network depth—is highly original and significant. It provides a principled and effective solution to the widely recognized problem of training very deep networks. The concept of "information highways" is intuitive and powerful, directly addressing the gradient flow issue that had previously limited network depth. This work opens the door to routinely training networks that are an order of magnitude deeper than what was previously feasible with standard architectures. The potential for other researchers to build upon this architectural innovation is substantial.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The experiments are rigorously designed and provide compelling support for the paper's claims.
    *   The optimization experiment in Section 3.1 is a crucial piece of evidence. The direct comparison between plain and highway networks of increasing depth clearly illustrates that highway networks overcome the optimization barrier that plagues plain networks. The fact that a 100-layer highway network outperforms a 20-layer plain network is a very strong result.
    *   The comparison with FitNets [25] demonstrates a significant practical advantage: highway networks enable the training of thin, deep models in a single stage, obviating the need for complex, multi-stage procedures like hint-based training.
    *   The analysis in Section 4 is a major strength. The authors do not just report performance metrics but also investigate the internal workings of the network. The visualizations of gate activity, the analysis of data-dependent routing, and the layer lesioning study provide valuable insights and strongly suggest that the gates learn a non-trivial, functional role beyond simply easing optimization.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the proposed architecture is explained with mathematical precision, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently. The figures are informative and effectively support the text. The authors provide enough detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Complexity of Gating Mechanism:** The proposed gating mechanism introduces additional parameters and computational overhead for each layer (for calculating `T(x)`). While the paper convincingly argues for the benefits of this adaptive, data-dependent routing, it does not explore whether a simpler, non-adaptive mechanism (e.g., a fixed, parameter-free identity connection) could achieve a similar effect in easing gradient flow. The necessity of the full multiplicative gating complexity for solving the optimization problem is not conclusively established against simpler potential alternatives.

2.  **Additional Hyperparameters:** The method introduces a new and seemingly important hyperparameter: the initial bias for the transform gates. While the authors provide a helpful heuristic (biasing towards carry behavior with values like -1 to -3), this adds another element to the hyperparameter search space. A more thorough analysis of the network's sensitivity to this initialization across different datasets and architectures would have strengthened the paper.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the field. It tackles a fundamental and timely problem—the training of very deep neural networks—with an elegant and effective solution. The claims are well-supported by thorough experiments and insightful analysis. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and demonstrates a clear advance over previous state-of-the-art methods for training deep models. While the gating mechanism adds some complexity, its demonstrated ability to enable stable training of networks with hundreds of layers is a breakthrough. The paper is an excellent fit for NIPS and is likely to have a major impact on future research in deep learning architectures.
