### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel neural network architecture designed to enable the training of extremely deep feedforward models. The core problem addressed is the difficulty of optimizing very deep "plain" networks due to degraded information and gradient flow across many layers. The authors draw inspiration from the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. The proposed architecture introduces "transform" and "carry" gates for each layer, which dynamically regulate the flow of information. A layer's output is a convex combination of its transformed input (the standard non-linear operation) and the input itself. By adjusting these gates, a layer can smoothly vary between transforming its input and passing it through unchanged, creating unimpeded "information highways."

The key contributions are: (1) the proposal of the Highway Network architecture; (2) an effective initialization strategy where the gates are biased towards carrying information, which facilitates training from the start; (3) extensive experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrating that highway networks with depths of 50 or 100 layers can be trained directly with SGD, whereas plain networks of similar depth fail to optimize; and (4) an analysis of the trained networks, suggesting that the gates learn a data-dependent routing mechanism and that the effective depth used by the network adapts to the complexity of the problem.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a highly significant and timely problem. At the time of this work, pushing network depth was a primary direction for improving performance, but was severely hampered by optimization challenges. This paper provides a simple, elegant, and effective solution that fundamentally changes what is possible, allowing for the direct training of networks an order of magnitude deeper than was previously feasible with standard architectures. The ideas presented are foundational and likely to be widely used and built upon.

2.  **Originality and Quality of the Idea:** While directly inspired by LSTMs, the application of adaptive gating to feedforward architectures to control information flow across depth is a novel and powerful concept. The formulation `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x))` is intuitive and effective. It provides a principled way to blend identity mappings with complex transformations, directly tackling the problem of signal propagation. This is a more flexible and powerful approach than fixed skip connections proposed in prior work.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Rigorous Experiments (Quality):** The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed set of experiments.
    *   The optimization experiment on MNIST (Section 3.1) is a clear and compelling demonstration of the core claim. It directly compares plain and highway networks of increasing depth, showing convincingly that while plain networks degrade, highway networks maintain their ability to optimize effectively up to 100 layers.
    *   The comparison to FitNets is highly relevant. Showing that a 32-layer highway network can be trained in a single stage to outperform a complex two-stage "hint-based" training procedure for a shallower network is a strong practical result.
    *   The performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, proving that the architecture is not just an optimization trick but can also achieve excellent generalization.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting test errors. The analysis in Section 4 is a major strength. The visualizations of gate activities provide valuable insight, suggesting that the network learns a sparse, data-dependent routing of information. The layer lesioning study (Figure 4) provides a novel way to quantify the "effective depth" a network learns for a given task, demonstrating that the network can utilize its depth for complex problems (CIFAR-100) while effectively ignoring unneeded layers for simpler ones (MNIST). This is a desirable property for deep models.

5.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core idea is introduced clearly with simple notation. The motivation is well-grounded in prior work, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently. The authors make their code and experimental details publicly available, which is commendable and enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

1.  **Limited Ablation of the Gating Mechanism:** The paper simplifies the carry gate to be `C = 1 - T`. While pragmatic, it would have strengthened the paper to include an ablation study where `C` is a separate, independently learned gate. This would help clarify if the coupled gating is essential or just a convenient simplification.

2.  **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While the gating mechanism is dynamic, a key function it serves is to allow for identity-like connections. A direct experimental comparison to plain networks with fixed, parameter-free identity skip connections (e.g., adding `x` to `H(x)`) would have been a valuable baseline to isolate the benefit of the adaptive gating versus simply having a shortcut path.

3.  **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The negative bias initialization for the transform gates (`bT`) appears critical for the method's success. While the authors provide a general guideline, the paper could benefit from a more systematic analysis of the training's sensitivity to this hyperparameter. It's unclear how robust the method is to different initial bias values.

### Detailed Comments and Recommendation

This paper presents a significant and well-executed piece of research. The central problem it tackles—enabling the training of very deep networks—is of paramount importance to the deep learning community. The proposed Highway Network architecture is both novel in its context and intuitively appealing.

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The direct comparison against plain networks on the optimization task is the paper's most powerful evidence. The subsequent results on CIFAR benchmarks demonstrate that this optimization advantage translates into strong generalization performance, rivaling more complex state-of-the-art models and training procedures.

The analysis of the learned gate behavior is a standout feature, providing a deeper understanding of how these networks function. It moves the contribution beyond a mere architectural trick to a more profound insight into how deep networks can manage information flow and adapt their effective complexity to the problem at hand.

The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to avenues for future work than to fundamental flaws in the current paper. A more detailed ablation or comparison to static skip connections would be interesting, but their absence does not undermine the core contribution. The work is self-contained, clearly presented, and its claims are well-supported.

**Conclusion:** This paper is of high quality, originality, and significance. It provides a foundational contribution that directly enables a new class of much deeper models to be trained effectively. The work is technically sound, empirically validated, and offers valuable insights. It is an excellent fit for NIPS and is likely to have a lasting impact on the field.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept (Oral/Spotlight Presentation).
