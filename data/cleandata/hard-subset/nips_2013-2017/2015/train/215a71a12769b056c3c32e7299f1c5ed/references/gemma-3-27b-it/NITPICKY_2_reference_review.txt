## Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper proposes “highway networks,” a novel architecture modification to facilitate training of very deep feedforward networks by introducing adaptive gating mechanisms inspired by LSTMs. The core idea – allowing information to flow across many layers without attenuation – is intuitively appealing and addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning. The authors demonstrate through empirical evaluation on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 that highway networks can be trained to significant depths (beyond 100 layers) with standard SGD, outperforming plain networks and achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art approaches.

However, despite the potentially significant contribution of this work, I find numerous deficiencies in the presentation, methodology, and analysis that lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the core idea is interesting, the paper is riddled with inconsistencies, lacks sufficient rigor, and suffers from a somewhat inflated sense of accomplishment.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The highway network architecture is a genuinely interesting idea that offers a potential solution to the vanishing/exploding gradient problem in deep networks.
*   **Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate that highway networks *can* be trained to greater depths than plain networks, which is a positive finding.
*   **Accessibility:** The concept is relatively easy to understand, and the mathematical formulation is clear.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The comparison to "plain" networks is not conducted fairly. While the authors mention parameter counts are “roughly identical” per layer, they fail to control for network width. The 50 blocks in the highway network versus 71 units in the plain network is a critical architectural difference that impacts performance. A more controlled comparison with identically sized networks (same number of parameters *overall*) is essential.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reporting:** The random search over hyperparameters is mentioned, but the specific ranges and search strategy are not detailed sufficiently. The selection of hyperparameters appears somewhat arbitrary, and there's a lack of sensitivity analysis. Reporting only “best” results without providing variance or confidence intervals is insufficient. Figure 1 shows training curves for the "best" networks, but doesn't show *how* those networks were selected from the 100 runs.
*   **Initialization Details:** While the authors propose a negative bias initialization, they state this "is best treated as a hyperparameter."  This is a significant understatement. The effectiveness of highway networks appears *highly* dependent on this initialization, yet the paper lacks a detailed discussion of its impact and optimal settings.  The range provided (-1, -2, -3) seems ad-hoc and lacks justification.
*   **Inconsistent Notation & Definitions:** The notation is occasionally confusing. For instance, the use of boldface for vectors and italicized capital letters for transformation functions is inconsistent and unnecessary. The definition of *H* in Equation (1) omits biases for clarity, but this is then not consistently applied throughout.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The authors repeatedly claim “ease of training” and superior generalization without providing compelling evidence beyond achieving slightly better results than existing methods. The claim that highway networks can "directly" train networks where a two-stage procedure was previously required feels exaggerated.
*   **Missing Baselines:** The comparison to FitNets is weak. The authors only compare against architectures *designed* for limited computational budgets. A comparison against standard CNNs of similar size and complexity is crucial.
*   **Analysis is Superficial:** The analysis section (Section 4) is largely descriptive and lacks depth. The observation that some layers become inactive is interesting, but the authors fail to provide a clear explanation or investigate the implications further. Figure 4’s lesioning experiment is poorly presented; the y-axis lacks units and the scale is unclear.
*   **Referencing Issues:** Several references are missing year information (e.g., [1], [31]). This is a minor point, but indicative of a lack of attention to detail.
*   **Formatting:** The formatting is inconsistent. Equation (5) is poorly presented and difficult to interpret.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The highway network architecture represents a novel attempt to address the difficulties of training very deep networks.
*   The empirical results demonstrate that highway networks can be trained to depths beyond what is typically achievable with plain networks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental setup lacks rigor and fairness in comparisons.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity is not adequately addressed.
*   The analysis is superficial and lacks depth.
*   The claims of “ease of training” and superior generalization are overstated.
*   The paper is marred by numerous inconsistencies and minor flaws.



In conclusion, while the idea behind highway networks is promising, the paper's methodological weaknesses, lack of rigorous analysis, and overstated claims prevent me from recommending acceptance. A significant revision addressing the issues outlined above is required before this work could be considered for publication in NIPS.
