Summary
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a deep feed-forward architecture inspired by LSTM gating. Each layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where T is a learned sigmoid “transform gate” and 1 − T is a “carry gate.” With negative bias initialization for T, information can traverse many layers with minimal attenuation (approaching identity when T ≈ 0). The authors claim this eases optimization at extreme depths while preserving generalization. Experiments show: (i) MLPs with up to 100 layers on MNIST optimize well compared to plain nets with normalized initialization; (ii) competitive CIFAR-10/100 performance in a single-stage training setting compared to FitNets that rely on teacher hints; (iii) qualitative analyses of gate biases/activities and a lesion study suggesting input-dependent routing and layer utilization.

Relation to prior work is drawn with LSTM gating, skip connections, improved initialization for deep nets, maxout/locally competitive units, and FitNets/distillation.

Strengths
- Simple, general architectural idea with clear intuition (learned gating interpolating between identity and transform), widely applicable to FC, conv, and recurrent settings.
- Practical training recipe (negative gate biases) enabling very deep nets with SGD; promising empirical evidence that optimization difficulty with depth is alleviated.
- Interesting qualitative analyses (gate activity patterns, class-conditional routing, lesion experiments) that give insight into how the model uses depth.
- Competitive CIFAR results with limited augmentation and relatively compact models; removes the need for teacher hints in analogous budgets.
- Code availability is claimed, which aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
Experimental rigor and missing details
- Many key comparisons rely on “best” runs after random search rather than means with error bars. For MNIST optimization, only best training curves are shown despite 100 random searches; distributions, medians, and variability are not reported. Claims of “orders of magnitude” improvement are on training loss; corresponding test performance is not analyzed.
- FitNet comparisons: unclear if parameter counts and FLOPs are strictly matched; maxout vs ReLU differences confound; training details (augments, weight decay, batch size, exact architectures, pooling/strides/normalization) are insufficient to fully assess fairness or reproduce results. The statement that hyperparameters were chosen on CIFAR-10 and reused for CIFAR-100 undermines optimality and comparability to tuned baselines.
- No ablation on critical design choices: C = 1 − T vs a free carry gate; per-unit vs per-layer gating; effect of T bias magnitude; effect of different activations for H; impact of gating parameterization (e.g., shared vs untied gates).
- Computational overhead of gates (params, wall-clock, memory) vs plain nets is not quantified, despite the architectural motivation being improved information flow and training speed. “Converged faster” is asserted but not measured (time-to-accuracy).
- For the MNIST depth-scaling study, widths are chosen so params per layer are “≈5000” yet the exact layer-wise counts, total parameters, and FLOPs are not reported; 50 blocks in highway vs 71 units in plain is ad hoc.
- Lesion analysis closes gates (T=0) after training, but gate saturation (sigmoid) means T never exactly 0/1; how lesioning is implemented numerically and whether re-normalization is needed is not described.

Theoretical/analytical gaps
- The Jacobian discussion is purely heuristic. There is no quantitative analysis of gradient flow under T bias initialization, nor of the trade-off between making T small (ease of gradient flow through x) and saturating the sigmoid (vanishing gradients into WT). Some formal analysis (even in linearized settings) would strengthen the claim that highways systematically improve conditioning.
- The constraint C = 1 − T is convenient but unexplored; allowing independent C could be beneficial or induce instabilities—evidence is anecdotal.

Positioning with prior work
- Connections to previous skip/shortcut connections are acknowledged, but there is no direct comparison to identity/linear skip connections without gates, which would be a strong and relevant baseline.
- Links to competitive interactions/locally competitive nets are noted but not disentangled experimentally.

Clarity and presentation
- Important architectural and training details are missing or scattered (e.g., exact conv layouts, pooling, data augmentation magnitudes, weight decay, momentum, batch size, training lengths, early stopping, data preprocessing).
- Several figures and tables are referenced but not sufficiently described in text (e.g., axes units, metrics).
- The paper uses “pilot experiments” language in several places and notes that a more complex block worked in pilots but is not reported; this weakens the completeness.

Formatting, typos, and references
- Numerous typos/grammar: “varying varying”, “as show”, “backpropogation”, inconsistent hyphenation (long shortterm), missing commas.
- Equation typography issues: T(x) = σ(WTTx + bT) suggests WTT rather than WT⊤; shapes/transpose not clarified.
- Footnotes appear as dangling numerals (1, 2, 3) in the main text.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (years/venues missing, placeholders like [cs], duplicated authors, inconsistent capitalization). Some citations are vague (“In Advances in… , , , ”).
- URLs include non-ASCII tilde; consider a short DOI or cleaner link.
- Claims like “for the first time” are overstatements; please tone down.

Quality
- Sound idea and generally supportive empirical evidence, but the experimental methodology lacks rigor and ablations; quantitative claims about speed and depth robustness are under-supported.

Clarity
- Writing is generally understandable but important reproducibility details are missing; numerous typos/formatting issues.

Originality
- The gated skip formulation is a novel and natural extension of LSTM ideas to deep feedforward nets. Relationship to existing skip connections is acknowledged but not empirically contrasted.

Significance
- Potentially high impact; the idea is simple, general, and practically useful. However, the current evidence base is thinner than desirable for a definitive NeurIPS paper.

Questions for the authors
- How do highways compare directly to identity skip connections without gates under matched budgets?
- What is the sensitivity to T bias initialization and to freeing C (not tying C = 1 − T)?
- What are the exact architectures and training hyperparameters for CIFAR models (per layer), and what is the variance across seeds?
- Can you report wall-clock time, memory, and parameter overhead vs plain nets?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea; improves trainability of very deep nets.
- Competitive results without teacher hints.
- Insightful qualitative analyses; code availability.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient experimental rigor and missing details; limited ablations.
- No direct comparison to ungated skip connections.
- Numerous presentation/formatting/reference issues.

Recommendation
- Borderline reject. The idea is strong and likely impactful, but the paper in its current form has too many methodological gaps and presentation issues for NeurIPS standards.

Reviewer confidence
- High on methodology/clarity assessment; moderate on exact empirical significance pending fuller details and stronger baselines.
