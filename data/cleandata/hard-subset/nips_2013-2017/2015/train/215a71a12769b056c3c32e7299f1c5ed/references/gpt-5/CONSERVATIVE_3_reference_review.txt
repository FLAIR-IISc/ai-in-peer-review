Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “Highway Networks,” a feedforward architecture with LSTM-inspired gating that blends a transformed pathway H(x) with a carry pathway x via a transform gate T(x) (with C=1−T). By initializing T’s bias negative, the network is initially biased to carry inputs forward, purportedly enabling gradient flow across many layers. The authors argue this alleviates depth-related optimization issues without relying on specialized activations or complex pretraining. They show: (i) optimization comparisons on MNIST with fully connected networks up to 100 layers, (ii) convolutional results on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100, including comparisons to FitNets, and (iii) analyses of gate activity and a lesion study to assess layer usage.

The work is positioned relative to initialization methods [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], alternative optimizers, local competition/maxout, skip/shortcut connections, deep supervision, and distillation (FitNets). The novelty is a gated skip mechanism in deep feedforward nets, taking inspiration from LSTM gating to create “information highways.”

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear architectural specification and practical training recipe (negative bias for transform gate).
- Solid optimization study showing plain deep nets degrade with depth while highway nets train stably up to 100 layers (with roughly matched parameters per layer in the FC setting).
- Useful analyses: gate bias/activity patterns, input-conditional routing, and lesioning to quantify layer importance.
- Code availability is a plus for reproducibility.

Concerns:
- Baselines are insufficient for the convolutional case. The comparisons to FitNets focus on teacher-student training vs single-stage training, but do not isolate the effect of gating versus simpler skip connections. In particular, there is no comparison to “plain + identity skip” without gates (a very pertinent control), or to ungated additive shortcuts. This is critical to attribute gains to the proposed gating rather than to skip paths per se.
- Parameter and compute matching in the conv experiments are not rigorously controlled. Gates add parameters and multiplications; the paper does not quantify the overhead or ensure fair capacity comparisons across methods in CIFAR experiments.
- Claims about scalability (“>1000 layers”) are based on pilot optimization observations without quantitative results or accuracy on real datasets at those depths.
- The empirical validation is limited to MNIST and CIFAR with modest augmentation and does not establish state-of-the-art or strong practical gains; results are “competitive” but not definitive. No ablation on gate bias values beyond heuristics, and no analysis of sensitivity to initialization or optimizer schedules beyond random search on MNIST.
- Speed claims (convergence faster) are based on loss curves but lack wall-clock, GPU, and memory footprint comparisons. The gating may significantly affect training dynamics and throughput; this is not measured.
- The decision to tie C=1−T is reasonable but untested; an ablation with independent carry and transform gates would inform whether coupling is necessary/helpful.
- The method’s advantage over well-initialized plain conv nets with small filters [VGG-style] and over locally competitive/maxout networks is not evaluated under matched parameter/compute with modern training recipes.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The core equations are clear. Some experimental details are under-specified for exact reproduction in the conv setting (precise architectures, parameter counts per layer, FLOPs, and training schedules per model variant). Figures referenced are informative, though the text should explicitly report parameter counts and compute for each CIFAR model.

Originality
Using LSTM-style gates to create adaptive skip pathways in deep feedforward nets is a novel and interesting architectural idea, distinct from earlier fixed skip/shortcut connections and from distillation/deep supervision. The paper draws a clear connection to gating in recurrent models and proposes a simple, widely applicable mechanism.

Significance
If robustly validated, the approach could materially improve training of very deep networks across modalities. However, the current empirical evidence is limited to small/medium-scale image benchmarks without strong baselines and controls, making it hard to assess the net benefit of gating versus simpler skips or increased capacity. The analysis sections are insightful but do not substitute for thorough comparative validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a strong ablation suite:
  - Highway vs. plain; vs. plain+identity skip (ungated residual) under matched parameters/compute.
  - C=1−T vs. independent C and T; gated vs. fixed T constants (e.g., T=0.5).
  - Sensitivity to gate bias initialization and to activation function in H.
- Report parameter counts and FLOPs for all CIFAR models; ensure fair matching to baselines.
- Provide wall-clock training times, memory usage, and throughput; include gradient norm propagation diagnostics across depth.
- Expand datasets and tasks (e.g., SVHN; a smaller-scale ImageNet experiment if feasible) to demonstrate generality and practical impact.
- Clarify hyperparameter search spaces and selection protocol to avoid inadvertent advantage to the proposed method.
- Where claiming “faster convergence,” include statistically robust comparisons over multiple seeds.

Pros
- Simple, generally applicable architectural idea with clear motivation.
- Demonstrates training stability at depth and provides useful mechanistic analyses.
- Public code and some hyperparameter search transparency.

Cons
- Missing critical baselines (ungated identity shortcuts) and parameter/compute matching in conv experiments.
- Limited datasets and modest performance improvements; no large-scale validation.
- Sparse ablations; reliance on heuristic gate bias without robustness analysis.
- Efficiency (time/memory) not quantified.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evaluation does not convincingly isolate the contribution of gating over simpler, established alternatives, nor does it demonstrate sufficient robustness and practical gains. A strengthened experimental section with the suggested ablations and fair, comprehensive baselines could significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: high.
