

# Peer Review: "Training Very Deep Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel architecture for deep feedforward neural networks that incorporates an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism to facilitate information flow across layers. The key innovation is a transform gate T(x,WT) that controls the proportion of information transformed (via H) versus carried (via identity mapping) through each layer, allowing highway networks to be trained directly with SGD even at extreme depths (100+ layers). The authors demonstrate superior training performance and generalization compared to plain networks on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, with analysis showing that highway networks learn dynamic input-dependent routing of information.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the problem of training very deep networks. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating that highway networks maintain excellent performance even at 100 layers where plain networks fail catastrophically. The analysis of transform gate behavior (Section 4) provides valuable insights into how these networks operate, particularly the dynamic routing of information based on input, which is a significant advancement over fixed skip connections.

The paper's experimental design is rigorous, with careful hyperparameter search, multiple datasets, and ablation studies (including lesioning experiments in Section 4.2 that show how highway networks utilize depth). The negative bias initialization strategy for transform gates is well-motivated and effective, providing a simple, general initialization scheme that works across different activation functions and network architectures.

## Weaknesses and Critical Concerns

**1. Insufficient comparison to ResNets:** The most significant weakness is the lack of meaningful comparison to Residual Networks (ResNets), which were published in the same year (2015) and achieved similar goals with a much simpler identity mapping. ResNets use a skip connection with a simple addition (y = H(x) + x), whereas highway networks use a more complex gating mechanism (y = H(x)·T + x·(1-T)). The paper's claim that highway networks "do not suffer from increasing depth" is undermined by the fact that ResNets (with their simpler architecture) were already achieving state-of-the-art results on ImageNet at 100+ layers. The authors cite [22, 23, 2, 24] but fail to properly contextualize their work relative to the ResNet approach, which was the dominant solution to the depth problem at the time.

**2. Theoretical justification is lacking:** While the paper provides a Jacobian analysis (Equation 5), it doesn't offer sufficient theoretical justification for why highway networks work better than plain networks. Why does the gating mechanism prevent the vanishing gradient problem? How does it differ fundamentally from skip connections? The paper relies primarily on empirical evidence without sufficient theoretical grounding.

**3. Overstated claims:** The paper claims highway networks "can be trained directly using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), in contrast to plain networks which become hard to optimize as depth increases." This claim is problematic because ResNets (which use a simpler skip connection) were already achieving this in the same time period. The paper also states that highway networks "generalize well to unseen data," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that they generalize better than ResNets, which were later shown to generalize extremely well.

**4. Implementation complexity:** The highway network architecture requires additional parameters for the transform gates, making it more complex than ResNets. The paper doesn't adequately address why this added complexity is justified given the simpler alternative of ResNets, which have been shown to train faster and achieve better results.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper properly cites relevant prior work on normalized initialization [16,17], skip connections [22,23], and knowledge distillation [25,26]. However, it fails to properly engage with the ResNet work (He et al., 2015), which was published concurrently and represented the dominant approach to training very deep networks. This omission is particularly problematic given that ResNets were already achieving state-of-the-art results on ImageNet with 100+ layers.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel architecture that successfully addresses the problem of training very deep networks, but its significance is substantially diminished by the concurrent emergence of ResNets, which achieved similar results with a simpler, more effective approach. The highway network concept is interesting and provides valuable insights into information routing in deep networks, but the paper fails to adequately contextualize its contribution relative to the ResNet approach, which was the dominant solution to the depth problem at the time.

The methodology is sound, and the experimental results are convincing for the specific claims made. However, the paper's impact would have been much greater had it properly compared to and discussed the ResNet approach. Without this comparison, the paper appears to be proposing a solution to a problem that was already effectively solved by another method.

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject**

The paper is technically sound and makes a valid contribution to the field, but its significance is limited by the concurrent development of ResNets, which achieved similar results with a simpler architecture. The paper would have been significantly stronger with a proper comparison to ResNets and a clearer articulation of how highway networks differ from and improve upon the skip connection approach. As it stands, the paper does not represent a substantial advancement over existing work in the field, particularly given the later success of ResNets.

The authors should revise the paper to include a thorough comparison with ResNets, address why highway networks might be preferable in certain scenarios (if any), and clarify the relationship between highway networks and skip connections. Without these changes, the paper's contribution appears marginal in the context of the broader literature on deep network training.
