

Summary: The paper introduces "Highway Networks," a modification to deep neural networks that incorporates an adaptive gating mechanism inspired by LSTM networks. The authors claim highway networks can be trained directly with SGD at extreme depths (100+ layers) without the optimization difficulties that plague plain networks. The paper presents experimental results on MNIST and CIFAR datasets showing highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, unlike plain networks.

Quality: The paper provides some evidence that highway networks can be trained at greater depths than plain networks on small-scale datasets. However, the evidence is insufficient to support the paper's main claims. The claim that "100-layer highway networks performed more than 2 orders of magnitude better compared to similarly-sized plain networks" lacks concrete accuracy numbers, making it impossible to assess the significance of the improvement. The paper fails to provide thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods beyond Fitnets, which are themselves limited to shallow networks. The theoretical justification for the highway architecture is superficial, merely noting that the Jacobian can approach identity when transform gates are near zero, without explaining why this leads to better optimization. The paper also doesn't address potential limitations, such as increased computational cost from the gating mechanism or sensitivity to hyperparameters.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a solid mathematical formulation of the highway network architecture. The experimental results are well-presented with helpful visualizations in Figures 2-4. However, the paper would benefit from greater clarity about the limitations of the approach and the specific conditions under which highway networks provide advantages over existing methods.

Originality: The core idea of using adaptive gating mechanisms is closely related to prior work on LSTMs and skip connections. The paper cites relevant prior work but fails to clearly distinguish how this contribution differs from existing techniques. The reference to "a similar LSTM-inspired model" [32] suggests the idea isn't as novel as claimed, and the paper doesn't adequately address this prior work. The highway network architecture represents a minor variation of established techniques rather than a significant innovation.

Significance: The paper's main claim—that highway networks enable training of extremely deep networks directly with SGD—is significant if true. However, the significance is severely limited by the paper's failure to demonstrate that highway networks outperform state-of-the-art methods on challenging benchmarks. The paper doesn't provide evidence that highway networks are better than existing approaches for practical applications. The main contribution appears to be a small architectural modification rather than a substantial advancement in deep learning.

Strengths: The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the highway network architecture. The analysis of information routing in highway networks (Section 4) is insightful and provides valuable intuition about how the architecture functions.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant advantages over plain networks at extreme depths. The experiments are limited to small-scale datasets without demonstration of practical utility on larger, more challenging benchmarks. The novelty is questionable given the similarity to prior work on LSTMs and skip connections. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the proposed approach.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The highway network architecture is a simple modification that could be useful in specific scenarios, and the paper provides some experimental evidence to support its claims.

Con-acceptance arguments: The evidence provided is insufficient to support the paper's claims, particularly regarding the "extreme" depth (100+ layers) and claimed performance improvements. The novelty is limited, and the significance is questionable given the lack of demonstration of substantial improvements over existing methods.

Overall, while the highway network architecture is a reasonable variation on existing techniques, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant advantages. The experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets without comparison to state-of-the-art methods. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison with existing approaches, including recent deep learning architectures, and from addressing the limitations of the proposed method. Given NIPS' high standards for methodological rigor and substantial contribution to the field, I recommend rejection of this paper. The claims are overstated relative to the evidence provided, and the contribution does not represent a meaningful advancement over established deep learning techniques.
