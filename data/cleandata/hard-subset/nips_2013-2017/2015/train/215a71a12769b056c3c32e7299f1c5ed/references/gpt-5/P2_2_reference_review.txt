Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “Highway Networks,” a feedforward architecture that augments each layer with input-dependent gates that blend a transformed signal H(x) with a carried signal x via y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 − T(x)). This design is explicitly inspired by LSTM gating and aims to create information highways across depth to preserve activations and gradients. The authors present: (1) controlled optimization studies on MNIST comparing thin plain nets vs. highway nets up to 100 layers; (2) convolutional highway models on MNIST; (3) CIFAR-10/100 experiments including comparisons to FitNets (teacher-student distillation) but trained in a single stage; and (4) analyses of gate behavior and layer lesioning to study data-dependent routing and layer importance.

This connects to prior work on training deep nets via better initialization, activation functions, skip-connections, and distillation. The novelty relative to existing skip-connection ideas is the learnable, input-dependent gating that can interpolate between identity and transformation, modeled after LSTMs. Compared with FitNets, the contribution is to make deep thin models trainable by architecture rather than by multi-stage supervision. The paper situates itself among normalized initialization, rectifiers, and local competition, and cites early forms of skip connections and LSTM variants.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is well-specified, with an intuitive gating mechanism T(x)=σ(Wx+b) and a practical biasing scheme (negative b) to encourage carry behavior at initialization. The connection to gradient flow (Jacobian tending to identity when T→0) provides a plausible explanation for trainability.
- Empirical support: The MNIST optimization study is carefully controlled: same per-layer parameter budget, random search over hyperparameters for both plain and highway nets, and consistent convergence advantages for highway nets as depth increases. CIFAR-10/100 results show competitive performance for limited parameter budgets, and single-stage training compared to FitNets’ staged procedure.
- Analysis: Gate activity visualizations and lesioning experiments provide insightful evidence of data-dependent routing and depth utilization varying with task complexity (MNIST vs CIFAR-100).
- Missing/limited aspects: No theoretical analysis of gradient preservation beyond intuition; limited ablations (e.g., effect of C=1−T vs learnable C, necessity of negative bias quantitatively). Important baselines like batch normalization and fixed skip connections are absent, and large-scale tests (e.g., ImageNet) are not included.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The architectural definition and training scheme are understandable; the initialization guidance is actionable. Figures explaining gate behavior are informative.
- Some details could be clearer for full reproducibility: precise convolutional architectures for CIFAR setups (filter counts per layer, kernel sizes for all layers), data augmentation settings, compute budgets (multiplications, wall-clock), and parameter counts for H and T to ensure fair capacity comparisons. The paper references code availability, which helps.

Originality
- The central idea—input-dependent gating to create depth-wise information highways in feedforward nets—is novel at the time, directly borrowing LSTM’s gating but applying it across depth rather than time and to convolutional blocks. It differs from hard-wired skips by making routing adaptive per input and per unit.
- The position relative to distillation (FitNets) is clear: the method enables single-stage training of deep thin nets under comparable budgets.

Significance
- Training very deep networks reliably is an important problem. The proposed architecture makes deep models easy to optimize without specialized supervision or heavy engineering and delivers competitive accuracy on standard benchmarks under constrained budgets.
- The analyses suggest broader utility: the gating provides dynamic computation and allows post-hoc assessment of layer importance.
- While not pushing SOTA, the work is likely to be influential as a general architectural principle and could inspire extensions (e.g., residual-like structures, different gate parameterizations, applications to larger datasets).

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural innovation with strong intuition (LSTM-inspired gating for depth).
- Solid optimization evidence: deep plain nets degrade with depth; highway nets maintain performance and converge faster.
- Practical training recipe (gate bias initialization), with guidance on depth vs bias.
- Insightful analyses (data-dependent routing, lesioning) that go beyond accuracy.
- Single-stage training of deep thin models with competitive performance relative to FitNets.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing comparisons to key contemporaneous techniques that also ease optimization (e.g., batch normalization, fixed skip/residual connections). Including a BN baseline and a plain skip (no gates) baseline would strengthen claims that gating, not just added paths, is critical.
- Limited ablations: quantify the effect of gate bias values (including b=0), separate carry gate C vs tying C=1−T, and the marginal parameter overhead’s contribution to accuracy.
- Fairness of capacity/compute: explicitly report parameter counts and FLOPs for H and T branches to ensure equal-footing comparisons; report training time.
- Scale: evaluate at least one substantially deeper convolutional model (e.g., >50–100 layers) and/or a larger dataset to validate scalability.
- Theoretical analysis: a brief derivation quantifying gradient propagation through many layers under the gating dynamics would bolster the intuition.

Questions for the authors
- How do highway nets compare to plain skip connections without gating under identical budgets and initialization?
- What is the quantitative impact of the negative gate bias on trainability and final accuracy? Does batch normalization reduce the need for such biasing?
- Have you tried learning an independent carry gate C rather than tying C=1−T? Any stability trade-offs?
- What is the runtime and memory overhead of the extra gating branch in practice?

Recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a simple, well-motivated architectural mechanism, provides convincing optimization evidence and useful analyses, and is likely to influence subsequent architectures. Strengthening with additional baselines/ablations and clearer experimental details would further solidify the contribution, but the current submission meets NIPS criteria on quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
