## NIPS Review: Training Very Deep Networks

**Summary:**

This paper introduces “Highway Networks,” a novel neural network architecture inspired by LSTMs, designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. The core idea is to introduce “highway” connections with adaptive gating mechanisms that allow information to flow more easily across many layers, mitigating the vanishing/exploding gradient problem and enabling successful training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard SGD. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach through experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, showing that highway networks can be trained directly, without the need for techniques like FitNets’ two-stage training or complex initialization schemes. The paper also provides analysis of the learned gating mechanisms, revealing interesting insights into information routing and layer importance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a long line of work addressing the challenges of training deep networks. It acknowledges the importance of depth in achieving state-of-the-art results in various machine learning tasks (e.g., ImageNet). The work directly addresses the difficulties posed by gradient propagation, which are a major bottleneck in training very deep feedforward networks. It situates itself in relation to existing strategies like better optimizers (Adam, Momentum), initialization techniques (Glorot/Xavier, He), activation functions (ReLU, Maxout), and skip connections (ResNets, DenseNets).  The key novelty lies in the LSTM-inspired gating mechanism which, unlike simpler skip connections, allows for *adaptive* information flow.  The reference to Neural History Compressors and distillation techniques highlights related approaches to transferring knowledge in deep networks. The comparison to FitNets, and the demonstration of comparable/superior performance without a two-stage training process, is a crucial contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Architecture:** The highway network architecture is a conceptually simple yet powerful innovation that effectively addresses the training difficulties associated with deep feedforward networks.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides convincing experimental evidence, demonstrating successful training of networks with hundreds of layers on benchmark datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100). The results consistently outperform or match those of previous methods, particularly in the context of direct training without complex procedures.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The analysis of the learned gating mechanisms (Figures 2 & 4) provides valuable insights into how highway networks operate internally.  The observations regarding bias reduction, selective activation, and layer importance are particularly interesting and offer avenues for future research.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the architecture is described in a straightforward manner.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and hyperparameters publicly available, which is commendable and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides an intuitive explanation for why highway networks work (facilitating gradient flow), a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the gating mechanism and its impact on the optimization landscape would be beneficial.  Specifically, a more formal justification for the bias initialization and its effect on convergence would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the need for hyperparameter tuning, particularly for the transform gate bias. While they provide guidelines, further investigation into the sensitivity of performance to these hyperparameters could provide useful practical advice.
* **Comparison to ResNets/DenseNets:** While FitNets are compared, a direct and comprehensive comparison to more recent architectures like ResNets and DenseNets, which also address the vanishing gradient problem through different mechanisms, would strengthen the contribution.  The authors mention skip connections, but don't directly address how highway networks differ and potentially improve upon these.  Are there cases where highway networks excel where ResNets might struggle?
* **Scaling to Larger Datasets:** The experiments primarily focus on relatively small datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100).  Demonstrating the scalability and performance of highway networks on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would further validate their effectiveness.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Introduces a novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks.
* Provides strong empirical evidence of the benefits of highway networks.
* Offers valuable insights into the inner workings of deep neural networks through analysis of the learned gating mechanisms.
*  Clear and well-written presentation, coupled with publicly available code.
* Addresses a significant challenge in deep learning – training very deep networks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical justification for the observed behavior.
* Lack of a direct comparison to state-of-the-art architectures like ResNets/DenseNets.
* Experiments primarily limited to smaller datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a compelling solution to a challenging problem in deep learning. The highway network architecture is a significant contribution, and the experimental results are convincing. While the paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis and comparisons to more recent architectures, the novelty, empirical performance, and insightful analysis make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I believe the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions - encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified regarding theoretical analysis and comparisons to ResNets/DenseNets)
