Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that augments each layer with input-dependent gating inspired by LSTM. A highway layer computes y = H(x) · T(x) + x · (1 − T(x)), where H is a learnable transform (e.g., affine + nonlinearity, possibly convolutional) and T is a transform gate (sigmoid). Setting the carry gate C to 1 − T forces a convex interpolation between transforming and passing-through inputs. With negative biases for T at initialization, layers are initially biased toward carrying, enabling unobstructed information flow and gradients across depth.

The work is motivated by the well-documented optimization difficulties of very deep networks and relates to a range of prior efforts: careful initialization for deep nets, e.g., Glorot and He initializations; improved optimizers; locally competitive activations and skip connections; deep supervision; layer-wise pretraining; teacher-student “hints” (FitNets). The key novelty over prior skip connections is that routing is input-dependent via learned gates, rather than hard-wired shortcuts, and the architecture borrows LSTM’s gating mechanism but applies it across depth instead of time. The authors also relate to work on Grid LSTM and discuss recent training procedures (e.g., FitNets) that require multi-stage training, aiming instead for single-stage SGD.

Empirically, the paper shows:
- On fully connected MNIST models with matched parameter budgets per layer, plain networks degrade rapidly with depth (up to 100 layers), while highway networks train well and converge faster, with >2 orders of magnitude better training loss at 100 layers.
- On MNIST, 10-layer convolutional highway networks achieve competitive accuracy with fewer parameters.
- On CIFAR-10/100, highway networks comparable to FitNet A/B train in a single stage and match or exceed test accuracy under a modest augmentation regime, with additional experiments using deeper/thinner configurations.
- Analyses visualize gate biases and activities, show input-dependent routing, and lesion layers post-training to quantify layer importance: many layers are essential on CIFAR-100 but fewer on MNIST, suggesting the network adapts effective depth to task complexity.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The proposed layer formulation is simple and well-defined. The initialization heuristic (negative gate biases) is justified by analogy to LSTM gating and validated empirically. The experiments convincingly demonstrate optimization advantages with increasing depth, using matched parameter budgets for plain vs highway networks in the MNIST fully connected setting.
- Empirical support: The optimization study uses random search over hyperparameters and shows best-case training curves per depth, supporting the claim that depth per se impedes plain nets but not highway nets. CIFAR experiments reuse a fixed LR schedule chosen on CIFAR-10 and show consistent single-stage training without teacher hints. The qualitative analyses (gate activity patterns, lesioning) are informative and align with the design intent.
- Weaknesses in evaluation: Results are on relatively small-scale datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100); there is no ImageNet study. Some comparisons use “best” from random search rather than mean ± std across seeds (though the paper provides that format where available). There is no ablation isolating the effect of the gates from simply adding parameters, nor a comparison to fixed skip connections of similar capacity. The constraint C = 1 − T is not ablated against an independent carry gate. The computational and memory overhead of gates is not quantified. The paper does not analyze gradient norms across depth to directly support “unimpeded gradient flow.”

Clarity
The core idea, equations, and training recipe are clearly presented. The paper explains how to construct fully connected and convolutional highway layers, and details initialization and optimization settings. Figures illustrating gate biases/activities and lesion studies help interpretability. Minor clarity issues include typos (e.g., “varying varying,” “backpropogation”), footnote markers appearing inline, and incomplete references. The CIFAR architectures are described at a high level (FitNet-based) with code links; including concise architecture summaries in the text would improve self-containment.

Originality
High. While skip connections and LSTM gating are known, this work proposes a specific, input-gated depth-wise routing mechanism for feedforward networks, with a simple initialization strategy enabling successful training of very deep models. The analysis of dynamic routing and layer importance further distinguishes the contribution.

Significance
Moderate to high. Enabling single-stage training of very deep networks addresses an important bottleneck. Results on CIFAR-10/100 are competitive under modest augmentation and without teacher hints. The lack of large-scale (e.g., ImageNet) evaluation and missing comparisons to fixed residual shortcuts limit immediate practical impact, but the architectural idea is likely to influence subsequent designs and analyses of depth and information flow.

Pros
- Simple, general layer formulation; easy to implement and combine with convolutional layers.
- Strong evidence that very deep models become trainable with standard SGD, with faster convergence.
- Single-stage training matches/exceeds FitNets trained with hints under comparable budgets.
- Insightful analyses of gating behavior, dynamic routing, and layer importance via lesioning.
- Public code and scripts enhance reproducibility.

Cons
- No large-scale benchmarks; significance demonstrated on MNIST/CIFAR only.
- Missing ablations: independent carry gate vs C = 1 − T; gating vs fixed skip; impact of gate bias magnitude; parameter-matched comparisons controlling for additional gate parameters.
- Lack of quantitative overhead analysis (compute/memory).
- Some related work omissions or limited discussion, e.g., batch normalization and other depth-enablers; more recent skip-connection variants could be contrasted.
- Reporting sometimes uses best-of-runs; broader statistics would strengthen claims.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: compare to identical architectures with fixed skip connections (residual summation), independent carry gate, and zero/learned gate biases to isolate mechanisms; report parameter- and FLOP-matched results.
- Quantify training/inference cost overhead due to gates and report wall-clock/time-to-accuracy.
- Include gradient norm and activation variance profiles across depth to substantiate the “information highway” effect quantitatively.
- Evaluate on a larger-scale dataset (or provide strong evidence why results generalize), even if with a smaller model.
- Compare with batch normalization and other modern training aids, and discuss complementarity.
- Provide mean ± std across multiple seeds for key results and simple tables summarizing CIFAR architectures.

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and original, with convincing evidence that gating enables training very deep networks and insightful analyses of learned routing. The main limitations are the scope of empirical validation and missing ablations against fixed skip connections and gating design choices.
