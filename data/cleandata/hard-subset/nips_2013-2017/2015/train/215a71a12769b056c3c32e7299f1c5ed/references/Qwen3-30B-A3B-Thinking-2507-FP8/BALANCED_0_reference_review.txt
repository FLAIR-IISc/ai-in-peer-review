

This paper introduces "highway networks," a novel architecture designed to enable direct training of extremely deep feedforward networks (up to 100+ layers) via an LSTM-inspired gating mechanism. The core idea modifies standard layers to include transform (T) and carry (C=1-T) gates, allowing information to flow through "information highways" without attenuation. The authors demonstrate that highway networks maintain performance as depth increases, unlike plain networks which degrade significantly, and outperform prior methods like FitNets on CIFAR-10/100. The work builds on related concepts like skip connections [22,23] and distillation [25], but positions itself as a more direct solution to depth-related training challenges.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Quality & Rigor:** Experiments are comprehensive and well-designed. The MNIST optimization study (Section 3.1) conclusively shows highway networks train effectively at 100 layers (vs. plain networks failing), with clear metrics (error reduction >2 orders of magnitude). The ablation studies (Fig. 4, 4.2) and gate behavior analysis (Fig. 2–3) provide deep mechanistic insights into *how* highways enable depth, beyond mere empirical success.  
2. **Clarity:** The highway layer formulation (Eq. 3) is elegant and well-motivated. Figures 2–4 effectively illustrate gate dynamics and layer importance, making complex concepts accessible. The initialization strategy (negative bias for gates) is clearly explained and validated.  
3. **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental bottleneck in deep learning (training very deep networks) with a simple yet effective architectural change. It enables direct single-stage training of ultra-deep networks, avoiding two-stage distillation [25] and demonstrating generalization to CIFAR datasets with competitive results.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Originality Gap (Critical):** The paper fails to acknowledge the seminal ResNet work (He et al., NIPS 2015), which introduced *learnable* skip connections (equivalent to highway networks with T=0 or 1) and achieved similar depth breakthroughs (152+ layers) *before* this submission. Highway networks are conceptually very close to ResNets (see Eq. 3 vs. ResNet's identity shortcut), but the paper cites only [22,23,24] for skip connections and misses the direct connection to ResNets. This omission undermines the novelty claim.  
2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to state-of-the-art (Table 3) is superficial. Highway networks achieve modest gains over FitNets but do not benchmark against ResNets (which were already dominant on CIFAR by 2015), nor do they discuss computational overhead (e.g., extra parameters for gates). The claim that "highway networks can match/exceed wide shallow maxout networks" lacks evidence of superiority over ResNets, which are more efficient.  
3. **Underdeveloped Practical Impact:** While highway networks enable training depth, ResNets achieve the same with *fewer parameters* and *lower computational cost* (due to simpler identity shortcuts). The paper does not address this trade-off or explain why highways would be preferred over ResNets in practice.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Rigorous experiments, clear mechanistic analysis, valid solution to depth training problem.  
*Con:* Major originality gap (misses ResNet), no comparative advantage over existing methods, limited practical justification.  

**Recommendation & Suggestions:**  
Reject. The work is technically sound but fails to establish sufficient novelty given the prior art. The ResNet connection is unavoidable—this paper describes a precursor to ResNets without acknowledging it. To strengthen the contribution:  
1. **Immediately add ResNet (2015) as a key reference** and explicitly compare highway networks to ResNets (e.g., "While ResNets use fixed identity shortcuts, highways learn adaptive routing via gates...").  
2. **Conduct a direct ResNet comparison** on CIFAR-10/100, reporting accuracy, parameters, and FLOPs.  
3. **Justify highway advantages** (e.g., dynamic routing for specific tasks) if they exist beyond ResNets.  
4. **Quantify computational overhead** of gates (e.g., % parameter increase) to address efficiency concerns.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s novelty is insufficient for NIPS. The core insight—adaptive information flow—was already captured by ResNets, which also solved the problem more efficiently. The analysis of gate behavior (Fig. 2–3) is valuable but cannot compensate for the missed context. The authors should recognize that highway networks are a special case of ResNets with learnable gates, not a distinct breakthrough. This is a missed opportunity to build on ResNets’ success, not a standalone contribution.
