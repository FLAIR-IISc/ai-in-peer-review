

# Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper proposes "highway networks" as an architectural modification to deep neural networks, introducing a gating mechanism inspired by LSTMs to facilitate information flow across layers. The authors claim that highway networks can be directly trained using SGD without the performance degradation seen in plain networks as depth increases.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work
The paper builds on prior work in deep learning (e.g., [16, 17, 20, 25, 26]) that has explored initialization schemes, activation functions, and skip connections to improve training of deep networks. The key novelty is the highway network architecture with transform and carry gates, which the authors claim enables direct training of extremely deep networks. However, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from similar works like residual networks (He et al., 2016), which were published around the same time.

## Strengths
The paper presents a clear architecture that addresses a well-known challenge in deep learning. The experimental results on MNIST and CIFAR datasets demonstrate that highway networks can be trained more effectively than plain networks at extreme depths. The analysis of transform gate behavior provides interesting insights into information routing in deep networks.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Flaws
1. **Critical Notation Inconsistency**: Equation (5) claims "dy/dx = { I, if T(x,WT) = 0, H'(x,WH), if T(x,WT) = 1" but immediately states "σ(x) ∈ (0, 1),∀x ∈ R, so the conditions in Equation 4 can never be met exactly." This is a contradiction - the Jacobian derivation assumes T can be exactly 0 or 1, but the activation function prevents this.

2. **Misrepresentation of Baseline Results**: The paper states "top-5 image classification accuracy on the 1000-class ImageNet dataset has increased from ∼84% [1] to ∼95% [2, 3]". However, [1] (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) reported 83.6% top-5 error (16.4% top-5 accuracy), not 84% accuracy. The 95% claim likely refers to top-1 accuracy, creating a confusing mix of metrics.

3. **Lack of Experimental Detail**: The claim that "the 100-layer highway network performed more than 2 orders of magnitude better compared to a similarly-sized plain network" lacks supporting numerical evidence. The paper doesn't provide specific error rates or test accuracies to substantiate this.

4. **Inconsistent Parameter Counting**: The paper states "each layer has 50 blocks for highway networks and 71 units for plain networks, yielding roughly identical numbers of parameters (≈5000) per layer" but fails to explain how these parameter counts were derived.

5. **Figure and Caption Issues**: The description "The first three columns show obtained via random search over hyperparameters to minimize the best training set error achieved using each configuration" appears to be a misplaced text fragment in the caption of Figure 2, making the figure description confusing.

### Clarity and Presentation Issues
1. **Confusing Gate Notation**: The paper defines T as the transform gate and C as the carry gate, then sets C = 1-T. This creates confusion about whether T or 1-T should be interpreted as the carry gate.

2. **Vague Claims**: The paper states "it is interesting to note that contrary to our expectations most biases decreased further during training" without explaining what the expectations were or why the observation was surprising.

3. **Inadequate Comparison to Residual Networks**: The paper mentions "a similar LSTM-inspired model was also proposed [32]" but fails to explain how this differs from their work or discuss the relationship to residual networks (which were published around the same time).

4. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation (e.g., "x · (1− T (x,WT))" in Equation 3 but "x · (1− T (x,WT))" in the text), and contains a typo in "Backpropogation" (should be "Backpropagation").

## Overall Assessment
While the highway network concept is interesting and the paper demonstrates some promising results, the technical inconsistencies, lack of experimental detail, and failure to adequately distinguish the work from concurrent approaches significantly undermine the paper's quality. The most concerning issues are the fundamental contradiction in the Jacobian derivation and the misrepresentation of baseline results, which suggest the authors may not have fully understood their own architecture.

The paper would benefit from a complete revision to address these technical inconsistencies, provide sufficient experimental detail, and properly contextualize the work against related approaches. As written, the paper contains multiple minor but substantial flaws that prevent it from meeting the quality standards expected for NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject. While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some interesting insights, the technical inconsistencies and lack of sufficient experimental detail prevent it from being considered a strong contribution to the field. The paper would benefit from a major revision to address the technical flaws before being reconsidered for publication.
