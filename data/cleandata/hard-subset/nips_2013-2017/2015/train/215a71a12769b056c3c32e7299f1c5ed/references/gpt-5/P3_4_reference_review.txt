Summary
The paper proposes Highway Networks, a feed-forward architecture that introduces input-dependent gating (inspired by LSTMs) between successive layers to facilitate information and gradient flow across very deep stacks. Each highway layer computes y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where H is a standard transform (e.g., affine+nonlinearity or convolution) and T is a sigmoid “transform gate” (the “carry gate” is tied as 1−T). A simple initialization (negative biases for T) biases early training toward carrying, enabling near-identity paths (“information highways”) across many layers. The authors demonstrate substantially improved trainability compared to plain networks as depth increases (up to 100 fully-connected layers on MNIST), competitive accuracy on MNIST with far fewer parameters, and single-stage training of thin, deep convolutional nets on CIFAR-10/100 that match or exceed FitNet baselines without teacher hints. They analyze gate behaviors, data-dependent routing, and layer importance via lesion studies.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely connected to: (1) LSTM gating for temporal credit assignment; (2) skip/shortcut connections in feed-forward networks and deep supervision; (3) initialization strategies for deep nets; and (4) teacher–student (FitNets) approaches. The novelty lies in using input-dependent multiplicative gates along depth to form dynamic skip paths in purely feed-forward architectures. The paper positions itself against plain deep nets with variance-preserving initialization and locally competitive activations, and as an alternative to hint-based training used in FitNets. A comparison to batch normalization (BN) and identity skip connections is notably absent; these are highly relevant for training deep nets.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: the gating formulation is straightforward to implement and broadly applicable (fully-connected and convolutional layers).
- Strong optimization findings: training curves show substantial robustness to depth and faster convergence compared to plain networks of matched capacity.
- Insightful analyses: visualization of gate biases/activities, evidence of input-dependent routing, and lesion studies that quantify layer contribution.
- Practical guidance: simple, effective gate-bias initialization; reuse across datasets; consistent training protocol.
- Reproducibility: code and scripts reported as available; hyperparameter search methodology described.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Missing baselines: No comparison to batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) or identity skip connections (residual learning), both directly relevant to mitigating vanishing gradients and enabling very deep training. Given BN’s documented impact, this omission weakens the case that gating is necessary for depth scalability.
- Architectural ablations are limited: The choice C=1−T constrains the layer to a convex interpolation between H(x) and x, unlike residual connections that allow simultaneous identity and transform contributions. There is no ablation showing whether untied carry gates (learned C) help, or whether the constraint limits expressivity.
- Theoretical analysis is shallow: The argument for gradient flow relies on extreme cases T≈0 or 1; there is no analysis of the full Jacobian that includes derivatives of T(x) or discussion of conditions under which gradients are preserved (e.g., bounds on singular values of dy/dx along depth).
- Limited scope of empirical evaluations: While the paper asserts training “>1000 layers” in pilots, reported experiments max at 100 layers on MNIST and ~32 conv layers on CIFAR. Results are not SOTA and use modest augmentation; although SOTA is not the stated aim, broader validation (depth vs. width trade-offs, parameter/FLOP efficiency, wall-clock cost) would strengthen significance.
- Fairness of comparisons: Plain baselines use normalized initialization but lack other commonly used stabilizers (BN, residual/skip, careful LR schedules). It is unclear whether highway gains persist when strong baselines are used.
- Reporting details: Some tables/figures are referenced but not fully described here; clearer tabulation of parameter counts, FLOPs, and accuracy (mean±std over multiple runs) for all comparisons would help.

Quality
Technically sound in its empirical methodology and analyses; experiments support claims about ease of optimization and reasonable generalization. However, the lack of stronger baselines and limited ablations leave open how much of the advantage stems from gating per se versus missing techniques in the baseline.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and motivation. Minor issues: some typos and missing derivations for the general Jacobian; more detail on block structure, gate dimensionality (per-unit vs per-channel), and training hyperparameters would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Introducing input-dependent gates along depth in feed-forward networks is a novel and timely idea that bridges LSTM-style gating with deep feed-forward architectures. Nevertheless, the idea is closely related to shortcut/skip connections; clarifying distinctions (dynamic, multiplicative, convex interpolation) versus static additive residual paths is useful.

Significance
High potential impact: making very deep nets easy to train is central to progress. The proposed mechanism is general and could influence architectural design. That said, significance would be stronger with comparisons to BN/identity residuals and more extensive evaluations on deeper convnets and larger-scale datasets.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines with batch normalization and with identity skip connections (y = x + H(x)), matched in depth/width/params, to contextualize highway advantages in trainability and accuracy.
- Provide ablations: (i) C untied vs C=1−T; (ii) effect of gate bias values and schedules; (iii) per-unit vs per-channel gates; (iv) replace sigmoid with other gates (hard-sigmoid, GLU-like) and report stability/accuracy.
- Report parameter and FLOP overhead due to gating, and wall-clock training/inference costs.
- Extend depth in conv experiments (e.g., 50–100 layers on CIFAR) and explore stronger but standard augmentations to test generality without aiming for SOTA.
- Elaborate on the full Jacobian and gradient propagation when T is in (0,1), including the role of ∂T/∂x, to better justify the mechanism theoretically.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea with strong empirical evidence of improved trainability.
- Novel application of gating for depth-wise information routing.
- Insightful diagnostics and analyses of learned behavior.
- Likely to inspire subsequent architectures and practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing critical baselines (BN, identity skips) undermines the central claim relative to the state of the art.
- Limited ablations and theoretical depth make it hard to attribute gains specifically to highway gating.
- Experimental scope modest in depth and datasets for convolutional settings.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and potentially influential contribution that addresses an important problem with a simple, general mechanism and thoughtful analysis. I lean toward acceptance contingent on strengthening comparisons to contemporary techniques (especially BN and skip/residual connections) and adding key ablations.
