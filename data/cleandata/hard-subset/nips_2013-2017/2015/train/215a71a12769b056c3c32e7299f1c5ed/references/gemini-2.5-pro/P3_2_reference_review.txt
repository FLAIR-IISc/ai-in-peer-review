**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the well-known challenge of training very deep feedforward neural networks. The authors posit that the difficulty arises from degraded information and gradient propagation through many stacked non-linear layers. Drawing inspiration from the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, they propose a novel architecture called "Highway Networks." The core innovation is the introduction of adaptive gating units at each layer that regulate the flow of information. Each layer can dynamically choose between transforming its input (like a conventional layer) and carrying it through unchanged. This creates unimpeded "information highways," allowing gradients to flow directly across many layers. The authors demonstrate empirically that this architecture allows for the stable training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard SGD, a feat not possible with equivalent "plain" networks. They provide strong experimental evidence on MNIST and CIFAR datasets, showing that not only are these networks trainable, but they also generalize well and can learn to use depth effectively, as demonstrated through insightful analysis and lesion studies.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposed mechanism is both simple and powerful. The core equation, `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x))`, provides an elegant, learnable interpolation between a standard transformation and an identity mapping. The connection to LSTMs provides a strong conceptual foundation, successfully translating a key idea from the recurrent domain to the feedforward domain to solve a related problem (long-range dependency vs. depth).

2.  **Compelling Empirical Evidence:** The optimization experiments in Section 3.1 are a cornerstone of the paper. Figure 1 provides a stark and convincing visualization of the method's effectiveness. While plain networks degrade catastrophically with increasing depth, highway networks maintain stable and efficient training up to 100 layers. This is a direct and powerful demonstration that the core hypothesis is correct and the proposed solution works as intended.

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Insights:** The paper goes far beyond simply reporting accuracy metrics. Section 4 provides a deep dive into the learned behavior of the network, which is a major strength. The visualization of the transform gate activities and the lesion studies (Figure 4) are particularly insightful. The finding that the network can learn to effectively "ignore" unnecessary layers for simpler tasks (MNIST) while utilizing its full depth for more complex ones (CIFAR-100) is a significant and novel result. It suggests that highway networks offer a mechanism for adaptive depth, which is a highly desirable property.

4.  **Practicality and Broad Applicability:** The method is straightforward to implement and requires only a minor modification to existing architectures. The proposed initialization strategy (negative bias for the transform gates) is a simple and effective heuristic that enables successful training out-of-the-box. The approach is general and not tied to a specific activation function or layer type (e.g., fully-connected or convolutional), making it broadly applicable.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **Relationship to Concurrent Work on Residual Connections:** The most significant point of discussion is the relationship between this work and the concurrently developed Residual Networks (ResNets) [He et al., 2015]. ResNets employ a parameter-free identity shortcut (`y = H(x) + x`), which is a special case of the highway formulation where the transform and carry gates are fixed to 1. While the authors cite a related work [32], a more direct comparison and discussion of the trade-offs would be invaluable. Is the added complexity of the learnable gates in Highway Networks necessary? Do they offer advantages (like the observed input-dependent routing) that justify the extra parameters and computation over a fixed identity shortcut? This is a crucial point for contextualizing the paper's contribution.

2.  **Sensitivity to Gate Bias Initialization:** The paper relies on initializing the transform gate bias `bT` to a negative value. The authors suggest this is a hyperparameter and provide a rough guideline. However, the robustness of the training process to this initialization is not fully explored. How does performance change as `bT` approaches 0? Does training fail if `bT` is initialized to be positive? A more systematic ablation study on this parameter would strengthen the claims of training stability.

3.  **Parameter Overhead:** The transform gates `T(x) = σ(W_T x + b_T)` introduce a new set of parameters (`W_T`, `b_T`) at each layer. While the MNIST experiments are controlled for parameter count, for convolutional layers this overhead could be non-trivial. A brief analysis of the parameter and computational overhead compared to plain networks and, by extension, residual networks would be useful.

**Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous experiments and thoughtful analysis. The experiments are well-designed, particularly the direct comparison between plain and highway networks at varying depths.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core idea is introduced clearly with clean notation. The motivation is strong, and the experiments are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The figures effectively communicate the key results.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using LSTM-style gating to facilitate training of very deep feedforward networks is highly original. While it builds on known concepts (LSTM, skip connections), the specific formulation and its successful application to this problem represent a novel and significant contribution. The primary point of contention regarding originality is the concurrent development of ResNets, but the learned, data-dependent gating mechanism of Highway Networks is a distinct and interesting approach in its own right.
*   **Significance:** This paper is highly significant. Along with the concurrent work on ResNets, it fundamentally changed the way the community approached the training of deep networks. It provided one of the first and most effective solutions to the deep-network optimization problem, paving the way for the development of architectures with hundreds or even thousands of layers. The ideas of unimpeded information flow and adaptive computation paths have had a lasting impact on the field. The analytical techniques for probing layer importance are also a valuable contribution.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, simple, and effective method for training very deep neural networks. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by strong empirical evidence and insightful analysis. It represents a significant step forward in our ability to harness the power of network depth. Despite the existence of similar concurrent work, the unique properties and contributions of Highway Networks are substantial. This paper is a clear and strong candidate for acceptance.
