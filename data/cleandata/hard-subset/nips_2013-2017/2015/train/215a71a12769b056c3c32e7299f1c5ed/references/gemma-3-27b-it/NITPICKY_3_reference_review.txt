## Review of "Training Very Deep Networks"

This paper investigates the challenges of training very deep feedforward networks and proposes “highway networks” inspired by LSTM architectures to facilitate information flow. The core idea – introducing adaptive gating mechanisms allowing for direct connections across multiple layers – is presented as a means to overcome vanishing/exploding gradient issues that plague training deep plain networks. The authors demonstrate that highway networks can be trained with SGD to significantly greater depths than plain networks, achieving competitive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The highway network architecture is a conceptually interesting and potentially impactful contribution. The analogy to LSTMs is well-motivated, and the resulting gating mechanism offers a compelling solution to the depth problem.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper presents a series of experiments demonstrating the efficacy of highway networks. The results on MNIST, particularly the comparison with plain networks at increasing depth (Figure 1), are quite strong and clearly support the main claim. Results on CIFAR-10/100 are also promising.
* **Ablation Studies/Analysis:** The analysis presented in sections 4.1 & 4.2, exploring layer importance and information routing, is commendable and provides some insight into the network’s internal workings. These analyses help to justify the design choices and demonstrate that the additional parameters are indeed utilized.
* **Code Availability:** Publicly releasing the code and hyperparameters is an excellent practice and greatly facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigor in Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the authors mention random search, the details are sparse. The statement that hyperparameters were selected “once based on validation set performance on CIFAR-10” and then *kept fixed* for all experiments feels overly simplistic. This raises concerns about whether the reported results are truly representative of the method's potential, or merely a consequence of lucky hyperparameter choices. The dependence on a single validation set for all datasets is problematic.
* **Insufficient Comparison to Established Techniques:** The comparison to FitNets is reasonable, but the paper lacks a more thorough comparison to other contemporary deep learning architectures and training techniques. Specifically, the influence of Batch Normalization (introduced around the same time) is not discussed or tested in combination with highway networks. This omission is significant, as Batch Normalization is often crucial for training deep networks.
* **Ambiguous Notation & Missing Details:** Several aspects of the notation are unclear. For example, the use of italicized capital letters for transformation functions is unusual and not consistently applied. The explanation of how dimensionality changes are handled (using subsampling/zero-padding or plain layers) lacks detail. It's not fully clear when each method is employed.
* **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from several minor inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface) and grammatical errors. While not major, these detract from the overall polish. Figure 2 is visually cluttered and lacks clear labels for axes.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim that the highway network "does not suffer from increasing depth" is somewhat hyperbolic. While it performs better than plain networks, there's likely a point where even highway networks would encounter optimization difficulties. This should be acknowledged.
* **Unclear Motivation for Simplification:** The authors state they only report results for a “much simplified form” of the LSTM-inspired block design. The justification for this simplification is weak; it would be more convincing to see results with the more complex, originally proposed architecture.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

* Section 2.1: “Another alternative is to use a plain layer (without highways) to change dimensionality…” – When *is* this alternative used?  The text doesn't provide clear guidance.
* Section 3.2: The statement “test set performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods” is vague. Specific numbers and comparisons to specific methods are needed.
* Section 4.1: The reasoning behind the decreasing transform gate biases is intriguing, but further investigation is warranted.
* Throughout: The authors often use vague language ("easily," "significant," etc.). Quantifying these statements would improve the paper’s scientific rigor.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The highway network architecture represents a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of deep learning.
* The empirical results, particularly on MNIST, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The included analysis provides some insight into the network’s internal workings.
* The publicly available code promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of rigorous hyperparameter tuning raises concerns about the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
* The omission of a comparison to Batch Normalization is a significant weakness.
* Several minor inconsistencies and ambiguities detract from the paper’s clarity and polish.
* The overstated claims and lack of detailed explanation reduce the overall scientific rigor.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially significant contribution, the numerous minor flaws and lack of thoroughness prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The core idea is strong, and the empirical results are promising, but the paper needs substantial revisions to address the concerns outlined above. Specifically, a more rigorous hyperparameter search, a comparison to Batch Normalization, clarification of the notation and missing details, and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the approach are necessary.  I lean towards **rejection**, but would encourage the authors to revise and resubmit.
