Summary
This paper proposes Highway Networks, a feedforward architecture that augments each layer with LSTM-inspired gates: a transform gate T(x) and a carry gate 1−T(x), allowing the layer output to interpolate between H(x) and identity. The key practical idea is to initialize the transform gates with negative biases so that early in training information can flow along “information highways” with minimal attenuation, mitigating optimization difficulties as depth increases. The authors show:
- Optimization experiments on thin fully-connected nets on MNIST: plain nets degrade with depth, whereas highway nets train well up to 100 layers with SGD.
- Convolutional highway nets on MNIST achieve competitive accuracy with relatively few parameters.
- On CIFAR-10/100, highway nets trained in a single stage achieve accuracy comparable to or better than FitNets trained with hint-based distillation, at similar parameter/compute budgets; deeper/thinner highway variants also train.
- Analysis: gate statistics, data-dependent routing, and a layer-lesion study suggesting that used depth scales with task complexity.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against a substantial literature on improving deep net trainability: careful initialization [Glorot & Bengio; He et al.], alternative optimizers, local competition/maxout, skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and teacher-student training (FitNets). The novelty here is a learnable, multiplicative gating mechanism in feedforward nets (inspired by LSTM) that subsumes a skip connection and can dynamically vary routing. This is related to earlier shortcut/skip ideas but differs by learning the gating per unit and per input, rather than hard-wiring identity shortcuts. The paper cites most relevant strands; however, comparisons to more recent and strong baselines (e.g., batch normalization and fixed identity shortcuts without gates) are missing.

Quality
- Positives: The architectural description is precise, the gating parameterization is clear, and the initialization heuristic is well-motivated. The optimization study is carefully controlled for parameter count per layer and shows substantial depth-related differences. Public code and multiple runs are appreciated. The analysis (gate activity, class-dependent routing, lesioning) is insightful.
- Concerns:
  1) Baselines: The strongest contemporary baselines are absent. In particular, batch normalization and fixed identity shortcut architectures (without gates) are not evaluated. It is unclear how much of the gain stems from having any skip/identity pathway versus learned gates. An ablation where T is fixed (e.g., T=const or learned but input-independent) or replaced by a simple residual connection with comparable parameter/FLOP budget would be essential.
  2) Breadth of evaluation: Claims of “extremely deep” training are supported primarily on MNIST FCNs and CIFAR. There is no large-scale evaluation (e.g., ImageNet) and only limited data augmentation. The improvements over FitNets on CIFAR appear modest; SOTA is not targeted.
  3) Hyperparameter control: While parameter counts per layer are matched in MNIST FCN studies, the CIFAR comparisons would benefit from exact parameter/FLOP matching and reporting training budgets. The gate bias initialization is treated as a hyperparameter; sensitivity analyses beyond a few suggested values are limited.
  4) Optimization claims: The statement that SGD “did not stall for networks with more than 1000 layers” is not substantiated with quantitative results or training curves beyond pilot remarks.
  5) Theoretical support: Aside from qualitative Jacobian observations, there is no analysis of gradient flow (e.g., norms across depth) to mechanistically support the optimization advantage.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Equations (1–5) are easy to follow. The figures (as described) seem informative. Some minor issues: typos (“varying varying”, “backpropogation”), and several architectural/training details are deferred to code/scripts; for reproducibility, more in-paper detail on data preprocessing, exact layer configurations, learning-rate schedules, and parameter/FLOP counts would help.

Originality
The combination of LSTM-like gating with feedforward depth is novel and interesting, beyond prior fixed skip connections and teacher-student schemes. The idea of initializing gates toward carry is borrowed from LSTM practice but appropriately adapted. That said, the core concept is a relatively straightforward synthesis of existing components.

Significance
If validated against strong baselines, this could be a useful architectural tool for training very deep nets and might influence future designs. As it stands, the significance is promising but not decisively demonstrated: results are on relatively small benchmarks and do not unequivocally separate the contribution of learnable gates from generic shortcutting.

Pros
- Simple, general architectural modification with clear motivation.
- Solid optimization study showing depth scaling where plain nets fail.
- Single-stage training of thin/deep CIFAR models comparable to two-stage FitNets.
- Insightful analysis of gating behavior and lesioning.

Cons
- Missing comparisons to batch norm and fixed residual/skip architectures; no ablation to isolate the effect of gates vs identity shortcuts.
- Limited scale and scope of experiments; modest gains over strong baselines.
- Insufficient sensitivity analyses (gate bias, activation choice, width/depth trade-offs) and quantitative evidence for the >1000-layer claim.
- Some reproducibility details deferred; minor typos.

Requests for revision
- Add ablations: (a) residual/identity skip without gates but parameter/FLOP-matched, (b) input-independent gates, (c) remove carry path. Report training curves and final accuracy.
- Compare against batch-normalized deep nets on CIFAR-10/100 with standard augmentations.
- Provide gradient norm statistics across depth and training to support the optimization claims.
- Expand sensitivity analysis for gate bias initialization and activation functions; report robustness.
- Clarify parameter and FLOP counts and training budgets for all models; include exact architectures in the paper.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is elegant and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evidence does not sufficiently disentangle the proposed mechanism from established skip/normalization techniques, nor does it convincingly demonstrate superiority or parity on stronger benchmarks. With the requested ablations and stronger baselines, I would lean toward acceptance.

Confidence
High.
