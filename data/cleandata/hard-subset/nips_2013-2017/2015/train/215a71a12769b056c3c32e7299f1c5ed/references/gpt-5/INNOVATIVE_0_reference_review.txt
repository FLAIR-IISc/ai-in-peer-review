Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Highway Networks: deep feedforward architectures equipped with LSTM-inspired, elementwise transform and carry gates that adaptively combine a nonlinear transform H(x) with the (optionally re-sized) input via y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)). Initializing the transform gate with a negative bias encourages early “carry” behavior and creates pathways for information and gradients to traverse many layers without attenuation. The authors demonstrate that very deep networks (tens to hundreds of layers, and in pilot trials >1000) can be trained directly with SGD, in contrast to plain nets where optimization degrades with depth. They validate on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with thin, deep convolutional and fully connected architectures, compare to FitNets (teacher-student training), and analyze gate dynamics and layer importance via lesion experiments.

This work is situated among: (a) initialization schemes that stabilize activations/gradients (Glorot & Bengio; He et al.), (b) architectures with local competition and maxout that ease optimization, (c) skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and (d) knowledge distillation/hint-based training (FitNets). Its key distinction from earlier skip connections is the learned, input-dependent gating that enables dynamic routing and multiplicative credit assignment, closely analogous to LSTM gating in time, but applied across depth. Unlike FitNets, it trains thin, deep nets in a single stage.

Strengths
- Novel architectural concept: LSTM-like gates across depth to form “information highways,” offering a principled way to mitigate vanishing/exploding dynamics in very deep feedforward nets.
- Clear, simple training recipe: a negative bias for T suffices across different H choices; works with standard SGD+momentum.
- Strong optimization evidence: systematic MNIST experiments show plain nets degrade with depth while highway nets remain trainable and converge faster; results include random search over hyperparameters and fair parameter matching.
- Practical performance: competitive or better accuracy than FitNets on CIFAR-10 under similar parameter/compute budgets, without teacher hints; sensible CIFAR-100 results using a uniform hyperparameter setting.
- Insightful analysis: visualizations of gate biases/activities, class-dependent routing, and layer lesioning revealing which depths are used by tasks of different complexity. This is rare and valuable.
- Reproducibility: code and scripts released; multiple runs with mean±std reported.

Weaknesses
- Scope of empirical evaluation: primarily MNIST and CIFAR; no larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability and wall-clock efficiency at modern scales.
- Comparisons: while FitNets are addressed, the paper does not compare against simpler identity skip connections without gating (fixed residual shortcuts), which would help isolate the incremental benefit of learned gates vs. fixed shortcuts.
- Ablations: limited exploration of design choices: C(x)=1−T(x) vs. learning C independently; per-unit vs. per-channel vs. per-layer gating; effect of removing the negative bias; sensitivity to the magnitude of bT beyond a few suggested values.
- Overhead and latency: no quantification of parameter/compute overhead of gating; an analysis of throughput and memory relative to plain nets would inform practical adoption.
- Generality beyond vision: claims about applicability to recurrent or other complex transforms are not empirically supported here.

Quality
The paper is technically sound, and the claims about ease of optimization with depth are convincingly supported on MNIST and CIFAR with careful controls and multiple runs. The analysis sections add credibility and understanding. Some missing ablations and broader comparisons prevent a fully definitive empirical picture, but the core contribution is well supported.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The highway block is clearly defined, the training procedure is described, and figures (as referenced) illustrate key behaviors. Minor issues: some typos, and more explicit architecture/training hyperparameters in the main text (e.g., learning-rate schedules, weight decay, exact random search ranges) would further aid reproducibility. A concise compute/param table per model would help.

Originality
High. Applying LSTM-style gating across depth to create adaptive, input-dependent skip pathways is a bold and fresh architectural idea for feedforward nets. The conceptual link “depth as time + gating” is elegant and differentiates the work from prior fixed skip connections or teacher distillation.

Significance
High. Enabling direct training of very deep networks without elaborate pretraining or distillation is impactful. The approach is simple, broadly applicable, and the analyses offer insights into depth utilization and data-dependent routing. Even if subsequent architectures refine or simplify the mechanism, this work opens a promising direction that others are likely to build on.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Compare to fixed identity shortcuts (no gates) with matched parameter budgets: does gating materially improve optimization/generalization beyond identity residual paths?
- Explore learning C(x) independently from T(x) and report the trade-offs. Does C=1−T suffice across tasks?
- Quantify compute/parameter overhead and training speed relative to plain nets and identity-skip baselines.
- Provide sensitivity plots for bT (including zero/positive), and report gate saturation statistics over training.
- Test per-channel or per-layer gating to reduce parameter cost and assess whether fine-grained per-unit gating is necessary.
- Include an additional dataset (e.g., SVHN) or a deeper conv setting on CIFAR to demonstrate scalability in convolutional regimes.

Pros
- Innovative, principled architecture addressing a central challenge (training very deep nets).
- Strong evidence of improved optimization with depth and competitive accuracy under constrained budgets.
- Simple, actionable training heuristic; code released.
- Insightful analyses (routing, lesioning) that enhance understanding.

Cons
- Limited dataset scope; no large-scale demonstration.
- Missing comparisons to identity skip connections and more systematic ablations.
- Overhead and efficiency not quantified.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new architectural concept with compelling empirical support and analysis. While some experiments could be broadened, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.

Confidence
High.
