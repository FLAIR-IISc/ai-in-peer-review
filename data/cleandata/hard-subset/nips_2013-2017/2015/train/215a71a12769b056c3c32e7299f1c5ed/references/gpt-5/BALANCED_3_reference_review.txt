Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces highway networks: deep feedforward architectures in which each layer outputs y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1−T(x)), where H is a standard transformation (e.g., affine + nonlinearity or convolution), and T is a data-dependent “transform gate” (a sigmoid of an affine function). Setting C = 1 − T yields a “carry” pathway that allows information to flow unimpeded across many layers when T is near zero. The authors motivate the design by analogy to LSTM gating, which enables long-range credit assignment in time; here, gating aids gradient and activation flow in depth. A simple but important training heuristic is to initialize gate biases negatively so that layers initially prefer carrying over transforming. The core empirical claim is that very deep networks (50–100 layers and beyond) become easy to optimize with plain SGD once equipped with such gates, without sacrificing generalization.

The work is well-situated in the context of optimization for deep nets (e.g., Glorot & Bengio; Sutskever et al.; Martens & Sutskever), improved initializations (He et al.; Sussillo & Abbott; Saxe et al.), activation designs and competitive mechanisms (maxout; local competition), skip/shortcut connections and deep supervision, and teacher–student “hint” training (FitNets). The proposed architecture is closely related in spirit to LSTM gating and to prior uses of skip connections, but here the skip strength is input-dependent and multiplicative, yielding an identity-mapping Jacobian when gates are closed, which helps avoid vanishing gradients in depth. The paper also notes contemporaneous LSTM-inspired feedforward variants.

Quality:
- Methodology: The architectural formulation is sound and clearly motivated; the Jacobian analysis explains why gradients can propagate when T is small. The negative bias initialization is simple, plausible, and empirically effective.
- Experiments: The optimization study on MNIST (up to 100 layers) fairly contrasts plain vs. highway nets under comparable per-layer parameter counts, with random hyperparameter search. Convergence speed and final training loss convincingly favor highway nets. Generalization is tested on MNIST and CIFAR-10/100 with thin, deep convolutional highway networks, showing competitive or improved accuracy relative to FitNets without multi-stage distillation. Analyses of gate statistics, data-dependent routing, and layer “lesioning” are insightful and strengthen the understanding of what the network learns.
- Limitations: The experimental scope is limited to relatively small-scale vision benchmarks; there is no ImageNet or large-scale speech/NLP study. There is no ablation comparing against fixed skip/residual connections of similar depth and width, which would more directly isolate the benefit of learned gates vs. simple additive identity shortcuts. The dependence on gate-bias initialization is acknowledged, but a systematic sensitivity analysis is missing. Compute and parameter overhead from gates are not quantified vs. baselines.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, with equations that make the mechanism transparent and useful figures that illustrate training dynamics and routing. Some notational choices (e.g., “blocks” vs. “units”) could be tightened, and architectural details (exact widths and receptive fields for each CIFAR model) could be tabulated for reproducibility. Training schedules are described succinctly, and code availability is a plus.

Originality:
The core contribution—gated, input-dependent skip connections in very deep feedforward networks—is novel relative to prior fixed shortcut connections and is a natural and elegant transfer of LSTM principles to depth. The particular C = 1 − T coupling simplifies the design and may aid stability. The lesioning and routing analyses are also original and informative.

Significance:
Enabling direct SGD training of 50–100+ layer networks without complex pretraining or teacher hints is important. Even though the empirical results are not pushing state of the art with heavy augmentation, the architectural idea is likely to influence practice and spur further work on deep architectures. The potential impact extends beyond vision to recurrent and convolutional models wherever depth hinders optimization.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated architecture with simple training heuristic (negative gate bias).
- Strong evidence that very deep nets become easy to optimize; faster convergence and much lower training loss than plain nets.
- Competitive CIFAR-10/100 results in thin/deep regimes, outperforming FitNets without distillation.
- Insightful analyses: gate activity, class-conditional routing, and layer lesioning.
- Code and scripts available.

Cons:
- No direct comparison to non-gated residual/shortcut baselines of equal depth/width to isolate gates’ benefits.
- Limited benchmark scope (no ImageNet or large-scale tasks); moderate improvements vs. strongest contemporaries.
- Sparse ablations: effect of C independent of T, removing gates, varying bias systematically, alternative nonlinearities for T.
- Overhead (parameters/compute) due to gating not quantified; no gradient-norm diagnostics to substantiate “highway” claim quantitatively.

Questions for authors:
1. How do highway nets compare to simple residual connections y = H(x) + x (no gates) under matched depth/width/params on CIFAR? Do gates still yield optimization/generalization gains?
2. How sensitive are results to the initial gate bias bT? Is there a principled schedule (e.g., annealing toward 0) that improves performance?
3. Have you tried decoupling carry and transform (learn C independently rather than 1−T)? Any stability or performance differences?
4. What is the runtime and parameter overhead of gating relative to plain or residual nets?
5. Can you report gradient norms/Jacobian singular values across depth to quantify “information highways”?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a residual-connection baseline and an ablation suite (C=1−T vs independent C, fixed vs learned gates, bias sweep), ideally on CIFAR-10/100.
- Provide a brief quantitative analysis of gradient flow (e.g., gradient norms across depth) to complement the qualitative figures.
- Report parameter counts, FLOPs, and wall-clock time to contextualize the cost of gating.
- Expand to an additional dataset (e.g., SVHN) to demonstrate robustness without heavy tuning; if feasible, include a smaller-scale ImageNet experiment.
- Clarify architectural details in a concise table for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept).
While the empirical scope is modest, the architectural idea is clear, principled, and practically useful, with convincing optimization benefits and thoughtful analyses. The work is likely to be influential and to catalyze deeper architectures across modalities. My enthusiasm would increase further with a direct residual-baseline comparison and more ablations. Confidence: medium.
