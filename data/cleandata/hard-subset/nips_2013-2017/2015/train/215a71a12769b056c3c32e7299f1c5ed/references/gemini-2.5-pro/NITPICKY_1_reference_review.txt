### **Summary**

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel feedforward neural network architecture designed to enable the training of extremely deep models. The core idea is inspired by the gating mechanisms in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent networks. Each layer in a highway network includes a "transform gate" and a "carry gate" that adaptively control the flow of information. Depending on the gate outputs, a layer can either transform its input, as in a conventional network, or pass its input through unchanged. This creates "information highways" that allow gradients and activations to propagate across many layers without attenuation. The authors demonstrate empirically that highway networks with up to 100 layers can be trained directly with SGD, overcoming the optimization difficulties that plague similarly deep "plain" networks. They provide experiments on MNIST and CIFAR datasets, showing that these deep networks are trainable and can achieve competitive performance. The paper also includes an analysis of the learned gate behavior, suggesting that the network learns data-dependent information routing and adapts its effective depth to the complexity of the task.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is technically plausible, and the experimental results demonstrating the trainability of deep highway networks are compelling. However, the work suffers from several methodological and experimental shortcomings that question its completeness and rigor.

*   The decision to constrain the carry gate as `C = 1 - T` is presented as a simplification without empirical justification. A more thorough investigation would explore the design space, for example, by learning `C` and `T` independently or coupling them via a softmax. Without this, it is unclear if this simplification harms performance or if it is a critical component of the design.
*   The hyperparameter search and model selection process lack rigor. In Section 3.1, the description of the random search is vague. More concerningly, in Section 4, the models chosen for analysis were selected to "minimize the best training set error" (footnote 3). This is a poor practice, as it favors overfitted models and may not reflect the behavior of models that generalize well.
*   The analysis in Section 4 is interesting but superficial. The lesion study (Section 4.2), for instance, only considers forcing the transform gates to zero (`T=0`), which makes the layer an identity function. It does not explore the opposite case (`T=1`) or lesioning the `H` transform itself, which would provide a more complete picture of each layer's contribution. The conclusion that 60% of layers on MNIST "don't learn to contribute" is a strong claim based on a single, one-sided experiment.
*   The excuse for not tuning hyperparameters for the CIFAR experiments in Section 3.3.2 ("it is quite possible to obtain much better results") actively weakens the paper's claims by admitting the presented results are likely suboptimal.

#### **Clarity**

The paper's central idea is conveyed reasonably well, but the manuscript is littered with inconsistencies and signs of careless preparation, which detract significantly from its overall quality.

*   The notation is inconsistent. The dot operator `(·)` is defined as element-wise multiplication in Section 2, but later in the same section, the asterisk `*` is used for the same purpose (`yi = Hi(x) * Ti(x) + ...`) without being defined.
*   The formatting is poor. Footnote 2 appears to be a dangling part of a sentence that should belong in the main text.
*   The list of references is exceptionally sloppy. Many entries are incomplete (e.g., [1] is missing authors, title, and page numbers), inconsistently formatted, and contain extraneous artifacts like "[cs]". This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a submission to a premier venue like NIPS.
*   The description of reported results as "Best (mean ± std.dev.)" is ambiguous. It is unclear if "Best" refers to the single best run or the hyperparameters that yielded the best mean performance.

#### **Originality**

The core contribution—adapting LSTM's gating mechanism to deep feedforward networks—is novel and insightful. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work on initialization, optimization, and layer-wise training.

However, a crucial baseline is missing. The paper contrasts its gated architecture only with "plain" networks. A much more relevant and simpler baseline would be a network with non-gated, parameter-free shortcut connections, such as `y = H(x, WH) + x`. This is a direct precursor to the proposed gated formulation (which can be seen as `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1-T(x))`). Without this comparison, it is impossible to determine whether the performance gains come from the shortcut connections themselves or from the added complexity and data-dependent behavior of the gating mechanism. This is a significant omission that limits the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, the discussion of concurrent work [32] is relegated to a footnote, which seems to downplay its relevance.

#### **Significance**

The paper addresses the highly significant problem of training very deep neural networks. The demonstration that networks with hundreds of layers can be trained with SGD is a substantial contribution that opens the door for exploring the benefits of depth in a way that was previously impractical. The analytical tools proposed, such as the lesion study, provide a new lens through which to examine the function of individual layers in a deep model.

However, the significance is undermined by the methodological flaws mentioned above. The lack of a simple additive skip-connection baseline makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed gating mechanism. While the work is promising, the numerous flaws in execution and presentation prevent it from being a definitive contribution.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  The paper introduces a genuinely novel and effective architecture for training very deep networks, a problem of central importance to the field.
2.  The empirical evidence that highway networks can be trained at depths of 100+ layers with standard SGD is a strong and significant result.
3.  The analysis, while preliminary, provides interesting insights into learned information routing and adaptive depth.

### **Arguments for Rejection**

1.  The manuscript is poorly prepared, with numerous errors in formatting, notation, and especially the reference list, indicating a lack of diligence.
2.  A critical baseline is missing: non-gated, additive shortcut connections (`y = H(x) + x`). Without this comparison, the necessity of the complex gating mechanism is unproven.
3.  The experimental methodology contains several weaknesses, including ambiguous reporting, questionable model selection criteria (based on training error), and an admission of incomplete hyperparameter tuning, which casts doubt on the robustness of the results.
4.  The analysis of the network's behavior is one-sided and incomplete.

### **Recommendation: Reject**

While the core idea of Highway Networks is innovative and the headline results are impressive, the manuscript in its current form falls short of the standards expected for NIPS. The combination of a critical missing baseline, questionable experimental practices, and a general lack of care in the paper's preparation makes it difficult to confidently accept the work. The numerous minor flaws collectively form a major concern about the rigor and completeness of the research. I would encourage the authors to revise the paper to address these significant issues, in particular by including a comparison to non-gated shortcut connections and by improving the overall quality and clarity of the presentation.
