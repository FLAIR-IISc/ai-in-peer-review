**Summary**

This paper addresses the well-known difficulty of training very deep feedforward neural networks due to vanishing/exploding gradients and degraded information flow. The authors propose a novel architecture, the "Highway Network," directly inspired by the gating mechanism of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. The core idea is to introduce adaptive gates for each layer that regulate the flow of information. Each layer's output is a dynamic combination of its transformed input and the original, untransformed input. This is controlled by a "transform gate," which allows the network to learn to pass information directly through many layers, creating unimpeded "information highways." The authors demonstrate empirically that this architecture allows for the direct training of networks with hundreds of layers using standard SGD, a feat not possible with conventional "plain" networks. They show that these deep networks achieve strong performance on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, outperforming comparable models that require complex, multi-stage training procedures.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Originality:** The central contribution of this paper is a truly novel and elegant architectural idea. The application of LSTM-style gating to feedforward architectures to solve the problem of depth is a brilliant conceptual leap. It moves beyond incremental improvements to optimizers or initialization schemes and proposes a fundamental change to the network building block itself. This is precisely the kind of bold, foundational work that drives the field forward.

2.  **Significant Impact:** This work directly tackles one of the most significant barriers in deep learning at the time: effectively training extremely deep networks. By demonstrating a simple and effective mechanism to do so, the paper opens up a vast new space for architectural exploration. The concept of learned, data-dependent information routing is powerful and has the potential to influence network design for years to come. The analysis showing that the network can learn to use only as much depth as a task requires (Fig. 4) is a fascinating and important finding.

3.  **Compelling Empirical Validation of the Core Idea:** The experiments, particularly the optimization comparison in Section 3.1, provide a stark and convincing demonstration of the architecture's power. The sight of a 100-layer plain network completely failing to train while a 100-layer highway network learns effectively is a powerful piece of evidence. The success in training deep but thin networks to rival wider, shallower models like FitNets (without their complex training procedure) further solidifies the practical value of the approach.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results. The analysis in Section 4, visualizing the gate activities and performing lesion studies, offers valuable insights into the network's learned behavior. The demonstration that the network learns class-specific routing patterns (Fig. 3) elevates the gating mechanism from a mere optimization trick to an integral part of the network's computational process.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper is excellent, a few minor points could be improved:

1.  **Limited Theoretical Grounding:** The intuition behind the improved gradient flow is well-explained by the Jacobian analysis (Eq. 5), but the paper lacks a more formal theoretical treatment of why highway networks are so much easier to optimize. Given the strength of the empirical results, however, this feels more like an opportunity for future work than a critical flaw in the present submission.

2.  **Heuristic Initialization:** The strategy of initializing the transform gate biases to small negative values is simple and effective but appears somewhat ad-hoc. While the authors provide a reasonable guideline, a more systematic study of the sensitivity to this hyperparameter or a more principled initialization method would strengthen the methodology. This is a minor point, as the key takeaway is that such a simple trick *is sufficient* to enable training.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an outstanding paper that introduces a significant and highly novel idea. It addresses a major, well-understood problem with a bold, creative, and surprisingly simple solution. While the experimental validation could be more exhaustive and the theoretical underpinnings could be deeper, these are minor concerns that are vastly outweighed by the importance and originality of the core contribution. The proposed Highway Network is not an incremental improvement; it is a new architectural paradigm that fundamentally changes our ability to train and reason about very deep models. This is precisely the type of high-impact, forward-looking research that NIPS should be championing. I recommend acceptance without hesitation.
