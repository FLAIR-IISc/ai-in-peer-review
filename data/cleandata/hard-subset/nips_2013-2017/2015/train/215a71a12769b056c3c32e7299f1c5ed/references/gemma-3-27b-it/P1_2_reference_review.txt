## Peer Review for "Training Very Deep Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces "Highway Networks," a novel neural network architecture inspired by LSTM, designed to facilitate the training of very deep feedforward networks. The core idea is to incorporate "highway connections" – adaptive gating mechanisms – that allow information to flow directly across many layers without attenuation. The authors demonstrate that these networks can be trained effectively with standard SGD, even to depths exceeding 100 layers, overcoming the optimization difficulties encountered with traditional “plain” networks. They validate their approach through experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, showing competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods, especially in scenarios with limited computational budgets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper rightly positions itself within the growing body of work attempting to address the challenges of training deep neural networks. It builds upon earlier efforts focusing on better optimizers (e.g., [13-15]), initialization strategies ([16, 17]), activation functions ([20, 21]), and skip connections ([22, 23, 2, 24]). The explicit inspiration from LSTM [29, 30] is a key contribution, applying concepts from recurrent networks to the feedforward domain. The comparison to FitNets [25] and the discussion of related work like neural history compression [26] provide good context.  The authors acknowledge similar, concurrently developed work [32], demonstrating awareness of the current research landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The highway network architecture is a genuinely novel approach to tackling the vanishing/exploding gradient problem in deep networks.  The LSTM-inspired gating mechanism is a clever adaptation.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing.  The clear demonstration of training networks with >100 layers, and the performance gains compared to plain networks (particularly in Figure 1), are strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Clarity of Explanation:** The mathematical formulation of highway networks (Equation 3) is concise and easy to understand. The authors clearly explain the function of the transform and carry gates.
* **Thorough Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply demonstrating performance and provides some insightful analysis of the learned gating behavior (Figures 2, 3, and 4).  The investigation of layer importance is particularly valuable.
* **Accessibility:** The authors have made their code and hyperparameters publicly available, promoting reproducibility and further research.
* **Addresses a core problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in deep learning – enabling the effective training of very deep networks – with a practical and well-supported solution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention the importance of initializing the transform gate bias, the discussion of hyperparameter tuning feels somewhat cursory.  More detailed analysis of the sensitivity of performance to different hyperparameter settings would be beneficial.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:**  While Figure 4 provides some insight into layer importance, more comprehensive ablation studies exploring the impact of different components of the highway network (e.g., different activation functions within the H transform, variations in the gating mechanism) would strengthen the paper.
* **CIFAR Results - Not State-of-the-Art:** While the CIFAR results are competitive, they do not represent a new state-of-the-art.  The authors acknowledge this, but framing the results more carefully (e.g., emphasizing the ease of training with comparable performance) might be useful.
* **Figure 2 Visualizations:** The visualizations in Figure 2 are a bit difficult to interpret.  Adding labels or annotations to clarify the patterns would improve their clarity.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined architecture and strong empirical support.  The analysis is thoughtful and provides insights into the network’s behavior. The claim that highway networks overcome optimization issues in deep networks seems justified.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and easy to follow.
* **Originality (8/10):** The application of LSTM-inspired gating mechanisms to feedforward networks is a novel idea. While building on existing concepts, the resulting architecture and its demonstrated performance represent a significant contribution.
* **Significance (8/10):**  The ability to train very deep networks with standard SGD is a significant advancement.  Highway networks have the potential to unlock the benefits of greater depth in a wider range of applications. The analysis provided offers valuable insights into how depth impacts learning.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Solves a crucial problem in deep learning - training very deep networks.
* Novel architecture inspired by a well-established and successful recurrent network structure.
* Strong empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
* Clear and well-written paper with publicly available code.
* Insightful analysis of the learned gating behavior and layer importance.

**Against:**

* Hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly investigated.
*  CIFAR results, while good, are not state-of-the-art.
* Limited ablation studies to dissect the network components.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The highway network architecture is a clever and effective solution to the challenges of training very deep networks. The experimental results are convincing, and the analysis provides insightful observations. While some aspects could be further explored (hyperparameter sensitivity, ablation studies), the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above.  Specifically, they should consider adding more detail regarding hyperparameter tuning, conducting more comprehensive ablation studies, and clarifying the visualizations in Figure 2.
