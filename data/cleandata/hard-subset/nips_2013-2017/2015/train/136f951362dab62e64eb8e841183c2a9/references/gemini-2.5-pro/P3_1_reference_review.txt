### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, addressing the limitations of traditional static recommendation models. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with a specific item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. This captures the phenomenon where past activities increase the likelihood of similar activities in the near future. To generalize this model to all user-item pairs, including unobserved ones, the authors propose representing the parameters of the Hawkes processes (the baseline intensity and the self-excitation magnitude) as two matrices. By assuming these matrices are low-rank, the model can transfer learned temporal patterns across users and items, effectively integrating collaborative filtering with temporal point processes.

To learn the model parameters, the authors formulate a convex optimization problem that maximizes the log-likelihood of the observed event data, regularized by the nuclear norms of the parameter matrices to enforce the low-rank structure. Recognizing the computational challenge of this formulation, they propose an equivalent formulation that decouples the non-negativity constraints from the non-smooth nuclear norm regularizers. They then develop a novel and efficient hybrid optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient steps for the non-negative parameters and conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) steps for the nuclear-norm-regularized parameters. This algorithm avoids costly full SVD computations at each iteration. The paper provides a theoretical convergence analysis for the algorithm and demonstrates its effectiveness through extensive experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real-world datasets, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The paper establishes a compelling and principled connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for recommendation. This is a significant step beyond existing temporal recommendation models that often rely on time-windowing, decay functions, or Markovian assumptions. The unified framework for simultaneously recommending items and predicting returning times is elegant and addresses a crucial need for modern interactive systems.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The formulation of the "Low Rank Hawkes Processes" is well-motivated. The main technical highlight is the novel optimization algorithm. The alternative formulation (Eq. 5) is a clever technique to make a difficult optimization problem tractable. The proposed hybrid algorithm, blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, is efficient, scalable, and tailored to the problem structure. Crucially, it replaces the expensive proximal operator for the nuclear norm (a full SVD) with a much cheaper linear minimization oracle (top singular vector pair computation), which is key to its practical utility.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach. Theorem 1 establishes the equivalence between the original and the alternative optimization problems. Theorem 2 provides a convergence rate analysis for the proposed algorithm, showing that it achieves an optimal O(1/t) rate. This theoretical backing adds significant weight to the algorithmic contribution.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors use a large synthetic dataset to verify the model's correctness and scalability, and three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, healthcare) to demonstrate its practical effectiveness. The comparison against a strong set of baselines, including tensor factorization, is well-chosen. The evaluation on two distinct tasks (item ranking and time prediction), particularly on entirely held-out sequences, robustly validates the model's superior performance. The use of the time-change theorem to validate the Hawkes process assumption on real data is an excellent piece of model diagnostics that strengthens the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limited Scope of Temporal Dynamics:** The model currently only considers self-excitation (i.e., activity on item `i` only influences future activity on item `i`). It neglects the arguably more complex but equally important phenomenon of mutual excitation, where interacting with one item can trigger interactions with other, related items (e.g., listening to one artist influences listening to another). While this is a reasonable simplification for a first paper on the topic, the authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss how the framework might be extended to a full multi-dimensional Hawkes process, contrasting it more directly with related work in that area (e.g., Zhou et al., [29, 30]).

2.  **Practicality of the Alternative Formulation:** The equivalence proven in Theorem 1 holds for a sufficiently large penalty parameter `ρ > ρ*`. However, `ρ*` is a problem-dependent constant that is unknown in practice. The paper states that `ρ` is tuned via cross-validation, which is standard. However, a discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm and its solution to the choice of `ρ` would be valuable. Does the performance degrade gracefully if `ρ` is suboptimal? Is there a heuristic for setting a reasonable range for this parameter?

3.  **Choice of Triggering Kernel:** For tractability, the paper uses a simple exponential triggering kernel. The authors briefly mention that other kernels are possible but do not explore this. In many real-world scenarios, influence might decay according to a power-law or a more complex function. A brief discussion or a small experiment on the sensitivity of the results to the kernel choice would strengthen the paper's practical guidance.

4.  **Clarity on Extreme Sparsity:** The model's ability to generalize depends on the low-rank assumption. In extremely sparse "cold-start" scenarios (new users or items with zero history), the model would predict based only on the baseline intensity, which would be zero for the corresponding row/column in Λ0 if not handled carefully. It would be beneficial to clarify how the model handles a completely new user or item. Does it fall back to a global average, or is this outside the scope?

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of recommendation systems. It introduces a novel and technically sophisticated model that elegantly unifies temporal point processes and collaborative filtering. The algorithmic contribution is significant and could be of independent interest. The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and supported by a thorough and convincing experimental evaluation. While there are some limitations and avenues for future work, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on more expressive temporal models for user behavior.

**Recommendation: Accept.**
