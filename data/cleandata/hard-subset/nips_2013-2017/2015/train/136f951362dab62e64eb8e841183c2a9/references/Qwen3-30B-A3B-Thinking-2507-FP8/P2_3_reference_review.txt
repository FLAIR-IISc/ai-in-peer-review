

# Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user activities as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to address two key problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next returning time of users to items. They develop a convex optimization formulation with a new algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate. The paper demonstrates scalability to large datasets and superior performance on both synthetic and real-world recommendation tasks compared to strong baselines.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with solid theoretical foundations. The authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis (Theorem 2) demonstrating the O(1/t) convergence rate of their algorithm, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic data generation and multiple real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). The experimental design properly evaluates the model on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks, with appropriate baselines including Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.

However, the paper has some quality limitations. The theoretical analysis doesn't fully address the approximation error introduced by the low-rank assumption, which is crucial for understanding the model's limitations. Additionally, while the paper shows the algorithm scales well, it lacks detailed computational complexity analysis beyond the convergence rate. The ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the low-rank structure versus the Hawkes process would have strengthened the empirical evaluation.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from background to proposed method to experiments. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the algorithm descriptions are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The background section on temporal point processes provides necessary context for readers unfamiliar with the concept.

Some aspects could be clarified for broader accessibility. The connection between the Hawkes process and low-rank matrix factorization could be explained more intuitively, particularly for readers less familiar with point processes. The penalty method in Section 4.2 (which introduces auxiliary variables Z1 and Z2) would benefit from more intuitive motivation. The paper also occasionally uses technical terms (e.g., "self-exciting," "triggering kernel") without sufficient explanation for a general machine learning audience.

## Originality
The paper makes several meaningful contributions to the field. The key novelty lies in establishing the connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation, which differs significantly from related work. The authors correctly identify a critical limitation of Kapoor et al.'s work [12, 11] (inability to recommend new items) and address it directly.

The convex formulation (Equation 4) and the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) blend proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods in a novel way. As the authors note, this is "first of its kind" to achieve the best of both worlds in handling both non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization. The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization for recommendation is a novel approach that hasn't been explored in previous NIPS papers.

The paper properly cites related work, including the recent work of Kapoor et al. [12, 11] and other point process applications in recommendation systems. However, it could have better differentiated from more recent work on Hawkes processes in recommendation, such as the "Dirichlet-Hawkes processes" by Du et al. [6] or "Learning social infectivity" by Zhou et al. [28].

## Significance
The problem addressed has clear practical significance for real-world applications. The ability to recommend items at the right time and predict user return times has direct implications for personalized services, targeted advertising, and healthcare monitoring (as the paper illustrates with the MIMIC II dataset). The demonstrated scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events makes the approach applicable to large-scale industrial systems.

The paper shows clear improvements over baselines on both recommendation and prediction tasks across multiple datasets. The empirical results (Figure 3) convincingly demonstrate that the Hawkes model better captures temporal dynamics than survival analysis models assuming i.i.d. inter-event gaps. The authors also provide useful insights about how the model can incorporate additional contextual information (spatial, textual, etc.), enhancing its practical utility.

The optimization algorithm could be valuable for other problems beyond recommendation, as the authors note, potentially advancing the field of convex optimization for large-scale problems.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Novel combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rate guarantee
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Demonstrated scalability to large-scale problems
- Practical relevance to real-world applications (advertising, healthcare)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of theoretical approximation error from low-rank assumption
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate contributions of key components
- Some technical sections could benefit from clearer explanations for broader audience
- Could better differentiate from more recent work on Hawkes processes in recommendation

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation by introducing a novel convex formulation and efficient optimization algorithm. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a practically important problem with clear empirical validation. While there are some areas for improvement in the theoretical analysis and presentation, these do not undermine the core contributions.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria well. It demonstrates high quality through solid theory and experiments, clarity through well-organized writing, originality through the novel combination of techniques, and significance through practical relevance and strong empirical results.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in time-sensitive recommendation and provides a valuable methodological contribution to the broader machine learning community. The proposed algorithm could be widely applicable to other problems involving temporal dynamics and low-rank structure.

## Constructive Suggestions
1. Add an ablation study showing the impact of the low-rank assumption versus the full-rank model
2. Include a brief discussion of the approximation error introduced by the low-rank assumption
3. Clarify the connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models in the introduction
4. Consider adding a comparison with more recent Hawkes process-based recommendation methods

This paper represents a strong contribution that would be valuable to the NIPS community.
