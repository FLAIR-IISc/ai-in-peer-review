- The paper models user–item interactions as Hawkes processes with exponential kernels and learns low-rank nonnegative base intensity Λ0 and excitation A via nuclear-norm regularization.  
- Authors propose a convex and a penalized formulation with auxiliary variables Z1, Z2, and an algorithm mixing proximal projection for nonnegativity and conditional-gradient steps using top singular vectors; experiments on synthetic and three real datasets evaluate ranking and return-time prediction.

- Positions work relative to survival models, time-aware factorization, and prior Hawkes/network infectivity learning, claiming novelty in combining per-pair Hawkes with low-rank nonnegativity and a convex estimator.

- Strength: Problem is important across domains (media, commerce, healthcare).  
- Strength: Hawkes + low-rank is a principled way to model short-term recency and transfer to unobserved pairs.  
- Strength: Convex formulation (negative log-likelihood + nuclear norm) is appealing versus nonconvex factorizations.  
- Strength: Algorithmic idea to decouple nonnegativity and nuclear norms and use top singular vectors is computationally attractive.  
- Strength: Empirical results are promising, with diagnostics showing Hawkes fits better than simple baselines and improvements on ranking and return-time tasks.

- Major concern: The conditional-gradient subroutine and its LMO (minimizing 〈p,Z〉+λ||Z||*) are nonstandard/ill-posed and require rigorous justification and correct formulation for guarantees to hold.  
- Concern: Equivalence claim between penalized and constrained formulations (Theorem 1) relies on unknown ρ* and has limited practical value; sensitivity to ρ should be discussed.  
- Concern: Convergence claims mix O(1/k^2) and O(1/k) terms; need clear conditions, boundedness assumptions, and precise statement when both blocks are active.  
- Missing baselines: Time-aware CF (e.g., TimeSVD++), dynamic Poisson/Bayesian count factorizations, multivariate/low-rank Hawkes (e.g., Zhou et al.), and modern large-scale proximal nuclear-norm solvers.  
- Modeling limitation: For new user–item pairs, excitation α has no effect and recommendations rely solely on Λ0, limiting temporal personalization for cold-start items.  
- Return-time prediction: Using Ogata sampling may be unnecessary; closed-form or cheaper deterministic computations exist for exponential-kernel Hawkes and should be compared.  
- Reproducibility: Missing implementation details (step-size/L, method for top singular vectors, per-iteration complexity/memory, hyperparameter sensitivity) and some terse notation.

- Quality: Modeling and convex formulation are sound, but the hybrid proximal/CG algorithm and convergence proof require clarification and rigor.  
- Clarity: Generally readable, but optimization section, LMO definition, and theorem assumptions need more precise statements and algorithmic detail for reproduction.  
- Originality: Combination of Hawkes per user–item with low-rank nonnegativity and a convex estimator is novel; the optimization variant is a nontrivial but not fully justified adaptation of known methods.  
- Significance: Important problem with principled approach and promising results; could be influential if algorithmic issues and baselines are addressed.

- Pros for acceptance: Well-motivated convex model, potentially scalable algorithmic design, solid multi-domain empirical results and diagnostics.  
- Cons for acceptance: Nonstandard LMO and convergence analysis need correction, limited baselines/ablations, modeling limits (no cross-item excitation, cold-start issues), and sparse reproducibility details.

- Recommendation: Borderline; lean toward rejection unless the authors rigorously fix/clarify the optimization subroutine, strengthen empirical baselines and ablations, and provide implementation details.
