- Models time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction as a low-rank Hawkes process capturing self-exciting temporal dynamics.
- Provides a convex formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm combining proximal and conditional gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence.
- Demonstrates scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
- Shows strong empirical performance on synthetic and real-world datasets, with informative ablation studies.
- Framework is general and extensible to incorporate contextual information like user profiles or item content.
- Paper situates work well within temporal recommendation and Hawkes-process literature, addressing limits of prior survival-analysis approaches.
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models offering a principled time-sensitive recommendation framework.
- Algorithm is well-engineered and theoretically grounded with solid convergence guarantees.
- Experiments are thorough, clearly described, and include real-world datasets.
- Paper is generally clear, well-organized, and mathematically well-presented.
- Model complexity and parameter tuning (rho, lambda, beta, kernel bandwidth, rank) may hinder practical adoption.
- Focus on the exponential kernel is limited; broader kernel analysis and discussion of kernel limitations are needed.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity (especially rho) and search strategies are not fully explored.
- Comparisons lack more recent state-of-the-art temporal/sequential recommendation baselines (e.g., RNN-based models).
- More details on real-world dataset preprocessing and sparsity would improve reproducibility and context.
- Technically sound, rigorous, and complete; some algorithmic sections (e.g., Algorithm 1) are dense.
- Combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling is original and significant for timing-critical applications.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with minor weaknesses that can be addressed.
- Recommendation: Strong Accept, with suggested revisions on hyperparameter analysis and expanded baseline/kernel comparisons.
