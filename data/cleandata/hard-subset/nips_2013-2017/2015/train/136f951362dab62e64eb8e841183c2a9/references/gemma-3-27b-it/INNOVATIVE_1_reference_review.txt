This paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, framing the problem through the lens of low-rank Hawkes processes. The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is a genuinely interesting and, to my knowledge, previously unexplored direction. The authors tackle a practically relevant problem – modeling temporally varying user preferences – and provide a theoretical justification alongside an efficient optimization algorithm. The convergence analysis, while technical, is a clear strength, establishing an optimal rate under reasonable assumptions.

The primary strength of this work lies in its *novelty*. While Hawkes processes have been applied in recommender systems (as the authors acknowledge with Kapoor et al.), the low-rank formulation and the explicit connection to recommendation *and* returning-time prediction in a unified framework is a significant contribution. The approach moves beyond simple rating prediction to model the dynamics of user engagement, addressing the limitations of traditional methods regarding data sparsity and noise. The convex formulation is also a clever move, enabling efficient optimization. The experimental results, although presented somewhat briefly, demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real datasets, bolstering the claims. The extension possibilities (incorporating contextual information) are clearly articulated, suggesting further avenues for research.

However, the paper isn’t without its weaknesses. The experimental section feels somewhat cursory. While the results demonstrate superiority over baselines, the details of dataset preprocessing, hyperparameter tuning, and statistical significance testing are lacking. A more in-depth analysis, including ablation studies to isolate the impact of the low-rank assumption, would strengthen the findings. The comparison to STiC feels a bit unfair as pointed out by the authors, but a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs would be valuable. The convergence analysis, while mathematically sound, relies on several assumptions that could be discussed in more detail—specifically, the Lipschitz constant L and its impact on practical performance. Furthermore, the connection to real-world applications, particularly in the medical domain, is presented as potential rather than demonstrated, weakening the impact of that claim.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The core idea of combining low-rank models with Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation is fresh and potentially impactful.
*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The convex formulation and convergence analysis provide a solid mathematical basis for the proposed approach.
*   **Promising empirical results:** The experiments demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real datasets, suggesting the approach is viable.
*   **Potential for broad applicability:** The framework is generalizable to incorporate various contextual factors, broadening its potential impact.
*   **Addresses a significant problem:** Moving beyond static recommendations to model dynamic user preferences is crucial for modern web services.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Experimental section is underdeveloped:** Lack of detailed analysis, ablation studies, and statistical significance testing.
*   **Limited discussion of practical considerations:** The assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis aren’t thoroughly addressed in the context of real-world applications.
*   **Application claims are speculative:** The potential applications in areas like healthcare are not substantiated with concrete results.
*   **Brief discussion of related work:** While Kapoor et al. are addressed, a more comprehensive review of related work on temporal point processes and their applications in recommender systems would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses in the experimental section, I believe the *novelty and potential significance* of this work outweigh its flaws. The paper introduces a compelling new approach to a challenging problem and provides a strong theoretical foundation. While more thorough experimentation and analysis would strengthen the paper, the core contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should address the concerns raised in the experimental section during a rebuttal period. This paper represents a bold step forward and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
