## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a low-rank Hawkes process model for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, developing a novel convex formulation and optimization algorithm. While the problem addressed – integrating temporal dynamics into recommendations – is certainly relevant, and the theoretical development appears sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor (but collectively substantial) issues that detract from its overall quality and reproducibility.

**Summary:** The core idea is to model user-item interactions as a Hawkes process, leveraging low-rank approximations to enable generalization to unseen user-item pairs. The authors develop an optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods and demonstrate scalability and performance on synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** Time-sensitive recommendation is a crucial area for improving user experience, and this work tackles it directly.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models appears well-motivated, and the convex formulation is a positive step. The theoretical convergence analysis, claiming an O(1/t) rate, is a strong point if accurate.
* **Scalability:** The reported scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events is encouraging.
* **Unified Framework:** The attempt to unify recommendation and returning-time prediction within a single framework is laudable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Clarity in Notation:** The paper is riddled with inconsistent or poorly defined notation. For example, the meaning of `ψu,i` shifts subtly between equations (3) and (5), making the formulation difficult to follow. There are numerous instances where variable subscripts are dropped or redefined without explicit explanation.
* **Implementation Details Omitted:** Despite claiming scalability, the paper lacks crucial implementation details. The choice of hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β), kernel bandwidth (σ), and latent rank are stated as “tuned to give the best performance” without specifying *how* this tuning was conducted (cross-validation splits, grid search ranges, etc.).  This severely hampers reproducibility.
* **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** While the paper presents results on both synthetic and real datasets, the experimental setup lacks rigor. The criteria for splitting the data (e.g., 20% hold-out) is mentioned, but the precise methodology for generating training/testing subsequences is vague. Reporting only MAE is insufficient; reporting precision@k and recall@k would provide a more nuanced evaluation of recommendation performance.
* **Weak Baselines:** The comparison to STiC is limited (only returning-time prediction) and the choice of baseline methods feels dated. More recent and competitive methods for temporal recommendation should be included. The discussion of Tensor Factorization variants also feels superficial.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of achieving "superb predictive performance" feels unsupported given the lack of comprehensive evaluation.  The synthetic data experiments, while demonstrating scalability, lack a strong comparison to simpler, well-established baselines.
* **Minor Typographical Errors:** Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies. While not individually critical, they contribute to the overall impression of sloppiness. For example, "WSDM" is used in the reference list without specifying the year.
* **Unnecessary Complexity:** The introduction of auxiliary variables Z1 and Z2 in the convex formulation (section 4.2) seems overly complex without a clear justification beyond simplifying the optimization. It introduces additional parameters that need tuning.
* **Repetitive Content:** Several sections, particularly the introduction and related work, repeat similar ideas and phrasing.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:** The problem is relevant, the theoretical foundation is sound, and the scalability results are promising. The proposed approach represents a novel contribution to the field.

**Against:** The lack of clarity, reproducibility, and comprehensive evaluation severely undermine the paper’s impact. The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality and suggest a lack of thoroughness. The reliance on a somewhat convoluted optimization formulation and weak baseline comparisons further weaken the case for acceptance. 

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core ideas presented in this paper are interesting and potentially impactful, the numerous weaknesses significantly outweigh the strengths. The paper reads more like a preliminary report of ongoing work than a polished, complete contribution ready for NIPS. I **recommend rejection** at this time. The authors should address the concerns regarding clarity, reproducibility, and experimental rigor before resubmitting. Specifically, they need to provide detailed implementation instructions, a more comprehensive evaluation with stronger baselines, and a clearer presentation of their results. A thorough proofreading pass is also essential.
