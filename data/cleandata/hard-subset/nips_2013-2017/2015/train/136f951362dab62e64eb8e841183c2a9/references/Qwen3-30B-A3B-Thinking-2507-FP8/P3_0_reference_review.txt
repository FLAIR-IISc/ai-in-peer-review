

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical gap in recommendation systems by proposing a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations that captures the temporal dynamics of user-item interactions. The authors model user activities as low-rank Hawkes processes, where the intensity function captures both user-specific preferences and the self-exciting nature of user behavior (i.e., past interactions influence future ones). They formulate the problem as a convex optimization task with nuclear norm regularization, develop a novel optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate the approach's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets for two key tasks: item recommendation and returning-time prediction.

The work builds on the observation that most recommendation systems focus on static preferences (e.g., predicting ratings), while real-world engagement depends on temporal patterns. The paper distinguishes itself from related work (particularly Kapoor et al.) by enabling recommendations for new items, not just predicting return times for observed items.

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The use of Hawkes processes to model self-exciting temporal dynamics is well-justified and appropriate for the problem. The connection to point processes is clearly explained in Section 2, establishing a solid theoretical foundation.

2. **Convex Formulation:** The convex optimization formulation with nuclear norm regularization (Equation 4) is well-motivated and provides a tractable solution to the low-rank matrix estimation problem. The authors correctly identify the limitations of non-convex approaches.

3. **Algorithmic Innovation:** Algorithm 1 combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods in a novel way to handle both non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is rigorous and provides the best of both worlds, achieving optimal convergence rates for both components.

4. **Scalability Demonstrated:** The experiments on large-scale synthetic data (100 million events) and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's scalability to real-world problems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clear Justification for Recommendation Task:** The paper does not sufficiently explain why the intensity function λu,i(t) directly corresponds to the "relevance" of item i for user u at time t. In recommendation systems, relevance is typically measured by user preferences or ratings, but the paper doesn't establish this connection. The recommendation procedure in Section 3.4 simply ranks items by λu,i(t), but without explaining why higher intensity implies higher relevance.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparisons:** The comparison with SVD and Tensor factorization is problematic. SVD is a static model that doesn't consider temporal dynamics, so it's not a fair comparison for a time-sensitive recommendation system. The paper claims "all temporal methods outperform the static SVD," but doesn't explain how SVD would be adapted to handle time-sensitive recommendations. Similarly, the Tensor factorization baselines are not clearly adapted to the time-sensitive recommendation task.

3. **Insufficient Detail on Synthetic Data Generation:** The synthetic data generation process (Section 5.2) lacks critical details. How exactly were Λ0 and A generated? What is the relationship between the ground-truth intensity function and the observed events? The paper states "we generate two 1,024-by-1,204 user-item matrices Λ0 and A with rank five as the ground-truth," but doesn't explain the sampling process or how the intensity function was defined.

4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details Missing:** The paper mentions tuning ρ, λ, β, but provides no details on the tuning procedure or how these values scale with dataset size. This is critical for reproducibility.

## Clarity Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. The paper is generally well-organized with clear section headings and logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

2. The mathematical formulations are clearly presented, with detailed explanations of the Hawkes process and the optimization approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Ambiguous Connection Between Intensity and Recommendation Relevance:** The paper's core contribution is time-sensitive recommendation, but the fundamental link between the intensity function and recommendation relevance is not clearly articulated. The paper states "the intensity function λu,i(t) indicates the tendency that user u will consume item i at time t," but doesn't explain why this tendency should correspond to recommendation relevance. This is a critical gap in the paper's logic.

2. **Insufficient Detail on Implementation:** While the algorithm is described in detail, some implementation details are missing. For example, how were the hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β) tuned in practice? What was the stopping criterion for the optimization? These details are crucial for reproducibility.

3. **Confusing Presentation of Results:** The results in Figure 3 are presented without sufficient context. It's unclear what "Total" and "Heldout" refer to in the performance metrics, and the comparison with survival analysis models is not fully explained.

## Originality Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. The paper makes a clear and novel contribution by applying low-rank Hawkes processes to the time-sensitive recommendation problem, which has been largely unexplored in the recommendation literature.

2. The convex formulation with nuclear norm regularization is novel for this application domain, and the optimization algorithm combines two techniques (proximal gradient and conditional gradient) in a way that's not been previously applied to this problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Differentiation from Prior Work:** While the paper mentions Kapoor et al. as the most related work, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach. The key difference (ability to recommend new items) is mentioned, but the paper doesn't explain why this capability is impossible in the survival analysis approach.

2. **Lack of Novelty in Hawkes Process Application:** The application of Hawkes processes to model user behavior is not entirely novel. Zhou et al. (ICML 2013) and Du et al. (KDD 2015) have previously used Hawkes processes for modeling user activities and social networks. The paper should more clearly state how its specific application to recommendation with low-rank structure differs from these prior works.

## Significance Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. The problem addressed (time-sensitive recommendations) is highly relevant to real-world applications, including personalized services, health monitoring, and marketing. The paper effectively demonstrates the practical importance of this problem.

2. The paper's ability to scale to large datasets (100 million events) is significant for real-world deployment.

3. The proposed framework's ability to incorporate contextual information (Section 3.3) is a valuable extension that could benefit many applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Practical Impact Demonstration:** While the paper shows the model outperforms baselines on standard metrics, it doesn't demonstrate concrete practical impact. For example, how much would a company save by using this model instead of current approaches? What specific business metrics would improve?

2. **Lack of Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper doesn't analyze when the model fails or what limitations it has. For example, how does the model perform with sparse user histories or for new users/items (cold start problem)?

3. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims "the algorithm scales up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events," but doesn't provide concrete timing measurements or scalability curves to support this claim.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper addresses an important problem in recommendation systems with a theoretically sound and practically scalable approach. The use of low-rank Hawkes processes to model time-sensitive user activities is a novel and promising direction. However, the paper has several critical weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be accepted.

The most significant issue is the lack of clear justification for using the intensity function λu,i(t) as a measure of recommendation relevance. Without this connection, the recommendation task as formulated is not well-motivated. The paper also needs to provide more detailed comparisons with baselines that are actually designed for time-sensitive recommendations.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel application of Hawkes processes to time-sensitive recommendations
- Solid theoretical foundation and convergence guarantees
- Demonstrated scalability to large datasets
- Clear connection to important real-world applications

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Weak justification for using intensity as recommendation relevance metric
- Inadequate baseline comparisons for time-sensitive recommendation
- Missing details on synthetic data generation and hyperparameter tuning
- Limited analysis of model limitations and failure cases

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the Connection Between Intensity and Recommendation Relevance:** Add a section or subsection explaining why λu,i(t) should be interpreted as a measure of item relevance for user u at time t. Provide a more rigorous justification, possibly through a probabilistic interpretation.

2. **Improve Baseline Comparisons:** For each baseline (SVD, Tensor factorization), explicitly describe how it would be adapted to the time-sensitive recommendation task. If a baseline cannot be adapted, state this limitation clearly.

3. **Provide More Detail on Synthetic Data Generation:** Describe the exact process for generating Λ0 and A, and how the intensity function was derived from these matrices.

4. **Add Analysis of Model Limitations:** Include a discussion of when the model might fail (e.g., for users with very few interactions) and how these limitations might be addressed.

5. **Provide Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** Include information on the hyperparameter tuning procedure, including the ranges searched and the number of trials.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper presents an interesting approach to time-sensitive recommendations with solid theoretical foundations and promising experimental results. However, the fundamental weakness in the justification of the recommendation mechanism (using intensity as a relevance measure) is significant enough to undermine the paper's core contribution. The paper needs to address this issue before it can be considered for publication at NeurIPS.

The authors should substantially revise the paper to clarify the connection between the intensity function and recommendation relevance, and to provide more rigorous comparisons with appropriate baselines. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
