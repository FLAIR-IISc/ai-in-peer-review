- Paper proposes convex low-rank Hawkes model for joint item recommendation and return-time prediction, estimating per user–item base Λ0 and excitation A with a shared kernel and nuclear-norm regularization for generalization.

- Introduces hybrid solver using proximal updates for nonnegativity and Frank–Wolfe for nuclear-norm blocks, with O(1/t) convergence and equivalence to original constraint under a penalty threshold ρ.

- Empirically shows improved ranking and reduced return-time MAE on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II).

- Strength: Principled continuous-time formulation using conditional intensity both for ranking and inter-event prediction.

- Strength: Convex low-rank formulation provides global optimality (for fixed kernel) and a natural way to generalize to unobserved pairs.

- Strength: Optimization design avoids full SVDs and includes theoretical guarantees plus acceleration heuristics.

- Strength: Demonstrates scalability on large synthetic data (100M events) and impact in a healthcare domain.

- Weakness: Very restricted Hawkes parameterization (per-pair scalars with global kernel) cannot model inhibition, cross-item excitation, periodicity, or time-varying baseline adequately.

- Weakness: Claim of recommending unseen/new items/users is unsupported—nuclear norm alone cannot handle true cold-start without content/context features.

- Weakness: Penalty equivalence result depends on an unknown problem-dependent threshold ρ*; sensitivity to ρ and bias from suboptimal ρ are not analyzed.

- Weakness: Algorithmic novelty over existing composite/conditional gradient and forward–backward schemes is not clearly articulated.

- Weakness: Smoothness/Lipschitz assumptions for stated rates are not justified; Hawkes log-likelihood gradients can be ill-behaved when intensities approach zero.

- Weakness: Per-iteration complexity and memory usage are not quantified, especially for gradients over hundreds of millions of events and dense X1 updates.

- Weakness: Frank–Wolfe atomic representation growth is not reported—no numbers on atoms at convergence, truncation, or compression strategies.

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to strong continuous-time baselines (multivariate/low-rank Hawkes, renewal processes, neural point-process models like RMTPP/Neural Hawkes).

- Weakness: Tensor baselines rely on discretization; sensitivity to binning and choice of intervals is not discussed.

- Weakness: Return-time estimation uses Monte Carlo sampling but lacks details on sample counts, runtime per prediction, and variance impact on MAE.

- Weakness: No ablation studies on kernel bandwidth, regularization strengths, or the contribution of self-excitation A; learned effective ranks are not reported.

- Weakness: Manuscript has typos, notation inconsistencies, incomplete references, and lacks derivations (e.g., Eq.3) and explicit gradient formulas for reproducibility.

- Question: How sensitive are results to the kernel bandwidth σ, and is σ shared globally or learned per user/item?

- Question: Can periodic components (e.g., additive Fourier terms) be incorporated in λu,i(t) while retaining convexity?

- Question: What is the typical number of FW atoms at convergence and the memory footprint for the largest experiment?

- Question: How do results change if A is set to zero—i.e., what is the benefit of self-excitation versus baseline only?

- Question: Can you report recall@k and wall-clock training/inference times?

- Pros: Principled continuous-time model, convex low-rank solver with theoretical rate, and demonstrated empirical improvements across datasets.

- Cons: Limited temporal expressivity, incomplete baselines/evaluation, missing complexity/implementation details, and unclear novelty relative to composite FW literature.

- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising direction and useful solver but needs stronger baselines, model extensions for temporal patterns, clearer novelty claims, and more implementation/evaluation detail.
