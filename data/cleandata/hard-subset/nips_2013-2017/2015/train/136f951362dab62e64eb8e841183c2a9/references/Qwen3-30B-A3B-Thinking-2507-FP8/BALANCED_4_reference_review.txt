

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendations using low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors address two key problems: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting users' next return time. They establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, develop a new optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate strong empirical performance on synthetic and real datasets. The work builds on related work by Kapoor et al. [12, 11], who used survival analysis for returning time prediction but could not generalize to new item recommendations.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Technical Contribution**: The connection between low-rank models and Hawkes processes for recommendation systems is genuinely novel. The authors provide a well-motivated convex formulation of the problem, which is a significant departure from previous work that typically focuses on predicting ratings rather than temporal dynamics.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a thorough convergence analysis (Theorem 2) for their proposed algorithm, establishing an O(1/t²) convergence rate, which is optimal for the problem class. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified.

3. **Practical Scalability**: The experimental results demonstrate excellent scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events), which is crucial for real-world applications.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates the approach on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), providing convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness across different domains.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations, good use of figures, and a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Problem Formulation Clarity**: The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify the connection between "time-sensitive recommendation" and the proposed Hawkes process model. The relationship between item recommendation and returning-time prediction needs more precise definition.

2. **Experimental Rigor**: While the experiments are comprehensive, the paper lacks statistical significance tests for the performance differences between methods. The parameter tuning process across different datasets is not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess reproducibility.

3. **Comparison with Relevant Baselines**: The comparison with state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive. For instance, the authors don't compare with other Hawkes process-based recommendation approaches (e.g., [16, 23, 30]), which would better demonstrate the novelty of their approach.

4. **Practical Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address potential limitations of their approach. For example, how does the model handle long-term dependencies beyond the observed history? How does it perform when the event history is sparse?

5. **Theoretical-Practical Gap**: The theoretical guarantees apply to the convex relaxation, but it's not clear how this translates to practical benefits beyond the stated convergence rate. The paper claims to achieve "optimal O(1/t) convergence" but actually demonstrates O(1/t²), which needs clarification.

## Specific Recommendations

1. Clarify the relationship between the two proposed tasks (item recommendation and returning-time prediction) in the introduction. Explain how the Hawkes process model unifies these two problems.

2. Add statistical significance tests to the experimental results to strengthen the claims about performance improvements.

3. Expand the comparison with baselines to include more recent Hawkes process-based recommendation methods. This would better establish the novelty of the proposed approach.

4. Discuss practical implementation challenges and limitations more thoroughly, particularly regarding sparse event histories and long-term dependency modeling.

5. Clarify the convergence rate claim in the abstract and throughout the paper to accurately reflect the O(1/t²) rate demonstrated in Theorem 2.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a novel technical contribution that addresses an important gap in recommendation systems research. The theoretical and empirical results are convincing, and the approach has clear potential for real-world applications in domains ranging from e-commerce to healthcare. While the paper has some weaknesses in experimental rigor and problem formulation clarity, these are addressable with reasonable revisions.

The work makes a significant contribution to the field by connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models for time-sensitive recommendations. It advances the state of the art in both recommendation systems and temporal point process modeling, with clear practical implications for web services seeking to improve user engagement through time-sensitive personalization.

The paper meets NIPS quality standards in terms of technical soundness, clarity, and significance. The main concerns relate to experimental rigor and problem formulation clarity, which could be addressed with minor revisions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the specific concerns outlined above.
