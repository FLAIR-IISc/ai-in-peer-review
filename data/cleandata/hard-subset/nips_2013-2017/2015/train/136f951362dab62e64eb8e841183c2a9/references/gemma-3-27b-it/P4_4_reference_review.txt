This paper introduces a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction by framing the problem as a low-rank Hawkes process. The core idea is to leverage the self-exciting nature of Hawkes processes to model recurrent user activities and then employ low-rank matrix factorization to generalize across user-item pairs. The authors develop an optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods with provable convergence guarantees. 

The work builds upon recent research in temporal point processes for recommendation, specifically citing Kapoor et al. [12, 11]. However, it distinguishes itself by offering a more generalizable approach—capable of recommending new items—and by providing a convex formulation and efficient optimization algorithm for the problem. It also connects Hawkes processes with low-rank models, a potentially fruitful direction. Related work in tensor factorization [23, 25, 13, 3] is also considered, though the authors emphasize the limitations of these methods in capturing the inherent temporal dynamics.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for this specific recommendation task is novel. Addressing both recommendation *and* returning-time prediction within a unified framework is also a strength.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous convex formulation of the problem and establishes a convergence rate for the proposed optimization algorithm. The theoretical justification of the low-rank assumption is also reasonable.
* **Scalability:** The authors claim and demonstrate (in experiments) that their algorithm scales to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs).
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation includes synthetic data and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), covering various scenarios and demonstrating competitive performance against several baselines. The inclusion of both MAE for returning-time prediction and ranking metrics for recommendation is good.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper clearly positions its contribution relative to existing work in temporal point processes and collaborative filtering.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Algorithm:** While the authors present an efficient optimization algorithm, the description is quite dense and requires significant expertise in optimization techniques to fully grasp. The numerous subroutines (Algorithms 1, 2, and 3) add to the complexity.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions tuning parameters ρ, λ, β, and the kernel bandwidth but doesn't provide much detail on how this tuning was performed. A more thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Choice:** The paper primarily focuses on the exponential kernel. While justified, exploring the impact of different kernel choices on performance might strengthen the analysis.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While several baselines are included, the comparison could be more extensive, particularly with more recent and sophisticated recommendation algorithms. It is unclear how the performance compares to state-of-the-art deep learning based recommendation systems.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of achieving the "best of both worlds" in convergence rate (combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient) feels overstated. While the convergence analysis is sound, the achieved rate may not be significantly better than existing methods in practice.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness and scalability of the proposed algorithm.
* The paper is well-written (though complex) and clearly explains the underlying concepts and contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the algorithm may hinder its practical adoption.
* The parameter tuning process is not fully explained.
* The baseline comparison could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the comprehensive experimental evaluation make it a valuable contribution to the field. While the complexity of the algorithm and the limited discussion of parameter tuning are minor drawbacks, they do not outweigh the paper’s strengths.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should consider:

* Providing more detail on the parameter tuning process.
* Simplifying the description of the optimization algorithm or providing a more intuitive explanation.
* Conducting a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art recommendation algorithms.
* Refining the claim regarding the convergence rate.
