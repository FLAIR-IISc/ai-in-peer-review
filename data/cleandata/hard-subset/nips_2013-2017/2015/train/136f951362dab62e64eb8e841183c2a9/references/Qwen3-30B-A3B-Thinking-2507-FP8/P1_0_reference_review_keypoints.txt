- Proposes a novel framework modeling user activities as low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation.
- Targets two problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting users' next return times.
- Establishes a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank matrix models to capture preferences and temporal dynamics.
- Develops a convex optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence.
- Evaluated on synthetic and multiple real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, healthcare) and outperforms state-of-the-art baselines.
- Strong theoretical foundation with Theorem 1 (equivalence of formulations) and Theorem 2 (convergence analysis).
- Empirical results consistently support theoretical claims and demonstrate scalability to large datasets.
- Clearly differentiates from prior work (e.g., Kapoor et al.) by recommending new items and using full event histories.
- Well-written and organized with helpful background on temporal point processes and clear algorithm pseudocode.
- Needs more detail on practical parameter tuning (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth) and how values were chosen.
- Limited discussion on handling non-exponential triggering kernels and how the model captures diverse temporal patterns.
- Low-rank assumption may be restrictive in scenarios with complex user-item dynamics and requires more practical discussion.
- Novel contribution in linking low-rank matrix models with Hawkes processes and in the blended optimization algorithm.
- High practical significance for applications like mobile services, marketing (ad bidding), and healthcare (disease progression).
- Strengths: novelty, theoretical guarantees, comprehensive experiments, clear relation to prior work, efficient algorithm, scalability, and practical relevance.
- Weaknesses: limited kernel generalization, insufficient parameter-tuning details, potentially restrictive low-rank assumption, and need for more parameter intuition.
- Recommendation: accept for NIPS — significant contribution with strong theory, experiments, and practical impact.
- Suggested improvements: discuss non-exponential kernels, detail parameter tuning ranges/methods, note low-rank limitations, visualize learned intensities, and mention extensions for multi-event types/context.
