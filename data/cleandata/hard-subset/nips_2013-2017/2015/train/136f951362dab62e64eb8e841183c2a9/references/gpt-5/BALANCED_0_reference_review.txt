Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses two coupled problems: (1) time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given moment) and (2) return-time prediction (predicting when a user will next interact with an item). The authors model each user–item interaction stream as a univariate Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), using an exponential triggering kernel. To enable generalization across sparse user–item pairs, the base intensity matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A are constrained to be low-rank via nuclear-norm regularization, with non-negativity constraints to maintain valid intensities. The negative log-likelihood of the Hawkes processes with fixed kernel is convex in these parameters; the authors propose an alternative “penalized splitting” formulation that introduces auxiliary variables to decouple nuclear norm regularization from non-negativity. They then develop an optimization algorithm that combines a proximal step (for non-negativity and smooth loss) with a conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) step (for the nuclear norms), with an accelerated scheme and an O(1/t) convergence guarantee. They evaluate on large synthetic data and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II), showing gains over Poisson, SVD, tensor factorization, and a survival-analysis-based baseline.

This work connects two active lines at NeurIPS and elsewhere: temporal point processes (e.g., Hawkes modeling for social influence and event prediction; AISTATS/ICML work by Zhou, Zha, Song) and low-rank models for recommendation and structured estimation (nuclear-norm minimization; conditional gradient for norm-regularized problems). The idea of learning Hawkes parameters under low-rank structure has antecedents in learning influence networks with low-rank constraints, but the formulation here is adapted to user–item intensities and a recommender setting, aiming at both ranking and return-time prediction.

Strengths:
- Clear problem motivation: unifying time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction with a single probabilistic model.
- Convex estimation with nonnegativity and nuclear norm regularization; the log-likelihood is concave in the linear parameters, enabling principled learning.
- Algorithmic contribution: a practical hybrid of proximal gradient (cheap projection for non-negativity) and conditional gradient (LMO for nuclear norms) with a convergence analysis. The LMO uses only top singular vectors, avoiding full SVDs.
- Scalability: synthetic experiments with up to ~100M events; runtime scaling plots support practicality.
- Empirical gains on real datasets for both tasks; the time-change diagnostic is a nice sanity check for model fit relative to survival models.
- The framework is extensible (contextual features, tensorization, nonparametric kernels).

Weaknesses:
- Modeling limitations: Every user–item pair is treated as an isolated univariate Hawkes process. This ignores cross-excitation across items for the same user (or across users for the same item), which is often critical (e.g., artist-to-artist transitions, disease co-progressions). Prior work on multidimensional/mutually-exciting Hawkes could model such effects; the paper only hints at tensor extensions without implementing them.
- Stability/positivity conditions: For exponential Hawkes, stability requires α times the kernel mass < 1. The paper does not state or enforce such constraints per (u,i). Without this, intensities can blow up or the process may be non-stationary. At minimum, discuss whether the fitted A respects stability and how violations are handled.
- Exact-penalty equivalence: Theorem 1 states equivalence of the split-penalty problem and the original constrained problem for ρ > ρ*. But ρ* is defined in terms of unknown optima, making the result non-constructive; in practice ρ is tuned. A sensitivity study on ρ and a practical rule-of-thumb would strengthen the claim.
- Baselines: For return-time prediction and time-sensitive ranking, comparisons omit other point-process-based recommenders (e.g., multidimensional Hawkes baselines, or marked point process models). Tensor baselines discretize time and may be disadvantaged relative to continuous-time modeling; this should be contextualized. Using more standard ranking metrics (Recall@k/NDCG@k) alongside average rank would help.
- Prediction procedure: Return-time is estimated via Monte Carlo sampling with Ogata’s thinning. This can be costly at scale; the paper does not report prediction-time overhead or sample counts. For exponential kernels, efficient simulation or quadrature approximations could be discussed.
- Clarity/presentation: Notation is sometimes inconsistent; pseudo-code is terse (e.g., LMO/line-search details and step-size policies), and important implementation details (how top singular vectors are computed, number of power iterations, stopping criteria, batch vs. incremental updates) are missing. The claim “achieves the best of both worlds” merits a more nuanced comparison to existing hybrid PG/FW methods (e.g., conditional gradient sliding and proximal-FW variants).

Quality:
- The core estimation problem is well-posed and convex; the algorithm is reasonable and accompanied by convergence guarantees. Proof sketches are deferred; the main text could better justify key assumptions (kernel choice, stability).
- Experiments are positive but could be broader and stronger in baseline coverage and metrics. Ablations (effect of rank/regularization; kernel bandwidth; number of samples for return-time) would help assess robustness.

Clarity:
- Generally understandable, with a useful background on point processes. However, notation cleanup and more detailed algorithmic descriptions would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The combination of low-rank nuclear-norm-regularized Hawkes for user–item intensities and a hybrid PG/CG solver tailored to nonnegativity + nuclear norms is a solid contribution. Some elements build on known ideas, but the integration in a recommender context is novel.

Significance:
- Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are important; a scalable convex method is valuable. The impact would be stronger with cross-excitation modeling or broader empirical validation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Unified, principled continuous-time model for two important tasks.
- Convex learning with an efficient and scalable algorithm; theoretical convergence provided.
- Empirical improvements on multiple datasets; good diagnostic evidence vs. survival models.
- Extensible framework likely to be useful to the community.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited modeling of dependencies (no mutual excitation across items/users); stability constraints not addressed.
- Non-constructive penalty equivalence; limited ablation/sensitivity analysis.
- Missing stronger baselines and standard ranking metrics; limited discussion of prediction-time cost.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Extend to (or at least empirically compare with) multidimensional Hawkes that capture cross-item/user excitation; discuss stability and enforce constraints (e.g., α·∫γ < 1).
- Add ablations on ρ, λ, β, rank, and kernel bandwidth; report sensitivity and practical guidance for ρ.
- Include stronger baselines (mutual Hawkes, other point-process recommenders) and report Recall@k/NDCG@k; report prediction-time overhead and sample sizes.
- Clarify algorithmic details (LMO implementation, step sizes, top singular vector computation, stopping rules) and provide code or pseudo-code sufficient for reproduction.
- Consider more efficient or approximate return-time estimators for exponential kernels.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, principled, and scalable approach with promising results. Addressing the modeling and empirical gaps would make it a strong contribution.
