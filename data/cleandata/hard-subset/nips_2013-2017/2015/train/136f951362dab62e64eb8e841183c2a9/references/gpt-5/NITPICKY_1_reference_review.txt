Summary
This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework based on self-exciting Hawkes processes for each user–item pair, with low-rank structure imposed on the base intensity Λ0 and the self-excitation coefficient matrix A via nuclear norms. To address the difficulty of joint non-negativity and nuclear norm constraints, the authors introduce auxiliary variables (Z1, Z2) and a quadratic penalty tying Z to the primal variables, allowing a hybrid algorithm that uses projected (nonnegativity) proximal gradient on (Λ0, A) and conditional gradient over (Z1, Z2). They claim an O(1/t) convergence rate and demonstrate scalability and predictive gains on synthetic (100M events) and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II). Returning-time prediction is done via Monte Carlo sampling from the conditional density using Ogata’s thinning.

Strengths
- Clear and useful framing of time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction via point processes; the user–item Hawkes formulation is appropriate and widely relevant.
- Convex log-likelihood for linear Hawkes with exponential kernels enables principled estimation.
- The split treatment of nonnegativity (projection) and low-rankness (LMO on the nuclear norm) is appealing and computationally pragmatic.
- Experiments include large synthetic data and multiple real domains, with both ranking and return-time metrics, plus time-rescaling diagnostics.
- The framework is readily extensible to contextual features and other kernels.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Methodology and theory
- Stability of Hawkes processes is not enforced. For exponential kernel, a sufficient condition is αu,i∫γ = αu,iσ < 1. Without constraints, intensities can explode, undermining both likelihood and return-time sampling. The paper neither constrains nor reports the branching ratios.
- The “exact penalty” claim (Theorem 1) for squared Frobenius penalties is non-standard. L2 penalties are not exact penalties for equality constraints in general; the threshold ρ* depends on unknown optimal quantities (Λ*, Z*), making the result of limited practical value. This casts doubt on the asserted equivalence of (4) and (5).
- The LMOψ definition minimizes ⟨p, Z⟩ + ψ(Z). This is not the standard Frank–Wolfe oracle (which minimizes a linear function over a compact convex set). The algorithmic step is closer to a rank-1 update with a linesearch against a composite objective, but the justification is unclear. A precise derivation (e.g., via convex conjugates or conditional gradient splitting) is missing.
- Convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides a bound with constants L, D1, D2 but no characterization of these constants for the problem at hand; without bounds on D1/D2, the result is hard to interpret. Moreover, claiming “optimal” rates needs careful qualification and comparison to known results (e.g., Harchaoui et al. 2015; FW variants for norm-regularized problems).
- Identifiability is not discussed. With fixed σ, Λ0 and A may still be weakly identified (especially under low-rank regularization). No diagnostics (e.g., rank recovery, parameter variance) are shown beyond synthetic MSE curves.
- Modeling only self-excitation at the user–item pair level ignores cross-item and cross-pair influences (e.g., substitution/complementarity), which are critical in many recommender settings. The paper mentions extensions but does not evaluate them.
- Returning-time prediction via Monte Carlo thinning is computationally heavy; the paper omits the number of samples, variance, and runtime overhead per prediction, and does not compare to closed-form or quadrature approximations that are feasible for exponential kernels.

Algorithmic clarity and reproducibility
- Important implementation details are missing: step-size selection (ηk), Lipschitz constant estimation, stopping criteria, spectral computations (SVD backend, tolerance), sparsity/exploitation of the exponential kernel recursion, and memory footprint for 100M events.
- No wall-clock runtimes, hardware specs, or per-iteration complexities are reported; the claim “scales linearly” is only supported by a single plot without axes/units. Given the scale, this is a significant omission.
- The paper tunes ρ, λ, β “to give the best performance”; it is unclear whether a proper validation split was used or whether test leakage occurred. For baselines, the hyperparameter tuning protocol is not detailed (risking unfair comparisons).
- Stability regularization/constraints are not considered during learning; even if empirical intensities seem finite, this is a critical modeling omission.

Evaluation and baselines
- Ranking metric is “predicted rank of true item,” which is sensitive to catalog size and does not reflect top-k utility (HR@k, NDCG@k would be more informative). No calibration analysis of λ across items is provided.
- Using only SVD (count-based) and Poisson process (static) as primary ranking baselines is weak. TimeSVD++ and time-aware MF (Koren, 2009) are cited but not compared; dynamic Poisson factorization or recent point-process recommenders are absent.
- For return-time, the STiC baseline is acknowledged to be limited on new items; this leads to an unfair comparison on held-out pairs. Survival models with covariates or renewal processes could provide stronger alternatives.
- No likelihood-based evaluation (test log-likelihood under time-rescaling) is reported; MAE can be misleading for heavy-tailed inter-event times.
- On MIMIC II, disease progression is often mutually-exciting across codes; a self-exciting-only model is likely misspecified. A multivariate Hawkes baseline (e.g., with low-rank structure) would be appropriate and is already cited.

Presentation and minor issues
- Numerous notation/formatting glitches: inconsistent superscripts/subscripts (e.g., “α u,i,” “t u,i j”), spaces in math, missing parentheses, and cross-references (“feature ψu,i in 3”) that don’t align with equation numbering.
- Figure references (2a–f, 3) lack sufficient captions and quantitative details (axes units, confidence intervals). The paper text relies heavily on unshown figures.
- The claim “first of its kind” for achieving “the best of both worlds” is overstated given existing conditional gradient methods for norm-regularized problems.
- The “general nonnegative matrix rank minimization with other convex losses” claim is not substantiated experimentally.

Questions for authors
- How do you ensure Hawkes stability (αu,iσ < 1) during training? If not enforced, how often do you observe instability?
- Please clarify the derivation and correctness of the LMOψ step. Is your method an instance of conditional gradient splitting? Provide a rigorous mapping to known frameworks.
- How are ηk and L chosen in practice? What are stopping criteria? Provide runtime and memory statistics for all datasets.
- What is the sample size m for Monte Carlo return-time estimation? What is the variance of the estimator and its runtime impact?
- Can you provide comparisons to TimeSVD++ and multivariate Hawkes (with low-rank) baselines?
- How is hyperparameter tuning performed (separate validation vs test)? Please specify.

Recommendation
Reject. While the problem is important and the approach promising, there are too many methodological ambiguities (penalty equivalence, LMO design), missing constraints (stability), and insufficiently rigorous/transparent experimental details and baselines. With clearer theoretical grounding, stronger baselines, stability guarantees, and thorough reproducibility details, this work could be much stronger.
