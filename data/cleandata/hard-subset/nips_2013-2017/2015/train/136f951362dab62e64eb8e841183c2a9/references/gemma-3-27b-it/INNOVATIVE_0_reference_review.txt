This paper presents a novel and promising approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, framing the problem through the lens of low-rank Hawkes processes. The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is a key contribution, and the proposed optimization algorithm, blending proximal and conditional gradients, addresses the practical challenges of scaling to large datasets. While the validation is somewhat limited in scope, the boldness of the idea and potential impact are significant.

**Summary:** The authors tackle the problem of dynamically adapting recommendations to user activity patterns, moving beyond static preference prediction. They model user interactions as a Hawkes process, capturing temporal dependencies, and leverage low-rank approximations to generalize to unseen user-item pairs. The core innovation lies in the convex formulation and the efficient algorithm for solving the resulting optimization problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of low-rank Hawkes processes to recommendation is a genuinely new idea. Combining point process modeling with matrix factorization techniques offers a powerful framework for capturing temporal dynamics.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The convex formulation and the convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm (O(1/t) rate) are significant theoretical contributions. The algorithm blends established techniques (proximal and conditional gradient) in a clever way to handle the specific challenges of the problem.
*   **Scalability:** The authors claim, and demonstrate to some extent, scalability to millions of user-item pairs and large event datasets – a critical feature for real-world applicability.
*   **Generality:** The model's ability to incorporate contextual information is a strength, hinting at broader applicability beyond the core recommendation task.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and establishes the need for time-sensitive modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the experiments on both synthetic and real datasets are a good start, they lack comprehensive comparisons with strong, state-of-the-art baselines, particularly those specifically designed for temporal recommendation. The comparison to STiC is useful, but more thorough benchmarking is needed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance likely depends heavily on the choice of hyperparameters (λ, β, ρ, kernel bandwidth, rank). The paper doesn’t fully address how these parameters were tuned and the sensitivity of the results to these choices.
*   **Kernel Choice:** The choice of an exponential kernel is somewhat arbitrary. Justification for this choice and exploration of alternative kernels would strengthen the work.
*   **Real-World Data Complexity:** The real-world datasets, while relevant, are relatively limited in size and complexity.  Evaluation on larger, more diverse datasets (e.g., large e-commerce platforms) would increase confidence in the model’s robustness.
*   **Parameter Estimation Details:** While the algorithm is presented, a deeper dive into the practical aspects of parameter estimation – e.g., initialization strategies and computational costs – would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is highly innovative and has the potential to significantly advance the field of recommendation systems.
*   The theoretical contributions (convex formulation, convergence analysis) are strong.
*   The preliminary experimental results are encouraging, demonstrating the feasibility and effectiveness of the approach.
*   The model’s generality and scalability are appealing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is not extensive enough to fully demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing alternatives.
*   The hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis are not sufficiently detailed.
*   The real-world data experiments lack the scale and diversity needed to fully assess the model’s robustness.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in the area of time-sensitive recommendation. The novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical foundations, and the promising preliminary results outweigh the current limitations. The paper introduces a compelling new framework that could inspire further research in this area. While more extensive validation and a deeper analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial, the bold idea and potential impact justify acceptance. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above in future work. Specifically, a more thorough empirical evaluation with stronger baselines is crucial.
