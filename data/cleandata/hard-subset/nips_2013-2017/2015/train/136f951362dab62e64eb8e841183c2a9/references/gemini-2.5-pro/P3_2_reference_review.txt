This paper presents a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing two key tasks: recommending the right item at the right moment and predicting a user's next return time to an item. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. To generalize across all user-item pairs and handle data sparsity, the authors elegantly propose to structure the Hawkes process parameters—the base intensity (Λ0) and the self-excitation magnitude (A)—into matrices and enforce a low-rank structure via nuclear norm regularization. This effectively creates a collaborative filtering framework for temporal point processes. A significant technical contribution is a new hybrid optimization algorithm, combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, to efficiently solve the resulting convex but non-smooth optimization problem. The model is validated on both synthetic and large-scale real-world datasets, demonstrating its superiority over several state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its novel synthesis of two powerful concepts: temporal point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) for modeling recurrent events and low-rank models for collaborative filtering. This connection is well-motivated and provides a principled way to share statistical strength across users and items in a dynamic setting, which is a significant step beyond static matrix/tensor factorization models.

2.  **Important Problem Definition:** The work moves beyond traditional rating prediction to tackle the more nuanced and practical problems of "what to recommend now" and "when will the user return." These questions are highly relevant for modern web services, from e-commerce and media streaming to digital health, as argued convincingly by the authors.

3.  **Technically Sound Optimization:** The proposed optimization algorithm is a non-trivial contribution. Directly solving the nuclear-norm regularized problem with non-negativity constraints is challenging. The authors develop a clever alternating scheme that uses a fast proximal step (projection) for the non-negative parameters and an efficient linear minimization oracle (top singular vector computation) for the nuclear-norm regularized auxiliary variables. The provided O(1/t) convergence guarantee is strong and, as the authors note, could be of independent interest for related matrix rank minimization problems.

4.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The use of synthetic data effectively demonstrates the algorithm's ability to recover the ground truth low-rank matrices and showcases its linear scalability with the number of events.
    *   The experiments on three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, clinical data) highlight the model's practical utility and generalizability.
    *   The choice of baselines is appropriate, covering static (SVD), history-aware but non-collaborative (STiC), and temporal collaborative (Tensor Factorization) methods.
    *   The use of the time-change theorem (Figure 3) to validate the goodness-of-fit of the Hawkes process assumption is an excellent piece of model diagnostics that adds significant credibility to the work.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Simplistic Triggering Kernel:** The paper primarily relies on a simple exponential triggering kernel with a single, globally shared bandwidth parameter (`σ`). While this is a standard choice for tractability, it is a strong assumption. The influence of past events likely decays differently for different types of items (e.g., a movie vs. a news article) or users (e.g., a power user vs. a casual one). The authors mention the possibility of using non-parametric kernels but do not explore this. A discussion on the sensitivity to the kernel choice and the `σ` hyperparameter, or even a simple extension to learn item-specific `σ`, would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Practicality of the Penalty Formulation:** The equivalence between the original problem (Eq. 4) and the penalized formulation (Eq. 5) shown in Theorem 1 depends on choosing ρ > ρ*, where ρ* itself depends on the unknown optimal solution. In practice, this means `ρ` becomes another critical hyperparameter to be tuned via cross-validation. While this is a common practice, the paper could be more transparent about this limitation and perhaps provide some intuition or heuristics for setting `ρ`.

3.  **Scalability with Users/Items:** The scalability analysis (Figure 2(d)) focuses on the number of events. However, a key computational step in the conditional gradient update is the calculation of the top singular vector pair of an m-by-n matrix, which can be computationally intensive for very large `m` and `n` (e.g., millions of users/items). While much faster than a full SVD, its cost is not trivial. A brief discussion of the complexity with respect to `m` and `n` and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., stochastic methods) would be beneficial.

4.  **Clarity on Cold-Start Recommendation:** The low-rank model implicitly handles recommendations for user-item pairs with no prior interaction history (a complete cold-start scenario). For such a pair (u,i), the self-exciting term is zero, and the intensity is simply λ_u,i(t) = Λ0(u,i). The recommendation is thus driven purely by the learned baseline preference. This is a crucial aspect of the model's utility and deserves to be stated more explicitly in Section 3.4.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant and high-quality contribution to the field of recommender systems. It proposes a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound model that elegantly combines Hawkes processes with low-rank factorization to address the critical problem of time-sensitive recommendation. The development of an efficient optimization algorithm with convergence guarantees is a strong technical result in its own right. The thorough experimental validation on diverse datasets convincingly demonstrates the model's effectiveness and superiority over existing methods.

Despite minor weaknesses regarding the simplicity of the triggering kernel and the practical implications of the optimization formulation, the strengths of this work are substantial. The problem is important, the proposed solution is original and powerful, and the execution is of high quality. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. I strongly recommend acceptance.
