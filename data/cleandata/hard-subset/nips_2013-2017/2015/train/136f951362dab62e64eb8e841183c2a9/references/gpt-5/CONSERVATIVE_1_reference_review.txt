Summary
This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework based on self-exciting Hawkes processes for each user–item pair, with an intensity comprising a nonnegative baseline and a history-dependent excitation term. To transfer knowledge to unobserved pairs, the authors impose low-rank structure via nuclear norms on the matrices of baselines and self-excitation coefficients, yielding a (claimed) convex formulation. Because the combination of nonnegativity and nuclear norms is awkward for standard proximal methods, they introduce a penalty reformulation with auxiliary variables and present an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient/LMO (for nuclear norms), with an O(1/t) convergence guarantee. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) evaluate two tasks: time-sensitive recommendation (ranking at the test time) and return-time prediction (MAE), showing improvements over Poisson, SVD/tensor baselines, and a survival/HMM method (STiC). They also include a time-change diagnostic suggesting the Hawkes assumption better fits real sequences than i.i.d. gap models.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem (time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction) where temporal dynamics matter and standard rating/implicit-feedback approaches struggle.
- Uses a well-established modeling tool (Hawkes processes) with a transparent interpretation of long-term vs short-term effects.
- The optimization approach is practical: proximal projection for nonnegativity and FW-style LMO for nuclear norms is sensible and scalable.
- Convergence analysis is provided, and the algorithm seems to scale in synthetic tests; real datasets are nontrivial in size.
- The model-checking via time-change theorem is a nice touch, albeit anecdotal.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is overstated. Low-rank structure in Hawkes models and convex regularized estimation have been explored (e.g., Zhou et al., Du et al. on sparse/low-rank Hawkes; composite FW/proximal schemes are also well-studied). The “first of its kind” claim for mixing nuclear norm and nonnegativity with optimal rates needs tempering and broader citation.
- Theoretical gaps: no discussion or constraint enforcing Hawkes stability (branching ratio < 1, i.e., α∫γ < 1). Without it, intensities can explode and identifiability between baseline and excitation is problematic; the MLE can trade off Λ0 and A in undesirable ways.
- The penalty reformulation depends on ρ > ρ*, where ρ* is problem-dependent and not computable; in practice ρ is tuned “to best performance,” which undermines the equivalence to the original constrained problem and raises concerns about bias and reproducibility.
- Experimental baselines are limited. Missing are stronger time-aware recommenders (e.g., TimeSVD++, factorization machines with temporal features), point-process recommenders beyond independent per-pair Hawkes, and more recent sequence models. The tensor baseline is discretized; mutual-excitation Hawkes (across items/categories) or renewal processes are not considered.
- Evaluation choices are narrow. For recommendation, only the rank of the ground-truth item is reported (no Recall@k/MAP/NDCG), and candidate set assumptions are unclear. For return-time, only MAE is reported; no calibration or probabilistic metrics. Hyperparameter selection procedures are insufficiently described.
- Some clarity and reproducibility issues: notation is occasionally sloppy; key implementation details (partial SVD, power iteration tolerance, event processing and caching for exponential kernels, wall-clock runtimes) are missing. Claims of scaling to “hundreds of millions of events” are not backed by detailed runtime/memory comparisons against baselines.

Quality
Technically, the model and likelihood are standard and sound when the kernel is fixed and parameters are constrained; however, the absence of stability constraints and identifiability considerations is a significant omission. The optimization algorithm is reasonable and accompanied by a convergence bound consistent with existing literature. Empirical evaluation shows promising gains, but the baseline coverage and evaluation metrics are not sufficiently comprehensive to justify strong claims.

Clarity
Generally understandable, but the manuscript would benefit from:
- A clear statement and enforcement of the Hawkes stability condition.
- Precise discussion of identifiability between Λ0 and A.
- A more thorough description of optimization details and computational costs (per-iteration complexity, top singular vector computation, event processing).
- Clear, reproducible hyperparameter tuning (validation splits, search ranges).
- Standard recommendation metrics and clearer candidate-set protocols.

Originality
The application to time-sensitive recommendation with low-rank Hawkes is incremental relative to prior work on low-rank/sparse Hawkes estimation and temporal recommendation. The algorithmic blend of proximal and conditional gradient is a known recipe; the paper’s novelty is mainly in applying it to this particular decomposition with a penalty trick. Related work should be expanded and novelty claims toned down.

Significance
The problem is important; a solid, rigorously validated point-process baseline for recommendation would be useful. However, the paper’s current theoretical omissions and limited empirical comparisons limit its impact. With stronger baselines, stability constraints, and broader evaluation, its significance could increase.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you ensure Hawkes stability (α∫γ < 1 per pair)? Is this enforced during training? What happens empirically if it is violated?
2. Can you discuss identifiability between Λ0 and A, and provide ablations (e.g., A=0, Λ0 only; Λ0=0, A only)?
3. How is ρ selected in practice? Can you provide evidence that the penalized solution matches the constrained one (e.g., small ||Λ0−Z1||F, ||A−Z2||F)?
4. Please include standard recommendation metrics (Recall@k, NDCG) and stronger baselines (e.g., TimeSVD++). Can you evaluate against a mutually-exciting Hawkes baseline?
5. Provide runtime/memory and wall-clock comparisons; detail partial SVD computations and event-processing optimizations.

Pros
- Relevant problem and interpretable model.
- Sensible optimization strategy with convergence analysis.
- Promising results on multiple datasets.
- Useful model-checking diagnostic.

Cons
- Overstated novelty; missing citations to closely related work.
- Missing stability constraints and identifiability discussion.
- Penalized reformulation equivalence is not practically verifiable.
- Baselines and metrics insufficient; limited reproducibility details.

Recommendation
Leaning toward reject in the current form. The approach is plausible and potentially useful, but the paper needs stronger theoretical rigor (stability, identifiability), more comprehensive and fair empirical validation against established time-aware baselines, clearer evaluation metrics, and toned-down novelty claims with appropriate citations.
