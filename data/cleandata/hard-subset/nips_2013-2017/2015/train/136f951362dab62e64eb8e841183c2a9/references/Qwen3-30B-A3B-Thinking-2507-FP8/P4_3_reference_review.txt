

# Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to address two related problems: (1) recommending relevant items at the right moment, and (2) accurately predicting users' next returning time to services. The approach extends previous work on survival analysis and Hawkes processes, particularly addressing limitations in Kapoor et al. [12, 11] by enabling recommendations for new items rather than just previously visited ones. The paper makes a clear contribution by formulating the problem as a convex optimization task with nuclear norm regularization, and develops an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.

The paper builds on the theoretical foundation of temporal point processes (Section 2) and extends it to the recommendation context. The authors establish a connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models to capture user-item interactions, which is a novel approach in the recommendation systems literature. Previous work in this area has primarily focused on rating prediction [16, 23, 15, 3, 25, 13, 21] or static recommendation, while this work explicitly models the temporal dynamics of user behavior. The connection to matrix factorization approaches is well-motivated, and the paper properly distinguishes itself from related work by addressing the limitation of previous models in recommending new items.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical formulation of the problem, with proper theoretical grounding in point process theory. The convex formulation (Equation 4) and the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) are well-justified, with a clear convergence analysis (Theorem 2) proving the O(1/t) convergence rate. The authors demonstrate the algorithm's scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events) on both synthetic and real-world data.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The algorithms (Algorithms 1-3) are presented with sufficient detail to be reproducible. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the model's key concepts and experimental results.

3. **Novelty:** The core contribution of connecting low-rank models with Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation is novel in the recommendation systems literature. The proposed optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods represents a technical advance, with the authors demonstrating it achieves optimal convergence rates for both components.

4. **Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem with clear applications in personalized recommendation systems, marketing, and healthcare. The demonstrated ability to predict returning times has direct implications for optimizing ad bidding strategies and proactive healthcare interventions. The model's flexibility to incorporate various contextual information (spatial, textual, etc.) enhances its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Cold-Start Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates good performance on observed user-item pairs, it does not adequately address the cold-start problem for new users or new items, which is a significant challenge in recommendation systems. The paper's evaluation focuses primarily on observed user-item pairs, leaving this important practical consideration underexplored.

2. **Comparison with Recent Tensor Factorization Work:** The paper compares with tensor factorization methods (Section 5.1), but does not engage sufficiently with more recent NIPS work on tensor factorization for recommendation, such as [22, 25, 13, 21] mentioned in the references. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art tensor-based approaches would strengthen the novelty claim.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates scalability, it lacks a detailed analysis of computational complexity compared to competing methods. The authors claim "superb predictive performance" but do not provide explicit runtime comparisons with baselines.

4. **Synthetic Data Limitations:** The synthetic data experiments use a fixed bandwidth for the triggering kernel (σ=1), which may not represent the full range of real-world temporal patterns. The authors could have explored more varied synthetic scenarios to better validate the model's robustness.

**Conclusion:**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, the optimization algorithm is well-motivated with strong theoretical guarantees, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The work makes a clear contribution to the literature by addressing the limitations of previous approaches in handling new item recommendations and modeling temporal dynamics.

The main weaknesses relate to the limited exploration of cold-start scenarios and insufficient comparison with the most recent tensor factorization work. However, these do not significantly undermine the core technical contribution, which is well-executed and validated.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation, strong theoretical foundations, and compelling empirical results. It addresses an important problem with clear practical implications and advances the state of the art in recommendation systems.
