## NIPS Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user activity as a recurrent process. The authors propose a novel formulation leveraging the connection between self-exciting Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix factorization. They develop an efficient optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving a provable convergence rate. The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance in both item recommendation and returning-time prediction compared to several baselines.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon recent work in temporal recommendation, particularly that of Kapoor et al. [12, 11], who utilized survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models. However, the authors distinguish themselves by proposing a more generalizable framework that *can* recommend new items, unlike the aforementioned approaches.  The work also draws from the broader literature on Hawkes processes [4, 5, 6], low-rank matrix factorization, and optimization techniques like proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models appears novel, offering a fresh perspective on modeling user behavior.  The paper appropriately cites related work in recommendation systems, temporal point processes, and optimization.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a significant contribution.  It offers a principled way to capture both long-term user preferences and short-term temporal dependencies.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a convex formulation of the problem and a provable convergence rate for the proposed algorithm. This adds significant credibility to the work.
* **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the scalability of their algorithm to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events).
* **Empirical Results:**  The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets convincingly demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed method compared to several strong baselines, including Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.
* **Clear Writing & Structure:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The inclusion of Figure 1(a) and (b) effectively illustrates the problem setup and the proposed model.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to predicting returning times has practical implications for ad bidding strategies and proactive healthcare, making the work potentially impactful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While the paper is well-written, the technical details, particularly concerning the optimization algorithm (Algorithms 1-3 and the associated theorems), are quite dense and require significant effort to fully grasp. A more intuitive explanation of the core ideas behind the algorithm would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Selection:** The paper primarily focuses on the exponential kernel for the triggering function. While justified, a more thorough exploration of different kernel options and their impact on performance would strengthen the work.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results show performance improvements with careful tuning of hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β). However, the paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to these parameters or provide guidance on how to select them in practice. This could be a challenge for users applying the method to new datasets.
* **Real-World Data Details:** While the real-world datasets are mentioned, more details about their characteristics (size, sparsity, data collection process) would provide valuable context for interpreting the results.  For example, how were the user-item interactions defined in the Tmall dataset?
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the authors compare with several baselines, the field of recommendation is rapidly evolving. A comparison against more recent state-of-the-art methods, particularly those employing deep learning approaches, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed method.
* The work has practical relevance and potential impact in various application domains.
* The authors have addressed a challenging problem in a rigorous and well-documented manner.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the optimization algorithm might hinder adoption.
* The lack of exploration of different kernel options is a minor limitation.
* The limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity could be problematic for practical use.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to contribute to the field of recommendation systems. The authors present a novel and well-motivated approach, backed by solid theoretical analysis and compelling empirical results. While the complexity of the algorithm and the limited exploration of certain aspects (kernel selection, hyperparameter sensitivity) are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the optimization algorithm.
2.  Briefly discuss the potential impact of different kernel choices.
3.  Offer some guidance on selecting appropriate hyperparameters.
4.  Add more details about the real-world datasets used in the experiments.
5.  Consider including a comparison against more recent state-of-the-art recommendation methods.
