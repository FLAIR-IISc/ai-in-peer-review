### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing two key problems: recommending the right item at the right time, and predicting a user's next return time to a service. The core idea is to model the sequence of interactions between each user and item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. To address the inherent data sparsity of recommendation settings and enable generalization to unobserved user-item pairs, the authors make a key assumption: the matrices representing the base intensity and the self-exciting parameters for all user-item pairs are low-rank.

This formulation elegantly connects the theory of temporal point processes with low-rank models from collaborative filtering. The authors frame parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem involving the negative log-likelihood of the observed event sequences, regularized by the nuclear norm of the parameter matrices to enforce the low-rank structure. To solve this challenging optimization problem, which contains both non-negativity constraints and non-smooth nuclear norm penalties, they propose a novel and efficient algorithm that hybridizes the proximal gradient and conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) methods. They provide a theoretical convergence analysis for this algorithm. The proposed model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), demonstrating superior performance over several baselines on both item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The paper's primary strength is its originality. The fusion of low-rank models, a cornerstone of modern recommendation systems, with the theory of self-exciting point processes is a significant conceptual leap. This moves the field beyond static preference prediction or ad-hoc time-binning approaches, offering a principled, continuous-time probabilistic framework for modeling recurrent user activities. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing technique but a genuinely new way to frame the problem of dynamic recommendation, which could inspire a significant new line of research.

2.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical and theoretical importance. Predicting *when* a user will interact with a service, in addition to *what* they will interact with, is crucial for applications ranging from targeted marketing and ad-bidding to proactive healthcare interventions. The proposed unified framework addresses both questions simultaneously and elegantly.

3.  **Solid Technical Contributions:** Beyond the novel formulation, the paper makes a valuable algorithmic contribution. The proposed optimization algorithm, which blends proximal and conditional gradient methods, is tailored to solve the specific structure of their optimization problem (non-negativity plus nuclear norm regularization). This avoids the prohibitively expensive full SVD computations required by standard proximal methods for the nuclear norm, making the approach scalable. The accompanying convergence analysis, demonstrating optimal rates for its respective components, provides strong theoretical grounding for the algorithm and could be of independent interest to the optimization community.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clear, and logically structured. It effectively motivates the problem, builds the model from first principles, explains the algorithmic solution, and presents compelling experimental results. The connection between the learned intensity function and the two target tasks (item ranking and time prediction) is made explicit and intuitive.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Model Simplicity:** The paper relies on an exponential triggering kernel for the Hawkes process. While this choice is justified for tractability and leads to a convex formulation, it represents a strong assumption about the temporal decay of influence. The authors mention that other kernels are possible, but the experimental validation is limited to this simple form. Real-world influence patterns could be more complex (e.g., non-monotonic or heavy-tailed), and it is unclear how the model's performance would be affected by this simplification.

2.  **Incomplete Experimental Validation:** While the experiments show strong results against the chosen baselines, the validation could be strengthened.
    *   The sensitivity to key hyperparameters, particularly the penalty parameter `ρ` which connects the original and alternative formulations, is not explored. Theorem 1 provides a theoretical condition for `ρ` but acknowledges that the threshold is difficult to estimate in practice. An empirical study of how performance varies with `ρ` would make the results more robust.
    *   The evaluation of item recommendation is based on ranking items at the exact timestamp of a held-out event. It would be insightful to understand how the model's rankings evolve over time and how they perform if evaluated at randomly sampled future timestamps, not just at event times.

3.  **Scalability Claims:** The algorithm is presented as highly scalable, and the linear scaling plot on synthetic data is promising. However, the core of the conditional gradient step requires computing the top singular vector pair of a dense gradient matrix. While much cheaper than a full SVD, this can still be a bottleneck for truly massive user/item catalogs (e.g., tens of millions). The experiments, while impressive in the number of events, are on matrices of moderate size. The practical scalability to industrial-scale recommender systems remains an open question.

### Justification for Rating

Despite some weaknesses in the completeness of the experimental validation, this paper presents a highly novel, elegant, and significant contribution. The core idea of modeling time-sensitive recommendations via low-rank Hawkes processes represents a bold and exciting new direction for the field. This is precisely the kind of work NIPS should champion: it prioritizes a powerful new concept over perfecting every minor methodological detail. The weaknesses identified are not fundamental flaws but rather avenues for future work that will surely build upon this strong foundation. The paper's technical contributions, both in the model formulation and the supporting optimization algorithm, are sound and well-executed. The potential impact of this work is substantial, offering a new, principled lens through which to view and solve dynamic user modeling problems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
