Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses two related problems: (1) time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given time), and (2) predicting the user’s next return time to an item. It models each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = λ0(u,i) + α(u,i)∑j γ(t, tu,ij), using an exponential triggering kernel. Generalization across unobserved pairs is achieved by assuming the base-intensity matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A are low-rank and learned via nuclear norm regularization under nonnegativity constraints. The main optimization contribution is a convex formulation with auxiliary variables and a quadratic penalty that decouples nonnegativity projections (handled by proximal gradient) from nuclear-norm regularization (handled by a conditional gradient/Frank–Wolfe LMO with top singular vectors), with an overall O(1/t) convergence guarantee; equivalence to the original constrained problem is claimed for sufficiently large penalty parameter ρ. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) suggest improvements over Poisson, SVD, a semi-Markov return-time model (STiC), and Poisson tensor factorizations.

The paper connects to a rich literature on temporal point processes and recommendation. Multivariate and Hawkes processes have been extensively studied (Cox & Lewis; Daley & Vere-Jones); in ML, learning Hawkes kernels and influence networks is well established (e.g., Zhou, Zha & Song, AISTATS/ICML). Low-rank structures in Hawkes parameters have also been explored for social infectivity networks. In recommendation, time-aware/dynamic MF (Koren), Poisson factorization, and tensor factorization have addressed temporal effects; more recent marked temporal point process models (e.g., RMTPP and Neural Hawkes) and sequential recommenders (FPMC, GRU4Rec, SASRec) offer strong time-sensitive baselines for ranking, while recurrent event survival models provide alternatives for return-time prediction. The paper’s primary novelty is a convex, low-rank Hawkes formulation tailored to user–item self-excitation with a hybrid proximal/conditional gradient algorithm.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation that unifies time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction via Hawkes intensities; intuitive decomposition of long-term preference (Λ0) and short-term self-excitation (A).
- Convex learning objective with nuclear norms under nonnegativity, and an optimization scheme that avoids full SVDs by combining projections with a Frank–Wolfe LMO; convergence analysis with explicit rates.
- Demonstrated scalability on large synthetic data and multiple real datasets; the hold-out protocol that includes entirely unseen user–item sequences is a meaningful stress test.
- Empirical evidence that Hawkes structure fits real event sequences better than i.i.d. inter-event time models (time-change QQ plot).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Modeling assumptions are restrictive: only self-excitation within a user–item pair is modeled; there is no mutual excitation across items (substitutes/complements) or across users (contagion). This limits the capacity to capture realistic dependencies (e.g., category effects in shopping or co-morbidities in EHR).
- Stability/branching ratio is not discussed. For exponential kernels, α(u,i)σ should be < 1 to ensure a stable Hawkes process; the optimization imposes nonnegativity but not stability. Without constraints or regularization toward stability, MLE can select explosive parameters.
- The equivalence theorem for the penalty formulation requires an unknown threshold ρ*. In practice ρ is tuned; guidance on sensitivity, duality gaps, or stopping criteria is lacking. It would be helpful to show that reasonable ρ yields solutions close to the constrained optimum.
- Empirical baselines are limited for NeurIPS standards. For time-sensitive ranking, comparisons to strong sequential recommenders (e.g., FPMC, HR@k/NDCG vs a rank-of-true-item metric) are missing. For return-time prediction, modern point-process baselines (RMTPP, Neural Hawkes) and multivariate Hawkes with low-rank or group structure are not included. Negative log-likelihood or proper scoring rules for timing (calibration) would be more informative than MAE alone.
- Algorithmic details that impact scalability are under-specified: cost to form gradients over events; use of sufficient statistics for exponential kernels; complexity per iteration for power iterations to get top singular vectors; wall-clock comparisons vs strong proximal-SVD variants or randomized SVD.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, but notation has glitches and some formulas are hard to parse. The pseudo-code omits several practical details (e.g., how to compute top singular vectors, step-size selection, stopping).
- The evaluation protocol is described, but reporting could be stronger: standard retrieval metrics for ranking; confidence intervals; ablations (effect of nuclear norm vs nonnegative-only; effect of the kernel bandwidth; sensitivity to rank).

Originality
- The low-rank Hawkes idea is not entirely new (prior work on low-rank/sparse Hawkes networks), but applying it to user–item self-excitation with two low-rank parameter matrices and a hybrid proximal/conditional gradient solver is a reasonable, if incremental, contribution.
- The optimization technique leverages known components (penalty-based splitting, nonnegativity projections, Frank–Wolfe for nuclear norms) with a specific coupling and rate analysis; this is useful but not groundbreaking.

Significance
- If validated against stronger baselines and with richer dependencies (cross-excitation), the approach could be valuable for applications that need both next-item ranking and return-time predictions. As it stands, the empirical evidence is promising but not conclusive for NeurIPS.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate stability constraints (e.g., α(u,i)σ ≤ c < 1) or penalize branching ratio to ensure well-posed Hawkes estimation.
- Add baselines: multivariate/low-rank Hawkes with mutual excitation; RMTPP/Neural Hawkes; strong sequential recommenders for ranking (FPMC, SASRec/GRU4Rec) and report standard top-k metrics (HR@k, NDCG@k).
- Evaluate timing with likelihood-based metrics and calibration plots, not just MAE; provide uncertainty estimates.
- Provide wall-clock and memory scaling vs ADMM with partial/randomized SVD and state-of-the-art nuclear-norm solvers; detail the complexity of top-SV computations.
- Explore cross-excitation across items (e.g., category-level kernels) or factorized intensity parametrizations λu,i(t) = a⊤u b i + c⊤u b i ∑j γ(·), which could remain convex under appropriate constraints and improve expressiveness.
- Discuss kernel choice and include nonparametric or multi-timescale kernels in experiments.
- Clarify the effect of ρ; report sensitivity and duality gaps; add ablations (no nuclear norm; only Λ0 low-rank; only A low-rank).

Arguments for acceptance
- Convex, scalable formulation with a practical algorithm and convergence guarantees.
- Unified treatment of time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction using a principled temporal point process.
- Promising empirical gains and diagnostic evidence that Hawkes fits real data better than i.i.d. gap models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to prior low-rank Hawkes and nuclear-norm optimization literature.
- Missing strong contemporary baselines and metrics; empirical validation not yet at NeurIPS bar.
- Modeling limitations (no cross-excitation, no stability handling) and under-specified algorithmic details.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has solid ideas and potential practical impact, but it needs stronger empirical comparisons, clearer algorithmic details, and more expressive modeling (or clearer justification of the current simplifications) to meet the NeurIPS standard. The authors’ contributions could be made significantly stronger by addressing the above points.
