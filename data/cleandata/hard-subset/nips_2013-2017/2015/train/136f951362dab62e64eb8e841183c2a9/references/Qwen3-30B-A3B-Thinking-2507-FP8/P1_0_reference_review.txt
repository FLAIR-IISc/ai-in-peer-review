

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user activities as low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes. The authors address two key problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next returning time of users to existing services. They establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank matrix models, which allows them to capture both user preferences and temporal dynamics. The authors develop a new convex optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving an optimal O(1/t) convergence rate. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets (including music streaming, e-commerce, and healthcare data), demonstrating superior performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines.

The work builds upon temporal point process theory while making a novel contribution to recommendation systems by explicitly modeling the temporal dynamics of user activities rather than focusing solely on static preferences. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work, particularly the recent work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12] that uses survival analysis for return time prediction.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including the derivation of the convex optimization problem and convergence guarantees for their proposed algorithm. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with both synthetic and real-world datasets across different domains (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare). The paper carefully compares their approach with relevant baselines, including Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.

The theoretical analysis is well-supported: Theorem 1 establishes the equivalence between the original and alternative formulations, and Theorem 2 provides a convergence rate analysis with a clear interpretation of the result. The empirical results consistently support the theoretical claims, with the proposed method outperforming baselines across multiple metrics.

The authors demonstrate appropriate caution in their evaluation, acknowledging limitations such as the assumption of exponential triggering kernel and the need for parameter tuning. The paper includes a thoughtful discussion of the model's applicability to different domains, particularly highlighting the healthcare application with disease progression prediction.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and motivates the need for time-sensitive recommendation. The background section on temporal point processes is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the topic. The model description is clear, and the pseudocode for the optimization algorithm is well-presented.

The paper could benefit from:
1. More detailed explanation of the parameter tuning process (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth) in practice
2. A clearer illustration of how the model captures different temporal patterns (beyond the exponential kernel)
3. More explicit discussion about the practical implications of the low-rank assumption

The figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively demonstrate the convergence behavior and scalability of the proposed algorithm. The real-world evaluation on healthcare data (MIMIC II) is particularly compelling and well-explained.

## Originality

This work is highly original in its approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The connection between low-rank matrix models and self-exciting Hawkes processes is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance over existing work. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related contributions, particularly the recent work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12] that uses survival analysis for return time prediction.

The key distinction is that their model can recommend new items (unlike survival analysis which is limited to observed items), and it considers the full history of events (unlike survival analysis which assumes i.i.d. inter-event times). This is a significant conceptual difference that enables broader applicability.

The optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods is also novel and represents a significant contribution to the field of convex optimization. The authors successfully demonstrate how their approach can be used for general nonnegative matrix rank minimization under mild assumptions.

## Significance

The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is highly relevant to real-world applications, including mobile services, healthcare, and marketing. The ability to predict when users will return to a service has clear business value for companies looking to optimize ad bidding strategies. The healthcare application described in the paper (predicting disease progression) is particularly compelling and demonstrates significant potential impact.

The paper advances the state of the art in recommendation systems by explicitly modeling temporal dynamics, which is often overlooked in existing work. The experimental results demonstrate superior performance compared to baselines across multiple datasets and tasks, suggesting that the approach is both theoretically sound and practically useful.

## Strengths

1. Novel connection between low-rank matrix models and self-exciting Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation
2. Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple domains (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare)
4. Clear differentiation from related work, particularly the work by Kapoor et al.
5. Efficient optimization algorithm with optimal convergence rate
6. Demonstrated scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events)
7. Practical relevance with clear applications in marketing and healthcare

## Weaknesses

1. Limited discussion of how the model handles non-exponential triggering kernels (beyond the exponential case)
2. Some details about parameter tuning could be more explicit (e.g., how ρ, λ, β are selected in practice)
3. The low-rank assumption might be restrictive in some real-world scenarios where user-item dynamics are more complex
4. The paper could benefit from more intuition about how different parameters affect the model's behavior

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing a critical gap in modeling temporal dynamics. The theoretical foundation is strong, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the approach has clear practical applications. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions relative to prior work.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a unified framework that solves both time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction problems. The healthcare application alone demonstrates significant potential impact beyond traditional recommendation systems.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3.3, provide more details on how the model would handle non-exponential triggering kernels, perhaps with a brief example.

2. In Section 5.2, include a brief discussion of how the parameters (ρ, λ, β) were tuned in practice, including the range of values considered and how the optimal values were selected.

3. In the discussion of the low-rank assumption (Section 3.2), consider adding a sentence about potential limitations when the true underlying structure is not low-rank.

4. Consider adding a visualization of the learned intensity functions for different user-item pairs to provide more intuitive understanding of the model's behavior.

5. In the conclusion, briefly mention potential extensions to the model, such as handling multiple types of events or incorporating more complex contextual information.

## Final Assessment

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing a critical gap in modeling temporal dynamics. The work is well-executed, with solid theoretical foundations, comprehensive experimental evaluation, and clear practical applications. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and has the potential to influence both research and practice in recommendation systems.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem with meaningful applications. It is a worthy contribution to NIPS and deserves acceptance.
