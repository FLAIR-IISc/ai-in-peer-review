Summary
The paper studies time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with exponential kernel. To transfer knowledge to unobserved pairs, the base intensity Λ0 and self-excitation amplitude A are constrained to be low-rank via nuclear norms. The authors propose a convex formulation with nonnegativity constraints and nuclear norm penalties, then introduce a penalty-splitting surrogate that allows handling nonnegativity via a proximal step and nuclear norms via a conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) step. They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate for the composite method and present experiments on synthetic data, last.fm streams, Tmall clicks, and MIMIC-II diagnoses for (i) top-k recommendations based on current intensity and (ii) return-time prediction using Ogata sampling.

Relation to prior work
Hawkes processes for temporal events are well established; low-rank structure in Hawkes parameterizations has been explored (e.g., sparse/low-rank network Hawkes; nonparametric kernel learning). Nuclear-norm regularization and conditional gradient methods are standard. Using Hawkes for recommendation/return-time is related to survival/renewal modeling and dynamic matrix/tensor factorization. The paper’s main novelty is a particular convex, low-rank, nonnegative estimation for per-pair self-exciting Hawkes intensities and an algorithmic hybrid of proximal and conditional gradient tailored to the split formulation.

Strengths
- Poses time-sensitive recommendation in continuous time with a principled probabilistic model rather than ad hoc recency features.
- Convex estimation under nonnegativity with nuclear norm regularization is appealing; the per-pair log-likelihood is concave in the parameters, enabling global optimization.
- The hybrid optimization is simple to implement: proximal projection for nonnegativity and a top singular-vector LMO for the nuclear norm parts.
- Scalability results on synthetic data and applications to three real datasets, with a held-out-sequence protocol that partially tests generalization to unseen user–item histories.

Weaknesses
- Modeling limitations: Each user–item pair is modeled as a univariate Hawkes with only self-excitation via a single scalar Aui. There is no mutual excitation across items (e.g., artist-to-artist influence), no seasonality/periodicity, and no user/item features or time-varying baselines. Many real behaviors (music listening, shopping, disease progression) exhibit strong cross-excitation and temporal cycles; the current model cannot capture these.
- Transfer/generalization: Low-rank Λ0 and A help share statistical strength, but when a pair has little or no history, recommending “at the right moment” relies entirely on Λ0, i.e., a static baseline. The claims about cold-start and context integration are not validated empirically.
- Algorithmic novelty is incremental. Splitting the nuclear-norm and nonnegativity via a penalty and using proximal+FW is a reasonable engineering choice, but related composite and splitting schemes exist (e.g., generalized conditional gradient, proximal-FW variants, away-step FW). The “first of its kind” claim seems overstated without a more precise comparison to these literatures.
- Equivalence via ρ: Theorem 1 hinges on choosing ρ > ρ*, where ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice ρ is tuned; thus the equivalence to the constrained problem is not actionable. The convergence guarantees apply to the penalized problem, not the original constrained one, and practical guidance on ρ’s magnitude is missing.
- Baselines are weak or mismatched. Missing are multivariate Hawkes with low-rank/structured kernels, renewal-process baselines with shared parameters, strong temporal recommenders (e.g., time-varying Poisson factorization), and modern point-process RNNs. STiC is disadvantaged (cannot generalize to new items), and tensor baselines depend on discretization granularity. The study does not show statistical significance or confidence intervals.
- Return-time prediction uses Monte Carlo sampling to approximate an expectation, which is both noisy and computationally heavy. For exponential kernels, survival integrals are available and numerical quadrature or median predictions would be more stable.
- Experimental protocol details are under-specified: how hyperparameters are tuned (appears to be on test), exact metrics, and computational budgets. Several references are incomplete and figures are not included here to verify claims.

Quality
The core likelihood and convexity are standard and sound. The optimization method is plausible but theoretically tied to a penalized surrogate with an uncomputable threshold; the novelty is limited. The empirical section suggests improvements but lacks strong baselines, ablations, and statistical rigor.

Clarity
Generally readable, but there are typos and notation inconsistencies. Important details (proofs, exact algorithmic complexity, hyperparameter selection, implementation specifics for the LMO and power iterations) are deferred or omitted. Claims about extensibility are not backed by experiments.

Originality
Moderate. Modeling user–item Hawkes with low-rank parameters and using a hybrid proximal/FW solver is a combination of known ideas. The connection between Hawkes and low-rank models is not deeply new relative to prior low-rank/matrix/tensor temporal models and low-rank Hawkes network work.

Significance
Potentially useful framing for continuous-time recommendation, but the current model’s expressiveness and the empirical evaluation against strong baselines are insufficient to establish clear advancement over the state of the art.

Pros
- Convex formulation with nonnegativity and nuclear norms.
- Simple, scalable solver with top-SV oracles.
- Application to multiple real datasets; reasonable protocol including held-out sequences.

Cons
- Over-simplified per-pair self-excitation; no cross-item effects or seasonality.
- Penalized surrogate with unclear ρ; theoretical claims do not cleanly translate to the constrained problem.
- Weak/mismatched baselines; lack of significance tests and ablations.
- Limited algorithmic novelty relative to existing composite optimization methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare to multivariate Hawkes with low-rank excitation matrices and to strong continuous-time recommendation baselines (e.g., dynamic Poisson factorization, renewal models, recent point-process RNNs).
- Incorporate and evaluate periodic components and/or covariates (user/item features, time-of-day).
- Provide a clean constrained solver or principled strategy for choosing ρ, and report sensitivity.
- Use validation splits for hyperparameter tuning; report confidence intervals and runtime/memory.
- Evaluate alternative return-time estimators (median or numeric integration).
- Analyze interpretability of learned low-rank factors and perform ablations (only Λ0 low-rank vs only A low-rank).

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising, but the model expressiveness, algorithmic novelty, and experimental rigor are not yet at the NeurIPS bar.

Confidence: 4/5
