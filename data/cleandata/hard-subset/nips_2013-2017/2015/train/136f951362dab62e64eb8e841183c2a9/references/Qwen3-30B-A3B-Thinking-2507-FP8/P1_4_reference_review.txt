

# Peer Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between temporal point processes and low-rank matrix models to address two key problems: (1) recommending relevant items at the right moment and (2) predicting user returning times. The paper develops a convex optimization formulation with nuclear norm regularization, and an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence. The work builds on point process theory [4-6] and extends previous recommendation approaches [11, 12] that focus on predicting returning times but cannot generalize to new items.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-founded theoretical framework. The authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis proving the optimal O(1/t) rate of their algorithm (Theorem 2), which is a significant contribution. The experimental section thoroughly validates the approach on both synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), showing superior performance compared to strong baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization). The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility, and the authors properly address the limitations of previous work (e.g., survival analysis approaches cannot generalize to new items).

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm development to experiments. The mathematical formulations are precise, though the connection between Hawkes processes and recommendation systems could be more intuitively explained for readers less familiar with point processes. The authors could better motivate why low-rank structure is appropriate for user-item interactions. The experimental results are presented clearly with informative figures.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing a novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation. While there is related work on point processes in recommendation (e.g., [12, 11]), this paper uniquely addresses the generalization to new items while simultaneously solving both recommendation and returning-time prediction. The convex formulation and optimization algorithm are novel contributions that distinguish this work from previous approaches.

## Significance

The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is highly significant for modern web services where the timing of recommendations greatly impacts user engagement. The authors demonstrate scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events), making their approach practically relevant. The unified framework for both recommendation and returning-time prediction is a notable advancement. The optimization algorithm could also be valuable for other matrix rank minimization problems, adding independent interest to the work.

## Strengths

1. Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous convergence analysis
2. Novel connection between point processes and low-rank matrix models
3. Practical scalability demonstrated on large real-world datasets
4. Unified framework addressing two important time-sensitive recommendation tasks
5. Well-structured experiments with comprehensive comparison to strong baselines

## Weaknesses

1. The paper could better explain why Hawkes processes are appropriate for recommendation systems, particularly the intuitive connection between point processes and item relevance.
2. The cold-start problem for new users/items is not addressed, which is a critical issue in recommendation systems.
3. The comparison with related work could be more thorough, particularly regarding how this approach differs from recent tensor-based recommendation methods [22, 25].
4. The explanation of how the rank-based recommendation translates to real-world scenarios is somewhat unclear.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing an important but underexplored problem: time-sensitive recommendations. The theoretical framework is sound, the optimization algorithm is novel and efficient, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over baselines. The paper is well-written and technically rigorous.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a meaningful problem with practical significance
- Strong theoretical foundation with novel convex formulation
- Demonstrates significant improvements over baselines on multiple datasets
- Provides an efficient optimization algorithm with proven convergence
- Makes a clear connection between point processes and recommendation systems

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Could better explain the intuitive connection between Hawkes processes and recommendation
- Lacks discussion of cold-start scenarios
- Comparison with tensor-based recommendation methods could be more comprehensive

## Final Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems with a novel approach that addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical and empirical results are strong, and the paper is well-written. While the connection between Hawkes processes and recommendation could be more intuitively explained, this is a minor issue given the paper's technical merits and significance. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in time-sensitive recommendation and provides a valuable framework that could influence future research in this area.
