

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user activities as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to formulate the problem as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization. They develop an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) for two tasks: item recommendation and returning-time prediction. The work relates to previous time-sensitive recommendation approaches like Kapoor et al. [11, 12] but differs by addressing the challenge of recommending new items (not just observed ones) and using a convex formulation rather than survival analysis.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The convex formulation (equation 4) is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) rigorously establishes the O(1/t) convergence rate, which is a significant contribution. The optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods in a novel way that achieves the best of both worlds. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with appropriate baselines (Poisson, STiC, SVD, Tensor factorization) on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors carefully address limitations (e.g., the need for parameter tuning) and provide thorough ablation studies (Figure 2). The paper is technically complete, with theoretical analysis supported by empirical results.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The background on temporal point processes (Section 2) provides necessary context for readers unfamiliar with the topic. The mathematical formulations are presented with appropriate notation and well-explained concepts. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented effectively through figures (Figures 2-3). The writing is generally accessible to a machine learning audience, though some technical details in the optimization section may require familiarity with conditional gradient methods. The paper adequately informs readers about the methodology, making it reproducible for experts.

## Originality
The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by establishing a novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation. The convex formulation (equation 4) and the associated optimization algorithm represent a significant departure from previous approaches that used survival analysis (Kapoor et al. [11, 12]) or traditional matrix factorization. The authors explicitly differentiate their work from related literature, noting that previous approaches cannot generalize to recommend new items. The low-rank Hawkes process formulation is not merely a combination of existing techniques but represents a new conceptual approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The paper's connection to point process theory is well-justified and adds theoretical depth to the problem.

## Significance
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in recommendation systems: delivering personalized experiences at the right moment rather than just predicting ratings. The applications in marketing, mobile services, and healthcare (MIMIC II) demonstrate practical significance. The model's ability to incorporate contextual information (spatial, textual, etc.) through the intensity function enhances its applicability. The scalability to "millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events" is a significant practical contribution. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a unified framework for both recommendation and returning-time prediction, addressing a gap in existing literature that focuses primarily on one-time preference prediction.

## Strengths
- Novel connection between point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Rigorous theoretical convergence analysis with optimal O(1/t) rate
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on multiple datasets
- Practical scalability demonstrated on large datasets
- Ability to incorporate contextual information for real-world applications

## Weaknesses
- The choice of exponential triggering kernel lacks justification compared to other alternatives (Weibull, Log-logistic) mentioned in Section 3.3
- The comparison with related work could be strengthened; for instance, the paper mentions "state-of-the-art" performance but doesn't compare with the most recent point process-based recommendation methods
- The real-world datasets, while relevant, are somewhat limited in size (e.g., MIMIC II has only 650 patients and 204 diseases)
- The paper claims the approach can be generalized to incorporate other contextual information but doesn't provide specific examples or implementation details

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation with a novel theoretical framework, rigorous analysis, and comprehensive evaluation. The technical soundness, clarity, and significance of the work meet NeurIPS standards. The minor weaknesses (kernel choice justification, dataset size) do not detract from the overall quality and novelty of the contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in recommendation systems by addressing a meaningful gap in modeling temporal dynamics of user activities.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical connection, strong technical contribution, comprehensive evaluation, practical significance.

Arguments against acceptance: Minor gaps in justification of kernel choice and comparison with most recent baselines.

This paper is recommended for acceptance at NeurIPS.
