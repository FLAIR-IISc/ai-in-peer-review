Summary
The paper tackles time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij). To share statistical strength across pairs and enable recommendations for pairs with few/no events, the base-intensity matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A are assumed low-rank and estimated via nuclear-norm regularization under nonnegativity constraints. The authors propose a convex formulation of the negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norms (Eq. 4), and introduce an alternative penalty formulation (Eq. 5) that decouples the nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity via auxiliary variables Z1, Z2 and a quadratic penalty parameter ρ. They design an algorithm that alternates between a projected gradient step on the nonnegative variables and a conditional-gradient (Frank–Wolfe) step on the nuclear-norm-regularized copies, with an accelerated scheme. They state a theorem claiming equivalence of the penalty and constrained formulations for sufficiently large ρ and a convergence bound of O(1/t) (with an O(1/t^2) component for the proximal part). Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) report improved ranking and return-time prediction compared to Poisson, SVD, a hidden semi-Markov baseline (STiC), and tensor Poisson factorization.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-explored setting: continuous-time, time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Sensible modeling choice: Hawkes captures short-term self-excitation while Λ0 captures long-term preference.
- Convex objective in Eq. 4 is attractive; low-rank structure across user–item pairs is intuitive.
- Algorithmic idea to mix proximal (for simple constraints) with conditional gradient (for trace norm) is interesting; top singular-vector oracles are practical in large scale.
- Experiments include both synthetic (with scale) and multiple application domains; use of time-change theorem to check model fit is welcome.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (theory and methodology)
- Theorem 1 (equivalence of quadratic-penalty formulation and constrained formulation for finite ρ) is highly nonstandard. Quadratic penalties are not exact penalties in general; exactness with a finite threshold typically holds for L1 penalties, not squared Frobenius norms. The given ρ* depends on unknown optimal solutions and differences of nuclear norms, and the statement lacks conditions under which exactness holds. This is the most serious theoretical concern; if Theorem 1 does not hold, the guarantees reported for the original problem are questionable.
- The LMO design is unclear. The paper writes LMOψ: minimize 〈p,Z〉 + ψ(Z) with ψ(Z)=λ‖Z‖*. This is not a linear minimization over a compact feasible set (as in standard Frank–Wolfe); it is a composite objective. The presented solution picks the top singular vectors and then performs a scalar line search (Eq. 6), which resembles a proximal step in a rank-1 subspace. The correctness and convergence of this surrogate oracle need justification and clearer derivation; citing [27] is not sufficient without showing that your setup matches their assumptions.
- Convergence result (Theorem 2) mixes O(1/k^2) and O(1/k) terms, yielding an overall O(1/k), which is fine. However, the text claims “best of both worlds” and “optimal O(1/t) convergence rate,” and suggests recovery of O(1/t^2) when the nuclear norm is absent. This is misleading: the stated bound is dominated by O(1/k); the accelerated O(1/k^2) holds only when the conditional-gradient block vanishes.
- Parameterization and likelihood: Eq. (3) writes log(wᵀφ) − wᵀψ with w=(Λ0(u,i), A(u,i))≥0. This is standard and concave in w, but positivity of wᵀφ must be ensured. With φ’s second component zero (no history) and Λ0 projected to zero by the proximal step, log(wᵀφ) is undefined. How is strict positivity enforced? An ε-offset or barrier is not mentioned.
- Modeling assumption mismatch: the motivation (e.g., “one disease could trigger another”) suggests cross-item excitation; the model is per-pair self-exciting only. This seems at odds with the claims about capturing inter-disease dependencies. Multivariate Hawkes with cross-excitation (e.g., Zhou et al. 2013/2012, which you cite) would be more appropriate.
- Return-time prediction via sampling from f(t|T) using Ogata’s thinning is computationally heavy at scale. The number of samples m and its effect on accuracy/runtime are not reported. For exponential kernels, one can often derive more efficient sampling/inversion schemes or approximations; none are discussed.

Clarity
- Numerous notation inconsistencies: Section 3.1 uses γ0 and α as scalars; Section 3.2 uses Λ0 and A as matrices; later λu,i0 appears. Be consistent and clearly define transitions. In Sec. 3.3, “feature ψu,i in 3 often has analytic forms” is confusing; ψ is defined in Eq. (3), not a feature.
- Algorithm 1/2/3 notational issues: Xk2 is overwritten (Xk2[Z1] first set to u1v1ᵀ then rescaled by α), which is confusing; better to use distinct symbols. The definition of Xk, Yk, Uk sequences and the role of δk require clearer exposition. L and Lipschitz constants for ∇f are not derived or bounded.
- Several undefined or overloaded symbols: T used both as horizon and total time; σ as kernel bandwidth but choice/tuning not systematically described; “feature” vs “vector”.
- Missing or incomplete implementation details: power method tolerance for top singular vectors, line search computation for α, stopping criteria, hardware, wall-clock times.

Originality
- The convex formulation with low-rank regularization is reasonable, but related work on learning low-rank multivariate Hawkes parameters (e.g., Zhou et al. AISTATS/ICML) is closely related; the difference (self-excitation per pair vs networked excitation) and the novelty of the mixed proximal/Frank–Wolfe approach should be more sharply contrasted with [27] and recent hybrid methods.
- The penalty-decoupling approach could be novel if correct, but as noted, the exactness claim is dubious.

Significance
- The application is timely and important; continuous-time recommenders are useful. However, empirical evidence is mixed: metrics are limited (average rank rather than recall/NDCG@k), and baselines do not include stronger modern temporal models (e.g., time-aware factorization with continuous-time decay, neural point processes). Claims about scaling to “hundreds of millions of events” are not substantiated by clear runtime plots and detailed settings; synthetic experiments mention 100M events but do not report training time or memory footprint.

Experimental concerns
- Data splits: it is unclear whether train/test splits are strictly chronological. If not, leakage is possible. Please clarify.
- Hyperparameter tuning: “tuned to give best performance” suggests test-set tuning; a validation split should be used and reported.
- Metrics: Only mean absolute error for return time and average rank for recommendations are reported. Include recall/NDCG@k and confidence intervals.
- STiC is not applicable to new items and is thus an unfair baseline for recommendation; for return-time, compare against renewal process models, Cox PH with time-varying covariates, or marked/multivariate Hawkes.

Minor issues and formatting
- Many typos and grammar (e.g., returningtime, “in present” → “in presence,” “Proceeedings,” “time-dependant,” “Acknowledge”).
- Incomplete references (missing years/venues, inconsistent capitalization).
- Eq. (6): define all terms precisely; show the derivation of the closed-form α. Specify constraints (e.g., ‖Z‖_* ≤ 1) if used.
- Figures referred to but not interpretable in text; axes/units not described.

Pros
- Convex, low-rank Hawkes recommendation framework.
- Interesting hybrid optimization idea with practical oracles.
- Broad applicability and some evidence of scalability.
- Clear motivation and continuous-time modeling.

Cons
- Theoretical core (Theorem 1) appears incorrect or at least unsubstantiated; LMO design lacks rigor.
- Notation/clarity issues and many inconsistencies.
- Experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines, robust metrics, and detailed reporting.
- Modeling assumptions misaligned with motivating cross-item dependencies.

Recommendation
Given the substantial theoretical and clarity issues, and the insufficient empirical support relative to the claims, I recommend rejection in the current form. Strengthening the theoretical foundations (especially the penalty equivalence and LMO justification), tightening the exposition, and expanding the experimental evaluation would make this a much stronger submission.
