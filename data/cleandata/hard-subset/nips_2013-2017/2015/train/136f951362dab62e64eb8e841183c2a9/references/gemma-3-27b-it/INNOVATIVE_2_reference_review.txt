This paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, framing both as a low-rank Hawkes process estimation problem. The core idea – connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models – is genuinely exciting and a significant departure from standard recommendation systems focused on static preferences. The proposed algorithm, blending proximal and conditional gradient methods, appears carefully designed and achieves a favorable convergence rate. The empirical validation, while extensive, is convincing, demonstrating scalability and superior performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets. This work tackles a very relevant problem (temporal dynamics of user behavior) with a surprisingly elegant and potentially powerful mathematical formulation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation is a truly novel contribution. This isn’t simply an incremental improvement; it's a different way of thinking about the problem.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convex formulation and convergence analysis (O(1/t)) provide a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm's demonstrated scalability to millions of user-item pairs is crucial for practical applicability.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The results consistently outperform existing methods on diverse datasets, validating the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Generality:** The ability to incorporate contextual information through the intensity function extends the model’s applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Kernel Parameterization:** While the paper mentions different kernel forms (Weibull, Log-logistic), the justification for choosing the exponential kernel isn't fully elaborated. A more thorough exploration of alternative kernels and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The process of tuning ρ, λ, and β is glossed over. More detail on the tuning strategy (e.g., cross-validation) would strengthen the empirical results.
*   **Real Data Detail:** The description of the real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) is relatively brief. More specifics on data preprocessing, the nature of the events, and potential biases would be helpful.
*   **Comparison Depth:** While comparisons are made to several baselines, a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of each baseline *specifically in the context of temporal dynamics* would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and represents a significant conceptual advance in recommendation systems.
*   The theoretical guarantees and demonstrated scalability address practical concerns.
*   The strong empirical results provide compelling evidence of the approach’s effectiveness.
*   The potential for generalization to incorporate contextual information is significant.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The relatively limited exploration of kernel parameterization and hyperparameter tuning.
*   The lack of extensive detail regarding the real-world datasets.
*   The possibility that minor methodological refinements could further improve performance, although this doesn't diminish the paper's fundamental contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a standout submission. While there are minor areas for improvement, the originality, theoretical soundness, and empirical success of the proposed approach are compelling. The work addresses a critical challenge in recommendation systems – capturing temporal dynamics – with a novel and mathematically rigorous framework. I believe this paper has the potential to significantly impact the field and recommend **acceptance** despite the minor flaws. The boldness of the approach and the potential for future research outweigh any concerns about methodological perfection. This is the type of work NIPS should champion – innovative, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated.
