Summary
This paper addresses time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with nonnegative baseline and excitation parameters, coupled across all pairs via low-rank structure. The authors propose a convex formulation with nuclear-norm penalties on the user–item baseline matrix Λ0 and excitation matrix A. To handle the combination of nonnegativity constraints and nuclear norms efficiently, they introduce auxiliary variables with a quadratic penalty and develop a blended optimization algorithm that alternates a projected (proximal) step for the constrained variables and a conditional-gradient (LMO) step for the nuclear-norm-regularized auxiliaries. They state an O(1/t) convergence rate and demonstrate empirical performance on synthetic data and three real-world datasets, reporting improvements in item ranking at test time and mean absolute error for return-time prediction. They also present a time-rescaling check to argue that Hawkes better fits event sequences than simple survival models.

Relation to prior work: The use of Hawkes processes for temporal modeling is established, as is low-rank structure in point processes (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song on sparse/low-rank Hawkes networks) and nuclear-norm-regularized convex formulations. The application to recommendation and the particular convex parameterization (per-pair baseline and self-excitation with low-rank coupling) is a natural extension. Survival-based return-time prediction and temporal factorization baselines are discussed; however, several relevant point-process and temporal recommendation works appear missing or only superficially contrasted.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for continuous-time modeling of user activity and explicit handling of self-excitation; the modeling choices are reasonable and interpretable (baseline vs. short-term excitation).
- Convex estimation objective (negative log-likelihood with nuclear norms) is attractive and avoids local minima of nonconvex factorization approaches.
- The blended proximal/conditional-gradient algorithm is simple to implement with scalable oracles (projection to nonnegatives, top singular vectors) and is plausibly efficient.
- The paper attempts to validate goodness of fit using a time-rescaling diagnostic and evaluates both ranking and return-time prediction, including a more challenging held-out sequence setting.
- Synthetic study suggests the method benefits from more observations and scales roughly linearly with data size.

Weaknesses
- Novelty appears incremental. Low-rank Hawkes modeling and nuclear-norm-regularized convex learning for point processes have close precedents; the main novelty is the particular instantiation for recommender tasks and the penalty-based split algorithm, which is a fairly standard way to decouple constraints/regularizers. The theoretical claim of a finite-threshold exact penalty (Theorem 1) is unusual; without proof details and a practical way to choose ρ, the result is of limited value. In practice the authors tune ρ, undermining the claimed equivalence.
- Theory is not fully convincing. The O(1/t) rate relies on Lipschitz continuity of ∇f; for log(w^Tφ) terms on the positive orthant, gradient Lipschitzness typically requires lower bounds on w^Tφ (or bounded domains). Such assumptions are not stated, and no bound/estimate for L is provided. The combination claim (“best of both worlds”) conflates O(1/t^2) rates for smooth + proximal with O(1/t) for CG; here the overall bound is O(1/t), not matching the accelerated proximal rate even when the nuclear-norm part is absent unless the algorithm degenerates to pure proximal updates.
- Baselines are weak or incomplete for both tasks. For ranking: SVD and tensor Poisson regression with coarse discretization are relatively dated; stronger established baselines include timeSVD++, dynamic Poisson factorization, factorization machines with time features, and point-process recommenders (e.g., modulated or reinforced Poisson models). For timing: nonparametric Hawkes, renewal/Weibull with low-rank coupling, Cox models with covariates, and more modern marked point process models are absent. The paper compares to STiC only on timing and to a Poisson process (a popularity heuristic) on ranking; this does not convincingly establish state-of-the-art.
- Evaluation design lacks standard metrics and statistical rigor. Reporting “rank of the true item” without Recall@k/NDCG or paired significance tests makes it hard to assess practical impact. For return-time, only MAE is reported; calibration and uncertainty are not analyzed. The time-rescaling check is shown on a single sequence, not systematically aggregated across users/items.
- Practical clarity issues: computational complexity per-iteration and wall-clock/memory profiles are missing; details on top singular vector computation at scale are absent. The handling of the exponential kernel (recursive updates, precomputation) is not described. The effect of bandwidth σ and ablations (e.g., only Λ0 low-rank vs. only A, or Hawkes vs. Poisson with identical low-rank regularization) are not presented. Cold-start for unseen items/users is not addressed beyond a brief mention of possible feature extensions, which are not implemented.

Quality
- The core optimization and likelihood are technically sound under standard Hawkes assumptions, but the theoretical guarantees are underspecified (assumptions for smoothness, choice of ρ). Experimental comparisons do not yet support strong claims of superiority over established alternatives. Important ablations and runtime analyses are missing.

Clarity
- Generally readable with a coherent flow, but notation shifts (γ0 vs. Λ0) and some typos make parts harder to follow. Algorithms 1–3 leave out important implementation details (e.g., how to maintain low-rank Z iterates efficiently, power iteration parameters). Add standard evaluation metrics and more thorough experimental descriptions.

Originality
- Moderate. The combination of Hawkes with low-rank nuclear-norm coupling for recommendation is a reasonable synthesis, but close to prior low-rank Hawkes and convex point-process work. The algorithmic split is standard in spirit. The exact-penalty theorem, if correct and practically actionable, could add originality, but as presented it is not compelling.

Significance
- Potentially useful model class for continuous-time recommendation and timing, but current empirical evidence against strong baselines is insufficient. If augmented with stronger comparisons, calibration studies, and scalability evidence, impact would increase.

Pros
- Convex formulation with interpretable parameters.
- Scalable, simple algorithm with reasonable convergence analysis.
- Continuous-time treatment and explicit self-excitation improve fit over static/discretized baselines.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to existing low-rank Hawkes/convex methods.
- Theoretical claims rely on unstated assumptions; exact-penalty result is not practically instantiated.
- Baselines and metrics insufficient to establish state-of-the-art; missing ablations and runtime details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger, established baselines for both ranking and timing (e.g., timeSVD++, dynamic Poisson factorization, Cox/Weibull with low-rank factors, nonparametric/marked Hawkes).
- Report standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG), statistical significance, and calibration via aggregated time-rescaling tests.
- Provide wall-clock, memory, and per-iteration complexity; detail top singular vector computations and event-kernel accumulation.
- Include ablations (only Λ0 vs. only A low-rank; Hawkes vs. Poisson with identical regularization; sensitivity to σ).
- Clarify assumptions underpinning Lipschitz continuity and specify how ρ is chosen; if Theorem 1 is central, make its conditions and implications practical.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper has promising ideas and a clean convex approach, but the novelty is limited and the empirical/theoretical support is not yet strong enough against established baselines and standards.
