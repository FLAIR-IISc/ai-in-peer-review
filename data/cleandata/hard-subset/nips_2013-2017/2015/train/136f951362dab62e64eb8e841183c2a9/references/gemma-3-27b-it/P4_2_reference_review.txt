This paper introduces a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction by combining Hawkes processes with low-rank models. The core idea is to model user activity as a self-exciting point process, leveraging the temporal dynamics inherent in user-item interactions. A convex formulation is presented, and an efficient optimization algorithm, blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, is proposed with proven O(1/t) convergence. The approach is evaluated on synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating strong performance.

The paper builds upon recent work using temporal point processes for recommendation, specifically citing Kapoor et al. [12, 11]. However, it distinguishes itself by proposing a low-rank formulation that enables generalization to new items – a limitation of the cited work.  It also relates to broader areas of collaborative filtering [16, 23, 15, 3, 25, 13, 21] and tensor factorization [24, 33] while focusing on a more dynamic and temporally-aware model. The connection to Hawkes processes is well-established [4, 5, 6, 10], but applying this in conjunction with low-rank approximation for scalable recommendation appears novel.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:**  The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a novel contribution. The ability to generalize to new items addresses a key limitation of existing temporal models.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation and establishes convergence guarantees for the proposed optimization algorithm. The theoretical analysis, including Theorem 1 and 2, is a significant strength.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm is designed to scale to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events), as evidenced by the experimental results.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines.  The inclusion of diverse datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) strengthens the findings.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical concepts and the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithm likely depends on careful tuning of hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth, rank). While the paper mentions tuning, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity to these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Kernel Choice:** The paper primarily focuses on the exponential kernel. While justified, a broader exploration of different kernel functions and their impact on performance could strengthen the results.  Discussing the trade-offs would be valuable.
*   **Competition Detail:** While several baselines are included, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art recommendation algorithms, specifically those designed for dynamic user preferences (beyond just temporal models), could provide a more complete picture of the method's strengths and weaknesses.
*   **Practical Implications:** The paper focuses on the technical aspects of the model. A discussion of practical challenges and considerations for real-world deployment (e.g., cold-start problem, data sparsity) would enhance its impact.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** Further ablation studies that isolate the impact of the low-rank assumption and specific components of the algorithm could provide deeper insights into the contributions of each element.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The proposed algorithm is scalable and demonstrates strong empirical performance on diverse datasets.
*   The clear writing and comprehensive experimental evaluation contribute to the paper's overall quality.
*   The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a potentially significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The sensitivity to hyperparameters and the limited exploration of kernel choices are potential weaknesses.
*   The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art recommendation algorithms and a discussion of practical implementation challenges.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a strong contribution with significant potential impact. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical grounding, and the empirical validation make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above (specifically regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and the broader competitive landscape).
