

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors aim to address two problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next returning time of users. The key technical contribution is a convex formulation that combines self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models, along with a new optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.

## Strengths

The paper makes a reasonable attempt to connect temporal point processes with recommendation systems, which is a relevant and timely problem. The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate good performance compared to several baselines, particularly showing advantages in capturing temporal dynamics. The convergence analysis provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed optimization algorithm, which is a valuable contribution.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Inconsistencies and Errors

The paper contains several significant technical errors and inconsistencies that undermine its credibility:

- The convergence rate claimed in Theorem 2 is stated as O(1/t) but the actual bound is O(1/k²), which contradicts the paper's claim. The paper states "F(Y^k)−ÔPT ≤ 4LD₁/(k(k+1)) + 2LD₂/(k+1)", which is O(1/k²), not O(1/k) as claimed.

- In equation (4), the objective function uses "−1/|O| ∑ ...", which is unconventional for likelihood-based models. Typically, maximization problems use the negative log-likelihood, but the paper should clarify why they're using this form.

- The mathematical notation is inconsistent throughout. For example, "‖Λ₀‖* ≤ λ'" uses "6" instead of "≤" in multiple places, and spacing in equations like "λ u,i 0" is incorrect.

- The synthetic data description is confusing: "two 1,024-by-1,204 user-item matrices" would be 1,024×1,204 matrices, but the paper then states "1,024-by-1,204 user-item pairs," which is inconsistent with the matrix dimensions.

### 2. Lack of Clear Novelty

The paper claims novelty in "establishing an under explored connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models," but the connection appears to be a straightforward extension of existing work. The authors cite Kapoor et al. [11, 12] as the most related work but fail to adequately explain how their approach differs in a meaningful way. The use of low-rank structure for the intensity matrices is common in matrix factorization approaches, and the Hawkes process component has been used in other recommendation contexts.

### 3. Experimental Issues

- The real data description contains inconsistencies: "last.fm consists of the music streaming logs between 1,000 users and 3,000 artists. There are around 20,000 observed user-artist pairs with more than one million events in total." With 1,000 users and 3,000 artists, 20,000 observed pairs is plausible, but the average of 50 events per pair seems low for a music streaming service.

- The paper claims to "report the total mean absolute error (MAE) and that specific to the set of entirely heldout sequences, separately," but doesn't explain why this distinction is meaningful or how the heldout sequences were constructed.

- The paper lacks sufficient detail about hyperparameter tuning. It states "all model parameters ρ, λ, β, the kernel bandwidth and the latent rank of other baselines are tuned to give the best performance," but doesn't provide any details on the ranges or methods used for tuning.

### 4. Theoretical Weaknesses

- Theorem 1, which claims that the alternative formulation (5) has the same optimum as the original (4), lacks a clear definition of the threshold ρ*. The expression "ρ* = max{...}" is incomplete and doesn't specify the domain of the maximization.

- The paper claims to "establish an under explored connection," but the connection between point processes and recommendation systems has been explored in prior work, including Du et al.'s "Dirichlet-hawkes processes" (KDD 2015), which should be properly compared.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in recommendation systems and presents some interesting empirical results, it suffers from multiple technical errors, inconsistent notation, and insufficient justification of its novelty. The claimed O(1/t) convergence rate is contradicted by the actual convergence bound provided, which is a serious error. The experimental setup also contains inconsistencies that raise questions about the validity of the reported results.

The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the theoretical claims are undermined by the technical errors. While the proposed algorithm may be useful in practice, the paper's presentation fails to establish its novelty and technical soundness.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper contains multiple significant technical errors that fundamentally undermine its claims, and the novelty is insufficient for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting.
