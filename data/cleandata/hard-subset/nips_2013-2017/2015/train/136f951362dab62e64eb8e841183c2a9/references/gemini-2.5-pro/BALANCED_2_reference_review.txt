**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to both recommend the right item at a specific moment and predict a user's next interaction time with an item. The core idea is to model the sequence of interactions for each user-item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process. This captures both a static, long-term user preference (the baseline intensity) and dynamic, short-term influences from recent activities (the self-exciting component). To address data sparsity and enable generalization, the authors link this point process framework with traditional collaborative filtering by assuming that the matrices representing the baseline intensities and self-excitation effects across all users and items are low-rank.

The main technical contribution is a novel convex formulation for learning the model parameters, which involves minimizing the negative log-likelihood with nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. To solve this challenging optimization problem efficiently, the paper proposes a hybrid algorithm that combines proximal gradient steps (for the non-negative parameters) with conditional gradient steps (for the nuclear-norm regularized auxiliary variables). This approach cleverly avoids the need for expensive full SVD computations at each iteration. The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis and conduct extensive experiments on synthetic and three real-world datasets, demonstrating that their method outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines in both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Model:** The paper proposes a compelling synthesis of two powerful modeling paradigms: collaborative filtering (via low-rank assumptions) and temporal point processes (via Hawkes processes). This provides a principled and expressive framework for modeling recurrent user activities, elegantly unifying long-term static preferences and short-term dynamic influences. This is a significant conceptual advance over static recommendation models or simpler temporal models that use ad-hoc time decay functions.

2.  **Efficient and Scalable Optimization:** The optimization procedure is a major strength of this work. The original formulation with coupled non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm penalties is difficult to scale. The proposed alternative formulation and the hybrid proximal/conditional gradient algorithm are technically sound and well-motivated. By replacing the proximal operator for the nuclear norm with a linear minimization oracle (which only requires computing top singular vectors), the algorithm becomes scalable to large matrices, as demonstrated in the experiments. The accompanying O(1/t) convergence analysis provides strong theoretical backing.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and robust. The authors test their model on both synthetic data (allowing for controlled evaluation of parameter recovery) and three diverse, real-world datasets. The inclusion of two distinct tasks—item recommendation and returning-time prediction—directly supports the paper's primary claims. The choice of baselines is appropriate, covering static, temporal, and tensor-based methods. The use of the time-change theorem to validate the model's goodness-of-fit (Figure 3) is a particularly nice touch that adds significant credibility to the modeling assumptions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Optimization and Hyperparameters:** While technically sound, the description of the optimization algorithm in Section 4.3 could be made more intuitive for the reader. A clearer explanation of the roles of the three sequences (`U`, `Y`, `X`) and how the proximal and conditional gradient steps are combined would improve readability. More importantly, the paper lacks a discussion on the sensitivity to key hyperparameters. The penalty parameter `ρ` is critical for the equivalence between the two formulations (OPT and ÔPT), yet Theorem 1 only provides a theoretical condition on it. In practice, how was `ρ` chosen, and how sensitive are the results to its value? A sensitivity analysis or at least a discussion of the tuning strategy for `ρ`, `λ`, and `β` would greatly enhance the paper's reproducibility and practical value.

2.  **Justification and Exploration of Model Assumptions:**
    *   The paper assumes that both the baseline intensity matrix `Λ_0` and the self-excitation matrix `A` are low-rank. The low-rank assumption for `Λ_0` is well-understood in collaborative filtering as capturing latent user/item factors. However, the intuition behind a low-rank self-excitation matrix `A` is less clear. A brief discussion on the interpretation of this assumption (e.g., common patterns of influence or "excitability" across users and items) would strengthen the paper's narrative.
    *   The experiments rely exclusively on an exponential triggering kernel. While this is a common and tractable choice, its memoryless property might be limiting for some applications. The paper mentions that other kernels are possible, but a brief discussion on how other choices (e.g., power-law) would impact the model—specifically the computation of `ψ_u,i` in Equation 3—and potentially model performance would be valuable.

3.  **Experimental Details:**
    *   The paper reports that the algorithm scales linearly, which is excellent. However, providing wall-clock training times for the real-world datasets would give readers a more concrete understanding of the computational cost in practice. A comparison of training times against the most competitive baseline (e.g., Tensor factorization) would also be informative.
    *   The rationale for the "Tensor-#-Last" baseline is unclear. Using only the last time interval seems likely to perform poorly by discarding historical information, making it a potentially weak baseline. Clarifying its purpose or focusing on the more competitive "Tensor-#-Avg" variant might be better.

**Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance**

*   **Pros:**
    *   Novel and significant combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation.
    *   Technically strong contribution in the form of a scalable optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
    *   Comprehensive and convincing experiments on multiple datasets and tasks.
    *   The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in the field.

*   **Cons:**
    *   Lack of discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the penalty term `ρ`.
    *   The intuition behind some modeling assumptions (e.g., low-rank self-excitation matrix) could be better explained.
    *   Minor lack of detail in experimental reporting (e.g., wall-clock times).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of recommendation systems. The proposed model is elegant and principled, the technical solution is novel and sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are relatively minor and mostly relate to requests for clarification and additional discussion, which could be addressed in a revision. The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses.
