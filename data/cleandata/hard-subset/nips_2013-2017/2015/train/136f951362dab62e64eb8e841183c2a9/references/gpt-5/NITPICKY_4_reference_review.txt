Summary
The paper addresses time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item pair as a univariate Hawkes process with an exponential kernel. To transfer knowledge to unobserved pairs, the base rate Λ0 and self-excitation α are promoted to m×n nonnegative matrices with low-rank structure enforced via nuclear-norm regularization. The authors propose a convex penalized likelihood with nonnegativity constraints, then introduce auxiliary variables Z1, Z2 and a quadratic penalty to decouple constraints and regularizers. They design an algorithm that combines projected gradient (for the nonnegative variables) with a conditional-gradient-like step (for the nuclear-norm terms), and prove a convergence bound with O(1/t) rate. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improvements over Poisson process, tensor factorization, SVD, and STiC on ranking and return-time MAE.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Jointly modeling item ranking and return-time prediction in continuous time is important and underexplored in recommender settings.
- Modeling choice: Hawkes processes provide a natural way to capture recency/self-excitation. The decomposition into a base rate and short-term excitation is intuitive.
- Scalable learning: The proposed scheme attempts to avoid full SVD via a CG-type oracle and uses simple projections for nonnegativity, hinting at good scalability.
- Empirical scope: Synthetic with large event counts and multiple real datasets across domains; cold-start per-user held-out items is a challenging setting.
- Theoretical effort: Nontrivial attempt to analyze convergence under a composite objective with both nonnegativity and nuclear norm.

Weaknesses

Quality (technical soundness, evaluation)
- Exactness of quadratic penalty (Theorem 1): Claiming equivalence of the split formulation (with squared Frobenius penalties) to the original constrained problem for finite ρ > ρ* is, in general, false for quadratic penalties; standard exact-penalty results require nonsmooth penalties (e.g., ℓ1) and additional regularity. Moreover, ρ* is defined in terms of unknown optimal quantities, making the theorem non-operational even if true. The text admits tuning ρ by validation, effectively abandoning the equivalence claim.
- LMOψ step: Minimizing ⟨p,Z⟩ + λ‖Z‖* over Z is unbounded unless ‖p‖2 ≤ λ; the proposed procedure instead picks the top singular vector and performs a linesearch on α. This resembles generalized conditional gradient for norm-regularized problems, but the derivation is not clearly justified. It is unclear whether the produced iterate is a valid descent for the original proximal subproblem, and how this matches the assumptions in Theorem 2.
- Stability of Hawkes: No constraint or discussion ensuring αu,i ∫ γ < 1 (branching ratio < 1) for exponential kernels (∫γ = σ). Without this, intensities can explode; at minimum, the feasible set should enforce αu,i σ ≤ 1 − ε or add a barrier/penalty.
- Likelihood and gradients: The log-likelihood linearization into w⊤φ and w⊤ψ is plausible, but the paper omits derivations of gradients and careful handling of the observation window T; reproducibility suffers.
- Return-time prediction: Using thinning-based Monte Carlo without specifying number of samples m, variance control, or using an inverse-CDF sampler (available for exponential kernels) is suboptimal and potentially noisy. No ablations on m or computational cost.
- Baselines: Missing strong continuous-time baselines, e.g., low-rank/multivariate Hawkes network inference (Zhou et al. AISTATS/ICML) adapted to user–item pairs, or self-exciting survival models. Tensor baselines rely on discretization and are disadvantaged; STiC is acknowledged to be unable to recommend new items. Comparisons to point process methods that model periodicity/time-of-day are absent.
- Evaluation protocol: Hyperparameters (“ρ, λ, β, the kernel bandwidth and the latent rank of other baselines”) are “tuned to give the best performance,” but no separate validation split is specified, risking test leakage. Ranking is evaluated only by the rank of the true item; standard metrics (Recall@k, NDCG, MRR) and k are not reported. No confidence intervals. For held-out items, items are held out per-user, not globally; the method’s ability to generalize to entirely new items (cold-start globally) is not assessed.
- Claims vs. evidence: The abstract claims scaling to “millions of user–item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.” Only the synthetic experiment reports 100M events; real datasets are considerably smaller. Wall-clock, memory, and SVD/top-singular-vector iteration counts are not reported.

Clarity (writing, organization)
- Notation inconsistencies: γ0 vs Λ0; α vs A; typos like “feature ψu,i in 3,” inconsistent equation references; sometimes Λ0 and A are described as constrained by nuclear-norm balls, elsewhere penalized.
- Algorithm descriptions: Pseudocode is hard to follow (undefined operators, ambiguous concatenation X=[X1;X2], missing details for computing top singular vectors and step sizes, no stopping criteria).
- Figures/tables: The paper relies on figures (e.g., Fig. 2, 3) without reporting exact numbers; no error bars. It is difficult to assess effect sizes from the text alone.
- References: Many entries incomplete or malformed (missing years/venues/pages; stray commas; inconsistent styles). Some relevant work is only cursorily cited; more comprehensive positioning vs. low-rank Hawkes literature would help.

Originality
- The combination of low-rank structure over user–item Hawkes parameters with a convex (penalized) formulation is a reasonable idea but close to existing lines (e.g., low-rank/sparse Hawkes network estimation). The algorithmic blend of projection and conditional gradient is interesting but not convincingly novel given existing CG for norm-regularized problems; the theoretical contribution is undermined by the questionable penalty exactness and unclear LMO justification.

Significance
- The task is significant, and a solid, reproducible, and theoretically sound method could be impactful. However, the current weaknesses in theory, evaluation, and clarity reduce confidence that others can build on or adopt the approach. Missing baselines and metrics make it hard to conclude state-of-the-art improvements.

Specific suggestions
- Replace quadratic penalty with a scheme that guarantees equivalence (e.g., ADMM with exact proximal maps, or Dykstra-like projections for the sum of nuclear-norm and indicator of nonnegativity), or clearly state the split as an approximation and analyze its bias.
- Tighten the theoretical analysis: formalize the composite CG step (cite and align with Harchaoui & Juditsky 2015 or related) and provide a rigorous convergence proof under your exact update.
- Enforce Hawkes stability (αu,i σ < 1) and discuss its effect.
- Provide full gradient derivations, complexity, and runtime/memory reports. Include wall-clock vs. data size.
- Use a proper validation split; report Recall@k/NDCG/MRR with confidence intervals; detail m for Monte Carlo and consider inverse-CDF sampling for exponential kernels.
- Strengthen baselines with continuous-time Hawkes/tpp methods; include time-of-day covariates as an ablation, or temper claims about generality.
- Fix notation, typos, and references.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem (continuous-time recommendation and return-time).
- Intuitive Hawkes-based model with low-rank transfer across user–item pairs.
- Attempts a scalable optimizer avoiding full SVDs.
- Promising empirical results across several datasets.

Cons
- Theoretical issues (questionable exactness of quadratic penalty; unclear LMOψ and convergence justification).
- Missing stability constraints for Hawkes.
- Evaluation protocol and metrics insufficient; weak/missing baselines.
- Clarity, reproducibility, and reference formatting concerns.

Recommendation: Reject. Despite a compelling problem and some promising ideas, the paper’s theoretical gaps, evaluation shortcomings, and presentation issues are substantial. Addressing the above could make a much stronger submission.
