### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to answer not only *what* item to recommend but also *when* to recommend it. The authors propose a unified framework to tackle two related tasks: (1) recommending the most relevant item at a given moment and (2) predicting the user's next return time to an item/service. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with a specific item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The intensity of this process, which represents the likelihood of an interaction at time `t`, is composed of a constant baseline intensity and a history-dependent term that captures the influence of past interactions.

To generalize this model across all users and items and enable recommendations for unobserved pairs, the authors organize the baseline intensities and the self-exciting coefficients into two matrices, `Λ0` and `A`. They impose a low-rank constraint on these matrices, leveraging the common assumption in collaborative filtering that user preferences and item attributes lie in a low-dimensional latent space. This leads to a novel "Low Rank Hawkes Process" model. The parameter estimation is formulated as a convex optimization problem involving the minimization of the negative log-likelihood, regularized by the nuclear norms of `Λ0` and `A` and subject to non-negativity constraints.

Because this optimization problem is challenging due to the mix of non-smooth nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints, the authors propose a novel and efficient algorithm. Their method blends a proximal gradient step for the non-negativity constrained parameters and a conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) step for the nuclear-norm regularized auxiliary variables. They provide a theoretical analysis showing that this algorithm achieves an optimal `O(1/t)` convergence rate. The paper's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on both synthetic and three large-scale real-world datasets, where it outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines on both recommendation ranking and return-time prediction tasks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The paper establishes a compelling and previously unexplored connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for recommendation. This combination provides a principled way to model the recurrent, time-varying nature of user activities while still being able to generalize to new or unobserved user-item pairs, overcoming a key limitation of prior work like STiC [11].

2.  **Significant and Practical Problem:** The work addresses a crucial, yet often overlooked, aspect of modern recommender systems: temporal dynamics. Predicting *when* a user will engage is highly valuable for applications ranging from proactive user engagement and dynamic ad bidding to clinical event prediction in healthcare (as shown with the MIMIC II dataset). The proposed model provides a direct and effective solution to this problem.

3.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The paper is technically sound. The convex formulation is well-motivated, and the proposed hybrid optimization algorithm is a novel contribution. Developing an algorithm that efficiently handles both non-negativity and nuclear norm constraints is non-trivial. The accompanying convergence analysis (Theorem 2), which proves an optimal rate, adds significant theoretical weight to the work and may be of independent interest to the optimization community.

4.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and robust.
    *   The use of synthetic data effectively demonstrates the algorithm's ability to recover ground-truth parameters and its linear scalability.
    *   Experiments on three diverse, real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, healthcare) showcase the model's wide applicability and consistently superior performance against strong baselines (Poisson, STiC, SVD, Tensor Factorization).
    *   The use of the time-change theorem (Figure 3) to validate the underlying Hawkes process assumption is a particularly nice touch, providing strong evidence that the model is a good fit for the data's generative process.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of the Alternative Formulation:** The proposed optimization algorithm solves an alternative formulation (Eq. 5) that is equivalent to the original problem (Eq. 4) only when the penalty parameter `ρ` is sufficiently large (`ρ > ρ*`). However, as noted in the paper, `ρ*` is a problem-dependent constant that is difficult to compute. In practice, `ρ` becomes another hyperparameter to be tuned via cross-validation. While this is a standard practice, it slightly weakens the connection between the theory and the practical implementation.

2.  **Clarity of the Algorithm Description:** While the high-level idea of the optimization algorithm is clear, the presentation in Section 4.3 and Algorithms 1-3 is quite dense. A more intuitive explanation of the interplay between the `X`, `Y`, and `U` sequences and the motivation behind the specific update rules could improve readability for a broader NIPS audience.

3.  **Computational Considerations:** The conditional gradient step involves computing the top singular vector pair of a gradient matrix at each iteration. While significantly cheaper than a full SVD, this can still be a bottleneck for extremely large-scale systems with millions of users and items. A discussion of the practical wall-clock time compared to the baselines would have been a valuable addition to the scalability analysis in Figure 2(d).

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   The choice of the exponential triggering kernel is made for simplicity. While the authors mention that other kernels are possible, it would be interesting to know how sensitive the model's performance is to this choice and whether learning the kernel non-parametrically [30] would provide significant gains at a reasonable computational cost.
*   In the experiments, how were the latent ranks for the baseline models (SVD, Tensor) and the proposed model chosen? Were they all tuned to their optimal values?
*   For the Tensor factorization baselines, it's not entirely clear why using just the "Last" interval or the "Avg" over all intervals are the best ways to apply them for point-wise prediction. Could the authors elaborate on the rationale or potential limitations of this adaptation?

### **Recommendation**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound solution to an important problem. The strengths—a creative and powerful model, a novel optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees, and extensive, convincing empirical validation—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work significantly advances the state-of-the-art in time-aware recommendation and is likely to inspire future research in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
