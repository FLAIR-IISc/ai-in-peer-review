- Proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework modeling user activities as low-rank Hawkes processes capturing self-excitation and user-item preferences.  
- Formulates a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization for low-rank matrix estimation.  
- Develops a novel algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods with rigorous convergence analysis (Theorem 2).  
- Demonstrates scalability on large synthetic (100M events) and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II).  
- Uses Hawkes processes to model temporal dynamics, providing a solid theoretical foundation linked to point processes.  
- Convex formulation is well-motivated and avoids pitfalls of non-convex approaches.  
- Algorithmic innovation handles non-negativity and nuclear norm regularization and achieves optimal convergence rates for both components.  
- Claims ability to recommend new items, distinguishing it from prior survival-analysis approaches.  
- Fails to justify why the intensity λu,i(t) should be interpreted as item relevance for recommendation.  
- Baseline comparisons are inadequate: static SVD and unclear tensor baselines are unfair or improperly adapted to time-sensitive tasks.  
- Synthetic data generation details (how Λ0 and A were sampled and intensity defined) are insufficient for reproducibility.  
- Hyperparameter tuning procedure and scaling behavior for ρ, λ, β are not reported, hindering reproducibility.  
- Paper is well-organized with clear mathematical presentation and logical flow.  
- Implementation details and stopping criteria for optimization are missing, creating reproducibility gaps.  
- Results presentation (e.g., labels like "Total" and "Heldout" and survival analysis comparisons) is confusing and under-explained.  
- Contribution of applying low-rank Hawkes processes to recommendation is novel and relevant to the literature.  
- Convex nuclear-norm approach and hybrid optimization application to this domain are original.  
- Limited differentiation from prior Hawkes-process work (e.g., Zhou et al., Du et al.) and insufficiently explained novelty over Kapoor et al.  
- Practical impact is not demonstrated with business metrics, timing measurements, or scalability curves.  
- Lacks analysis of failure cases such as sparse histories or cold-start scenarios.  
- Some claims about scaling to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events are overstated without empirical timing/scalability evidence.  
- Overall recommendation: weak rejection due to the critical unanswered question linking intensity to recommendation relevance and inadequate baseline/comparison details.  
- Suggested improvements: rigorously justify intensity-as-relevance, adapt and explain baselines for time-sensitive tasks, detail synthetic data generation, analyze limitations/failure cases, and report hyperparameter tuning procedures.
