Summary
The paper addresses two coupled problems in time-sensitive personalization: (1) recommending the right item at the right time, and (2) predicting a user’s next return time to an item/service. The authors model each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i)∑γ(t,tu,ij), where Λ0 and A are nonnegative base and self-excitation matrices. To generalize from sparse observations and enable cold-start across user–item pairs, Λ0 and A are assumed low-rank and estimated via nuclear-norm regularization. This yields a convex estimation problem (negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norms under nonnegativity). To avoid expensive SVDs under nonnegativity, the authors introduce auxiliary variables with a quadratic penalty and propose an algorithm that combines a projected proximal gradient step (for nonnegativity) with a conditional-gradient/Frank–Wolfe step (for nuclear norms). They provide a convergence bound of order O(1/k) and show scalability to 100M events. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) compare against Poisson, tensor factorization (Poisson regression), SVD, and a survival/HsMM baseline (STiC), reporting better ranking-by-intensity and return-time MAE.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical Hawkes processes and convex MLE for point processes, uses nuclear-norm surrogates for low rank, and conditional-gradient methods for norm-regularized smooth convex optimization (e.g., Harchaoui et al.; Jaggi-style Frank–Wolfe). It is related to multivariate/low-rank Hawkes learning (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song AISTATS’13; nonparametric kernels Du et al., Zhou et al.), and to temporal/tensor factorization and time-aware recommendation (Koren; Xiong et al.; Chi & Kolda; Poisson regression tensors). It differs from survival-analysis approaches to return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.) by modeling recurrent, history-dependent events and enabling generalization to unseen user–item pairs via low-rank structure.

Strengths
- Technically sound convex formulation: for exponential kernels, the log-likelihood is concave in Λ0 and A, and nuclear norms yield a principled convex estimator with nonnegativity constraints.
- Algorithmic contribution: a practical blend of projected proximal gradient and Frank–Wolfe that avoids repeated full SVDs; per-iteration costs dominated by top singular vectors and first-order gradients; a clear O(1/k) convergence guarantee.
- Unified treatment of two application objectives: instantaneous intensity ranking for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction via the learned intensity.
- Scalability: convincing synthetic setup (100M events) and linear scaling plot; real datasets of reasonable size for this domain.
- Diagnostics: time-rescaling check suggests Hawkes fits better than simple survival models.
- Clear pathway to extensions (contextual features, nonparametric kernels, tensorizing contexts).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Exactness of quadratic-penalty reformulation (Theorem 1): Quadratic penalties are generally not exact for equality constraints; the claimed threshold ρ* depends on unknown optimal quantities, making the result hard to use in practice. Please clarify conditions under which the penalized and constrained optima coincide and/or provide an augmented Lagrangian/ADMM alternative with guarantees.
- Stability/branching constraints: For Hawkes with exponential kernel, stationarity typically requires A(u,i)∫γ ≤ 1 (e.g., A(u,i)σ < 1). No constraints or penalties are imposed to ensure stability; this can lead to degenerate intensity growth and ill-posedness. Please discuss whether stability holds empirically and consider adding constraints or regularization to enforce it.
- Modeling assumptions: Only self-excitation within each user–item pair is modeled; cross-excitation across items for the same user (or across users) is ignored. Many behaviors (e.g., artist-to-artist transitions) exhibit mutual excitation. At minimum, discuss this limitation and impact; ideally compare to a multivariate Hawkes baseline capturing cross-item excitation at the user level.
- Baselines: Comparisons omit recent time-varying Poisson factorization/dynamic matrix factorization approaches for implicit feedback (e.g., Gopalan et al., 2015; Charlin et al., 2015) and Hawkes-based recommenders/influence models. Including such baselines would strengthen the case that the proposed approach advances the state of the art.
- Evaluation metrics: For recommendation, only average rank of the ground-truth item is reported. Hit@k / Recall@k / MRR would be more standard and interpretable. For temporal modeling, held-out log-likelihood/time-rescaled KS tests across sequences would complement MAE.
- Return-time prediction via sampling: Ogata thinning per test case can be expensive; in many Hawkes settings, quantile-based root-finding on the survival function or bounds-based moment approximations are feasible. A note on computational overhead would be helpful.
- Hyperparameters and reproducibility: The paper states that ρ, λ, β, σ are tuned for best performance; more details on the search ranges, validation protocol, and sensitivity would improve reproducibility. Also clarify stopping criteria and wall-clock times.

Quality
Overall technically solid, with a reasonable convex formulation and a practical algorithm. The convergence analysis is useful, but the exact-penalty claim needs clarification. Empirical evaluation is positive but would benefit from stronger baselines and metrics, and from stability checks.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The optimization section is clear, with informative pseudocode. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., consistent vectors/matrices for X blocks; explicitly state the stability condition). Figures are referenced appropriately, though adding exact dataset sizes and wall-clock times would help. Minor typos exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
Combining low-rank modeling across user–item Hawkes intensities with a convex estimator and a hybrid prox/Frank–Wolfe algorithm is a novel and practical combination. Related low-rank Hawkes work typically targets network inference across dimensions rather than per-pair self-excitation for recommendation. The algorithmic blend is incremental but well-motivated.

Significance
Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are important problems; a convex, scalable approach that generalizes to unseen pairs is valuable. With stronger comparisons and clarified theory, this has potential impact in both recommender systems and event modeling.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify Theorem 1: conditions for exactness, practicality of choosing ρ, and whether ADMM with nonnegativity projections was considered.
- Do you enforce or monitor A(u,i)σ < 1 during training? What fraction of pairs violate stability at the solution?
- What are per-iteration costs and typical numbers of iterations/wall-clock times on real datasets?
- How sensitive are results to σ and to the choice of exponential kernel vs. other kernels?
- Can you report Hit@k and held-out log-likelihood, and add a dynamic Poisson factorization baseline?

Pros for acceptance
- Convex, scalable method with convergence guarantees
- Unified treatment of recommendation and return-time
- Strong performance on multiple datasets
- Practical algorithmic contribution avoiding full SVDs

Cons for acceptance
- Exactness of penalty reformulation not fully convincing
- Missing stability constraints and discussion
- Baselines and metrics could be stronger/more standard
- Limited modeling of cross-excitation

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The work is solid and promising, but would benefit from clarifying the penalty equivalence, adding stability constraints/analysis, and strengthening empirical comparisons and metrics.
