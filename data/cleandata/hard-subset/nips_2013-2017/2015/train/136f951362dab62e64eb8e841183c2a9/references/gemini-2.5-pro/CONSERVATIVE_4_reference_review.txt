### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to predict not only *what* item a user will interact with next but also *when*. The authors propose a novel model that combines self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization. For each user-item pair, a Hawkes process is used to model the event sequence, capturing a baseline preference (base intensity) and history-dependent excitement (triggering kernel). To generalize to unobserved user-item pairs and handle data sparsity, the matrices representing the base intensity and self-excitation parameters for all users and items are assumed to be low-rank. The authors formulate this as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization. To solve this problem, which involves both non-negativity constraints and non-smooth nuclear norms, they propose a new optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and provide a convergence analysis showing an O(1/t) rate. The model is evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets on two tasks: item recommendation at a given time and returning-time prediction.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem in recommendation systems that is often overlooked by traditional static models. The framing of the problem through the lens of point processes, specifically to predict both the item and the time, is compelling.
2.  **Modeling Approach:** The core idea of representing user-item specific temporal dynamics with Hawkes processes and using a low-rank assumption to share statistical strength across users and items is elegant. It provides a principled way to combine long-term static preferences (the base intensity) with short-term, history-dependent dynamics (the self-exciting component).
3.  **Optimization Contribution:** The authors identify a challenging optimization problem with a combination of a smooth loss, non-negativity constraints, and nuclear norm regularization. Their development of a hybrid proximal/conditional gradient algorithm is a non-trivial technical contribution. The accompanying convergence analysis lends theoretical soundness to the proposed solver.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** The central idea of combining low-rank assumptions with Hawkes processes is not entirely new. The work of Zhou et al. (2015) [29], which is cited, explores a very similar formulation for learning social infectivity in networks. While the application domain (recommendation vs. social networks) is different, the underlying technical machinery appears to be an adaptation of this existing framework. The paper could do a better job of clearly delineating its methodological novelty from this and other related works.

2.  **Methodological Soundness of the Optimization:** The proposed algorithm solves an alternative formulation (Eq. 5) of the original problem (Eq. 4). The equivalence between the two is established in Theorem 1, but this holds only when the penalty parameter `ρ` is chosen to be larger than a problem-dependent threshold `ρ*`. This threshold is defined in terms of the optimal solutions, making it impossible to know *a priori*. In practice, `ρ` becomes another hyperparameter that needs to be tuned, which complicates the optimization and weakens the claim of solving the original problem. This approach feels more like a heuristic to make the optimization tractable rather than a principled solution to the original formulation.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper.
    *   **Weak Baselines:** The comparison is made against relatively simple or outdated baselines. The SVD model is static and the Poisson model ignores history dependence. While tensor factorization is a reasonable choice, the paper does not compare against the vast and highly relevant literature on sequence-aware recommendation, particularly methods based on Recurrent Neural Networks (e.g., GRU4Rec) or self-attention (e.g., SASRec). While these models are typically not designed for continuous-time prediction, they represent the state of the art for the sequence-based *item recommendation* task and would provide a much more challenging and informative baseline. The absence of such a comparison makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the reported improvements.
    *   **Modest Performance Gains:** The empirical improvements on the real-world datasets, while consistent, are not substantial. For instance, in Figure 3, the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for returning-time prediction on the held-out set is only marginally better than the simple Poisson baseline. Given the significant increase in model complexity, one would expect a more dramatic improvement to justify the proposed approach over simpler, more established methods.
    *   **Synthetic Data Evaluation:** The strong performance on synthetic data is expected, as the data is generated from the model's own generative process. While this serves as a useful sanity check for the implementation, it provides little evidence of the model's effectiveness in real-world scenarios where the underlying assumptions may not hold.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the optimization section (4.3) is quite dense. A more intuitive explanation of why this specific hybrid algorithm is necessary and how the different components (Proximal Gradient, Conditional Gradient, line-search) fit together would improve readability.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

The paper presents a technically sound and interesting approach to the important problem of time-sensitive recommendation. The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models is conceptually elegant, and the development of a custom optimization algorithm is a solid technical effort.

However, the paper falls short in its positioning and empirical validation. The methodological novelty is arguably incremental when viewed in the context of prior work combining Hawkes processes and low-rank structures. More critically, the experimental evaluation is not convincing enough to demonstrate a clear state-of-the-art advance. The baselines are not representative of modern sequence-aware recommenders, and the performance gains over simpler models are modest. The complexity of the proposed model and its specialized optimization algorithm requires a higher bar of evidence for its utility, which has not been met in this work.

While the direction is promising, the current manuscript does not provide sufficient evidence to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The work would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison against stronger, contemporary baselines from the sequence-aware recommendation literature.

**Recommendation: Reject**
