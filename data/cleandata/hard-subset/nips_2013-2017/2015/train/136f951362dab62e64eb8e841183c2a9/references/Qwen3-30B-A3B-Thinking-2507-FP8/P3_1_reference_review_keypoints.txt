- Proposes time-sensitive recommendation via low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes modeling baseline preferences and triggering effects.  
- Introduces a convex formulation with nuclear-norm regularization and a hybrid proximal/conditional-gradient optimization algorithm.  
- Evaluates on synthetic and real datasets for item recommendation and return-time prediction, connecting Hawkes processes to recommendation systems.  
- Builds on temporal point process literature and relates to Kapoor et al.'s STiC, claiming broader generalization to new items.  

- Limited baseline comparison: omits recent neural temporal-recommendation methods (e.g., DeepHawkes, RNNs, Transformers).  
- Modest empirical gains: real-world improvements are small (≈5–10% MAE) and may not justify added model complexity.  
- No ablation studies to isolate effects of low-rank assumption, Hawkes vs Poisson, or kernel choices.  
- Scalability claim unsupported: real datasets used are relatively small compared to modern recommendation scales.  

- Generally well-written and organized with accessible background and clear model/algorithm exposition.  
- Notation inconsistent (e.g., Λ0 vs A) making connections to point-process literature harder.  
- Some technical details (e.g., proofs) are deferred to the appendix rather than included in main text.  
- Weak articulation of differences/improvements over closely related methods like Kapoor et al.'s STiC.  

- Novelty: combining low-rank matrix structure with Hawkes processes for recommendations is a meaningful, underexplored idea.  
- Claims about easy generalization to contextual information are not demonstrated with examples or experiments.  
- The optimization approach is interesting but the claim of being first to combine these constraints lacks comprehensive literature support.  
- Fails to acknowledge many recent neural approaches that could offer competitive alternatives.  

- Problem significance is high for real applications (personalization, health, ads) and multiple real datasets are used.  
- Practical impact is not quantified (e.g., economic benefit, engagement gains) so real-world usefulness is unclear.  
- No comparison to simple time-based heuristics common in practice.  
- Lacks case studies or deployment scenarios demonstrating concrete benefits.  

- Strengths: well-motivated problem, solid theoretical foundation, novel convex formulation, rigorous convergence analysis, and mixed synthetic/real experiments.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient SOTA comparisons (especially neural methods), small performance gains, missing ablations, limited-scale experiments, and unclear practical significance.  

- Overall recommendation: weak accept — technically sound but needs stronger empirical validation and clearer significance.  
- Suggested revisions: add comparisons with neural baselines, include ablation studies, quantify practical/economic impact, evaluate on larger datasets, and strengthen related-work comparisons.
