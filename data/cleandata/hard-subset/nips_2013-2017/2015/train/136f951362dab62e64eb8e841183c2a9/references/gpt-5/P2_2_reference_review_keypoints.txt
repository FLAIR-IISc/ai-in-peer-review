- Paper proposes low-rank convex Hawkes modeling for user–item intensities with nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity constraints to enable generalization and tractable complexity.  
- Introduces an auxiliary-variable penalized formulation and a block-splitting algorithm combining proximal (nonnegativity) and conditional-gradient (rank-1 updates) steps with O(1/t) convergence.  
- Demonstrates scalability to large event volumes (up to 100M events) and improved performance on ranking and return-time prediction across synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II).  
- Connects multivariate temporal point processes and low-rank recommendation, differing from prior work by offering a convex low-rank Hawkes estimator with an efficient solver.  
- Modeling is appropriate: Hawkes captures baseline preferences and short-term excitation; likelihood is convex under nonnegativity and nuclear norms are a standard low-rank surrogate.  
- Optimization is effective: auxiliary-variable decoupling avoids full SVDs, needs only top singular vectors per iteration, and the convergence analysis is plausible.  
- Experiments support scalability and estimation quality; Hawkes fits event dynamics better than i.i.d. inter-event assumptions per time-change QQ analysis.  
- Concern: paper omits discussion/enforcement of Hawkes stability (branching ratio < 1), risking explosive generative behavior or confounding of baseline and excitation.  
- Concern: kernel modeling is limited to a single global exponential bandwidth σ; per-user/item kernels or learned mixtures could improve flexibility.  
- Concern: baseline comparisons are lacking stronger time-aware MF methods (e.g., TimeSVD++) and alternative Hawkes estimators, weakening empirical positioning.  
- Concern: return-time estimation relies on sampling (Ogata) which can be expensive; analytic/quadrature approximations or timing vs. accuracy trade-offs are not discussed.  
- Concern: reproducibility gaps — missing per-iteration cost, caching details, number of CG updates, stopping criteria, runtimes, and memory footprints; code/pseudocode insufficiently detailed.  
- Clarity: paper is generally readable but has notation inconsistencies (e.g., scalar α vs. A(u,i)), and kernel/σ parameterization needs clearer exposition.  
- Originality: framing low-rank Hawkes for user–item intensities and the proximal+FW hybrid solver is a novel combination for recommendation/return-time prediction.  
- Significance: approach addresses important continuous-time recommendation problems; convex formulation and scalable solver have broader applicability.  
- Suggested experiments/questions: clarify how stability is ensured, report sensitivity to ρ and propose adaptive schedules, and provide runtime/memory and Ogata sampling costs.  
- Suggested comparisons/ablations: add TimeSVD++ and Hawkes baselines (ADMM/proximal), ablate nuclear norm on Λ0 vs A, vary rank-1 update budget, and test global vs per-dataset σ.  
- Suggested extension: discuss modeling mutually exciting cross-item effects, which are practically important.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled convex model, scalable algorithm with guarantees, and strong empirical results on large datasets.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing Hawkes stability discussion, weaker baselines, and clarity/reproducibility shortcomings.  
- Overall recommendation: solid and timely contribution with a lean toward acceptance pending addressing stability, stronger baselines, and expanded empirical details.
