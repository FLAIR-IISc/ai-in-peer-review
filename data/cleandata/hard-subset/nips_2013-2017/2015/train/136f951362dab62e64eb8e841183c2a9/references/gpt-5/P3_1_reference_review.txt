Summary
The paper addresses two tightly related problems in time-sensitive personalization: (i) recommending the right item at the right moment and (ii) predicting a user’s next return time to an item/service. It models each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with linear intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i)∑γ(t, tj), where Λ0 and A are nonnegative m×n matrices of base intensities and excitation coefficients. Generalization to unobserved user–item pairs is achieved by imposing low-rank structure via nuclear norms on Λ0 and A, yielding a convex estimation problem.

To solve it efficiently, the authors introduce an equivalent penalized formulation with auxiliary variables (Z1,Z2) and quadratic penalties, enabling a hybrid algorithm: proximal gradient on the nonnegativity-constrained block (simple projection) and conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) on the nuclear-norm-regularized block (top singular vector oracle plus line search). They establish an O(1/t) convergence guarantee for the composite objective and show, under a threshold on the penalty parameter ρ, equivalence with the original constrained problem. Empirically, on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II), the method reports improved item ranking at event times and reduced return-time MAE versus Poisson, survival/STiC, SVD, and Poisson-tensor baselines.

Strengths
- Clear, unified continuous-time formulation for both recommendation and return-time prediction, grounded in point process theory. Using the conditional intensity simultaneously as a ranking score and as a predictor for inter-event times is principled.
- Convex low-rank factorization via nuclear norms offers global optimality (for a fixed kernel) and a clean way to transfer to unobserved user–item pairs.
- The optimization method is well motivated: proximal updates handle nonnegativity cheaply; Frank–Wolfe avoids full SVDs for the nuclear norms. Theoretical guarantees are provided, and the acceleration scheme is reasonable.
- The paper demonstrates scalability on synthetic data with 100M events and shows gains on three domains, including a healthcare application, which speaks to potential impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
Modeling capacity and assumptions
- The model uses a very restricted Hawkes parameterization: per-pair scalars Λ0(u,i) and A(u,i) with a global exponential kernel bandwidth. It cannot capture inhibition/fatigue, cross-excitation across items, or periodicity (time-of-day/weekly cycles) often crucial for “right moment” recommendation. The baseline Λ0 is constant in time; thus seasonality must be absorbed indirectly by history, which is limiting.
- The claim of “recommending new items in future time” is not supported for cold-start items/users: without content/context features, nuclear norm regularization cannot predict for unseen items outside the training index set. The paper mentions possible extensions, but the experiments do not include them.

Theory and algorithmic novelty
- The penalty equivalence (Theorem 1) depends on an unknown problem-dependent threshold ρ*. In practice ρ is tuned. While common, the paper should clarify how sensitive performance is to ρ and whether suboptimal ρ induces bias relative to the original constrained problem.
- The hybrid proximal/Frank–Wolfe design is sensible, but similar ideas exist (e.g., conditional gradient for norm-regularized smooth objectives; block-coordinate and composite Frank–Wolfe; generalized forward–backward). The paper should articulate more clearly what is new beyond prior composite/conditional gradient schemes and how its acceleration differs from existing analyses.
- Smoothness/Lipschitz assumptions needed for the stated rates are not fully justified. For Hawkes log-likelihood, Lipschitzness of the gradient can be delicate when intensities approach zero; the paper should state the conditions under which L is finite.

Scalability and implementation details
- Per-iteration complexity and memory are not quantified. Gradients over hundreds of millions of events require careful recursion for exponential kernels; these engineering details are missing. Moreover, X1 = [Λ0; A] is updated via dense projected gradient; how is memory handled for very large m×n? Do the authors maintain sparse/low-rank structure, or does X1 become dense quickly?
- The Frank–Wolfe part grows an atomic representation for Z1, Z2. How many atoms are typically used at convergence? Is there any truncation/compression? This affects both memory and time.

Evaluation
- Baselines miss strong continuous-time competitors: multivariate/low-rank Hawkes models with shared kernels, renewal processes, or neural/parametric point-process recommenders (e.g., RMTPP, Neural Hawkes, or other marked point process models). Given the focus on point processes, comparisons to such baselines are important.
- The tensor baselines depend heavily on discretization; more discussion of binning sensitivity and the choice of the “# intervals” is needed. For recommendation, standard ranking metrics (Recall@k/NDCG) are preferable to mean rank.
- Return-time estimation via Monte Carlo sampling can be expensive; the paper should report the number of samples, runtime per prediction, and whether variance affects MAE. For exponential kernels some analytic or numerically stable quadrature might be more efficient.
- No ablation on the kernel bandwidth, low-rank regularization strengths, or the benefit of the self-excitation term (A) versus baseline only. Reporting learned effective ranks would help interpretability.

Clarity and presentation
- The manuscript contains several typos and notation inconsistencies. Some references are incomplete. A brief derivation of Equation (3) from the Hawkes likelihood and explicit gradient expressions would improve reproducibility. A discussion on how to compute the cumulative kernel terms efficiently is also warranted.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the kernel bandwidth σ? Is it shared globally or learned per user/item?
- Can you incorporate periodic components (e.g., additive Fourier terms) in λu,i(t) while keeping convexity?
- What is the typical number of FW atoms at convergence, and what is the memory footprint for the largest experiment?
- How do results change if A is allowed to be zero (no self-excitation)? This would quantify the added value of Hawkes dynamics.
- Can you report recall@k and training/inference wall-clock times?

Pros
- Principled continuous-time modeling and unified treatment of two tasks
- Convex low-rank formulation; scalable solver with theoretical rate
- Demonstrated improvements on multiple datasets; healthcare application

Cons
- Limited expressivity for real-world temporal patterns
- Incomplete baselines and evaluation metrics
- Missing complexity analysis and implementation details
- Unclear algorithmic novelty relative to composite FW literature

Recommendation: Weak reject. The direction is promising and the solver is useful, but the empirical baselines and modeling assumptions need strengthening, and clarity/novelty claims should be sharpened. With additional comparisons, implementation details, and modest extensions to the temporal model (periodicity, richer kernels), this could be a strong contribution. Confidence: High.
