

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user activities as low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes. The authors address two key problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next returning time of users to existing services. The core contribution is a convex formulation that connects self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models, along with an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.

The paper is well-motivated by the limitations of existing recommendation systems, which primarily focus on one-time user preferences (e.g., integer ratings) rather than time-varying preferences. The authors argue that user return times are a more relevant metric for user satisfaction than traditional feedback signals, which are often sparse and noisy. While related work by Kapoor et al. [12, 11] has explored predicting return times using survival analysis, the authors correctly identify that their approach is fundamentally different as it can generalize to recommend new items, whereas Kapoor's method is limited to observed items.

The theoretical foundation builds on temporal point processes, particularly Hawkes processes, which have been previously used for modeling event sequences in various domains. The paper makes a novel contribution by incorporating low-rank matrix structures to enable knowledge transfer across user-item pairs, which is a significant extension to the standard Hawkes process modeling.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-justified theoretical foundation. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is novel and well-supported.
- The optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is carefully designed with convergence guarantees (Theorem 2), which is a significant contribution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, showing the algorithm achieves the best of both worlds - optimal convergence rates for both proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic data (with controlled ground truth) and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II medical dataset). The results consistently show superior performance compared to strong baselines.
- The ablation studies (Figures 2b-2d) provide convincing evidence of the model's scalability and the importance of the low-rank structure.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm in practice. While the convergence rate is O(1/t), the actual time to convergence for large-scale problems (millions of user-item pairs) isn't thoroughly discussed.
- The comparison with related work is somewhat limited. While the paper correctly identifies the key difference with Kapoor et al. [12, 11], it doesn't engage deeply with other recent work on temporal recommendation systems, such as the work by Xiong et al. [25] on temporal collaborative filtering or Du et al. [8] on learning networks of heterogeneous influence.
- The real-world applications (e.g., electronic health records) are mentioned but not thoroughly validated. The MIMIC II dataset is used for evaluation, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how the approach could specifically impact clinical decision-making.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The progression from problem formulation to theoretical development to experimental evaluation is logical and easy to follow.
- The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout the paper.
- The figures (particularly Figures 1 and 2) are well-designed and effectively illustrate key concepts and results.
- The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection between the theoretical formulation and practical implementation could be slightly clearer. For instance, the paper mentions "the kernel bandwidth" as a parameter to tune, but doesn't explain how this is determined in practice.
- Some of the more technical aspects (e.g., the derivation of the convex formulation in Section 4.1) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with point processes or convex optimization.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The core contribution—using low-rank Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation—is highly original. The connection between point processes and low-rank matrix models for recommendation is novel and not previously explored in the literature.
- The optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods is a significant technical contribution, especially given the non-negativity constraints that make the problem challenging.
- The paper successfully addresses a gap in the literature: most recommendation systems focus on static preferences, while this work captures the time-varying nature of user preferences.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from other recent work on temporal recommendation. For instance, the tensor factorization approaches mentioned in Section 5.1 (e.g., [3]) also attempt to model temporal dynamics, and the paper could provide a more nuanced comparison.
- The use of the exponential triggering kernel is somewhat standard in Hawkes process literature, and the paper doesn't explore whether more sophisticated kernel functions could improve performance.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed is highly significant for modern recommendation systems, where timing is increasingly important for user engagement.
- The proposed framework has broad applicability across domains, as demonstrated by the three different real-world datasets (music streaming, e-commerce, and healthcare).
- The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence of the approach's superiority, which is crucial for adoption in practice.
- The optimization algorithm could be useful for other problems involving non-negative matrix rank minimization with convex losses.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the practical implementation challenges or the potential barriers to adoption in real-world systems.
- The healthcare application is mentioned but not deeply explored. The paper could have more explicitly demonstrated how the time-sensitive recommendations could impact clinical outcomes.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models for recommendation.
- The proposed optimization algorithm is novel and theoretically sound with strong convergence guarantees.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and consistently demonstrate superior performance across multiple datasets.
- The problem is highly relevant to current challenges in recommendation systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The comparison with related work could be more thorough, particularly with respect to recent temporal recommendation approaches.
- The practical implementation details for large-scale systems are not sufficiently addressed.
- The healthcare application, while mentioned, isn't deeply explored in the experiments.

## Overall Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The theoretical framework is novel, the optimization algorithm is well-designed with strong convergence guarantees, and the experimental results are compelling across multiple datasets.

The paper addresses a meaningful gap in recommendation systems literature and provides a unified framework that can be applied across various domains. While there are some minor weaknesses in the comparison with related work and the practical implementation details, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution.

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings, as it advances the state of the art in temporal recommendation systems and provides a theoretically grounded approach with strong empirical validation.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the related work section to include more recent temporal recommendation approaches (e.g., Xiong et al. [25], Du et al. [8]) and provide a more nuanced comparison.

2. Add a subsection discussing the practical implementation challenges and potential barriers to adoption in real-world systems.

3. Provide more detailed discussion of the healthcare application, particularly how the time-sensitive recommendations could impact clinical decision-making.

4. Consider including a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm in terms of time and memory requirements for large-scale problems.

5. Clarify how the kernel bandwidth parameter is selected in practice, as this is an important hyperparameter for the model.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The minor suggestions above would help further strengthen the paper before publication.
