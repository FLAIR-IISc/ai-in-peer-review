## NIPS Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation and next-event prediction by connecting self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank models. The core idea is to model user-item interactions as a Hawkes process, leveraging its ability to capture temporal dependencies, and then employ low-rank approximations to generalize to unseen user-item pairs. A proximal-conditional gradient algorithm is developed for efficient parameter estimation, and the approach is empirically validated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating strong performance in both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies Kapoor et al.'s work on return time prediction as the most closely related. However, it highlights a key distinction: the proposed model allows for recommending *new* items, a capability absent in the prior work which focuses on predicting return times for existing interactions. The paper also acknowledges the broader context of temporal point processes and collaborative filtering, drawing connections to related research in areas like tensor factorization and survival analysis. The authors appropriately position their work as bridging the gap between these areas by providing a scalable and generalizable framework for time-sensitive recommendation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for recommendation is a significant contribution.  It offers a principled way to incorporate temporal dynamics into recommendation systems, going beyond traditional static approaches.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The convex formulation and convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm are strong.  Demonstrating an O(1/t) convergence rate is a valuable result, suggesting good scalability.
* **Scalability:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the scalability of their algorithm to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events). This is critical for real-world applicability.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets are compelling. The model consistently outperforms baseline methods across both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.  The ablation studies (e.g., comparison to Poisson process) provide insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Generality:** The ability to incorporate contextual information (spatial, textual, user profiles) enhances the versatility of the model.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Selection:** While the paper mentions different kernel options (Weibull, Log-logistic), the justification for choosing the exponential kernel isn't fully elaborated.  A brief discussion on the impact of kernel choice on performance would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The sensitivity of the algorithm to the regularization parameters (λ, β, ρ) isn’t thoroughly explored. A more systematic analysis of parameter tuning would strengthen the results.
* **Real-World Dataset Details:**  The descriptions of the real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) are somewhat brief. More details about the data preprocessing steps and the characteristics of the datasets (e.g., sparsity, event rate distributions) would be helpful for reproducibility and better understanding the results.
* **Comparison Depth:** While the comparison to baselines is good, further comparing to recent state-of-the-art recommendation algorithms that *do* incorporate temporal information (beyond just the STiC method) would be valuable. For example, models based on recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or attention mechanisms are increasingly used for sequential recommendation.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Challenges:** The paper focuses primarily on the algorithmic aspects. A brief discussion of potential practical challenges in deploying such a system (e.g., cold-start problem, computational costs of real-time prediction) would be useful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* The theoretical analysis and experimental results are convincing.
* The scalability of the algorithm is a significant advantage.
* The potential impact of the work on areas like personalized recommendations, marketing, and healthcare is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of parameter sensitivity and kernel selection.
* The comparison to the state-of-the-art in sequential recommendation is somewhat limited.
* The lack of discussion of practical deployment challenges is a minor drawback.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of recommendation systems.  The proposed framework is novel, theoretically well-grounded, and empirically validated. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh them. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. I suggest the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding parameter sensitivity and comparisons to related work in sequential recommendation. Adding a section on potential practical challenges would also strengthen the paper.
