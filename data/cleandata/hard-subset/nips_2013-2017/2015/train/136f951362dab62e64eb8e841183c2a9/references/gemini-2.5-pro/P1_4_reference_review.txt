### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by jointly modeling *what* to recommend and *when* to recommend it. The authors propose a novel framework that models the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The intensity function of this process, which governs the likelihood of a future interaction, is composed of a constant baseline intensity (representing long-term preference) and a self-exciting term that captures the influence of past interactions (short-term effects).

The key contribution is to embed this point process model into a collaborative filtering setting. The authors arrange the baseline intensities and self-excitation coefficients for all user-item pairs into two matrices, `Λ0` and `A`, and assume these matrices are low-rank. This assumption allows the model to transfer knowledge across users and items, overcoming data sparsity and enabling recommendations for unobserved pairs.

To learn the model parameters, the authors formulate a convex optimization problem that minimizes the negative log-likelihood of the observed event data, regularized by the nuclear norms of `Λ0` and `A` to enforce the low-rank structure. Since standard solvers are inefficient for this problem structure (non-negativity constraints plus non-smooth nuclear norm), they propose a novel and efficient optimization algorithm. This algorithm cleverly blends a proximal gradient method to handle the non-negativity and smooth loss, and a conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) method to handle the nuclear norm regularization. The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis for their algorithm, showing an O(1/t) rate.

Finally, the paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation on both synthetic and three large-scale real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC-II). The proposed model, named Hawkes-Recommender, is shown to outperform strong baselines, including static matrix factorization, tensor factorization methods, and other temporal models, on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper establishes an elegant and powerful connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for collaborative filtering. This provides a principled way to model continuous-time event streams for recommendation, a significant step beyond traditional static models or methods that discretize time. The resulting framework is capable of addressing the dual problems of item recommendation and returning-time prediction in a unified manner.

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution in Optimization:** The paper makes a solid contribution to optimization. Recognizing the challenges of the proposed objective function, the authors develop a custom hybrid algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. This approach is well-motivated and avoids the expensive full SVDs required by other methods. The theoretical convergence analysis (Theorem 2) adds significant value, providing guarantees for the algorithm's performance. This optimization technique could be of independent interest for other problems with similar structure.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors use both synthetic data to validate the algorithm's correctness and scalability, and three diverse, large-scale real-world datasets to demonstrate its practical effectiveness. The comparison against a strong set of baselines, including state-of-the-art tensor factorization methods, is thorough. The use of the time-change theorem (Figure 3) to qualitatively show that the Hawkes model assumption is a better fit for the data than alternatives is a very nice and insightful piece of analysis.

4.  **High Significance and Potential Impact:** The problem of modeling the precise timing of user actions is of great practical importance for web services, marketing, and healthcare. This work provides a general and effective solution that advances the state of the art in time-aware recommendation. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers and could be impactful for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Exploration of Triggering Kernels:** The experiments rely exclusively on an exponential triggering kernel. While this is a standard choice and simplifies some calculations, the model's performance may be sensitive to this choice. A discussion on the implications of using more flexible or non-parametric kernels, and the associated computational challenges (e.g., the integral in `ψ_u,i` may no longer be in closed form), would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Practicality of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The model introduces several hyperparameters, including regularization parameters (λ, β) and the penalty parameter (ρ) from the alternative formulation. Theorem 1 provides a theoretical condition for `ρ` but does not offer a practical way to set it, as it depends on the unknown optimal solution. More details on the hyperparameter tuning strategy and the model's sensitivity to these parameters would increase the paper's practical utility and reproducibility.

3.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The presentation of the optimization algorithm in Section 4.3 is very compact. While clear to experts in this area, readers less familiar with the interplay of proximal and conditional gradient methods might find it difficult to follow the exact update steps. Expanding slightly on the intuition and mechanics of Algorithm 1 directly in the text could improve readability.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, technically sound, and significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems and temporal point process modeling. The fusion of Hawkes processes with low-rank collaborative filtering is elegant and effective. The work is supported by a novel optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees and a strong set of experiments. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate more to potential extensions and minor points of clarity rather than fundamental flaws in the work. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a clear advance on an important problem.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem with a novel and principled approach.
*   Strong technical contributions in both modeling and optimization.
*   The proposed algorithm is supported by a theoretical convergence analysis.
*   Extensive and convincing experimental validation on diverse, large-scale datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is limited to a single type of triggering kernel.
*   Lack of discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity and selection.

I believe the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It is an excellent contribution that is well-suited for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
