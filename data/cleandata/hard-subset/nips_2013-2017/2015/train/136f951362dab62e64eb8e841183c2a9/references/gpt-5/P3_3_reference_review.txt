Summary
The paper tackles time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an exponential kernel. To generalize to unobserved user–item pairs, the base intensity Λ0 and self-excitation coefficient A are learned as low-rank nonnegative matrices via nuclear-norm regularization. The authors propose a convex formulation and an alternative penalized formulation that introduces auxiliary variables Z1, Z2 to decouple nonnegativity and nuclear norms. They develop an algorithm that blends proximal gradient on the nonnegative variables (simple projection) and conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe-like) steps on the nuclear-norm variables using only top singular vectors, and provide a convergence bound of O(1/t) (with an O(1/t^2) component when the nuclear-norm term is absent). Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II) evaluate both item ranking at a given time and return-time prediction, and include a model-checking step via time-rescaling QQ plots.

Relation to prior work
The work connects point processes to recommendation, contrasting with (i) survival models for return-time that do not support recommending unseen items and often assume i.i.d. inter-event gaps; (ii) time-aware matrix/tensor factorization that models counts in time bins; and (iii) prior Hawkes learning for networks/infectivity (e.g., Zhou et al. AISTATS/ICML), which focus on multi-dimensional excitation. The paper’s novelty lies in combining Hawkes modeling at the user–item level with low-rank nonnegative structure and a convex estimator plus a hybrid optimization scheme.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Time-sensitive personalization and return-time prediction are well motivated across domains (media, commerce, healthcare).
- Modeling: A Hawkes process cleanly captures short-term recency effects atop a long-term baseline. The low-rank constraints provide a principled way to transfer to unobserved pairs and mitigate data sparsity.
- Convexity: The negative log-likelihood is convex in the nonnegative parameters due to the log of an affine positive function; adding nuclear norms preserves convexity. This is appealing vs. nonconvex factorizations.
- Algorithmic idea: Decoupling nonnegativity (prox with projection) from nuclear norms (rank-1 CG steps) is computationally attractive compared to full SVDs in proximal nuclear-norm methods. Using top singular vectors is scalable.
- Empirics: Large-scale synthetic experiments and multiple domains; the time-rescaling diagnostic shows Hawkes fits better than simple survival baselines; consistent improvements on both ranking and return-time tasks.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Conditional-gradient subroutine on penalized nuclear norm: The algorithm uses an LMO defined as argminZ〈p,Z〉+λ||Z||*. This is not a standard Frank–Wolfe oracle (which minimizes a linear functional over a compact convex set, e.g., a nuclear-norm ball). Minimizing a linear term plus a norm without a constraint can be ill-posed (unbounded below unless ||p||2≤λ) or reduces to a proximal step rather than an LMO. The paper’s workaround—taking top singular vectors and then a scalar line search—resembles generalized CG for atomic norms, but the exact objective and guarantees need to be carefully justified. As written, the LMOψ definition and Algorithm 3 are nonstandard and potentially incorrect without additional constraints or a duality argument. Please clarify the exact subproblem being solved, its well-posedness, and how Theorem 2 follows from Lan/Harchaoui-style analyses.
- Penalty reformulation equivalence: Theorem 1 asserts equivalence of the penalized and constrained problems for ρ>ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice ρ is tuned; thus the “exactness” claim is of limited practical value. Please discuss sensitivity to ρ, and whether the method is stable to under/over-penalization.
- Convergence claims: The bound mixes O(1/k^2) and O(1/k) terms and the text claims “optimal O(1/t)”. When both blocks are active, the overall rate is O(1/k). It would help to state clearly the conditions (smoothness constants, bounded domains) required, how the nuclear-norm piece is handled in the analysis (penalized vs constrained), and whether the iterates stay in a bounded set to justify Lipschitz constants.
- Baselines: Important missing comparisons:
  - Time-aware CF such as TimeSVD++ (Koren 2009) and dynamic Poisson factorization or Bayesian count factorizations for implicit feedback over time.
  - Multivariate or low-rank Hawkes methods that learn excitation across items or users (e.g., Zhou et al. AISTATS/ICML), which are close in spirit. Even if the proposed model is self-exciting per pair, a comparison to mutual-excitation (cross-item) would be informative.
  - Modern large-scale proximal methods for nuclear norm (e.g., proximal gradient with partial randomized SVD, PROPACK), to substantiate the claimed computational advantage over ADMM.
- Modeling limitations: The intensity for a new user–item pair with no history reduces to the baseline λ0; α plays no role. Thus “at the right moment” recommendations for entirely unseen items are driven solely by Λ0; any temporal personalization comes only from items with history. This limitation should be discussed, and the benefit of self-excitation for cold-start items is likely limited.
- Return-time prediction by thinning: For 1D Hawkes with exponential kernels, survival and density after tn have closed forms or are cheaply computable; resorting to Ogata sampling may be unnecessarily expensive/noisy. Please justify and compare to deterministic approximations.
- Reproducibility/clarity: Key implementation details are missing: step-size selection and L, method for top singular vectors (power iterations, tolerance), per-iteration complexity and memory footprint for large m×n, and hyperparameter sensitivity (λ, β, ρ, kernel bandwidth). Some notation is terse (e.g., w, φ, ψ definitions) and Algorithm 1 could be clarified.

Quality
The modeling and convex formulation are sound. The main technical concern is the correctness and clarity of the hybrid proximal/conditional-gradient steps for a penalized nuclear-norm objective, and the rigor of the convergence proof under the proposed LMO. Empirical results are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and more detailed runtime/memory reporting.

Clarity
Generally readable, but the optimization section needs more precise statements. Please define the LMO subproblem rigorously, include the exact assumptions for Theorems 1–2, and expand algorithmic details sufficient for reproduction.

Originality
The combination—Hawkes processes for user–item temporal dynamics with low-rank nonnegative structure and a convex estimator—is novel for recommendation. The optimization idea is a nontrivial variant of known proximal/CG methods, but needs clearer justification to be claimed as novel.

Significance
The problem is important, the approach is principled, and results suggest practical gains. If the algorithmic issues are clarified and stronger baselines added, this could influence future time-sensitive recommendation and point-process learning at scale.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated, principled model with convex learning
- Scalable algorithmic design (if rigorously justified)
- Solid empirical results across domains and useful diagnostics

Cons for acceptance
- Nonstandard LMO and convergence analysis need clarification/correction
- Limited baseline comparisons and ablations
- Some modeling limitations (no cross-item excitation, cold-start dynamics rely only on Λ0)
- Reproducibility details are sparse

Recommendation
Borderline. I lean toward rejection unless the authors clarify the optimization subroutine and provide stronger empirical baselines and implementation details. The paper has clear potential; a revised version addressing the above could be strong.
