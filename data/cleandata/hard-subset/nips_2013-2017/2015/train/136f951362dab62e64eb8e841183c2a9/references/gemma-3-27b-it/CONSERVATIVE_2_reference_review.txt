This paper proposes a low-rank Hawkes process model for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The core idea is to leverage the temporal dynamics of user-item interactions, modeled as a self-exciting point process, and to address scalability using low-rank approximations. While the problem addressed – modeling temporal dependencies in recommendations – is relevant, and the proposed approach builds on established concepts (Hawkes processes, low-rank modeling), I find the presentation and validation insufficient to warrant acceptance to NIPS at this time.

The paper suffers from a lack of novelty in its fundamental ideas. Hawkes processes have been applied to recommendation before (Kapoor et al., cited in the paper), and low-rank methods are ubiquitous in machine learning. The contribution, therefore, lies in the specific combination and convex formulation, along with the proposed optimization algorithm. However, the authors don’t adequately justify why this particular combination provides significant advantages over existing alternatives, especially given the increased complexity.  The claim that their method can “recommend any new item in future time” in contrast to Kapoor et al. is overstated. Survival analysis, while perhaps not explicitly designed for *recommending* new items, doesn’t inherently prevent predicting the probability of interaction with unseen items – it simply requires adapting the model to generalize over items.

Methodologically, the paper leans heavily on established techniques without providing sufficient insight into the practical implications of the choices made. The use of a nuclear norm penalty for low-rank regularization is standard, but the justification for its selection, and its impact on performance, isn’t thoroughly explored. The optimization algorithm, while presented with convergence guarantees, relies on proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods which are well-known. The claim of an "optimal O(1/t) convergence rate" feels uninspired; it's standard for these methods and lacks specific details about constant factors or practical convergence speed. The parameter tuning details are also vague.

The experimental evaluation, while extensive, doesn’t convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong baselines. The synthetic data experiments, while useful for verifying convergence, don’t reflect real-world complexity. The results on the real datasets show improvement, but the baselines are not always state-of-the-art or thoroughly tuned. Comparing against a Poisson process, while sensible, is a very weak baseline. The comparison to STiC is more relevant, but the explanation for the performance difference (dependence on the entire history vs. last event) feels superficial. Tensor factorization methods are included, but the description of their implementation is brief and lacks crucial details, hindering a fair comparison.  The analysis focusing on whether the Hawkes model fits the data better than survival models is somewhat tangential; the crucial question isn't whether the data *fits* a Hawkes process, but whether a Hawkes-based *recommendation* system performs better than alternatives.

Clarity is also a concern. While the writing is generally clear, the paper is dense and requires significant effort to follow. The notation is heavy, and the connection between the theoretical formulation and the practical implementation isn’t always obvious.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem – time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Combines established techniques (Hawkes processes, low-rank modeling) in a potentially useful way.
*   Provides convergence guarantees for the proposed optimization algorithm.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of significant novelty; incremental improvement over existing work.
*   Insufficient justification for methodological choices.
*   Incomplete experimental evaluation with insufficiently strong baselines.
*   Dense writing and challenging notation.
*   Overstated claims regarding advantages over related work.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper presents a technically sound approach but lacks the originality, impact, and rigorous validation necessary for acceptance to NIPS. The authors need to provide a more compelling demonstration of the advantages of their method over existing alternatives, with a more thorough and fair experimental evaluation. I suggest a major revision that addresses the points raised above before considering resubmission to a different venue.
