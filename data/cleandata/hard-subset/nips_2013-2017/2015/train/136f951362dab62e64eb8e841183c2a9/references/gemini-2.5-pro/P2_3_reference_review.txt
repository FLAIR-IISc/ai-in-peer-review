### Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to predict both *what* item a user will interact with and *when* that interaction will occur. The authors propose a novel framework that models the sequence of user-item interactions as a multivariate Hawkes process. The core contribution is to manage the model's complexity and generalize to unobserved user-item pairs by imposing a low-rank structure on the parameter matrices of the Hawkes process (specifically, the base intensity and self-excitation matrices). This elegantly connects the theory of self-exciting point processes with the well-established paradigm of low-rank models in collaborative filtering. To solve the resulting convex but non-trivial optimization problem (log-likelihood maximization with nuclear norm and non-negativity constraints), the paper introduces a new efficient algorithm that hybridizes the proximal gradient and conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) methods. The efficacy of this "Low-Rank Hawkes Process" model is demonstrated through extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets from diverse domains (music, e-commerce, healthcare), showing superior performance on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

### Detailed Evaluation

**Quality: Very Good**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation of the problem is well-grounded in the theory of temporal point processes. The connection to low-rank models is a natural and powerful way to introduce a collaborative effect. The optimization problem in Eq. (4) is a correct representation of the modeling goal. The subsequent reformulation in Eq. (5) and the accompanying Theorem 1, which establishes equivalence under certain conditions, provide a solid theoretical basis for the proposed algorithm. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is a strong point, demonstrating that the algorithm achieves optimal rates for its constituent parts (O(1/t) for the conditional gradient part).
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough. The use of synthetic data is crucial for validating the algorithm's correctness and convergence properties, which Figures 2(a-d) clearly demonstrate. The evaluation on three distinct real-world datasets showcases the model's general applicability. The choice of two complementary tasks (item ranking and time prediction) provides a comprehensive assessment of the model's capabilities. The "time-change theorem" analysis (Figure 3) is a particularly nice touch, providing empirical evidence that the Hawkes process assumption is a better fit for the data than simpler survival models.
*   **Weaknesses:** One minor weakness is the practical handling of the hyperparameter `ρ`. Theorem 1 guarantees equivalence for `ρ > ρ*`, but `ρ*` is a problem-dependent constant that is not known a priori. The paper states that `ρ` is tuned, but a discussion of the model's sensitivity to this parameter and the practical strategy for setting it would have strengthened the paper. Additionally, while the algorithm is shown to scale linearly, the per-iteration cost of the LMO step involves computing the top singular vector pairs of the gradient matrices, which can still be computationally intensive for extremely large user/item matrices. A brief discussion on the practical implementation (e.g., using randomized SVD or power iteration) would be beneficial.

**Clarity: Good**

The paper is generally well-written and organized, but some sections could be improved for better readability and reproducibility.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction and motivation are excellent. They clearly articulate the limitations of existing recommendation models and the importance of modeling temporal dynamics. The background on point processes is concise and sufficient for understanding the model. The model description in Section 3 is logical and easy to follow.
*   **Areas for Improvement:** The description of the optimization algorithm in Section 4.3 and Algorithm 1 is quite dense. The notation `∇1(f(Uk−1))` and `∇2(f(Uk−1))` is not explicitly defined, though it can be inferred as the partial gradients with respect to `X1` and `X2`. The update steps within the LMO (Algorithm 3) could be explained with more prose to guide the reader. For full reproducibility, providing explicit forms for the gradients would be very helpful. The presentation of the tensor factorization baselines is slightly confusing; it is not immediately clear what "Last" and "Avg" refer to without reading the description carefully.

**Originality: Excellent**

The paper makes a highly original contribution by synthesizing two distinct fields in a novel and effective way.

*   **Novel Combination:** While both low-rank models and Hawkes processes are well-known, their combination for collaborative recommendation is, to the best of my knowledge, novel. It moves beyond simply adding a time feature to matrix factorization and instead models the underlying generative process of user activity, which is a significant conceptual leap.
*   **New Algorithm:** The proposed optimization algorithm, which blends proximal and conditional gradient methods to handle the specific structure of the objective function (smooth loss + non-negativity + nuclear norm), appears to be a new contribution in this context. While the components are known, their application and the accompanying convergence analysis for this specific problem are novel.
*   **Related Work:** The paper does a good job of positioning its contribution relative to prior work, particularly the survival analysis-based methods of Kapoor et al. [11, 12], clearly highlighting its key advantage in being able to recommend new items.

**Significance: Excellent**

The results and ideas presented in this paper are likely to have a substantial impact on both researchers and practitioners in the field of recommender systems.

*   **Advancing the State of the Art:** The paper addresses a critical and increasingly relevant problem: modeling the continuous-time dynamics of user engagement. By outperforming strong baselines, including temporal tensor factorization, it demonstrably advances the state of the art.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The framework is general. As demonstrated by the experiments on music, e-commerce, and healthcare data, the model can be applied to any domain where event data with timestamps is available. The authors also correctly point out that the framework is extensible to include other forms of context (e.g., via tensor formulations), opening up many avenues for future work.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The ability to predict *when* a user will return is highly valuable for practical applications like targeted marketing, dynamic resource allocation, and proactive user engagement. The proposed model provides a principled and effective way to tackle this problem. The optimization algorithm itself may be of independent interest for other problems involving non-negative matrix rank minimization.

---

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Originality:** Proposes a novel and elegant synthesis of low-rank models and Hawkes processes for a key problem in recommendation.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** Includes a new, efficient optimization algorithm with a formal convergence guarantee.
3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The model is validated on both synthetic data and three diverse, real-world datasets, consistently outperforming strong baselines.
4.  **High Significance:** Addresses a crucial, practical problem and provides a powerful, generalizable framework that is likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Clarity of Optimization Section:** The description of the algorithm is dense and could be improved for easier understanding and reproducibility.
2.  **Practical Details Omitted:** A more detailed discussion on hyperparameter tuning (especially for `ρ`) and the practical complexity of the SVD step would strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, technically sound, and significant contribution to the field of recommender systems. I strongly recommend **acceptance**. The clarity issues in the optimization section can be easily addressed during the revision process.
