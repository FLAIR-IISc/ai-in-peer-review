This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling recurrent user activities as a collection of self-exciting Hawkes processes. The central idea is to address the data sparsity problem inherent in recommendation by assuming that the parameters of these Hawkes processes—specifically, the baseline intensity matrix and the self-exciting magnitude matrix—are low-rank. This elegantly unifies the temporal modeling power of point processes with the collaborative knowledge-sharing mechanism of low-rank matrix factorization. The authors propose a convex formulation for this problem using nuclear norm regularization and develop a novel, efficient optimization algorithm that hybridizes proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to solve it. The learned model is then used to address two key tasks: recommending the most relevant item at a given moment and predicting a user's next return time to a service. This approach is contrasted with prior work in temporal recommendation, such as tensor factorization which discretizes time, and survival analysis models which typically do not generalize to unobserved user-item pairs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. The synthesis of multivariate Hawkes processes with low-rank models for collaborative recommendation is a bold and powerful conceptual leap. It moves the field beyond static preference prediction and discrete time models towards a more principled, continuous-time understanding of user behavior. This framework provides a new and compelling direction for research in recommendation systems, and its potential applications in areas like ad-bidding and proactive healthcare are significant and well-motivated. The ability to simultaneously recommend *what* and predict *when* in a unified model is a major advance.

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** Beyond the novel formulation, the paper makes a solid algorithmic contribution. The proposed optimization method, which blends proximal and conditional gradient steps, is cleverly designed to handle the specific structure of the objective function (non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization). This avoids the prohibitive cost of full SVDs required by standard proximal methods for the nuclear norm, making the approach scalable. The accompanying convergence analysis, which establishes an optimal rate, adds theoretical rigor to the work. This algorithm could be of independent interest for other problems involving non-negative matrix rank minimization.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors test their model on both large-scale synthetic data (allowing for controlled evaluation of parameter recovery and scalability) and three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, and medical records). The comparison against relevant and strong baselines, including static matrix factorization, tensor-based temporal models, and dedicated return-time prediction models (STiC), effectively demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach. The use of the time-change theorem to empirically validate the choice of the Hawkes process model over simpler alternatives is a particularly nice touch that adds confidence in the model's underlying assumptions.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The paper primarily relies on a simple exponential triggering kernel for the Hawkes process. While the authors mention that the framework supports other kernels, the empirical validation is limited to this choice. The dynamics of user influence might be more complex than a simple exponential decay, and exploring more flexible or non-parametrically learned kernels could further improve performance. However, for a paper introducing a new foundational framework, this simplification is understandable and acceptable.

2.  **Practicality of Theoretical Conditions:** The equivalence established in Theorem 1 between the original and the penalized optimization problems depends on a threshold `ρ*` which itself depends on the unknown optimal solution. In practice, this means `ρ` becomes a hyperparameter that must be tuned via cross-validation. This is a minor point and a common feature of penalty-based methods, but it slightly diminishes the practical utility of the theoretical guarantee.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The presentation of the optimization algorithm is quite dense. While technically sound, a more intuitive explanation of the interplay between the proximal and conditional gradient steps and how they separately address the different components of the objective function could improve the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

The work presents a significant and novel contribution by introducing a new class of models for recommendation systems. The core idea of "collaborative Hawkes processes" via a low-rank assumption is elegant, powerful, and opens up many avenues for future research. While there are minor weaknesses related to simplifying assumptions and the presentation of the algorithm, these are far outweighed by the paper's strengths. The high degree of novelty, the importance of the problem being solved, the solid technical contributions, and the thorough experimental validation make this a standout paper. It is exactly the kind of bold, conceptually innovative work that pushes the field forward and should be highlighted at NIPS.
