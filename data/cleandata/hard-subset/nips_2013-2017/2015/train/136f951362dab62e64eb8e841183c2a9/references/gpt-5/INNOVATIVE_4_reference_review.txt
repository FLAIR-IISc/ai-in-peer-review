Summary
The paper addresses two coupled problems in time-sensitive personalization: (1) recommending the right item at the right moment; (2) predicting a user’s next return time. It models each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + αu,i Σj γ(t, tu,ij), where Λ0 and α are nonnegative base and self-excitation parameters. To enable generalization across the unobserved majority of user–item pairs, the authors impose low-rank structure on Λ0 and A via nuclear-norm regularization, yielding a convex formulation over all pairs. They then propose an optimization method that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity via simple projection) with a conditional-gradient/LMO step (for nuclear-norm regularization) using an auxiliary splitting/penalty reformulation. They prove an O(1/t) convergence guarantee and establish conditions under which the penalized and original formulations coincide. The method scales to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events in synthetic data, and shows improved ranking and return-time prediction over Poisson, STiC, SVD, and Poisson tensor factorization baselines on last.fm, tmall, and MIMIC II.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The paper makes a neat, under-explored connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for recommender systems. Casting the joint estimation of per-pair base/self-excitation parameters as a convex low-rank problem is elegant and practically compelling.
- Optimization contribution: The hybrid proximal–conditional gradient scheme, along with the penalty-based decoupling of nonnegativity and nuclear-norm terms, is simple, scalable, and avoids repeated full SVDs. The convergence analysis is clear and the obtained rates align with known optimal rates for the constituent parts.
- Practicality and scope: The framework cleanly supports time-sensitive ranking (via λu,i(t)) and return-time prediction (via Ogata sampling), and naturally accommodates additional covariates or nonparametric kernels.
- Empirical breadth: Experiments span synthetic data at large scale and three real domains (music, ecommerce, EHR), with consistent gains on both ranking and return-time prediction. The time-change diagnostic is a nice touch to validate the Hawkes assumption.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Modeling limitations: The current model uses only self-excitation per user–item pair (no cross-excitation across items or inhibition). In many domains, inter-item influence (substitution/complementarity) and saturation are key. The paper mentions extensions but does not evaluate them; this limits the realism of the deployed model.
- Baselines: For time-sensitive recommendation, comparisons omit strong sequential or dynamic baselines (e.g., FPMC, timeSVD++/dynamic MF, factorized personalized Markov chains, more recent survival point-process recommenders). For return-time prediction, only STiC is included beyond Poisson/tensors. This weakens the empirical case in favor of the proposed method.
- Penalty equivalence: Theorem 1’s equivalence condition (ρ > ρ*) is not actionable, and in practice ρ is tuned. Some guidance on sensitivity to ρ and a practical schedule would help, as would a brief comparison to ADMM with partial SVDs or to recent FW variants for composite objectives.
- Algorithmic details: The L, D1, D2 constants, step-size choices, and line-search details are only sketched. Reproducibility would benefit from more specifics (e.g., how many Ogata samples are used for return-time, runtime/memory vs. event count, handling of empty histories, setting σ).
- Evaluation protocol: The ranking metric is “rank of the ground-truth item” at t; reporting standard metrics (recall@k/HR@k/NDCG) would aid comparison. For held-out sequences, it would be helpful to explicitly quantify cold-start generalization (e.g., users/items unseen in training vs. sparse regimes).
- Claims about “best of both worlds”: The final rate is dominated by O(1/t); while the decomposition is insightful, the “best of both worlds” phrasing reads stronger than what the bound shows. A toned-down statement would be more precise.

Quality
Technically sound overall: convexity is well motivated; the hybrid algorithm is reasonable and analyzed; experiments are carefully constructed. Some parts need more detail for full reproducibility, and the baseline suite could be broader.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Minor issues: several typos, occasionally overloaded notation (φ, ψ), and terse pseudocode. A figure/table summarizing datasets and metrics would help.

Originality
High. While Hawkes processes and low-rank regularization are known, their combination for per-pair self-excitation in time-sensitive recommendation with a convex estimator, plus the proposed hybrid solver, is a fresh and valuable direction. Related Hawkes low-rank work is cited, and the positioning vs. survival/HMM/tensor methods is fair.

Significance
High practical potential. The approach is broadly applicable to time-sensitive personalization and healthcare event modeling. Given its convexity and scalability, it is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to mutual-excitation tensors and context features).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of kernel bandwidth σ? Do you learn σ or tune per dataset?
- How many Ogata samples are used for return-time prediction, and what is the runtime impact?
- Have you tried mutual-excitation across items (low-rank 3-way factorization of excitation), and if so, how does it affect performance?
- Can you report standard top-k metrics and include dynamic MF/FPMC baselines?
- Any guidance or heuristics for choosing ρ that work robustly across datasets?

Pros
- Conceptually novel and bold connection; convex estimator
- Scalable hybrid optimization with theory
- Strong and consistent empirical gains across tasks/domains
- Clear path to incorporating rich context

Cons
- Limited baselines for sequential/dynamic recommendation
- Self-excitation only; no cross-item influences evaluated
- Practical guidance on ρ and runtime/memory details could be strengthened
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). This paper proposes a timely, novel, and practically impactful direction with solid theory and promising results. Despite some missing baselines and modeling simplifications, the conceptual contribution and scalability merit acceptance.

Confidence
High.
