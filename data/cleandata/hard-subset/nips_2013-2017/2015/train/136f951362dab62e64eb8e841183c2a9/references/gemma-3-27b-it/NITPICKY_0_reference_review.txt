## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction based on low-rank Hawkes processes. The core idea of connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models is interesting, aiming to capture the temporal dynamics of user behavior. The authors present a convex formulation and an optimization algorithm blending proximal and conditional gradient methods, claiming optimal convergence. They demonstrate results on synthetic and real datasets, showcasing scalability and predictive performance.

While the paper tackles a relevant and increasingly important problem – modeling temporal user behavior – a multitude of concerns regarding technical depth, clarity, experimental rigor, and presentation significantly diminish its impact. Despite claiming novelty, several aspects feel underdeveloped or lack sufficient justification.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Formulation:** The motivation for considering time-sensitive recommendations and predicting returning times is well articulated and relevant.
*   **Hawkes Process Integration:** The choice of Hawkes processes to model recurrent user activities is reasonable, given their ability to capture temporal dependencies.
*   **Scalability Claims:** The claim of scalability to millions of user-item pairs is potentially valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Rigorous Theoretical Justification:** The claim of “optimal O(1/t) convergence rate” is stated without sufficient theoretical backing. The convergence analysis presented in Section 4.4 relies heavily on existing results (Lan, 2013; Lan, 2017) and lacks a detailed, self-contained proof specifically tailored to their formulation. The connection between the theoretical convergence guarantees and practical performance isn’t discussed. The derivation in Theorem 1 feels rushed and lacks crucial details.

2.  **Insufficiently Motivated Low-Rank Assumption:** The justification for assuming low-rank structures in Λ0 and A is weak. The argument about categorizing user behaviors and item attributes is hand-wavy and lacks supporting evidence or empirical validation. A more thorough discussion of the implications of this assumption is needed.

3.  **Implementation Details are Vague:** Despite the claim of scalability, crucial implementation details are omitted. The choice of parameters like ρ, λ, and β is merely mentioned as being "tuned", with no discussion of the tuning process or sensitivity analysis. The exact implementation of the LMOψ subroutine is not fully elaborated, hindering reproducibility.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation Deficiencies:** The experimental setup is flawed in several ways.
    *   **Synthetic Data:** The experimental setting with synthetic data lacks sensitivity analysis with respect to rank. The authors pick rank five arbitrarily.
    *   **Real Data:** The description of the real datasets is too brief. More details regarding preprocessing, data splits, and the specific context of the datasets would be beneficial.
    *   **Baseline Comparisons:** The choice of baselines is questionable. Comparing against a static SVD and a survival analysis method (STiC) that explicitly disclaims recommendation capability is not a strong benchmark. The Tensor Factorization models are poorly described, and the distinction between Tensor-#-Last and Tensor-#-Avg is unclear.
    *   **Evaluation Metrics:** The sole reliance on MAE for returning-time prediction is insufficient. Metrics like RMSE or precision@k for recommendation would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
    *   **Statistical Significance:** No statistical significance tests are reported, making it difficult to assess whether the observed performance improvements are statistically meaningful.

5.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from numerous minor clarity issues. Equations are often presented without sufficient explanation or context. The figure captions are inadequate. The writing is occasionally verbose and repetitive. Grammatical errors are present throughout (e.g. "last.fm consists of the music streaming logs between 1,000 users and 3,000 artists.")

6.  **Related Work Oversimplification:** The related work section does not accurately portray the state-of-the-art. The dismissal of survival analysis’s applicability to recommendation is overly simplistic. Survival analysis and Hawkes processes are both applicable in sequential recommendation, and more nuance is required when comparing the two.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Against:** The numerous technical and experimental flaws, coupled with the lack of rigorous justification for key assumptions, outweigh the potential novelty of the approach. The paper reads more like a preliminary investigation than a polished, conclusive contribution.
*   **For:** The problem is important, and the idea of combining Hawkes processes with low-rank models is potentially promising.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and introduces an interesting idea, the numerous deficiencies in technical depth, experimental rigor, and clarity prevent it from meeting the standards of a NIPS publication. The authors need to address the concerns raised above with significant revisions, including a more thorough theoretical analysis, a more robust experimental evaluation, and a clearer presentation of the method. A complete rewrite is likely necessary to elevate the quality of this work.
