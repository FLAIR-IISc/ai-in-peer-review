## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation by bridging self-exciting point processes and low-rank models. The proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated on both synthetic and real-world datasets. While the convergence analysis is thorough and the experimental results compelling, the paper could benefit from a more critical discussion of limitations and a clearer articulation of the practical challenges in deploying such a model. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the important problem of time-sensitive recommendation, moving beyond traditional rating-based approaches to model the recurrent temporal dynamics of user behavior. The core idea is to represent user-item interactions as a Hawkes process, leveraging low-rank approximations to facilitate scalability and knowledge transfer. This allows for both predicting the next item a user will interact with *and* forecasting the time until their next interaction.  

The related work section correctly identifies Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015) as the most relevant prior work.  However, the authors effectively highlight the key distinction – their model's ability to generalize to *new* items, a critical limitation of the survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov model approaches.  The paper also appropriately positions itself within the broader context of point processes, tensor factorization, and collaborative filtering.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for time-sensitive recommendation is a significant contribution.  The explicit connection between these two areas is well-established and cleverly exploited.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of the model is strong, with a clear convex formulation and a well-defined optimization algorithm. The convergence analysis (O(1/t)) is impressive and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying optimization landscape.
* **Scalability:** The algorithm is explicitly designed for scalability, addressing a crucial practical concern with point process models. The demonstration of performance on millions of user-item pairs is compelling.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets are strong.  The ablation studies (Figure 2) clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach over various baselines, including Poisson processes, STiC, and tensor factorization methods.
* **Generality:** The paper acknowledges and briefly discusses the potential for incorporating additional contextual information (spatial, textual, etc.), increasing the model’s applicability.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Choice Justification:** While the exponential kernel is a reasonable starting point, the paper lacks a detailed justification for this choice. Exploring other kernel families (e.g., Weibull, Log-logistic) and analyzing their impact on performance would strengthen the work.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the model likely depends significantly on hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, σ, rank). The paper briefly mentions tuning these parameters, but a more thorough discussion of their sensitivity and best practices for setting them would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates scalability, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed algorithm (e.g., runtime as a function of dataset size and rank) would be helpful. A comparison of the computational complexity with existing methods would be particularly insightful.
* **Real-World Deployment Challenges:** The paper focuses primarily on offline evaluation.  A discussion of the practical challenges associated with deploying such a model in a real-world system (e.g., cold-start problem for new items/users, handling noisy data, online learning) would increase the paper’s impact.
* **Limited Discussion of Model Interpretability:** While the low-rank structure suggests potential for interpretability, the paper doesn’t explicitly explore this aspect. Analyzing the learned latent factors could provide valuable insights into user preferences and item characteristics.




**4. Significance & Originality:**

The paper addresses a significant problem – delivering personalized experiences at the right moment – which has substantial practical implications for various applications, including recommendation systems, mobile advertising, and healthcare. The originality of the work lies in the novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling, which allows for scalable and accurate time-sensitive recommendations. The results have the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in this field and inspire further research.




**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  Technically sound and novel approach.
*  Strong empirical validation on real-world datasets.
*  Addresses an important and practical problem.
*  Scalable algorithm.
*  Clear writing and organization.

**Con:**

*  Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*  Lack of detailed computational cost analysis.
*  Missing analysis of real-world deployment challenges.
*  Kernel choice justification is weak.




**6. Recommendation & Suggestions for Revision:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the following:

* **Expand the discussion of kernel choice and justify the selection of the exponential kernel.** Consider experimenting with other kernel families and reporting the results.
* **Provide a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.** Include a discussion of best practices for setting the hyperparameters and potentially conduct a sensitivity analysis.
* **Include a discussion of the computational cost of the algorithm and compare it with existing methods.**
* **Add a section discussing the practical challenges of deploying the model in a real-world system.**
* **Consider exploring the interpretability of the learned latent factors.**




This is a valuable contribution to the field, and addressing these minor points will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
