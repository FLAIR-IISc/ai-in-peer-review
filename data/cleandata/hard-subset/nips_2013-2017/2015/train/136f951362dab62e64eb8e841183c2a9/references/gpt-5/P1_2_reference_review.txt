Summary
This paper tackles two coupled problems in time-sensitive recommendation: (1) ranking items for a user at any time, and (2) predicting the user’s next return time to an item/service. The core modeling choice is a self-exciting Hawkes process per user–item pair, with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), typically with an exponential kernel. To transfer information to unobserved user–item pairs, Λ0 and A are learned jointly across all pairs under low-rank assumptions enforced via nuclear norms, yielding a convex program with nonnegativity constraints. Because the combination of nuclear norm and nonnegativity is awkward for standard proximal methods, the authors introduce auxiliary variables with a quadratic penalty (ρ) to decouple constraints and regularizers, and propose a hybrid algorithm that applies proximal gradient for the nonnegative block and a conditional gradient (LMO/top singular vector) for the nuclear-norm block, with an accelerated scheme. They prove (i) equivalence between the penalized and original problems when ρ exceeds a problem-dependent threshold, and (ii) an O(1/t) convergence rate (with a sharper O(1/t^2) term tied to the proximal block) for the hybrid method. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II) show improved ranking and return-time prediction over Poisson, tensor factorization baselines, and a survival/HsMM model (STiC) on the tasks considered.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges point processes (Hawkes) with low-rank recommendation. It is related to: survival-based return-time prediction (Kapoor et al., WSDM’15; KDD’14), tensor methods for temporal recommendation, and multivariate/low-rank Hawkes literature (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS/ICML; Du et al., KDD/AISTATS). The paper’s novelty lies in the convex low-rank formulation at the user–item dyad level with self-excitation and the hybrid optimization scheme that separates nonnegativity and nuclear norms.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Clear and compelling joint view of time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction under a unified Hawkes framework.
- Convexity and identifiability: Linear-in-parameters Hawkes intensity yields a concave log-likelihood; nuclear norms encourage sharing across user–item pairs.
- Algorithmic contribution: Practical hybrid of proximal and conditional gradient with acceleration; per-iteration costs dominated by cheap projections and top singular vector computations.
- Theory: Convergence analysis with explicit rates; equivalence result between penalized and constrained formulations (under a threshold ρ).
- Empirics: Large-scale synthetic showing sample complexity and scalability; real-data gains on both ranking and return-time prediction; model-check via time-rescaling QQ plots provides evidence Hawkes captures dependence better than i.i.d. gap models.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
Quality
- Stability/branching ratio: For Hawkes with exponential kernel, non-explosion/stability requires A(u,i) ∫ γ < 1 (i.e., A(u,i)σ < 1). The paper does not state or enforce such constraints. Please discuss whether instability occurs, how α (A) is constrained in practice, and whether enforcing Aσ ≤ c < 1 affects learning.
- Equivalence theorem practicality: ρ* is defined in terms of unknown optimal quantities; in practice ρ is tuned. Without a continuation scheme or a verifiable bound, the equivalence guarantee is hard to use. A practical recipe (e.g., increasing-ρ schedule with feasibility checks) would strengthen the claim.
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to point-process baselines beyond Poisson—e.g., per-dyad Hawkes without low-rank sharing; low-rank factorized dyadic Hawkes (e.g., CP/UV decomposition of Λ0/A); or mutually-exciting (cross-item) Hawkes. This would disentangle the benefits of self-excitation vs. low-rank sharing vs. mutual excitation.
- Evaluation protocol: The “held-out entire sequence” is per user–item pair, but items remain observed elsewhere. This does not test truly cold-start items/users; the claim about generalizing to “new items in future time” should be qualified. Please clarify and, if possible, include a stricter cold-start setting (brand-new items or users).
- Return-time prediction: Using Monte Carlo sampling via Ogata to approximate E[Tn+1] can be expensive; numerical quadrature over the survival function or median/time-to-event might be more stable. Report computational cost and variance of the estimator.

Clarity
- Notation can be tightened: distinguish clearly between α(u,i) and A(u,i); define all vectors (φ, ψ) upon first use; fix minor typos and subscripts.
- Complexity: Provide per-iteration time and memory complexity in terms of number of events, users, items, and rank; detail how top singular vectors are computed (power/Lanczos), and convergence tolerances.
- Hyperparameters: Report how λ, β, ρ, and kernel bandwidth σ are tuned; sensitivity analyses would be useful.

Originality
- The modeling connection (Hawkes + low-rank nuclear norms) is interesting and, to my knowledge, less explored for dyadic recommendation. The hybrid optimization approach relates to prior work on conditional gradient for norm-regularized smooth objectives (e.g., Jaggi; Harchaoui & Juditsky; Lan’s CG variants) and block-structured or composite CG/prox hybrids. The contribution is an effective instantiation rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic principle. Positioning relative to “hybrid proximal conditional gradient,” “conditional gradient sliding,” and projection-free methods for composite minimization would clarify novelty.

Significance
- The tasks are important and the results are promising. Demonstrating that the method scales to 100M events is a plus. Broader impact could be increased by:
  - Incorporating and evaluating mutually exciting effects across items (substitution/complementarity).
  - Using side information (features) for base intensities to address true cold start.
  - Providing ablative studies (only Λ0 low-rank; only A low-rank; fixed A=0) to quantify self-excitation’s contribution.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated problem with practical importance.
- Convex formulation with a scalable solver and nontrivial theory.
- Strong empirical gains across multiple datasets and tasks.
- Useful diagnostic (time-rescaling) showing model appropriateness.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing stability constraints and discussion for Hawkes.
- Practical gap in the equivalence theorem (unknown ρ*).
- Incomplete baselines (no dyadic Hawkes w/o low-rank; no mutual excitation).
- Limited cold-start evaluation; some clarity and reproducibility gaps.

Suggestions/questions to authors
- Add or enforce stability constraints (e.g., Aσ < 1) and report their effect.
- Include a baseline: per-pair Hawkes with independent MLE, plus a factorized variant (e.g., Λ0 ≈ UV^T, A ≈ U’V’^T) to isolate low-rank sharing benefits.
- Provide runtime/memory and top-SV computation details; report wall-clock comparisons to ADMM/Prox-SVT.
- Expand sensitivity analyses for σ, λ, β, ρ; consider a continuation strategy for ρ.
- Discuss extending to mutually exciting kernels across items and show a small-scale experiment if feasible.
- Clarify the held-out protocol vis-à-vis true cold-start and temper claims accordingly; consider an additional split for entirely new items/users.

Overall
This is a solid and timely contribution with a clean convex formulation, a practical hybrid solver, and convincing empirical results. Addressing the noted gaps (stability, baselines, clarity) would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance after revisions.
