This paper introduces a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, framing the problem as a low-rank Hawkes process. The core idea is to model user activity as a self-exciting point process and leverage low-rank approximations to generalize across user-item pairs. The authors develop a convex formulation and an optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, demonstrating scalability and strong empirical performance on synthetic and real datasets.

This work builds upon recent advancements in modeling temporal dynamics in recommendation systems, particularly the work of Kapoor et al. [12, 11] which used survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models. However, the authors explicitly differentiate their approach by enabling recommendations for *new* items, a crucial advantage over prior work. The connection to Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling is a novel contribution, and the proposed optimization algorithm addresses the challenges of scale and non-negativity constraints effectively. The paper situates itself well within the existing literature and highlights its distinct contributions clearly.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for time-sensitive recommendation is a significant and novel contribution. The problem formulation is well-motivated, and the connection to self-exciting point processes offers a principled way to capture temporal dependencies.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The authors provide a rigorous mathematical formulation of the problem, including a convex optimization formulation and a convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm. The theoretical analysis lends credibility to the approach.
*   **Scalability:** The authors address the scalability concerns of low-rank estimation by proposing an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal and conditional gradient methods. Empirical results demonstrate the algorithm's ability to handle large datasets (millions of user-item pairs).
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach compared to several strong baselines, including Poisson processes, STiC, and tensor factorization methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the key contributions. The mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably clear manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Kernel Choice:** The paper largely focuses on the exponential kernel for the triggering function. While justified, a more thorough exploration of other kernel options and their impact on performance would strengthen the work. A discussion of the limitations of the exponential kernel in certain scenarios would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithm likely depends on the careful tuning of several hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, bandwidth). A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and guidelines for setting these parameters would improve the practicality of the approach.
*   **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons are made to several baselines, some recent and highly relevant models for sequential recommendation (e.g., Transformer-based models, Recurrent Neural Networks with attention mechanisms) are not included. Incorporating these comparisons would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
*   **Real-World Datasets:** The real-world datasets used are relatively standard. Applying the proposed method to more challenging and diverse real-world scenarios would further validate its robustness and generalizability.
*   **Detailed Computational Cost Analysis:** While scalability is mentioned, a more detailed computational cost analysis of the proposed algorithm compared to the baselines would be helpful, especially in terms of memory usage and runtime.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The proposed method demonstrates strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The algorithm is scalable and addresses the key challenges of low-rank estimation in this context.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited exploration of kernel options and hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The lack of comparison to more recent and sophisticated sequential recommendation models.
*   The reliance on relatively standard real-world datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should consider expanding the discussion on kernel choices, providing more guidance on hyperparameter tuning, and incorporating comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art sequential recommendation models.
