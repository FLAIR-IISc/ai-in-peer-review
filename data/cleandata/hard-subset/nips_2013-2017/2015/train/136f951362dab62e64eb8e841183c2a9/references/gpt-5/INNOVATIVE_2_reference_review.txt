Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles two intertwined problems in event-driven recommendation: (1) recommending the right item at the right time; and (2) predicting a user’s return time to an item/service. The key modeling idea is to treat each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) Σk γ(t, t_k), where Λ0 and A are nonnegative user–item matrices for baseline and self-exciting strength, respectively, and γ is a (primarily exponential) triggering kernel. To enable generalization to unobserved user–item pairs, Λ0 and A are assumed to be low-rank, enforced via nuclear norm penalties. This yields a convex formulation for maximum likelihood estimation with nuclear norm regularization and nonnegativity.

The optimization is nontrivial due to the combination of nonnegativity constraints and nuclear norm penalties. The authors introduce an auxiliary-variable penalty formulation that decouples these components and present an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity-constrained variables) with conditional gradient/Frank–Wolfe (for the nuclear-norm terms), with an accelerated scheme and an overall O(1/t) convergence rate. They provide a condition under which the penalized problem is equivalent to the original constrained one. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improved ranking and return-time prediction compared to Poisson, STiC (survival/HsMM), SVD, and Poisson-regression tensor factorization.

This work bridges temporal point processes (Hawkes) and low-rank recommendation models. It goes beyond prior survival/hazard approaches for return-time prediction [Kapoor et al.] and complements existing Hawkes learning work (e.g., Zhou et al., Du et al.) by imposing low-rank structure over the user–item parameter matrices and casting the whole estimation as a convex program suitable for large-scale recommendation.

Strengths
- Novel and timely formulation: A convex low-rank Hawkes framework for recommendation and return-time prediction is, to my knowledge, new and conceptually elegant. It simultaneously addresses cold-start over user–item pairs and temporal dynamics in a unified way.
- Algorithmic contribution: The proximal–conditional gradient hybrid with an acceleration scheme is well-motivated for this structure. The linear-oracle design (using top singular vectors) is practical at scale, avoiding repeated full SVDs.
- Theoretical support: Clear convexity of the main objective; equivalence result between the constrained and penalized formulations; convergence rate analysis with interpretable constants. The O(1/t) rate is appropriate for the nuclear-norm part and matches known optimal rates in the limiting cases.
- Empirical scale and relevance: Experiments include >100M events synthetically and million-scale real logs, with strong results on both item ranking and return-time MAE. The time-rescaling goodness-of-fit analysis is a nice, principled diagnostic in real data.
- Practical utility: The ability to recommend new items at specific times and to predict return times has clear applications across media, e-commerce, and healthcare.

Weaknesses and questions
- Penalization equivalence and tuning: The theorem requires ρ > ρ*. In practice, ρ* is unknown and depends on the unknown optimum. While the authors note they tune ρ, guidance on stability/sensitivity would help. How does performance vary with ρ? Any heuristic schedule?
- Modeling limitations: The current model only includes self-excitation within a user–item pair. Many domains (e.g., comorbid diseases, playlists) exhibit cross-excitation across items and/or competition effects. Although extensions are mentioned, no experiments assess mutual excitation or cross-effects; this limits expressiveness relative to multivariate Hawkes baselines.
- Baselines: Missing a Hawkes baseline without low-rank sharing (fit per observed pair) to isolate the gain from low-rank transfer. Also, time-aware factorization baselines (e.g., TimeSVD++) would be informative on the ranking side. For modern sequence models (RNN-based point processes), contemporaneous comparisons may not have been common, but a discussion would be useful.
- Prediction procedure: Return-time prediction via Ogata sampling can be computationally heavy at inference time for large-scale systems. Are there approximations (e.g., inversion for exponential kernels or quadrature) or batching strategies, and what are the practical latencies?
- Evaluation metrics: For recommendation, only the rank of the true item at the event time is reported. Additional metrics like recall@k or time-aware AUC would improve interpretability. Some detail on how ties/popularity biases are handled would help.
- Clarity/notation: Some notation (e.g., reuse of γ) and vectorization is terse; a complexity analysis (per-iteration time, memory footprint, dependence on number of users/items/events) would improve reproducibility.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with solid convex analysis and a reasonable algorithmic design. Proof sketches are deferred, but the main claims align with known properties of log-likelihoods for linear Hawkes and convex surrogates. Experimental methodology is reasonable, though a few stronger baselines and more metrics would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. A few sections are notation-heavy; adding pseudo-code complexity and a brief paragraph on practical implementation choices (e.g., how top singular vectors are computed, stopping criteria) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- High. Establishing a low-rank Hawkes recommender with a convex estimation framework and an efficient hybrid optimizer is, to my knowledge, a novel combination. The link to time-sensitive recommendation is compelling.

Significance
- High. This is a clean, extensible direction likely to influence temporal recommendation and event modeling. The optimization approach may be of independent interest for other nonnegative low-rank problems with convex losses.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a per-pair Hawkes baseline (no sharing) to quantify the benefit of low-rank transfer.
- Include TimeSVD++ or comparable time-aware factorization on ranking.
- Provide sensitivity plots for ρ, λ, β; and brief guidance for setting the kernel bandwidth.
- Discuss inference-time complexity for return-time prediction and any practical speedups.
- Consider a small experiment with mutually-exciting interactions across items to demonstrate extensibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong conceptual novelty connecting low-rank recommender structure with Hawkes processes in a convex framework.
- Useful and scalable optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
- Demonstrated improvements on both ranking and return-time prediction across multiple datasets.
- Clear practical relevance and potential for follow-on work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited baseline coverage (no per-pair Hawkes; no strong time-aware factorization).
- Cross-excitation and richer kernels are only discussed, not validated experimentally.
- Some practical aspects (ρ tuning, inference cost) are underexplored.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper introduces a significant and fresh direction for time-sensitive recommendation with a principled probabilistic foundation and an efficient solver. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the (addressable) experimental limitations.
