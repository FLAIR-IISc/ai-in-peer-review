Summary
This paper tackles two linked problems: (1) time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at time t) and (2) return-time prediction for user–item pairs. The authors model each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with exponential triggering, where the baseline intensity and self-excitation coefficient form two nonnegative m×n parameter matrices (Λ0 and A). To transfer knowledge to unobserved pairs and enable recommendation of unseen items, they impose low-rank structure via nuclear-norm regularization on both matrices. The key technical contribution is a convex formulation and an efficient hybrid optimization algorithm that decouples the nonnegativity constraints and nuclear-norm penalties using auxiliary variables and a penalty parameter ρ, then updates the variables via a blend of proximal gradient (for the constrained, smooth block) and conditional gradient/linear oracle steps (for the nuclear-norm-regularized block). They prove convergence with an O(1/t) rate and show that, with sufficiently large ρ, the penalized formulation matches the original constrained problem. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) demonstrate scalability (to hundreds of millions of events) and improved accuracy for both ranking and return-time prediction versus Poisson, survival/HMM (STiC), SVD, and Poisson tensor factorization baselines.

Strengths
- Novel and bold formulation connecting Hawkes processes to low-rank collaborative modeling for time-sensitive recommendation. This directly addresses an important gap: predicting when, not just what.
- Elegant optimization idea: auxiliary-variable penalty to separate nonnegativity and nuclear norm, enabling a hybrid proximal/conditional-gradient scheme with cheap iterations (projections and top singular vectors). The analysis is careful and provides a clean O(1/t) rate and a condition (ρ > ρ*) under which the penalized and original problems coincide.
- Scalable implementation and large synthetic study; real-data results substantiate both tasks. The model handles entirely held-out user–item pairs (cold-start within matrix) using the low-rank inductive structure.
- Useful diagnostics: QQ-style time-change checks showing Hawkes better matches inter-event structure than memoryless survival models.
- Broad applicability and extensibility (context features, tensorization, nonparametric kernels) are well-motivated.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on unknown ρ*: The theoretical equivalence between the penalized and original problems requires ρ > ρ*, which depends on the unknown optimum. In practice ρ is tuned. This weakens the formal guarantee that the returned solution solves the original constrained problem. A continuation scheme in ρ, or a computable lower bound on ρ* (or a certificate ex post), would strengthen the contribution.
- Baselines: While the paper cites multidimensional Hawkes learning with sparsity/low-rank structure (e.g., Zhou et al., Du et al.), there is no direct comparison. Even if a full m×n multidimensional Hawkes is infeasible, constrained baselines (e.g., per-user or per-item Hawkes, or grouped items/users) would be informative. Also, tensor methods are included, but no time-aware CF baselines optimized for ranking (e.g., temporal factorization with implicit feedback) beyond SVD are reported.
- Modeling assumptions: The self-excitation is within each user–item pair only; there is no cross-excitation across items (e.g., a play of artist A influencing the near-term intensity for artist B). For media, shopping, or clinical comorbidities, such cross-effects can be important. The authors mention tensor extensions but do not evaluate them; even a small-scale cross-excitation ablation would help.
- Return-time estimation: Using Ogata thinning to sample return times for expectation introduces variance and algorithmic overhead. For the exponential kernel, closed-form or efficient numerical quadrature for E[T] might be feasible; at least report the number of samples, variance, and wall-clock.
- Evaluation metrics: Item ranking is evaluated by the rank of the true item. Reporting standard top-k metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k) and calibration of return-time predictions (e.g., coverage of prediction intervals) would make the practical impact clearer.
- Clarity of complexity: The paper claims linear scaling with events, but per-iteration time/memory breakdowns (e.g., cost of gradient via exponential-kernel recursions, SVD vector computations) and absolute wall-clock numbers on real datasets would strengthen the scalability claim.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The convexity of the negative log-likelihood given linear intensity is appropriate; the optimization scheme is well-motivated, with a clear convergence analysis. The main theoretical caveat is the reliance on an unknown ρ* for equivalence; nonetheless, the empirical success suggests the penalty approach is effective. Proof details are deferred to the appendix (acceptable).

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Algorithmic steps are concise; it would help to (i) state explicit per-iteration complexity and memory, (ii) explain the practical choice of ηk and how the Lipschitz constant L is obtained or estimated, and (iii) specify the number of thinning samples used. Minor typos exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
High. The fusion of low-rank collaborative modeling with Hawkes for time-sensitive recommendation, cast as a convex program and solved by a proximal–conditional-gradient hybrid with provable rate, is a fresh direction. While low-rank and Hawkes modeling each have prior art, this specific formulation and optimization design for recommendation/return-time is, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
High. Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are impactful problems. The proposed approach offers a principled, scalable way to leverage recurrent activity patterns and share strength across user–item pairs, with demonstrated gains. The optimization technique may also be of independent interest for broader convex problems combining nonnegativity and nuclear-norm penalties.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a continuation scheme for ρ and/or an empirical study of sensitivity to ρ; consider a stopping certificate that bounds the gap to the original problem.
- Add stronger temporal baselines (grouped/multidimensional Hawkes; temporal implicit CF) and report standard ranking metrics.
- Include an ablation on cross-excitation (even small scale) or a discussion of when within-pair excitation suffices.
- Report wall-clock times, memory usage, and sample counts for return-time estimation; consider deterministic approximations to E[T].
- Explore kernel robustness (e.g., a sum of exponentials) and show whether the method’s performance improves with richer kernels.

Pros
- Novel, well-motivated problem formulation; principled convex approach.
- Clever and efficient hybrid optimization with theory.
- Strong empirical results on both tasks; scalability.

Cons
- Theoretical equivalence relies on an unknown penalty threshold.
- Missing comparisons to closely related Hawkes learning baselines; limited exploration of cross-excitation.
- Evaluation metrics could be broader; complexity details sparse.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper introduces a compelling new direction—low-rank Hawkes for time-sensitive recommendation—with a neat optimization contribution and solid empirical evidence. Despite some missing baselines and theory-practice gaps around ρ, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.
