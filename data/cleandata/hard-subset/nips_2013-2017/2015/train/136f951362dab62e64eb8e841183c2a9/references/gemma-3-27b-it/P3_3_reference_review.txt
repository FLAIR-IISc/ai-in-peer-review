## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation by connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank models. The formulation is elegant, the proposed algorithm is well-motivated and demonstrably converges, and the experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets are compelling. While the connection to Hawkes processes isn’t entirely new in the recommender systems field (Kapoor et al. being the most relevant work), the low-rank extension and the specific algorithmic approach for solving the resulting optimization problem are significant contributions. I believe this paper is a strong submission and recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary of Ideas and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the critical problem of delivering personalized recommendations *at the right time*, moving beyond traditional approaches focused solely on predicting static preferences. It argues that modeling the temporal dynamics of user activity – specifically, the recurring patterns of interaction with items – is crucial for improving user engagement and for applications like targeted advertising and proactive healthcare. The core idea is to model user-item interactions as a Hawkes process, which captures self-exciting temporal dependencies. To handle the scalability challenges, the authors introduce a low-rank structure to the intensity function, leading to a novel convex formulation. They then develop an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to solve the optimization problem.

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of recommender systems and temporal point processes.  It acknowledges and builds upon the work of Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015) on survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models for return time prediction.  A key distinction highlighted (and accurately so) is the ability of their model to generalize to *new* items, unlike the prior work which focuses on predicting returns to *observed* items. The paper also appropriately cites foundational work in temporal point processes (Cox & Isham, Daley & Vere-Jones) and related optimization techniques.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling is a significant contribution. It provides a principled and scalable way to capture both the self-exciting temporal dynamics and the underlying latent factors governing user preferences.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous and well-justified. The convex optimization framework and the proposed algorithm are clearly presented and supported by a convergence analysis (Theorem 2). The approximation used to handle the non-negativity constraints is a clever approach.
* **Algorithm Efficiency:**  The algorithm, blending proximal and conditional gradient methods, is explicitly designed for scalability. The experimental results demonstrate its ability to handle large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events).
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents strong experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II).  The comparison with several baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, Tensor Factorization) consistently demonstrates the superior performance of the proposed approach. The inclusion of multiple datasets is valuable.
* **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivations are clearly explained, and the technical details are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.  Figure 1 effectively illustrates the problem setting and the overall modeling framework.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The analysis relating to the equivalence of the original and approximated formulations (Theorem 1) adds significant value and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying optimization problem.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Choice:** While the exponential kernel is a reasonable starting point, the paper acknowledges the possibility of other kernel choices (Weibull, Log-logistic). A more thorough exploration of the impact of different kernel shapes on performance would strengthen the results. Although, this might be more suited for an extended version or future work.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning ρ, λ, and β, but provides limited details on the specific tuning process.  A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the strategies used for setting these parameters would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** Although the scalability is demonstrated empirically, a more detailed computational complexity analysis of the proposed algorithm, specifically highlighting the computational cost of each step, would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Models:** While comparisons to standard recommender systems models are present, the field is rapidly evolving with deep learning-based approaches. A brief comparison, even if it demonstrates the continued advantage of the proposed method, would be worthwhile.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant technical contribution in combining Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling.
* Rigorous mathematical formulation and a well-motivated algorithm with convergence guarantees.
* Strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Addresses an important problem with practical relevance.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited exploration of alternative kernel choices.
* Limited details on hyperparameter tuning.
* Lack of comparison to more recent deep learning-based recommender systems.




**5. Overall Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper presents a novel, technically sound, and empirically validated approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of recommender systems and related areas.
