- Paper models each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process with baseline Λ0 and excitation scale A, and enforces low-rank structure via nuclear norms to generalize to unobserved pairs.
- Authors introduce an auxiliary-variable penalized formulation to decouple nonnegativity constraints from nuclear norms and propose a hybrid proximal–Frank–Wolfe optimization algorithm.
- They prove O(1/t) convergence for the composite method and recover O(1/t^2) when the nuclear norm is absent.
- Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II) show improved ranking and return-time MAE versus Poisson, STiC, SVD, and tensor Poisson factorization.
- Work connects Hawkes temporal point processes with low-rank recommendation and convex nuclear-norm estimation, related but distinct from multivariate Hawkes influence-network approaches.
- Differs from survival-analysis approaches by explicitly modeling history dependence and recurrent events via Hawkes processes.
- Strength: principled modeling that captures baseline preferences and short-term self-excitation relevant to return-time prediction and time-sensitive recommendation.
- Strength: convex estimation with a global optimum given a fixed kernel and nuclear-norm regularization preserving convexity.
- Strength: practical hybrid algorithm leveraging simple nonnegativity projections and rank-1 LMOs, with matching convergence analysis in limiting cases.
- Strength: demonstrated scalability (100M-event synthetic experiment) and computationally attractive use of top singular vectors per iteration.
- Strength: empirical improvements and use of Hawkes-implied survival functions for return-time evaluation instead of heuristics.
- Weakness: model only captures per user–item self-excitation, ignoring cross-item mutual excitation, competition, and substitution effects.
- Weakness: stability/identifiability concerns—paper does not enforce or analyze Hawkes stationarity conditions (e.g., A·∫γ < 1) or report violations.
- Weakness: equivalence theorem for the penalized formulation depends on unknown threshold ρ*, raising practical sensitivity concerns about chosen ρ.
- Weakness: optimization details lacking—how Lipschitz constant L is chosen, per-iteration cost (power iterations), and wall-clock runtimes on real data are not reported.
- Weakness: return-time prediction uses Ogata thinning sampling without reporting sampling budget m or its effect on MAE/runtime; analytic alternatives for exponential kernels are not explored.
- Weakness: baselines and metrics are incomplete—standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k) and stronger temporal baselines (TimeSVD++, dynamic Poisson factorization, multivariate Hawkes estimators) are missing.
- Weakness: generalization claims overstate cold-start abilities; low-rank completion requires observed users/items or additional features, but no feature experiments are shown.
- Weakness: data-splitting, preprocessing, kernel bandwidth selection, hyperparameter ranges, and convergence iteration counts are underreported, hindering reproducibility.
- Weakness: reported empirical superiority lacks statistical significance measures (confidence intervals or hypothesis tests).
- Questions: do you enforce or monitor Hawkes stability, and what fraction of pairs violate it after training?
- Questions: how sensitive are results to kernel bandwidth σ and was σ learned per item or per latent factor?
- Questions: what sampling budget m was used for return-time prediction and how does MAE vary with m?
- Questions: can you report Recall@k/NDCG@k and include TimeSVD++ and other temporal baselines?
- Questions: how does performance change if mutual excitation is modeled at the latent-factor level instead of per user–item pair?
- Clarity: paper is generally clear but needs tighter notation, explicit algorithmic details (step sizes, stopping criteria), and wall-clock timings.
- Significance: combines point processes and low-rank recommendation in a convex, scalable estimator with a useful hybrid solver, making a timely contribution despite related prior work.
- Pros: principled continuous-time model, convex globally-optimizable estimator, scalable algorithm with convergence guarantees and promising empirical results.
- Cons: limited cross-item dynamics and stability analysis, missing stronger temporal baselines and standard ranking metrics, and practical details to operationalize theoretical claims.
- Recommendation: borderline accept—lean positive for principled modeling and scalable convex approach but needs stronger baselines, stability analysis, and fuller empirical reporting.
- Confidence: reviewer is highly confident on methodology/related work and moderately confident about empirical assessment.
