- Tackles joint problems of time-sensitive recommendation (ranking) and return-time prediction using temporal point processes.  
- Models each user–item interaction as a univariate Hawkes process with exponential kernel; base intensity Λ0(u,i) for long-term preference and A(u,i) (α) for self-excitation/recency.  
- Imposes low-rank structure via nuclear-norm regularization on Λ0 and A to generalize to unobserved pairs and keep problem convex.  
- Proposes a hybrid optimization algorithm combining proximal steps (nonnegativity/projection) and conditional gradient/Frank–Wolfe (nuclear norm) using an auxiliary-variable penalty to decouple constraints.  
- Proves penalized and original constrained formulations are equivalent for sufficiently large penalty ρ and gives O(1/t) convergence for the composite algorithm.  
- Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) show improved ranking and return-time prediction versus Poisson, SVD, tensor factorization, and STiC survival baseline.  
- Time-rescaling Q–Q diagnostics indicate Hawkes fits data better than survival alternatives.  
- Builds on prior work in Hawkes processes, survival models, and low-rank temporal recommendation; novelty is convex low-rank formulation over user–item pairs and scalable hybrid solver.  
- Optimization approach relates to conditional gradient methods for norm-regularized problems but contributes a decoupling of non-negativity and nuclear norm with provable rate.  
- Strong problem relevance: useful for music, e-commerce, EHR and other time-sensitive recommender systems.  
- Modeling choice is appropriate: Hawkes naturally captures recency and low-rank sharing aids generalization.  
- Convex formulation and exponential kernels allow closed-form integrals for efficient likelihood computation.  
- The hybrid proximal–FW approach avoids full SVDs, uses top singular vectors per iteration, and has a clear convergence guarantee.  
- Scalability demonstrated with synthetic experiments up to ~100M events and linear-scaling evidence.  
- Empirical results are consistent and diagnostic tests support modeling assumptions.  
- Theorem on penalty equivalence depends on unknown ρ*; practical sensitivity to ρ is unclear and should be analyzed (suggest sensitivity plot/guidance).  
- Missing baselines: per-pair Hawkes, ADMM-based convex solver, nonnegative matrix factorization variants, and renewal/self-correcting processes.  
- Model ignores cross-excitation between items; need discussion or small-scale experiment justifying independence assumption.  
- Stability/branching ratio not reported or enforced; must report learned branching ratios and discuss explosion risk.  
- Evaluation metrics: ranking uses nonstandard metric (rank of true item); recommend adding Recall@k/Hit@k/NDCG@k and additional return-time metrics like NLL or prediction interval coverage.  
- Return-time prediction via Ogata sampling may be noisy/expensive; report number of samples m, runtime/variance, and consider deterministic alternatives (median/mode/quadrature).  
- Reproducibility: hyperparameter search ranges, validation splits, wall-clock times, memory usage, per-iteration costs, and code release are missing.  
- Clarity/notation issues: inconsistent notation (α vs A), missing definitions (L, D1, D2), unclear algorithmic details (step sizes, stopping criteria), and minor typos.  
- Originality: novel combination of low-rank Hawkes for user–item pairs with a scalable proximal–FW algorithm and convergence analysis.  
- Significance: likely useful for practitioners and researchers; extensible to include features/context.  
- Overall recommendation: lean accept, contingent on addressing evaluation/baseline gaps, penalty/stability discussion, and improved experimental/reporting details.  
- Key author questions: sensitivity/adaptation for ρ and σ; whether αu,i satisfy stability constraints; per-iteration cost vs ADMM; number of samples m for return-time prediction and faster surrogates; and how to incorporate user/item features.
