

# Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user activities through low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors propose a unified approach to two related problems: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting users' next returning time. The core insight is connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix factorization, which allows for efficient modeling of temporal dynamics while maintaining scalability.

The paper's main novelty lies in recognizing that temporal patterns in user activities (which are often overlooked in traditional recommendation systems) can be effectively modeled using Hawkes processes with low-rank structure. This is a bold conceptual leap from conventional recommendation approaches that focus primarily on static user-item preferences. The authors demonstrate how this framework can naturally handle both item recommendation (by ranking items based on their intensity functions) and returning time prediction (via sampling from the intensity function's density).

The theoretical contribution is significant, with the authors providing a convex formulation of the problem and developing a novel optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The algorithm achieves the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate, which is a notable improvement over existing approaches. The authors prove the convergence rate and demonstrate scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on both synthetic data (with ground-truth parameters) and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, and MIMIC II medical records). The results consistently show that their approach outperforms strong baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods) on both recommendation and returning time prediction tasks. The ablation studies on synthetic data effectively demonstrate the model's ability to recover low-rank parameters and scale with data size.

The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work. While Kapoor et al. [12, 11] also address returning time prediction, their approach cannot generalize to recommend new items. The authors convincingly argue that their low-rank Hawkes process framework overcomes this limitation. The connection to point processes is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the theoretical foundation.

The paper's strengths are substantial: the conceptual novelty, theoretical rigor, scalability demonstration, and practical applicability across multiple domains (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare). The writing is clear and well-organized, with appropriate mathematical formalization and sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main weaknesses are relatively minor. The paper doesn't deeply explore the limitations of the low-rank assumption (e.g., what happens when the true parameter matrices aren't low-rank), and the real-world evaluation, while solid, is limited to three datasets. However, these are acceptable limitations for a paper of this novelty.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in recommendation systems. It moves beyond the conventional static preference modeling to address a critical but underexplored aspect of user engagement: temporal dynamics. The authors successfully bridge point process theory with recommendation systems in a way that is both theoretically sound and practically valuable.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper's core contribution compelling. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation is genuinely new and offers a fresh perspective on time-sensitive recommendation. The empirical results validate the theoretical claims, and the approach has clear potential for broader adoption in industry applications.

The paper does not merely incrementally improve existing methods but rather introduces a fundamentally different approach to a key problem in recommendation systems. This aligns perfectly with the spirit of NeurIPS, which values bold, novel contributions that open new research directions.

I recommend accepting this paper. It offers a significant conceptual contribution that is likely to influence future work in temporal recommendation systems. The minor weaknesses in the evaluation scope do not outweigh the paper's substantial novelty and theoretical contributions.

Pro: Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation; strong theoretical guarantees; clear applications across multiple domains; comprehensive empirical evaluation.

Con: Limited exploration of low-rank assumption limitations; somewhat limited real-world dataset scope.

This paper makes a strong case for a new direction in recommendation systems research, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS.
