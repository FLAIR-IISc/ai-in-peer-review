

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations and returning-time prediction using low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, develop an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate superior performance on synthetic and real datasets. The problem is well-motivated, with clear applications in recommendation systems and healthcare. However, numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting issues, and theoretical contradictions prevent this paper from meeting NIPS standards.

**Major Technical Inconsistencies:**

1. The paper claims in the introduction that the algorithm "achieves the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate," but Theorem 2 states the convergence rate is O(1/t²). This is a critical contradiction that undermines the paper's technical credibility. The authors must clarify which rate is correct and reconcile this discrepancy.

2. In equation (1), the likelihood expression contains "∫ T λ(τ) dτ" which is mathematically ambiguous. The integral should be from tn to t, not "T." This is a fundamental notational error that affects the entire theoretical foundation.

3. The paper states "the two matrices of coefficients Λ0 and A contain too many parameters" but fails to quantify this issue or explain why the low-rank assumption is necessary for the problem at hand. This weakens the theoretical motivation.

**Formatting and Presentation Issues:**

1. The paper references Figure 1(a) in the introduction but fails to include the figure, creating a major presentation flaw.

2. Mathematical notation is consistently inconsistent: "λu,i0" vs "λ_{u,i}^0", "T u,i" vs "T_{u,i}", "w>u,i" vs "w_{u,i}^T", and inconsistent spacing around operators (e.g., "λ(t)dt = P {event" vs "λ(t)S(t|T)").

3. In Algorithm 1, the output is "Y1 = [Λ0;A]" but the algorithm outputs "Y^k" in the loop, creating confusion about the final output.

4. Theorem 1 claims "See appendix for the complete proof" but no appendix is provided with the submission, violating submission guidelines.

5. The reference list contains numerous formatting inconsistencies, with extra commas and missing information (e.g., "Springer,, ,").

**Technical Weaknesses:**

1. The paper claims "the algorithm scales up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events" but provides no concrete evidence of this scalability in the experiments. The synthetic data section mentions "100 million events" but doesn't demonstrate actual runtime or memory usage at scale.

2. In section 5.1, the authors state that "STiC... can only make recommendations specific to the few observed items visited before," but they don't provide evidence that the observed items in their datasets are indeed "few" compared to the total item space.

3. The paper doesn't address how the low-rank assumption might fail in real-world scenarios where user-item relationships might not be well-approximated by low-rank structures.

4. The experimental setup for the "Heldout group" is unclear. How exactly were the sequences held out? The paper states "for each user, we randomly pick 20-percent of all the items she has consumed and hold out the entire sequence of events," but it's unclear how this differs from the training/testing split.

**Minor but Significant Issues:**

1. The paper claims "the intensity function λu,i(t) dominates the point patterns along time" but doesn't provide any evidence for this claim beyond the mathematical formulation.

2. In section 3.4, the returning-time prediction method uses "Ogata's thinning algorithm" but doesn't provide sufficient details about implementation choices for this sampling method.

3. The paper states "we can readily generalize to incorporate other contextual information" but provides no experimental evidence supporting this claim.

**Strengths:**

- The problem is well-motivated with clear applications in recommendation systems and healthcare.
- The connection between point processes and low-rank models is novel and potentially valuable.
- The optimization approach combines two complementary techniques in an interesting way.
- The experimental results on synthetic data are promising, and the real-world datasets are appropriate for the problem.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper addresses an important problem with a potentially valuable approach, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and theoretical contradictions significantly undermine its credibility. The contradiction in the convergence rate claim is particularly serious as it directly relates to the main theoretical contribution. The inconsistent mathematical notation throughout the paper creates substantial barriers to understanding the methodology. These issues, while seemingly minor individually, collectively indicate a lack of careful review and preparation that would be unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

The paper's potential significance is overshadowed by these technical shortcomings. Given NIPS's emphasis on technical rigor and precise presentation, I recommend rejection. The authors should address these fundamental issues before resubmitting to a venue that values meticulous attention to detail.
