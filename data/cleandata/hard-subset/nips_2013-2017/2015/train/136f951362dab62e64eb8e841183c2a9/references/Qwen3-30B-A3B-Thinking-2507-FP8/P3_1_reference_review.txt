

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the important problem of time-sensitive recommendations, where the goal is to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment, rather than just predicting user preferences. The authors propose modeling user-item interactions as low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes, where the intensity function captures both baseline preferences (long-term) and triggering effects from past interactions (short-term). The key innovation is the convex formulation of the problem with nuclear norm regularization, combined with a novel optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The paper demonstrates the approach on both synthetic and real-world datasets for two tasks: item recommendation and returning-time prediction. The work builds on the temporal point processes literature (particularly Hawkes processes) and connects it to recommendation systems, which is an interesting and underexplored intersection.

The paper relates to previous work by acknowledging the limitations of traditional recommendation systems (which focus on one-time preference prediction) and survival analysis approaches (which cannot generalize to new items). The closest related work is Kapoor et al.'s STiC, which focuses on returning time prediction but cannot recommend new items.

## Detailed Review

### Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a solid theoretical foundation. The Hawkes process modeling of user-item interactions is well-motivated and appropriate for the problem. The convex formulation with nuclear norm regularization is a good choice to handle the high dimensionality of user-item interactions, and the convergence analysis (Theorem 2) appears rigorous.

However, I have several concerns about the experimental evaluation:

1. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The paper compares with Poisson processes, SVD, Tensor factorization, and STiC, but fails to include recent neural approaches for temporal recommendation, such as DeepHawkes (Zhou et al., 2016), Temporal Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) for recommendation, or Transformer-based approaches. This omission weakens the claim that their method achieves "superb predictive performance."

2. **Modest Performance Gains**: The performance improvements over baselines (particularly in real-world datasets) are modest (e.g., 5-10% improvement in MAE). While statistically significant, these gains may not be practically significant for real-world applications. The paper doesn't provide a clear justification for why these modest gains are worth the added complexity of the Hawkes process model.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show the contribution of different components (e.g., low-rank assumption, Hawkes process vs. Poisson process, different kernel functions). This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the model are truly driving the performance improvements.

4. **Real-World Dataset Size**: The real-world datasets (last.fm with ~1,000 users and ~3,000 artists; tmall.com with ~26,000 users and ~2,500 stores) are relatively small compared to modern recommendation system datasets. The paper claims scalability to "millions of user-item pairs," but the experiments don't demonstrate this on large-scale datasets.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The background on temporal point processes is concise and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning. The model description and optimization algorithm are well-explained.

However, there are some areas where clarity could be improved:

1. **Notation Consistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for key components (e.g., "Λ0" for base intensity matrix, "A" for self-exciting matrix). In the Hawkes process literature, it's common to use different notations for these components (e.g., "μ" for baseline intensity, "α" for triggering strength), which would make the paper more accessible to readers from the point process community.

2. **Missing Technical Details**: Some key details are relegated to the appendix (e.g., proof of Theorem 1), which is acceptable for a conference paper, but the paper would be stronger if it included more of this information in the main text.

3. **Connection to Related Work**: The paper mentions Kapoor et al.'s work as most related, but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon these methods. A more detailed comparison with recent methods would strengthen the paper.

### Originality

The core idea of using low-rank Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendations is novel. The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization for recommendation systems is not common in the literature, which makes this a meaningful contribution.

However, the paper could better articulate its novelty:

1. The paper mentions that their model "can be readily generalized to incorporate other contextual information," but doesn't provide concrete examples or experiments demonstrating this capability.

2. The optimization algorithm (blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods) is interesting, but the paper doesn't clearly state how this differs from previous work on combining these methods. The claim that it's "first of its kind" for handling both nuclear norm and non-negativity constraints is strong but not fully justified with a thorough literature review.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the fact that there are many recent neural approaches for temporal recommendation that might achieve similar or better results with less complex modeling assumptions.

### Significance

The problem of time-sensitive recommendations is indeed important for real-world applications like personalized services, health monitoring, and ad targeting. The paper demonstrates the approach on several relevant real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), which is a strength.

However, the significance could be stronger:

1. **Practical Impact**: The paper mentions that predicting returning times could help with ad bidding, but doesn't quantify the potential economic benefit. How much would a company save by using this method instead of a simpler time-based heuristic? This is crucial for establishing the real-world significance of the work.

2. **Comparison with Simple Heuristics**: The paper doesn't compare against simple time-based heuristics (e.g., recommending items based on time of day or day of week), which are commonly used in practice and might be competitive with the proposed method.

3. **Lack of Case Studies**: The paper doesn't provide concrete case studies showing how the method would be deployed in real applications (e.g., "For a music streaming service, using this model would increase user engagement by X%").

## Overall Assessment

### Strengths:
- Well-motivated problem addressing an important gap in recommendation systems
- Solid theoretical foundation with a novel convex formulation
- Rigorous convergence analysis for the proposed optimization algorithm
- Good experimental design with both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Clear connection to the temporal point processes literature

### Weaknesses:
- Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods (particularly neural approaches)
- Modest performance gains over baselines that may not justify the complexity
- Incomplete evaluation of model components (no ablation studies)
- Real-world datasets are relatively small for the claimed scalability
- Lack of clear justification for practical significance and economic impact

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper presents a technically sound approach to an important problem with a novel convex formulation and a well-analyzed optimization algorithm. However, the experimental evaluation is not as comprehensive as it could be, and the practical significance of the method is not fully established.

To strengthen the paper, I would recommend:
1. Adding comparisons with recent neural approaches for temporal recommendation (e.g., DeepHawkes, RNN-based methods)
2. Including ablation studies to show the contribution of different model components
3. Providing a clearer justification for the practical significance of the method (e.g., quantifying potential economic benefits)
4. Expanding the real-world evaluation to larger datasets to better demonstrate scalability
5. Improving the connection to related work, particularly recent neural methods

With these revisions, this paper could make a strong contribution to the field and be a valuable addition to NIPS proceedings.

## Final Verdict

The paper is technically sound and addresses an important problem, but the experimental evaluation and significance claims need strengthening. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
