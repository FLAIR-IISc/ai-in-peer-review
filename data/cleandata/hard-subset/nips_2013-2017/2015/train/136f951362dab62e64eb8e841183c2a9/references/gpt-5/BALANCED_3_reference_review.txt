Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses two linked problems in time-sensitive recommendation: (1) ranking items at the right moment and (2) predicting users’ next return time. The authors model each user–item pair as a univariate Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = λ0(u,i) + α(u,i)∑j γ(t, tu,ij). To enable generalization across the large, sparse user–item space, they posit that the matrices Λ0 and A (collecting λ0 and α over all pairs) are low rank, enforced via nuclear norms. They formulate parameter estimation as a convex program: minimizing the negative log-likelihood of Hawkes processes plus nuclear-norm penalties under non-negativity constraints.

A technical difficulty is the combination of non-negativity constraints with non-smooth nuclear norm penalties. The paper proposes an alternative penalty formulation introducing auxiliary variables Z1, Z2 and a quadratic penalty ρ‖Λ0−Z1‖^2_F + ρ‖A−Z2‖^2_F, then designs an algorithm that alternates a proximal gradient step for the non-negative block and a conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) step for the nuclear-norm block, with an accelerated scheme. They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate for the composite method and give a condition (ρ > ρ*) under which the penalized and original formulations share the same optimum. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II) show improvements in item ranking and return-time prediction over Poisson baselines, survival/semi-HMM (STiC), SVD, and tensor factorization with Poisson regression. A time-change diagnostic suggests Hawkes provides a better generative fit than survival models.

The work connects to: Hawkes processes and learning triggering kernels (Hawkes 1971; Zhou et al., AISTATS/ICML), low-rank structure in Hawkes networks (Zhou et al., AISTATS), time-aware CF such as TimeSVD++ (Koren) and Poisson/tensor factorization (Xiong et al., Rendle, Chi & Kolda), and return-time prediction via survival analysis and HSMM (Kapoor et al.). Relative to these, the novelty lies in a convex low-rank Hawkes formulation for user–item modeling and a blended proximal/conditional-gradient solver tailored to non-negativity plus nuclear norms.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for modeling recurrent behavior and timing; using Hawkes captures self-excitation at the pair level.
- Convex formulation with nuclear norms enables transfer to unobserved pairs and avoids nonconvex factorization pitfalls.
- Algorithmic contribution is practical and principled; the block-wise prox/FW scheme neatly separates constraints and penalties, and the convergence analysis is solid for the proposed surrogate.
- Large-scale synthetic study and multiple real datasets; metrics cover both ranking and next-event timing.
- The time-change diagnostic is a nice sanity check supporting Hawkes over i.i.d. gap models.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Stability/branching conditions: For Hawkes with exponential kernels, stationarity typically requires α(u,i)∫γ ≤ 1 (branching ratio < 1). The paper does not enforce or analyze this, nor discuss implications for estimation and prediction.
- Identifiability and confounding: With λ0 and α both nonnegative and linear in the intensity, their contributions can be confounded. The paper does not study identifiability or provide sensitivity analyses (e.g., to the kernel bandwidth σ).
- Penalized equivalence: The theorem requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice ρ is tuned; more guidance or empirical sensitivity to ρ would help, as well as a discussion of the gap when ρ < ρ*.
- Baselines: Important temporal recommendation baselines are missing, notably Dynamic Poisson Factorization (e.g., Charlin et al., NIPS 2015) and other continuous/discrete-time point-process recommenders. Tensor methods are considered, but comparisons to DPF or time-evolving PF would strengthen claims.
- Modeling scope: Each user–item pair is modeled independently (self-exciting only). Cross-item effects (e.g., artist-to-artist influence, disease comorbidity) are not modeled, although they are central in some applications. Even a simple ablation or small-scale cross-excitation experiment would be informative.
- Practical details: Limited information on wall-clock time, hardware, memory, and convergence criteria; unclear how L or step sizes are set in practice. The return-time prediction relies on MC sampling; its computational cost and variance are not discussed.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details and hyperparameter selection procedures (including σ) are sparse; no code availability is mentioned.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The convexity of the negative log-likelihood with linear intensities plus nuclear norms is appropriate. The algorithm is well-motivated and the convergence analysis for the surrogate problem is convincing. However, omission of Hawkes stability constraints is a notable gap, and theoretical generalization or statistical consistency is not addressed.
- Empirical support: Results are promising, but the fairness and breadth of baselines could be improved; confidence intervals or statistical significance are not shown.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized. Some notational overload (e.g., mixing feature vectors φ, ψ and kernel γ) could be streamlined. Minor typos persist. Provide explicit gradients and complexity per iteration. Clarify how L is estimated and how ηk is chosen in practice.

Originality
- Combining nuclear-norm low-rank regularization with Hawkes for user–item modeling is a valuable idea; the block prox/FW algorithm under non-negativity plus nuclear norm is a neat contribution. Related low-rank Hawkes work exists (Zhou et al.), but this paper’s bipartite formulation and solver are distinct.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the approach is useful for several domains (recommendation, health). The impact would be higher with stronger baselines, analysis of stability/identifiability, and demonstration of cross-excitation or contextual extensions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Convex low-rank Hawkes formulation for recommendation is timely and relevant.
- Algorithmic contribution is principled and potentially reusable beyond this application.
- Strong synthetic and real-data performance; large-scale feasibility suggested.
- Diagnostic evidence that Hawkes better fits recurrent behavior than survival models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing stability constraints and identifiability discussion for Hawkes parameters.
- Incomplete baselines (e.g., dynamic Poisson factorization) and limited ablations.
- Practical details (runtime, hyperparameters, step sizes) and statistical reporting are thin.
- The equivalence theorem is of limited practical guidance since ρ* is unknown; sensitivity to ρ not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce or analyze Hawkes stability (e.g., α(u,i)∫γ < 1), report the proportion of pairs violating it, and assess impact on predictions.
- Add comparisons to dynamic Poisson factorization/time-evolving MF; consider a simple multi-dimensional Hawkes with limited cross-excitation as an ablation.
- Provide runtime/memory scaling figures, iteration-wise wall-clock, and details on step-size selection and L estimation; add CIs over multiple splits.
- Include sensitivity analyses for ρ, λ, β, and σ; discuss identifiability and regularization trade-offs between Λ0 and A.
- Validate contextual extensions on at least one dataset (e.g., item metadata or location).
- Release code or detailed pseudo-code sufficient for reproduction.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This is a promising and well-motivated paper with a solid optimization contribution and encouraging empirical results. However, the omission of Hawkes stability considerations, limited baselines, and lack of key experimental and methodological details make it borderline for NeurIPS. Addressing the above issues would substantially strengthen the work.
