Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles two related problems: (1) time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given time), and (2) predicting user return times. It models each user–item pair as a univariate self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij). Here Λ0 captures long-term baseline preference and A scales the short-term self-excitation from past events via a triggering kernel (exponential in experiments). To transfer knowledge across unobserved user–item pairs, the paper imposes low-rank structure on the matrices Λ0 and A via nuclear norm regularization with nonnegativity constraints, yielding a convex estimation problem.

A key algorithmic contribution is an optimization method for the constrained, nuclear-norm regularized negative log-likelihood: introduce auxiliary variables Z1, Z2 with quadratic penalties (Λ0 − Z1, A − Z2) and treat (Λ0, A) via projected gradient (simple nonnegativity projection) while handling (Z1, Z2) with a conditional gradient (LMO) on the nuclear norm. An accelerated scheme with δk = 2/(k+1) and step sizes tied to a Lipschitz constant is used. The paper states an O(1/t) convergence bound for the composite objective and provides a sufficient condition (ρ larger than a problem-dependent threshold) under which the penalized formulation is equivalent to the original constrained problem.

Experiments cover large synthetic data (∼100M events over ≈10^3×10^3 user–item pairs) and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II). Baselines include Poisson (constant intensity), STiC (survival/semi-Markov), SVD (implicit feedback), and Poisson tensor factorization with time bins. The method outperforms baselines on item ranking and return-time MAE, including on entirely-held-out user–item sequences. The time-change theorem Q–Q plots suggest the Hawkes assumption fits real temporal patterns better than i.i.d. gap models.

Strengths
- Formulation: Clear, convex estimation problem for a Hawkes-based recommendation model with low-rank structure across user–item pairs; separates long-term baseline and short-term excitation in a principled way.
- Algorithm: Practical solver combining projected gradient (for nonnegativity) and conditional gradient (for nuclear norms) that avoids full SVDs; per-iteration operations are scalable (top singular vectors via LMO).
- Theory: Convergence analysis with explicit O(1/t) rate for the composite scheme; discussion of equivalence between penalized and constrained formulations (under a sufficient ρ).
- Evaluation: Large-scale synthetic benchmark and diverse real datasets; consistent improvements on both time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction; additional model-checking via time-change theorem.
- Scope: Natural extensions to context features and nonparametric kernels are outlined; the approach directly addresses cold-start across user–item pairs via low-rank sharing.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Exactness of penalty reformulation: Quadratic penalty methods are not generally exact; the stated equivalence theorem requires an unknown problem-dependent threshold ρ*. This limits guidance for choosing ρ and weakens the guarantee that solutions strictly satisfy Z=Λ, Z=A. Providing a constructive lower bound or adopting augmented Lagrangian/ADMM with convergence guarantees under nonnegativity would strengthen the theory.
- Stability of Hawkes processes: For exponential kernels, stationarity typically requires the branching ratio α ∫ γ < 1. The paper does not impose or discuss such constraints. Without them, intensities can grow unbounded in principle; even on finite windows, this may affect estimation and prediction. Clarification on whether the learned A respects stability (e.g., via implicit regularization) or adding constraints would improve soundness.
- Modeling assumptions: The process per user–item pair is strictly self-exciting; there is no cross-excitation across items or within a user’s item set, which may limit realism in domains with substitution or complementary effects. Related Hawkes-network models (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS/ICML 2013; Du et al., AISTATS/KDD 2015) consider mutually-exciting structures; comparisons to such baselines are missing.
- Return-time prediction: The expected return time is approximated via Monte Carlo sampling (thinning). For exponential kernels and piecewise-exponential intensities, numerical quadrature or root finding on the inverse survival function could yield faster, deterministic estimates; runtime/accuracy trade-offs are not discussed.
- Experimental details: Some implementation specifics are missing, e.g., computational cost per iteration (top singular vector computation method and tolerance), total runtime vs. baselines, memory footprint for 100M events, and sensitivity to kernel bandwidth. Baseline tuning (e.g., number of time bins for tensor methods) and fairness controls could be elaborated.
- Notation/clarity: Minor notation inconsistencies (e.g., references to “feature” ψ in 3.3), and Algorithm 1 initialization statements are ambiguous.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The convex modeling and likelihood derivations are standard for linear Hawkes and well-founded; the optimization scheme is plausible with a reasonable convergence bound. The exactness claim for the penalty method is the most debatable aspect; at minimum, the practical implication is that ρ is tuned rather than guaranteed.
- Empirical support: Results indicate gains over reasonable baselines, including cold-start on held-out pairs. However, stronger time-series baselines (e.g., nonhomogeneous Poisson GLMs, Hawkes with shared parameters or mutually-exciting structures) are not included, so the empirical margin against the closest prior Hawkes models remains unquantified.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with clear motivation and algorithmic presentation. Some algorithmic details and notation could be clarified, and additional complexity analysis would help.

Originality
Moderate. Low-rank structure with Hawkes processes has appeared in prior work (e.g., sparse low-rank networks). The novelty lies in: focusing on time-sensitive recommendation/return-time at the user–item-pair level with nuclear norms on Λ0 and A; the blended proximal/conditional gradient solver under nonnegativity; and the specific application and evaluation at scale.

Significance
Practical significance is solid: time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction are relevant, and the method scales to large event logs. Methodological significance is incremental but useful, particularly the solver for nonnegative, nuclear-norm regularized Hawkes estimation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a constructive bound or adaptive scheme for ρ, or use an augmented Lagrangian approach to ensure exact satisfaction of Z=Λ, Z=A at convergence.
- Discuss and, if possible, enforce Hawkes stability constraints; report empirical branching ratios.
- Compare with mutually-exciting Hawkes baselines that share structure across items/users.
- Detail computational complexity, runtime, and memory, including the cost of sampling for return-time predictions; consider deterministic approximations for expected times.
- Clarify algorithm initialization and notation; include implementation details for computing top singular vectors.

Arguments for acceptance
- Convex, scalable model for time-sensitive recommendation with solid empirical gains.
- Useful optimization contribution with convergence guarantees and practical efficiency.
- Careful experimental setup including cold-start evaluation and model checking.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical gap regarding the exactness of the quadratic penalty reformulation.
- Limited comparisons to the most closely related Hawkes-based baselines and absence of stability analysis.
- Modeling excludes cross-excitation, potentially limiting applicability in some domains.
