Summary
The paper tackles two tightly coupled problems in time-sensitive personalization: (1) recommending the right item at the right time and (2) predicting user return times. The core idea is to model each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process whose intensity has a nonnegative baseline term (long-term affinity) plus a history-dependent triggering term (short-term recency effects). To generalize across unobserved user–item pairs, the authors impose low-rank structure (via nuclear norms) on the matrices of baselines and excitation magnitudes across all pairs. They formulate parameter estimation as a convex program with nonnegativity constraints and nuclear-norm penalties, and propose an optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradients (to handle nonnegativity) with conditional gradients/LMO (to handle nuclear norms) under a penalty reformulation that decouples constraints. They prove equivalence of the penalty reformulation to the original problem for sufficiently large penalty and provide a convergence guarantee with O(1/t) rate. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, and MIMIC-II) show improved item ranking at event times and reduced return-time MAE vs. Poisson, survival-model baselines (STiC), SVD, and Poisson tensor factorization. Model checking via time-change theorem suggests Hawkes better fits observed temporal patterns than i.i.d. gap models.

Relation to prior work
The work connects multivariate/self-exciting point processes (Hawkes) to recommendation, extending beyond survival/hazard models aimed at single terminal events. It builds on Hawkes process learning (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song) but shifts focus to personalized, time-sensitive recommendation with low-rank transfer. Compared to STiC/hazard-based return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.), this approach can recommend unseen items and models recurrent excitation. Relative to classic matrix/tensor factorization for implicit feedback, it replaces time discretization with continuous-time self-excitation and provides a convex, low-rank formulation.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, unified treatment of time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction via low-rank Hawkes processes. The connection between self-excitation and collaborative low-rank transfer is compelling.
- Methodological contribution: A convex formulation with nonnegativity and nuclear norms; the penalty-based decoupling and hybrid proximal–conditional gradient algorithm is elegant and practical, with clear per-iteration oracles and an O(1/t) rate.
- Scalability: Empirical evidence of linear scaling and handling up to 100M events in synthetic data.
- Empirical relevance: Demonstrates both ranking-at-time and return-time prediction; includes model checking via time-change theorem to justify the Hawkes assumption on real data.
- Generalizability: The framework naturally accommodates contextual features and alternate kernels; the optimization approach could be reused for other nonnegative, low-rank convex problems.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Stability/branching ratio: Hawkes stability conditions (e.g., α ∫ γ < 1) are not discussed or enforced. Without constraints, intensities could in principle diverge; even if rare in data, some theoretical or practical treatment would strengthen the model.
- Identifiability and interpretability: Baseline vs. excitation trade-offs can be hard to disentangle; no sensitivity or ablation (e.g., fixing/excluding excitation) is reported.
- Return-time inference: Monte Carlo sampling via thinning is workable but potentially expensive; no discussion of variance, number of samples, or alternatives (e.g., mode or quantile via numerical root finding) for exponential kernels.
- Baselines: For ranking, baselines are largely static (SVD, Poisson, Poisson-tensor). The comparison lacks stronger sequential models (e.g., timeSVD++, factorization with temporal dynamics) and, if permissible by timeline, sequence models. That said, the continuous-time orientation differentiates this work from discrete-time baselines.
- Hyperparameters: Limited guidance on choosing the penalty parameter ρ beyond tuning; Theorem 1’s threshold ρ* is not operational. More practical guidance or a continuation scheme would help.
- Cross-excitation: The model assumes self-excitation within each user–item pair and transfers via low rank, but does not capture cross-item influences (e.g., a song triggering another) or user–user couplings; this is an opportunity for future extensions.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the negative log-likelihood is convex under the chosen parametrization; the nuclear norm leads to a convex relaxation of rank. The penalty reformulation is standard; the hybrid optimization method is well-motivated, and the convergence analysis is plausible (though details are deferred). Experiments are thoughtfully designed and include both synthetic recovery/scaling and real datasets with appropriate metrics. Some omissions (stability constraints, ablations, and richer baselines) temper the completeness but do not undermine the main claims.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The likelihood derivation and feature construction (φ, ψ) are helpful. The algorithm description is concise; small additions (explicit gradients, step-size guidance, complexity per iteration, and pseudocode formatting) would improve reproducibility. Minor typos/notation inconsistencies appear but are not obstructive.

Originality
High. The low-rank Hawkes formulation for time-sensitive recommendation and the proximal–conditional gradient hybrid under nonnegativity/nuclear-norm constraints are novel and timely. The paper opens a distinct direction relative to common discrete-time or survival-based approaches.

Significance
High potential impact. Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are important in multiple domains (media, e-commerce, healthcare). A scalable, convex, continuous-time framework with transfer across pairs is valuable and likely to spur follow-up work (e.g., mutual excitation, richer kernels, context).

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss/ensure Hawkes stability; add a constraint or a penalty on α ∫ γ to keep branching ratio < 1.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for σ, λ, β, and ρ; include ablations (baseline-only, excitation-only, without nuclear norm).
- Report computational cost for return-time sampling and accuracy vs. number of samples.
- If space allows, compare with a time-aware MF baseline (e.g., timeSVD++) or stronger sequential recommenders; or clarify why such comparisons are impractical for continuous-time settings.
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Pros
- Novel, bold formulation bridging Hawkes processes and low-rank recommendation
- Elegant convex program with a scalable, provable algorithm
- Strong empirical results across tasks and datasets, plus model checking
- Broad applicability and extensibility

Cons
- Missing stability constraint/discussion for Hawkes
- Limited ablations and guidance on ρ; sampling-based return-time prediction left unquantified
- Baselines for time-aware ranking could be stronger
- No modeling of cross-excitation across items/users

Recommendation
Accept (8/10). Despite some methodological and empirical gaps, the paper introduces a significant new concept and direction with solid theory and promising results.

Confidence
High (0.8).
