### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, which consists of two tasks: recommending the most relevant item at a specific moment and predicting a user's next return time to an item/service. The authors propose a unified framework that models the sequence of interactions for each user-item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The key novelty lies in formulating this problem as a low-rank matrix estimation task. Specifically, the intensity of the Hawkes process for a user-item pair is determined by a base intensity and a self-excitation coefficient. The authors assume that the matrices of these parameters across all users and items are low-rank, allowing for knowledge transfer to sparse or unobserved user-item pairs, a critical aspect of recommendation systems.

This approach connects the theory of temporal point processes with low-rank models, a well-established technique in collaborative filtering. The problem is formulated as a convex optimization problem involving the minimization of the negative log-likelihood of the observed event sequences, regularized by the nuclear norms of the parameter matrices to enforce low-rank structure, along with non-negativity constraints. To solve this challenging optimization problem, the authors propose a novel and efficient algorithm that blends the proximal gradient method (to handle non-negativity) and the conditional gradient method (to handle the nuclear norm). They provide a theoretical analysis showing that this algorithm achieves an optimal O(1/t) convergence rate.

The proposed method is evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) for both item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks. It is compared against several baselines, including static models (SVD, Poisson process) and other temporal models (STiC, Tensor Factorization). The experimental results demonstrate the superior performance and scalability of the proposed approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and significant connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for the recommendation task. While Hawkes processes and low-rank models are individually established, their synthesis to address time-sensitive recommendation is new and well-motivated. This provides a principled way to model the continuous-time dynamics of user behavior while handling the inherent data sparsity of recommendation problems. The problem itself is of high practical importance for web services, advertising, and healthcare.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The model is built on a solid theoretical foundation of point processes. The convex formulation with nuclear norm regularization is well-justified. A significant technical contribution is the development of a new optimization algorithm that efficiently handles the combined non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides strong theoretical guarantees for this algorithm, showing that it achieves the optimal rate, which is a commendable result.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It starts with a clear motivation, provides necessary background on temporal point processes, systematically develops the model formulation, details the optimization algorithm, and presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The distinction from related work, such as survival analysis models [11, 12] that cannot generalize to new items, is clearly articulated.

4.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The use of synthetic data effectively validates the algorithm's convergence, scalability, and ability to recover ground-truth parameters. The evaluation on three distinct real-world datasets demonstrates the model's general applicability. The inclusion of two distinct evaluation tasks (item recommendation and return-time prediction) provides a holistic assessment of the model's capabilities. Furthermore, the use of the time-change theorem to qualitatively validate the Hawkes process assumption (Figure 3) is a nice touch that adds confidence in the modeling choices.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Model Assumptions and Generalizations:** The experiments rely on a simple exponential triggering kernel. While the paper mentions that the framework supports more complex or non-parametric kernels, the sensitivity of the model's performance to this choice is not explored. The assumption of a single kernel shape for all user-item interactions might be a simplification, as different dynamics could govern different types of items or users.

2.  **Practicality of the Optimization Formulation:** The alternative formulation (Equation 5) used for optimization depends on a penalty parameter ρ. Theorem 1 guarantees equivalence to the original problem only for ρ greater than a problem-dependent threshold ρ*. In practice, determining ρ* is difficult, and the authors resort to tuning it as a hyperparameter. While this is a standard practical approach, it slightly weakens the connection to the original problem formulation, as there is no guarantee that the tuned ρ satisfies the theoretical condition.

3.  **Details of Implementation and Complexity:** The paper could provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm. The linear minimization oracle (LMO) in the conditional gradient step requires computing the top singular vector pair of a gradient matrix. While this is computationally cheaper than a full SVD, its cost can still be significant for large user-item matrices. A discussion on the practical complexity and potential use of randomized SVD methods would have been beneficial for reproducibility and for assessing its applicability in even larger-scale industrial settings.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The paper proposes a novel and elegant framework for a significant and practical problem.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the new optimization algorithm and its convergence analysis, are solid and could be of independent interest.
*   The empirical results are strong, comprehensive, and demonstrate clear superiority over relevant state-of-the-art methods across multiple datasets and tasks.
*   The work is well-written, clearly motivated, and positions itself well within the existing literature.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   The empirical validation is based on a simplified model assumption (exponential kernel) without exploring more flexible alternatives mentioned in the text.
*   The practical choice of the hyperparameter ρ is not directly guided by the theory that justifies the alternative optimization formulation.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, technically sound, and empirically validated contribution to an important area. The fusion of point process theory with collaborative filtering techniques is a promising direction, and the proposed optimization algorithm is a valuable contribution in its own right.
