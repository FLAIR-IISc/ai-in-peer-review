- Proposes modeling user activities as low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Introduces a convex formulation connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models to enable transfer across user-item pairs.
- Develops an efficient optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods with convergence guarantees (Theorem 2).
- Targets two problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting user return times.
- Motivated by limitations of static recommendation approaches and sparsity/noise in traditional feedback signals.
- Distinct from Kapoor et al.’s survival-analysis methods because it can recommend new (unobserved) items.
- Builds on temporal point processes / Hawkes processes and extends them with low-rank structure—novel contribution.
- Experimental evaluation includes synthetic data and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) showing superior performance over strong baselines.
- Ablation studies indicate model scalability and confirm the importance of the low-rank assumption.
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and shows optimal convergence behavior combining both algorithmic families.
- Weakness: practical computational complexity and actual time-to-convergence for very large-scale settings (millions of pairs) not fully analyzed.
- Weakness: limited comparisons to other recent temporal recommendation work (e.g., Xiong et al., Du et al.).
- Weakness: healthcare application (MIMIC II) is evaluated but its potential impact on clinical decision-making is not demonstrated.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, with consistent notation and reproducible experimental descriptions.
- Some implementation/practical details are unclear, e.g., how kernel bandwidth is selected and how theory maps to practice.
- Originality: novel combination of low-rank matrix models with Hawkes processes and a tailored optimization method under non-negativity constraints.
- Critique: insufficient differentiation from tensor-factorization/other temporal approaches and reliance on a standard exponential triggering kernel without exploring alternatives.
- Significance: addresses an important, timely problem with broad applicability across domains and both theoretical and empirical support.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, novel algorithm with guarantees, comprehensive experiments, and high relevance.
- Arguments against acceptance: need for deeper related-work comparison, more practical scalability analysis, and stronger validation of the healthcare use case.
- Overall recommendation: reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
- Suggested improvements: expand related work comparisons, add practical implementation and complexity discussion, clarify kernel-bandwidth selection, and better articulate healthcare impacts.
