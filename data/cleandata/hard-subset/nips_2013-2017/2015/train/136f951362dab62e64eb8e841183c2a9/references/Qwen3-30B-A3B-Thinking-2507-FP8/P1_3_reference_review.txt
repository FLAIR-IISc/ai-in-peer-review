

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary

This paper addresses an important gap in recommendation systems: most existing approaches focus on predicting one-time user preferences (e.g., ratings), while failing to capture the temporal dynamics of user behavior. The authors propose modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes to solve two time-sensitive recommendation problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) accurately predicting the next returning time of users. The key innovation is connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models, developing a convex optimization formulation, and creating a novel algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. This work differs from related approaches (e.g., Kapoor et al.'s survival analysis methods) by enabling recommendations for new items, not just previously observed ones.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper establishes a clear connection between temporal point processes and low-rank matrix models, providing a theoretically sound convex formulation. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) demonstrates the algorithm achieves the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2. **Comprehensive experiments**: The authors evaluate their approach on both synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II medical data) across multiple metrics. The experiments thoroughly compare with state-of-the-art baselines (Poisson, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization) and demonstrate superior performance on both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

3. **Practical significance**: The work addresses a critical real-world problem where timing matters (e.g., music streaming, healthcare), with clear applications in improving user engagement and marketing strategies. The method scales to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events, making it applicable to large-scale systems.

4. **Novel algorithm**: The proposed optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) effectively combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to handle non-negative constraints and nuclear norm regularization, which is a significant contribution to the optimization literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent tensor-based methods**: While the paper compares with tensor factorization approaches, it doesn't adequately address the most recent work on tensor-based temporal recommendation (e.g., "Temporal Collaborative Filtering with Bayesian Probabilistic Tensor Factorization" by Xiong et al., KDD 2017), which might have more relevant comparisons.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contributions of specific components (e.g., the low-rank assumption vs. the Hawkes process structure). It would be valuable to know how much of the performance gain comes from the low-rank structure versus the temporal modeling.

3. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The authors don't thoroughly discuss scenarios where their approach might fail (e.g., when the low-rank assumption is invalid, or for users with very sparse interaction histories). This is particularly important since the paper claims the method can generalize to new items.

4. **Insufficient explanation of parameter sensitivity**: While the paper mentions tuning hyperparameters, it doesn't provide guidance on how sensitive the performance is to these choices, which would be valuable for practitioners.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and comprehensive experimental validation. The convex formulation and convergence analysis are rigorous, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over strong baselines. The work represents a complete piece of research rather than a position paper, though the discussion of limitations could be strengthened.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear mathematical formulations. The algorithms are precisely described, and the experimental setup is well-explained. However, some parts (particularly the connection between the Hawkes process and the low-rank structure) could benefit from additional visualization to aid understanding. The writing is generally clear, but the paper would be more accessible with a more detailed explanation of the practical implications of the model's parameters.

## Originality Assessment

The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for recommendation is novel, and the proposed optimization algorithm represents a significant technical contribution. The paper clearly differentiates from related work (e.g., Kapoor et al.), particularly by enabling recommendations for new items. However, the paper could better establish how this differs from other recent work on temporal recommendation (e.g., tensor factorization approaches), which is a minor limitation.

## Significance Assessment

This work addresses a significant practical problem in recommendation systems with clear applications across multiple domains (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare). The method's ability to predict both item relevance and returning times simultaneously represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. The scalability to large datasets makes it particularly relevant for real-world applications. The optimization algorithm also has potential broader applications beyond recommendation systems.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The work presents a novel and well-justified approach to time-sensitive recommendation with strong theoretical foundations, comprehensive experiments, and significant practical implications. While there are some limitations in the comparison with recent work and the discussion of limitations, these do not undermine the paper's overall contribution.

## Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix models for recommendation
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with optimal convergence rate
- Strong empirical results on multiple datasets demonstrating superior performance
- Practical scalability to large real-world datasets
- Novel optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods

**Cons:**
- Limited comparison with the most recent tensor-based temporal recommendation methods
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate key contributions
- Limited discussion of failure cases and limitations of the approach
- Parameter sensitivity analysis could be more thorough

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing the critical yet underexplored problem of time-sensitive recommendations. The theoretical insights and practical implementation make it a strong candidate for NIPS.
