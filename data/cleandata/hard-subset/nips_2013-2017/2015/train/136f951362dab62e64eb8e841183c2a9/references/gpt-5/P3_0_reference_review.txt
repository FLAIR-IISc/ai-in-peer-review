Summary
The paper tackles two linked problems: time-sensitive item recommendation and prediction of user return times. It models each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), where Λ0 captures baseline preference and A scales self-excitation. To generalize to unobserved user–item pairs, the authors impose low-rank structure on Λ0 and A via nuclear norms, yielding a convex estimation problem. They introduce an alternative penalized formulation with auxiliary variables to decouple nonnegativity constraints from nuclear norms, and propose a hybrid optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient/Frank-Wolfe (for nuclear norms). They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate for the composite setting, recover O(1/t^2) when the nuclear norm is absent, and present experiments on large synthetic and three real datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II) showing improved ranking and return-time MAE versus Poisson, STiC (survival/HsMM), SVD, and tensor Poisson factorization.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on temporal point processes (Hawkes), prior low-rank matrix/tensor models for recommendation, and convex optimization with nuclear norms. It is related to multi-dimensional Hawkes learning with low-rank/sparse structure (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song AISTATS/ICML), but differs by focusing on independent per-pair self-excitation and leveraging low-rank sharing across user–item matrices rather than cross-item mutual excitation. Compared to survival analysis for return time (Kapoor et al.), the Hawkes formulation explicitly models history dependence and recurrent events. The optimization combines ideas from proximal methods and conditional gradient (e.g., Harchaoui et al., Lan), adapted to this specific composite structure with nonnegativity and nuclear norm penalties.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling: Hawkes processes capture both long-term baseline preferences and short-term self-excitation in a way directly aligned with return-time prediction and time-sensitive recommendation.
- Convex estimation with global optimum: with fixed parametric kernel, the negative log-likelihood is convex in Λ0 and A; adding nuclear norms preserves convexity.
- Algorithmic contribution: a practical hybrid proximal–Frank-Wolfe scheme that exploits simple projections for nonnegativity and rank-1 LMOs for nuclear norms; convergence analysis is provided and matches known rates in limiting cases.
- Scalability: convincing synthetic experiment (100M events) and qualitative scaling plot; the use of top singular vectors per iteration is computationally attractive for large matrices.
- Empirical results: improvements over static baselines and time-discretized tensor models; return-time evaluation leverages the survival function implied by Hawkes rather than ad hoc heuristics.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Modeling limitations: The process is purely self-exciting per user–item pair. It ignores cross-excitation and competition/substitution between items and within a user’s portfolio, which are often critical in recommendation (e.g., listening to artist A makes B more likely). Prior work on multivariate Hawkes with learned influence networks should be discussed as an alternative baseline and design choice.
- Stability/identifiability: Hawkes stationarity requires A(u,i) ∫ γ < 1 (for exponential kernel, A(u,i) σ < 1). The paper neither enforces nor analyzes this, risking explosive intensities. How often do estimated parameters violate stability? Is there implicit regularization preventing this?
- Alternative formulation equivalence: Theorem 1 asserts exact equivalence for ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice ρ is tuned; please report sensitivity and whether solutions meaningfully change with larger ρ.
- Optimization details: The convergence proof assumes a known Lipschitz constant L and sets ηk accordingly. In practice, how is L chosen (backtracking, heuristic)? What is the per-iteration cost (number of power iterations for top singular vectors, number of nonzero entries touched), and wall-clock times on real datasets?
- Return-time prediction: The expectation is approximated by sampling via Ogata thinning; sampling budget m and its effect on MAE and runtime are not reported. For exponential kernels, the survival integral is tractable enough for root-finding to compute quantiles deterministically; why not use such methods?
- Baselines and metrics: For recommendation, standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k) are preferred over average rank. Time-aware recommendation baselines like TimeSVD++, factorization machines with temporal features, or dynamic Poisson factorization are absent. For temporal point processes, comparisons to multivariate/low-rank Hawkes estimators (e.g., ADM4/NetRate variants) would strengthen claims.
- Generalization claims: The low-rank model enables completion for unobserved user–item pairs that appear in the training matrix. It does not handle true cold-start users/items without features; the paper mentions extensions, but no experiments with contextual features are provided.
- Data splitting and preprocessing: Details on the train/test split within sequences, kernel bandwidth selection, hyperparameter search spaces, and the number of iterations to convergence are limited. Reproducibility would benefit from fuller disclosure.
- Statistical significance: The qualitative statements about “best performance” should be backed with numerical results and confidence intervals or paired tests.

Questions for authors
- Do you enforce or monitor Hawkes stability (A(u,i) σ < 1)? What fraction of pairs violate it after training?
- How sensitive are results to kernel bandwidth σ, and did you try learning σ per item or per latent factor?
- What is the sampling budget m for return-time predictions, and how does MAE vary with m?
- Could you report Recall@k/NDCG@k and include TimeSVD++ as a baseline?
- How does the method perform if you allow mutual excitation at the latent-factor level (lower-dimensional Hawkes) rather than per user–item pair?

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well structured. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., consistent use of α vs A(u,i); define φ, ψ once with clear dimensions). Please add algorithmic details (step sizes, stopping criteria), and include wall-clock timings. The critique of survival analysis as limited to terminal events is too strong; recurrent event models (e.g., Andersen–Gill) exist and should be cited.

Significance and originality
The formulation is a compelling and timely bridge between point processes and low-rank recommendation, with a convex estimator and a practical large-scale solver. While related strands exist (low-rank Hawkes, temporal CF), the specific combination for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction, along with the hybrid optimization scheme and large-scale experiments, make this a potentially useful contribution.

Pros
- Principled time-continuous model aligned with tasks
- Convex, globally optimizable estimator with scalable algorithm and convergence guarantees
- Strong scalability and promising empirical results

Cons
- Limited modeling of cross-item dynamics and stability
- Missing strong temporal baselines and standard ranking metrics
- Some theoretical claims (penalty equivalence) are hard to operationalize; practical details sparse

Recommendation
Borderline accept. I lean positive due to the principled modeling and scalable convex approach, but the paper would benefit from stronger baselines, stability analysis, and more comprehensive empirical reporting.

Confidence
High on methodology and related work; medium on empirical assessment due to limited details provided.
