## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The authors tackle an important and increasingly relevant problem – moving beyond static preference prediction to capture the dynamic nature of user engagement. The technical contribution, particularly the convex formulation and efficient optimization algorithm, is significant. While the writing could be more concise, the paper is generally well-structured and the experimental results are convincing. I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of predicting both *when* a user will interact with an item again (returning time) and *which* item to recommend at a given moment. It argues that traditional recommendation systems, focused on static preferences, are insufficient for capturing the temporal dynamics of user behavior. The authors propose a Hawkes process model, leveraging low-rank approximations to scale to large datasets.  The connection to point processes is clearly explained, building on established theory. The related work section appropriately highlights Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015) as the most relevant existing work, and clearly differentiates their approach by emphasizing the ability to recommend *new* items, a key advantage of the proposed method.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for time-sensitive recommendation is a novel and compelling approach.  The theoretical justification for this connection is well-developed.
* **Technical Contribution:** The formulation of a convex optimization problem and the development of an efficient algorithm (combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods) is a significant technical achievement. Achieving an O(1/t) convergence rate is commendable.
* **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate that their algorithm scales to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events), which is critical for real-world applications.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed method compared to several baselines, including Poisson processes, STiC, and tensor factorization methods. The analysis of the convergence rate and the effect of data scale in the synthetic experiments is particularly insightful.
* **Generality:** The model's ability to incorporate contextual information (spatial, textual, categorical, user profile) is a significant strength, highlighting its adaptability to different application domains.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Writing & Conciseness:** While generally clear, the paper could benefit from more concise writing.  Some sections, particularly the introduction, contain repetitive phrasing.  The explanations of some algorithms could be streamlined.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth, latent rank) but lacks detail on the tuning process itself. More specifics on how these parameters were chosen would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.
* **Theoretical Justification Depth:** While the theoretical foundation is sound, a more in-depth discussion of the assumptions underlying the low-rank approximation and its implications for the model’s performance would be beneficial.



**4. Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis (convex formulation, convergence rate) and experimental results strongly support the claims. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for improvement.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is mostly clear, but could benefit from streamlining and more concise phrasing. The organization is logical, but the introduction feels somewhat lengthy.
* **Originality:** The approach is highly original. Combining Hawkes processes with low-rank models in this context is a novel contribution. The authors adequately cite related work.
* **Significance:** The results are important and address a challenging problem in recommendation systems. The potential impact of this work is significant, as it offers a more realistic and effective approach to personalized recommendations, with applications in various domains (e-commerce, healthcare, mobile platforms).

**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* Scalable algorithm with favorable convergence properties.
* Potential for significant impact in various application domains.

**Con:**

* Writing could be more concise.
* Limited details on hyperparameter tuning.
* More in-depth theoretical discussion of low-rank assumptions would be beneficial.

**6. Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The technical contribution is substantial, the experimental validation is convincing, and the potential impact is significant. Addressing the minor suggestions regarding writing and hyperparameter details could further enhance the paper’s quality.
