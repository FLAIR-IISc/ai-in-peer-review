This paper proposes a low-rank Hawkes process model for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The core idea is to leverage the self-exciting nature of user interactions to model temporal dynamics, combined with low-rank factorization to generalize to unseen user-item pairs. While the problem setting is relevant and the motivation for considering temporal dynamics is sound, the paper suffers from a lack of convincing novelty and insufficient validation against strong, established baselines.

The authors position their work as addressing limitations in traditional recommendation systems and highlighting the importance of temporal patterns. However, the core idea of applying Hawkes processes to recommendation is not entirely new; Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015) already explored temporal point processes for this purpose. The key contribution, the low-rank extension, is presented as a means to generalize to unseen items, which is a standard technique in recommendation.  The connection to low-rank models feels relatively straightforward and doesn’t represent a breakthrough conceptual leap. The convex formulation and optimization algorithm, while described in detail, don’t appear to overcome fundamental challenges in a significantly new way.  The claimed O(1/t) convergence rate is positive, but the algorithm's complexity, relying on proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, is not demonstrably superior to existing approaches for low-rank matrix factorization.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While the authors test on both synthetic and real datasets, the comparisons are not robust enough to justify strong claims of superiority.  The baselines chosen – Poisson process, STiC, and several tensor factorization methods – are not consistently state-of-the-art.  For instance, more recent and sophisticated collaborative filtering techniques (e.g., Neural Collaborative Filtering, LightGCN) are absent. The performance gains over these simpler baselines, while observed, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance, particularly given the added complexity of the proposed model. The justification for choosing specific parameter settings and the details of hyperparameter tuning are also somewhat lacking. The observation that the Hawkes model aligns better with observed data via time-change theorem is interesting, but it doesn't automatically translate into superior predictive performance. Further, the experimental setup’s separation of “Heldout” vs. “Total” MAE feels contrived and doesn't provide a clear understanding of real-world performance.

The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical formulation is reasonably clear. However, the extensive background material on temporal point processes, while thorough, feels somewhat disconnected from the core contribution. The writing could be more concise by focusing more sharply on the novelty of the low-rank extension and its practical implications.

**Strengths:**

*   Relevant problem setting: Time-sensitive recommendation is an important and increasingly relevant area.
*   Well-defined mathematical formulation: The model is clearly articulated.
*   Detailed algorithm description: The optimization procedure is presented with sufficient detail.
*   Convergence analysis: Theoretical guarantees regarding the algorithm's convergence are provided.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty: The core ideas build upon existing work with incremental improvements.
*   Insufficient baseline comparison: The selected baselines are not sufficiently competitive.
*   Modest performance gains: The observed performance improvements are not compelling.
*   Lack of practical impact demonstration: It’s unclear how this model would perform in a real-world, large-scale recommender system.
*   Extensive background material: The detailed explanation of temporal point processes adds length without significantly enhancing the core contribution.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a mathematically sound formulation of a relevant problem.
*   The algorithm is theoretically well-behaved, with a proven convergence rate.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is limited.
*   The experimental evaluation is not convincing.
*   The performance gains over existing methods are not substantial enough.
*   The paper doesn’t sufficiently demonstrate the practical value of the proposed model.

**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. While technically sound and well-written, this paper lacks the novelty and compelling experimental validation required for acceptance at NIPS. The proposed approach feels like a relatively incremental extension of existing work, and the performance gains over simpler baselines are not substantial enough to justify its added complexity.  I would encourage the authors to focus on more rigorous comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and to demonstrate a more significant impact on real-world recommendation tasks.
