

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by modeling recurrent user activities using low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, develop a new optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate superior performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

## Strengths

The paper addresses an important problem in recommendation systems - time-sensitive recommendations that account for users' evolving preferences. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm is rigorous, and the paper demonstrates good empirical performance on both synthetic and real datasets. The problem statement is well-motivated with relevant examples from different domains (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare).

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper contains numerous minor but persistent technical and formatting issues that undermine its credibility and clarity. These issues collectively constitute a substantial number of minor flaws that would prevent me from recommending acceptance:

1. **Consistent notation errors**: The paper uses inconsistent mathematical notation throughout. For example:
   - "w>u,iφ u,i j" should be "w_{u,i}^T φ_{u,ij}"
   - "‖Λ0‖∗ 6 λ′" should be "‖Λ0‖∗ ≤ λ'"
   - "ρ‖Λ0 −Z1‖2F" should be "ρ‖Λ0 - Z1‖_F^2"
   - "t i n+1" should be "t_{n+1}^i"

2. **Formatting inconsistencies**: Subscript formatting is inconsistent (e.g., "tu,ij" vs "t_{u,ij}"), and the paper uses "6" instead of "≤" for inequality. The reference formatting in the bibliography is inconsistent, with some entries including "pages" before page numbers and others not.

3. **Theoretical presentation issues**: Theorem 1 contains a critical formatting error in the expression for ρ*, which should be a fraction with the numerator and denominator properly formatted. The paper claims "the proposed algorithm, up to our knowledge, is first of its kind" without sufficient justification or comparison to related work that might have achieved similar results.

4. **Experimental details**: The paper states "the bandwidth for the triggering kernel is fixed to one" without clarifying what "bandwidth" refers to in the context of an exponential kernel (which has a scale parameter σ). The scalability claims ("scales up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events") lack concrete runtime numbers or detailed scalability analysis.

5. **Minor but persistent typos**: "nosiness" should be "noisiness," and there are multiple instances of missing spaces in mathematical expressions.

6. **Citation issues**: The paper cites "Ogata's thinning algorithm [19]" but doesn't provide the specific citation details for the algorithm in the reference section, which is a critical omission for reproducibility.

7. **Inconsistent presentation of the threshold ρ**: The paper states "it is inefficient to directly estimate the exact value of the threshold value for ρ" but provides no evidence or explanation for why this is the case.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The technical content is sound, but the numerous notation errors and formatting inconsistencies significantly reduce the quality of the presentation. The theoretical results are presented with minor but critical errors that would require correction before publication.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized, but the inconsistent notation and formatting issues make it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations. A reader would need to constantly decipher the intended meaning of expressions.

**Originality**: The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models appears novel, but the paper could better distinguish itself from related work (e.g., [11] which also addresses user return time prediction).

**Significance**: The problem addressed is important, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach is significantly better than existing methods for real-world applications. The scalability claims lack supporting evidence.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation with sound theoretical underpinnings, the numerous minor technical and formatting errors throughout the paper are too pervasive to ignore. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues indicate a lack of attention to detail that would undermine the paper's credibility. The errors are not just superficial - they include mathematical notation errors that could confuse readers and potentially lead to misinterpretation of the results.

The paper makes an interesting contribution to the field, but the numerous minor flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly proofread the paper, fix all notation inconsistencies, provide complete citation details for Ogata's algorithm, and include concrete scalability metrics before resubmitting.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but persistent technical and formatting errors that collectively undermine the paper's quality and credibility.
