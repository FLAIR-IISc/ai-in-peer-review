Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: (1) recommending relevant items at the right moment, and (2) predicting users’ next return time to an item/service. It models each user–item interaction as a univariate self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), where Λ0 captures long-term preference and A scales self-excitation from past events via a triggering kernel (exponential in experiments). To generalize to unobserved user–item pairs and combat sparsity, the base intensity matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A are assumed low-rank and estimated under nuclear-norm regularization with nonnegativity constraints. The authors propose a convex formulation, then introduce an equivalent penalized formulation with auxiliary variables to decouple nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity. They develop an optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient (handling nonnegativity) and conditional gradient (handling nuclear norms), and prove a convergence rate bound with an O(1/t) term. The method is used for time-sensitive ranking (by current intensities) and return-time prediction (via sampling from the conditional density using Ogata’s thinning). Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) compare against Poisson, survival/HMM (STiC), SVD, and Poisson-regression tensor factorization. The paper also uses the time-change theorem to show Hawkes fits real sequences better than survival baselines.

The work is related to: (i) survival analysis and HMM-based return-time prediction [11,12], which do not generalize to new items; (ii) temporal/tensor factorization for implicit feedback [2,3,13,22,25], which discretize time and do not model continuous-time dependency on full histories; and (iii) Hawkes process modeling and learning [4–8,10,14,30,31], where this paper’s novelty is coupling univariate Hawkes models across a user–item matrix via low-rank constraints to enable generalization, with a new optimization algorithm to handle nonnegativity plus nuclear norms at scale.

Quality
- Formulation: The negative log-likelihood in Eq. (4) is convex in Λ0 and A (sum over terms −log(w^Tφ) + w^Tψ), and the nuclear norm regularizers preserve convexity. The alternative penalized formulation (5) is standard; Theorem 1 gives conditions (ρ > ρ*) under which it is equivalent to (4). While ρ* is problem-dependent and unknown, tuning ρ is acceptable in practice; the theoretical statement is reasonable.
- Algorithm: The proximal step for nonnegativity and the LMO for nuclear norms (rank-1 updates via top singular vectors) are standard components; their coupling via an accelerated scheme is plausible. The bound in Theorem 2 matches an O(1/k) rate dominated by the conditional gradient block, with an O(1/k^2) term from proximal updates.
- Technical caveats:
  - Domain/positivity: The likelihood requires w^Tφ > 0. The algorithm projects to nonnegative values via (·)+. If entries are set to zero, log(w^Tφ) is undefined. The paper states Λ0,A > 0 in (4) but uses nonnegativity projection; ensuring strict positivity (e.g., with an ε-floor) should be clarified.
  - Stability: For Hawkes with exponential kernel, preventing explosion typically requires the branching ratio (A(u,i)∫γ) < 1 per process. No stability constraint or regularizer to enforce this is discussed. This affects both well-posedness and interpretability.
  - Lipschitzness: The convergence analysis assumes a Lipschitz gradient for f. This can fail near the boundary where w^Tφ is small. Clarifying the conditions (e.g., lower bounds) would strengthen the result.
- Experiments: The synthetic study is large-scale and reports convergence, sample complexity trends, and runtime scaling. Real-data experiments compare against appropriate baselines; the time-change theorem diagnostic is appropriate and supports the model choice. However, full numerical results are not detailed in the text (referenced figures only), and some baselines (e.g., mutually-exciting models or nonparametric Hawkes) are absent.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The modeling intuition (baseline vs. self-excitation) and how low rank enables generalization are explained. Algorithm pseudocode is provided. There are minor notational typos (e.g., references to “feature ψu,i in 3”) and occasional grammar issues, but not obstructive. Important implementation details (initialization, handling strict positivity, computing L or step sizes, number of SVD iterations, sampling budget for return time) should be clarified for reproducibility.

Originality
- Conceptual: Linking per-pair Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix structure to generalize to unobserved user–item pairs is a novel and useful combination relative to survival/HMM or tensor factorization approaches. Prior Hawkes work has studied network inference and kernel learning; this paper frames the recommender setting with low-rank parameter sharing differently.
- Algorithmic: The blended proximal/conditional gradient method for convex problems with simultaneous nonnegativity and nuclear-norm terms is a nontrivial contribution with a convergence analysis. The overall rate is standard (O(1/k)), but the separation of constraints/regularizers is neat.

Significance
The approach addresses two practically relevant tasks and demonstrates improvements on several datasets. The formulation is scalable and convex, which is appealing for deployment. The ability to handle cold-start user–item pairs via low rank is significant. The optimization technique may be of broader interest for other convex problems with similar structure. Limitations (univariate self-excitation only, stability/positivity constraints, reliance on a shared exponential kernel) reduce immediate generality; extensions to mutual excitation and richer kernels would increase impact.

Pros
- Clear formulation of time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction in a unified continuous-time framework.
- Convex low-rank Hawkes model enabling generalization to unobserved pairs.
- Efficient algorithm combining proximal and conditional gradient with convergence guarantee.
- Large-scale synthetic and real-data experiments; diagnostic using time-change theorem is appropriate.
- Practical procedures for ranking and return-time prediction.

Cons
- No explicit stability constraint for Hawkes parameters; potential for explosive dynamics not addressed.
- Positivity vs. nonnegativity mismatch may cause undefined log terms; details on maintaining strict positivity are missing.
- Lack of mutually-exciting interactions across items/users limits expressiveness for many recommender settings.
- Limited discussion of hyperparameter selection, step sizes, and computational cost of sampling for return-time prediction at scale.
- Baselines do not include more modern point process models (e.g., mutually-exciting or nonparametric Hawkes) and time-aware recommenders beyond Poisson/tensor.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce stability (e.g., constraints or penalties ensuring A(u,i)∫γ < 1) and strict positivity (e.g., projecting to [ε, ∞)) and reflect this in the analysis.
- Report ablations: low rank on Λ0 only vs. A only; effect of rank/regularization strengths; different kernel bandwidths, and possibly per (u,i) bandwidths.
- Include mutually-exciting extensions (cross-item or cross-user) or discuss complexity and performance trade-offs.
- Provide full quantitative results for all datasets (means, variances), computational budgets (iterations, SVD computations), and details for return-time sampling (number of samples, runtime).
- Add ranking metrics (e.g., Recall@k/HR@k/NDCG) alongside ground-truth rank and report cold-start performance explicitly.

Overall
A technically sound and largely well-executed paper with a novel low-rank Hawkes recommender formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm, showing promising empirical results. Addressing stability/positivity and expanding comparisons would strengthen the contribution.
