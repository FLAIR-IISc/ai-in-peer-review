

# Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors connect self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models to capture temporal dynamics in user activities. They develop a convex optimization formulation with nuclear norm regularization and propose an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The approach aims to solve two related problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and predicting the next returning time of users. The paper demonstrates scalability to large datasets and shows improvements over baselines on both synthetic and real-world data.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a valuable connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix models for recommendation systems. The convex formulation with nuclear norm regularization is well-motivated and provides a principled approach to learning low-rank intensity matrices. The optimization algorithm is novel, combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and the convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is rigorous, showing optimal O(1/t) convergence rate.

2. **Technical Rigor**: The paper provides clear mathematical formulations, detailed proofs, and comprehensive experimental evaluation. The synthetic experiments demonstrate the algorithm's convergence properties and scalability, while the real-world experiments on last.fm, tmall.com, and MIMIC II datasets provide strong evidence of practical utility.

3. **Scalability**: The authors demonstrate that their approach scales to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events, which is a significant practical contribution for real-world recommendation systems.

4. **Clear Structure**: The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical development, algorithm design, and experimental validation. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts and results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misalignment between "Time-Sensitive" Claim and Methodology**: The paper's core claim is about "time-sensitive" recommendation, but the recommendation approach is fundamentally static at time t (it computes λu,i(t) at a given time t and recommends items based on this instantaneous value). The paper does not adequately demonstrate how the model captures true temporal dynamics beyond the current moment. The recommendation task is evaluated using standard ranking metrics (e.g., item rank), which do not inherently capture the time-sensitive aspect of the problem.

2. **Inadequate Baselines**: The baselines for the recommendation task are weak. For example, using a Poisson process (which assumes constant intensity) as a baseline for a time-sensitive recommendation task is problematic since the Poisson process cannot model temporal dynamics. The comparison to SVD and tensor factorization also misses more relevant baselines like state-of-the-art time-aware recommendation methods.

3. **Lack of Practical Time-Sensitive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates returning-time prediction, but for the recommendation task, it doesn't assess how well the model recommends items *at the right time*—a key aspect of time-sensitive recommendation. The evaluation metrics don't directly measure whether the recommended items are appropriate for the current temporal context.

4. **Limited Discussion of Implementation Challenges**: While the paper demonstrates scalability, it doesn't address practical challenges of implementing this approach in real-world systems, such as handling missing data, cold-start problems, or real-time performance constraints.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to modeling user temporal dynamics using Hawkes processes with low-rank structure. The theoretical contributions are strong, and the empirical results demonstrate good scalability and performance. However, the paper's core claim of "time-sensitive recommendation" is not adequately supported by the methodology or evaluation. The recommendation approach is fundamentally static rather than truly time-sensitive, and the evaluation does not demonstrate how the model captures the temporal aspect beyond computing an instantaneous intensity function.

This is a well-executed technical contribution that would be valuable for the point process and recommendation systems communities. However, for NIPS, the paper would benefit from significantly strengthening the connection between the proposed method and the time-sensitive recommendation problem, particularly by modifying the evaluation to directly measure time-sensitive performance.

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. **Refine the problem formulation**: Clearly define what "time-sensitive recommendation" means in this context and ensure the methodology and evaluation directly address this problem.

2. **Strengthen the evaluation**: For the recommendation task, introduce metrics that measure whether items are recommended at appropriate times (e.g., by comparing recommended items to those actually consumed at specific time intervals). Consider adding a case study demonstrating how the model would perform in a real-world time-sensitive recommendation scenario.

3. **Improve baselines**: Compare against more relevant baselines for time-sensitive recommendation, such as recent temporal recommendation methods that explicitly model time dynamics.

4. **Clarify the contribution**: The paper's title and introduction emphasize "time-sensitive" aspects, but the methodology doesn't fully realize this. The authors should either refine the problem statement to better match the method or develop a more time-sensitive recommendation approach.

With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field and better align with the time-sensitive recommendation problem it aims to solve.
