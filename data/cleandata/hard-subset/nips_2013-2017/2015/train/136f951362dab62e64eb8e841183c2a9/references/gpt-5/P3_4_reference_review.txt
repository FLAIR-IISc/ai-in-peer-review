Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models each user–item pair as a univariate self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), with γ being an exponential kernel. To enable generalization over sparse user–item observations, Λ0 and A are assumed low-rank and are estimated via nuclear-norm regularization. The learning problem is posed as a convex program: minimizing the negative Hawkes log-likelihood plus nuclear norms under nonnegativity constraints. To scale optimization, the authors introduce an auxiliary-variable penalized formulation and propose a blended algorithm that applies proximal gradient updates on the nonnegative variables and conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) updates on the nuclear-norm variables. They claim an O(1/t) convergence rate for the penalized problem and, via an “exact penalty” theorem, equivalence to the original formulation for sufficiently large penalty. The method is evaluated on synthetic data (100M events) and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II), showing improved time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction versus Poisson, STiC (semi-HMM survival), SVD, and Poisson tensor factorization baselines.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects temporal point processes (Hawkes) to low-rank recommendation, with nuclear norms used to transfer knowledge across user–item pairs. It is related to: (i) multivariate and low-rank Hawkes estimation (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song, AISTATS/ICML), which typically model cross-excitation networks; (ii) time-aware collaborative filtering (e.g., TimeSVD++, time-varying Poisson factorization, tensor factorization); and (iii) return-time prediction via survival analysis and semi-Markov models (Kapoor et al.). The key novelty claimed is a convex low-rank Hawkes formulation for per-pair self-excitation and a scalable optimization scheme that blends proximal and conditional gradient updates under nonnegativity.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation for both time-sensitive recommendation (ranking at event time) and return-time prediction.
- Elegant convex formulation of Hawkes parameter estimation with nuclear norms and nonnegativity; the per-pair linear parametrization makes the log-likelihood concave in the parameters.
- Practical algorithmic design: splitting variables to separate nonnegativity and nuclear norm, using cheap projections and top singular vectors per iteration; the theoretical O(1/t) rate is plausible for the penalized objective.
- Strong scale claims: synthetic experiments with 100M events and linear scaling plots; real-data results across diverse domains demonstrate utility.
- The use of time-rescaling plots to compare model fit versus survival baselines is a nice diagnostic.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness
- Exact-penalty claim (Theorem 1): Using a quadratic (squared Frobenius) penalty to tie Z to Λ0 (and A) yields a Moreau envelope of the nuclear norm; in general, this is not an exact penalty for any finite ρ. The standard exact penalty theory applies to ℓ1-type penalties on equality constraints, not squared penalties. As written, the theorem asserting existence of a finite threshold ρ* so that the penalized and original problems have identical optima is not generally true. Since later convergence claims for the original problem depend on this equivalence, this is a critical issue that must be clarified or corrected (e.g., by analyzing convergence to the penalized objective and providing bounds on the approximation gap as a function of ρ).
- Stability of Hawkes processes: For exponential kernels, stationarity requires α ∫ γ < 1 (per pair). With pair-specific A(u,i) and fixed σ, this constraint is not enforced. Without stability constraints, intensities can blow up, affecting both likelihood and return-time sampling. Please clarify whether constraints like A ⊙ σ < 1 were imposed, or justify why instability is not an issue (e.g., finite horizons).
- Return-time prediction via Monte Carlo: The paper suggests Ogata thinning to estimate the expectation but omits the number of samples and variance/efficiency analysis. In high-throughput settings, MC sampling at test time may be expensive; alternatives (median, mode, numerical quadrature) could be considered.

Modeling choices
- No cross-excitation: The introduction motivating inter-disease triggering (MIMIC) implies mutually exciting structure across items/diseases. The proposed model only allows self-excitation within a user–item pair. This mismatch should be acknowledged; modeling cross-item excitation (even via a low-rank kernel basis) would be more faithful and has prior art.
- Kernel parameterization: It appears a single bandwidth σ is used/tuned globally. Heterogeneous or learned kernels (basis expansions, nonparametric) are mentioned but not instantiated; this limits expressivity.
- Identifiability: With both Λ0 and A nonnegative and low-rank, there may be trade-offs between baseline and excitation in finite windows. Some discussion of identifiability (or regularization to disentangle baseline vs. excitation) would be helpful.

Evaluation
- Baselines: For recommendation, comparisons omit strong time-aware CF models (TimeSVD++, factorization with time features, Poisson factorization with temporal dynamics) and modern sequence models (RNN-based). For Hawkes-related baselines, low-rank/multivariate Hawkes (e.g., Zhou et al.) are not compared, even though they are close in spirit. This weakens the empirical case.
- Metric design: For ranking, the evaluation is the rank of the ground-truth item at the event time across all items. This can favor popularity-heavy baselines (as observed) and does not assess diversity or calibration. Consider reporting top-k precision/recall/HR@k and time-dependent AUC.
- Reproducibility: Important details are missing: step-size selection (L), number of iterations, stopping criteria, SVD computation details (truncated? power iterations?), wall-clock times and memory, number of MC samples for return-time, and hyperparameter search ranges. Code availability is not mentioned.

Clarity and presentation
- Generally clear, but notation is occasionally inconsistent (e.g., switching between bold and scalar notation, reuse of A). Several typos and minor grammatical issues.
- The “LMOψ” terminology is nonstandard; what is actually solved is a Frank–Wolfe step on the smooth part plus a line search that incorporates the nuclear-norm penalty; this could be clarified.

Questions for the authors
1) Please rigorously justify Theorem 1, or revise the approach (e.g., use an exact penalty or ADMM with inexact but efficient proximal operators) and state guarantees for the penalized problem. If only approximate equivalence holds, quantify the approximation error as a function of ρ.
2) Were stability constraints (A(u,i)σ < 1) enforced? If not, what fraction of pairs were supercritical? Any mitigations?
3) How many MC samples were used for return-time prediction, and what is the computation time per prediction?
4) Can you report wall-clock time and memory for the 100M-event synthetic and largest real datasets, including SVD compute costs per iteration?
5) How sensitive are results to σ and to λ, β, ρ? Provide ablations.
6) Why not include multivariate/low-rank Hawkes or TimeSVD++ as baselines?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, clearly motivated problem (timing-aware recommendations and return-time prediction).
- Conceptually appealing combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank transfer across user–item pairs.
- Scalable algorithmic design with convergence rate analysis for the penalized objective.
- Promising results on large-scale synthetic and multiple real datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- The exact-penalty theorem appears incorrect as stated; theoretical guarantees for the original problem hinge on this.
- Missing strong baselines and limited analysis of modeling assumptions (no cross-excitation; stability).
- Some reproducibility gaps and lack of detailed computational reporting.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject (assuming Theorem 1 cannot be fixed easily). If the penalty-equivalence issue is resolved (or the theory reframed appropriately) and stronger baselines are added, this would be a solid contribution.

Confidence: High (familiar with temporal point processes, Hawkes estimation, and low-rank optimization).
