

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors introduce a convex formulation that connects self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models, develop an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate its effectiveness on synthetic and real-world datasets for both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in recommendation systems by shifting focus from static preferences to time-sensitive user behavior. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is theoretically sound, and the optimization algorithm achieves a theoretically optimal convergence rate. The empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the model's scalability and predictive performance.

## Weaknesses
Despite the paper's promising contributions, numerous minor but significant technical and presentation flaws undermine its quality as a NIPS submission:

1. **Notational Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous inconsistent notations that make the mathematical presentation difficult to follow. For example:
   - Equation (3) uses "w>u,i" instead of standard transpose notation "w_{u,i}^T" or "w_{u,i}^\top"
   - The paper writes "φ u,i j" instead of "φ_{u,i,j}" and "ψ u,i" instead of "ψ_{u,i}"
   - The inequality "‖Λ0‖∗ 6 λ′" should use "≤" instead of "6"

2. **Formatting Errors in Theoretical Results**:
   - Theorem 1 contains a critical formatting error: "ρ∗ = max { λ (‖Λ∗0‖∗ − ‖Z∗1‖∗) + β (‖A∗‖∗ − ‖Z∗2‖∗) ‖Λ∗0 −Z∗1‖2F + ‖A∗ −Z∗2‖2F }" is missing a closing brace for the max expression
   - In Theorem 2, "ηk = (δk)−1/L" should be "ηk = 1/(Lδk)" or "ηk = L^{-1}δ_k^{-1}"

3. **Algorithm Presentation Issues**:
   - Algorithm 1 states "Choose to initialize X01 and X02 = X01" which is confusing and non-standard
   - The pseudocode uses inconsistent notation like "Y k" instead of "Y^k" for superscripts
   - The initialization step "X02 = X01" lacks justification and appears to be a typo

4. **Citation Inconsistencies**:
   - The paper references "Kapoor et al. [12, 11]" but then cites "Kapoor et al. [11]" for STiC, while [11] refers to a different paper than [12] (see references)
   - The related work section misattributes the STiC model to [11] when it's actually referenced in [12]

5. **Missing Figure References**:
   - The paper repeatedly references "Figure 1(a)" and "Figure 2" but no actual figures are included in the submission
   - The text states "see for example Figure 1(a)" but the figure is not present

6. **Minor Theoretical Omissions**:
   - The paper claims to achieve "the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate" but Theorem 2 states a rate of "4LD1/(k(k+1)) + 2LD2/(k+1)" which is actually O(1/k^2), not O(1/k)
   - The paper states "the algorithm scales up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events" but provides no specific scalability metrics or runtime analysis

7. **Reference Formatting Issues**:
   - Multiple references have inconsistent punctuation and spacing (e.g., "Springer,, ,")
   - The reference list contains some entries with incomplete information

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The problem addressed (time-sensitive recommendation) is relevant and timely
- The theoretical connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is novel
- The empirical results demonstrate good performance on multiple datasets
- The optimization algorithm achieves a theoretically optimal convergence rate

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The paper contains numerous technical and notational inconsistencies that undermine its credibility
- The presentation is sloppy, with inconsistent notation, missing figure references, and formatting errors
- The theoretical claims are misrepresented (e.g., convergence rate)
- The paper fails to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem with potentially valuable contributions, the numerous technical and presentation flaws significantly diminish its quality as a scientific contribution. The notational inconsistencies, formatting errors in theorems, and missing figure references are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, which should exemplify high standards of technical writing and presentation. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues before being considered for publication.

The paper's core ideas show promise, but the current version fails to meet the rigorous standards expected at NIPS due to these preventable errors. A revised version addressing all these technical and presentation issues would be worth reconsidering.
