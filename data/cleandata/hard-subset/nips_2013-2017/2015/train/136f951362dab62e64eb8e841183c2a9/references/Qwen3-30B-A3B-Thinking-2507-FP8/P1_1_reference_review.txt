

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction by modeling user activities as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors address two key problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) accurately predicting the next returning time of users. The core innovation lies in establishing a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, enabling efficient transfer learning across user-item pairs. The authors develop a new optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving optimal convergence rates. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II medical dataset), showing superior performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines.

The work relates to recent NIPS contributions on point processes for recommendation [11, 12, 19], but extends them by introducing the low-rank structure for scalability and generalization to unseen items. While Kapoor et al. [11, 12] focus on predicting returning time for observed items using survival analysis, this paper addresses the more challenging problem of recommending *new* items at the right time, which is crucial for practical applications.

## Strengths

### Quality
1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous convex formulation of the problem and establishes convergence guarantees for the proposed optimization algorithm (Theorem 2). The convergence rate analysis is thorough and well-motivated.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct extensive experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, comparing with multiple relevant baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, Tensor factorization). The evaluation covers both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks, which are complementary.

3. **Scalability Demonstration**: The paper convincingly demonstrates scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events, addressing a critical concern for real-world recommendation systems.

4. **Adequate Baseline Comparison**: The authors properly compare with relevant baselines, including the recent work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12], and explain why their approach is better suited for the recommendation task.

### Originality
1. **Novel Connection**: The paper makes a novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for recommendation systems, which has not been explored in prior work.

2. **Optimization Algorithm**: The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods for nonnegative low-rank matrix estimation appears to be novel and addresses a specific challenge (non-negativity constraints) that has been overlooked in previous work.

3. **Problem Formulation**: The paper correctly identifies the gap in existing recommendation systems that focus on one-time preferences rather than time-sensitive recommendations, and formulates a meaningful problem that addresses this gap.

### Significance
1. **Practical Impact**: The proposed framework has clear applications in personalized services (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare), where delivering recommendations at the right moment can significantly improve user engagement.

2. **Broader Applicability**: The optimization algorithm can be applied to other nonnegative matrix rank minimization problems with convex losses, extending the paper's impact beyond recommendation systems.

3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The model achieves superior performance on both time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks across multiple datasets, demonstrating a meaningful advancement over existing methods.

## Weaknesses

### Quality
1. **Limited Discussion of Model Limitations**: The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the exponential triggering kernel assumption. While the authors mention using more flexible parametric families (Weibull, Log-logistic), they don't explore how these alternatives might impact performance or computational complexity.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the model is to hyperparameter choices (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth), which could affect reproducibility and practical adoption.

3. **Theoretical Gap in Model Assumptions**: The paper assumes that user behaviors and item attributes can be categorized into a limited number of prototypical types (justifying the low-rank assumption), but doesn't provide empirical evidence supporting this assumption for the real datasets used.

### Clarity
1. **Optimization Algorithm Details**: The connection between the original problem (4) and the alternative formulation (5) could be clearer. The paper states "with the condition ρ > ρ∗" without explaining how to determine ρ* in practice, which is important for implementation.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions using Ogata's thinning algorithm for returning-time prediction, but doesn't provide details on the number of samples (m) used, which could affect the accuracy of the prediction.

3. **Comparison with Related Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it could better clarify how their approach differs from recent NIPS papers on temporal recommendation [e.g., 11, 12, 19].

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Clarify the Relationship Between Formulations**: Provide a more detailed explanation of how ρ* can be estimated in practice, or at least discuss the implications of choosing ρ too close to ρ*.

2. **Expand the Discussion of Model Limitations**: Add a subsection discussing limitations of the exponential triggering kernel and potential alternatives, including a brief analysis of how these alternatives might affect performance.

3. **Include Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis**: Add a section or figure showing how the model performance varies with different values of key hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β).

4. **Strengthen the Connection to Real-World Applications**: Elaborate more on how the time-sensitive recommendations could be integrated into specific service contexts (e.g., how a music streaming service might use the model to time recommendations for different user segments).

5. **Improve the Comparison with Related Work**: Create a table that explicitly compares the key features (e.g., scalability, ability to recommend new items, handling of temporal dynamics) of the proposed method with those of the main baselines.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The technical quality is high, with a novel theoretical connection between point processes and low-rank models, rigorous optimization algorithm with convergence guarantees, and compelling experimental results. The problem addressed is important for real-world applications, and the solution advances the state of the art in time-sensitive recommendation.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, though there is room for improvement in addressing the limitations of the model and providing more details about implementation choices. The theoretical and empirical contributions are substantial and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments For Acceptance**:
- Novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees
- Demonstrated scalability to large datasets
- Superior empirical performance on both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks
- Practical applications in multiple domains (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare)
- New optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods

**Arguments Against Acceptance**:
- Limited discussion of model limitations (exponential kernel assumption)
- Lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis
- Some technical details in the optimization section could be clearer
- The low-rank assumption could be better justified for the real-world datasets used

## Final Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing an important but overlooked aspect of time-sensitive recommendations. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and would be of interest to the NIPS community. While there are some minor weaknesses in the presentation of limitations and implementation details, these are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's substantial contributions.
