

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors address the limitation of existing recommendation systems that focus on static user preferences rather than dynamic temporal patterns. They establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank matrix models, developing a convex optimization formulation that enables efficient learning of temporal dynamics. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) a unified framework for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, and (2) a novel optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods with theoretical convergence guarantees.

The work builds on the recent application of point processes to recommendation (Du et al., KDD'15; Zhou et al., AISTATS'15), but distinguishes itself from Kapoor et al. (WSDM'14, KDD'14) who used survival analysis and semi-hidden Markov models. Unlike their approach, which cannot generalize to new items, the authors' framework enables recommendations for unobserved user-item pairs through the low-rank structure. The paper also relates to tensor-based approaches (Karatzoglou et al., RecSys'10; Xiong et al., SDM'12) but addresses their limitations in capturing temporal dynamics.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical foundations, including a convex formulation (Equation 4), convergence analysis (Theorem 2), and a proof of equivalence between the original and alternative formulations (Theorem 1). The convergence rate of O(1/t) is particularly valuable for large-scale problems.

2. **Novel Optimization Approach**: The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a significant contribution, effectively blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to handle both non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization. This "best of both worlds" approach appears to be novel in the literature.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) demonstrate strong empirical performance across both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks. The ablation studies (Figure 2) effectively show the algorithm's convergence and scalability.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper clearly articulates the importance of time-sensitive recommendation for diverse applications (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare), making the work highly relevant to both industry and academia.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Justification for Kernel Choice**: The authors use an exponential triggering kernel throughout, but provide insufficient justification for this choice compared to other flexible parametric families (Weibull, Log-logistic) mentioned in Section 3.3. This is particularly problematic since the kernel shape significantly impacts the model's ability to capture different temporal dynamics.

2. **Overstated Scalability Claims**: While the paper claims "scales up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events," the real-world datasets are relatively modest (tmall.com: 26,376 users, 2,563 stores; MIMIC II: 650 patients, 204 diseases). The synthetic data generation (1,024×1,204 matrices with 1,000 events each) doesn't fully demonstrate the claimed scalability.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites Du et al. (KDD'15) but doesn't sufficiently contrast with more recent Hawkes-based recommendation approaches like Zhou et al. (ICML'16) or Du et al. (AISTATS'17), which also use point processes for recommendation. The distinction from Kapoor et al. (WSDM'14) is clear, but the paper doesn't fully establish how it advances beyond other point process approaches.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the low-rank assumption, particularly how it might fail when user-item interactions exhibit complex, non-linear temporal patterns that cannot be captured by a low-rank structure.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The theoretical foundations are strong, the optimization algorithm is novel and well-justified, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over baselines. The paper addresses an important practical problem with clear applications across multiple domains.

The main weaknesses lie in the limited justification for the exponential kernel choice, the somewhat overstated scalability claims, and insufficient differentiation from more recent point process-based recommendation approaches. However, these do not undermine the core technical contributions, which are significant and well-executed.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a novel theoretical framework, an efficient optimization algorithm, and comprehensive empirical validation. The work advances the state of the art in time-sensitive recommendation by providing a unified framework that handles both recommendation and returning-time prediction while scaling to large datasets. The theoretical analysis and practical relevance make this a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the kernel choice, a clearer comparison with the most recent point process approaches for recommendation, and a more nuanced discussion of the low-rank assumption's limitations. However, these are minor concerns that do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The work represents a substantial advancement over existing approaches, particularly in its ability to generalize to new items while capturing temporal dynamics.
