## Review of “Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities”

This paper proposes a low-rank Hawkes process model for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The authors aim to unify these two tasks under a single framework, leveraging the temporal dynamics of user-item interactions. They present a convex formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal and conditional gradient methods, with claimed optimal convergence rates. While the core idea of connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank models is intriguing, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet significant, flaws in presentation, technical detail, and experimental validation, ultimately hindering its potential contribution.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the limitations of traditional recommendation systems in capturing temporal user preferences. Building upon prior work in temporal point processes (Cox & Isham, Daley & Vere-Jones) and Hawkes processes (Hawkes, 1957), the authors propose a low-rank extension to model user behavior. They position their work as addressing gaps in recent approaches like Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015) by enabling recommendation of *new* items, a crucial distinction. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive but lacks nuance in comparing the algorithmic complexities and scalability of proposed and existing methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Unified Framework:** The attempt to formulate recommendation and returning-time prediction within a single probabilistic model is conceptually appealing.
*   **Low-Rank Assumption:** Employing low-rank structure offers a plausible approach to scalability and generalization.
*   **Algorithm Development:** The proposed algorithm, blending proximal and conditional gradient methods, is potentially efficient. The claimed convergence rate, if rigorously proven, would be a significant theoretical contribution.

**Weaknesses – A Detailed Critique:**

1.  **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity Issues:** The paper is plagued by numerous notational inconsistencies. For instance,  `tu,ij` is used inconsistently to represent both a specific event time and an index.  The definition of `ψu,i` in Equation (3) is incomplete and requires clarification, especially regarding the limits of integration. The transition between Equation (1) and Equation (3) is abrupt and lacks adequate explanation.
2.  **Technical Gaps & Unjustified Claims:** The claim of a “convex formulation” in Equation (4) is questionable. The nuclear norm is a *convex relaxation* of rank, not a guarantee of convexity of the entire problem. The justification for using the nuclear norm is weak; a more thorough discussion of alternative rank constraints would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the connection to convex optimization in formulation (5) is stated without sufficient detail. The explanation of Algorithm 1 is superficial; crucial steps (e.g., the line search in Algorithm 3) are glossed over. The "optimal" convergence rate claimed for Algorithm 1 needs far more rigorous justification and referencing of relevant literature.  Theorem 1, while presented, lacks a clear explanation of the condition `ρ > ρ*` and how this threshold would be determined in practice.
3.  **Experimental Rigor & Reporting:**  The experimental setup is inadequately described. Crucial details such as the parameter tuning process (beyond simply stating “tuned to give the best performance”) are missing. The choice of rank (5 for synthetic data) is arbitrary and lacks justification.  The use of different evaluation metrics (MAE for returning time, rank for recommendation) makes direct comparison across methods difficult. Figure 2(e-f) are not adequately labeled and require clearer captions. The real datasets' descriptions are sparse – key statistics (e.g., event density) are absent. The claim of better explaining observed data in Figure 3 relies on visual inspection of slopes, which is a subjective and insufficient form of evaluation. Why does the model outperform survival analysis? A more detailed discussion is needed.
4. **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline missing is a simple, non-parametric Hawkes process estimation. It's necessary to establish the benefit of adding low-rank structure *over* a standard Hawkes model.
5. **Minor errors:** There are some minor typographical errors throughout the manuscript. e.g., “g(t)” in the section "Background on Temporal Point Processes" should be defined.

**Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Significant lack of clarity and notational inconsistencies.
*   Weak technical justification for key claims (convexity, convergence).
*   Insufficient experimental rigor and reporting.
*   Missing baseline comparison.
*   Numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality.

**For:**

*   The underlying idea of combining Hawkes processes and low-rank models is promising.
*   The authors address an important problem in recommendation systems.
*   The proposed algorithm, if implemented correctly, could offer computational advantages.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its potentially interesting core idea, this paper is severely hampered by a lack of technical rigor, clarity, and thorough experimental validation. The numerous flaws, even if individually minor, accumulate to create a deeply problematic manuscript. The arguments for acceptance are outweighed by the significant weaknesses. 

**Recommendation: Reject.**

The authors should significantly revise the paper, addressing the issues outlined above. A more rigorous mathematical treatment, clearer writing, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation are essential for this work to be considered for publication at NIPS. A detailed sensitivity analysis of the hyper parameters should be reported.
