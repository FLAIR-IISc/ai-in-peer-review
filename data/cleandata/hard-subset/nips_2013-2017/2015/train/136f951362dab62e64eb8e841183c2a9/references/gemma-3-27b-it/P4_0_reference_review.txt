This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction based on low-rank Hawkes processes. The core idea is to model user-item interactions as self-exciting point processes and leverage low-rank approximations to handle scalability and generalization to unseen items. The authors develop a convex formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, demonstrating convergence guarantees and empirical effectiveness on both synthetic and real datasets.

The work builds upon the recent surge of interest in temporal point processes for recommendation systems, particularly citing Kapoor et al. [12, 11]. However, it distinguishes itself by proposing a low-rank factorization approach allowing recommendation of *new* items, a limitation of the prior work based on survival analysis or hidden semi-Markov models. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel contribution, as is the proposed optimization algorithm and its convergence analysis. Related work in tensor factorization [23, 25, 13, 3] is acknowledged, but the authors argue their method surpasses these approaches by directly modeling the temporal dynamics through the Hawkes process framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for time-sensitive recommendation is a novel and promising approach. The theoretical connection established is valuable.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation appears mathematically sound, with a convex optimization problem and a clearly described algorithm. The convergence analysis provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to scale to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events, a crucial aspect for real-world applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method compared to several baselines, including SVD, tensor factorization methods, and the related work of Kapoor et al. [12, 11]. The analysis includes ablation studies on real datasets.
*   **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The equations are well-defined and the notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Kernel Selection:** The choice of the exponential kernel appears somewhat arbitrary. While justified as a simplification, a more detailed discussion of the impact of different kernel choices (e.g., Weibull, Log-logistic) on performance would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters, the specific methods and sensitivity analysis are not detailed enough. A more thorough discussion would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
*   **Limited Baselines:** While the comparison includes relevant baselines, several more recent and sophisticated temporal recommendation models (e.g., those based on recurrent neural networks) are not considered. Including such baselines could further strengthen the empirical evaluation.
*   **Real-World Data Limitations:** The description of the real-world datasets is concise. Providing more details on their characteristics, preprocessing steps, and potential biases would be helpful.
*   **Implementation Details:** While the algorithm is described, the availability of code or more details about the implementation environment would improve reproducibility.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
*   The theoretical contributions (convex formulation, convergence analysis) are significant.
*   The empirical results demonstrate superior performance compared to relevant baselines on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The scalability of the algorithm makes it suitable for real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The choice of the exponential kernel is somewhat arbitrary and lacks detailed justification.
*   The hyperparameter tuning process is not fully transparent.
*   The comparison with more recent and sophisticated temporal recommendation models is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the formulation and algorithm, and the compelling empirical results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
