### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by modeling recurrent user activities. The authors propose a unified framework to tackle two related tasks: recommending the most relevant item at a given moment and predicting the next return time of a user to an item/service. The core of their approach is to model the event sequence for each user-item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process. To generalize to unobserved user-item pairs and enable collaborative filtering, they represent the baseline intensity and self-excitation parameters as matrices, which are assumed to be low-rank. The model is learned via a convex optimization problem that minimizes the negative log-likelihood with nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. To solve this problem efficiently, the authors propose a novel algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) methods. They demonstrate the scalability and superior predictive performance of their method on both synthetic and three real-world datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper tackles an important and practical problem in modern recommendation systems. The proposed model, which connects low-rank models with self-exciting point processes, is elegant and provides a principled way to incorporate temporal dynamics into collaborative filtering. The experimental results are comprehensive and generally show a significant improvement over several baselines.

However, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological details, and theoretical exposition. These issues, while individually minor in some cases, collectively cast doubt on the work's maturity and rigor.

**Quality:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** Theorem 1 establishes the equivalence between the proposed formulation (5) and the original problem (4) for a sufficiently large penalty parameter `ρ > ρ*`. However, the threshold `ρ*` depends on the unknown optimal solution, rendering the theorem of limited practical utility. The authors concede this by stating they tune `ρ` as a hyperparameter. This significantly weakens the theoretical contribution, as the connection between the solved problem and the original one is not guaranteed in practice.
*   **Algorithmic Description:** The descriptions of the algorithms are imprecise and contain errors. In Algorithms 1 and 3, the transpose operator is incorrectly denoted by `>`. For instance, `wu,i = (Λ0(u, i),A(u, i)) >` should use a `T`. More critically, Algorithm 3 contains the line `Xk2 [Z1] = α k 1X k 2 [Z1]`, which is a recursive definition that seems incorrect. It should likely be `Xk2 [Z1] = α k 1 u1 v1^T`, but as written, it is ambiguous. In Algorithm 1, `X0` is used without a clear definition. These notational and logical errors make the core algorithm difficult to understand and reproduce.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. The paper states that for 80% of items, sequences are split into "training/testing subsequences," but fails to specify how this split is performed (e.g., chronological split, random split). This is a critical detail for time-series evaluation. Furthermore, the adaptation of the STiC baseline for held-out items (averaging predictions from observed items) seems like a weak heuristic that may not represent the baseline fairly, potentially inflating the relative performance of the proposed model.

**Clarity:**

*   **Writing and Formatting:** The manuscript suffers from numerous formatting and typographical errors. Figure 1(a) is referenced in the introduction but is not included in the provided paper. The list of references is poorly formatted, with several entries containing extraneous commas (e.g., [1, 4, 28, 21]). The prose could also be more precise; phrases like "more imperative and meaningful than ever before" are colloquial.
*   **Model Details:** The paper assumes a simple exponential triggering kernel with a single, global bandwidth parameter `σ`. This is a strong assumption, as different user-item dynamics might require different temporal decay rates. While other kernels are mentioned as possible extensions, the implications of this simplifying assumption are not discussed. Additionally, when predicting return times, the expectation is approximated via sampling, but there is no discussion of the number of samples `m` used or the variance of this estimator.

**Originality and Significance:**

*   The core idea of using low-rank Hawkes processes for modeling network dynamics has been explored before (e.g., Zhou et al. [29], cited by the authors). The novelty lies in its specific application to the time-sensitive recommendation problem and the development of a hybrid optimization algorithm.
*   The optimization algorithm is an interesting blend of methods. However, the convergence rate claim in Theorem 2, `O(1/k)`, is dominated by the conditional gradient part and is a standard rate for such methods. The claim of achieving "the best of both worlds" is an overstatement, as the overall rate does not improve upon the slower of the two components.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in time-aware recommendation.
2.  The proposed model, combining Hawkes processes and low-rank assumptions, is a sensible and powerful approach.
3.  The empirical evaluation is extensive and demonstrates strong performance on multiple datasets, suggesting the model is effective in practice.

**Against Acceptance:**
1.  The paper is replete with notational errors, formatting inconsistencies, and unclear descriptions (e.g., missing figure, flawed algorithm pseudo-code), indicating a lack of careful preparation.
2.  The theoretical contributions are weaker than claimed. The main equivalence theorem (Theorem 1) relies on an unknowable parameter, reducing its practical relevance.
3.  Critical details regarding the experimental methodology are missing, hindering reproducibility and raising questions about the fairness of baseline comparisons.
4.  The large number of minor flaws cumulatively detracts from the paper's scientific credibility and suggests it is not yet ready for publication at a premier venue like NIPS.

### Recommendation

While the paper presents a promising direction for time-sensitive recommendations, the execution is unfortunately flawed. The combination of sloppy presentation, imprecise algorithmic details, weakened theoretical claims, and ambiguous experimental protocol prevents me from recommending acceptance. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous issues outlined above. Therefore, my recommendation is to **reject** the paper in its current form.
