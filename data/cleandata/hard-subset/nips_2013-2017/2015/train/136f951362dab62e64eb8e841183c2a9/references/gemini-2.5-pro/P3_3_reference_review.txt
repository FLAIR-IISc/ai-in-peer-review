### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by tackling two related tasks: (1) recommending the right item at a specific moment and (2) predicting a user's next return time to an item/service. The core contribution is a novel framework that models the sequence of user-item interactions as a multivariate Hawkes process. To generalize to unobserved user-item pairs and manage model complexity, the authors propose representing the parameters of the Hawkes processes—the base intensity matrix (Λ₀) and the self-excitation matrix (A)—as low-rank matrices. This elegantly connects the theory of self-exciting point processes with collaborative filtering via matrix factorization.

To learn the model parameters, the authors formulate a convex optimization problem involving the maximization of the log-likelihood of the observed event sequences, regularized by the nuclear norms of the parameter matrices Λ₀ and A, subject to non-negativity constraints. Recognizing the difficulty of solving this problem directly, they propose an alternative formulation that decouples the constraints. This enables the development of an efficient hybrid optimization algorithm that combines a proximal gradient step for the non-negatively constrained variables and a conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) step for the nuclear-norm regularized variables. The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing an O(1/t) convergence rate for this algorithm. The proposed method is evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance over several baselines, including static matrix factorization, tensor factorization, and other time-aware models.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is its elegant synthesis of two powerful modeling paradigms: Hawkes processes for temporal event data and low-rank models for collaborative filtering. This provides a principled and interpretable way to capture both long-term static user preferences (via the base intensity Λ₀) and short-term, history-dependent dynamics (via the self-exciting term A). This is a significant conceptual advance over traditional recommendation models that are either static or use ad-hoc temporal features.

2.  **Addressing Two Key Problems Simultaneously:** The proposed framework naturally addresses both the *what* (item recommendation via intensity ranking) and the *when* (return-time prediction via the conditional density) of recommendation. This is a practical and powerful feature, as many real-world systems need to optimize both aspects.

3.  **Sophisticated and Efficient Optimization:** The optimization problem is challenging due to the combination of a non-smooth regularizer (nuclear norm) and non-negativity constraints. The proposed hybrid algorithm is a clever solution. By reformulating the problem to decouple these complexities and applying the appropriate optimization tool to each part (proximal gradient for simple projections, conditional gradient for cheap linear oracles), the authors have developed a scalable and well-motivated algorithm. The inclusion of a convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides strong theoretical grounding for the method.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed.
    *   The use of a synthetic dataset where the ground truth is known effectively validates the correctness of the model and the convergence of the algorithm.
    *   The evaluation on three diverse, real-world datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II) demonstrates the broad applicability of the method.
    *   The choice of baselines is strong, including classic CF (SVD), advanced temporal CF (Tensor Factorization), and a closely related survival analysis model (STiC).
    *   The model diagnostic using the time-change theorem (Figure 3) is an excellent addition that goes beyond simple predictive accuracy, providing evidence that the Hawkes process is indeed a better distributional fit for the data than simpler alternatives.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Simplistic Triggering Kernel:** The paper primarily relies on a simple exponential triggering kernel `γ(t, ti) = exp(-(t-ti)/σ)`. While standard, this assumes that the influence of all past events decays exponentially at the same rate (governed by a global `σ`). In reality, the decay patterns could be more complex and might differ across users or items. While the authors briefly mention the possibility of using non-parametric kernels, the paper would be stronger if it included a discussion or experiment on the sensitivity to the kernel choice and the parameter `σ`. It is unclear if `σ` is a global hyperparameter or learned per-item/user.

2.  **Scalability Concerns for Large `m` and `n`:** The conditional gradient step requires computing the top singular vector pair of the gradient matrix. While cheaper than a full SVD, this operation's cost is O(mn) for dense gradients. The gradient calculation itself `∇f(X)` involves a sum over all observed user-item event sequences. For web-scale systems with millions of users and items, the memory and computational cost per iteration could still be prohibitive. The scalability plot (Figure 2(d)) shows linear scaling with the number of *events*, but the dependence on the number of users (`m`) and items (`n`) is not explicitly analyzed.

3.  **Practicality of the Reformulation (Eq. 5):** Theorem 1 shows the equivalence of the original and alternative formulations for a sufficiently large penalty parameter `ρ > ρ*`. However, `ρ*` depends on the unknown optimal solution, making it impossible to set a priori. In practice, `ρ` becomes another critical hyperparameter to tune. The paper could benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of the model's performance and convergence to the choice of `ρ`.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Model Extensions:** The paper mentions the potential to extend Λ₀ and A to tensors to incorporate contextual information like location. This is an exciting direction. Could the authors elaborate on how the optimization algorithm would adapt? Would the conditional gradient step, which relies on a top singular vector pair computation, extend naturally to a tensor LMO (e.g., via higher-order SVD)?
*   **Cold Start Problem:** How does the model handle the cold-start problem for new users or items? Presumably, their corresponding rows/columns in Λ₀ and A would be initialized to zero or some average. The model could predict a non-zero base intensity if the low-rank factors are learned, but the self-exciting component would be zero until the first event occurs. A brief discussion on this would be useful.
*   **Comparison to Neural Approaches:** In recent years, RNN-based models have become popular for modeling event sequences (e.g., "Recurrent Marked Temporal Point Processes" by Du et al., KDD 2016). These models can potentially capture more complex temporal dependencies than a Hawkes process with a fixed kernel. It would strengthen the related work section to position this work relative to such neural point process models for recommendation.

### Final Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems. The proposed connection between low-rank models and Hawkes processes is novel, theoretically sound, and practically relevant. The work is well-executed, with a sophisticated optimization algorithm supported by theoretical guarantees and a thorough empirical validation. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws. The paper is well-written, clear, and should be of high interest to the NIPS community, both for its specific application and for the general optimization technique presented. I strongly recommend acceptance.
