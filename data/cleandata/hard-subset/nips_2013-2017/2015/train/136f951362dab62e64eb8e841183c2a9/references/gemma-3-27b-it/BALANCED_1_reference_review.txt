This paper tackles the problem of time-sensitive recommendation and predicting user returning times, framing it within a Hawkes process and low-rank model. The core idea of connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models is a promising direction, as it allows for generalization to new items, a limitation of prior work like Kapoor et al. [12, 11] which rely on survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models. The authors propose a convex formulation and an algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving an O(1/t) convergence rate and demonstrating scalability to large datasets. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank models for recommendation is a novel approach, offering a unified framework for both item recommendation and returning-time prediction.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The convex formulation and convergence analysis provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The O(1/t) convergence rate is a strong result.
* **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the scalability of their algorithm to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events), which is crucial for real-world applications.
* **Empirical validation:** Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) show competitive or superior performance compared to several baselines, including Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.
* **Clear exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the key concepts and contributions accessible. The explanation of the theoretical background (temporal point processes) is concise and helpful.
* **Thorough experimentation:** The experiments are well-designed, including an ablation study to justify the use of the Hawkes model and comprehensive comparisons with relevant baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of implementation:** The proposed algorithm, while theoretically sound, appears complex to implement.  The reliance on a blend of proximal and conditional gradient methods with associated line searches and parameter tuning could present practical challenges.  More details about the implementation specifics and computational cost breakdown (e.g., time spent in each subroutine) would be helpful.
* **Parameter sensitivity:**  While the paper mentions tuning parameters ρ, λ, and β, it lacks a detailed discussion of their sensitivity and practical guidance on how to select appropriate values. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.
* **Kernel choice:** The choice of the exponential kernel is justified, but the potential impact of other kernel families (e.g., Weibull, Log-logistic) is not explored. An analysis of different kernel choices could provide further insights.
* **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper does not explicitly address potential limitations of the model, such as its assumptions about low-rank structure or its sensitivity to noisy data.
* **Presentation of results:** Some figures (e.g. Figure 2) are quite dense and could benefit from clearer labeling and a more streamlined presentation.  The justification for comparing against certain baselines isn't always entirely clear.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem (time-sensitive recommendation).
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness and scalability of the proposed method.
* The authors address a significant gap in the literature by generalizing to unseen items, which is a key limitation of prior work.
* The convergence analysis and detailed experiments suggest a high-quality contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The complexity of the algorithm and lack of detailed implementation guidance could hinder adoption.
* The parameter sensitivity and limited exploration of kernel choices raise concerns about robustness.
* A more thorough discussion of the model’s limitations would be beneficial.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The core idea is novel, the theoretical grounding is solid, and the experimental results are compelling. While the complexity of the algorithm and the lack of detailed implementation guidance are concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I believe the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of recommendation systems and would be suitable for acceptance to NeurIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I recommend the authors add a section detailing the practical considerations of implementing the algorithm, including parameter tuning guidelines and computational cost analysis, as well as a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach.
