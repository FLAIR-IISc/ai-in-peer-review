Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles two coupled problems: (i) time-sensitive item recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given time), and (ii) returning-time prediction (predicting the next event time for a user–item pair). It models each user–item interaction sequence as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an exponential triggering kernel. To generalize to unobserved user–item pairs, it places low-rank structure (via nuclear norms) on two m×n parameter matrices: the baseline intensities Λ0 and the self-excitation coefficients A. The authors propose a convex formulation with nonnegativity constraints and nuclear-norm regularizers, then introduce an auxiliary-variable quadratic-penalty reformulation to decouple nonnegativity and nuclear-norm terms. They devise an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity-constrained smooth part) and conditional gradient/Frank–Wolfe (for the nuclear-norm terms) with an accelerated scheme, and prove an O(1/t) convergence rate (and recovery of O(1/t^2) when the nuclear-norm term is absent). Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) compare against Poisson, SVD, tensor factorization, and a survival/HMM-based return-time baseline (STiC), showing improved ranking and return-time prediction, plus a time-change diagnostic suggesting better fit than survival models.

This work is related to prior Hawkes-based learning of low-rank/sparse infectivity matrices (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song; Du et al.) and to temporal recommendation via factorization (Koren; tensor factorization). The novelty here is framing time-sensitive recommendation as low-rank Hawkes over user–item grids with a convex nuclear-norm approach for both baseline and excitation matrices, and providing an optimization scheme that handles nonnegativity and nuclear norms jointly at scale.

Quality
Strengths
- Modeling choice is appropriate for recurrent, bursty user–item interactions; the Hawkes formulation captures short-term excitation while Λ0 captures long-term preference.
- Clear convex formulation with nonnegativity and nuclear norms; the optimization algorithm is well-motivated, leveraging efficient LMO via top singular vectors and simple projections for nonnegativity.
- Convergence analysis gives a concrete rate and recovers known rates in special cases; the bound structure reflects the hybrid nature of the method.
- Empirical evaluation spans synthetic stress tests (including scaling) and multiple real domains; the time-change diagnostic is a nice sanity check of temporal fit; results are consistently better than static baselines and a survival/HMM approach.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling assumptions limit cross-excitation: the process is per user–item with self-excitation only. Many domains (especially comorbidities in EHR) require mutually exciting effects across items/diseases. The paper mentions extensions but does not implement or evaluate them; this is most noticeable in the MIMIC II claims, where observed dependencies are cross-disease.
- Exactness of the quadratic-penalty reformulation (Theorem 1): quadratic penalties are generally not exact for equality constraints except in the limit ρ→∞. The theorem states a finite threshold ρ*, but it is problem-dependent and defined using unknown optimal quantities. The proof sketch is deferred; without a constructive bound or practical criterion, this “equivalence” is more a justification for using a sufficiently large ρ than a guarantee. Practically the paper tunes ρ, but the theory as stated is nonstandard and would benefit from clarification.
- Evaluation details: hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth) are “tuned to give the best performance,” but the train/validation/test split used for tuning is not specified. This raises concerns about potential test leakage. Also, returning-time prediction uses Monte Carlo via Ogata’s thinning; more details on the number of samples, runtime, and stability would help.
- Baselines: Missing stronger temporal point process baselines that perform marked sequence modeling and return-time prediction (e.g., mutually exciting Hawkes over items for each user; nonparametric Hawkes; or more recent point-process recommendation models). Within tensor methods, treating time continuously vs. coarse bins is a key distinction; results could be complemented by a continuous-time factorization or renewal-process baseline.
- Reproducibility: Important implementation details are missing (initialization, step sizes/Lipschitz estimates or line search, stopping criteria, how top singular vectors are computed at scale, handling of numerical stability for log terms). No code is provided.

Clarity
Overall well-organized and readable. The modeling and algorithm sections are clear, with helpful notation and pseudocode. A few suggestions:
- Unify notation: αu,i and A(u,i) are used interchangeably; stick with A(u,i).
- Explicitly state assumptions ensuring λu,i(t)>0 and gradient Lipschitzness (bounds on φ; role of nonnegativity).
- Clarify train/val/test protocol and how held-out “entire sequences” are constructed and used.
- Provide computational complexity per iteration and wall-clock comparisons; detail the SVD computation (power method, tolerance, number of iterations).

Originality
Moderately original. Using Hawkes for time-sensitive recommendation is natural but not ubiquitous; the low-rank convex parameterization over user–item pairs for both baseline and excitation is a useful twist. The hybrid proximal–Frank–Wolfe scheme under nonnegativity and nuclear norms is interesting, though conceptually close to existing composite optimization with LMOs. Prior low-rank Hawkes work focused on influence networks; this paper’s contribution is its user–item instantiation and application to recommendation/return-time with a convex estimator.

Significance
The problem is important, and a convex, scalable estimator is valuable. If widely adopted, the approach could influence time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction in practice. However, the lack of cross-excitation limits applicability in domains where inter-item dependencies are central. The optimization idea may be of broader interest, contingent on clarifying the exactness claim.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled continuous-time model.
- Convex formulation with a scalable algorithm and convergence guarantees.
- Solid empirical gains across multiple datasets; useful diagnostic via time-change theorem.
- Clear exposition and practical procedure for ranking and return-time prediction.

Cons for acceptance
- Questionable exactness claim for the quadratic-penalty reformulation; needs clarification or a more standard augmented Lagrangian/ADMM treatment.
- Limited modeling capacity (no cross-item excitation) versus the motivating applications.
- Baseline coverage and evaluation protocol details are insufficient; risk of optimistic tuning.
- Reproducibility details and computational comparisons need strengthening.

Suggestions to improve
- Either (a) replace the quadratic penalty with an augmented Lagrangian/ADMM variant, or (b) justify Theorem 1 rigorously and provide a practical procedure to set ρ with guarantees.
- Add and evaluate a mutually exciting variant (low-rank factorization across items) or include cross-item excitation for a subset of items/diseases.
- Provide a clear validation protocol, ablations (e.g., only Λ0 low-rank; only A low-rank), and sensitivity to σ, ρ, λ, β; report calibration of return-time predictions.
- Include runtime and memory comparisons; detail the top singular vector computations.
- Release code or pseudocode sufficient for reproduction.

Overall assessment relative to NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Good ideas and analysis; experiments promising; some theoretical and experimental details need tightening.
- Clarity: Generally clear; minor issues as noted.
- Originality: Moderate; a thoughtful combination with some novel elements.
- Significance: Potentially impactful, especially if extended to cross-excitation and with stronger empirical validation.
