## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation, leveraging the connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem, particularly in the context of increasingly personalized web services. While the method builds upon existing work in both temporal point processes and matrix factorization, the combination is novel and the practical results are compelling. I believe this paper is a strong contribution and recommend acceptance.

**Summary of Ideas:** The authors tackle the problem of recommending items at the right time and predicting user return times by modeling user-item interactions as a Hawkes process. A key contribution is the incorporation of low-rank structures within the intensity function of the Hawkes process, enabling generalization to new items and efficient estimation. They develop a novel optimization algorithm based on a blend of proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving a favorable convergence rate.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing improvements over existing techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Combining Hawkes processes with low-rank models for recommendation is a genuinely new approach.  While Hawkes processes have been used for modeling user behavior (Kapoor et al.), the low-rank formulation allows for generalization to unseen items, a crucial advantage.
* **Technical Soundness:** The formulation is mathematically rigorous, and the derivation of the optimization algorithm is well-explained. The convergence analysis is a strong point, providing theoretical guarantees for the algorithm's performance.
* **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the algorithm's ability to scale to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs), which is essential for real-world applications.
* **Empirical Evaluation:**  The experimental results are thorough and convincing.  The comparisons against several baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, Tensor Factorization) show consistent improvements, both in item recommendation and return-time prediction. The analysis on real-world datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II) further strengthens the claims.
* **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical notation is used consistently, and the explanations are accessible.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – time-sensitive recommendation – is highly relevant to modern web services and has practical implications for advertising, healthcare, and other domains.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors present a detailed algorithm, the implementation likely requires significant effort. Providing a publicly available code repository would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.
* **Kernel Selection:** The authors primarily focus on the exponential kernel. While justified, a more detailed discussion of the impact of different kernel choices, and perhaps an automated kernel selection mechanism, would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but lacks detailed information about the specific strategies used.  A more rigorous analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameters would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons are made to relevant baselines, the field of time-sensitive recommendation is evolving.  Comparing to more recent deep learning-based approaches (e.g., recurrent neural networks with attention mechanisms for sequential recommendation) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.  However, this is not a *critical* weakness given the strong performance against existing methods.




**Originality:** The paper demonstrates significant originality. The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel contribution. While the individual components are not new, their integration provides a fresh perspective on time-sensitive recommendation. The authors acknowledge related work appropriately, particularly the work of Kapoor et al.  The paper clearly articulates how their approach differs from and improves upon existing techniques.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. The ability to accurately predict user return times and recommend items at the right moment has substantial practical implications.  The method's scalability and generalizability make it potentially useful for a wide range of applications. The theoretical results (convergence analysis) and practical demonstrations provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a relevant problem.
* Strong theoretical guarantees (convergence analysis).
* Compelling empirical results on synthetic and real datasets.
* Scalability to large-scale data.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Implementation complexity.
* Limited exploration of kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning.
* Lack of comparison to the most recent deep learning-based approaches. (This is a minor point).




**Final Recommendation:** **Accept**

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems. The novelty, technical soundness, and empirical results justify its acceptance to NIPS. While there are minor areas for improvement (e.g., code release, more detailed hyperparameter analysis), these do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. I believe this paper will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
