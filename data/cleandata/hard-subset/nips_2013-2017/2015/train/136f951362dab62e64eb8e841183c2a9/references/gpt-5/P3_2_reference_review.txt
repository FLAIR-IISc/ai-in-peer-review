Summary
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework based on a collection of univariate Hawkes processes, one per user–item pair, with exponential triggering kernels. To enable generalization to unobserved user–item pairs, the model places low-rank structure on two m×n parameter matrices: the baseline intensities Λ0 and the self-excitation coefficients A. The estimation problem is posed as a convex program: minimizing (average) negative log-likelihood plus nuclear-norm penalties on Λ0 and A, with nonnegativity constraints. Because the nuclear norm and nonnegativity are awkward to handle jointly, the authors introduce auxiliary variables and a quadratic penalty (ρ) that decouples the nuclear-norm terms from the nonnegativity constraints. They then propose an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for the nonnegative Λ0,A blocks) with conditional gradient/LMO steps (for the nuclear-norm-regularized Z1,Z2 blocks). They establish an O(1/t) convergence rate for the composite scheme, give an exact-penalty-style equivalence condition (Theorem 1), and evaluate on large synthetic data and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II), reporting improved item ranking and return-time MAE compared to Poisson, SVD/MF, tensor Poisson factorization, and a survival-analysis baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on temporal point processes and Hawkes processes, and is related to low-rank estimation with nuclear norms. It differs from prior return-time prediction via survival analysis and HSMMs by modeling recurrent events and enabling transfer across pairs via low rank. It is also related to prior Hawkes learning in networked settings (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song) and structured rank minimization with conditional gradients (Harchaoui et al., Lan et al.). The paper claims novelty in the convex low-rank Hawkes formulation and the mixed proximal/conditional gradient optimizer.

Strengths
- Clear, unified formulation linking time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction via point processes; the intensity ranking is a principled time-aware scoring function.
- Convex objective with global optimality guarantees (modulo the penalty approximation), avoiding nonconvex MF-style local minima.
- Algorithmic contribution: a practical method that avoids repeated full SVDs by using LMOs with top singular vectors, and a proof of an O(1/t) rate for the composite scheme.
- Sensible experimental protocol including held-out user–item pairs to test transfer; good use of time-rescaling QQ plots to diagnose model fit.
- Demonstrated scalability on large synthetic data; real-data results show consistent gains over static MF and tensor Poisson baselines.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling assumptions:
  - Independence across user–item pairs: no cross-excitation among items or competition effects; the model cannot capture substitution or co-consumption patterns that multivariate Hawkes can.
  - The univariate Hawkes stability condition (branching ratio < 1) is not discussed or enforced. With exponential kernels, ασ < 1 should hold per pair; otherwise the process can be supercritical. How is this handled in training and prediction?
  - Using a single scalar αu,i per pair means all past events on that pair contribute identically up to decay; richer history features (e.g., marks, periodicities) or multiple kernels would likely help.
- Penalty-equivalence (Theorem 1): the “exactness” threshold ρ* depends on unknown optimal solutions, making it unusable in practice; the method effectively solves a surrogate tuned by CV. Please discuss sensitivity to ρ and whether violations of equivalence materially change solutions.
- Convergence claims: Theorem 2 establishes O(1/t). The remark that the algorithm “achieves the best of both worlds” and “recovers O(1/t^2) when no nuclear norm” is potentially misleading in the composite case; the presented bound is O(1/t). Clarify precisely under which settings the faster O(1/t^2) holds (pure smooth + proximal step), and do not imply faster rates for the mixed case.
- Computational details:
  - Although LMOs only require top singular vectors, the paper lacks wall-clock comparisons versus alternative solvers (e.g., factorized low-rank parameterization with projected gradient; randomized/truncated SVD proximal steps). Memory representation of Z1,Z2 as sums of rank-1 atoms and its growth over iterations is not discussed.
  - Online return-time prediction via Ogata’s thinning and Monte Carlo averaging is expensive. How many samples are used? What is the latency for real-time prediction? Closed-form or root-finding solutions for the next-event time with exponential kernels may be more efficient.
- Evaluation:
  - Ranking metric is true-item rank; more standard metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k) would aid interpretation.
  - Baselines omit strong sequential recommenders (timeSVD++, factorization with temporal features) and Hawkes-based or RNN/point-process sequence models. While some are newer, at least a low-rank Poisson-only version of the proposed convex model (Λ0 with nuclear norm, no Hawkes) would be a critical ablation to isolate the gain from self-excitation vs low-rank transfer.
  - For held-out pairs with no history, the model reduces to ranking by Λ0; it would be helpful to disentangle cold-start gains due purely to low-rank transfer from gains due to Hawkes dynamics when there is history.
  - Statistical significance and variance across users/items are not reported.
- Scope of “new items”: The paper claims generalization to “any new item,” but without content/context features the method cannot score items never seen during training. Please clarify the intended notion of “new” (new to a user vs globally new).

Quality: The modeling and optimization are mostly sound, and the empirical results are promising. However, the stability issue, limited baselines/ablations, and practical aspects of prediction latency need attention.

Clarity: Generally well written, but Algorithm 1–3 notations are terse; define all brackets and blocks clearly; fix minor typos and inconsistent numbering. Explicitly state computational complexity per iteration and memory usage.

Originality: The combination of convex low-rank Hawkes with a mixed proximal/conditional gradient solver appears novel and useful, though related components exist. The exact-penalty formulation for handling nonnegativity plus nuclear norms is interesting.

Significance: The problem is important. If strengthened with better baselines, ablations, and stability/latency analysis, this could be impactful for time-sensitive recommendation and broader convex PP learning.

Questions for authors
- How do you ensure Hawkes stability (ασ < 1) per pair? Did you ever observe exploding intensities?
- What is the runtime per iteration and total training time on the largest real dataset? How do ranks of Z1,Z2 grow, and do you truncate?
- How many samples are used for return-time prediction, and what is the average per-query latency?
- Can you report Recall@k/NDCG@k and add a low-rank Poisson-only ablation?
- Can you incorporate simple periodic basis functions (time-of-day/week) or multiple exponential kernels?

Pros for acceptance
- Principled time-sensitive model with convex learning.
- Scalable algorithmic contribution with convergence analysis.
- Empirically strong results on multiple datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baselines/ablations; unclear where gains come from.
- Stability and prediction-latency issues not addressed.
- Some overstatements about convergence and “new items.”

Recommendation: Weak accept if the above concerns (especially stability, ablations, and clearer computational analysis) can be addressed; otherwise borderline. Confidence: High.
