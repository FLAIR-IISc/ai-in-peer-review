- Proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework modeling each user–item pair as a univariate self-exciting Hawkes process with Λ0 and A low-rank and estimated via nuclear-norm regularization.  
- Poses learning as a convex program (negative Hawkes log-likelihood + nuclear norms, nonnegativity) and scales via an auxiliary-variable penalized formulation.  
- Introduces a blended optimization algorithm using proximal-gradient on nonnegative variables and conditional-gradient (Frank–Wolfe) on nuclear-norm variables, claiming O(1/t) convergence for the penalized problem.  
- Asserts an “exact penalty” theorem claiming equivalence between penalized and original formulations for sufficiently large penalty ρ.  
- Evaluates on large synthetic data (100M events) and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II), claiming improved ranking and return-time prediction vs several baselines.  
- Connects to prior work on multivariate/low-rank Hawkes estimation, time-aware collaborative filtering, and return-time prediction via survival/semi-Markov models.  
- Key novelty: convex low-rank Hawkes formulation for per-pair self-excitation plus a scalable proximal/conditional-gradient optimization under nonnegativity.  
- Strength: clear motivation for time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction tasks.  
- Strength: elegant convex formulation with per-pair linear parametrization making the log-likelihood concave in parameters.  
- Strength: practical algorithmic design separating nonnegativity and nuclear-norm constraints, using cheap projections and top singular-vector computations.  
- Strength: plausible theoretical O(1/t) rate for the penalized objective and large-scale experiments demonstrating scalability.  
- Strength: use of time-rescaling plots as a diagnostic to compare model fit to survival baselines.  
- Major technical concern: Theorem 1’s exact-penalty claim (squared Frobenius penalty) is likely incorrect—squared penalties do not generally produce exact equivalence for finite ρ.  
- Major technical concern: theoretical guarantees for the original problem depend on the flawed penalty equivalence and need clarification or correction.  
- Stability concern: no enforcement or discussion of Hawkes stationarity constraints (A(u,i)σ < 1), risking supercritical intensities.  
- Return-time estimation concern: Monte Carlo/Ogata thinning details (number of samples, variance, runtime) are omitted; test-time cost may be high.  
- Modeling concern: model disallows cross-excitation between items despite motivating examples (e.g., inter-disease triggering), limiting fidelity.  
- Modeling concern: single global kernel bandwidth σ is used/tuned; heterogeneous or learned kernels are not implemented, limiting expressivity.  
- Identifiability concern: potential trade-offs between Λ0 and A in finite windows; no discussion or regularization to disentangle baseline vs excitation.  
- Evaluation concern: missing strong time-aware CF baselines (TimeSVD++, temporal Poisson factorization, RNNs) and low-rank Hawkes baselines, weakening empirical claims.  
- Metric concern: ranking metric (rank of ground-truth at event time) can favor popularity-heavy methods; top-k metrics and time-dependent AUC are suggested.  
- Reproducibility concern: missing details on step-size, iterations, stopping criteria, SVD implementation, wall-clock times, memory, MC samples, and hyperparameter search; code availability not mentioned.  
- Presentation issues: occasional inconsistent notation, typos, and nonstandard terminology (e.g., “LMOψ”) that need clarification.  
- Key questions for authors: justify/repair Theorem 1 or quantify approximation gap vs ρ; report whether stability constraints were enforced and fraction supercritical; give MC sample counts and prediction runtimes; provide wall-clock/memory and SVD costs; show sensitivity to σ, λ, β, ρ; explain omission of multivariate Hawkes and TimeSVD++ baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: important problem, appealing Hawkes + low-rank transfer idea, scalable algorithmic design, promising large-scale results.  
- Cons for acceptance: flawed exact-penalty theorem as stated, missing strong baselines, limited analysis of modeling assumptions (no cross-excitation, stability), and reproducibility gaps.  
- Overall recommendation: weak reject unless Theorem 1 is fixed/reframed and stronger baselines and additional analyses are provided.
