### NIPS Review

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel method for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling recurrent user activities. The core contribution is the unification of two powerful modeling paradigms: self-exciting Hawkes processes for capturing temporal dynamics within a single user-item interaction sequence, and low-rank matrix factorization for collaborative filtering and knowledge transfer across all user-item pairs. The authors propose representing the key parameters of the Hawkes processes—the baseline intensity (Λ₀) and the self-excitation magnitude (A)—as two matrices. By assuming these matrices are low-rank and regularizing them with the nuclear norm, the model can predict interaction likelihoods and returning times even for user-item pairs with no prior history. To solve the resulting non-smooth convex optimization problem with non-negativity constraints, the paper develops a novel and efficient algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) methods, for which they provide a theoretical convergence guarantee. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on both synthetic and three real-world datasets, outperforming several state-of-the-art baselines on tasks of both item recommendation and returning-time prediction.

### Detailed Review

**Quality:**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is well-founded, and the claims are rigorously supported by both theoretical analysis and strong empirical evidence.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation in Equation 4 is a principled approach to the problem, combining the negative log-likelihood of the point process with nuclear norm regularization. The derivation is clear and correct. The main theoretical contribution is the development of an efficient optimization algorithm and its convergence analysis (Theorem 2). The strategy of using an alternative formulation (Equation 5) to decouple the non-negativity constraints from the nuclear norm penalty is clever and effective. The provided O(1/t) convergence rate is optimal for this class of problems and represents a solid theoretical result.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    1.  The use of synthetic data is excellent for validating the algorithm's convergence, scalability, and ability to recover the ground-truth parameters.
    2.  The evaluation on three diverse, real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) demonstrates the model's general applicability.
    3.  The choice of baselines is strong, covering static collaborative filtering (SVD), simpler temporal models (Poisson), recent related work (STiC), and more complex tensor-based methods.
    4.  A particularly strong point is the model validation using the time-change theorem (Figure 3), which provides direct evidence that the Hawkes process assumption is a better fit for the data's underlying dynamics than competing survival analysis models. This adds significant credibility to the modeling choices.
    5.  The evaluation on both held-out subsequences and entirely held-out user-item sequences properly tests the model's ability to handle both "warm-start" and "cold-start" temporal prediction scenarios.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The motivation is compellingly laid out in the introduction, and the background on point processes is concise and sufficient. The model is developed logically from single user-item pairs to the full collaborative model.

However, there are areas where clarity could be improved:
*   The description of the optimization algorithm (Section 4.3 and Algorithm 1) is quite dense. While experts in composite optimization might follow it, a more accessible explanation or a more detailed pseudo-code could significantly broaden the paper's audience. For instance, the transition between Uk-1, Xk, and Yk in Algorithm 1 could be motivated more intuitively.
*   The connection between the original problem (Eq. 4) and the alternative formulation (Eq. 5) relies on Theorem 1, which states the equivalence holds for a sufficiently large ρ > ρ*. Since ρ* is problem-dependent and hard to compute, this introduces a critical hyperparameter. The paper states that ρ is tuned, but a brief discussion on the practical challenges of setting ρ and the model's sensitivity to it would be valuable.

**Originality:**

The work is highly original. While both Hawkes processes and low-rank models are established techniques, their combination in this context is novel and creates a powerful new tool for recommendation systems.

*   Previous work on temporal recommendations often discretizes time (e.g., tensor factorization) or models return times for *observed* items only (e.g., STiC, survival analysis). This paper's key innovation is its ability to make continuous-time predictions for *any* item, observed or not, by learning a collaborative low-rank structure on the temporal process parameters. This is a significant conceptual leap.
*   The proposed optimization algorithm, which blends proximal and conditional gradient methods to handle the specific structure of this problem (non-negativity + nuclear norm), appears to be novel and could be of independent interest to the optimization community.
*   The related work section is adequate, correctly positioning the paper with respect to key prior art like Kapoor et al. [11, 12] and various tensor factorization methods.

**Significance:**

The paper's contributions are significant and likely to have a substantial impact on both research and practice in recommender systems.

*   It addresses a critical and often-overlooked aspect of recommendation: *timing*. Predicting not just *what* a user might like but *when* they are most receptive is a valuable problem for user engagement and resource management (e.g., ad bidding, as mentioned).
*   The proposed framework is elegant and generalizable. As the authors note, it can be extended to include other contextual features by modifying the intensity function, making it a flexible foundation for future research.
*   The demonstrated scalability to hundreds of millions of events suggests the method is practical for real-world applications.
*   By providing a robust method that outperforms strong baselines, the paper advances the state of the art in time-aware recommendation and provides a new, powerful tool for modeling recurrent user events in various domains (e-commerce, healthcare, etc.).

---

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):

1.  **Novel and Impactful Formulation:** The core idea of embedding Hawkes process parameters in low-rank matrices is a highly original and powerful way to unify temporal dynamics with collaborative filtering.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper presents a novel optimization algorithm with a proven optimal convergence rate, adding significant technical depth.
3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic and diverse real-world datasets, strong baselines, and insightful model validation checks.
4.  **Addresses an Important Practical Problem:** The paper moves beyond static ratings to model the crucial dimension of time in user behavior, with clear applications in many industries.

### Arguments for Rejection (Cons):

1.  **Clarity of Optimization Algorithm:** The description of the algorithm is dense and could be made more accessible, potentially hindering reproducibility for non-experts.
2.  **Practicality of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The reliance on a theoretically-justified but practically-difficult-to-set hyperparameter (ρ) is a minor weakness. A sensitivity analysis would have strengthened the paper.
3.  **Limited Exploration of Kernels:** The experiments rely solely on the exponential triggering kernel. While this is a standard choice, the model's performance could be sensitive to this assumption, and this is not explored.

**Overall Recommendation:**

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, technically sound, and empirically validated solution to a significant problem. The fusion of point processes and low-rank models is an elegant and impactful contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
