Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses two linked problems: (1) time-sensitive item recommendation (choosing items that are most likely to be consumed at a given time), and (2) predicting the next return time for a user-item pair. The core modeling choice is to represent each user–item pair as a univariate self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i)∑j γ(t, tu,ij), using an exponential kernel γ. To generalize to unobserved pairs and reduce parameters, the authors impose low-rank structure on the base intensity matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A via nuclear norm penalties. They formulate a convex estimation problem (negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norms with nonnegativity constraints), introduce a variable-splitting penalty formulation to decouple nuclear norms and nonnegativity, and propose an optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for the nuclear norms). They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate and show scalability to large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II). Empirically, they outperform Poisson, tensor factorization baselines (Poisson regression loss), and a survival-analysis/HsMM baseline for return-time prediction, and they use time-change Q–Q diagnostics to support the Hawkes assumption.

This builds on temporal point processes (Aalen et al.; Daley & Vere-Jones), Hawkes processes (Hawkes 1971), and learning Hawkes parameters in networks (e.g., Zhou et al. AISTATS’13; Du et al. KDD’15), and on low-rank matrix/tensor factorization in recommendation. The algorithmic approach relates to conditional gradient methods for norm-regularized smooth convex problems (Harchaoui et al.) and proximal/conditional gradient hybrids.

Strengths
- Clear, unified probabilistic framework that directly ties intensity to both time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction.
- Convex formulation with global optimality for the maximum likelihood objective under linear intensity in parameters; practical nonnegativity enforcement.
- Optimization method is well-motivated, simple to implement (projection to nonnegative orthant + top singular vectors), and has a stated convergence guarantee.
- Demonstrated scalability to 100M events and large user–item matrices with encouraging wall-clock scaling.
- Empirical performance is consistently strong across synthetic and multiple real datasets; the Hawkes vs survival Q–Q plots are a nice diagnostic.
- The model naturally handles prediction for unseen user–item pairs via low-rank sharing.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Exactness of the variable-splitting penalty: Theorem 1 claims equivalence between the constrained formulation and the quadratic-penalty formulation for finite ρ > ρ*. Quadratic penalties are generally not exact; exact penalties typically use L1-type terms or augmented Lagrangians. The provided expression for ρ* depends on unknown optimal quantities. This needs clarification or a more standard argument (e.g., showing convergence of solutions as ρ→∞, or switching to an augmented Lagrangian/ADMM scheme).
- Stability/identifiability of Hawkes parameters: There is no discussion of conditions such as A(u,i)∫γ < 1 (branching ratio) to avoid explosive processes, nor of how stationarity/regularization prevents pathological solutions. This is standard in Hawkes modeling and should be addressed.
- Modeling assumption limits: The process is self-exciting per user–item pair, with no cross-excitation across items for the same user (e.g., a play of artist A triggering plays of similar artists). Multivariate Hawkes with cross-excitation could improve recommendation and return-time accuracy; at minimum, the limitation should be discussed.
- Baselines and metrics: Tensor baselines are reasonable, but stronger time-aware recommenders (timeSVD++, context-aware ranking) and sequential models could be considered. For ranking, only average rank is reported; top-k hit rate/recall and NDCG would be more standard. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Optimization details: While the algorithm avoids full SVD, top singular vectors still require iterative methods; per-iteration complexity and number of power iterations are not reported. The Lipschitz constant L used in step sizes is unspecified. ADMM baselines need not use full SVD at each step (partial randomized SVDs are common); the “full SVD” comparison may be unfair without careful tuning.
- Return-time estimation relies on Monte Carlo sampling; for exponential kernels closed-form sampling is possible (via thinning with piecewise-constant hazard integrals), and sometimes expectations can be approximated analytically. Runtime/variance trade-offs are not discussed.

Quality
- Generally technically sound: the convexity of the negative log-likelihood with linear intensity and nonnegativity is correct. The algorithm and convergence analysis are plausible and reference established accelerated schemes. Theorem 1 is the main point needing stronger justification.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough in scale and includes multiple tasks and datasets, but could be broadened in baselines and metrics, and include significance testing and ablations (e.g., effect of nuclear norms, Λ0-only vs A-only).

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well structured. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., φ, ψ definitions) and the optimization section could better motivate the hybrid scheme and specify L and per-iteration cost.
- Typos and minor formatting issues exist; figures should include axes, units, and confidence intervals.

Originality
- Modeling low-rank user–item Hawkes intensities for recommendation/return time is a novel and useful combination of established ideas. The optimization blend is a reasonable variant of known approaches, applied to this specific structure.

Significance
- The problem is important; the approach is likely to be useful to practitioners and researchers working on temporal recommendation and event modeling. The scalability and convexity make it attractive.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the validity of Theorem 1 for finite ρ. Is there a rigorous exact-penalty argument, or should this be stated as an approximation whose solutions converge as ρ increases?
- Do you enforce any stability constraints on A (e.g., Aσ < 1 for exponential kernel bandwidth σ)? Empirically, do you observe explosive behavior?
- What are the per-iteration costs (number of power iterations for top singular vectors), and wall-clock vs accuracy trade-offs? How is L chosen/estimated?
- How sensitive are results to the kernel bandwidth and to λ, β, ρ? Provide a sensitivity plot.
- Can you report standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k) and significance tests?

Pros for acceptance
- Convex, scalable method with convergence guarantees
- Strong empirical results on large, real datasets
- Clear connection between time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction
- Potentially impactful for temporal recommendation

Cons against acceptance
- Questionable exactness claim for quadratic penalty reformulation
- Limited baseline comparisons/metrics; missing statistical rigor
- No discussion/enforcement of Hawkes stability; limited cross-excitation modeling
- Some missing implementation details hindering reproducibility

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward accept if the authors clarify the penalty equivalence, add standard ranking metrics and significance, and discuss stability/identifiability. Otherwise, this is a solid, well-motivated contribution with convincing scale and practical relevance.
