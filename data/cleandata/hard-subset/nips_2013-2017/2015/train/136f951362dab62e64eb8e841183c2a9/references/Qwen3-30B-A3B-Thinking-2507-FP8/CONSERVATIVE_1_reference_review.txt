

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors claim to address two key problems: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting user returning times. They formulate the problem as a convex optimization task with nuclear norm regularization, develop a combined proximal gradient and conditional gradient algorithm, and demonstrate their method on synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized, technically sound, and presents a rigorous convergence analysis (Theorem 2). The mathematical formulation is clear, and the experimental design appears reasonable. The connection between point processes and low-rank models is interesting, and the optimization algorithm is well-motivated. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their approach, and the paper is clearly written with sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Weaknesses:** The primary concern is the lack of sufficient novelty compared to existing work. The core idea of using Hawkes processes for recommendation is not new (see Du et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2013, 2016), and the "low-rank" extension is a standard technique applied to a new context rather than a truly novel contribution. The related work section fails to adequately distinguish the authors' approach from previous methods. For instance, they cite Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015) for time-sensitive recommendation, but don't clearly explain how their approach differs from or improves upon these methods.

The experimental validation is insufficiently thorough. The authors compare against a limited set of baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization variants), but fail to compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods for time-sensitive recommendation. The synthetic data experiments are somewhat artificial, and the real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) are relatively small and limited in scope. The paper claims "superb predictive performance" but doesn't provide enough evidence to justify this claim, particularly when compared to the most recent approaches in the field.

The paper also makes an unsubstantiated claim that their model "can be readily generalized to incorporate other contextual information," but provides no empirical evidence to support this assertion. The experimental results on the MIMIC II medical dataset are particularly underdeveloped, with no clear demonstration of how this method specifically advances healthcare applications beyond standard recommendation systems.

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical contribution is overstated. The optimization algorithm is a combination of existing techniques (proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods), not a fundamentally new approach. While the convergence rate analysis is technically correct, it doesn't represent a major advance over the existing literature on these methods.

**Significance:** The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is important, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how their approach advances the state of the art in a meaningful way. The claimed improvements over baselines are marginal, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that their method is substantially better than existing approaches. The real-world impact of their method is unclear, particularly for the medical application they mention.

**Comparison to NIPS Work:** Recent NIPS papers on time-sensitive recommendation (e.g., Wang et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020) have developed more sophisticated approaches to modeling temporal dynamics in recommendation systems, often incorporating deep learning techniques. The current paper's approach appears to be a simpler, more traditional method that doesn't clearly outperform these more recent approaches.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it lacks sufficient novelty and fails to provide adequate experimental validation against strong baselines. The authors have not convincingly demonstrated that their approach represents a meaningful advance over existing methods for time-sensitive recommendation. The paper reads more like an incremental extension of existing work rather than a significant contribution to the field.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Providing a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods
2. Demonstrating the approach on larger, more diverse datasets
3. Providing stronger justification for the choice of nuclear norm regularization
4. More clearly articulating the differences between this work and previous applications of Hawkes processes to recommendation
5. Including a more detailed discussion of limitations and potential failure cases

Without these improvements, the paper does not meet the high standards expected for NIPS, particularly given the conference's emphasis on novel contributions to the field of machine learning.
