This paper presents a novel and compelling framework for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, cleverly connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The core idea – modeling recurrent user activities as Hawkes processes and leveraging low-rank structure for generalization – is highly original and represents a significant departure from traditional recommendation approaches focused on static preferences. The authors tackle a genuinely important problem, particularly in the context of increasingly personalized and dynamic web services, and even extend the applicability to healthcare, demonstrating a broad vision. The proposed algorithm, blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, appears well-developed and the reported convergence rate is promising. The empirical evaluation, including both synthetic and real datasets, is reasonably thorough and supports the claims of superior performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation is a genuinely novel contribution. This is not simply an incremental improvement but a fundamentally different approach.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed – capturing time-varying user preferences and predicting future engagement – is highly relevant and impactful. Successful solutions have significant practical implications.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears rigorous, and the proposed optimization algorithm is well-motivated and theoretically grounded (O(1/t) convergence).
*   **Scalability:** The demonstrated scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events is crucial for real-world applicability.
*   **Generalizability:** The extension to incorporate contextual information adds to the versatility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons are made, some are to rather basic baselines (Poisson process, SVD). A more comprehensive comparison to recent, sophisticated temporal recommendation models beyond Kapoor et al. (which the authors *do* address well) would strengthen the work.  Specifically, the comparison to Tensor Factorization is not entirely convincing, as the variants tested are described as differing only by interval size.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors acknowledge tuning ρ, λ, and β, but do not discuss the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters.  A brief ablation study or sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
*   **Kernel Choice:** The justification for using the exponential kernel is somewhat brief.  While it's a common choice, exploring other kernel functions or even learning the kernel directly (as mentioned in the paper) could further strengthen the results.
*   **Real-world Dataset Exploration:** The experiments on real datasets could benefit from more detailed analysis. A deeper dive into the specific characteristics of each dataset and how the model’s performance varies across different user/item groups would provide more insightful results.  The descriptions of the datasets are a little thin.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially transformative approach to recommendation systems.
*   The technical contribution – the formulation and algorithm – is significant and well-developed.
*   The empirical results, while not exhaustive, are encouraging and support the claims.
*   The potential impact of this work is high, with applications spanning multiple domains.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The comparison to state-of-the-art methods could be more thorough.
*   The discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and kernel choice is limited.
*   The analysis of real-world datasets could be more in-depth.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a significant step forward in the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The novelty of the approach and the potential for impact are substantial. While some areas could be strengthened with further experimentation and analysis, these shortcomings are outweighed by the originality and significance of the work. I lean strongly towards acceptance.  This paper prioritizes a novel and bold idea over absolute methodological perfection, which is exactly what NIPS should reward. I would rate this as a strong accept, and recommend a relatively quick turnaround in revisions to address the relatively minor concerns outlined above.
