Summary
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user-item interactions with Hawkes processes and transfers knowledge across unobserved user–item pairs via low-rank structure. Each user–item pair is a self-exciting Hawkes process with nonnegative base intensity and self-excitation coefficient. Two matrices Λ0 (base intensities) and A (self-excitation magnitudes) are shared across all pairs and regularized with nuclear norms to induce low rank. The learning objective is convex: minimize the negative Hawkes log-likelihood plus nuclear-norm penalties under nonnegativity constraints. To solve it at scale, the authors introduce a penalty reformulation with auxiliary variables that decouples nonnegativity and nuclear norms, and develop an accelerated algorithm that combines proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity block) and conditional gradient/Frank-Wolfe (for the nuclear-norm block). They prove that, for sufficiently large penalty parameter ρ, the reformulation is equivalent to the original problem, and establish an O(1/t) convergence rate (with a term recovering O(1/t^2) when the nuclear-norm term vanishes). Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) show improved item ranking at time t and next-return-time prediction versus Poisson, survival/STiC, SVD, and Poisson-regression tensor factorization.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of temporal point processes for user behavior modeling and low-rank recommendation. It is closely related to multivariate Hawkes learning with low-rank or sparsity (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song), and time-aware recommendation via tensor factorization. The paper differs by (i) casting time-sensitive recommendation as a low-rank Hawkes matrix estimation problem, and (ii) proposing a convex composite optimization method that jointly handles nonnegativity and nuclear norms via a mixed proximal/Frank-Wolfe scheme. The authors cite relevant Hawkes and tensor literature, and articulate differences from survival modeling approaches that assume i.i.d. gaps and cannot recommend new items.

Quality
Strengths:
- Formulation: The negative log-likelihood for Hawkes with linear nonnegative parameters is convex; adding nuclear norms preserves convexity. The resulting model is principled and aligns with the goal of transferring to unobserved pairs.
- Optimization: The penalty reformulation that separates nonnegativity and nuclear norms is sensible; the algorithm uses efficient projections (simple thresholding) and low-cost LMOs (top singular vectors) with closed-form line search scaling. The convergence result is clear and matches known optimal rates for the constituent methods.
- Experiments: The synthetic setup demonstrates recovery, sample efficiency, and linear scaling. Real-data evaluation spans different domains; the time-change theorem plots give some validation of the Hawkes assumption; the method improves both time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction.

Weaknesses:
- Equivalence theorem and penalty parameter: The equivalence requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima, offering no constructive guidance. In practice ρ is tuned; there is no sensitivity analysis, schedule (e.g., continuation), or empirical evidence that suboptimal ρ does not harm solutions. This weakens the theoretical-to-practical bridge.
- Stability/branching ratio: Standard Hawkes modeling often enforces subcriticality (e.g., α times kernel mass < 1) to avoid explosive dynamics and ensure interpretability. The paper does not discuss or enforce stability constraints; although estimation on finite horizons may be fine, this omission should be acknowledged and, ideally, constraints incorporated.
- Evaluation breadth: For recommendation, only rank of the ground-truth item at test time is reported; top-k recall/precision or NDCG@k would offer a fuller view. Important baselines are missing (e.g., more recent time-aware matrix factorization, sequence models), though some may be contemporaneous. There are no wall-clock comparisons to ADMM or other solvers to substantiate the scalability claim beyond plots. Ablations (e.g., sensitivity to σ, λ/β/ρ, learned rank) are limited.
- Return-time estimation: The expectation is approximated by Ogata sampling without variance/computation discussion; for exponential kernels, efficient numerical quadrature or bounds might be possible and could be compared.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable, with intuitive motivation and clear linkage between modeling and tasks. The algorithm is presented succinctly with pseudocode. Some issues:
- Notational slips and typos in places (e.g., vector/matrix subscripts and concatenation notation); ensure consistent notation in Algorithms 1–3.
- Provide complexity per-iteration and per-pass over events; specify how gradients are computed efficiently over large event logs.
- More detail on hyperparameter tuning (search ranges, validation) and on the data split protocol would aid reproducibility.
- Briefly discuss numerical stability in evaluating the integral terms and the Lipschitz constant used for step sizes.

Originality
Moderately high. Low-rank structure in Hawkes has been studied, but applying it to user–item recommendation with a convex matrix formulation and a mixed proximal/Frank-Wolfe scheme that cleanly handles nonnegativity plus nuclear norms is a nice algorithmic contribution. The explicit unification of time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction under this model is also appealing.

Significance
The work addresses a relevant and difficult problem: making recommendations at the right time and predicting return times. The convexity and scalability are attractive for practitioners. The algorithmic idea could transfer to other convex problems with nonnegativity and nuclear norms. The empirical improvements are solid but would be more convincing with broader metrics and stronger baselines and with runtime comparisons.

Pros
- Principled, convex modeling of time-sensitive recommendation via Hawkes + low rank.
- Efficient algorithm with theoretical guarantees; practical to implement.
- Good empirical performance across tasks and datasets; synthetic scaling study.
- Framework extensible to context features and other kernels.

Cons
- Non-constructive penalty equivalence; no sensitivity/ablation on ρ.
- No stability constraints for Hawkes; potential for supercritical regimes unaddressed.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines could be stronger; missing runtime comparisons.
- Limited discussion of cold-start for unseen users/items beyond low-rank extrapolation.

Questions for authors
- How do you choose ρ in practice? Have you explored continuation schemes or provided bounds on a sufficient ρ?
- Do you enforce subcriticality (e.g., α times kernel mass < 1) or observe violations? What is the impact?
- What are per-iteration costs and wall-clock times vs ADMM or proximal-SVRG with partial SVDs?
- Sensitivity to kernel bandwidth σ and to λ/β; any automatic kernel learning tried?
- How does the method perform for entirely new users/items (cold-start) without side information?

Minor suggestions
- Report top-k metrics (Recall@k/NDCG@k) and calibration of return-time predictions.
- Include a small ablation on ρ and on rank (via nuclear norm strength).
- Release code or detailed implementation notes.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is technically sound, introduces a useful and principled approach with a solid optimization algorithm and theory, and shows promising empirical results. Addressing the noted evaluation and modeling gaps would further strengthen it. Confidence: high.
