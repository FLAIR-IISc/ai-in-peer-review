

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes. The authors establish an important connection between temporal point processes and low-rank matrix factorization, which has not been previously explored in recommendation systems. The key innovation lies in formulating the problem as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization, enabling efficient learning of temporal dynamics for both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

The paper makes a compelling case for why time-sensitive recommendation matters, arguing that traditional recommendation systems focus on predicting static user preferences rather than the temporal dynamics of user engagement. The authors demonstrate that modeling user activities as Hawkes processes (which capture self-exciting temporal patterns) and incorporating low-rank structure to handle large-scale data represents a significant departure from prior work, particularly Kapoor et al.'s survival analysis approach that cannot generalize to new items.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with a novel convex formulation of the problem and a carefully designed optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The convergence analysis is rigorous, showing an optimal O(1/t) rate, and the authors prove that their approach achieves the best of both worlds when handling both non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates strong performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II medical data). The ablation studies showing the algorithm's scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events are particularly convincing. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms baselines including Poisson processes, SVD, and tensor factorization methods on both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**
- Truly novel connection between point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Strong theoretical foundation with a well-motivated convex formulation and convergence analysis
- Practical scalability demonstrated on large datasets
- Clear application to multiple domains (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare)
- Theoretical guarantees that the algorithm achieves optimal convergence rates
- Well-structured paper that clearly articulates the problem, approach, and results

**Weaknesses:**
- The comparison with more recent work on time-sensitive recommendations could be more comprehensive (e.g., some newer methods using deep learning for temporal modeling)
- The real-world datasets, while diverse, are somewhat limited in scope (only three datasets)
- The paper doesn't fully explore the relationship between the latent rank and model performance in practice
- The implementation details of the optimization algorithm could be slightly more detailed for reproducibility

Despite these minor weaknesses, this paper represents a significant conceptual advance in recommendation systems. The idea of modeling time-sensitive recommendation through the lens of low-rank Hawkes processes is genuinely novel and addresses an important gap in the literature. The authors make a compelling argument that their approach is not just an incremental improvement but rather a fundamentally different way of thinking about recommendation systems that accounts for temporal dynamics.

The paper's strength lies in its bold conceptual contribution rather than methodological perfection. The convex formulation and optimization algorithm are well-justified and theoretically sound, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the approach's effectiveness. The work opens up new research directions for time-sensitive recommendation that could be applied to many real-world domains where timing is critical.

In my view, the novelty of this work significantly outweighs its minor weaknesses. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by establishing a new framework for time-sensitive recommendation that has not been explored before. The theoretical rigor, practical scalability, and strong empirical results make this a compelling contribution to NIPS.

This paper is an excellent example of the kind of bold, novel research that NIPS values. It takes a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem (recommendation systems) and creates a new conceptual framework that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The connection between point processes and low-rank models is particularly insightful and opens up new avenues for research at the intersection of temporal modeling and recommendation systems.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The conceptual contribution is significant and represents a meaningful advance beyond incremental improvements to existing methods. The paper clearly articulates the importance of the problem, presents a compelling solution, and demonstrates strong results across multiple domains.
