This paper tackles the problem of time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction using a Hawkes process framework combined with low-rank modeling. The core idea of connecting self-exciting point processes to low-rank models is interesting and potentially valuable. However, despite the novelty of the approach, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from superficial experimental validation to inconsistencies in the presentation and a lack of thorough engagement with closely related work.

**Summary:** The authors propose a low-rank Hawkes process model to capture recurrent user activities. They formulate the problem as a convex optimization task and develop an algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach for item recommendation and returning-time prediction.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for this particular problem is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Convergence:** The analysis of the proposed algorithm and the established convergence rate (O(1/t)) are commendable.
*   **Scalability:** The claim of scalability to millions of user-item pairs is promising.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Related Work Deficiency:** The authors claim that their work is significantly different from Kapoor et al. [12, 11] because their model can recommend *new* items. This is a weak distinction.  Kapoor et al.’s limitation isn't about inability to recommend new items *per se*, but the reliance on survival analysis which doesn't naturally extend to a recommendation task in the same manner as a Hawkes process. This subtle but important point is glossed over. Furthermore, the discussion of related work is lacking depth, especially regarding tensor factorization methods which are directly comparable, and recent advances in temporal point process modeling beyond standard Hawkes processes.  Several relevant papers are missing, including work on time-varying intensities and non-parametric Hawkes models.
*   **Experimental Rigor Concerns:** The experimental setup is not described with sufficient detail. The choice of hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, bandwidth) is mentioned as being tuned for best performance, but the specific search space or criteria are not reported. The experimental results, while demonstrating improvement over baselines, lack statistical significance testing. The error bars are missing, preventing a proper assessment of the consistency of the reported gains.
*   **Implementation Details & Reproducibility:** The paper lacks details crucial for reproducing the results. For example, the specific implementation of the proximal operator and conditional gradient steps is not provided. The code is not available, making verification impossible.
*   **Parameter Estimation Issues:** The use of nuclear norm regularization is a standard technique, but the choice of λ and β is arbitrary without a proper cross-validation scheme.  The theoretical justification for the chosen values is lacking.  Moreover, the discussion around determining ρ* (the threshold for the penalty term) is vague (“inefficient to directly estimate the exact value”).
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing is often verbose and lacks precision.  There are several instances where mathematical notation is introduced without sufficient explanation. For example, the definition of ψu,i lacks clarity, and the notation used in Algorithm 1 is difficult to follow.  Figure 1(a) is overly complex and doesn't contribute significantly to understanding the problem.
*    **Minor Technical Issues**: Equation (1) is missing a closing parenthesis. The discussion of Ogata's thinning algorithm in section 3.4 doesn't mention the acceptance/rejection step, which is critical for its correct implementation.  The explanation regarding why the nuclear norm serves as a convex surrogate for rank is perfunctory and doesn't cite relevant literature on matrix completion.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel approach.
*   The theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees are valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently thorough related work discussion.
*   Lack of statistical significance testing in experiments.
*   Insufficient experimental detail and lack of code for reproducibility.
*   Vague parameter tuning and lack of justification for regularization parameters.
*   Clarity issues in writing and presentation.
*   The perceived novelty doesn’t outweigh the methodological shortcomings.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially interesting idea, its execution falls short of NIPS standards. The numerous weaknesses, especially concerning experimental rigor, reproducibility, and clarity, significantly detract from the value of the contribution. The claims made regarding performance are not sufficiently supported by evidence. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.

**Recommendation:** **Reject.**  The authors should address the aforementioned weaknesses, including providing a more thorough literature review, conducting a more rigorous experimental evaluation with statistical significance testing, releasing the code for reproducibility, and improving the clarity of the presentation, before resubmitting.  A stronger justification for the theoretical choices (e.g., nuclear norm regularization) is also needed.
