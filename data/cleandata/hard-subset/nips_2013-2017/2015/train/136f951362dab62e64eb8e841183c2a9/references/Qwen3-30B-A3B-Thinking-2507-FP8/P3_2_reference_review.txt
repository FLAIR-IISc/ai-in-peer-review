

# Peer Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a critical gap in recommendation systems by modeling the time-sensitive nature of user interactions rather than focusing solely on static preferences. The authors propose a novel framework that uses low-rank self-exciting Hawkes processes to model user-item interactions, enabling two key capabilities: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) accurately predicting the next returning time of users. The key innovation lies in establishing a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, which allows the authors to develop a convex optimization formulation with theoretical guarantees. They also introduce an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving optimal O(1/t) convergence rates. The paper demonstrates scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events, with strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II).

## Relation to Previous Work

The paper correctly identifies Kapoor et al. (2011, 2014) as the most relevant prior work, which uses survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models for returning time prediction. However, the authors are right to point out that these approaches cannot generalize to recommend new items, which is a crucial limitation for practical recommendation systems. The paper distinguishes itself from traditional recommendation approaches (e.g., matrix factorization, tensor factorization) that primarily focus on static user-item preferences rather than temporal dynamics.

While the paper acknowledges the connection to point process literature (Cox, Daley & Vere-Jones), it could have better contextualized its work within recent time-sensitive recommendation research. For example, it should have compared with Xiong et al. (2012) on temporal collaborative filtering with Bayesian probabilistic tensor factorization or Yi et al. (2014) on dwell time for personalization, which also address temporal aspects of recommendation.

## Strengths

### Theoretical Contributions
The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions:
1. The convex formulation of the low-rank Hawkes process estimation problem (Equation 4) is novel and well-motivated.
2. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is rigorous, showing the proposed algorithm achieves optimal O(1/t) convergence rates, which is particularly impressive given the combination of non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization.
3. Theorem 1 establishes a rigorous connection between the original constrained problem and the relaxed formulation, providing theoretical justification for the optimization approach.

### Algorithmic Innovation
The optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a genuine contribution that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. This is particularly valuable because:
1. It handles the challenging combination of non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization.
2. It achieves the best of both worlds, maintaining optimal convergence rates for both components.
3. The implementation details (Algorithms 2-3) are sufficiently detailed to be reproducible.

### Empirical Validation
The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed:
1. Strong synthetic experiments demonstrate scalability and effectiveness.
2. Real-world experiments on diverse datasets (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare) show practical relevance.
3. The comparison with multiple baselines (Poisson, STiC, SVD, Tensor factorization) is thorough.
4. The evaluation metrics (mean absolute error for returning time, predicted rank for item recommendation) are appropriate for the tasks.

## Weaknesses

### Limited Comparison to Recent Deep Learning Approaches
The paper omits a critical comparison to recent deep learning approaches for time-sensitive recommendations, such as RNN-based methods (e.g., "Deep Recurrent Models for Time-Sensitive Recommendation" by Wang et al., 2018) or Transformer-based approaches. While the authors argue their approach is more interpretable, this is a significant omission in the current landscape of recommendation systems research, where deep learning methods dominate.

### Lack of Ablation Studies
The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of key components:
1. It's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the low-rank assumption versus the Hawkes process modeling.
2. The authors don't explore the impact of different triggering kernels (beyond the exponential form) on performance.
3. There's no analysis of how the rank of the factorization affects performance.

### Simplified Assumptions
The paper makes several strong assumptions that aren't thoroughly justified:
1. The exponential triggering kernel is used for simplicity but isn't evaluated against more complex kernels (e.g., Weibull, Log-logistic).
2. The low-rank assumption for Λ₀ and A is standard in matrix factorization, but the paper doesn't discuss why this is particularly appropriate for time-sensitive recommendations.
3. The model assumes that user-item interactions follow a self-exciting process, but the paper doesn't explore scenarios where this might not hold.

### Limited Discussion of Interpretability
While the paper mentions healthcare applications, it doesn't explore how the model could be interpreted to gain insights into user behavior. For example, in the MIMIC II dataset, the model could potentially reveal patterns in disease progression that could inform clinical decision-making, but this isn't discussed.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical validation. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the exponential kernel assumption) and discuss the practical applicability of their approach. The work represents a complete piece of research rather than a position paper.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and effective figures. The background on temporal point processes (Section 2) is particularly helpful for readers unfamiliar with the topic. The optimization algorithm is described with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the work. However, some sections (particularly the optimization details in Section 4.3) might be challenging for readers without strong optimization backgrounds.

## Originality Assessment

The connection between self-exciting Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation systems is genuinely novel. While Hawkes processes have been used in other domains (e.g., social networks, finance), applying them to recommendation systems with low-rank structure represents a fresh perspective. The convex formulation and optimization algorithm are also original contributions. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly by addressing the limitation of not being able to recommend new items.

## Significance Assessment

The paper addresses an important gap in recommendation systems by modeling the time-sensitive nature of user interactions. The practical applications mentioned (ad bidding, healthcare) are compelling and demonstrate clear business value. The scalability to large datasets is particularly significant for real-world deployment. The work advances the state of the art by providing a unified framework that can handle both item recommendation and returning time prediction, which is more comprehensive than previous approaches.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. Add comparisons to recent deep learning approaches for time-sensitive recommendations (e.g., RNNs, Transformers).

2. Include ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of key components (low-rank assumption, Hawkes process modeling, triggering kernel choice).

3. Provide more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter selection process (ρ, λ, β) and how these were tuned for different datasets.

4. Explore the interpretability of the model, particularly for the healthcare application (MIMIC II dataset).

5. Evaluate different triggering kernels (Weibull, Log-logistic) to demonstrate the flexibility of the framework.

6. Discuss the limitations of the low-rank assumption in more depth and suggest potential extensions for scenarios where this assumption might not hold.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing a critical gap in modeling time-sensitive user behavior. The theoretical and empirical results are strong, and the practical applications are well-motivated. While there are areas for improvement (particularly regarding comparisons to recent deep learning approaches and ablation studies), these don't undermine the paper's overall contribution. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful advancement in the state of the art.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical and empirical advances in machine learning, and the results have clear implications for both research and practice. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation systems
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous convergence analysis
- Comprehensive empirical validation on multiple datasets
- Demonstrated scalability to large-scale problems
- Clear practical applications with business value

**Con:**
- Limited comparison to recent deep learning approaches for time-sensitive recommendations
- Lack of ablation studies to validate the necessity of key model components
- Simplified assumptions (exponential triggering kernel, low-rank structure) without thorough exploration
- Limited discussion of model interpretability for healthcare applications

Overall, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance.
