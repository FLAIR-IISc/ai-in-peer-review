## Review of “Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities”

This paper proposes a low-rank Hawkes process model for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The authors aim to unify these two tasks within a single framework, leveraging the temporal dynamics of user-item interactions. While the core idea of combining Hawkes processes with low-rank modeling is interesting, and the paper demonstrates a significant effort in developing an optimization algorithm, a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, issues ultimately lead me to recommend rejection.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the limitations of traditional recommendation systems focusing on one-time preferences and highlights the importance of temporal dynamics. The approach builds upon recent work in temporal point processes, specifically Hawkes processes, and attempts to address scalability issues through low-rank approximations. The connection to related work, particularly Kapoor et al. [12, 11], is appropriately discussed, with a clear distinction made regarding the ability to recommend *new* items.  The motivation stemming from diverse applications (e-commerce, healthcare) is well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty (Moderate):** The combination of low-rank modeling with Hawkes processes is a reasonable and potentially valuable approach.
* **Technical Depth:** The development of an optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods demonstrates significant effort. The theoretical convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is a positive aspect.
* **Scalability:** The authors claim and demonstrate (in experiments) scalability to large datasets.
* **Unified Framework:** The attempt to address both recommendation and returning-time prediction within a single model is commendable.

**Weaknesses (Numerous & Significant):**

* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is often inconsistent (e.g., `t u,ij` vs. `tu,ij`). Definitions are sometimes vague or lack sufficient context. For instance, the meaning of "event" is assumed rather than explicitly defined in the context of the healthcare application. Figure 1(a) is visually cluttered and doesn’t effectively convey the intended information.
* **Minor Technical Flaws:** Several technical aspects are not fully justified or rigorously explained. The justification for using the nuclear norm as a rank proxy is weak; a discussion of alternative rank regularization techniques (e.g., trace norm with adjustments) is missing. The line-search procedure in Algorithm 3 (step (6)) lacks detailed explanation regarding the handling of constraints and potential convergence issues. The claim of “optimal” convergence rate (O(1/t)) needs stronger justification, especially considering the approximations involved.
* **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The parameter tuning process is vaguely described (“tuned to give the best performance”). What metrics were used for tuning? What search space was explored? The comparison with existing methods feels uneven. The reasons for choosing specific hyperparameter settings are not explained. The choice of exponential kernel is simply stated as a convenience, but lacks a justification.
* **Overstatement of Contributions:** The claim that the algorithm "blends" proximal and conditional gradient methods is somewhat misleading. It’s more accurate to describe it as an alternating application of these methods. The assertion that the model can "readily generalize" to incorporate additional contextual information is not sufficiently supported. This would likely require significant modifications to the proposed formulation.
* **Redundancy & Verbosity:** The paper is excessively verbose and contains redundant phrases. For example, the rationale behind using the Hawkes process is repeated multiple times.
* **Formatting & Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typos exist throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent spacing, missing punctuation, grammatical errors). These detract from the overall professionalism of the work.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The idea of combining low-rank modeling with Hawkes processes is novel and potentially useful.
* The authors have demonstrated a significant effort in developing a scalable optimization algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from numerous clarity and presentation issues.
* The technical details are not always rigorously explained or justified.
* The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail and rigor.
* The claims of novelty and significance are overstated.
* The pervasive minor flaws (formatting, typos) indicate a lack of attention to detail.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the interesting core idea, the numerous weaknesses and lack of polish significantly detract from the paper's overall quality. The paper feels incomplete and requires substantial revision to address the clarity, technical rigor, and experimental validation concerns.  The persistent minor flaws suggest a lack of meticulousness that, while perhaps not intentional, undermines confidence in the soundness of the presented work.  Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should carefully address the points raised in this review, significantly improve the clarity and rigor of their presentation, and provide a more compelling validation of their proposed approach before resubmission.
