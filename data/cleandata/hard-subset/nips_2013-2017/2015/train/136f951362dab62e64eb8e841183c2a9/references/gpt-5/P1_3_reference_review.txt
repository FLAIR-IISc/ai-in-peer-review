Summary
The paper tackles two coupled problems in time-sensitive recommendation: (1) ranking items for a user at a particular time, and (2) predicting the next returning time for a user–item pair. It models each user–item interaction as a univariate self-exciting Hawkes process with an exponential triggering kernel. The base intensity Λ0(u, i) captures long-term preference and the self-excitation coefficient A(u, i) (denoted αu,i) captures short-term recency effects. To enable generalization to unobserved user–item pairs and control complexity, the authors impose low-rank structure via nuclear norm regularization on Λ0 and A, yielding a convex estimation problem. They propose a new optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for non-negativity/projected steps) and conditional gradient/Frank-Wolfe (for nuclear norms) using an auxiliary-variable penalty formulation that decouples constraints from penalties. They provide a theorem stating the penalized and original constrained formulations coincide for sufficiently large penalty ρ, and a convergence bound of order O(1/t) for the composite algorithm. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) show improved ranking and return-time prediction compared to Poisson-process, SVD, tensor factorization, and a survival/HSMM baseline (STiC). A time-rescaling Q–Q diagnostic suggests Hawkes fits better than survival alternatives.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on temporal point processes and Hawkes processes (Hawkes ’71) and leverages low-rank modeling via nuclear norms. It relates to survival-based return-time models (Kapoor et al.) and to tensor/matrix factorization for temporal recommendation. On Hawkes learning, it is adjacent to Zhou et al. (AISTATS/ICML) and Du et al. (AISTATS/KDD), who learned networked or nonparametric/multivariate Hawkes; here the novelty is a convex low-rank formulation over user–item pairs and a scalable hybrid optimization scheme. The optimization approach is related to conditional gradient methods for norm-regularized problems (Harchaoui et al.; Lan), but the specific decoupling of non-negativity and nuclear norm with a provable rate is useful.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Jointly modeling time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction is important for many applications (music, e-commerce, EHR).
- Modeling choice: Hawkes processes naturally capture recency/self-excitation. The low-rank structure enables generalization to unobserved user–item pairs.
- Convex formulation: Using nuclear norms on Λ0 and A gives a convex objective; exponential kernels yield closed-form integrals for efficient likelihood computation.
- Optimization: The proximal–FW hybrid avoids full SVDs, uses only top singular vectors per iteration, and has a clear convergence guarantee. The equivalence theorem motivates the penalty approach.
- Scalability: Synthetic experiments up to ~100M events and linear scaling plots suggest practical viability.
- Empirical results: Consistent gains over strong baselines for both ranking and return-time tasks; time-rescaling diagnostics support the Hawkes assumption.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Penalization equivalence: Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima, so in practice it is tuned. Please clarify how sensitive performance is to ρ and whether suboptimal ρ can bias solutions; include a sensitivity plot and practical guidance.
- Missing baselines: Compare against (i) per-pair Hawkes without low-rank (to quantify the benefit of sharing), (ii) ADMM-based convex solver the paper itself cites (Yu et al. 2014) on medium-scale data, (iii) nonnegative matrix factorization variants (since parameters are nonnegative), and (iv) renewal processes (e.g., Weibull) or self-correcting processes for return-time.
- Cross-excitation: The model uses independent univariate Hawkes per user–item pair. In many settings, events on one item influence others (substitution/complementarity). Please discuss and, if possible, provide a small-scale experiment with mutually exciting terms or justify why cross-excitation is negligible in these domains.
- Stability/branching ratio: For exponential Hawkes, stationarity requires αu,i ∫ γ ≤ 1. The paper does not enforce or report this. Please report learned effective branching ratios and whether constraints are needed to avoid explosive intensities.
- Evaluation metrics: For recommendation, reporting only the rank of the true item is nonstandard. Please include Recall@k/Hit@k and NDCG@k. For return-time, also report negative log-likelihood or calibrated coverage of prediction intervals in addition to MAE.
- Prediction via sampling: Approximating E[t] with Ogata sampling can be expensive and noisy. Provide m (number of samples), runtime, and variance. Consider reporting the mode/median or numerical quadrature; for exponential Hawkes the conditional time distribution allows efficient inverse-transform sampling and numerical expectations.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters are “tuned for best performance.” Please specify search ranges, validation splits, and report wall-clock times, memory footprint, and per-iteration costs. Code release would materially aid impact.
- Clarity/notation: Some notational inconsistencies (α vs A(u,i)), missing spaces/superscripts, and algorithm boxes could be clearer. Algorithm 1’s proximal step is simply a projected gradient; state this explicitly. Define L, D1, D2 in the main text. Ensure consistent use of σ and provide how it is selected. Fix minor typos.

Quality
Overall technically sound with clear derivations for the exponential kernel case, a reasonable convex objective, and a principled optimization algorithm. The theory is appropriate but the practical equivalence condition (ρ) and stability issues merit more discussion. Empirical results are convincing but would be stronger with additional baselines and standardized metrics.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, but could benefit from cleanup of notation, explicit algorithmic details (step sizes, stopping criteria), and more complete experimental reporting. The time-rescaling analysis is a nice touch.

Originality
The combination of low-rank Hawkes over user–item pairs with a scalable proximal–FW algorithm and convergence analysis appears novel. Related Hawkes work focused on network inference or nonparametric kernels; related optimization work did not target this specific composite structure with non-negativity and nuclear norms.

Significance
Likely to be useful to practitioners working on temporally aware recommenders and to researchers in point process learning. The approach advances the state of the art and is extensible to include contexts and features.

Pros
- Relevant problem; principled Hawkes-based modeling with low-rank sharing
- Convex formulation with scalable hybrid optimization and rates
- Strong empirical gains and diagnostics; large-scale synthetic study

Cons
- Practical gap in equivalence condition; sensitivity to ρ unclear
- Missing key baselines (per-pair Hawkes, ADMM solver, NMF, renewal)
- No treatment of cross-excitation or stability constraints
- Evaluation metrics and reporting could be more standard and thorough

Questions for authors
1. How sensitive are results to ρ and σ? Can you provide guidance or an adaptive scheme?
2. Do learned αu,i satisfy stability? Did you constrain them?
3. What is the per-iteration cost and wall-clock time versus ADMM with partial SVDs?
4. How many samples m are used for return-time prediction? Is there a faster deterministic surrogate?
5. Can the method incorporate user/item features in Λ0 and A (e.g., via feature-embedded low-rank models)?

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a solid model and an efficient algorithm, and shows clear empirical benefits. Addressing the evaluation and baseline gaps, clarifying the penalty/stability issues, and improving reporting would make it stronger.
