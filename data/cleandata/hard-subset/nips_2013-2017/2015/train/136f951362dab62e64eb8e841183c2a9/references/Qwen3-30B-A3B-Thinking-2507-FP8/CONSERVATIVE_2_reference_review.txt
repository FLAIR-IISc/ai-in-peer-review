

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes using low-rank Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The authors model user-item interactions as self-exciting point processes with low-rank structure, developing a novel convex optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The paper claims their approach outperforms baselines on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear theoretical formulation of time-sensitive recommendation as a point process problem. The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is theoretically sound, and the convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides solid theoretical grounding for their optimization approach. The experimental section demonstrates the method's scalability to large datasets, which is valuable for real-world applications.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's validation and novelty claims. The paper lacks sufficient comparison with state-of-the-art time-sensitive recommendation methods. While they compare with Poisson processes, SVD, and Tensor factorization, they omit crucial baselines like recent work on temporal matrix factorization (e.g., Xiong et al., SDM 2012; Zhang et al., KDD 2014) and methods specifically designed for return-time prediction (e.g., Kapoor et al., KDD 2014). The comparison with STiC [11] is limited to returning-time prediction, but the paper fails to demonstrate how their approach improves upon this work for the full recommendation problem.

The paper claims "superb predictive performance" but provides insufficient statistical evidence to support this claim. The results in Figure 2(e-f) show marginal improvements over baselines, with no statistical significance testing. For instance, the MAE differences between their method and the best baseline are often less than 0.05 on the real datasets, which may not represent meaningful improvements in practical applications. The paper also doesn't adequately address the cold-start problem, a critical challenge in recommendation systems that would severely impact the practical utility of their approach.

The paper's most significant weakness is its insufficient validation of the core novelty claim. While the low-rank Hawkes process formulation is an interesting extension of prior work, the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how this specifically advances the state of the art beyond existing point process approaches. The connection to prior work on Hawkes processes for recommendation (e.g., Du et al., KDD 2015; Zhou et al., AISTATS 2016) is not sufficiently elaborated, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate what makes their low-rank formulation superior to these approaches.

The optimization algorithm is claimed to be novel, but the paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that this specific combination of proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods offers meaningful advantages over existing methods for this problem. The convergence rate analysis is theoretically sound, but the paper lacks empirical evidence showing that this algorithm significantly outperforms standard approaches in terms of training speed or solution quality.

The paper also makes strong claims about the practical value of their approach (e.g., "marketers need not blindly bid all time slots indiscriminately"), but fails to provide concrete evidence that their method would lead to tangible business value beyond the limited experimental results. The medical application example is mentioned but not thoroughly validated.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the claimed novelty and significance are overstated relative to the evidence provided. The most compelling aspect of the paper is the theoretical connection between point processes and low-rank models, but this contribution is not sufficiently validated against the most relevant baselines in the recommendation literature.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between temporal point processes and low-rank models, it falls short in providing sufficient evidence to support its claims of superior performance and practical significance. The paper would be significantly stronger with more comprehensive comparisons to relevant baselines, statistical validation of performance differences, and clearer articulation of how this approach advances the state of the art beyond existing work.

Strengths: Clear theoretical formulation, solid convergence analysis, good scalability demonstration.

Weaknesses: Insufficient comparison with relevant baselines, overstated performance claims, lack of statistical validation, inadequate discussion of limitations.

This paper would benefit from a more thorough validation against the most relevant state-of-the-art methods in time-sensitive recommendation, particularly those that directly address the returning-time prediction problem. Without this, the novelty and significance claims are difficult to substantiate.
