Summary
The paper tackles time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item dyad as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t)=λ0(u,i)+α(u,i)∑γ(t,tu,ij). To transfer knowledge across dyads, the base-intensity matrix Λ0 and self-excitation matrix A are constrained to be low-rank via nuclear norms with non-negativity constraints, yielding a convex estimation problem. The authors propose an alternative penalized formulation introducing auxiliary variables Z1,Z2 to decouple nuclear norms and non-negativity, and design an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for non-negativity) and conditional gradient (for nuclear norms), with a stated O(1/t) rate. They report favorable results on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) for item ranking over time and return-time MAE.

Relation to prior work
Hawkes processes for user behavior modeling and low-rank structure in multivariate Hawkes have been explored (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song, AISTATS’13; ICML’13); the paper differs by modeling independent self-exciting dyads with low-rank Λ0 and A for transfer. Time-aware factorization (tensor/temporal MF) and survival-analysis-based return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.) are used as baselines. The proximal–conditional gradient hybrid draws on composite convex optimization (e.g., Harchaoui & Juditsky; Lan).

Strengths
- A clean convex formulation for dyadic Hawkes parameter estimation with nuclear-norm regularization and non-negativity, enabling principled transfer to unobserved dyads.
- Simple intensity parameterization that makes the log-likelihood linear in parameters outside the log-term, leading to tractable optimization.
- A practical algorithmic idea to separate non-negativity and nuclear norms via auxiliary variables and to use projection for one block and LMO for the other.
- Reasonable evaluation protocol that includes prediction on entirely held-out user–item pairs, which tests generalization beyond observed dyads.
- Empirical results suggest gains over strong heuristics (Poisson popularity) and temporal factorization baselines.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Technical soundness
- Exactness of the penalized formulation (Theorem 1): For quadratic penalties, exact equivalence to the constrained problem at finite ρ is generally not guaranteed. The theorem states existence of ρ*>0 depending on unknown optima and gives an expression involving optimal quantities, which is unusable in practice and, more importantly, is atypical for quadratic penalty methods. Without a convincing proof sketch or specific regularity conditions, this exactness claim is doubtful; in practice the method becomes a heuristic requiring large ρ and may suffer from bias otherwise.
- Convergence analysis (Theorem 2): The rate depends on a Lipschitz constant L of ∇f(X). For negative log-likelihood with terms −∑log(wᵀφ), L can be unbounded unless λ(t)=wᵀφ is uniformly lower-bounded away from zero. The paper does not impose such bounds (e.g., wᵀφ≥ε>0 or upper bounds on parameters) nor proves restricted smoothness; thus the stated rate lacks validated assumptions.
- Stability of Hawkes: The model with exponential kernel requires α(u,i)∫γ<1 (i.e., α(u,i)σ<1) for stability. The paper neither enforces nor discusses this, risking ill-posed intensity and exploding likelihoods.
- LMO derivation: The “LMO with norm penalty” step effectively minimizes 〈P,Z〉+λ‖Z‖∗ by picking a rank-1 atom u vᵀ with a scalar α from a quadratic line search. This is not the standard Frank–Wolfe LMO (which minimizes a linear objective over a norm ball). The proposed step blends atom selection with scaling due to the penalty; a formal derivation and conditions under which this yields a valid decrease for the composite objective are missing.
- Notational inconsistencies obscure correctness: baseline intensity is denoted γ0 early and later Λ0; T denotes both time horizon and event history; minor but pervasive inconsistencies make verification hard.

Experimental rigor
- Hyperparameter tuning is underspecified. The text states that ρ, λ, β, bandwidth, and baseline ranks are “tuned to give the best performance,” but does not describe a validation protocol. Using test data for tuning would invalidate comparisons.
- Baseline configuration details are lacking: number/size of tensor time bins, regularization, and training details; STiC is evaluated in a way that seems unfavorable (averaging inter-event times for held-out pairs) and not necessarily reflective of best use.
- Metrics are nonstandard for recommendation: reporting the rank of the true item at the event time is acceptable but no k or standard metrics (MRR, Hit@k, NDCG) or confidence intervals are provided. No variance/SE bars or statistical tests.
- Scalability claims are anecdotal: no wall-clock times, hardware specs, memory footprint, or per-iteration complexity. Figure 2(d) claims linear scaling but lacks axes and units in the text.
- Return-time estimation by sampling is expensive and noisy; no discussion of sample sizes, variance, or alternatives. For exponential kernels, analytical or quasi-analytical expectations may be derivable or bounds provided.

Scope/modeling choices
- The model captures only self-excitation within a dyad. Many behaviors exhibit cross-excitation across items (e.g., artist-to-artist, disease comorbidities), which the paper motivates but does not model; proposed “extensions” are not implemented or evaluated.
- Single global kernel bandwidth vs. per-dyad variability is unclear for real data. Fixing σ globally can be restrictive.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “returningtime,” “under explored,” “Acknowledge,” inconsistent notation (γ0 vs Λ0), hats/accents on OPT, undefined symbols in algorithms (e.g., L, ηk, gradient blocks), and incomplete references (missing years/venues).
- Algorithms omit key details: computation of ∇f, backtracking or step-size strategy, stopping criteria, initialization, and memory considerations (e.g., accumulating atoms for Z).

Originality and significance
- The combination of low-rank nuclear-norm regularization with convex Hawkes parameter estimation for dyadic recommendation is interesting, but its novelty is incremental relative to prior low-rank/multivariate Hawkes work. The algorithmic blend is plausible but not convincingly justified theoretically. Practical impact is undermined by missing experimental rigor.

Questions for the authors
- Under what explicit assumptions is ∇f Lipschitz? Can you provide lower bounds on λ(t) during optimization?
- Can you enforce α(u,i)σ≤1−ε during training to ensure stability?
- What is the precise validation protocol for hyperparameter tuning?
- Please provide wall-clock runtime, memory usage, and per-iteration complexity, plus hardware details.
- Can you compare against a cross-exciting Hawkes baseline (even low-dimensional) to justify the self-excitation-only assumption?

Pros
- Convex formulation enabling transfer to unseen dyads.
- Practical algorithm that avoids full SVDs.
- Addresses a timely problem (time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction).
- Promising empirical results on multiple datasets.

Cons
- Questionable exactness of the penalized formulation and incomplete convergence assumptions.
- Insufficient experimental detail and baseline fairness; nonstandard metrics without statistical rigor.
- Stability and modeling limitations not addressed.
- Numerous presentation and notation issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is appealing, but the theoretical claims need firmer foundations and the empirical evaluation must be tightened. With clearer assumptions for convergence/stability, a rigorous validation protocol, stronger baselines/metrics, and improved clarity, this work could be competitive in a future submission.

Reviewer confidence: High.
