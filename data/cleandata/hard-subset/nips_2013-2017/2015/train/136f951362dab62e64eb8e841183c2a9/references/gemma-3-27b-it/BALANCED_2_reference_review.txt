This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction by formulating the problem as a low-rank Hawkes process. The core idea is to leverage the self-exciting nature of user activities to model temporal dependencies and utilize low-rank models to transfer knowledge between observed and unobserved user-item pairs. The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, demonstrating its scalability and predictive performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work recognizing the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that focus on static preferences. It acknowledges recent work using temporal point processes (e.g., Kapoor et al., [12, 11]) but differentiates itself by enabling recommendations for *new* items, a significant advantage. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel contribution, as is the specific optimization algorithm proposed. The authors appropriately reference relevant work in temporal point processes [4, 5, 6], collaborative filtering [16, 23, 15, 3, 25, 13, 21], and matrix/tensor factorization [24, 33].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a fresh perspective.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The convex formulation and convergence analysis of the optimization algorithm are strong points, demonstrating a solid theoretical understanding.  The convergence rate analysis is particularly commendable.
* **Scalability:** The authors address the practical challenge of scalability with a tailored optimization algorithm and demonstrate its performance on large datasets.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets provide compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The comparisons against relevant baselines are thorough.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the core concepts are explained clearly, with Figure 1(b) effectively illustrating the model setup.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to predicting user return times has practical implications for ad bidding and healthcare, increasing the significance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Parameterization:**  While the paper mentions the possibility of exploring different triggering kernel families, the focus remains on the exponential kernel. A more in-depth discussion of the impact of kernel choice and a comparison of different kernel options would strengthen the analysis.
* **Contextual Information Integration:** The paper notes the ability to incorporate contextual information, but the examples given (user profiles, item contents, location) are relatively high-level.  A more detailed description of *how* this integration is implemented and its impact on performance would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The tuning of parameters like ρ, λ, and β is crucial.  The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters and a justification for the chosen values.  A sensitivity analysis would be helpful.
* **Synthetic Data Generation:** The synthetic data generation process could be elaborated on. Specifically, details on how the ground truth low-rank matrices were created and the rationale behind the rank selection would be beneficial for understanding the experimental setup.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results show overall good performance, a deeper error analysis—identifying *which* types of recommendations/predictions are frequently incorrect and *why*—could offer valuable insights for future improvement.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
* The proposed method demonstrates strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The optimization algorithm is efficiently implemented and scalable to large datasets.
* The work has potential practical applications in areas like ad bidding and healthcare.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a detailed exploration of different kernel choices and hyperparameter sensitivity weakens the robustness of the findings.
* The contextual information integration remains somewhat superficial, lacking a detailed implementation and performance evaluation.
* While the experiments are comprehensive, a more in-depth error analysis could reveal further areas for improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and solid empirical validation.  While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this work represents a significant step forward in the field of time-sensitive recommendation and deserves to be accepted.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Conduct a more thorough investigation of different triggering kernel families and their impact on performance.
* Provide a more detailed description of how contextual information is integrated into the model and evaluate its performance.
* Perform a sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β) to assess the robustness of the results.
* Elaborate on the synthetic data generation process, including the rationale behind the chosen rank and the creation of the ground truth matrices.
* Include a more detailed error analysis to identify areas for improvement.
* Consider adding an ablation study to quantify the contribution of the low-rank component and the choice of the Hawkes process.
