Summary
This paper studies time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item interaction stream as a univariate Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i)∑j γ(t, tu,ij). To share statistical strength and enable generalization to unseen user–item pairs, the base intensity matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A are learned jointly under nuclear-norm (low-rank) regularization with nonnegativity constraints, yielding a convex maximum-likelihood estimation problem. The authors then introduce an alternative penalty formulation with auxiliary variables to decouple nuclear-norm regularization from nonnegativity, and propose an optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient (for the nonnegative block) and conditional gradient/LMO (for the nuclear-norm block), with an O(1/t) convergence bound. They apply the learned intensities to rank items at any time and to sample next-event times for return-time prediction. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improved ranking and time-prediction accuracy over Poisson baselines, survival/HMM models (STiC), SVD, and Poisson tensor factorization.

Relation to prior work: The use of Hawkes processes for temporal activity modeling is well established [10] and has been used for influence/network inference [8, 27, 30]. Low-rank regularization for high-dimensional parameter sharing is common in recommender systems and network Hawkes estimation (e.g., sparse/low-rank infectivity). Temporal recommenders based on factorization and Poisson tensor models are established [3, 22, 25]. Return-time prediction via survival/hazard models was studied in [11, 12]. The paper’s main conceptual link is combining self-exciting point processes with low-rank parameter sharing for recommender settings and providing a convex formulation with a scalable solver blending proximal and conditional gradients.

Quality
- Modeling: The negative log-likelihood for the Hawkes model with λu,i(t) affine in nonnegative parameters is convex; adding nuclear norms preserves convexity. The self-excitation is restricted to within the same user–item stream; cross-item or cross-user excitation is not modeled, though the authors discuss extensions. The approach naturally generalizes to unseen pairs via Λ0. One modeling caveat is stability/branching constraints: for exponential kernels, ensuring a finite intensity typically requires the kernel integral times A(u,i) < 1; this is not discussed or enforced.
- Optimization: The penalty formulation with auxiliary Z1, Z2 decouples nonnegativity from nuclear norm. Theorem 1 claims equivalence to the constrained problem for ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima, making the result non-operational; in practice ρ is tuned. The proposed block method uses a simple nonnegative projection for X1 and rank-1 LMO steps plus line search for X2; the convergence bound provides O(1/t) overall, with a refined O(1/t^2) term when the nuclear-norm component vanishes, recovering known rates for smooth proximal gradient, and O(1/t) when only the nuclear-norm block remains, consistent with Frank–Wolfe behavior. The LMO design for norm-regularized smooth objectives follows prior work on conditional gradients for norms. The analysis seems plausible; full proofs are deferred to the appendix.
- Experiments: Synthetic data demonstrate convergence and scaling. Real-data experiments include diverse domains and evaluate both ranking and return-time prediction, including a time-change diagnostic supporting Hawkes vs i.i.d. gap models. However, baseline coverage is limited: tensor Poisson models and SVD are included, but no comparison to other continuous-time recommendation models or multidimensional Hawkes with structured sharing. Some details are unclear: the synthetic data description appears inconsistent (1,024×1,204 pairs with 1,000 events each would exceed 100M events); computational resources and runtime breakdown are not reported; sampling-based return-time prediction may be costly at scale.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with a clear statement of the model, objective, and algorithmic blocks. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., vectorization of parameters, explicit definitions of ∇1/∇2 and L), and Algorithm 1 would benefit from clearer specification of the LMO for the regularized setting and complexity per iteration. The stability condition and practical guidance for ρ are missing. The experimental setup (evaluation metrics, top-k values, numbers in figures) could be more detailed and quantitative.

Originality
- Modeling contribution: Applying low-rank nuclear-norm sharing to Hawkes intensities for recommender/return-time tasks in a convex framework is a useful combination; prior Hawkes recommender formulations are less common.
- Algorithmic contribution: The “prox-meets-FW” scheme for a composite of nonnegativity and nuclear norm with an explicit convergence bound is of interest and appears novel in this specific setting, though related to existing conditional gradient methods for norm-regularized objectives.

Significance
The framework addresses two relevant tasks jointly and is potentially impactful for large-scale time-sensitive recommendation. If the scalability claims hold and extensions to context/cross-excitation are realized, this could influence practice. Current empirical validation is promising but would be stronger with broader baselines and runtime reporting.

Strengths
- Convex formulation enabling joint learning of base and excitation parameters with sharing across user–item pairs.
- Scalable algorithm with theoretical convergence guarantee and simple primitives (nonnegative projection, top singular vector).
- Clear linkage from intensity to ranking and return-time prediction; practical diagnostic via time-change test.
- Experiments across multiple domains show consistent gains.

Weaknesses
- Lack of discussion/enforcement of Hawkes stability (branching ratio) constraints.
- Theorem 1 relies on an unknown threshold ρ*; practical selection is by tuning only.
- Baselines do not include stronger continuous-time or Hawkes-based recommenders; no comparison to sparse/low-rank multidimensional Hawkes with cross-excitation.
- Inconsistent synthetic data description; limited reporting on computational cost and sampling overhead for return-time prediction.
- The paper understates survival analysis for recurrent events; there exist models beyond single terminal events.

Questions for authors
- How do you ensure or check stability (e.g., α times kernel integral < 1) per user–item Hawkes? Did you observe explosive behavior?
- What are the per-iteration and total runtimes, memory usage, and number of singular vector computations on the real datasets?
- How sensitive is performance to ρ? Can you provide a principled heuristic or continuation scheme?
- Have you evaluated cross-excitation between items (e.g., mutually exciting processes) with low-rank structure?
- Can return-time predictions be computed without sampling (e.g., via closed-form mean for exponential kernels or numerical quadrature)?

Suggestions
- Include stability constraints or a discussion on their impact, and report the fraction of learned parameters within the stable region.
- Add stronger baselines: multidimensional Hawkes with shared kernels/infectivity, and continuous-time factorization models.
- Clarify the synthetic data scale and provide runtime/complexity analysis and ablations on rank and kernel bandwidth.
- Expand the algorithmic section to detail the LMO under nuclear-norm regularization and provide implementation tips (e.g., power iterations, warm starts).
- Calibrate the discussion of survival analysis to acknowledge recurrent-event models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Convex, principled formulation with a scalable algorithm and provable convergence.
- Addresses an important and timely problem with dual tasks (ranking and return-time).
- Empirical gains on diverse real datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing stability considerations and limited baseline comparisons reduce confidence in robustness and generality.
- Some technical aspects (penalty equivalence, LMO design) would benefit from clearer, more operational guidance.
- Experimental reporting lacks quantitative depth and computational metrics.
