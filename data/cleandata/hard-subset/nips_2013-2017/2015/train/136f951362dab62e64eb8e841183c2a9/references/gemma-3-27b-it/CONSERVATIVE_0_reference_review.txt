This paper proposes a low-rank Hawkes process model for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The core idea is to leverage self-exciting point processes to model recurrent user activities, combined with low-rank factorization to generalize to unseen user-item pairs. While the problem setting is relevant and the motivation clear, the paper suffers from a lack of convincing novelty and thorough evaluation against well-established baselines, raising significant concerns about its contribution to NIPS.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem, pointing out limitations of traditional recommendation systems and the increasing importance of temporal dynamics. However, the connection drawn between the proposed approach and existing work feels weak. The authors claim their approach surpasses Kapoor et al. [12, 11] by generalizing to new items, yet this generalization capability is a standard feature of collaborative filtering methods, including matrix factorization, which is presented as a baseline but not thoroughly compared. The claim that survival analysis is unsuitable for repeated events is a simplification; time-varying coefficient survival models readily address such scenarios. 

The technical contribution—a convex formulation and optimization algorithm for low-rank Hawkes processes—is presented with a good level of detail. The derivation of the optimization problem (formulation 5) appears sound, and the algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods is a reasonable approach. However, the discussion of convergence (Theorem 2) feels largely disconnected from the practical considerations of applying this algorithm to large datasets. The complexity analysis is present but lacks concrete insights into the scalability benefits compared to simpler alternatives.

The experimental evaluation is a major weakness. While the authors evaluate on both synthetic and real datasets, the choice of baselines is questionable. The Poisson process serves as a basic sanity check, but more sophisticated temporal point process models, beyond Kapoor et al., are missing. The comparison with SVD and tensor factorization models feels insufficient, especially considering the recent advances in these areas. The statement that the proposed model “automatically adapts different contributions of each past event” feels like hand-waving without a clear explanation of how this adaptation manifests in the learned parameters. Furthermore, the presentation of results lacks sufficient statistical rigor; standard error bars or statistical significance tests are absent, making it difficult to assess the reliability of the reported improvements. The real dataset descriptions are brief, and details regarding preprocessing and parameter tuning are lacking. 

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. However, the writing style occasionally leans towards self-promotion, emphasizing the “superior” performance without providing sufficiently strong evidence. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and increasingly important problem.
*   Presents a mathematically sound framework for combining Hawkes processes and low-rank models.
*   Demonstrates scalability to large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty. The core idea of combining Hawkes processes with low-rank methods feels incremental.
*   Weak comparison with existing literature. The chosen baselines are either too simple or not representative of state-of-the-art techniques.
*   Insufficient experimental validation. Lack of statistical rigor and limited details regarding experimental setup.
*   The justification for the model's advantages (automatic adaptation of event contributions) lacks clarity. 
*   The algorithmic improvements (proximal/conditional gradient blend) lack clear justification of practical advantage over existing methods for low-rank matrix factorization.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am inclined to **reject** this paper. While the technical work is solid, the lack of significant novelty and the insufficient experimental validation do not meet the standards for NIPS. The paper feels like an initial exploration of an idea rather than a mature contribution to the field. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide a more thorough comparison with relevant baselines, and better articulate the practical advantages of their approach. A more compelling narrative regarding the model's interpretability and the insights it provides would also be beneficial.
