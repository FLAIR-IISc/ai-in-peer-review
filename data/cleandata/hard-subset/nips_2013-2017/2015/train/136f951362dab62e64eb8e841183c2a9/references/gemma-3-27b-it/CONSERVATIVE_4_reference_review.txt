This paper proposes a low-rank Hawkes process model for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction. The core idea is to combine self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix factorization to model recurrent user activities. While the problem setting is relevant – capturing temporal dynamics in recommendation is important – the proposed solution feels like a complex engineering effort built on established techniques rather than a truly novel contribution, and the presentation lacks the rigor expected at NIPS.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to unify recommendation and returning-time prediction within a single framework. The connection to Hawkes processes is well-motivated given the sequential nature of user interactions. However, the novelty of applying Hawkes processes in this context is limited; recent work (Kapoor et al., cited) already explores temporal dynamics in recommendation. The key claim of being able to recommend *new* items is incremental, as it hinges on the low-rank assumption – a common technique in collaborative filtering. 

My major concerns revolve around the methodological soundness and evaluation. The convex formulation and optimization algorithm, while mathematically presented, feel overly complex and the justification for the chosen proximal/conditional gradient blend isn't compelling. The theoretical convergence guarantees are appreciated but don't necessarily translate to practical advantages over simpler approaches. More importantly, the experimental validation is insufficient. The synthetic experiments are useful for demonstrating scalability but lack realistic complexity. The real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) are used, but the experimental setup is vaguely described (e.g., how are events discretized for tensor factorization?). The comparisons are with a mixed bag of baselines, some quite simple (Poisson process, SVD) and others more sophisticated (STiC, tensor factorization).  A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art recommendation algorithms (e.g., neural collaborative filtering models that incorporate temporal information) is crucial.  The claim of “superior performance” feels overstated without such a benchmark.  Furthermore, the ablation studies are missing – how much does the low-rank assumption *actually* contribute to the performance gains?

The paper also suffers from clarity issues. The notation is dense and the explanation of the optimization algorithm is difficult to follow. While detailed, the supplementary material would significantly benefit from detailed pseudocode. The motivation feels somewhat fragmented; the jump from healthcare applications to general recommendation seems weak and lacks concrete examples.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem – modeling temporal dynamics in recommendation.
* Provides a mathematically grounded approach, with convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm.
* Demonstrates scalability on large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty – the combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models is not a breakthrough.
* Insufficient experimental validation – lacks comparison to strong baselines and thorough ablation studies.
* Overly complex methodology – the optimization algorithm feels convoluted and lacks clear justification.
* Clarity issues – dense notation and difficult-to-follow explanations.
* The connection between different applications feels tenuous.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound, it lacks the originality and compelling empirical evidence required for NIPS. The complexity of the proposed solution doesn’t appear to translate into substantial improvements over existing methods, and the evaluation is not rigorous enough to support the strong claims made.  The authors should consider simplifying the model, performing more comprehensive experiments, and focusing on a more specific application domain to strengthen their contribution.  A more focused paper with fewer moving parts and a clearer narrative would be much more competitive.
