This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction by framing the problem as a low-rank Hawkes process. The core idea is to connect self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture the recurrent temporal dynamics of user behavior. The authors present a convex formulation of the problem and develop an optimization algorithm based on a blend of proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, guaranteeing an O(1/t) convergence rate. The paper demonstrates the scalability and effectiveness of their method on both synthetic and real-world datasets, outperforming several baseline approaches.

The paper addresses an important and increasingly relevant problem in recommendation systems – incorporating temporal dynamics beyond simple rating predictions. The motivation is well-articulated, pointing out the limitations of traditional methods and the value of understanding users’ evolving preferences and returning behavior. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a clever insight, allowing for generalization to unseen user-item pairs. The proposed algorithm appears to be well-designed, addressing challenges like non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization effectively. The experiments are comprehensive, including both synthetic data for controlled evaluation and real-world datasets from diverse domains (music, e-commerce, healthcare). The convergence analysis provides theoretical guarantees, which is a significant strength. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with the relevant background. The authors adequately cite related work, particularly highlighting the connection to Kapoor et al. [12, 11] and clearly differentiating their approach by focusing on recommendation of *new* items.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the algorithm’s convergence rate is established, the practical implications of the constant factor in O(1/t) are not discussed. How does this constant compare to other optimization methods in practice?  The experimental section, while thorough, could benefit from a more detailed ablation study.  For example, how much does the low-rank assumption contribute to the performance gains? What is the impact of the choice of the triggering kernel (specifically, the exponential form)? The comparisons to baseline methods are comprehensive, but a deeper analysis of *why* the proposed method outperforms them would be valuable.  For instance, is the improvement due to better capturing temporal dependencies, improved generalization, or a combination of both?  The discussion of parameter tuning (ρ, λ, β) is brief; detailing the sensitivity of the performance to these parameters would be helpful. The detailed explanation of the algorithm (especially in Section 4) is quite dense and relies heavily on mathematical notation. While technically sound, it could be made more accessible with a higher-level overview and perhaps some illustrative examples.  The synthetic data experiments, while useful, might benefit from exploring a wider range of low-rank structures and dataset sizes to assess the robustness of the method.  The claim of addressing a unified framework for different domains (introduction) isn’t fully realized. While the model is general, the experiments don’t deeply investigate domain-specific adaptations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach: The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel and promising direction for time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Theoretical guarantees: The convergence analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm.
*   Empirical results: The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Scalability: The algorithm scales well to large datasets, making it practical for real-world applications.
*   Clear writing: The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Algorithm complexity: The algorithm is complex and may be difficult to implement and tune.
*   Limited ablation study: A more detailed ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of different components.
*   Lack of detailed parameter sensitivity analysis: The impact of key parameters on performance is not fully explored.
*   Dense mathematical notation: The algorithm description could be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation systems. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical guarantees, and strong empirical results outweigh them.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the practical implications of the convergence rate and compare the constant factor to other methods.
2.  Conduct a more detailed ablation study to assess the contribution of the low-rank assumption and the choice of triggering kernel.
3.  Provide a more in-depth analysis of why the proposed method outperforms the baselines.
4.  Elaborate on the parameter tuning process and sensitivity analysis.
5.  Simplify the algorithm description with a higher-level overview and illustrative examples.
