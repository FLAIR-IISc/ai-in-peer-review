Summary
The paper tackles time-sensitive recommendation and user return-time prediction by modeling each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑γ(t, tj). To enable generalization across unobserved pairs, the authors impose low-rank structure on Λ0 and A via nuclear-norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem. They introduce a penalty-based reformulation that separates nonnegativity constraints from nuclear norms, and propose an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for trace norm) and proves an O(1/t) convergence rate. Experiments on synthetic data (∼100M events) and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) suggest improved ranking and return-time MAE over baselines (Poisson, SVD, tensor factorization; STiC for time prediction). The method can, in principle, incorporate additional context via the base intensity or tensor extensions.

Relation to prior work
The use of multivariate Hawkes processes to model recurrent events is well-established, and learning their parameters from data has a substantial literature (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS/ICML). Low-rank structure for high-dimensional intensity parameters and conditional gradient approaches for trace-norm regularization are also known. The paper’s contribution is to instantiate a convex, low-rank Hawkes model specifically for user–item processes, and to propose a practical algorithm that avoids full SVDs. The related work on time-aware recommendation via temporal/tensor factorization is discussed, as is survival analysis for return-time prediction. However, prior point-process-based recommenders and low-rank Hawkes/network learning are not fully engaged with.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: negative log-likelihood is convex in Λ0, A under nonnegativity; nuclear norms yield a tractable convex program.
- Algorithmic practicality: avoiding full SVD via conditional gradient is sensible; the penalty decomposition separates constraints and regularization.
- Theoretical guarantees: convergence bound and a (penalty) equivalence statement when ρ exceeds a threshold.
- Scalability claims: synthetic scale (100M events) and illustrations of linear scaling; closed-form terms for exponential kernels are used.
- Applications across domains: recommendation and return-time prediction on music, e-commerce, and clinical data; use of time-change theorem for a sanity check.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling limitations: The model only captures self-excitation at the per-pair level. It ignores inter-item or cross-user influences (mutual excitation) and cannot model inhibition (nonnegativity forces α ≥ 0). In many recommender settings, self-inhibition or cross-excitation (e.g., within categories) is important.
- Novelty relative to established work: Low-rank structure in multivariate Hawkes and conditional-gradient optimization for trace-norm penalties are not new. The combination here feels incremental; claims such as “first of its kind” and “best of both worlds” seem overstated given an overall O(1/t) rate.
- Penalty equivalence: Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* is problem-dependent and not computable; in practice ρ is tuned. This weakens the claim that the penalized problem solves the original constrained problem.
- Baselines: For recommendation, comparisons omit strong time-aware baselines (e.g., TimeSVD++, dynamic Poisson factorization with temporal components, factorization machines with temporal/context features). For return-time prediction, there are no comparisons to mutually exciting models or point-process baselines beyond Poisson and STiC. The tensor baselines depend heavily on binning choices; it is unclear if they were well-tuned.
- Evaluation design: Reporting only rank and MAE omits likelihood-/calibration-based metrics standard for point processes. The time-change QQ plots are shown for individual sequences rather than aggregated goodness-of-fit. For “held-out” pairs, the model effectively reduces to low-rank Λ0 (since no history), but it is not compared directly to a strong matrix completion baseline in that setting.
- Practical details missing: No wall-clock times/hardware, memory footprint, or per-iteration complexity; unclear how feature sums φu,ij are computed efficiently; no ablations (e.g., effect of kernel bandwidth, ρ, λ, β). Return-time prediction via Monte Carlo sampling (Ogata) may be expensive; there are known alternatives for exponential kernels (or at least cheap numerical quadrature).
- Identifiability and interpretability: Λ0 and A may be confounded on dense pairs; nonnegativity and nuclear norms help but the paper does not analyze identifiability or sensitivity.
- Clarity/notation: Some notation inconsistencies and typos; the algorithm is terse, and key steps (e.g., line-search derivation) are not fully explained; details relegated to the appendix are essential for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the kernel bandwidth σ and to the choice of exponential kernel? Have you tried learned/nonparametric kernels?
2. Can you report wall-clock times, top singular-vector costs per iteration, and memory usage on the largest runs?
3. How did you set the number of Monte Carlo samples for return-time prediction, and what is its runtime impact?
4. How does your method compare to a strong matrix completion baseline on the entirely held-out user–item pairs (where A plays no role)?
5. Can you include stronger temporal baselines (e.g., TimeSVD++, dynamic Poisson factorization) and a mutually-exciting point-process baseline?
6. Did you observe inhibitory effects in data, and how would the framework handle them?
7. How is ρ chosen in practice, and how sensitive is performance to it?

Minor issues
- Several reference entries are incomplete; some related works on low-rank Hawkes/point-process recommenders seem missing.
- Clarify whether φ and ψ are precomputed per pair and the complexity of doing so.
- Specify k for top-k recommendation and whether popularity priors bias results.

Assessment
- Quality: Moderate; mathematically sound core but several claims are weakly supported; experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines and comprehensive metrics.
- Clarity: Generally clear but missing crucial experimental and algorithmic details for reproducibility.
- Originality: Incremental; combination is reasonable but not particularly novel.
- Significance: Potentially useful, but empirical evidence against strong, established baselines is insufficient.

Recommendation: Lean reject. The paper presents a reasonable convex instantiation of a self-exciting recommendation model with a practical optimization scheme, but the modeling assumptions are restrictive, the novelty is limited relative to established literature, and the experimental validation does not meet the bar of rigor against strong baselines.

Confidence: High.
