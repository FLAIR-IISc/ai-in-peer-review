

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors claim to address the underexplored problem of predicting both the most relevant item at the right moment and the next returning time of users. They connect self-exciting point processes with low-rank models, develop a new optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and claim superior performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper's main strength is its clear exposition of the theoretical foundation of temporal point processes and Hawkes processes. The background section is well-written and provides a solid foundation for the proposed model. The authors also provide a theoretically grounded optimization algorithm with convergence guarantees, which is a positive aspect.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's novelty, experimental validation, and theoretical claims, which prevent me from recommending acceptance at NIPS.

First, the core idea of using Hawkes processes for recommendation is not novel. Du et al. (KDD'15) already proposed "Dirichlet-hawkes processes" for clustering continuous-time document streams, and Zhou et al. (AISTATS, ICML) have applied Hawkes processes to social networks and recommendation systems. The "low-rank" aspect is a standard technique in matrix factorization for recommendation systems (e.g., SVD, tensor factorization), and combining it with Hawkes processes does not constitute a significant methodological innovation. The paper fails to adequately differentiate itself from these prior works.

Second, the experimental validation is insufficient. The paper compares with Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and Tensor factorization as baselines, but these are not the most relevant state-of-the-art methods for time-sensitive recommendation. For instance, the authors don't compare with recent temporal recommendation methods like those by Xiong et al. (SDM 2012), Koren (KDD 2009), or Yi et al. (RecSys 2014) that specifically address temporal dynamics. The experiments on real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) are limited in scope and don't demonstrate scalability to truly large-scale settings as claimed. The synthetic data experiments are well-designed but don't provide sufficient evidence that the method works well in realistic recommendation scenarios.

Third, the theoretical claims are overstated. The paper claims their optimization algorithm "achieves the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate," but this is a combination of existing techniques (proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods), not a novel algorithm. The convergence analysis is technically sound but doesn't provide evidence that this combination yields significant practical advantages over existing methods for this specific problem. The claim that their method "can be readily generalized to incorporate other contextual information" is unsubstantiated in the experiments.

Fourth, the paper's main claim about the superiority of their approach over existing methods is not adequately supported. In Figure 2(e-f), the paper shows that their method outperforms baselines, but the margin of improvement is modest (e.g., 5-10% better on synthetic data). The paper doesn't demonstrate that this small improvement is meaningful in real-world recommendation systems where small gains can be difficult to detect. The paper also doesn't address whether the computational complexity of their method is acceptable for large-scale recommendation systems, despite claiming "scalability to millions of user-item pairs."

Finally, the paper's relation to the most relevant prior work is inadequately discussed. The related work section cites Kapoor et al. [12, 11] but doesn't properly address how their approach differs from these works. The paper claims Kapoor's method "cannot generalize to recommend any new item in future time," but doesn't provide evidence for this claim or explain why their approach can do so while Kapoor's cannot.

While the paper is technically sound in its exposition of the underlying theory and provides a well-written optimization algorithm, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or significant practical advantages over existing methods. The proposed approach appears to be a straightforward combination of established techniques without substantial evidence of superior performance or methodological innovation. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of "superb predictive performance" and "scalability to millions of user-item pairs."

I believe this paper would be better suited for a conference focused on applied machine learning rather than NIPS, where the bar for methodological innovation and rigorous validation is higher. The paper represents a modest incremental contribution to the field rather than a significant advancement. The claims about novelty and performance are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented.

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from substantially stronger experimental validation against more relevant baselines, clearer differentiation from prior work, and more compelling evidence of the practical advantages of their approach.
