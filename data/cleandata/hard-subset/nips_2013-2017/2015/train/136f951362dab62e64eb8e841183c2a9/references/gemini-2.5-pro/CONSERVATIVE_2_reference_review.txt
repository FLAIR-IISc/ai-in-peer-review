**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to predict not only *what* a user might be interested in, but also *when*. The authors propose a model based on the combination of self-exciting Hawkes point processes and low-rank matrix factorization. For each user-item pair, a Hawkes process models the sequence of interactions, with a baseline intensity capturing long-term preference and a self-exciting component capturing short-term dependencies on past events. To handle data sparsity and enable collaborative filtering, the matrices of baseline intensities and self-exciting coefficients across all users and items are assumed to be low-rank. The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem involving nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. A novel hybrid optimization algorithm, blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, is proposed to solve this problem efficiently, with an O(1/t) convergence rate guarantee. Experiments on synthetic and three real-world datasets show that the proposed method outperforms several baselines, including Poisson processes, SVD, and tensor factorization, on item ranking and returning-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The proposed model is a principled and logical combination of established theories—point processes for temporal events and low-rank models for collaborative filtering. The derivation of the log-likelihood and the convex optimization objective is clear. The proposed optimization algorithm is non-trivial and is accompanied by a theoretical convergence analysis (Theorem 2). The experimental methodology is generally good, utilizing multiple real-world datasets and evaluating two distinct and relevant tasks. The analysis of model fit using the time-change theorem (Figure 3) is a nice touch that adds confidence in the modeling assumptions over simpler alternatives.

However, the empirical evaluation, while extensive in some ways, has a critical weakness: the choice of baselines. The paper fails to compare against modern deep learning-based sequential models, particularly Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) with LSTM or GRU cells. Such models are a standard and often state-of-the-art approach for modeling user event sequences and can be adapted to predict both the next item and the time to the next event. Without this comparison, the claims of "superb predictive performance" are not fully substantiated against the current state of the art.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation for time-sensitive recommendation is compelling, and the background on temporal point processes is helpful. The model is introduced in a step-by-step manner, which makes the formulation easy to follow. The description of the algorithm and its subroutines is also clear. The figures effectively communicate the results of the experiments.

**Originality:**
The core components of the model—Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix factorization—are well-established. Their combination for modeling user activity is a natural idea, and similar approaches have been explored before, for instance, in the context of learning influence networks (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS 2015 [29]). The paper could do a better job of clearly delineating its contributions from this closely related work. The primary novelty appears to lie in the specific convex formulation for recommendation (Eq. 4) and, more significantly, the hybrid proximal-conditional gradient algorithm developed to solve it. This algorithm for non-negative matrix rank minimization may be of independent interest.

**Significance:**
The problem of modeling temporal user dynamics is of high significance for practitioners and researchers in recommender systems. The proposed method offers an elegant, interpretable framework that captures both static preferences and dynamic, self-exciting user behavior. The model's scalability, demonstrated in Figure 2(d), is a significant practical advantage. If its performance holds up against stronger baselines, this work could be an important contribution. However, as it stands, the significance of the empirical results is diminished by the incomplete set of competitor methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles an important and relevant problem in recommender systems, moving beyond static models to capture temporal user dynamics.
2.  The proposed model is principled, providing an interpretable fusion of point process theory and collaborative filtering.
3.  A novel and scalable optimization algorithm is presented with theoretical convergence guarantees, which could be of broader interest.
4.  The empirical evaluation is conducted on multiple datasets and demonstrates strong performance against the chosen baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Crucial Missing Baselines:** The experimental evaluation lacks a comparison to state-of-the-art sequential models like RNNs (LSTMs/GRUs). These are extremely strong and standard baselines for user sequence modeling, and their omission makes it difficult to assess the true performance of the proposed method.
2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The conceptual novelty of combining Hawkes processes with low-rank assumptions is somewhat limited given prior work (e.g., [29]). The paper needs to better articulate its unique contributions in this context.
3.  **Practicality of Optimization:** The proposed optimization scheme introduces a new formulation (Eq. 5) that is equivalent to the original problem only when a penalty parameter `ρ` is sufficiently large. In practice, `ρ` becomes another hyperparameter that must be tuned, and the paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the model to its choice.

In summary, this is a solid paper with a sound methodology and a potentially useful contribution. However, the lack of comparison to standard and powerful deep learning baselines is a major flaw that undermines the central claims of superior performance. While the optimization work is interesting, for a venue like NeurIPS, a more rigorous and complete empirical validation is expected. I would be willing to reconsider my evaluation if the authors could provide results against a strong RNN-based competitor.
