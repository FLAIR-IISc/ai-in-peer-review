## NIPS Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, framing the problem through the lens of low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of temporal point processes and optimization techniques. While the convergence analysis and experimental validation are comprehensive, the practical gains over existing, simpler baselines could be more substantial to warrant strong acceptance. I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions, contingent on a clearer articulation of practical benefits and a more compelling demonstration of scalability beyond synthetic data.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the crucial problem of modeling time-varying user preferences, moving beyond static recommendation systems. It proposes a novel convex formulation linking self-exciting point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) to low-rank models. This allows for knowledge transfer between user-item pairs and efficient parameter estimation via a proximal gradient and conditional gradient hybrid algorithm with provable convergence guarantees. 

The authors correctly position their work concerning related research. They highlight the limitations of survival analysis (Kapoor et al., 2014, 2015) in generalizing to new items and its assumptions of independent inter-event times.  They also acknowledge work on tensor factorization for recommendation (e.g., Karatzoglou et al., 2010) but emphasize their focus on temporal dynamics. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models, however, feels less explicitly tied to prior work – a more in-depth discussion of existing applications of low-rank approximations within point process modeling would be beneficial.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for this application is a significant contribution.  The theoretical connection is well-motivated and provides a powerful framework.
* **Technical Soundness:** The convex formulation is well-defined, and the proposed optimization algorithm is rigorously analyzed with an optimal convergence rate (O(1/t)). The derivation and proof sketches appear sound.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate their method on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). The inclusion of diverse datasets strengthens the evaluation.
* **Scalability:** The algorithm's claimed scalability to millions of user-item pairs is a strong point, although the experiments focus on relatively modest dataset sizes for real-world scenarios.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction for various applications (marketing, healthcare).
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The use of well-established point process theory and optimization techniques adds credibility to the approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Gains:** While the paper demonstrates statistically significant improvements over baselines, the magnitude of these improvements in the real-world datasets (Figures 3e-f) appears modest, particularly for the item recommendation task.  A clearer demonstration of how this translates to tangible user experience improvements (e.g., increased click-through rate, conversion rate) is needed.
* **Baseline Comparison:** The baselines, while standard, could be more competitive.  More recent and sophisticated recommendation algorithms (e.g., deep learning-based approaches incorporating temporal features) should be included for a more thorough comparison.  The reliance on STiC feels dated given the rapid advancements in this field.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, bandwidth, rank).  A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the performance to these parameters and a justification for the chosen values would be helpful.
* **Scalability Demonstration:**  While the authors claim scalability, the experimental results don't fully showcase this.  Running the algorithm on much larger datasets with millions of users and items would further validate its practicality.
* **Kernel Choice Justification:** The exponential kernel is used throughout the experiments. While a common choice, a discussion of alternative kernels and their potential impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Real-World Dataset Size:** The real-world datasets, while relevant, feel relatively small. Demonstrating performance on a significantly larger-scale dataset, like those used in industry (e.g., Netflix, Amazon) would be highly valuable.



**4. Arguments Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel theoretical framework combining Hawkes processes and low-rank models.
* Strong theoretical guarantees for the optimization algorithm.
* Thorough experimental evaluation on multiple datasets.
* Clear motivation and potential applications.

**Con:**

* Modest practical gains over existing baselines.
* Limited demonstration of scalability to truly large datasets.
* Relatively weak comparison against state-of-the-art recommendation algorithms.
* The impact of hyperparameter tuning isn't thoroughly discussed.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide more details on the practical interpretation of the performance improvements observed in the real-world experiments?  How do these improvements translate to user experience metrics (e.g., click-through rate, conversion rate)?
* What is the computational cost (e.g., training time, prediction time) of your algorithm compared to the baselines on the larger datasets?
*  How sensitive is the performance of your algorithm to the choice of hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, bandwidth, rank)?
*  Have you considered incorporating more recent and sophisticated recommendation algorithms into your baseline comparison?
* Could you elaborate on the potential benefits of exploring alternative triggering kernels beyond the exponential kernel?




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept** with major revisions. The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach. However, the authors need to strengthen the demonstration of practical benefits, address the limitations in the baseline comparison, and provide a more compelling validation of scalability.  Addressing these points will significantly enhance the paper's impact and justify its inclusion in NIPS.
