

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors connect self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to address two related problems: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting users' next returning time. The paper presents a convex formulation of the problem, develops a new optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrates scalability to large datasets with superior predictive performance.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important gap in recommendation systems by focusing on time-sensitive recommendations rather than just one-time preferences. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is novel and potentially valuable for both recommendation and temporal point process modeling. The optimization algorithm achieves an optimal convergence rate, and the experimental results on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate strong performance. The paper also provides a comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines across multiple datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Notation and Formatting Issues**: The paper contains numerous minor but significant technical inconsistencies in mathematical notation that undermine its precision. Equation (1) incorrectly writes the integral as "∫ T λ(τ) dτ" instead of "∫_{t_n}^T λ(τ) dτ". The nuclear norm constraints are written as "‖Λ0‖∗ 6 λ′" (with "6" instead of "≤"). Equation (3) uses inconsistent notation like "w>u,i" instead of "w_{u,i}^T". Equation (4) incorrectly writes "− 1 |O|" instead of "− (1/|O|)".

2. **Mathematical Inconsistencies**: In section 3.1, the equation λ(t) = γ0 + α ∑ ti∈T γ(t, ti) uses "ti∈T" where T is defined as "the list of event time {t1, t2, . . . , tn}" rather than a set, creating ambiguity. The paper states that "the intensity function λu,i(t) indicates the tendency that user u will consume item i at time t" for item recommendation, but λu,i(t) is a rate (intensity), not a cumulative score for ranking, which would require integration over a time interval.

3. **Algorithmic Description**: Algorithm 3 contains unclear notation like "(u1, v1), (u2, v2) top singular vector pairs" without specifying how to handle multiple singular values. The paper claims "the linear minimization for our situation requires only" but doesn't specify how the top singular vectors are computed.

4. **Experimental Details**: The MIMIC II medical dataset evaluation lacks critical details about ethical considerations, data anonymization, and specific challenges related to medical time series. The paper mentions "the unit time for both dataset is hour" for last.fm and tmall.com but doesn't clarify the unit for MIMIC II (which is stated as "week" later).

5. **Theoretical Claims**: Theorem 1 states "the optimal value ÔPT of the problem 5 coincides with the optimal value OPT in the problem 4" but doesn't provide sufficient context for the condition ρ > ρ*. The proof is relegated to the appendix without a clear indication of its length or complexity.

6. **Formatting and Typos**: The reference list contains an extra comma after "Springer" in the first entry. Section 3.2 contains a typo: "matrixA" should be "matrix A". The paper uses "O" for the set of all event sequences, which is confusing as "O" typically denotes order or optimal.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a novel framework with promising results, the numerous technical inconsistencies and formatting issues significantly undermine its quality as a scientific contribution. These minor flaws collectively indicate a lack of careful proofreading and attention to detail that is expected in NIPS papers. The paper's core contribution is interesting, but the technical presentation contains enough errors to raise concerns about the rigor of the work.

The paper's main contribution is a convex formulation and optimization algorithm for time-sensitive recommendation, but the presentation errors make it difficult to verify the correctness of the mathematical formulations. In particular, the inconsistent notation in the equations and the unclear description of the optimization algorithm detract from the paper's scientific merit.

The authors should address all the mathematical notation issues, clarify the experimental details (especially for the medical dataset), and ensure consistent formatting throughout the paper before it can be considered for publication at NIPS. Without these corrections, the paper's technical contributions cannot be properly evaluated.

Given the numerous minor but significant technical errors that affect the paper's credibility, I recommend rejection. While the problem addressed is important and the approach shows promise, the presentation errors are too severe to overlook in a NIPS paper. The authors should focus on improving the precision and clarity of their mathematical formulations before resubmitting.
