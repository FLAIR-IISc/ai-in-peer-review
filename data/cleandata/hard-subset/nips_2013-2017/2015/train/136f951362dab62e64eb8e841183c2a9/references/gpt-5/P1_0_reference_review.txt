Summary
The paper addresses time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling recurrent user-item activities as Hawkes point processes with history-dependent intensities. For each user-item pair (u, i), the intensity is λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), with a nonnegative baseline Λ0 and self-excitation coefficient A. To transfer knowledge to unobserved pairs and reduce parameters, the authors assume Λ0 and A are low-rank and estimate them via a convex program with nuclear-norm penalties and nonnegativity constraints. They introduce an alternative penalized formulation with auxiliary variables that decouples nuclear-norm regularization from nonnegativity and propose an optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) and conditional gradient (for nuclear norms). They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate in objective value for the composite problem, recovering O(1/t^2) when only the smooth/nonnegative part remains and O(1/t) when only nuclear-norm regularization remains. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II) show better item ranking and return-time MAE than Poisson, survival/HMM (STiC), matrix/tensor factorization baselines; they also demonstrate linear scaling.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects survival analysis-based return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.) and temporal factorization methods with continuous-time point processes (Hawkes). It is related to learning Hawkes parameters and influence networks (Du et al.; Zhou, Zha, Song), but differs by modeling per user-item pair intensities with low-rank sharing and by casting estimation as a convex nuclear-norm-regularized problem under nonnegativity. On the optimization side, it builds on proximal and conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) methods for composite objectives; the decoupling with auxiliary variables and the particular accelerated scheme are close to recent conditional gradient for norm-regularized smooth objectives.

Strengths
- Clear, unified continuous-time formulation for recommendation and return-time prediction, leveraging Hawkes to capture short- and long-term effects.
- Convex low-rank estimation across user-item pairs with nonnegativity constraints; practical and principled.
- An efficient hybrid optimization method with a nontrivial analysis showing optimal rates in the limiting cases; uses only top singular vectors in the LMO, enabling scalability.
- Solid experimental protocol with challenging “entirely held-out” user-item sequences; improvements over strong baselines (Poisson, tensor Poisson regression).
- Good diagnostic use of the time-change theorem to validate Hawkes vs. survival assumptions on real sequences.
- Demonstrated scaling to 100M events and millions of pairs on synthetic data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Stability/identifiability: For Hawkes with exponential kernel, finiteness typically requires A(u,i) ∫ γ < 1 (branching ratio). The paper enforces nonnegativity but not explicit stability; how is explosion avoided in training? Please discuss whether you constrain A or observe implicit regularization keeping Aσ < 1.
- Equivalence theorem and penalty parameter: Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice ρ is tuned; this weakens the “equivalence” claim for (4). Provide guidance on selecting ρ, and whether you observe sensitivity.
- Modeling assumption: The model treats each user-item pair as an independent Hawkes process; there is no cross-excitation across items or categories. This is a limitation in domains with substitution/complementarity. Please discuss practical impact and whether a low-rank mutually-exciting extension is feasible.
- Baselines and fairness: Discrete-time tensor methods are strong, but their continuous-time disadvantage is known. Consider including a continuous-time Hawkes baseline with factorized parameters (nonconvex MF) or learned kernels (e.g., nonparametric Hawkes), to isolate the benefit of (i) Hawkes vs (ii) convex low-rank estimation. For return-time, MC sampling via Ogata introduces variance—how many samples m were used?
- Evaluation metrics: Item recommendation is evaluated by rank of the ground-truth item. Precision@k/Recall@k or time-aware metrics (e.g., time-weighted MAP) would give a fuller picture. Calibration of return-time predictions (e.g., CRPS or coverage of prediction intervals) would be informative.
- Clarity/notation: Some notational inconsistencies (e.g., φ, ψ dimensions in Eq. 3; missing details for γ parameter σ; definitions of D1, D2 and L; minor typos) impede reproducibility. Complexity per iteration and memory footprint should be quantified explicitly.
- Extensions are only sketched (context features, tensors); none are empirically evaluated.

Quality
- Generally technically sound. The convexity of (4) follows from log-likelihood concavity in linear intensities and convex nuclear norms; however, stability constraints are not discussed. Optimization analysis is careful and matches known rates in special cases. Experiments are well run, but more ablations/sensitivity (ρ, λ, β, rank) and stronger continuous-time baselines would bolster claims.

Clarity
- The paper is readable and well-organized, but the notation around Eq. 3–6 could be tightened. Add a subsection on computational complexity, explicitly state the number of samples for return-time prediction, provide pseudocode with all hyperparameters, and clarify data split details. Ensure figures are self-contained.

Originality
- Novel combination: convex low-rank Hawkes with nonnegativity and a hybrid proximal/Frank-Wolfe algorithm achieving optimal rates in limiting cases. While related to prior Hawkes network learning and conditional gradient literature, the specific formulation and algorithmic blend appear original.

Significance
- The problem is important for time-sensitive recommendation and healthcare. The method is likely to be useful to practitioners needing continuous-time models with scalability and cold-start generalization across user-item pairs. The optimization technique may be of independent interest for other nonnegative low-rank problems.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with a principled continuous-time model.
- Convex low-rank estimation and an efficient algorithm with convergence guarantees.
- Strong empirical results and scalability.
- Useful diagnostic validation of modeling assumptions.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of explicit stability constraints and discussion.
- Equivalence theorem hinges on a practically uncheckable ρ; limited guidance on tuning.
- Limited continuous-time baselines; no ablations on key hyperparameters.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps.

Suggestions
- Add stability constraints (e.g., Aσ ≤ 1 − ε) or empirical checks; report fraction of pairs violating stability.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for ρ, λ, β and sample size m for return-time estimates.
- Include a Hawkes MF baseline and/or nonparametric kernel baseline.
- Report per-iteration complexity and memory; release code if possible.
- Expand evaluation metrics (precision@k, recall@k, time-weighted metrics; calibration for return-time).

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a solid, well-motivated contribution at the intersection of recommendation and point processes, with promising empirical results and a useful optimization technique. Addressing the stability and baseline concerns would further strengthen it. Confidence: high.
