## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, framing the problem within a Hawkes process and leveraging low-rank matrix factorization for scalability. The paper is well-written and addresses a relevant and increasingly important problem in recommendation systems. While the experimental results are strong, the presentation could benefit from clearer articulation of the improvements over existing methods in the context of practical applications. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of dynamic user preferences, arguing that traditional recommendation systems focused on static ratings are insufficient. It proposes modeling user activities as a Hawkes process, enabling the prediction of both the next relevant item and the time until the user’s next interaction. A key contribution is the use of low-rank approximations to make this approach scalable to large datasets. The paper appropriately positions itself against existing work, particularly highlighting the limitations of Kapoor et al.’s survival analysis approach (inability to recommend new items).  The connection drawn between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a central novelty.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix factorization for time-sensitive recommendation is a novel and promising approach. Leveraging the self-exciting nature of Hawkes processes to model user behavior is a smart choice.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed convex formulation and optimization algorithm (blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient) appear well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The convergence analysis is a significant strength.
* **Scalability:** The low-rank approximation addresses a key challenge in applying Hawkes processes to large-scale datasets. The experimental results demonstrate that the algorithm scales effectively.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, consistently outperforming baseline methods. The comparison to Tensor Factorization methods is particularly informative.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details.  The inclusion of Figure 1(a) to illustrate the problem motivation is helpful.
* **Generalizability:** The authors explicitly note the potential for incorporating contextual information, suggesting a path for extending the model’s applicability.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Significance:** While the experiments demonstrate statistically significant improvements, the practical *magnitude* of these improvements isn't always clearly articulated.  For example, how much does a more accurate returning-time prediction translate to cost savings in ad bidding, or improved user engagement?  Providing concrete examples or a more focused discussion on the implications of the results would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning parameters (ρ, λ, β, bandwidth, rank) but doesn’t detail the sensitivity of the results to these choices. A brief analysis of this sensitivity could be beneficial.
* **Algorithm Complexity:** While the authors establish a convergence rate, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm (beyond the scaling claim) would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Kernel Choice:** The paper primarily focuses on the exponential kernel. A brief discussion of the rationale for this choice, and potential benefits/drawbacks of alternative kernels, would be valuable.



**4. Evaluation of Criteria:**

* **Quality (Strong):** The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical justification and strong experimental support. The algorithm is well-designed, and the convergence analysis is robust.
* **Clarity (Good):** The writing is clear and the concepts are explained adequately. Some sections could benefit from a slightly more intuitive explanation for a broader audience.
* **Originality (Good):** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models is novel, and the convex formulation and optimization algorithm are original contributions.
* **Significance (Good):** The problem addressed is important, and the results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods. Further emphasis on the practical implications of these improvements would elevate the significance.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Strong experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Scalable algorithm.
* Rigorous theoretical analysis (convergence proof).

**Con:**

* Lack of concrete quantification of practical benefits.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Could benefit from more detailed analysis of algorithm complexity.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors:

*  Expand the discussion of the practical implications of their results, providing concrete examples of how the improved accuracy translates to real-world benefits.
*  Briefly address the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters.
*  Consider adding a more detailed complexity analysis of the algorithm.
