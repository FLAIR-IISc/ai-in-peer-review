

# Review: "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary

This paper addresses the important but underexplored problem of time-sensitive recommendation, where the goal is to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment and predict when users will return to services. The authors propose modeling user activities using low-rank Hawkes processes, establishing a novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank matrix models. They develop an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving optimal convergence rates. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets across two tasks: item recommendation and returning-time prediction.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a well-justified convex formulation of the problem (Equation 4) with proper theoretical grounding. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is rigorous and shows the algorithm achieves optimal rates. The experimental section is comprehensive, evaluating on multiple datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) and comparing against several relevant baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, Tensor factorization). The results consistently show their approach outperforms alternatives, with clear statistical evidence.

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding the need to tune hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β) and the specific assumptions about the triggering kernel. The synthetic data experiments provide valuable insights into the algorithm's convergence behavior and sample complexity.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and its significance, while the background section on temporal point processes provides necessary context for non-experts. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained throughout. The algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1-3), and the experimental results are effectively visualized in Figures 2 and 3.

The paper's structure follows a logical flow: problem formulation → theoretical foundation → model development → optimization → experiments. The authors effectively distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly the STiC method by Kapoor et al., which they appropriately identify as a key related work.

## Originality

This paper makes a clear and significant original contribution. The connection between low-rank models and Hawkes processes for recommendation is novel, addressing a gap in the literature where most recommendation models focus on one-time preferences rather than time-sensitive behaviors. The authors explicitly contrast their work with previous approaches, noting that STiC cannot generalize to recommend new items, which is a crucial limitation for practical applications.

The optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods for low-rank point process estimation appears to be a novel contribution. The authors correctly note that this is "first of its kind" for handling both nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints simultaneously, achieving the best of both worlds in terms of convergence rates.

## Significance

The problem of time-sensitive recommendation has high practical significance across multiple domains, as the authors demonstrate with compelling examples from music streaming, e-commerce, and healthcare. The ability to recommend at the right moment and predict user return times has clear business value (e.g., more efficient ad bidding) and potential to improve patient outcomes in healthcare.

The paper's empirical results demonstrate strong performance across multiple datasets and tasks, with the proposed method consistently outperforming baselines. The scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events makes the approach practically viable for real-world applications. The model's ability to incorporate additional contextual information (spatial, textual, categorical) further enhances its applicability.

## Strengths

1. Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation.
2. Rigorous theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
3. Well-designed experiments demonstrating superiority across multiple datasets and tasks.
4. Clear distinction from related work, particularly STiC.
5. Practical significance with real-world applications in multiple domains.
6. Scalable algorithm that handles large-scale data efficiently.

## Weaknesses

1. The rationale for the low-rank assumption could be strengthened with more concrete justification beyond "users' behaviors and items' attributes can be categorized into limited prototypical types."
2. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the impact of different triggering kernel forms on performance, though they mention the exponential form as a starting point.
3. The hyperparameter tuning process (ρ, λ, β) is not fully detailed, which could make full reproducibility challenging.
4. The computational complexity comparison with alternatives is not explicitly provided, though they mention linear scalability.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong, well-supported contribution to the field of recommendation systems. The problem is important, the approach is novel, the technical foundation is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The authors have addressed a meaningful gap in the literature and provided a practical solution with real-world applicability.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is of high quality with rigorous theory and thorough experiments; it is clearly written and well-organized; it makes a significant original contribution at the intersection of point processes and recommendation systems; and it addresses a problem with clear practical significance.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The work represents a meaningful advance in time-sensitive recommendation, with strong theoretical foundations, comprehensive empirical validation, and clear practical implications. The paper's contribution is significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS, and it would be valuable to the broader machine learning community.
