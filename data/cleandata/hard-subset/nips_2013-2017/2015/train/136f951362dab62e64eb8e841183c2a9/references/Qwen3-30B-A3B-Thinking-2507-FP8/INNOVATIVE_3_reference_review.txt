

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations by modeling user activities as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors address two key problems: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting the next returning time of users. The core innovation lies in establishing a connection between self-exciting point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) and low-rank matrix models, which enables the authors to formulate a convex optimization problem that captures both the temporal dynamics of user behavior and the collaborative filtering aspects across users and items.

The paper builds upon the observation that most existing recommendation systems focus on static preference prediction (e.g., ratings), while neglecting the temporal dynamics of user behavior. The authors argue that the temporal patterns of user returns are more indicative of engagement than traditional feedback signals, which is particularly relevant in domains like health records where predicting the timing of disease occurrences can inform proactive medical interventions.

The main technical contribution is the development of a new optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. This algorithm achieves the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate for solving the low-rank point process estimation problem. The authors demonstrate that their approach scales to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events, while achieving superior predictive performance on both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Conceptual Connection:** The paper makes a bold and novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamentally new direction that bridges two distinct areas of research. The authors convincingly argue why this connection is valuable for time-sensitive recommendations, which is a significant gap in current literature.

2. **Theoretical Contribution:** The development of the optimization algorithm that achieves optimal convergence rates while handling non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization is technically impressive. The authors' proof of convergence (Theorem 2) is rigorous and demonstrates that their method achieves the "best of both worlds" in terms of convergence rates for the two components of their approach.

3. **Practical Significance:** The problem addressed (time-sensitive recommendations) has clear practical applications across domains like music streaming, e-commerce, and healthcare. The authors demonstrate their approach on real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) showing meaningful improvements over baselines.

4. **Scalability Claims:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that their approach scales to large datasets, which is crucial for real-world recommendation systems. The experimental results (Figure 2) show linear scaling with dataset size.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty Explanation:** While the connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is novel, the paper could better articulate why this particular combination is transformative rather than just another technical extension. The related work section adequately references Kapoor et al. [12, 11] but doesn't fully contrast their approach with the current paper's novelty.

2. **Experimental Scope:** The empirical evaluation, while solid, is limited to three datasets. More comprehensive evaluation across diverse recommendation domains would strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the practical challenges of implementing this approach in production systems, such as real-time prediction requirements or handling concept drift in user behavior.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to time-sensitive recommendations by establishing a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models. The technical contribution—particularly the optimization algorithm with optimal convergence rates—is substantial and well-justified. While the experimental evaluation could be more extensive, the core idea represents a significant conceptual advancement beyond incremental improvements in recommendation systems.

The paper's strength lies in its fundamental shift from static preference modeling to capturing the temporal dynamics of user behavior through a novel mathematical framework. This aligns with NIPS's interest in innovative theoretical approaches with practical implications. The authors successfully demonstrate how their method can be applied to both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks, addressing two related but previously separate problems.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems and temporal data analysis. The novelty of the approach, combined with strong theoretical and empirical results, makes it a valuable addition to the literature. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a compelling case for its novel direction.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations
- Significant theoretical contribution with optimal convergence rate analysis
- Strong empirical validation on multiple datasets showing clear advantages over baselines
- Addresses a meaningful gap in recommendation systems literature (time-sensitive recommendations)

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Experimental scope limited to three datasets
- Some aspects of the novelty could be more clearly articulated
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges

This paper exemplifies the type of bold, novel research that NIPS should prioritize. The authors have identified a meaningful problem that has been largely overlooked in the recommendation literature and developed a fundamentally new approach to solve it. The technical rigor and clear demonstration of the approach's advantages make this a strong candidate for acceptance.
