

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors claim to address two related problems: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting users' next returning time. They propose a convex optimization formulation with low-rank constraints on the intensity parameters, along with a new algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.

The paper makes several claims: (1) their approach better captures temporal dynamics than existing methods; (2) their optimization algorithm achieves optimal convergence rates; (3) their model scales to large datasets; and (4) it outperforms baselines on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a theoretically sound framework connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models, and the optimization algorithm does present an interesting blend of techniques. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is technically sound, and the authors do acknowledge limitations of previous work (e.g., STiC's inability to generalize to new items).

**Major Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated novelty:** The paper claims "novel convex formulation" while Hawkes processes have been used for recommendation before (e.g., Du et al., KDD 2015). The low-rank constraint is a standard technique in matrix factorization, not a novel contribution. The authors fail to adequately distinguish their approach from existing work, particularly from Du et al. (2015) and Zhou et al. (ICML 2013) who have applied Hawkes processes to similar problems.

2. **Inadequate experimental validation:** The synthetic data is generated under the exact model assumptions (low-rank, exponential kernel), which is not representative of real-world data. The real-world datasets are small (e.g., 1,000 users on last.fm) compared to the scale claims ("millions of user-item pairs"). The paper does not compare with more recent, strong baselines like the temporal matrix factorization approaches by Xiong et al. (SDM 2012) or the deep learning approaches by Wang et al. (WWW 2018).

3. **Misleading convergence rate claim:** The paper claims "optimal O(1/t) convergence rate" but Theorem 2 actually proves O(1/t²) convergence, which is standard for accelerated proximal gradient methods. This misrepresentation undermines the paper's technical credibility.

4. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper does not demonstrate how much the low-rank constraint contributes to performance, nor does it explore how different kernel choices affect results. The "exponential kernel" is used without justification or comparison to other kernels.

5. **Unconvincing real-world relevance:** The "time-sensitive recommendation" task is not meaningfully distinguished from standard time-aware recommendation. The paper claims their approach is "more imperative" than existing methods but fails to demonstrate this with clear, compelling evidence. The medical application example (MIMIC II) is particularly weak as it doesn't address the actual clinical problem.

6. **Excessive scale claims:** The paper claims to "scale up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events," but the real-world datasets used are orders of magnitude smaller (e.g., 100K shopping events on tmall.com). The scalability claim is unsupported by meaningful experiments.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound optimization approach for a specific problem formulation, the claims of novelty, significance, and empirical superiority are not sufficiently supported. The experimental validation is limited to small datasets and synthetic data generated under the model's own assumptions, which is insufficient to justify the claims of "superb predictive performance" and "scalability to millions of user-item pairs." The comparison to baselines is inadequate, as it excludes several relevant recent methods.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a minor technical improvement to the optimization algorithm, not a fundamentally new approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The field has seen more significant advances in temporal recommendation through deep learning approaches (e.g., GRU4Rec, SASRec) that capture complex temporal dynamics without making strong parametric assumptions.

The paper would benefit from: (1) more comprehensive comparison with recent baselines, (2) experiments on standard recommendation benchmarks, (3) ablation studies demonstrating the value of the low-rank constraint, and (4) a clearer articulation of how this work differs from prior Hawkes process applications in recommendation.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper does not present a sufficiently novel, rigorously validated approach to justify acceptance at NIPS. The claims of significance and novelty are overstated, and the experimental evidence is insufficient to support the paper's central claims. While the optimization technique has some merit, it is not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS.
