

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, develop an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate superior performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets for two time-sensitive tasks: item recommendation (recommending the most relevant item at the right moment) and returning-time prediction (predicting when users will return to a service). The work addresses an important gap in recommendation systems by moving beyond one-time preference prediction to capture the temporal dynamics of user activities.

## Strengths
1. **Technical quality**: The paper presents a well-motivated convex formulation with rigorous theoretical guarantees on convergence rate (O(1/t)). The optimization algorithm is novel and well-justified, with a clear analysis showing it achieves optimal rates for both proximal gradient and conditional gradient components.

2. **Novelty**: The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation is not commonly explored in the literature, especially with the proposed optimization approach. The framework's ability to incorporate contextual information (spatial, textual, etc.) adds to its novelty.

3. **Experimental validation**: The extensive experiments on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) demonstrate strong performance compared to multiple baselines, including Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods. The ablation studies on convergence, scalability, and model assumptions are particularly convincing.

4. **Theoretical contribution**: The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides a rigorous foundation for the proposed algorithm, showing it achieves optimal rates for both proximal gradient and conditional gradient components.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited comparison to recent point process-based methods**: The paper cites Kapoor et al. as the most related work but fails to compare with more recent point process-based recommendation methods such as "Temporal Point Processes for Recommendation" (WWW 2020) or "Deep Recurrent Models for Time-Aware Recommendations" (KDD 2019). This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of discussion on model limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address when the low-rank assumption might fail or how to diagnose it. For instance, it doesn't discuss scenarios where user-item interactions don't follow low-rank patterns, or how to select the appropriate rank.

3. **Insufficient scalability details**: While the paper claims the algorithm scales to "millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events," it lacks concrete runtime measurements or detailed analysis of computational complexity as the problem size grows.

4. **Evaluation metrics**: The use of MAE for returning-time prediction doesn't align well with business objectives (e.g., revenue impact). For recommendation tasks, rank-based metrics alone don't fully capture ranking quality.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong technical contribution with a novel framework that addresses an important gap in recommendation systems. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling, and the work has clear potential for impact in both academia and industry.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Add a comparison with recent point process-based recommendation methods (e.g., WWW 2020, KDD 2019) to better establish novelty.

2. Provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm, including concrete runtime measurements on large datasets.

3. Discuss the limitations of the low-rank assumption and when the model might fail, including potential diagnostics for when the assumption is inappropriate.

4. Consider adding a business-oriented evaluation metric for returning-time prediction (e.g., revenue impact) to strengthen practical relevance.

5. Include a discussion of when the proposed method might not outperform simpler baselines (e.g., Poisson process for certain datasets).

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in recommendation systems. With these minor revisions, it would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.
