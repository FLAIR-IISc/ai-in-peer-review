Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij). To enable generalization to unobserved pairs and control complexity, the base intensity matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A are assumed low-rank and learned via a convex program that minimizes negative log-likelihood with nuclear-norm regularization under nonnegativity constraints. Because the simultaneous presence of nonnegativity and nuclear norms makes standard proximal methods expensive (full SVD in ADMM), the authors introduce an equivalent penalized formulation with auxiliary variables Z1,Z2 (for sufficiently large ρ) and propose a block-splitting algorithm that alternates a proximal step (projection onto nonnegativity for Λ0,A) and a conditional-gradient step (rank-1 updates for Z1,Z2) with an accelerated schedule. They establish an O(1/t) convergence rate to the optimum and demonstrate scalability (up to 100M events) and superior predictive performance on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) for two tasks: (a) ranking items at a time t using intensities, and (b) predicting next event time via sampling from the conditional density using Ogata’s thinning.

This work connects multivariate temporal point processes and low-rank modeling for recommender systems. Related efforts include survival analysis and HSMM approaches for return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.), time-aware matrix/tensor factorization, and low-rank/multidimensional Hawkes estimation (Zhou, Zha, Song). The present contribution differs by offering a convex, low-rank Hawkes formulation tailored to user–item intensity modeling with an efficient solver combining proximal and conditional gradient steps.

Quality
- Modeling: The use of Hawkes processes to capture both baseline preference and short-term self-excitation is appropriate. The negative log-likelihood is convex in the parameters under nonnegativity, and nuclear norms are a standard convex surrogate for low rank.
- Optimization: The auxiliary-variable formulation decouples nuclear norms from nonnegativity and enables an efficient algorithm that only needs top singular vectors per iteration. The convergence analysis is plausible; the bound F(Yk) − OPT ≤ O(1/k) is consistent with the presence of a FW block. The equivalence theorem (with ρ > ρ*) is reasonable, although ρ* is problem-dependent and not computable; in practice ρ is tuned.
- Experiments: Large synthetic experiments support scalability and estimation quality; real-data benchmarks show improvements over Poisson, survival/HSMM, SVD, and tensor factorization in both ranking and time prediction. The time-change QQ analysis provides evidence that Hawkes better fits event dynamics than i.i.d. inter-event assumptions.

Concerns:
- Stability/identifiability: The paper does not discuss Hawkes stability (branching ratio < 1; for exponential kernels, A(u,i)·∫γ dt < 1) or how it is enforced during learning. Without such constraints, the generative process could be explosive or confound baseline and excitation. Clarifying whether regularization and nonnegativity suffice in practice, or adding explicit stability constraints, would strengthen the technical soundness.
- Kernel specification: Experiments appear to use a single global exponential bandwidth σ; modeling flexibility might be limited. Discussion of per-user/item kernels or learned mixtures (even via a small dictionary) would be valuable.
- Baselines: For time-sensitive recommendation, stronger time-aware MF baselines (e.g., TimeSVD++, Koren 2010) are cited but not reported. For point processes, comparisons to alternative Hawkes estimators (e.g., low-rank network Hawkes or marked Hawkes with EM/prox methods) would help position the gains from the proposed convex formulation versus the modeling choice.
- Practicality of return-time estimation: Relying on sampling to approximate E[Tn+1|history] can be expensive. Analytic or quadrature approximations (for exponential kernels) could be discussed, or at least timing vs. accuracy trade-offs reported.
- Reproducibility: Important implementation details are sparse (e.g., per-iteration cost, caching of sufficient statistics for exponential kernels, number of CG rank-1 updates per iteration, stopping criteria, wall-clock times, and memory footprints). Code or pseudocode is given at a high level; fuller details would aid reproduction.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The convexity and algorithmic choices are laid out clearly, and the experimental protocol is described. Some notation is inconsistent early on (scalar α vs. A(u,i)), and the kernel parameterization and choice of σ deserve clearer exposition. It would help to explicitly state whether Λ0 and A share the same rank budgets or regularization strengths across datasets and to provide sensitivity plots for ρ, λ, β.

Originality
- The problem framing (low-rank Hawkes for user–item intensities) and the convex formulation with nuclear norms are a novel combination for recommendation/return-time prediction.
- The optimization method—proximal on the nonnegative block and conditional gradient on the nuclear-norm block with acceleration—is a thoughtful hybridization of known techniques; while related to prior FW/proximal literature, this specific blend for nonnegative low-rank point-process estimation appears original.

Significance
The approach addresses an important and timely challenge: modeling recurrent user behavior in continuous time to enable both when and what to recommend. The convex formulation and scalable solver are likely to be useful beyond this application (e.g., other nonnegative low-rank Poisson/Hawkes problems). The empirical improvements and the scale of experiments suggest practical impact.

Specific questions and suggestions
- How do you ensure Hawkes stability during training? Did you observe cases with large excitation that risk instability? Would adding constraints on A(u,i)·∫γ dt < 1 be feasible in your framework?
- How sensitive are results to ρ, and can you propose an adaptive schedule increasing ρ to approach the constrained solution?
- Please report runtime per iteration, total runtime, and memory across datasets; include the cost of Ogata sampling at test time.
- Consider adding TimeSVD++ (or similar) and a Hawkes baseline trained with standard proximal/ADMM as comparisons.
- Provide ablations: nuclear norm on Λ0 only vs. on A only; rank-1 update budget; global vs. per-dataset σ.
- Discuss potential extensions to mutually exciting cross-item effects, which seem important in practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, convex modeling of a relevant problem with a practical, scalable algorithm and convergence guarantees.
- Strong empirical results on large synthetic and real datasets for both ranking and return-time prediction.
- Method is general and potentially reusable in other domains with nonnegative low-rank temporal point processes.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing discussion/enforcement of Hawkes stability and potential confounding between Λ0 and A.
- Baseline set could be stronger for time-aware recommendation and Hawkes estimation.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps (kernel choice, hyperparameter sensitivity, runtime details).

Overall
A solid and timely contribution that advances continuous-time recommendation with a principled low-rank Hawkes formulation and an efficient solver. Addressing stability, adding stronger baselines, and expanding empirical details would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
