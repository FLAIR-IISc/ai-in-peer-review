Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommender based on independent Hawkes processes per user–item pair with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), using an exponential triggering kernel. To transfer knowledge to unobserved pairs, the base intensity matrix Λ0 and excitation matrix A are assumed low rank via nuclear-norm penalties. The authors cast parameter estimation as a convex problem with nonnegativity constraints and nuclear-norm regularization, introduce an auxiliary-variable penalty formulation that decouples the constraints (Λ0,A > 0) from the nuclear norms (Z1,Z2), and design an optimization method that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient/LMO (for nuclear norms), with an O(1/t) convergence rate. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) for (i) time-aware item ranking and (ii) returning-time prediction.

This work connects temporal point processes and low-rank modeling for recommendation. Related strands include multivariate/nonparametric Hawkes learning (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song), time-aware MF/TF (Koren; tensor Poisson factorization), and dynamic Poisson factorization for implicit feedback. The use of a convex Hawkes likelihood with nuclear norms resembles prior low-rank point-process learning, but here the parameters are per user–item pair with low-rank coupling across pairs.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: time-sensitive recommendations and return-time prediction are important, under-served by rating-based CF.
- Modeling choice: Hawkes processes are a principled way to capture short-term self-excitation on top of long-term propensity.
- Convexity: for the chosen parametrization, the (negative) log-likelihood is convex in Λ0 and A; nuclear norms are standard surrogates for low rank.
- Algorithmic idea: separating the nonnegativity constraint and nuclear-norm penalties with an auxiliary-variable scheme allows one to use a simple projection for positivity and LMO for nuclear norms; the mixed prox/CG scheme is practical.
- Scalability claims: synthetic experiments suggest linear scaling with number of events.

Weaknesses and questions
- Non-constructive equivalence (Theorem 1): The penalty equivalence requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima (Λ*,A*,Z*). In practice you “tune ρ, λ, β to give the best performance.” This undermines the theoretical guarantee. Please provide a constructive choice or sensitivity analysis over ρ, and report whether suboptimal ρ changes solutions materially.
- Stability/feasibility of Hawkes: For exponential kernels, stationarity requires A(u,i) ∫ γ < 1 per process. No constraint or discussion is provided. In finite windows this can still cause likelihood pathologies or numerical instability. Clarify whether you enforce bounds on A (or on α∫κ) and how often the learned processes violate stability.
- Independence assumption: Modeling each user–item as an independent univariate Hawkes ignores cross-excitation between items and within-user sequences (e.g., artists triggering each other; comorbid diseases). This conflicts with the motivating examples and with known benefits of multivariate Hawkes. Please justify this modeling choice and quantify any loss vs. multivariate alternatives.
- Baselines: Important temporal baselines are missing. In particular: TimeSVD++/time-aware MF; dynamic Poisson factorization for implicit feedback; multivariate Hawkes with learned kernels (e.g., Zhou et al.); nonparametric Hawkes; and modern point-process recommenders. STiC is not competitive for ranking; SVD is static; tensor Poisson models are discretized but you only show “Last/Avg” summaries. Without stronger baselines, claims of “superb predictive performance” are not well supported.
- Evaluation protocol and leakage: You state parameters “are tuned to give the best performance.” This suggests test-set tuning. Please clarify the use of a validation split and re-run if necessary. Also, return-time assessment via MAE alone is weak for point processes; report test log-likelihood or time-rescaled KS statistics across users systematically, not only a single illustrative plot.
- Returning time inference: Sampling next-event times via Ogata is computationally expensive if done per user–item at recommendation time. How many samples m are used? What is the runtime overhead compared to baselines?
- Algorithmic novelty/claims: The “first of its kind” claim for mixing prox and CG under nonnegativity + nuclear norms seems overstated. There is prior work on composite/structured optimization with conditional gradients and on handling constraints via projections or Dykstra/ADMM with partial SVD. Please better situate your method and include a runtime/memory comparison against a well-tuned ADMM/prox-splitting baseline with partial SVD.
- Identifiability/regularization: Λ0 and A can trade off substantially in Hawkes models. Beyond nuclear norms, is there any safeguard (e.g., penalizing the branching ratio) to avoid degenerate fits or over-excitation? An ablation (only Λ0, only A, both) would help.
- Reproducibility/clarity: Many references are incomplete; some notation is sloppy; figures are referenced but not shown; key hyperparameters (σ, m, step sizes) and implementation details are missing.

Quality
- The convex formulation and optimization are sound in isolation, but the exactness condition in Theorem 1 is non-constructive, and the empirical validation is not rigorous enough (weak baselines, possible tuning leakage, limited goodness-of-fit testing, no ablations). Missing stability discussion is concerning.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but the exposition should be tightened. Define σ and stability constraints; provide computational complexity per iteration; clarify data splits and hyperparameter tuning; improve references.

Originality
- The high-level idea (Hawkes + low-rank across user–item pairs) is a reasonable combination of known tools. The mixed prox/CG scheme is incremental rather than fundamentally new. Novelty is modest.

Significance
- The problem is important, but due to modeling simplifications, missing baselines, and evaluation gaps, it is hard to conclude state-of-the-art advancement at this point. With stronger empirical evidence and comparisons, impact could increase.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong temporal baselines (TimeSVD++, dynamic Poisson factorization, multivariate/nonparametric Hawkes).
- Use a validation set; report test log-likelihood and time-rescaling KS across many sequences; add calibration plots.
- Analyze sensitivity to ρ and provide a constructive setting; discuss stability constraints and violations.
- Include ablations (only Λ0 vs only A; effect of rank via λ,β); report runtime/memory vs ADMM with partial SVD.
- Clarify next-time sampling cost and practical deployment (candidate generation for ranking).
- Improve references and notation; provide code or detailed pseudocode sufficient for reproduction.

Pros
- Convex formulation; practical optimization method.
- Addresses important time-sensitive recommendation tasks.
- Scales to large synthetic datasets.

Cons
- Non-constructive theory and possible test-set tuning.
- Missing stability considerations.
- Important baselines absent; evaluation metrics limited.
- Independence assumption may be too restrictive for claimed applications.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The paper has promise, but requires stronger empirical validation, clearer and more constructive theory, and broader comparisons to justify publication.
