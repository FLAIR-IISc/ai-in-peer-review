Summary
This paper tackles two tightly coupled problems: time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given moment) and return-time prediction (forecasting when a user will return to an item/service). The authors model each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an exponential triggering kernel, where the intensity decomposes into a nonnegative base rate Λ0(u,i) and a nonnegative self-excitation weight A(u,i). To transfer signal to unobserved user–item pairs, they impose low-rank structure via nuclear-norm regularization on Λ0 and A. They then introduce a convex estimation problem and propose a new large-scale optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for nuclear norms) by introducing auxiliary variables and a quadratic penalty that decouples constraints. They prove an equivalence result for the penalty formulation under sufficiently large ρ and derive an O(1/t) convergence rate. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improved ranking and return-time MAE over Poisson, tensor factorization baselines, STiC, and SVD. They also include QQ-plot diagnostics supporting the Hawkes assumption.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: A clear and useful bridge between low-rank modeling and Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation. The convex formulation and the “proximal meets conditional gradient” algorithmic design are interesting and broadly applicable beyond this task.
- Technical soundness: The negative Hawkes log-likelihood with exponential kernel plus nuclear-norm penalties is a natural convex objective (with nonnegativity ensuring valid intensities). The penalty-based decoupling and LMO built from top singular vectors are well motivated; the rate analysis is credible and practically relevant.
- Practical significance: The framework handles millions of user–item pairs and >100M events, and directly optimizes objectives aligned with ranking and return-time prediction. The approach supports recommendation for unobserved user–item pairs via low-rank transfer.
- Empirical validation: Strong synthetic study (recovery vs. sample size, linear scaling plots) and competitive real-data results across domains, including healthcare. Diagnostic QQ-plots are a nice addition to show model-data fit.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Modeling limitations: The intensity uses only self-excitation per user–item pair (no mutual excitation across items, categories, or users). In many recommendation settings, cross-item influences and substitution/complementarity matter. Including a low-rank mutually-exciting structure (e.g., low-rank item–item kernel matrix or user/item latent factors driving cross-kernels) would be a substantial extension.
- Theoretical detail on Theorem 1: The exact penalty equivalence needs a data-dependent threshold ρ*, which is unknown and defined via optimal solutions. In practice ρ is tuned; it would help to (i) provide a practical schedule or adaptive rule for ρ, (ii) bound ρ* in terms of observable quantities, or (iii) empirically show robustness of performance across ρ.
- Baselines: While Poisson/tensor/STiC/SVD are reasonable, stronger time-aware recommenders could be considered (e.g., factorization with temporal drift, timeSVD++, or explicit low-rank factorized variants of the proposed convex model). A baseline with factorized Λ0=UV^T and A=U'V'^T trained via projected SGD would test whether the convex nuclear-norm approach is essential.
- Calibration and uncertainty: Return-time is evaluated by MAE with sampling via Ogata’s thinning. Since the exponential kernel yields closed-form integrals, consider reporting calibration metrics (QQ for all sequences, PIT histograms), and evaluating quantile/interval accuracy. Also compare sampling vs. numerical quadrature for expectation.
- Clarity and presentation: Some notation is terse or inconsistent (e.g., “feature ψu,i in 3”, typos in indices). The algorithms (particularly LMO line-search) could benefit from more explicit pseudocode and complexity discussion (how top singular vectors are computed at scale; number of power iterations; wall-clock).
- Ablations: It would be valuable to isolate the contributions of Λ0 vs. self-excitation A (e.g., set A=0 or Λ0=0), and to study sensitivity to kernel bandwidth σ and rank (via λ, β). Also report cold-start generalization to entirely new items/users if possible.

Quality
Overall technically solid. The convexity is correct under standard Hawkes assumptions and nonnegativity. The algorithmic contribution is meaningful and seems to achieve the advertised rate. Proof sketches are referenced; full details in appendix would be important for camera-ready. Empirically, the setup is reasonable, though stronger baselines and ablations would further strengthen the case.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-structured, but could be improved by tightening notation, fixing typos, and expanding algorithmic details (LMO, complexity, memory). Figures are informative; more comprehensive reporting of runtime and convergence (wall-clock vs. iterations) would help.

Originality
High. The low-rank Hawkes for user–item time-sensitive recommendation, combined with a convex formulation and a hybrid proximal/conditional-gradient solver, is a fresh and impactful combination. Related work on low-rank Hawkes (e.g., network inference, sparse+low-rank) is cited, and the differences here (user–item transfer and recommendation) are clear.

Significance
High. Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are important. The method is scalable, generalizable, and likely to be adopted or extended (e.g., to include covariates, cross-excitation, nonparametric kernels). The optimization technique could be reused in other nonnegative low-rank problems.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually new and unifying approach linking Hawkes processes with low-rank transfer for recommendation.
- Convex, scalable estimator with a principled, efficient algorithm and nontrivial convergence analysis.
- Strong empirical performance across diverse domains; compelling diagnostics.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited modeling of cross-item and contextual influences; only self-excitation per pair.
- The exact-penalty equivalence relies on an unknown threshold; practical guidance limited.
- Baseline suite could be stronger; missing ablations and more thorough runtime analysis.

Questions to authors
- Can you report wall-clock time vs. iterations, and the cost of computing top singular vectors (method, iterations)?
- How sensitive are results to σ, λ, β, and ρ? Any adaptive schemes?
- Have you tried a factorized (nonconvex) alternative for Λ0 and A, and how does it compare?
- Can you incorporate simple cross-item effects (e.g., category-level mutual excitation) and report the gains?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel direction with solid theory and promising empirical results. The limitations are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. I expect this work to stimulate follow-on research in time-sensitive recommendation and scalable learning for point processes.

Confidence
High.
