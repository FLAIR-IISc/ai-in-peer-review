Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles two coupled problems: (1) time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at a specific time), and (2) predicting a user’s next return time to an item/service. The authors model each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an exponential triggering kernel and impose shared structure across all user–item pairs via low-rank matrices for the baseline intensity Λ0 and the self-excitation parameter A. They propose a convex formulation: minimize the negative log-likelihood with nuclear-norm penalties on Λ0 and A under non-negativity constraints. To address the computational challenge posed by the combination of nuclear norms and non-negativity, they introduce a penalized splitting formulation with auxiliary variables and develop an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for non-negativity) and conditional gradient/Frank-Wolfe (for nuclear norms). They prove convergence (O(1/t) rate for the mixed setting) to the optimum of the penalized problem, and show equivalence to the original constrained problem when the penalty parameter ρ exceeds a problem-dependent threshold. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II) report strong performance on both ranking and return-time prediction versus Poisson, tensor factorization baselines, SVD, and a survival/HMM approach (STiC).

This builds on temporal point process literature (Hawkes processes) and on low-rank models for recommendation. It connects to work on low-rank/sparse Hawkes estimation and conditional gradient methods for nuclear-norm regularization. Relative to prior survival-analysis-based return-time prediction, it can generalize to unseen items and handles recurrent dependencies.

Quality:
- Modeling and optimization: The likelihood formulation is convex in the nonnegative parameters because it is a sum of -log(affine) plus linear terms; adding nuclear norms preserves convexity. The proximal/Frank-Wolfe hybrid is a sensible and efficient approach for the split penalized problem; the LMO via top singular vectors is standard and cheap. The convergence theorem for the penalized objective is clear and aligns with known optimal rates for FW-type and proximal methods.
- Theoretical caveat: The equivalence Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima; in practice ρ is tuned. This limits the practical force of the claim that the penalized solution equals the constrained optimum. A continuation or provable lower bound for ρ would strengthen the theory.
- Modeling caveats: (i) Hawkes stability: with exponential kernel of scale σ, the branching ratio is α·σ; stability typically requires α·σ < 1. The paper does not enforce or discuss this constraint; adding α ≤ 1/σ (per entry) would be simple and convex. (ii) Independence across user–item pairs: the model captures self-excitation within a pair but not mutual excitation across items/diseases; this undercuts part of the disease co-progression motivation. Extending to low-rank multidimensional Hawkes or cross-excitation via shared factors would address this.
- Empirical evaluation: The split protocol is reasonable and explicitly challenges generalization to entirely held-out user–item pairs. The reported results suggest consistent gains on both tasks. However, some baselines are limited: SVD is not time-aware; stronger time-aware recommenders (e.g., TimeSVD++, FPMC) and modern Poisson factorization with temporal drift would be relevant for the ranking task. For return-time, they compare to STiC and Poisson; additional renewal/hazard models would be informative. Runtime/memory and wall-clock comparisons are limited; the scalability claim would benefit from concrete timing and resource tables. Return-time estimation via Monte Carlo thinning is reasonable but potentially expensive; discussion of its cost/variance would help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized: good background on point processes, a compact reparameterization of the likelihood, and readable algorithmic pseudocode. Some notation is dense and there are minor typos/formatting issues. Important implementation details (gradient expressions, step-size choices, stopping criteria) could be elaborated for reproducibility. The stability assumption and how test-time histories are constructed should be explicitly stated.

Originality:
The combination of (i) a convex low-rank Hawkes formulation for recommender-style problems and (ii) a hybrid proximal/conditional gradient method that separates non-negativity and nuclear norms is novel and interesting. While both low-rank Hawkes estimation and FW for nuclear norms exist, their integration here to solve time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction at scale is original. Theoretical guarantees for the hybrid scheme are a plus.

Significance:
Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time modeling are important in practice. Providing a single, interpretable model that yields both item scores and return-time densities is valuable. If the scalability claims hold, this can be impactful for large platforms and for clinical event modeling. The lack of mutual excitation limits applicability to domains with strong cross-item dependencies, but the framework is a solid step.

Pros:
- Convex, principled model with interpretable components (baseline vs. self-excitation).
- Efficient hybrid optimization with theoretical convergence; leverages top-SVD oracles.
- Strong empirical results on both ranking and return-time across diverse datasets.
- Scales to large numbers of events; synthetic study is extensive.

Cons:
- Equivalence to the original constrained problem hinges on an unknown ρ; no guidance to set it beyond tuning.
- No enforcement/discussion of Hawkes stability (α·σ < 1).
- Independence across user–item pairs contradicts part of the motivational narrative (disease interactions).
- Baselines for time-aware recommendation could be stronger; limited runtime reporting; reproducibility details sparse.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Incorporate stability constraints (e.g., α ≤ 1/σ) and analyze impact.
- Provide a practical strategy for ρ selection (e.g., continuation schedule) and sensitivity analysis; or an a priori lower bound.
- Add stronger time-aware baselines (TimeSVD++, FPMC, Poisson factorization with temporal drift) and report wall-clock/runtime and memory.
- Include ablations: no self-excitation, no low-rank, different kernels; report k@N metrics for recommendation.
- Clarify test-time procedures and provide code to aid reproducibility.
- Discuss extensions to mutual excitation across items using low-rank multidimensional Hawkes.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a technically sound, novel, and potentially impactful approach with solid theory and promising results, though addressing stability, baseline breadth, and ρ selection would strengthen it further.
