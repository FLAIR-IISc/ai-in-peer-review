

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix models. The authors propose modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes, where the intensity function captures both long-term user preferences (through a base intensity matrix) and short-term temporal dependencies (through a self-exciting matrix). The key innovation lies in recognizing that the intensity matrix between users and items naturally possesses low-rank structure, enabling efficient learning and generalization to unseen user-item pairs.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it establishes a theoretically grounded connection between temporal point processes and low-rank models that has not been previously explored in recommendation systems. The authors prove that their convex formulation with nuclear norm regularization leads to a computationally efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate. This is a significant theoretical advance that addresses the computational challenges of scaling Hawkes processes to large recommendation systems.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating the approach's effectiveness on both synthetic data (where ground truth parameters are known) and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, and MIMIC II medical records). The results consistently show that their method outperforms strong baselines including Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks. The authors also provide valuable empirical validation of their modeling assumptions using the time-change theorem.

What makes this paper particularly compelling for NIPS is its bold conceptual leap. While most recommendation systems focus on static item preferences or short-term session-based interactions, this work recognizes that user preferences evolve over time in a self-exciting manner, and that these temporal dynamics can be modeled through a unified framework. The authors effectively argue that this perspective is crucial for modern applications like personalized health monitoring and marketing optimization, where timing is as important as content.

The paper's strengths far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The related work section clearly positions the contribution against existing approaches (notably Kapoor et al.'s survival analysis-based methods), highlighting the key limitation of not being able to generalize to new items. The technical exposition is clear and well-structured, with sufficient detail to understand the model and algorithm. While the real-world datasets could be expanded, the results on the existing datasets are convincing.

The main potential weakness is that the paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of the low-rank assumption in certain scenarios (e.g., when user-item interactions don't naturally cluster into a small number of prototypical patterns). However, this is a minor point given the paper's strong theoretical and empirical contributions.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a fundamentally new approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is novel, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The authors have successfully bridged two distinct areas of research (point processes and matrix factorization) to create a powerful new framework for time-sensitive recommendation.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This work represents a substantial advance that opens up a new research direction at the intersection of temporal modeling and recommendation systems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field that will likely inspire significant follow-up work. The novelty and conceptual boldness of the approach align perfectly with NIPS's focus on innovative machine learning research.
