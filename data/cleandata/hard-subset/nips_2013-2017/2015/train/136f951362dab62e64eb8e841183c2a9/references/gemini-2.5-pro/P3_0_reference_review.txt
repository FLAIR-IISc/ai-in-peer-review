### **Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by jointly predicting *what* item a user will interact with and *when* this interaction will occur. The authors frame this problem using the theory of temporal point processes. Specifically, they model the sequence of interactions for each user-item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The key innovation is to address the inherent data sparsity of this approach (most user-item pairs have no interactions) by connecting it to the low-rank modeling paradigm common in collaborative filtering. They assume that the matrices representing the base intensity (`Λ0`) and the self-excitation strength (`A`) for all user-item pairs are low-rank. This allows the model to share statistical strength and generalize to unobserved pairs.

To learn the model parameters, the authors formulate a convex optimization problem involving the maximization of the log-likelihood of the observed event sequences, regularized by the nuclear norms of the parameter matrices. Recognizing the difficulty of solving this problem due to the coupled non-negativity constraints and non-smooth nuclear norm regularizers, they propose a novel penalty-based reformulation. This enables an efficient hybrid optimization algorithm that combines a proximal gradient step for the non-negative part and a conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) step for the low-rank part. The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis showing an `O(1/t)` rate. The proposed "Hawkes-Recommender" is evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks compared to several relevant baselines.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and elegant synthesis of two powerful modeling frameworks: temporal point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) and low-rank models (from collaborative filtering). This connection is non-trivial and provides a principled way to tackle the crucial but often overlooked temporal dimension in recommender systems. The problem of predicting not just *what* but also *when* is of high practical importance for applications like ad-bidding, user engagement, and clinical decision support, making the work highly significant.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically solid. The formulation of the low-rank Hawkes process is well-motivated. The main technical contribution in the optimization is clever and impactful: the reformulation in Section 4.2 to decouple the non-negativity and nuclear norm constraints is a key enabler for the efficient algorithm. The proposed hybrid algorithm, blending proximal and conditional gradient methods, is well-suited for this structure. The inclusion of a convergence rate analysis (Theorem 2) adds significant theoretical rigor to the paper.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The use of synthetic data with a known ground truth effectively demonstrates the algorithm's ability to recover the underlying low-rank parameters and its favorable scaling properties.
    *   The evaluation on three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, healthcare) showcases the model's broad applicability.
    *   The choice of baselines is strong, including a static CF model (SVD), a simpler point process model (Poisson), and modern time-aware methods (STiC, Tensor Factorization).
    *   The evaluation on two distinct tasks (ranking and time prediction) thoroughly assesses the model's capabilities.
    *   The use of the time-change theorem (Figure 3) to validate the Hawkes process assumption against alternatives is an excellent piece of model diagnostics, lending strong support to the paper's core hypothesis.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The optimization section (4.3) is dense and could benefit from more detail. The paper does not explicitly provide the gradients `∇f(X)` used in the updates. While derivable from the objective function, their inclusion would significantly improve clarity and reproducibility. Furthermore, the practical implementation details are sparse. For example, the step-size `ηk` depends on the Lipschitz constant `L`, and it is unclear how this was set in the experiments. Similarly, the hyperparameter tuning process for `ρ`, `λ`, and `β` is described as being "tuned to give the best performance," which is insufficient for a rigorous scientific paper; details on the validation methodology and search ranges should be provided.

2.  **Model Limitations and Discussion:** The model, while powerful, has some inherent limitations that are not discussed.
    *   **Self-Excitation Only:** The current model only considers self-excitation, meaning an interaction with item `i` only influences future interactions with the *same* item `i`. It does not capture cross-item influence (e.g., listening to one artist makes you more likely to listen to a similar artist). This is a significant simplification of user behavior. While extending the model to a full multivariate Hawkes process would be more complex, acknowledging this limitation and discussing it as future work would strengthen the paper.
    *   **Choice of Kernel:** The paper uses a simple exponential triggering kernel for its analysis and experiments. While it mentions other kernels are possible (Sec 3.3), the sensitivity of the model's performance to this choice is not explored. An ablation study or discussion on this point would be valuable.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The conditional gradient step requires computing the top singular vector pair of the gradient matrix at each iteration. While much cheaper than a full SVD, this can still be a computational bottleneck for extremely large user-item matrices (e.g., millions of users/items). A brief discussion of the per-iteration complexity and potential trade-offs with other large-scale optimization methods (like stochastic gradient descent) would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's practical scalability.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of recommender systems. The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank modeling is a powerful idea, and the authors support it with a solid theoretical foundation and extensive, convincing experiments. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity in the optimization section and a lack of discussion around model limitations, which are addressable. The strengths in originality, significance, and technical quality far outweigh these weaknesses. This work advances the state of the art in time-aware recommendation and is likely to inspire follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
