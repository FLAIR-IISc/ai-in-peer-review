Summary
The paper tackles two linked problems: time-sensitive recommendation (which item to suggest at a given time) and returning-time prediction (when a user will next interact) from recurrent user activities. The authors model each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) Σγ(t, t_prev), using an exponential triggering kernel. To generalize to unobserved user–item pairs, they impose low-rank structure on the matrices Λ0 and A via nuclear-norm regularization. They formulate parameter estimation as a convex problem: minimizing negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norms under nonnegativity constraints. To solve it at scale, they introduce a penalty-based reformulation that separates nonnegativity and nuclear-norm terms, and propose an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient/LMO (for nuclear norms), with a convergence bound that combines O(1/k^2) and O(1/k) terms; overall O(1/k). Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improvements over Poisson, SVD, survival/HSMM (STiC) for return-time, and Poisson tensor factorization baselines for recommendation, alongside scalability claims. They also use time-change diagnostics to argue Hawkes dynamics fit the real data better than i.i.d. gap models.

Relation to prior work
This builds on temporal point processes (Hawkes) and low-rank modeling. Prior Hawkes learning has focused on network infectivity matrices and kernel learning (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS/ICML 2013), often with sparsity or low-rank in a multivariate process; here the novelty is low-rank sharing across a matrix of per-dyad scalar Hawkes parameters for recommendation. On optimization, there is related work on ADMM for nuclear-norm objectives (e.g., Yu et al., NIPS) and conditional-gradient methods for norm-regularized problems (Harchaoui et al.). The proposed block-splitting with a penalty and mixed proximal/Frank–Wolfe updates is a useful variant tailored to nonnegativity + nuclear norms.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: aligning recommendation with temporally varying preferences and predicting return-times is important and under-served by static recommenders.
- Sound modeling: Hawkes captures self-excitation/history dependence; convexity arises because intensity is an affine nonnegative function of parameters.
- Algorithmic contribution: a practical scheme that avoids full SVDs by using top singular vectors in an LMO and simple nonnegativity projection; a clean convergence analysis with interpretable rates.
- Empirical evidence: synthetic experiments demonstrate convergence and scaling; real-data results show consistent gains on both tasks; time-change diagnostics strengthen the modeling choice.
- Generalizability: the framework can incorporate features and other kernels (discussed, though not implemented).

Weaknesses
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to stronger time-aware recommenders (e.g., timeSVD++, dynamic matrix factorization, recurrent/sequential models) and to Hawkes-based recommenders or multivariate Hawkes with cross-excitation. Tensor baselines are limited to Poisson regression variants; ranking metrics like Recall@k/NDCG are not reported.
- Limited modeling capacity: Only self-excitation per user–item pair with a single exponential bandwidth; no cross-item or cross-user excitation, inhibition, or heterogeneous kernels are evaluated. This may underfit domains where inter-item influence matters.
- Penalty equivalence: Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima; in practice ρ is tuned. More guidance on safe/robust choices or a continuation strategy would improve reliability.
- Convergence and complexity details: The Lipschitz constant and its dependence on data are not made explicit; cost of computing top singular vectors and sampling-based return-time prediction is not profiled vs. ADMM/partial-SVD alternatives. No empirical comparison to ADMM, proximal Frank–Wolfe, or stochastic variants.
- Evaluation/reporting: For recommendation, reporting average rank is less standard than Recall@k/MAP/NDCG; confidence intervals/statistical tests are not shown. Return-time prediction uses sampling via Ogata; sensitivity to number of samples and runtime overhead is not discussed.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The convexity of the estimation problem under nonnegativity and fixed kernel families is appropriate; the algorithm is reasonable and the analysis consistent with known rates (O(1/k) overall, with an O(1/k^2) component). Proof details are deferred; assuming they establish Lipschitzness and correctness of the LMO/line-search, the claims are well supported. Experimental design is decent, though stronger baselines and more standard metrics would better substantiate impact.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The modeling setup, likelihood, and optimization steps are described understandably. Some notation is terse (e.g., φ/ψ features), and Algorithm 1–3 could include more precise definitions (closed-form α, computational costs). Minor typos exist, but an expert reader could reproduce the method with reasonable effort; code or more implementation details would help.

Originality
Moderate. Combining low-rank sharing with Hawkes for recommendation is a useful idea, and the specific proximal–conditional gradient blend with a penalty for separating constraints is a neat optimization twist. However, both ingredients (Hawkes modeling; low-rank nuclear-norm regularization; Frank–Wolfe/proximal methods) are established, and related works on low-rank Hawkes/network inference exist.

Significance
Good practical significance for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction. The convex, scalable approach is attractive for practitioners. To maximize impact, comparisons to stronger modern baselines and extensions to cross-excitation would be valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: timeSVD++/dynamic MF, sequence models (e.g., RNNs), and multivariate Hawkes recommenders if available.
- Use standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG) and report statistical significance.
- Provide runtime comparisons to ADMM with partial SVDs and to proximal Frank–Wolfe variants; profile the cost of top singular vector computations and Ogata sampling.
- Ablations/sensitivity: bandwidth σ, number of samples for return-time, effect of ρ, λ, β; show robustness.
- Discuss identifiability/separability between Λ0 and A and potential regularization to avoid degeneracy.
- Consider cross-excitation across items/users or context features in experiments to demonstrate extensibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with a principled, convex, and scalable method.
- Solid theoretical grounding with convergence guarantees.
- Consistent empirical gains across multiple datasets and tasks.
- Practical algorithmic contribution that may be of broader interest.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing comparisons to stronger, modern time-aware/sequential recommendation baselines.
- Modeling restrictions (only self-excitation, single exponential kernel) may limit applicability.
- Practical guidance on choosing ρ and more thorough runtime analyses are lacking.
- Evaluation metrics could be improved and statistical rigor increased.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper is technically solid and practically relevant, with a clear contribution at the intersection of point processes and low-rank modeling. Strengthening baselines and empirical analyses would make it a clear accept.
