### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to predict not only which item a user will prefer, but also when they are likely to interact with it. The authors propose a novel approach that models the sequence of user-item interactions as a multivariate Hawkes process. The core idea is that past interactions with an item (self-excitation) and a user's baseline interest jointly influence the probability of a future interaction. To generalize across all user-item pairs and handle data sparsity, the parameter matrices governing the baseline intensities (Λ0) and the self-excitation magnitudes (A) are assumed to be low-rank.

The problem is formulated as a convex optimization task involving the maximization of the log-likelihood of the observed event data, regularized by the nuclear norms of the parameter matrices to enforce the low-rank assumption. To solve this problem, which involves non-smooth regularizers and non-negativity constraints, the authors develop a novel optimization algorithm that combines elements of proximal gradient and conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) methods. This algorithm is shown to achieve an optimal convergence rate and is demonstrated to be scalable. The proposed model, "Hawkes-Recommender," is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets on two tasks: item recommendation at a specific time point and prediction of a user's next return time to an item.

This work connects several lines of research. It extends traditional collaborative filtering (e.g., matrix factorization) by explicitly modeling continuous-time event dynamics, a direction also explored by temporal tensor factorization models [3, 25] and models incorporating temporal decay [16]. Its most direct intellectual predecessors are works using survival analysis [12] or hidden semi-Markov models [11] for return-time prediction. However, the proposed Hawkes process framework is more expressive, as it models the influence of the entire event history, not just the last event, and naturally generalizes to unobserved user-item pairs via the low-rank assumption. The optimization approach builds on recent work in efficient large-scale convex optimization for structured matrix problems [28, 27].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the recommendation problem using low-rank Hawkes processes is well-motivated and mathematically coherent. The derivation of the log-likelihood and the final convex optimization objective (Eq. 4) is correct. A significant strength is the development of a custom optimization algorithm designed to handle the specific structure of the problem (non-negativity, nuclear norm penalty). The inclusion of a convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm.

However, the connection between the original problem (Eq. 4) and the solved approximation (Eq. 5) relies on a condition on the penalty parameter ρ (Theorem 1) that is not practically verifiable, as it depends on the unknown optimal solution. This weakens the claim that the algorithm directly solves the problem of interest; it solves an approximation, and the quality of this approximation is not empirically studied.

The experimental evaluation is a major point of weakness. While the use of synthetic data is appropriate for validating the algorithm's correctness and convergence, its utility ends there. Strong performance on data generated from the model's own assumptions is expected and provides little evidence of its effectiveness on real-world data where these assumptions are unlikely to hold perfectly. The evaluation on real data is more convincing but suffers from a limited selection of baselines. The comparison to modern, state-of-the-art temporal recommenders, particularly those based on recurrent neural networks (e.g., GRU4Rec, SASRec), is notably absent. These models are highly effective at capturing sequential dynamics and represent a critical benchmark for any new temporal recommendation method. The tensor factorization baseline is appropriate, but the paper could have provided a more thorough comparison against different tensor models and configurations.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the background on point processes is helpful. The model is presented logically, progressing from the single-pair Hawkes process to the full low-rank formulation.

However, the optimization section (4.3) is very dense. While the high-level idea of blending proximal and conditional gradient methods is clear, the details of the updates and the accompanying pseudo-code are difficult to follow without deep familiarity with this specific sub-field of optimization. Reproducing the algorithm from the provided description would be challenging. A more detailed walk-through of the update steps or a simpler illustrative example would have improved clarity.

**Originality:**

The primary contribution is the combination of multivariate Hawkes processes with a low-rank matrix structure for the specific application of recommendation. While both components are established techniques in their respective fields (point processes and collaborative filtering), their synthesis into a unified, convex framework for time-sensitive recommendation is novel. The proposed optimization algorithm, which handles the specific combination of non-negativity and nuclear norm regularization, also appears to be a new contribution. The work is clearly distinguished from prior art, particularly the work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12].

**Significance:**

The problem of predicting *when* a user will engage with a service is of high practical importance for areas like ad-bidding and user engagement management, and is academically less explored than standard rating prediction. This paper presents a principled and elegant approach to this problem. If the model proves to be robust and significantly outperforms existing methods, it could have a substantial impact.

However, the current evidence is not sufficient to claim a significant advance in the state of the art. The empirical gains over simpler baselines like the Poisson process or tensor factorization are present but not consistently overwhelming. Without a more comprehensive comparison against stronger, more established temporal recommendation models, the true significance of the proposed method remains uncertain. The introduced complexity of the Hawkes process model and the specialized optimization algorithm must be justified by a clear and substantial performance improvement, which is not fully demonstrated in the current manuscript.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pros:**
1.  **Novel Formulation:** Proposes an elegant and principled framework for time-sensitive recommendation by combining Hawkes processes and low-rank models.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** Includes a new, scalable optimization algorithm with a formal convergence analysis, which may be of independent interest.
3.  **Addresses an Important Problem:** Tackles the under-explored but practically significant problem of predicting user return times.

**Cons:**
1.  **Insufficient Experimental Comparison:** The evaluation lacks comparisons to strong and highly relevant baselines, particularly state-of-the-art RNN-based sequential recommenders. This makes it difficult to assess the practical value and state-of-the-art status of the proposed method.
2.  **Weakness of Synthetic Experiments:** The heavy reliance on synthetic data generated from the model's own distribution overstates its performance and does not test its robustness to model mismatch.
3.  **Justification for Complexity:** The performance gains shown are modest and may not fully justify the additional complexity of the model and optimization machinery compared to simpler established methods.
4.  **Clarity Issues in Optimization Section:** The description of the core algorithm is dense and lacks the detail required for easy understanding and reproducibility.

### Conclusion

This paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to an important problem in recommendation systems. The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank assumptions is novel and theoretically well-founded. However, the paper's central claims of superior predictive performance are not yet substantiated by a rigorous and comprehensive experimental evaluation. The absence of key state-of-the-art baselines is a critical flaw that undermines the paper's conclusions about its own significance. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, a NIPS paper on a new recommendation method must demonstrate a clear empirical advantage. As it stands, the evidence provided is insufficient to endorse this new, complex approach over existing, well-established techniques. I would be willing to reconsider my evaluation if the authors provided a more thorough comparison against stronger baselines.
