

# Peer Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a critical gap in recommendation systems by proposing a unified framework for time-sensitive recommendations that considers both the "right item" and "right moment" for recommendations. The authors model user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes, leveraging the connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank matrix models. This approach differs from traditional recommendation systems that focus on one-time preference prediction and from recent work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12] that uses survival analysis for returning time prediction but cannot generalize to new items. The paper makes significant contributions through a novel convex formulation, an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and thorough empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies that most recommendation systems overlook the temporal dynamics of user behavior, which is crucial for time-sensitive applications. The authors make a strong case for why modeling the next returning time is important for both business applications (e.g., ad bidding) and healthcare (e.g., predicting disease progression).

2. **Theoretical Contributions:** The convex formulation (Equation 4) is well-motivated and represents a significant contribution. The authors prove that their approach achieves the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate (Theorem 2), which is particularly valuable given the non-smooth nuclear norm regularization. The connection between the convex formulation and the low-rank matrix model is elegantly established.

3. **Algorithmic Innovation:** Algorithm 1, which blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, is theoretically sound and practically efficient. The paper demonstrates how to handle the non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization separately, which is a significant technical contribution that addresses a key limitation of previous approaches.

4. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are thorough, evaluating both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). The paper carefully compares against multiple baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization) and provides detailed analysis of convergence behavior, scalability, and predictive performance. The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

## Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Limited Discussion of Model Assumptions:** The paper assumes a homogeneous exponential triggering kernel (γ(t, ti) = exp(-(t-ti)/σ)), but doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of this choice. For instance, the exponential kernel might not capture more complex temporal dependencies (e.g., periodic patterns in user behavior). The authors should discuss whether their model could be extended to more flexible kernel forms.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper cites Kapoor et al. [11, 12] as the most related work, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of how their approach differs in terms of handling new items. The paper states that their model "can generalize to recommend any new item," but doesn't explain the mechanism for this. A more thorough comparison with recent work on temporal recommendations (e.g., Du et al. [8, 30] on Hawkes processes for recommendation) would strengthen the paper.

3. **Cold-Start Problem:** The paper doesn't address how their model handles cold-start scenarios for new users or new items. This is a critical limitation for real-world recommendation systems, as the model requires historical interaction data to learn the parameters. The authors should discuss potential strategies for handling cold-start cases.

4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions tuning ρ, λ, and β, it doesn't provide analysis of how sensitive the model is to these hyperparameters. This is particularly important given the theoretical guarantees depend on ρ being properly chosen (Theorem 1). A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper doesn't include an ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component (e.g., the low-rank assumption, the Hawkes process formulation, the optimization algorithm). This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the approach are most important for the observed performance gains.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation systems. The theoretical foundation is sound, the algorithmic innovation is valuable, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper successfully addresses an important gap in the literature and provides a practical solution with clear real-world applications.

The main limitations relate to insufficient discussion of model assumptions, limited comparison with recent work, and lack of analysis for cold-start scenarios. These are important considerations for practical adoption but don't undermine the core contributions of the paper.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It addresses a real problem with significant practical implications, presents a novel and well-justified approach, and provides strong empirical validation. The theoretical guarantees and scalability results are particularly impressive. While the weaknesses noted above should be addressed in the camera-ready version, they don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

The paper advances the state of the art in recommendation systems by explicitly modeling temporal dynamics and providing a scalable, convex optimization approach. The proposed algorithm could be valuable beyond recommendation systems for other low-rank matrix problems with temporal dynamics. Given the increasing importance of time-sensitive applications in industry, this work is highly relevant to NIPS readers.
