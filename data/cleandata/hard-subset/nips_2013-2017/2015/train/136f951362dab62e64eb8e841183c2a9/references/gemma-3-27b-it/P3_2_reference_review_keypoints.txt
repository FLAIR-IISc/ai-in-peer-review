- Overall assessment: novel, technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation using low-rank Hawkes processes; recommend weak accept with major revisions.
- Main contribution: convex formulation linking Hawkes processes to low-rank models for time-varying user preferences.
- Optimization: proximal + conditional gradient hybrid algorithm with provable O(1/t) convergence.
- Theoretical grounding: strong use of point process theory and rigorous convergence analysis.
- Experiments: evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II).
- Scalability claim: algorithm is claimed to scale to millions of user-item pairs, but experiments use modest dataset sizes.
- Practical gains: improvements over baselines are statistically significant but modest in real-world recommendation tasks.
- Baselines: comparisons rely on standard but potentially outdated baselines (e.g., STiC); lacks recent deep temporal recommendation methods.
- Hyperparameters: sensitivity to ρ, λ, β, bandwidth, and rank is insufficiently explored and justified.
- Kernel choice: uses exponential kernel throughout without investigating alternative kernels or their impact.
- Real-world data scale: real datasets feel relatively small; need validation on much larger industry-scale datasets.
- Relation to prior work: connection to low-rank approximations in point processes could be tied more explicitly to existing literature.
- Questions for authors: clarify practical interpretation of improvements, computational cost vs baselines, hyperparameter sensitivity, inclusion of modern baselines, and alternative kernels.
- Acceptance pros: novel framework, strong theory, and diverse experiments.
- Acceptance cons: modest practical gains, limited scalability demonstration, weak baseline comparisons, and unclear hyperparameter impact.
