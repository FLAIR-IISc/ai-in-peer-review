This paper proposes a novel model for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing two key questions: which item to recommend and when to recommend it. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. To generalize this to a full user-item recommender system and handle data sparsity, the parameters of these processes—the baseline intensity and the self-exciting magnitude—are collected into matrices which are assumed to be low-rank. This leads to a convex optimization problem involving the minimization of the negative log-likelihood subject to non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization. To solve this problem efficiently, the authors develop a hybrid optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, for which they provide a convergence rate analysis. The proposed model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance on item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks compared to several baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The connection established between multi-dimensional Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix models for recommendation is elegant and well-motivated. It provides a principled probabilistic framework for modeling recurrent user activities, capturing both long-term static preferences (baseline intensity) and short-term, history-dependent dynamics (self-excitation). This is a significant departure from traditional static collaborative filtering models.
2.  **Optimization Algorithm:** The proposed optimization algorithm is a non-trivial contribution. The strategy of decomposing the problem and applying proximal gradient steps for the non-negativity constrained variables and conditional gradient steps for the nuclear-norm regularized variables is clever. This avoids the costly full SVD required by standard proximal methods for the nuclear norm.
3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a convergence analysis for the proposed algorithm (Theorem 2), showing that it achieves an optimal rate. This adds significant theoretical rigor to the work.
4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is reasonably thorough, including a synthetic data experiment to verify the model's correctness and parameter recovery, and evaluations on three distinct real-world datasets. The comparison against relevant baselines, including temporal models like STiC and Tensor Factorization, is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of issues related to clarity, technical precision, and methodological depth.

1.  **Clarity and Notational Imprecision:** The technical presentation is frequently unclear, making the work difficult to fully comprehend and reproduce.
    *   In Equation (3), the transition from the standard point process likelihood to the vectorized form `w > u,i φ u,i j` is abrupt. While likely correct, a brief derivation would greatly improve readability. Furthermore, the notation `w_u,i = (Λ0(u, i), A(u, i)) >` seems to contain a typo; `>` should presumably be the transpose symbol `T`.
    *   The pseudocode in Algorithms 1, 2, and 3 is overly high-level and ambiguous. For instance, `∇1(f(Uk−1))` and `∇2(f(Uk−1))` are not explicitly defined. The final update in Algorithm 1, `Y k = (1− δk)Y k−1 + δkXk`, is confusing as `Xk` is not defined; it is presumably composed of `Xk1` and `Xk2`.
    *   The notation in Algorithm 3 is opaque. It is unclear what `−∇2(f(Uk−1))[Z1]` represents. Is it the block of the gradient corresponding to `Z1`? This should be specified. Similar notational ambiguity exists throughout the optimization section.
    *   Several minor formatting and typographical errors are present: "nosiness" instead of "noisiness" (Sec 1), inconsistent hyphenation of "returning-time", `‖Λ0‖∗ 6 λ′` instead of `≤` (Sec 3.2), misplaced punctuation in Sec 3.4(a), and incorrect notation for fractions (`1m ∑...`) and transposes (`u1v > 1`) in Sec 3.4(b) and Algorithm 3.

2.  **Methodological Gaps and Unaddressed Limitations:**
    *   **Practicality of Theorem 1:** The theorem states that the proposed formulation is equivalent to the original one for a sufficiently large `ρ > ρ*`. However, `ρ*` depends on the unknown optimal solution, rendering the theorem of limited practical use for parameter setting. The paper should acknowledge this and discuss the sensitivity to the choice of `ρ` more formally.
    *   **Scalability:** The claim that the algorithm "scales up to millions of user-item pairs" seems overstated given the experiments. The largest dataset has ~26K users. The LMO step in Algorithm 3 requires computing the top singular vector pair of an `m x n` gradient matrix, which costs O(mn) per iteration. This is computationally prohibitive for the millions of users/items found in many real-world systems. A discussion of this limitation and potential scalability solutions (e.g., stochastic or distributed methods) is missing.
    *   **Cold Start Problem:** The model does not address the classic cold-start problem for new users or items, as it relies on existing interaction histories. This is a critical limitation for a practical recommender system and should be explicitly discussed.
    *   **Kernel Choice:** The paper relies exclusively on the exponential triggering kernel. While this simplifies the formulation, it is a restrictive assumption. The brief mention of non-parametric alternatives is insufficient. A discussion of how a more flexible kernel would affect the model's formulation and the tractability of the optimization (specifically, the closed form of `ψ_u,i`) is needed.

3.  **Referencing:** The citation for the nuclear norm as a convex surrogate for rank [24] points to a paper on the NP-completeness of related problems. While relevant to the difficulty of rank minimization, it is not the standard reference for the convex relaxation itself (e.g., Fazel, 2002; Recht et al., 2010). This suggests a lack of care in literature review.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant synthesis of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for an important problem in recommendation.
*   The proposed optimization algorithm and its theoretical analysis are solid contributions.
*   The model demonstrates strong empirical performance on multiple datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a significant lack of clarity and notational precision, particularly in the description of the optimization algorithm, which severely impacts reproducibility.
*   Methodological limitations, including scalability to truly large datasets and the cold-start problem, are not adequately discussed.
*   The accumulation of numerous minor errors (typos, formatting, imprecise statements, questionable citations) detracts from the overall quality of the manuscript and suggests a lack of careful preparation.

While the core contribution of this paper is interesting and potentially significant, the execution is flawed. The technical presentation is not at the level of clarity and precision expected for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The numerous small issues, when taken together, constitute a substantial weakness that prevents a confident assessment of the work's technical soundness and reproducibility. Therefore, I recommend **rejection** for the paper in its current form. I would be open to reconsidering a thoroughly revised version that addresses the aforementioned issues of clarity, precision, and methodological discussion.
