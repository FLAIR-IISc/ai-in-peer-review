Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses two coupled problems: time-sensitive item recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given time) and returning-time prediction (estimating when a user will next interact with an item). It models each observed user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with exponential triggering kernel: λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij). The baseline Λ0 captures long-term preference; the self-exciting term captures short-term, history-dependent bursts. To enable generalization across the unobserved majority of user–item pairs, the paper imposes low-rank structure on the m×n parameter matrices Λ0 and A via nuclear-norm constraints, yielding a convex estimation problem with nonnegativity constraints.

A direct proximal method is costly due to repeated SVDs and the entanglement of nonnegativity with the nuclear norm. The authors introduce an equivalent (for sufficiently large penalty ρ) formulation with auxiliary variables Z1, Z2 and quadratic penalties that decouple the nonnegativity and nuclear norms. They propose an efficient algorithm that alternates proximal-gradient updates on the nonnegative block (simple projection) with conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) updates on the nuclear-norm block (requiring only top singular vectors and a closed-form line search). They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate for the composite algorithm; in special cases it recovers O(1/t^2) (no nuclear norm) or O(1/t) (no nonnegativity).

Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) compare against: homogeneous Poisson (static popularity baseline), STiC (survival/HSMM for return-time only), SVD on implicit counts (static recommendation), and Poisson tensor factorizations with time discretization. The proposed method shows improved ranking and return-time MAE, including on sequences entirely held out per user. A Q–Q analysis using the time-change theorem suggests the Hawkes assumption fits event dynamics better than i.i.d. inter-event models. The approach relates to prior temporal recommendation work based on matrix/tensor factorization and Koren-style temporal dynamics, but differs by modeling continuous-time, history-dependent intensities. It builds on Hawkes process literature (e.g., Du, Song, Zha; Zhou et al.) by introducing a low-rank user–item parametrization and an efficient convex solver.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Unifies time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction under a continuous-time point process with clear semantics for long-term vs. short-term effects.
- Convex estimation: Low-rank structure enforced via nuclear norms with nonnegativity leads to a convex objective, avoiding nonconvex factorization pitfalls.
- Algorithmic contribution: Blended proximal/conditional-gradient method with acceleration and closed-form line search; requires only top singular vectors per iteration; proven O(1/t) convergence.
- Scalability: Synthetic scaling experiments (up to ~10^8 events) and linear empirical scaling with training size; practical for large logs.
- Empirical performance: Consistent improvements over strong static and time-discretized baselines on both ranking and return-time MAE; careful evaluation on held-out sequences and time-change-based goodness-of-fit diagnostics.
- Generality: Framework can incorporate additional context and more flexible kernels; potential applicability beyond recommendation (e.g., EHR).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Equivalence via penalty ρ: Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* is unknown; experiments tune ρ empirically. Provide guidance for setting ρ, sensitivity analysis, or an adaptive scheme. Clarify practical ranges where equivalence holds.
- Missing ablations: Include “Hawkes without low-rank” and “low-rank Poisson only” ablations to isolate the benefit of self-excitation vs. low-rank transfer.
- Model expressiveness: Only self-excitation per user–item; no cross-item (mutual) excitation to capture substitution/complementarity or co-consumption. Discuss/experiment with low-rank multidimensional Hawkes (e.g., structured cross-kernels) or category-level excitation.
- Stability conditions: Hawkes stability (branching ratio < 1) is not discussed. State and enforce conditions (e.g., A(u,i)∫γ < 1) during training; report whether fitted parameters satisfy stability.
- Return-time prediction: Monte Carlo sampling via Ogata may be costly. For the exponential kernel and piecewise-constant history, derive or approximate expectations or use inverse time-change sampling efficiently; report runtime.
- Evaluation metrics: For recommendation, include standard top-k metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k) in addition to rank; report calibration metrics for return-time (e.g., time-rescaled KS test across many sequences, NLL/CRPS).
- Baselines: Compare against continuous-time factorization or recurrent models if available; clarify that held-out items are “new to user” rather than globally unseen. Provide runtime comparisons vs. ADMM or proximal-SVD baselines.
- Clarity: Some notation inconsistencies (γ0 vs. Λ0, missing parentheses in ψ expressions); specify step-size strategy (L or backtracking), kernel bandwidth selection, and hyperparameter ranges. Include memory/time per iteration.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The modeling and optimization are technically sound, leveraging established Hawkes likelihoods and convex surrogates. The algorithmic design is appropriate and the convergence analysis is clear, though the practical handling of ρ warrants more guidance. Experimental protocol is reasonable; additional ablations and baselines would strengthen causal attribution.
- Clarity: Generally well organized with intuitive motivation. Some notation/typo issues and missing implementation details can be improved. Pseudocode is helpful; more detail on gradient computation and complexity would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: The combination of low-rank user–item Hawkes modeling with a convex formulation and a hybrid proximal/Frank–Wolfe solver is novel. Prior Hawkes work focuses on network inference or diffusion; prior recommendation work often discretizes time or omits self-excitation.
- Significance: The approach addresses an important practical problem (timing-aware recommendation) and demonstrates gains on large, real datasets. The optimization technique may be useful beyond this application for nonnegative, low-rank convex problems.

Reasons for acceptance
- Clear, principled continuous-time model that directly targets time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction.
- Convex low-rank formulation with an efficient, provably convergent algorithm.
- Strong empirical performance and scalability.

Reasons for rejection
- Limited analysis of the penalty parameter and lack of ablations to disentangle contributions.
- Restricted model (no cross-item effects) and missing comparisons to closer continuous-time baselines.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps (stability constraints, hyperparameter selection, full metric suite).
