### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to both recommend the right item at the right moment and predict when a user will next interact with an item. The authors propose a unified framework that models the sequence of interactions for each user-item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The intensity of this process for a given pair (u, i) is composed of a constant baseline intensity, representing long-term preference, and a self-exciting component where past interactions increase the probability of future interactions.

To address data sparsity and enable generalization to unobserved user-item pairs, the authors assume that the matrix of baseline intensities (Λ0) and the matrix of self-exciting coefficients (A) are both low-rank. This leads to a convex optimization problem to learn the model parameters by minimizing the negative log-likelihood of all observed event sequences, regularized by the nuclear norms of Λ0 and A, and subject to non-negativity constraints.

A key contribution is a novel and efficient optimization algorithm to solve this problem. Standard methods are either slow (requiring full SVDs at each step) or cannot easily handle the combination of nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. The proposed algorithm reformulates the problem using a penalty method and then blends a proximal gradient step for the non-negative parameters with a conditional gradient (Frank-Wolfe) step for the nuclear-norm regularized variables. This hybrid approach avoids expensive SVDs and comes with a theoretical convergence rate of O(1/t).

The model is evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) on the tasks of item recommendation and returning-time prediction. It demonstrates superior performance against several baselines, including static matrix factorization, Poisson processes, tensor factorization, and a hidden semi-markov model from related work [11].

The work connects several lines of research. It extends classical collaborative filtering [16] to the continuous-time domain, offering a more principled temporal model than discrete-time tensor factorization methods [3, 25]. It builds upon the theory of temporal point processes [10] but applies it to the recommendation setting with a novel low-rank formulation, distinguishing it from prior work that models sequences independently [11, 12]. The optimization technique contributes to the literature on efficient algorithms for structured matrix rank minimization [28, 29].

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Model:** The formulation of time-sensitive recommendation as a low-rank Hawkes process is elegant and well-motivated. It naturally captures both static user preferences (baseline intensity) and dynamic, history-dependent behavior (self-excitation) in a continuous-time framework. This provides a more expressive model than static methods or those that rely on time discretization.

2.  **Technically Sound and Novel Optimization Algorithm:** The paper's primary technical contribution is the development of a scalable algorithm for a challenging optimization problem that combines a smooth loss, non-smooth nuclear norm regularization, and non-negativity constraints. The proposed hybrid of proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods is clever, avoiding the high computational cost of repeated SVDs associated with standard proximal methods for nuclear norm regularization. The provided convergence analysis (Theorem 2), which establishes an optimal O(1/t) rate, provides strong theoretical support for the algorithm. This optimization method could be of independent interest for other problems with similar structure.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The use of synthetic data effectively demonstrates the algorithm's fast convergence, scalability, and ability to recover the ground-truth low-rank matrices.
    *   Experiments on three real-world datasets from different domains (music, e-commerce, healthcare) show the model's wide applicability.
    *   The comparison against a well-chosen set of state-of-the-art baselines is strong, and the proposed model consistently outperforms them on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.
    *   The analysis using the time-change theorem (Figure 3) provides compelling evidence that the Hawkes process is a better fit for the underlying data generating process than alternative survival models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Parametric Kernel Assumption:** The model and experiments rely on a simple exponential triggering kernel. While this choice simplifies computation (e.g., the integral in ψu,i has a closed form), the model's performance could be sensitive to this specific parametric assumption. The paper briefly mentions that non-parametric methods [30] could be used, but the implications of using a more flexible kernel on the model's complexity and the optimization algorithm are not discussed. An ablation study with a different kernel (e.g., power-law) or a discussion on this limitation would strengthen the work.

2.  **Practicality of the Reformulation:** The equivalence between the original problem (4) and the tractable reformulation (5) is established in Theorem 1, which holds for a sufficiently large penalty parameter ρ > ρ*. However, ρ* is a problem-dependent constant that is unknown in practice. The paper follows the standard procedure of treating ρ as a hyperparameter to be tuned via cross-validation. While this is a practical solution, it slightly weakens the theoretical guarantee, as there is no formal guidance on how to set ρ to ensure the equivalence holds.

3.  **Clarity of Scalability Claims on Real Data:** The paper claims scalability to hundreds of millions of events, which is demonstrated on synthetic data. The real-world datasets used are of moderate scale (the largest having ~100K events). While the linear scaling shown in Figure 2(d) is promising, demonstrating this performance on a real-world dataset of a scale comparable to the synthetic one would make the scalability claims more concrete.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes significant contributions to both the field of recommender systems and the area of efficient large-scale optimization. The problem is important, the proposed model is novel and technically sound, and the experimental results are strong. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the core contributions of the work. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and places itself well within the existing literature. It presents a complete piece of research with solid theoretical and empirical backing that is likely to be impactful and inspire future work.
