This paper introduces a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction by connecting self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank models. The core idea is to model user activity as a Hawkes process, leveraging a low-rank structure to generalize across users and items. The authors propose a convex formulation and an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods for parameter estimation. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared to existing approaches.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that often ignore the temporal dynamics of user behavior. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a significant contribution, offering a unified framework for both recommendation and returning-time prediction. The proposed algorithm addresses the challenges of non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization through a clever reformulation, and the convergence analysis provides theoretical guarantees. The experimental results showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method on diverse datasets, including music streaming, e-commerce, and medical records. The inclusion of both synthetic and real data provides a comprehensive evaluation. The extensive related work section and thorough experimental setup are commendable.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. While the technical details are present, they are dense and can be difficult to follow without a strong background in point processes and optimization.  The notation is heavy and could be simplified. The algorithm description, while outlining the core steps, lacks sufficient detail for easy implementation.  Specifically, the line search step in Algorithm 3 (finding αk1 and αk2) is glossed over; a more explicit description of how this is solved is needed.  The connection between the choice of the exponential kernel and the applicability of the closed-form solution in the optimization is not clearly articulated.  While the authors demonstrate empirical superiority, a more detailed ablation study would be beneficial to isolate the impact of different components of the model, such as the low-rank assumption and the specific kernel function.  The convergence analysis, while present, relies on Lipschitz constants which are often hard to establish in practice. Further discussion on practical initialization strategies, and sensitivity to the choice of ρ could be useful.  Finally, the impact of different rank choices `k` on the performance is not clearly illustrated in the experiments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models offers a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The convex formulation and the proposed optimization algorithm are technically sound and supported by a convergence analysis.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to improve the performance of recommendation systems in dynamic environments.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on diverse datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is technically dense and can be difficult to follow.  Better explanation and more detailed algorithmic description are required.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** A more thorough ablation study would help to understand the contribution of each component of the model.
*   **Practical Considerations:** Discussion of practical initialization strategies and parameter tuning would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The technical novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation are strong points. However, the clarity issues and the lack of a comprehensive ablation study slightly detract from the overall impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and provide a more detailed analysis of the model's components. Specifically, the authors should expand on the algorithmic details, provide more intuitive explanations of the technical concepts, and conduct a more thorough ablation study.
