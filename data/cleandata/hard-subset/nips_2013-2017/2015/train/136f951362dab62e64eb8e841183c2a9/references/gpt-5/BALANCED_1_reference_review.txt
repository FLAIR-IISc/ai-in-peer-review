Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user-item interactions as Hawkes processes with pair-specific intensities λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), where Λ0 and A are nonnegative base and self-exciting parameter matrices. To enable generalization across unobserved user-item pairs and combat sparsity, the authors impose low-rank structure via nuclear-norm penalties on Λ0 and A and learn them by maximizing the Hawkes log-likelihood. The key technical contribution is an optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity constraints) and conditional gradient/Frank–Wolfe (for the nuclear norms) on an equivalent penalized problem with auxiliary variables Z1,Z2. The method comes with a convergence guarantee of O(1/t) in function value for the composite objective and an equivalence theorem relating the penalized and constrained problems when the penalty parameter ρ exceeds a problem-dependent threshold. Empirically, the method is evaluated on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) for two tasks: time-sensitive item recommendation (ranking by current intensity) and returning-time prediction (Monte Carlo using Ogata’s thinning). Comparisons include Poisson process, survival-analysis-based STiC, SVD MF, and Poisson tensor factorization.

This work relates to temporal point processes and Hawkes processes (Hawkes ’71; Daley & Vere-Jones), survival analysis for return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.), and low-rank models for recommendation, including tensor factorization with Poisson loss. It is also related to learning Hawkes parameters with low-rank/structured assumptions (e.g., Zhou et al.; Du et al.) but differs in focusing on user-item pairwise self-excitation with convex low-rank matrix regularization and a hybrid optimization scheme.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: Casting time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction into a Hawkes-process framework with low-rank parameter sharing is well-motivated and convex.
- Algorithmic contribution: The proximal–conditional gradient hybrid is elegant and practical—projection for nonnegativity, FW for nuclear norms—avoiding full SVDs and offering provable O(1/t) convergence on the composite objective.
- Empirical scope: Synthetic study at large event counts (100M) and three real datasets across distinct domains; the evaluation includes both ranking and time prediction, and a “held-out items per user” setting that tests generalization.
- Interpretability and extensibility: The decomposition into baseline and history-driven components is interpretable; extensions to contextual features and kernels are discussed.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Stability constraints: For Hawkes processes with exponential kernels, subcriticality (branching ratio A(u,i)∫γ < 1) is typically required for stability/finite intensity. The estimation imposes nonnegativity but appears not to enforce subcriticality. Please discuss whether constraints like A(u,i)σ < 1 are imposed, their impact, or how instability is avoided in practice.
- Equivalence theorem practicality: Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice, ρ is tuned. Please clarify sensitivity to ρ, provide ablations, and discuss how one can select ρ reliably without overfitting.
- Baselines: The tensor baselines (“Last” and “Avg”) are not necessarily state-of-the-art choices for time-varying recommendation; stronger temporal factorization baselines (e.g., dynamic Poisson tensor models with smoothly varying factors) or marked point process models with learned kernels would strengthen the case. For returning time, likelihood-based evaluations (time-rescaling KS or log-likelihood on held-out events) would be complementary to MAE.
- Returning-time estimation: Monte Carlo thinning per test case may be computationally heavy. For exponential kernels, can you exploit the conditional Poisson superposition to derive efficient sampling or approximations to E[Tn+1|history]? A complexity analysis would help.
- Statistical guarantees: While optimization convergence is proved, there is no discussion of statistical consistency or sample complexity for recovering low-rank Λ0 and A under event noise. Even a brief discussion of identifiability and conditions under which the convex program recovers the low-rank structure would add rigor.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. However:
  - Please define ψ and φ explicitly when first introduced, and ensure dimensions are clear.
  - Provide per-iteration time/memory complexity, including how you compute the history sums efficiently (e.g., recursions for exponential kernels) and the cost of top singular vectors.
  - Report standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k, MRR) in addition to raw rank for interpretability, and clarify whether ties or unobserved items are handled.
  - Include more details on hyperparameter tuning, validation splits, and whether the same validation scheme is used across baselines to ensure fairness.

Originality
- The modeling combination (pairwise Hawkes with low-rank sharing via nuclear norms) and the hybrid proximal–FW algorithm for nonnegativity+nuclear-norm constraints are novel and interesting. Related work on low-rank Hawkes parameterizations (e.g., network Hawkes with low-rank influence) should be contrasted more explicitly to highlight differences (e.g., per-pair self-excitation vs. cross-excitation networks).
- Please discuss the novelty relative to prior mixed proximal/FW block-coordinate schemes, if any, and why your analysis is new.

Significance
- The setting—time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction—is important. The convex formulation with scalable optimization is potentially impactful.
- That said, the empirical validation, while promising, falls short of fully demonstrating scalability “to millions of user-item pairs”: real datasets are modest in pair count, though event counts are large. Including a larger-scale real-world run, wall-clock comparisons, and convergence plots would strengthen claims.
- The model currently captures only self-excitation within user-item pairs; many applications (e.g., co-morbidities) involve cross-excitation across items. A discussion or small experiment with mutual-excitation (block low-rank or tensorized A) would broaden significance.

Questions for the authors
1. Do you enforce subcriticality constraints on A(u,i)? If not, did you observe unstable sequences or exploding intensities?
2. How sensitive are results to the kernel bandwidth σ, and how was it selected?
3. What is the computational cost of return-time prediction per test case, and how does it scale?
4. How are cold-start items (never seen for any user) handled? Can side information be incorporated in Λ0 as suggested?
5. Can the method be extended to model cross-excitation between items for the same user (i.e., multi-dimensional Hawkes), and would convexity be preserved?

Pros
- Convex, well-motivated model linking Hawkes processes and low-rank recommendation.
- Hybrid optimization with clean convergence analysis and practical efficiency.
- Solid empirical gains on both ranking and return-time prediction; reasonable generalization to held-out user-item pairs.

Cons
- Missing stability constraints and statistical recovery guarantees.
- Baselines and metrics could be stronger/more standard; limited large-scale real validation.
- Practical selection of ρ not grounded; sensitivity analyses lacking.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium
This is a solid, thoughtfully executed contribution at the intersection of point processes and recommendation. Addressing the noted concerns—especially stability, stronger baselines/metrics, and more detailed scalability evidence—would further strengthen the paper.
