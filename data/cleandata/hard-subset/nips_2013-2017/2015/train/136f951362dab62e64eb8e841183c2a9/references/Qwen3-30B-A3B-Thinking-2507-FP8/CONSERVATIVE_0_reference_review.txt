

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors claim to address two related problems: recommending relevant items at the right time and predicting user returning times. The core idea is to connect self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix factorization, proposing a convex optimization formulation with nuclear norm regularization. The authors develop an optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, claiming to achieve O(1/t) convergence rate.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper addresses an important problem in recommendation systems - capturing temporal dynamics of user behavior rather than focusing solely on static preferences.

2. The theoretical foundation using Hawkes processes is well-motivated for modeling self-exciting event sequences, and the connection to low-rank matrix factorization is conceptually reasonable.

3. The paper presents a theoretically grounded optimization algorithm with convergence rate analysis, which is a valuable contribution to the optimization literature.

4. The experimental evaluation includes both synthetic data and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), providing some evidence of practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of significant novelty:** While the paper claims "novel convex formulation," the connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation is not substantially different from prior work. Kapoor et al. [11, 12] already explored similar temporal modeling for recommendation, and the low-rank assumption is standard in collaborative filtering. The paper fails to clearly articulate how their approach fundamentally differs from these prior works.

2. **Insufficient empirical validation:** The experimental results show marginal improvements over baselines (e.g., SVD, Poisson process, tensor factorization). The paper claims "superb predictive performance" but Figure 2(e-f) shows only modest gains (e.g., 5-10% improvement in MAE). Crucially, the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the gains are meaningful.

3. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art:** The paper compares against Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization, but fails to include more recent, established methods for time-sensitive recommendation. For instance, the paper cites "Collaborative filtering with temporal dynamics" [16] but doesn't compare against this seminal work. The comparison with STiC is also limited to returning-time prediction, ignoring STiC's inherent limitation of not being able to recommend new items (which the authors claim is "a crucial difference" without sufficient evidence).

4. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims to "solve" the time-sensitive recommendation problem, but the experiments don't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method substantially outperforms existing approaches. The phrase "superb predictive performance" is unsubstantiated by the data presented.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't adequately address when the low-rank assumption might fail, how the method handles extremely sparse user-item interactions, or how the exponential triggering kernel choice affects performance. The claim that "the proposed model can be readily generalized to incorporate other contextual information" lacks specific evidence.

**Critical Evaluation:**

While the paper presents a technically sound approach that builds on established concepts (Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix factorization), it fails to demonstrate a meaningful advance over existing methods. The optimization algorithm is a blend of known techniques rather than a novel contribution, and the empirical results don't provide compelling evidence that this approach is significantly better than established baselines for the time-sensitive recommendation problem.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. More thorough comparison with state-of-the-art time-sensitive recommendation methods
2. Statistical significance testing for performance improvements
3. Clearer articulation of how the approach differs from prior work (particularly Kapoor et al.)
4. A more balanced discussion of limitations and failure cases

**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper presents a technically sound approach, it fails to provide sufficient evidence of significant improvement over established methods. The novelty claim is overstated, and the empirical validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims. The contribution appears to be incremental rather than a substantial advance in the field. The paper would benefit from more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods and stronger empirical evidence before being considered for publication.
