This paper presents a novel and ambitious framework for time-sensitive recommendation and returning-time prediction, connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The core idea – leveraging Hawkes processes to model recurrent user activities and then employing low-rank approximations to scale to large datasets – is genuinely interesting and represents a significant departure from traditional recommendation approaches focused on static preferences. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the proposed optimization algorithm, blending proximal and conditional gradient methods, demonstrates a commendable effort to tackle the computational challenges. The convergence analysis, achieving O(1/t), further strengthens the technical contribution.

The strength of this work lies in its novelty. Bridging Hawkes processes and low-rank models in this context is a clever idea with potential for broad impact, especially in applications requiring dynamic personalization and prediction. The ability to model dependencies between items (or diseases, as highlighted in the introduction) is a significant advantage over methods assuming independence. The authors also acknowledge and address scalability concerns, which is crucial for real-world applications. The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets provide supporting evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach, consistently outperforming several baselines. The inclusion of detailed convergence analysis and algorithmic complexity is also appreciated.

However, despite the strong conceptual framework, some aspects require further refinement. The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, could benefit from more detailed ablation studies. For instance, a clearer demonstration of the benefits of the low-rank approximation would be valuable.  How much performance is lost by *not* using the low-rank structure? Further, the comparisons to tensor factorization methods (Tensor-#-Last and Tensor-#-Avg) feel somewhat superficial. The authors state these methods predict returning time by looking at different time intervals, but don’t fully explain *why* their method outperforms them in this regard.  A deeper analysis of the limitations of these baselines in the context of the specific problem would enhance the paper’s impact.

The presentation, while generally clear, is occasionally dense. Certain sections, particularly those detailing the optimization algorithm, could benefit from more intuitive explanations and visual aids. While the authors acknowledge the choice of the exponential kernel, a brief discussion on the robustness of the results to different kernel choices would be beneficial. Finally, while the authors address potential extensions (incorporating contextual information), demonstrating this in the experiments would strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The core idea of combining Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is genuinely novel.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the convergence analysis is well-established.
*   **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines.
*   **Potential Impact:** The proposed framework has the potential to significantly advance the field of recommendation systems and beyond (e.g., healthcare).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Experimental Depth:** Ablation studies and a more in-depth comparison to tensor factorization methods are needed.
*   **Presentation Clarity:** Certain sections could benefit from improved clarity and visual aids.
*   **Limited Exploration of Extensions:** The claimed extensibility to incorporate contextual information is not experimentally validated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a significant and potentially groundbreaking contribution to the field of recommendation systems. While some refinements to the experimental evaluation and presentation are desirable, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising empirical results strongly outweigh the weaknesses. The authors have tackled a challenging problem with a bold and innovative approach. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above. The potential impact of this work justifies overlooking the imperfections. This is the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that NIPS should be supporting.
